Summary and contributions
- The paper augments standard text-based sentiment analysis with “cognitive” features derived from human eye-tracking while annotators read the same sentences. It defines basic gaze statistics (fixations, saccades, regressions) and graph-based “gaze-saliency” features, and reports cross-validated results on two small public datasets with eye-tracking. It further analyzes a held-out subset enriched with sarcasm/irony.
- As I see it, the primary contribution is an empirical exploration of whether gaze-derived features correlate with sentiment polarity (and especially with sarcasm/irony cases) on datasets where eye-tracking is available. A secondary contribution is the proposal of specific engineered gaze features, including graph-based degrees meant to capture surprisal/incongruity. A tertiary contribution is a limited analysis suggesting these features can help on irony/sarcasm cases.

Strengths
- Interesting problem framing grounded in psycholinguistics. Using gaze as a proxy for processing difficulty/incongruity is a plausible hypothesis for capturing phenomena hard to detect from text alone (sarcasm, thwarting). The feature design is thoughtful and reasonably motivated by prior reading research.
- Use of two public eye-tracking datasets and consistent cross-validation within each. The paper also separates a held-out subset enriched with complex constructs, which is the right place to test the intended benefits.
- Clear ablations show that textual sarcasm/thwarting features add more than basic sentiment features, and that gaze provides additional (albeit modest) gains on top. The held-out subset indicates relatively larger improvements on sarcasm/irony, which aligns with the paper’s hypothesis.
- Some attempt at statistical testing and feature importance analysis, and discussion of error sources and feasibility constraints.

Weaknesses
- Unrealistic test-time assumption. The proposed classifier requires eye-tracking features at inference. This severely limits practical utility and undercuts the contribution as a sentiment analysis method. The “feasibility” section is speculative (mobile eye trackers, user consent) and does not resolve deployment. A more convincing approach would treat gaze as privileged information available only during training (e.g., SVM+, distillation, multi-view learning), or learn a text-only model that imitates gaze signals; neither is attempted.
- Weak baselines and limited comparisons. The paper excludes contemporary neural baselines (CNN/RNN/Transformer) on the grounds of being “feature based,” but that is precisely what strong baselines are for. There is no comparison to fine-tuned transformer models on these datasets (text-only), nor to strong lexicon+syntax baselines optimized in-domain. The only external baselines are trained on other corpora and then tested on D1/D2, which is not directly comparable to the cross-validated models trained on D1/D2. As a result, the reported gains are hard to interpret.
- Modest absolute gains from gaze given the cost. After adding sarcasm/thwarting features, gaze adds only 0.6–1.5 F1 on the main datasets. Given the substantial additional infrastructure and privacy overhead implied by eye-tracking, the cost-benefit is unconvincing for general SA. The held-out irony subset shows larger relative gains, but still rests on the unrealistic test-time requirement.
- Methodological concerns. (a) The use of McNemar’s test is described “on F-scores,” which is improper; McNemar is for paired accuracy on the same instances, not F1. If the test was on accuracy-based contingency, this must be clarified. (b) PCA for dimensionality reduction should be fit on training folds only; it is unclear if this was enforced to avoid leakage. (c) There is no discussion of class imbalance handling, hyperparameter selection, or nested CV. (d) The two datasets use different eye-tracking hardware (500Hz vs 120Hz), yet the paper provides no normalization across devices/participants (e.g., per-participant z-scoring, duration normalization), which can confound duration-based features. (e) Mapping fixations to words, outlier handling, and graph-construction details are insufficiently specified for reproduction.
- Possible circularity and annotation-process bias. Eye movements were recorded while annotators explicitly assigned sentiment labels to these same sentences. Gaze patterns in this task setting may reflect the labeling process (including strategic rereading) rather than natural reading, and may be correlated with the eventual labels. This raises questions about whether the model is learning artifacts of the annotation process rather than generalizable signals of sentiment.
- Limited external validity. The datasets are small (~1K each) and heavy in sarcasm/irony; there is no demonstration on standard benchmarks (e.g., SST-2) or cross-domain generalization. The “held-out” set is a subset of Dataset 1, not an independent corpus. The feature-importance table (Table 3) is referenced but not shown here; the robustness of results across domains and readers remains unclear. References and presentation have several inconsistencies, impairing clarity.

Questions to authors
- How do you envision practical inference without gaze? Can you provide results for a text-only model trained with gaze as privileged information (e.g., distillation/SVM+) and report whether it recovers the “gaze gains” at test time?
- Did you normalize eye-tracking features per participant and per device (e.g., z-scoring durations, saccade metrics) to address hardware differences and reader variability? If so, please detail. If not, can you re-run with normalization?
- Was PCA fit within each training fold only? Please clarify all steps taken to avoid train–test leakage (e.g., feature scaling, lexicon construction, sarcasm phrase bootstrapping).
- What exactly was tested with McNemar? Please report paired accuracy contingency tables and p-values on the same test splits, and avoid testing on F1.
- How are labels aggregated (majority vote?) and are readers whose gaze contributed to a sentence’s features the same ones whose labels determined gold truth? Could you evaluate with leave-one-reader-out setups to reduce circularity?
- Can you include competitive text-only baselines (e.g., fine-tuned BERT/RoBERTa) on D1/D2 and on a standard SA benchmark to contextualize the added value of gaze?

Overall, while the analysis is interesting, the reliance on eye-tracking at test time, methodological gaps, and weak baselines make the current evidence insufficient for acceptance.
