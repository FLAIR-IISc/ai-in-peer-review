- Paper augments sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking and defines basic and graph-based gaze features.  
- Evaluates gaze features with NB/SVM/MLP on two eye-tracking datasets, showing small but significant F1 gains overall and larger gains on ironic/sarcastic texts.  
- Contributions: empirical evidence of gaze benefits for SA, a set of engineered gaze features including a gaze-saliency graph, and analysis showing many gaze features rank highly by chi-squared.  

- Strength: Focuses on hard SA phenomena (implicit sentiment, sarcasm, thwarting) where gaze gives additive improvements (e.g., +0.6–1.5 F1 overall; +4.2 F1 on ironic subset).  
- Strength: Observes cross-dataset consistency despite different eye-tracker hardware, suggesting some robustness.  
- Strength: Provides feature significance and ablation analyses showing gaze features often among top features.  
- Strength: Uses statistical testing (McNemar) to assess significance of small gains.  
- Strength: Discusses deployability and feasibility of eye-tracking in real-world scenarios.  

- Weakness: Assumes gaze is available at inference time, which is unrealistic for most applications; doesn’t explore learning-with-privileged-information alternatives.  
- Weakness: Lacks strong modern text-only baselines (CNN/RNN/transformers or richer n-gram/embedding models), making incremental value of gaze unclear.  
- Weakness: Absolute F1 gains on full datasets are small (0.6–1.5 points), raising cost–benefit concerns; larger gains shown only on an internal subset.  
- Weakness: Gaze-graph feature definitions are ambiguous, inconsistently named, and hard to reproduce.  
- Weakness: Potential confounds from hardware differences, sentence length, and annotator behavior; normalization and control analyses are insufficiently detailed.  
- Weakness: Labels come from same annotators whose gaze is used, risking annotator-specific bias driving results.  
- Weakness: Some reported table results appear inconsistent, suggesting possible reporting or implementation issues.  

- Question: Do models require gaze at test time; can a training-only (privileged/distillation) variant be provided and evaluated?  
- Question: How are fixation durations/saccade lengths normalized across devices and readers (ms vs samples; per-sentence/reader z-normalization)?  
- Question: How are gold labels determined and are gaze features aggregated across the same annotators; were leave-one-annotator-out analyses tried?  
- Question: Please verify inconsistent Table 2 entries (e.g., Sn+Gz on D2 under SVM) and explain cases where Gz outperforms Sn+Gz.  
- Question: Can stronger text-only baselines (pretrained embeddings, CNN/LSTM, transformer fine-tuning) be added to contextualize gains?  
- Question: Will code and feature extraction scripts, especially for gaze-saliency graphs, be released for reproducibility?  

- Additional: Report per-phenomenon results (sarcasm/irony/non-irony) on full datasets, not only the held-out subset.  
- Additional: Clarify PCA, vocabulary construction, and tokenization/POS/NE pipelines.  
- Additional: Tone down “first of its kind” claims given related prior work.  

- Overall: Innovative and interesting empirical signals, but practical impact and baseline comparisons are currently insufficiently convincing; strengthening framing, baselines, feature clarity, and reproducibility is needed.
