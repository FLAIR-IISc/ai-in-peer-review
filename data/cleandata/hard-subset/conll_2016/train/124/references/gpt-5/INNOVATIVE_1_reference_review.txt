Summary and claimed contributions
This paper augments traditional text-based sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye movements while annotating short texts. It introduces both basic gaze features (fixations, saccades, regressions, skips) and more complex graph-based “gaze-saliency” features, and evaluates their utility on two eye-tracking datasets rich in sarcasm/irony. The authors report consistent gains over text-only baselines, with larger improvements on sarcastic/ironic cases.

Contributions as I see them (in order of significance):
1) Introduction of gaze-based cognitive features for sentiment analysis, including a novel saliency-graph formulation. This is a bold and genuinely new direction for SA.
2) Empirical evidence that gaze features help most on semantically/pragmatically complex phenomena (sarcasm/irony/thwarting), supported by ablations and significance tests.
3) Feature analysis indicating that multiple gaze features rank highly by chi-squared importance, suggesting nontrivial signal beyond text features.

Strengths
- Novel and ambitious signal for SA: Using eye-tracking to capture cognitive processing during reading addresses precisely those aspects where surface text features struggle (implicit sentiment, irony, incongruity). The saliency-graph idea is creative and grounded in psycholinguistic intuition (e.g., regressions for reanalysis, saccade length for structural complexity).
- Targeted evaluation on genuinely hard cases: The chosen datasets are known to contain sarcasm/irony, and the paper verifies that standard SA systems underperform here. The held-out analysis further shows larger gains for complex constructs (6.1% relative) than simple ones (2.1%).
- Careful ablations and significance checks: The study isolates the contributions of sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting features, and gaze features. McNemar tests are reported, and a chi-squared feature ranking shows multiple gaze features among the top predictors.
- Cross-dataset use and hardware variability: Despite different eye-trackers (500Hz vs 120Hz) and genres, gaze-augmented models consistently improve over text-only ones. This suggests the cognitive signal is robust enough to survive some variability.
- Clear articulation of a research direction: The paper opens a promising line toward cognition-aware NLP. Even if immediate deployment is challenging, the conceptual advance is valuable for the community.

Weaknesses
- Practicality at inference time: The approach requires gaze at test time. The feasibility discussion is interesting but speculative; no concrete pathway is provided to make a text-only model benefit from gaze (e.g., knowledge distillation/multi-view training to transfer cognitive signals to models that don’t need gaze at inference).
- Modest absolute gains from gaze: On top of sentiment+sarcasm features, gaze yields 0.6–1.5 F1 points. Most of the total improvement is from sarcasm/thwarting features. This raises the bar for justifying the added complexity and data requirements of eye-tracking.
- Potential confounds and missing controls: The two datasets use different eye-tracker sampling rates (500Hz vs 120Hz). Duration-based features can be sensitive to sampling and calibration; the paper does not describe normalization across devices or subjects (e.g., per-subject z-scoring or rate correction), which could confound cross-dataset results.
- Limited generalization analysis: Gaze features are averaged across participants. There is no reader generalization study (e.g., leave-one-annotator-out), sensitivity to the number of readers, or breakdown of variance that might indicate robustness when only a single reader is available.
- Baselines lag current practice: The paper does not compare against strong text-only neural baselines for SA/sarcasm (e.g., transformer-based models), which would put the incremental value of gaze in sharper relief. Some classifier choices/hyperparameters are “default,” and unigram PCA choices are under-specified, reducing the strength of the empirical claim.
- Reproducibility/clarity issues: The saliency-graph feature definitions (weights for F1H/F2H, PSH/PSD, RSH/RSD, etc.) need crisper mathematical specification, and it’s unclear whether code will be released.

Questions to authors
1) How did you normalize duration-based features across the 500Hz and 120Hz devices and across readers? If no explicit normalization was done, can you recompute using rate-corrected or per-subject z-scored measures and report the impact?
2) What happens under reader generalization? Please report results with leave-one-participant-out and with a single reader’s gaze at test time. How many readers are needed before gains become reliable?
3) Can the model be used without gaze at inference? For example, train with gaze as an auxiliary view/teacher and deploy a text-only student. Any preliminary results?
4) How sensitive are the gains to stronger text baselines (e.g., transformer-based models fine-tuned on sentiment and sarcasm)? Do gaze features still add value?
5) Please formalize the saliency-graph features precisely: define edge weights for each variant, and how “highest/second-highest weighted degree” is computed, especially when nodes can repeat for repeated word fixations.
6) Did you control for annotator bias (gaze recorded during labeling the same target)? Could reading behavior be reflecting decision strategy rather than generalizable cognitive load?
7) Ethical aspects: did you obtain IRB/ethics approval for eye-tracking and data sharing? How are privacy concerns addressed?

Overall, I find the central idea novel and exciting for CoNLL. Despite practical constraints and some methodological gaps, the paper opens a distinctive, cognitively grounded direction for sentiment analysis and provides initial evidence that the signal is most useful where text-only cues falter. I encourage the authors to bolster normalization, reader generalization, and text-only deployment pathways; even preliminary results there would strengthen the case substantially.
