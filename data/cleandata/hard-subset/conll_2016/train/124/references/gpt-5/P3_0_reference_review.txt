Summary and contributions
- This paper investigates augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking during sentiment annotation. It designs both basic gaze features (fixations, saccades, regressions) and graph-based “gaze-saliency” features, and evaluates their utility alongside traditional sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features.
- On two small, publicly available eye-tracking datasets with many sarcastic/ironic items, the paper reports modest but statistically significant gains from adding gaze features to text-based features, and larger gains on a held-out subset rich in complex constructs.
- The paper provides a feature-importance analysis suggesting multiple gaze features rank among the most informative.

Strengths
- Focus on hard cases: The datasets intentionally contain sarcasm/irony and implicit sentiment—settings where text-only features often struggle. The ablation on a held-out irony subset shows larger gains from gaze (6.1% absolute F1), aligning with the hypothesis that gaze captures processing difficulty/incongruity beyond surface cues.
- Methodological breadth of cognitive features: The feature design covers both low-level measures (first fixation duration, regressions, skips) with psycholinguistic grounding and higher-level graph-based measures. Even if some graph definitions need clarification, the attempt to model interaction across words is novel within SA.
- Cross-dataset evaluation and hardware diversity: Results are reported on two different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000, Tobii T120). The presence of gains in both settings suggests some robustness to sensor quality.
- Statistical testing and feature importance: The use of McNemar testing (albeit incompletely reported) and chi-squared feature ranking adds rigor and interpretability; the prominence of gaze features among top-ranked features supports the central claim.
- Clear articulation of feasibility and applications: The discussion on mobile eye-tracking and potential application scenarios (e-commerce reviews, etc.) is thoughtful and highlights a path—albeit speculative—towards practical use.

Weaknesses
- Limited and outdated baselines: The paper does not compare against modern text-only baselines (e.g., BiLSTMs/CNNs, contextual transformers such as BERT/RoBERTa fine-tuned on sentiment or sarcasm). Given that strong text models often capture subtle cues, the incremental gains from gaze (0.6–1.5% absolute over Sn+Sr on D1/D2) might vanish against contemporary baselines. Ruling out deep learning because the approach is “feature-based” weakens the empirical claim.
- Dataset size, design, and generalization: Both datasets are small (994 and 843 instances after filtering), curated, and collected under an annotation protocol that may entangle gaze behavior with the act of labeling itself (potential label leakage through task demands). The cross-validation is intra-dataset; no subject-level or domain-level splits are reported. It is unclear if gains would hold on out-of-domain text or on eye-tracking acquired during natural reading rather than annotation.
- Methodological clarity and potential leakage: PCA to 500 dimensions for unigrams and for “implicit incongruity” phrases is used, but it is not stated whether PCA and any feature normalization are fit only on training folds (nested within CV) to avoid test leakage. Similarly, feature selection/chi-squared ranking appears to be done globally; details are missing. The statistical test is reported only for D1; the significance for D2 (where the incremental gain is 1.5%) is not reported.
- Ambiguities/inconsistencies in gaze graph features: Several graph-based feature definitions are unclear or duplicated (e.g., F1H/F1S vs F2H/F2S, PSH/PSS vs PSDH/PSDS, RSH/RSS vs RSDH/RSDS), and the choice of “highest and second highest weighted degree” lacks a principled justification beyond qualitative observations. This hinders reproducibility and undermines interpretability.
- Baseline sanity checks are confounded: The preliminary NB/SVM/RB systems trained on Pang+Lee and Sentiment140 and tested on D1/D2 conflate distribution shift with intrinsic difficulty, making it hard to conclude that D1/D2 are “challenging.” Stronger in-domain baselines (including sarcasm detectors trained on similar domains) should be included for a fairer assessment.
- Practicality and ethics: The feasibility discussion relies on speculative availability of mobile eye-tracking and does not adequately address privacy, consent granularity, demographic bias in gaze behavior, calibration requirements, or deployment costs. Without a realistic data acquisition protocol, the practical impact remains limited.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Can you include comparisons to strong modern text-only baselines (e.g., fine-tuned BERT/RoBERTa; a state-of-the-art sarcasm detector) on D1/D2? How do gaze gains compare when the text baseline is stronger?
- Cross-validation protocol: Was PCA, feature scaling, and any feature selection fit exclusively on the training fold within each CV split? If not, please re-run with a leakage-free, nested pipeline.
- Subject-level splits: Did you try splits where sentences read by annotator A do not appear in both train and test, or where you train on one device and test on the other? Even though you average across annotators per sentence, user-specific gaze idiosyncrasies could still affect generalization.
- Statistical significance on D2: Is the 1.5% absolute F1 improvement from adding gaze over Sn+Sr on Dataset 2 statistically significant via a paired test? Please report the contingency table or exact p-value.
- Gaze-only vs text-only: Gaze-only features perform close to chance. Can you probe whether only a subset of gaze signals (e.g., regressions or RSF/LREG) is responsible for the gains when combined with text? An ablation within gaze features and confidence intervals would help.
- Graph features: Please provide precise, non-overlapping definitions, equations, and a rationale for each graph-based feature. Why “largest/second-largest degree” rather than distributional statistics? Did you try simpler sequence-aware features (e.g., position-weighted regressions)?
- Annotation confound: Since gaze was collected during sentiment labeling, how do you rule out task-induced artifacts (e.g., readers re-checking text after deciding polarity) versus linguistic difficulty? Any evidence from natural reading without labeling instructions?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and exact train/test splits, and are the gaze datasets fully accessible? Can you provide per-class P/R, and performance specifically on sarcastic vs non-sarcastic portions for both datasets?

Overall, the idea of leveraging gaze for sentiment analysis on complex constructs is interesting and potentially impactful in cognitive NLP, but the current empirical evidence is not yet compelling against modern text baselines, and several methodological and clarity issues need to be addressed.
