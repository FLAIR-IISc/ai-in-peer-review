Summary and claimed contributions
The paper investigates augmenting traditional sentiment analysis (SA) features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking. Using two publicly available eye-tracking datasets of short texts (rich in sarcasm/irony), the authors engineer (i) standard sentiment features, (ii) features targeted at sarcasm/thwarting, and (iii) gaze features, including a novel “gaze saliency graph.” They report that adding gaze features improves F1 over textual features alone, with larger gains on instances involving complex constructs (irony/sarcasm). They include feature-importance analyses and significance tests, and discuss feasibility via mobile eye-trackers.

In my view, main contributions (in order):
1) Empirical evidence that eye-tracking–based cognitive features can improve SA for texts with complex pragmatic phenomena (irony/sarcasm), with careful ablations including a held-out subset that isolates such cases.
2) A concrete set of gaze-derived features, including graph-based “saliency graph” features, and an analysis showing these features rank highly by chi-squared importance.
3) A comprehensive feature-engineering baseline that integrates sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting cues, providing a strong textual baseline against which gaze features are evaluated.

Strengths
- Clear empirical signal that gaze features help on complex cases. On the held-out subset, adding gaze features to sentiment+sarcasm features improves F1 more strongly for irony/sarcasm (4.2 F1 absolute, 6.1% relative) than for simpler texts (1.7 absolute, 2.1% relative), aligning with the paper’s motivation. This is an informative, task-relevant analysis that goes beyond average scores.
- Novel and interpretable gaze features, including a graph-based representation. The gaze saliency graph and regression-related features are intuitively linked to psycholinguistic constructs (surprisal, reanalysis), and the feature-importance analysis (7–10 of top-20 are gaze features, depending on dataset) supports their utility.
- Competitive results on a challenging dataset. On Dataset 1 (rich in sarcasm/irony), the best model (SVM with Sn+Sr+Gz) achieves F1=73.5, clearly surpassing the external SVM/NB baselines (64.9/66.2), suggesting that modeling sarcasm/thwarting and gaze can close gaps left by standard SA.
- Statistical rigor and ablations. The paper performs 10-fold CV, ablation across feature groups, and uses McNemar tests to assess significance between close results. This increases confidence in reported gains.
- Interesting cross-disciplinary angle. Bringing cognitive evidence into SA is timely and conceptually valuable; the paper connects features to known psycholinguistic findings (e.g., regressions for reanalysis), which strengthens the rationale.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and external validity: gaze required at test time. The approach assumes access to eye-tracking signals for inference. While feasibility is discussed, the current evaluation does not simulate realistic deployment (no results using only text at test time). The reported gains, therefore, may not translate to practical SA systems. A stronger study would explore: (a) learning to predict gaze proxies from text, or (b) semi-supervised/multiview setups where gaze is only used during training.
- Missing comparisons to stronger textual baselines (e.g., modern deep models). The paper explicitly sets aside deep learning. Yet state-of-the-art SA (even on short texts) uses CNN/RNN/transformers; pre-trained encoders can model complex phenomena to some extent. Without such baselines, the “state-of-the-art improvement” claim is overstated. Moreover, on Dataset 2, the best gaze-augmented SVM (71.8 F1) underperforms the external SVM baseline trained on large corpora (76.8 F1), raising questions about generality.
- Limited clarity and some inconsistencies in feature definitions and evaluation. Several graph feature definitions are ambiguous or duplicated (e.g., F1H/F2H vs F1S/F2S; PSDH/PSDS; RSDH/RSDS appear similarly defined). Details on gold label aggregation (majority vote? how ties are handled), tokenization/AoI mapping, and how the held-out subset is trained/evaluated (trained on remaining D1 folds? hyperparameters tuned where?) are missing. Typos and formatting issues hinder reproducibility.
- Potential confounds and leakage. Eye-tracking signals reflect general reading difficulty (length, frequency, syntax) as well as sentiment-related pragmatics. While the paper includes readability and length features, a more thorough control (e.g., frequency, surprisal, syntax depth) would help isolate the contribution of cognitive cues to sentiment per se. Additionally, it is unclear whether the LP feature trained on external data could introduce domain leakage; more detail is needed.
- Modest incremental gains from gaze over strong textual features. The main gains come from adding sarcasm/thwarting features to sentiment features (e.g., +3.1 and +7.8 F1), with gaze features adding a smaller increment (+0.6 and +1.5 F1). While the held-out irony analysis is compelling, average improvements are modest and sometimes inconsistent across datasets.

Questions to authors
- Labels: How are gold labels obtained from multiple annotators (majority vote? how are ties resolved)? Are any items filtered due to low agreement?
- Held-out subset: What is the exact training protocol? Is the model trained on the remaining D1 items only? Any tuning on the held-out set?
- Gaze aggregation: Are features averaged over readers per sentence? Have you tried subject-level cross-validation (leave-one-subject-out) to ensure generalization across readers?
- Feature definitions: Please clarify the saliency graph features (F1H/F2H vs F1S/F2S; PSDH vs PSDS; RSDH vs RSDS) and provide formal definitions. Will you release code?
- Baselines: Can you compare against a strong textual deep baseline (e.g., CNN/LSTM/BERT/RoBERTa fine-tuned) to contextualize the added value of gaze?

Additional comments
- The claim of “state-of-the-art improvement” should be qualified given the absence of modern deep baselines and the lower performance on Dataset 2 vs external SVM/NB.
- Consider experiments where gaze is used only during training (e.g., multi-view/co-training, distillation to a text-only model), which would greatly improve practicality.
- Please proofread; several typos and formatting glitches impede readability.
