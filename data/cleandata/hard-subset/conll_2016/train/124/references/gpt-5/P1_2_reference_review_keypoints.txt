- Summary: Augmenting sentiment analysis with engineered eye-tracking (gaze) features yields modest but statistically significant F-score improvements, larger on irony-heavy examples.

- Contribution 1: Empirical evidence that eye-tracking features can slightly but significantly improve short-text SA, especially for irony/sarcasm, across two datasets and hardware setups.

- Contribution 2: A large set of engineered gaze features, including graph-based gaze-saliency representations, that summarize saccade/fixation patterns.

- Contribution 3: Focused analysis on an irony-rich held-out subset showing larger gains for figurative and complex sentiment constructs.

- Strength: Targets a hard SA slice (sarcasm/implicit sentiment) where text-only methods struggle and shows larger gains on ironic content.

- Strength: Cross-dataset evaluation and statistical testing (including McNemar’s test) support some robustness to hardware variation.

- Strength: Comprehensive feature engineering and ablation studies isolating contributions of unigrams, SA features, sarcasm features, and gaze across classifiers.

- Strength: Timely relevance bridging psycholinguistics and NLP; eye movement plausibly reflects processing difficulty tied to figurative language.

- Strength: Use of public eye-tracking datasets promotes reproducibility and follow-up work.

- Weakness: Requires gaze at inference time, which is impractical in most real-world SA applications and feasibility discussion is speculative.

- Weakness: Overall gains are modest and inconsistent across classifiers; gaze-only features underperform strong text features.

- Weakness: Potential confound as gaze was recorded during labeling, risking circularity between labeling behavior and gaze signals.

- Weakness: Averaging gaze across annotators may overstate stability and lacks leave-one-reader-out testing to show generalization across readers.

- Weakness: Weak textual baselines (unigram-PCA) and lack of modern in-domain baselines (CNN/RNN/transformers) make it unclear how gaze compares to stronger text models.

- Weakness: Missing experimental details (normalization, hyperparameters, preprocessing, class imbalance handling), omitted Table 3, typos, and absent effect sizes/confidence intervals.

- Question: How do you envision deployment—will predictions use a single reader’s gaze and what is the minimum gaze required?

- Question: Can you run leave-one-reader-out experiments to test generalization to new readers on the same sentences?

- Question: Can you decouple labeling and gaze (e.g., use gaze from different annotators or reading-only tasks) to test label/gaze coupling?

- Question: Can you compare against stronger text-only baselines (SVM with rich n-grams, CNN/LSTM, fine-tuned transformers) to quantify incremental gaze gains?

- Question: How robust are results to fewer/noisier readers, lower sampling rates, or missing gaze spans—please ablate number of readers and noise?

- Question: How will consent, privacy, and potential unintended inferences from gaze be managed, and how will demographic bias be mitigated?

- Additional comment: Release code and scripts for feature extraction (especially gaze-saliency graphs) to improve reproducibility.

- Additional comment: Report effect sizes with confidence intervals and perform significance testing on the second dataset as well.

- Additional comment: Improve writing quality, fix typos, and correct incomplete references.
