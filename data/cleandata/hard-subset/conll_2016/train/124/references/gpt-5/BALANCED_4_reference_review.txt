Summary
This paper investigates whether cognitive signals from eye-tracking can improve sentiment analysis (SA), especially for texts with higher-level phenomena such as irony/sarcasm and thwarted expectations. The authors augment standard textual SA features with (a) features designed for sarcasm/thwarting and (b) gaze-derived cognitive features computed from fixation/saccade statistics and a proposed “gaze-saliency” graph. On two public eye-tracking datasets (movie reviews, tweets; one with many sarcastic texts), SVM/NB/MLP classifiers with the combined feature set show small but statistically significant gains over textual features alone, with larger gains reported on a held-out set of ironic texts. The paper also provides a feasibility discussion about mobile eye-tracking.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that gaze-derived features can yield measurable improvements for SA on challenging, sarcasm-heavy data, with analysis (feature rankings, held-out subset) suggesting the added value is concentrated on complex constructs.
2) A set of gaze features for SA, including both basic fixation/saccade measures and graph-based gaze-saliency features, which are novel in this context.
3) An evaluation protocol combining standard SA features with sarcasm/thwarting features and cognitive features, tested on two public eye-tracking datasets, with significance testing.

Strengths
- Novel signal for SA, well motivated by psycholinguistics: Bringing eye-tracking to SA is original and timely, especially as sarcasm/irony often elude purely textual cues. The proposed basic and graph-based gaze features are clearly described and grounded in reading research (e.g., regressions and surprisal).
- Consistent improvements with significance on challenging data: On both datasets, adding gaze on top of sentiment+sarcasm features improves F1 (SVM: +0.6 and +1.5 points), with a larger relative gain on the irony subset of the held-out set. The McNemar test for D1 confirms significance; the held-out analysis supports the paper’s central hypothesis.
- Feature analysis suggests gaze is informative: The chi-squared ranking (10/20 and 7/20 gaze features among top features for D1/D2) indicates gaze features carry predictive signal beyond readability/length controls.
- Thoughtful feature engineering and ablations: The paper systematically studies Unigrams vs sentiment features vs sarcasm features vs gaze features, and combines them, helping isolate where gains come from. The inclusion of readability/length controls reduces confounds.
- Use of public datasets and attempt at reproducibility: Using two public eye-tracking datasets helps future verification and follow-up work.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and deployment story remain speculative: The approach requires gaze at inference time. The mobile eye-tracking feasibility discussion is aspirational and not evaluated. No analysis of cost–benefit, latency, or robustness with consumer-grade hardware. Without a clear inference path, the contribution risks being of limited practical impact.
- Modest incremental gains from gaze and heavy reliance on sarcasm features: Most of the improvement comes from sarcasm/thwarting features (e.g., +3.1 and +7.8 F1), while gaze adds only +0.6 and +1.5 points thereafter. The effect sizes, while statistically significant for one dataset, are small, and there is no significance report for D2. Gaze-only models perform poorly (F1 ~49–53), underscoring that the gains are incremental rather than transformative.
- Missing comparisons to stronger textual baselines: The paper does not compare to modern text-only models (e.g., CNN/RNN/LSTM with pretrained embeddings) that often outperform feature-based methods on short-text SA and sarcasm detection. This weakens claims that gaze helps “over state-of-the-art” rather than over a specific feature-based baseline.
- Potential confounds and experimental design concerns: Eye-tracking was recorded during sentiment annotation, which may prime readers and bias gaze patterns toward the target label. It is unclear whether PCA and other preprocessing are fitted only on training folds (risk of test leakage). The small data regime and 10-fold CV over sentences may overestimate generalization. No cross-dataset generalization or robustness to sampling-rate differences (500Hz vs 120Hz) is reported.
- Limited error analysis and ablations within gaze features: Error analysis is brief and qualitative; no concrete examples. Ablations that separate basic vs graph-based gaze features, and normalization for reader/sentence length effects, would clarify which gaze signals matter. Aggregating gaze over readers may lose informative individual variance.

Questions to authors
- In the 10-fold CV, are dimensionality reduction (PCA) and any feature selection performed within each training fold only? Please clarify to rule out leakage.
- Were readers’ gaze recorded while they were explicitly labeling sentiment? If so, how do you mitigate priming/annotation-task confounds? Can you provide evidence that similar gains hold for passive reading gaze?
- Can you report significance for D2 (Sn+Sr vs Sn+Sr+Gz) and provide confidence intervals? Given the small effect sizes, this is important.
- How robust are the gaze features to hardware sampling rate and accuracy differences? Any normalization across devices/readers (e.g., z-scoring per participant)?
- Please add comparisons to strong textual baselines (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN with pretrained embeddings) and, if possible, sentiment models fine-tuned on sarcasm datasets.
- Can you provide ablations of gaze feature groups (basic vs graph-based; regressions vs fixations) and examples where gaze corrected a wrong textual prediction?
- What is the intended inference-time setting? If gaze is not available, could you distill gaze-informed models into text-only students, or predict gaze proxies from text?
- The held-out “irony” subset: how was it sampled, and is there any overlap with training data from the same source? Please clarify.

Overall evaluation
Interesting and well-motivated work showing that gaze carries complementary signal for sentiment, with clearer benefits on sarcasm/irony. However, the practical applicability is unclear, the incremental gains are small, and the evaluation lacks comparisons to strong modern text-only baselines and deeper robustness checks. I lean toward rejection in its current form. Strengthening the experimental rigor (leakage controls, robust baselines, ablations, cross-dataset tests), clarifying the deployment pathway (or offering a knowledge distillation angle), and expanding error/qualitative analysis would substantially improve the paper.
