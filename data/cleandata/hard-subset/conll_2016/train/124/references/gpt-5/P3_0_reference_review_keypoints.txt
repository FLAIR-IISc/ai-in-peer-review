- Paper investigates augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking, designing basic gaze (fixations, saccades, regressions) and graph-based “gaze-saliency” features, evaluated alongside traditional sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features.  
- Reports modest but statistically significant F1 gains from adding gaze on two small eye-tracking datasets, with larger gains on a held-out irony-rich subset; feature-importance analysis ranks several gaze features highly.

Strengths
- Focus on hard cases: datasets include sarcasm/implicit sentiment where text-only features struggle; gaze gives notably larger gains on an irony subset (6.1% absolute F1).  
- Methodological breadth: combines low-level psycholinguistic gaze measures with novel graph-based measures modeling interactions across words.  
- Cross-dataset and hardware diversity: experiments use two different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000, Tobii T120), showing gains across sensor qualities.  
- Statistical testing and interpretability: employs McNemar testing and chi-squared feature ranking, with gaze features among top contributors.  
- Practical discussion: considers feasibility and applications (mobile eye-tracking, e-commerce), outlining potential real-world use-cases.

Weaknesses
- Outdated/limited baselines: no comparisons to strong modern text-only models (e.g., fine-tuned BERT/RoBERTa or SOTA sarcasm detectors), so gains may disappear versus contemporary baselines.  
- Dataset size/design and generalization concerns: small, curated datasets (994/843 instances), possible label leakage from collecting gaze during annotation, and no subject-level/domain-level splits reported.  
- Methodological clarity and leakage risk: unclear whether PCA, scaling, and feature selection were fit within training folds (risk of test leakage); statistical tests incompletely reported (only D1).  
- Ambiguous/duplicated graph features: several graph-based feature definitions are unclear or duplicated and lack principled justification, harming reproducibility and interpretability.  
- Confounded baseline sanity checks: cross-domain NB/SVM/RB tests conflate distribution shift with intrinsic dataset difficulty, requiring stronger in-domain baselines.  
- Practicality and ethics under-addressed: speculative reliance on mobile eye-tracking without adequate discussion of privacy, consent, demographic bias, calibration, or deployment costs.

Questions / Requests to Authors
- Include comparisons to strong modern text-only baselines (fine-tuned BERT/RoBERTa and SOTA sarcasm detectors) on D1/D2.  
- Clarify CV protocol: were PCA, scaling, and feature selection fit exclusively on training folds? If not, rerun with a leakage-free nested pipeline.  
- Provide subject-level and device-level splits (no annotator overlap between train/test) to test generalization across readers and hardware.  
- Report statistical significance for the 1.5% F1 gain on D2 (contingency table and exact p-value).  
- Perform ablations within gaze features to identify which signals drive improvements and report confidence intervals.  
- Provide precise, non-overlapping definitions and equations for all graph-based features and justify choices like top-1/top-2 degree statistics; compare to simpler sequence-aware alternatives.  
- Address annotation confound: evidence distinguishing task-induced gaze artifacts from natural reading behavior.  
- Commit to releasing code, exact splits, datasets (if allowed), and per-class precision/recall and sarcastic vs non-sarcastic performance.

Overall
- Leveraging gaze for sentiment analysis on complex constructs is an interesting, potentially impactful direction, but current empirical evidence is not yet compelling against modern text baselines and several methodological, clarity, and reproducibility issues must be resolved.
