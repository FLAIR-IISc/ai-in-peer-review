Summary
This paper proposes augmenting traditional text-based sentiment analysis (SA) with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-tracking while they annotate short texts. The authors engineer a large set of gaze features (basic fixation/saccade statistics and graph-based “gaze-saliency” features), combine them with standard sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features, and evaluate with 10-fold cross-validation on two public eye-tracking datasets containing reviews/tweets with substantial irony/sarcasm. They report modest but statistically significant F-score gains from adding gaze features, with larger relative improvements on an irony-heavy held-out subset.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that eye-tracking features can provide small but statistically significant improvements to SA on short texts, especially those containing irony/sarcasm, across two datasets and hardware settings.
2) A collection of engineered gaze features, including a graph-based formulation (gaze-saliency graphs) that summarizes saccade patterns, which may be of independent interest to cognitive NLP.
3) A focused analysis on an irony-rich held-out set showing larger gains for complex constructs, suggesting where gaze is most useful.

Strengths
- Targeting a hard slice of SA: The datasets emphasize sarcasm/irony and implicit sentiment, where text-only features often struggle. The held-out analysis further shows larger gains on ironic content (e.g., +6.1% relative).
- Cross-dataset evidence and significance testing: Results are shown on two publicly available datasets captured with different eye-trackers (500Hz Eyelink vs 120Hz Tobii), and the key improvement on D1 is tested with McNemar’s test (p=0.0001). This supports robustness to some hardware variation.
- Comprehensive feature engineering and ablations: The paper compares unigrams, traditional SA features (Sn), sarcasm/thwarting features (Sr), and gaze (Gz) in multiple combinations and across three classifiers, helping isolate where gains come from. The chi-square feature ranking (though not fully shown) indicates several gaze features are among the most informative.
- Relevance and timeliness: Bridging psycholinguistics and NLP for SA is valuable, and the paper situates itself in a growing line of cognitive-NLP work. The observation that eye movement reflects processing difficulty aligns well with the challenges posed by figurative language.
- Use of public datasets: Leveraging existing eye-tracking corpora facilitates reproducibility and encourages further research; the paper’s methodology could be reapplied by others.

Weaknesses
- Practicality at inference time: The approach requires access to readers’ gaze at test time, which is uncommon in real-world SA pipelines. The “feasibility” section is speculative (mobile gaze is not yet pervasive; privacy/consent challenges remain). A more convincing path would include proxy signals (e.g., cursor tracking, dwell time) or simulation studies showing how the model behaves with single-user noisy gaze.
- Modest gains and inconsistent behavior: The incremental benefit of gaze over Sn+Sr is small on average (e.g., +0.6% F on D1 and +1.5% on D2 with SVM), and gaze-only features underperform strong text features. Improvements across classifiers are not uniform (e.g., SVM helps most; NB and NN gains are limited), suggesting sensitivity to modeling choices and potential overfitting given small data.
- Potential confounds and experimental design: Gaze was recorded as annotators produced sentiment labels. This risks circularity: the gaze may reflect the labeling process itself, not generalizable reading behavior. The paper averages gaze over multiple annotators per sentence, which may inflate stability compared to the practical scenario of a single unseen reader. There is no leave-one-reader-out or reader-conditioned analysis to establish generalization across readers.
- Weak textual baselines and domain mismatch: Modern SA baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN, contextual models) are not included; the unigram-PCA baseline is weak. The “existing SA systems” are trained on external corpora and tested on these datasets, conflating difficulty with domain shift. It remains unclear how gaze compares to stronger in-domain text-only baselines (e.g., tuned SVM with n-grams/char n-grams, sentiment lexicons, contextual embeddings).
- Missing details and clarity: Important aspects are underspecified (feature normalization, hyperparameters, preprocessing, handling of class imbalance, selection of 500 PCA components). The promised feature-importance Table 3 is not shown. Writing contains numerous typos/grammar issues, and some references are incomplete. Effect sizes and confidence intervals are not reported.

Questions to authors
- Inference scenario: How do you envision deployment? Will predictions be made from a single reader’s gaze? What is the minimum amount of gaze needed? Can you report performance using a single annotator’s gaze per instance (randomly chosen) to approximate a more realistic setting?
- Reader generalization: Can you perform leave-one-reader-out (or leave-some-readers-out) experiments to show the model trained on gaze from some readers generalizes to new readers on the same sentences?
- Label/gaze coupling: Since gaze was collected during labeling, could you decouple the two by using gaze from one set of annotators and labels from another (when possible), or by reusing eye-tracking from a reading-only task without labeling, to test whether performance persists?
- Stronger text-only baselines: Can you compare against more competitive textual models (e.g., SVMs with richer n-gram/char-gram features and negation handling; CNN/LSTM; pretrained transformers fine-tuned in-domain)? How do gaze gains compare when starting from such baselines?
- Robustness to scarce/noisy gaze: What happens with fewer annotators (e.g., 1), lower sampling rates, or missing gaze spans? Please include an ablation varying the number of readers and noise injection.
- Ethics and privacy: How will users’ consent be managed in realistic applications? What safeguards prevent unintended inferences from gaze (e.g., health/emotional states), and how will demographic bias in gaze patterns be mitigated?

Additional comments
- Please release code/scripts for feature extraction (especially gaze-saliency graphs and “in-house” resources) to improve reproducibility.
- Consider reporting effect sizes with confidence intervals and conducting significance tests on D2 as well.
- Tighten writing and fix reference formatting.
