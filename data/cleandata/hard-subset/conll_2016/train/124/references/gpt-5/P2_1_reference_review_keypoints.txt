- Proposes augmenting text-based sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking (fixations, saccades, regressions) including graph-based gaze-saliency features.
- Evaluates on two public eye-tracking datasets (~1k instances each, containing irony/sarcasm) and reports modest F-score gains when adding gaze features.
- Claims contributions: empirical evidence that aggregated eye features help SA, a reusable inventory of gaze-derived features (including saliency graphs), and larger benefits on an irony subset.
- Novelty: first use of gaze as a predictive signal for sentiment analysis with psycholinguistically motivated feature design.
- Robustness: results replicate across two kinds of eye-tracking hardware (Eyelink-1000 and Tobii T120) and multiple classifiers (NB/SVM/MLP).
- Targeting hard cases: focuses on irony/sarcasm and shows larger absolute gains on a held-out irony subset (e.g., Sn+Sr F1 60.1 → 64.3 with gaze).
- Statistical analysis: McNemar tests and chi-squared feature rankings indicate some gaze features are significant predictors.
- Reproducibility: uses public datasets and standard toolkits, facilitating replication given access to gaze traces.
- Practicality concern: requires eye-tracking at inference, with consumer-grade acquisition and privacy/consent issues making deployment unclear.
- Missing strong baselines: no comparison to modern text-only models (CNN/RNN/transformers), so unclear if gaze adds value beyond current state-of-the-art.
- Modest effect sizes: absolute F1 gains over best text config are small (+0.6 on D1, +1.5 on D2), raising questions about practical significance.
- Confounds: gaze recorded during annotation may reflect task-induced attention or annotator uncertainty rather than inherent linguistic signals.
- Aggregation issues: aggregating across annotators may obscure individual patterns or encode annotator disagreement; inter-annotator variance not analyzed.
- Experimental clarity gaps: some feature definitions are confusing, and key details (label derivation, MLP hyperparameters, PCA dims, normalization) are missing.
- No cross-dataset generalization tests (train on D1, test on D2 and vice versa) to assess robustness to domain/device shifts.
- Feature-set confusion: boundary between feature groups (e.g., “Uni” vs “Sn” and inclusion of NGRAM_PCA) is unclear.
- Request to authors: clarify inference scenario—are gaze features expected at test time or can they be approximated from text (distillation/text-to-gaze)?
- Request to authors: explain gold label derivation and analyze correlation between gaze features and annotator disagreement/entropy.
- Request to authors: report cross-dataset transfer results to probe robustness across devices and domains.
- Request to authors: compare against competitive text-only baselines (BiLSTM/CNN/transformer) to establish whether gaze still helps.
- Request to authors: provide detailed feature ablation showing which gaze features drive gains (e.g., regressions vs. other features).
- Request to authors: address ethics and privacy—how would consent, storage, and gaze-data handling be managed in practice?
- Overall evaluation: original and well-motivated work with careful experiments, but practical applicability, modest gains, and lack of strong baselines lead to a leaning toward rejection unless stronger text baselines or a path to approximate gaze at inference are provided.
