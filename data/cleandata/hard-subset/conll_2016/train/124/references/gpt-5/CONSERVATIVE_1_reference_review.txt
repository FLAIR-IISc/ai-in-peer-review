Summary and contributions
- The paper augments standard textual sentiment features with “cognitive” features derived from readers’ eye-tracking during sentiment annotation. The goal is to better handle nuanced phenomena (sarcasm, irony, thwarting) that are hard for conventional SA.
- On two small public datasets with gaze, adding sarcasm/thwarting textual features improves over sentiment-only features, and adding gaze yields further gains (typically +0.6–1.5 F1 over the already-augmented textual features with SVM; total +3.7 and +9.3 F1 over sentiment-only on the two datasets).
- A held-out subset analysis suggests gaze helps more on “complex” (sarcastic/ironic) texts than on simpler ones.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An engineered set of gaze-derived features (including graph-based “gaze-saliency” features) for sentence-level sentiment classification, evaluated on two eye-tracking datasets.
2) An empirical study showing modest but statistically significant improvements from gaze features over strong hand-engineered textual baselines on these datasets.
3) A feature-importance analysis indicating several gaze features rank highly among predictive features.

Strengths
- Addresses a recognized challenge in SA (sarcasm/irony, implicit sentiment). The idea of leveraging cognitive signals is interesting and has prior psycholinguistic motivation.
- Careful, interpretable feature engineering (basic and graph-based gaze features) and a reasonably thorough ablation across sentiment-only, +sarcasm features, and +gaze features.
- Some statistical testing (McNemar) and chi-squared feature-importance analysis. The held-out split focusing on complex vs non-complex texts is a useful diagnostic demonstrating larger relative gains on sarcastic/ironic items.
- Uses public datasets with eye-tracking, enabling at least partial reproducibility of the data side.
- The improvements over sentiment-only features are sizeable once sarcasm/thwarting features are included, and gaze adds consistent, if small, gains on top.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and deployment: The approach requires eye-tracking at inference time. The paper’s feasibility section is speculative (mobile eye-tracking “coming soon”) and does not address who provides gaze at prediction time (the review reader? multiple readers? the original author?). Without a realistic, consented, and scalable test-time gaze source, the method is not actionable for standard SA scenarios (e.g., batch classification of reviews/tweets).
- Missing strong baselines: No comparison to established modern textual models (e.g., CNN/RNN/LSTM, pretrained contextual encoders) that often handle sarcasm reasonably with appropriate data. Authors explicitly exclude deep learning despite acknowledging their performance. It remains unclear whether a contemporary text-only baseline would match or surpass the reported gains, making the incremental value of gaze ambiguous.
- Methodological concerns and risk of confounds:
  - Gaze collected during sentiment annotation may reflect task-specific strategies and decision processes, potentially encoding the label or task difficulty rather than generalizable reading behavior. This threatens external validity for ordinary reading settings.
  - Small datasets (~1k items) with many features increase overfitting risk. Dimensionality reduction (PCA) and defaults in Weka are mentioned but details on nested CV, feature scaling, hyperparameter tuning, and fitting PCA within folds are missing. Without strict partitioning of all preprocessing within folds, results may be optimistic.
  - The “existing SA systems” check trains on Pang & Lee / Sentiment140 and tests on D1/D2, conflating domain shift with dataset difficulty. Poor performance there does not demonstrate intrinsic hardness beyond cross-domain mismatch.
  - Statistical testing is limited (select pairwise tests). Confidence intervals or comprehensive significance tests across all key comparisons are not provided.
- Limited generalization analysis:
  - No cross-dataset transfer (train on D1, test on D2) or cross-subject analyses indicating robustness to different reader populations and domains.
  - The held-out set is carved from D1; more convincing would be leave-one-dataset-out or external benchmarks.
- Clarity and presentation: Numerous typos and stylistic issues, inconsistent references, and some missing details on the gaze-saliency graph construction and exact feature dimensionality. Reproducibility is hindered by lack of code or a precise feature specification.

Questions to authors
- Inference-time scenario: Who supplies gaze at prediction time, and under what usage model? Is the prediction meant to be personalized to each reader’s gaze, or is one averaged “canonical” gaze used per sentence? How would this system operate on a new, unseen review with no gaze available?
- Baselines: How does your best text-only model compare to a strong contemporary baseline (e.g., fine-tuned transformer on your datasets without gaze)? Please provide those results.
- Preprocessing and CV: Was PCA (for unigrams and IMPLICIT features) fitted only on training folds in each CV split? Were any other preprocessing steps (e.g., scaling, feature selection) confined within folds? Were hyperparameters tuned with nested CV?
- Gaze-label confound: Since gaze was collected while annotators performed sentiment annotation, how did you control for task-induced confounds (e.g., attention to cue words after deciding label)? Did annotators see any hints about polarity or receive feedback during the task?
- Robustness: Provide cross-dataset evaluation (train on D1, test on D2 and vice versa) and, if possible, cross-subject validation (e.g., leave-one-group-of-readers-out). Are the gains from gaze consistent across these settings?
- Ablations: Which specific gaze features drive the gains? Please report per-feature (or grouped) ablations and variance across folds. Are improvements uniform across classes (positive vs negative) and across sarcastic subtypes?

Overall, while the idea is intriguing and the initial gains are suggestive, the lack of practical deployment pathway, absence of strong text-only baselines, and methodological uncertainties make the evidence insufficient for acceptance at this time.
