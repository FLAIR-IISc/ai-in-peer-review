Summary and Contributions
- This paper augments sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking while annotating short texts. It proposes both basic gaze features (fixations, saccades, regressions, skips) and a novel gaze-saliency graph with engineered graph features. These are combined with standard sentiment features and features tailored to sarcasm/thwarting.
- Empirically, on two public eye-tracking datasets, adding gaze features improves F-scores over textual features alone, with especially notable gains on an irony/sarcasm subset (+6.1% F). Feature-importance analyses show many gaze features among the top-ranked predictors.
- The paper argues for feasibility via emerging mobile eye-trackers and sketches use cases.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) Introduction of cognitive (eye-tracking) features for sentiment analysis, including the gaze-saliency graph representation—a bold and novel direction for SA.
2) Evidence that cognitive signals particularly help on linguistically complex phenomena (irony/sarcasm), with statistically significant gains across two datasets and a dedicated held-out subset.
3) A feature-engineering recipe that integrates sarcasm/thwarting cues with standard SA features, providing a strong text baseline to assess cognitive add-ons.

Strengths
- Novel and ambitious direction: Using gaze for SA, and especially the proposed gaze-saliency graph, is original and pushes the field toward cognitively informed NLP. The engineered features (e.g., RSF, LREG) are grounded in psycholinguistic intuition about surprisal and reanalysis.
- Targeting genuinely hard cases: The focus on sarcasm/irony and thwarted expectations is apt; the datasets contain many such instances, and the observed +6.1% F improvement on the complex subset is compelling. This matches the hypothesis that cognitive signals matter most when textual cues are subtle.
- Solid, if classic, empirical setup: Two public datasets with different trackers (500Hz Eyelink vs 120Hz Tobii) suggest some robustness to hardware variation. Statistical tests (McNemar) and chi-squared feature rankings strengthen the claims.
- Interpretability: Many gaze features are interpretable (e.g., regressions, saccade lengths), and the feature-importance results highlight that cognitive features are not merely decorative—they rank among the top predictive cues.
- Potentially impactful direction: Even if near-term deployment is challenging, this line of work can seed a broader research stream on integrating cognitive evidence into NLP models, beyond sentiment (e.g., emotion analysis, QA).

Weaknesses
- Limited baseline strength: The textual baselines (NB, linear SVM, shallow NN) and feature sets are dated relative to contemporary transformer-based models. It remains unclear whether gaze still adds value when compared against, or combined with, strong contextual encoders (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa) fine-tuned for sarcasm/irony. This limits the strength of empirical claims.
- Practicality at inference time: The approach requires gaze data to classify new texts. While the paper argues feasibility with mobile eye-tracking, it does not demonstrate performance under realistic mobile conditions or with single-reader inputs. Gaze-only features underperform, suggesting synergy is needed; the paper does not quantify the minimum viable gaze signal (number of readers, sampling rate, noise).
- Modest absolute gains on full datasets: Beyond the complex subset, adding gaze to Sn+Sr yields only +0.6 (D1) and +1.5 (D2) absolute F, albeit statistically significant. The practical benefit outside irony may be limited.
- Possible confounds and normalization: Eye movements were recorded while annotators decided sentiment, raising concerns about circularity between cognitive process and labels (e.g., different annotators’ decision strategies). The paper averages per-annotator gaze features but does not detail normalization across readers (reading speed, calibration drift) or a leave-one-reader-out analysis to test generalization to new users.
- Reproducibility details: Some features rely on in-house resources (e.g., discourse connector list). Details for IMPLICIT PCA and LP feature training, and any safeguards against train-test leakage when constructing those resources, are insufficiently specified. Code availability is not discussed.

Questions to Authors
- How does the approach fare when combined with modern text-only baselines (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa, or sarcasm-aware transformers)? Does gaze still add significant gains?
- Reader normalization and generalization: What normalization do you apply to account for reader-specific baselines (e.g., z-scoring fixation durations per reader)? Can you report a leave-one-reader-out experiment to demonstrate generalization to unseen readers?
- Data provenance for IMPLICIT PCA and LP features: On which corpora were these trained/bootstrapped? How do you avoid leakage into the target datasets?
- Sensitivity to eye-tracker quality: Can you downsample the 500Hz dataset to 120Hz (or add synthetic noise) to approximate mobile conditions and report robustness?
- Ablations: Which gaze features contribute most to the +6.1% gain on the irony subset (basic vs graph-based)? Can you provide per-feature-group ablations?
- Inference with limited gaze: What is performance with a single reader’s gaze vs the averaged 5–7 readers? How does performance scale with the number of readers?

Overall evaluation
This paper proposes a bold and novel direction—leveraging cognitive gaze signals for SA—with concrete, interpretable feature designs and evidence that cognitive features help where text-only models struggle (sarcasm/irony). While baselines are not state-of-the-art and practicality is not fully addressed, the conceptual advance and promising results on complex phenomena justify acceptance at CoNLL, which benefits from incubating new paradigms. I recommend acceptance, encouraging the authors to (a) include a comparison to a modern transformer baseline and (b) analyze generalization to unseen readers and low-quality gaze.
