Summary and claimed contributions
The paper studies whether cognitive signals from eye-tracking can improve sentence-level sentiment analysis, especially for linguistically challenging cases such as sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations. Using two public eye-tracking datasets (snippets annotated for sentiment while readers’ gaze was recorded), the authors design a feature set comprising: (i) standard sentiment features, (ii) features targeting sarcasm/thwarting, and (iii) cognitive features derived from fixations/saccades and a proposed “gaze-saliency graph.” With SVM, Naive Bayes, and a shallow NN, they report that adding gaze features to textual features yields gains in F1 on both datasets, with larger gains on an irony/sarcasm subset.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical evidence that gaze-derived cognitive features provide incremental improvements for sentiment classification beyond strong textual features, with the largest relative gains on snippets containing irony/sarcasm.

2) A set of gaze-based features, including graph-derived saliency features, and a feature-importance analysis indicating several cognitive features rank highly.

3) A curated evaluation on two datasets collected with different eye-trackers, suggesting some robustness of the approach across hardware.

Strengths
- Targeting hard cases in SA: The datasets emphasize implicit sentiment, sarcasm, and thwarted expectations—settings where bag-of-words and standard syntactic features often struggle. The held-out analysis shows larger relative gains (+6.1% F1) on the irony subset, supporting the hypothesis that cognitive signals are most beneficial for complex constructs.

- Cross-dataset evaluation and statistical testing: Results are reported on two independently collected datasets with distinct eye-tracking hardware (500Hz vs 120Hz), and significance tests (McNemar) are used to support claims when differences are small. A feature-importance analysis (chi-squared) further corroborates the utility of gaze features.

- Concrete, implementable feature design: The paper enumerates both basic gaze measures (fixation durations, regressions, skips) and higher-level saliency graph features, offering a clear starting point for replication and further exploration in cognition-aware NLP.

- Transparent ablations: The stepwise improvements from sentiment-only (Sn) to Sn+sarcasm (Sr) to Sn+Sr+gaze (Gz) are presented for multiple classifiers and both datasets, allowing readers to isolate the added value of each feature group.

Weaknesses
- Practicality at inference time: The approach requires eye-tracking data for each test snippet, which is atypical for sentiment analysis deployment. The feasibility section is speculative; no demonstration is given that accurate gaze can be obtained non-intrusively at scale, nor is an application scenario evaluated end-to-end. This limits real-world impact unless a clear use case with available gaze is established.

- Lack of subject-level generalization analysis: Gaze features are averaged across annotators and evaluated via sentence-level cross-validation. There is no leave-one-subject-out (or train-on-some-readers/test-on-held-out-readers) evaluation to verify that features generalize to unseen readers rather than capturing annotator-specific idiosyncrasies. This is critical for cognitive-feature validity.

- Modest gains over strong textual baselines on full datasets: While Sn→Sn+Sr provides substantial improvements, the additional gain from gaze on top of Sn+Sr is relatively small (≈0.6–1.5 F1 points). The overall benefit may not justify the cost/complexity of collecting gaze in many settings.

- Baseline and evaluation design concerns: The “existing SA systems” baseline is trained on out-of-domain corpora, confounded by domain mismatch; it is not a strong comparator. No comparison to modern neural text-only baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN/Transformer) trained in-domain is provided, despite acknowledging their performance in prior work.

- Incomplete methodological details and potential leakage risks: Several implementation aspects are underspecified: whether PCA and feature scaling were fit within training folds; SVM/NN hyperparameters and class weighting; normalization of gaze metrics across readers; handling of skipped words; and the exact definition of graph weights (e.g., F1H/F2H, PSDH/PSDS, RSDH/RSDS are ambiguously described). Table 3 is referenced but not shown. Given the small datasets, clarity on leakage prevention is essential.

- Possible label-conditioning of gaze: Gaze was recorded while participants performed sentiment annotation. This may entangle reading behavior with task-specific decision processes and the eventual labels. It reduces the ability to claim that gaze captures text-intrinsic difficulty rather than annotation behavior. A stronger design would use gaze from neutral reading or from readers blinded to the sentiment task.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
1) Subject generalization: Did you run leave-one-subject-out or train-on-subset-of-readers/test-on-held-out-readers experiments? If so, how do results compare to the reported sentence-level CV? If not, can you provide such an analysis?

2) Preprocessing and leakage: Were PCA, feature scaling/selection, and hyperparameter tuning performed within each training fold only? Please detail the pipeline to rule out train–test contamination.

3) Gaze feature definitions: Please precisely define the gaze-saliency graph weights and the F1H/F2H/PSH/PSDH/RSH/RSDH features. Are degrees computed over weighted, directed graphs? How are multiple saccades aggregated? How are skipped words treated?

4) Reader normalization: How are gaze measures normalized across readers (e.g., per-subject z-scoring) before averaging, given differences in reading speed and tracker noise?

5) Deployment and use-case: In what realistic scenarios will gaze be available at inference time? Can you quantify the cost/latency and demonstrate a proof-of-concept with mobile eye-tracking?

6) Stronger text-only baselines: Can you include in-domain neural baselines (e.g., CNN/BiLSTM/Transformer) trained on the same folds, and report whether gaze still provides gains on top of them?

7) Reproducibility: Will you release code and the exact feature extraction scripts/lists (e.g., discourse connectors, LP training setup) to enable replication?

Overall, the paper proposes an interesting and plausible use of cognitive signals and presents evidence of incremental gains, especially on sarcastic text. However, the absence of subject-level generalization, modest overall gains over strong textual features, and unclear deployment path are significant limitations that need to be addressed.
