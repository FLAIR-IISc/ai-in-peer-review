Summary and contributions
This paper augments traditional text-based sentiment analysis (SA) with cognitive features extracted from eye-tracking recordings collected while annotators label short texts. The authors (i) define a suite of basic and graph-based gaze features (including a gaze-saliency graph) alongside standard sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, and readability features; (ii) evaluate these features with NB/SVM/MLP on two eye-tracking datasets containing difficult phenomena (sarcasm, implicit sentiment), showing small but statistically significant gains when adding gaze; and (iii) show larger gains on a held-out subset of ironic/sarcastic texts, arguing gaze helps with complex constructs.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence that gaze-derived features, when available, can yield consistent improvements for SA, especially on sarcastic/ironic inputs.
2) A set of engineered gaze features, including a novel gaze-saliency graph and associated degree-based measures.
3) An analysis indicating many gaze features rank highly in chi-squared feature importance.

Strengths
- Focus on hard SA phenomena. The datasets emphasize implicit sentiment, sarcasm, and thwarting—cases where lexical cues are weak. The authors show that adding sarcasm/thwarting features (Sn+Sr) improves over standard sentiment features (Sn) and that adding gaze (Gz) further improves F1: +0.6 points on D1 (72.9→73.5) and +1.5 on D2 (70.3→71.8) with SVM. The held-out subset underscores larger benefits for ironic texts (+4.2 absolute F1; 60.1→64.3), aligning with the hypothesis that gaze captures cognitive difficulty.
- Cross-dataset consistency despite hardware differences. The two datasets use different eye-trackers (EyeLink-1000 at 500Hz vs Tobii T120 at 120Hz). Improvements are observed on both, suggesting some robustness of the proposed features to acquisition conditions (though normalization details should be clarified).
- Feature significance and ablations. The study reports chi-squared feature ranks where gaze features appear frequently among top features (10/20 for D1; 7/20 for D2), supporting their additive value beyond text-only features. Ablations (Sn, Sn+Sr, Gz, combinations) help isolate contributions.
- Statistical testing. The authors apply McNemar tests to compare close systems and report significance (e.g., p=0.0001 for D1 best vs second-best), which is good practice given small absolute gains.
- Feasibility discussion. The paper explicitly discusses deployability concerns, potential for mobile/embedded eye-tracking, and application scenarios, demonstrating awareness of practical constraints.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and problem framing. The approach assumes gaze at inference time, which is unrealistic for most SA applications (batch classification of reviews, social media streams). The paper frames feasibility via future mobile eye-trackers, but this remains speculative. A more convincing framing would be “learning with privileged information” (e.g., SVM+, distillation) where gaze guides training but is not required at test time; this is not explored.
- Limited and uneven baselines. The primary comparisons are within-dataset 10-fold CV with engineered features and a cross-domain baseline trained on Pang & Lee + Sentiment140. Strong modern text-only baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN/transformers, or at least well-tuned linear models with richer n-grams/embeddings) are absent by design. Consequently, the incremental value of gaze over competitive text-only models remains unclear. Also, some table entries appear inconsistent (e.g., Sn+Gz on D2 under SVM being worse than Gz), suggesting potential reporting or implementation issues.
- Small absolute gains on full datasets. The reported improvements with gaze on D1/D2 are modest (0.6–1.5 F1 points). While statistically significant, the effect size is small relative to the additional sensing cost and complexity. The held-out subset shows larger gains for irony, but that subset is derived from D1 rather than being an external test set.
- Ambiguity and clarity of gaze-graph features. The definitions of weighted-degree features (F1H/F1S/F2H/F2S; PSH/PSS vs PSDH/PSDS; RSH/RSS vs RSDH/RSDS) are difficult to follow and sometimes appear duplicated or inconsistently named. It is unclear exactly how weights are computed (source vs destination, durations vs counts vs distances) and how multiple participants are aggregated. This hinders reproducibility.
- Potential confounds and normalization. Hardware differences (sampling rate), sentence length, and annotator behavior could confound gaze measures. Although LEN and Flesch scores are included, the paper does not detail normalization of fixation/saccade measures across devices or readers, nor control analyses to ensure gains are not primarily due to length/complexity proxies. Moreover, labels are derived from the same annotators whose gaze is used, raising the possibility that gaze correlates with annotator-specific labeling behavior rather than text-intrinsic sentiment.

Questions to authors
- Test-time assumption: Do your models require gaze features at inference? If yes, can you provide a variant that uses gaze only during training (privileged information/distillation) and evaluate its performance without gaze at test time?
- Normalization: How are fixation durations/saccade lengths normalized across devices with different sampling rates and across readers? Are features computed in milliseconds or samples? Any per-sentence or per-reader z-normalization?
- Labels and annotators: How are gold labels determined (majority vote)? Are the gaze features aggregated across the same annotators who provided labels? Could annotator-specific biases be driving gains? Did you try leave-one-annotator-out aggregation to reduce this coupling?
- Inconsistencies in Table 2: Please verify Sn+Gz results on D2 (SVM), and clarify why Gz alone sometimes outperforms Sn+Gz under NB. Are these formatting errors or model issues?
- Stronger baselines: Can you add competitive text-only baselines (e.g., pretrained embeddings, simple CNN/LSTM, or transformer fine-tuning) to contextualize the incremental value of gaze?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and feature extraction scripts, especially for gaze-saliency graphs?

Additional comments
- Consider reporting per-phenomenon results (sarcasm/irony/non-irony) on the full datasets, not only the held-out subset.
- Clarify PCA and vocabulary construction details, and ensure consistent tokenization/POS/NE pipelines.
- Tone down the “first of its kind” claim; prior work has explored gaze for sentiment-related tasks, though not exactly in this setup.

Overall recommendation
Innovative direction with interesting empirical signals, but practical impact and baseline comparisons are insufficiently convincing in the current form. Strengthening the problem framing (privileged information), adding strong text-only baselines, clarifying feature definitions/normalization, and resolving table inconsistencies would substantially improve the paper.
