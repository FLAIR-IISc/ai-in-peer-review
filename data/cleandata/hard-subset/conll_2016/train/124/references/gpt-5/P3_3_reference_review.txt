Summary and contributions
This paper augments standard, text-based sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movement (gaze) during annotation. The authors engineer basic and graph-based gaze features (fixations, saccades, regressions, saliency graphs), combine them with sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features, and evaluate on two public datasets with gaze collected under different hardware conditions. They report modest but consistent gains in F1 over textual baselines, with larger gains on sarcastic/ironic subsets.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence that gaze-derived features provide complementary signal to textual features for sentiment polarity classification, especially for complex phenomena (sarcasm, irony, thwarting).
2) A feature set design that operationalizes gaze traces into both simple aggregates and graph-derived measures (saliency graphs), and an analysis suggesting several gaze features rank highly by chi-squared.
3) A demonstration across two datasets with different eye-tracking hardware that the effect is robust, albeit small.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and relevance: Using eye-tracking to inform sentiment classification is a novel and interesting angle that connects psycholinguistics and NLP. Prior work used gaze for related tasks (WSD, annotation difficulty, subjectivity extraction); to my knowledge there are few, if any, direct applications to sentiment polarity at the time of the cited work. The paper is thus a meaningful step in cognition-aware NLP.

- Empirical signal on hard phenomena: The held-out evaluation that separates sarcastic/ironic vs simpler instances is compelling. Gains from gaze are larger on the harder subset (e.g., +4.2 F1 absolute over Sn+Sr on Irony), aligning with the hypothesis that gaze captures surprisal/incongruity and reanalysis.

- Cross-dataset robustness and hardware variation: The study includes two datasets with different domains and very different trackers (Eyelink-1000 vs Tobii T120). Despite weaker gaze-only performance and lower sampling rate in D2, the incremental gains from adding gaze to strong textual features still appear.

- Feature engineering thoughtfulness: The authors do not stop at simple aggregates; they introduce saliency graphs and degree-based features reflecting localized processing difficulty and inter-word transitions. Even if some definitions could be tightened, the attempt to structurally model scanpaths is valuable.

- Statistical checks and feature importance: The use of McNemar tests (though see caveats below) and chi-squared ranking helps support the claim that gaze features matter and are not washed out by textual features alone.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and deployment story: The reliance on gaze at inference time is a major limitation. While the authors discuss future availability of mobile eye-tracking and possible application scenarios, today’s sentiment pipelines cannot assume access to user gaze. The reported gains (+0.6 to +1.5 F1 overall; larger on sarcasm) may not justify the cost/latency/consent hurdles for most applications. A more realistic scenario would be using gaze only at training time (e.g., as supervision or distillation), then deploying a text-only model; this is not explored.

- Methodological clarity and potential confounds: Several feature definitions (e.g., PSH/PSS vs PSDH/PSDS; RSH/RSS vs RSDH/RSDS) are hard to parse, with possible redundancy and naming inconsistencies. It is unclear how edge weights are computed and normalized across readers and sentences, and how features are scaled. The gaze features may conflate length, punctuation, or readability; although the authors include LEN and Flesch scores, more rigorous controls (e.g., partial correlations or mixed-effects regression) are needed to ensure unique gaze contribution.

- Limited baselines and model tuning: The strongest textual baseline relies on unigram PCA to 500 dims and lexicon features. Stronger text-only baselines common even in pre-deep era (character n-grams, word/phrase embeddings, tf-idf with regularized linear models without PCA, sentiment-specific embeddings) are missing. The neural baseline is under-specified and likely underpowered. Hyperparameters are left at Weka defaults; no tuning or variance across folds is reported.

- Statistical testing and reporting: The paper applies McNemar but discusses it in terms of F-score differences. McNemar is defined on paired disagreements (error counts), not F1, and the reported p=0.0001 for a 0.5 F1 difference on ~1k items is surprising without showing the contingency table. Across-fold variance or confidence intervals are not reported. The held-out set significance claims are not substantiated with test details.

- Data usage and reader effects: Gaze features are averaged across annotators who also provided the gold labels. This risks entangling annotator-specific reading behavior with the final label and may inflate performance. There is no analysis of inter-reader variability, no leave-one-reader-out evaluation, and no demonstration that a single new reader’s gaze suffices. At minimum, a partitioning that withholds all gaze from certain readers at train time would help.

Questions to authors
- Inference assumption: Do you envision the model requiring gaze at deployment time? If not, can you explore training-time use of gaze (e.g., multitask learning, feature distillation, rationales) to produce a text-only model that retains gains on sarcasm?

- Reader generalization: How sensitive are results to the number of readers used to compute gaze features? Please report performance as a function of readers (1, 2, …, all) and a leave-one-reader-out setup to test generalization to unseen readers.

- Statistical testing: Please clarify how McNemar was applied (on per-instance decisions of two systems?) and provide the contingency tables and exact n. Also report mean±std across CV folds or confidence intervals.

- Stronger textual baselines: Can you add regularized linear models on tf-idf word/char n-grams, and a simple RNN/CNN baseline? This will help isolate the unique contribution of gaze against stronger text-only methods.

- Saliency graph features: Please provide precise mathematical definitions (weights, normalization, handling multiple saccades, self-loops), and an ablation of basic vs graph-based gaze features to quantify their incremental value.

- Control for confounds: Beyond LEN/RED, can you add controls for punctuation density, POS distributions, and sentence position effects, and run partial correlation or mixed-effects analyses to quantify unique variance explained by gaze?

Overall, this is an original and thought-provoking paper with promising evidence that cognitive features can help sentiment analysis on linguistically complex inputs. However, the practical constraints, missing stronger baselines, methodological ambiguities, and limited analysis of reader generalization weaken the case for broad impact. With clarifications and additional experiments as above, the contribution would be substantially strengthened.
