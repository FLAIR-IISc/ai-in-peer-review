Summary and Contributions
This paper studies sentiment analysis (SA) on short texts by augmenting standard textual features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking during annotation. The authors develop two classes of gaze features—basic measures (e.g., fixation durations, regressions) and graph-based features derived from a “gaze-saliency graph.” Experiments on two public datasets that include eye-tracking data show that adding gaze features to textual features yields modest but statistically significant improvements in F1, with larger gains on instances containing sarcasm/irony. They also present feature-importance analyses and a targeted evaluation on a held-out subset rich in complex constructs.

Contributions (as assessed, strongest first):
1) Empirical evidence that gaze-derived features provide complementary signal to textual features for SA, especially for sarcastic/ironic content, across two datasets collected with different eye-trackers.
2) A set of engineered gaze features, including a novel gaze-saliency graph with degree-based measures, and an ablation over feature groups (textual vs. sarcasm/thwarting vs. gaze).
3) Analysis indicating that a substantial fraction of top-ranked features (chi-squared) are gaze-based, and a held-out evaluation highlighting larger relative gains on complex texts.

Strengths
1) Demonstrated utility of cognitive signals for complex sentiment phenomena. The results consistently show that adding gaze features to text features improves F1, with relative gains larger for sarcasm/irony (e.g., +6.1% relative on the held-out subset). This supports the hypothesis that eye-movement patterns capture higher-level processing not easily captured by lexical/syntactic features.
2) Clear and comprehensive feature design. The paper carefully enumerates standard SA features, sarcasm/thwarting features, and detailed gaze features, including both basic and graph-derived measures. This level of specification facilitates replication on the same datasets.
3) Multi-dataset evaluation and targeted analysis. Testing on two public datasets with different recording hardware (Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120) adds some robustness. The held-out subset focusing on complex constructs provides additional evidence that cognitive features help where textual cues are weak or indirect.
4) Feature importance and significance testing. The chi-squared rankings (reported as 10/20 and 7/20 gaze features in the top 20 for D1 and D2) and McNemar tests support the claim that gaze features contribute meaningfully. While results with gaze alone are weak, the combination gains over strong text baselines (Sn+Sr).
5) Positioning within and beyond SA. The related work situates the contribution relative to SA, sarcasm detection, and prior cognitive/eye-tracking studies, and the paper motivates potential downstream applicability where pragmatics matter.

Weaknesses
1) Practicality/external validity of requiring gaze at inference. The approach assumes availability of eye-tracking for new inputs, averaged over multiple readers in the datasets. The deployment discussion is speculative; there is no empirical demonstration with commodity/mobile eye-tracking, no calibration-free setup, and no analysis of performance with single-reader gaze or minimal gaze sampling. This limits real-world applicability and undermines external validity.
2) Modest gains over strong text-only baselines; lack of modern baselines. The incremental gains of adding gaze to Sn+Sr are small on the main datasets (0.6–1.5 F1 absolute with SVM). The paper does not compare to contemporary strong text-only models (e.g., transformer-based fine-tuning), leaving unclear whether gaze would still help when textual modeling is stronger. The claim of improving over the state of the art is therefore not fully substantiated.
3) Potential methodological leakage and under-specified protocol. PCA dimensionality reduction appears to be applied but it is unclear whether PCA and other mappings (e.g., IMPLICIT_PCA, LP logistic regression) are fit only on the training folds in cross-validation. If fitted on the full dataset, that would leak test information. Similarly, details about how the bootstrapped incongruity patterns are learned and from which data are missing. The McNemar test is reported on F-score differences; it should be applied to paired prediction disagreements, not to F1 aggregates—clarification is needed.
4) Limited generalization and ablations on gaze features. The paper averages gaze over annotators per sentence, which obscures variability and prevents assessing cross-participant generalization (e.g., training on gaze from some readers and testing on unseen readers, or sensitivity to the number of readers). There is no train-on-D1/test-on-D2 (and vice versa) setup with gaze to evaluate cross-dataset robustness. Ablations do not separate basic vs. graph-based gaze features to quantify their respective contributions.
5) Dataset and labeling details under-specified. The procedure for deriving gold labels (e.g., majority vote) and handling disagreement is not stated. Differences in annotation accuracies and inter-rater agreements across datasets might confound results, and there is no analysis of how label uncertainty affects models or interacts with gaze.

Questions to Authors
1) How are gold labels determined from annotator labels (majority vote, weighted by confidence, etc.)? What fraction of items have annotator disagreement, and how does performance vary with agreement?
2) Were PCA and any learned feature mappings (e.g., IMPLICIT_PCA, LP) fit strictly within each training fold? If not, please re-run with leakage-free preprocessing.
3) From what data were the implicit incongruity patterns bootstrapped? Is there any overlap with the evaluation texts?
4) How does performance scale with the number of readers per item? Report curves for 1, 2, … readers (random subsampling) to assess feasibility with sparse gaze.
5) Can you provide ablations separating basic gaze features from gaze-saliency graph features to quantify their individual contributions?
6) How do results compare to a strong transformer-based text-only baseline (fine-tuned on D1/D2)? Does gaze still yield gains?
7) Can you report cross-dataset generalization (train on D1, test on D2, and vice versa) with and without gaze, and discuss the impact of differing eye-tracker hardware?
8) Please clarify the statistical testing procedure: was McNemar’s test applied to paired prediction disagreements on the same test instances? If so, report the contingency counts.
9) Do you plan to release code/feature extraction scripts to ensure reproducibility?
