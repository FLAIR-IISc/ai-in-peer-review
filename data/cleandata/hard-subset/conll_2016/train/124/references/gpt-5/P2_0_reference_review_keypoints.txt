- Paper studies augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive (eye-tracking) features on two gaze datasets containing many sarcastic/ironic items.
- Main empirical claim: adding gaze features to text improves binary sentiment classification, with larger gains on sarcastic/ironic instances (e.g., Irony F: 58.2 → 64.3).
- Contribution: proposes interpretable gaze feature set including novel gaze-saliency graph features tied to fixation/regression/saccade measures.
- Contribution: cross-dataset evaluation across different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000 vs Tobii T120) suggesting some robustness.
- Strength: tackles a hard SA subtask (sarcasm/irony) where textual cues are weak and shows targeted gains on those subsets.
- Strength: novel combination of gaze data with downstream SA classification and graph-derived gaze features.
- Strength: feature design is well-motivated by psycholinguistic evidence (fixation durations, regressions, saccade length, skip rates).
- Strength: some statistical analyses (McNemar tests, chi-squared feature ranking) indicate gaze features contribute distinct signal.
- Strength: demonstration on datasets from different devices mitigates device-specific artifact concerns.
- Weakness: requires gaze at inference time; feasibility, privacy, consent, and reliability for deployment are speculative.
- Weakness: no training-only gaze approach (e.g., distillation to text-only model) is explored to make deployment realistic.
- Weakness: lacks comparison to strong modern text-only baselines (BiLSTM/CNN/transformers), so incremental value of gaze is unclear.
- Weakness: within feature-based methods, sarcasm/thwarting features drive most gains; gaze-only gains on full datasets are modest (0.6–1.5 F).
- Weakness: missing/unclear experimental details (label aggregation, per-sentence vs per-annotator features, fold design, normalization across sampling rates, MLP hyperparameters, PCA protocol).
- Weakness: potential confounds—gaze may reflect annotation difficulty or reader idiosyncrasies; averaging could wash out signals or encode confusion.
- Weakness: statistical testing is limited (significance reported for only one setting); ablations controlling length/readability and fuller significance testing are missing.
- Weakness: limited scope—short texts, controlled eye-tracker conditions; unclear generalization to longer texts, other domains or languages.
- Requested clarification: do you require gaze at inference, or can gaze be used only at training (student model)?
- Requested clarification: how are labels aggregated and are folds stratified to prevent annotator-specific leakage?
- Requested clarification: how are gaze features normalized across devices and readers (sampling rates, fatigue, baseline speed)?
- Requested clarification: please compare to at least one strong deep text-only baseline and a strong sarcasm detector used as a feature.
- Requested clarification: provide full ablation results controlling for length/readability and significance tests across both datasets.
- Requested clarification: provide finer error breakdown by phenomenon (negation, implicit sentiment, thwarting) showing where gaze helps or hurts.
- Overall: interesting and novel direction with suggestive evidence for gaze helping on sarcasm, but limited impact due to practicality, baseline gaps, confounds, and clarity issues.
