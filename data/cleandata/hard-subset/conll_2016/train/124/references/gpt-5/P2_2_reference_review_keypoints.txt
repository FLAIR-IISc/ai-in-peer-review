- Paper studies whether eye-tracking–derived cognitive features improve sentence-level sentiment analysis, especially for sarcasm/irony, by combining gaze features with standard textual features.
- Empirical result: adding gaze features to text yields modest but statistically significant F1 gains on two public eye-tracking datasets, with larger gains on a sarcasm/irony subset.
- Methodological contribution: introduces “gaze-saliency graphs” and degree-based features to encode complex gaze patterns, with discussion of feasibility and limitations.

Strengths:
- Novel signal: using reader gaze for sentiment analysis is an interesting, underexplored idea that plausibly complements textual cues.
- Stronger gains on hard cases: larger relative improvements on sarcastic/ironic texts support the psycholinguistic hypothesis about regressions/saccades.
- Rigorous analysis: includes feature ablations, significance testing, and chi-square feature ranking showing many top features are gaze-based.
- Reproducibility potential: uses public datasets collected with different hardware, reducing the chance of device-specific effects.
- Well-motivated feature design: mapping gaze behaviors to linguistic difficulty is grounded in psycholinguistic literature.

Weaknesses:
- Practicality/external validity: approach requires eye-tracking at inference, with feasibility discussion speculative and no realistic deployment evaluation (single noisy reader, mobile devices).
- Modest overall effect and limited baselines: small average F1 gains and missing comparisons to strong contemporary text-only neural baselines weaken the empirical claim.
- Possible train–test leakage and sparse procedural detail: unclear whether learned features (PCA, lexical polarity models) were fit only on training folds; gaze-graph parameterization and normalization details are thin.
- Data/task confounds: gaze collected during annotation may reflect annotation strategies rather than natural reading; dataset filtering (e.g., removing objective instances) may bias results and no cross-dataset generalization is reported.
- Insufficient analysis of gaze feature utility: unclear which specific gaze features drive improvements and whether graph-based features outperform simple gaze summaries.

Questions to authors:
- At inference, what is the intended gaze source (single reader vs aggregated)? How sensitive are results to reader count and device quality—please report single-reader and noisy/downsampled results.
- Were PCA components, implicit incongruity patterns, and lexical polarity models recomputed within each training fold to avoid leakage? Clarify cross-validation procedures.
- Can you report cross-dataset transfer (train on D1/test on D2 and vice versa) for text-only vs text+gaze to assess robustness to hardware and domain differences?
- Do basic gaze features alone account for most gains, or are saliency-graph features necessary? Please provide an ablation separating basic vs graph-based gaze features.
- Please include stronger text-only baselines (e.g., contemporary neural models or tuned linear classifiers with richer n-grams/char features) to quantify the incremental benefit of gaze.

Overall assessment:
- Interesting and novel idea with suggestive evidence that gaze helps for sarcasm/irony, but practical applicability, effect sizes, methodological clarity, and baseline comparisons are insufficient; recommend rejection in current form with suggestions to address leakage, stronger baselines, transfer tests, and realistic deployment pathways or gaze prediction.
