Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes augmenting standard sentiment analysis (SA) features with “cognitive features” derived from eye-tracking (fixations, saccades, regressions), including a novel gaze-saliency graph representation. The goal is to better capture difficult phenomena such as sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations.
- Evaluation on two public datasets with gaze collected during sentiment-related annotation shows modest gains when adding gaze features to text-based features (0.6–1.5 F1 absolute over sentiment+sarcasm features; 3.7–9.3 absolute over a simpler sentiment feature set). A held-out subset suggests larger relative gains for sarcastic/ironic instances.
- A chi-squared feature analysis ranks several gaze-based features among the top predictors.

Strengths
- Interesting idea and nonstandard signal: Using eye-tracking to enrich SA features is thought-provoking and could illuminate where text-only models struggle (sarcasm, implicit sentiment). The saliency-graph features are a creative way to summarize gaze dynamics.
- Multi-dataset evaluation with different eye trackers: Results on two datasets, one with a high-quality tracker and one with a lower-quality tracker, suggest the approach is not tied to a single hardware setting. The authors also analyze a held-out set focusing on complex constructs.
- Statistical testing and feature analysis: The McNemar test (for the best vs. second-best on D1) and chi-squared ranking provide some rigor beyond raw scores. The finding that several gaze features rank highly is informative.
- Attention to confounds: The inclusion of readability and length features acknowledges that gaze can reflect generic reading difficulty, not just sentiment phenomena.

Weaknesses
- Missing comparison to strong text-only baselines and SOTA: The paper does not compare to modern neural baselines for SA and sarcasm detection (e.g., CNN/RNN, transformers like BERT/RoBERTa), despite acknowledging their existence. This is a major omission given that current SOTA text-only models often excel on short texts, including sarcastic content. Moreover, Table 1 shows an external SVM trained on standard corpora achieves 76.8 F1 on Dataset 2, which exceeds the best reported result with gaze (71.8). The claim of “improvement over the state of the art” is unsubstantiated.
- Practicality and applicability remain speculative: The approach requires gaze at test time. The paper argues mobile eye-tracking will become available, but this is speculative and lacks a concrete deployment scenario or feasibility study. There is no analysis of performance with a single reader (vs. averaging over 5–7), nor sensitivity to the number of readers. Without such evidence, practical utility is unclear.
- Experimental design and reporting gaps: 
  - The main results use 10-fold CV within each dataset; no cross-dataset generalization (train on D1, test on D2, and vice versa) is reported. 
  - It is unclear whether the gold labels are majority votes from the same readers whose gaze is used and whether this introduces task-coupled biases.
  - The paper does not report whether F1 is macro or micro; given class imbalance on D1, this matters. Majority-class baselines are not reported.
  - Hyperparameters are mostly Weka defaults, which may handicap baselines; there is little evidence of tuning for fairness.
  - The unigram and incongruity features are reduced to 500 PCA components on small datasets; risk of overfitting is not analyzed.
- Effect size is small and inconsistent: The incremental benefit of gaze over sentiment+sarcasm features is 0.6 (D1) and 1.5 (D2) F1, which is modest. “Gaze-only” performs near random on D2. The held-out subset improvement for irony is encouraging, but the setup lacks comparison to strong text-only irony detectors and does not quantify variance across random subsets.
- Novelty is incremental relative to prior gaze work: Prior studies have already used gaze for sentiment-related tasks (e.g., Mishra et al., 2014; Joshi et al., 2014). The main new element is the saliency-graph feature set, which is interesting but not deeply analyzed (e.g., ablation of individual graph features, visualization/interpretation, robustness).

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Can you include comparisons to strong text-only baselines (e.g., linear models with rich n-grams/character features, CNN/LSTM, BERT/RoBERTa fine-tuned on in-domain data)? For the held-out irony subset, please compare against modern sarcasm detectors.
- Clarify Table 1 vs Table 2: On Dataset 2, an external SVM baseline attains 76.8 F1, higher than your best 71.8. How do you reconcile this with claims of improving over the state of the art? What is the training data for Table 2 models vs Table 1?
- Metric and class balance: Is F1 macro or micro? Please report per-class F1 and majority-class baselines for D1 and D2.
- Train/test protocol: How are labels aggregated (majority vote?) and are gaze features averaged over annotators? Could the same annotators’ task-specific gaze patterns bias the features toward the chosen labels? Please evaluate cross-dataset transfer (train D1, test D2, and vice versa).
- Practicality: What is performance when only one reader’s gaze is available? How does F1 vary with the number of readers (1, 2, 3, …)? Are there per-annotator normalization/calibration steps? Any privacy/consent considerations and data governance plans?
- Feature ablations: Which specific gaze features (basic vs. graph-based) drive the gains? Please provide a thorough ablation and significance testing per feature family.
- Robustness: Have you controlled for reading difficulty confounds more explicitly (e.g., stratifying by readability/length and re-evaluating gains)? Any domain effects (tweets vs. reviews)?

Overall evaluation
This paper presents an intriguing direction—leveraging gaze for sentiment analysis—and offers some evidence of small gains, particularly on sarcastic/ironic instances. However, given the lack of comparisons to modern strong text-only baselines, the modest effect sizes, questions about practicality and generalization, and limited analysis of confounds, I am not convinced of the methodological or empirical maturity needed for acceptance. Strengthening the baselines, clarifying the evaluation, and providing practical scenarios (e.g., single-reader settings, cross-dataset transfer) would be necessary to assess the real contribution of gaze features.
