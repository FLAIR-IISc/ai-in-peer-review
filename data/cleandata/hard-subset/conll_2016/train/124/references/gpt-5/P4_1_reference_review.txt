Summary and contributions
This paper investigates sentiment analysis of short texts by augmenting standard textual features with cognitive features derived from human eye-movement during reading. Two gaze-annotated datasets (with moderate inter-annotator agreement and differing eye-tracking hardware) are used. The authors engineer (i) standard sentiment features, (ii) sarcasm/thwarting features, and (iii) a set of basic and graph-based gaze features plus readability controls. They evaluate SVM, Naive Bayes, and a shallow neural network via 10-fold cross-validation and analyze feature importance and statistical significance.

Contributions (as assessed):
1) Introduction and evaluation of gaze-derived cognitive features for sentiment classification, including novel graph-based gaze-saliency features, showing consistent albeit modest gains over text-only features across two datasets.
2) Empirical evidence that gaze features yield larger relative improvements on texts with complex constructs (sarcasm/irony) compared to simpler texts, via a held-out subset analysis.
3) A comprehensive feature ablation and significance analysis indicating that multiple gaze features are among the top-ranked predictors.

Strengths
- Novel use of cognitive signals for SA: Incorporating eye-tracking features directly into a sentiment classifier for text classification is distinctive relative to prior work that used gaze for annotation difficulty or sarcasm understandability. The paper contributes a concrete feature set (basic and graph-based) and demonstrates their utility.
- Empirical gains, especially on challenging phenomena: Across both datasets, adding sarcasm/thwarting features to standard sentiment features yields meaningful improvements (e.g., +3.1 F1 on D1, +7.8 on D2), and adding gaze features further improves performance (+0.6 and +1.5 F1). On the irony subset of a held-out set, gaze adds +6.1 F1 relative improvement, supporting the hypothesis that cognitive signals help with higher-level pragmatic phenomena.
- Multi-dataset evaluation with differing eye-trackers: Results are reported on two public datasets with different sampling rates (Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120), showing that gaze features remain useful even with lower-quality hardware, albeit with smaller gains.
- Feature significance analysis and statistics: Chi-squared rankings indicate that a substantial fraction of top features are gaze-based (10/20 for D1; 7/20 for D2). McNemar tests are used to assess significance (e.g., p=0.0001 on D1 best vs. second-best), lending rigor to the reported improvements.
- Clear feature engineering and task framing: The paper details the construction of sentiment, sarcasm, and gaze features, including rationale grounded in psycholinguistics (e.g., regressions, fixation durations), which enhances clarity and interpretability.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and inference-time assumptions: The approach requires eye-tracking at test time. While feasibility is discussed, no concrete deployment setting or user study is provided to validate that real-world systems can collect and use gaze reliably and ethically at scale. As a result, the method’s applicability currently appears limited to settings where gaze can be captured during inference.
- Baseline and comparison inconsistencies: Table 1 shows an SVM “existing system” baseline achieving F=76.8 on Dataset 2, which exceeds the best in-domain cross-validated result with gaze (F=71.8). This contradicts the claim of state-of-the-art improvement and suggests that, at least on D2, the proposed approach does not surpass a strong text-only system. The paper should reconcile this discrepancy and clarify what constitutes the “state of the art” baseline.
- Potential methodological leakage and missing details: PCA for feature reduction (e.g., NGRAM_PCA, IMPLICIT_PCA) must be fit on training folds only; this is not stated. The use of McNemar tests over 10-fold CV predictions requires paired per-instance predictions; the exact testing protocol is unclear. Defaults are used for classifiers without nested cross-validation, increasing the risk of suboptimal hyperparameters or overfitting on small datasets (~1k instances). It is also unclear whether external models used to compute features (e.g., LP from Joshi et al.) had any overlap with the evaluation data.
- Conflation of gaze and readability effects: The “Gz” group includes both gaze and reading-difficulty features, making it hard to isolate the contribution of gaze alone. In some cases, gaze-only features degrade performance (e.g., SVM on D2: F=48.9), suggesting instability or sensitivity to device quality. An ablation separating readability and gaze signals is needed.
- Generalization across readers and devices: Gaze signals are averaged across multiple annotators per sentence; the evaluation does not test generalization to unseen readers or devices explicitly. Given known inter-subject variability and hardware differences, it remains unclear how robust the learned models are to new users or platforms.

Questions to authors
- Data processing: Was PCA (for unigrams and implicit incongruity) fit exclusively on training splits within each cross-validation fold? Were any other preprocessing steps (e.g., vocabulary selection, standardization) confined to training data?
- Statistical testing: How were McNemar tests conducted in the 10-fold CV setting (e.g., pooled out-of-fold predictions per instance)? Were significance tests run for Dataset 2 as well?
- External feature sources: For LP and IMPLICIT features, what training data were used? Is there any overlap with D1/D2 items? Please clarify to rule out data leakage.
- Baseline reconciliation: Why does the SVM baseline in Table 1 achieve higher F1 on D2 than your best in-domain system with gaze? What is the precise feature set used for the Table 1 SVM, and can the proposed features be added to that baseline for a fair comparison?
- Gaze vs. readability: Can you report ablations that separate gaze features from length/readability controls to quantify the net contribution of gaze?
- Generalization: Did you assess performance when training on gaze from a subset of annotators and testing on gaze from disjoint annotators (cross-subject evaluation)? How do results vary across the two eye-tracking devices?

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and clearly described integration of cognitive features for sentiment analysis with evidence of benefits on complex language phenomena. Addressing the methodological and baseline issues above would strengthen the technical soundness and clarify the practical value.
