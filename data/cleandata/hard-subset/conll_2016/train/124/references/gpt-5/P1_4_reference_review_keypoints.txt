- Paper investigates whether eye-tracking–derived cognitive features improve sentiment polarity classification, especially for sarcasm/irony/thwarted expectation.  
- Authors combine standard sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting features, gaze features (aggregate + graph-based gaze-saliency), and readability controls.  
- Evaluation uses SVM, NB, and MLP with 10-fold CV on two public eye-tracking datasets (~1k items each), plus a small held-out ironic vs non-ironic test.  
- Contribution: empirical evidence that adding gaze features yields small but statistically significant F1 gains, larger on ironic/sarcastic texts.  
- Contribution: a catalog of engineered cognitive features for sentiment analysis, including a gaze-saliency graph and degree-based metrics.  
- Contribution: analysis showing several gaze features rank highly by chi-squared importance, implying added signal beyond text.  
- Strength: tackles hard sentiment cases with implicit sentiment/sarcasm where lexical features often fail; held-out analysis shows notable gains on ironic items.  
- Strength: cross-dataset evaluation with different eye-trackers (500Hz vs 120Hz) suggests some robustness despite hardware and dataset size differences.  
- Strength: statistical testing (McNemar’s test) and chi-squared feature importance strengthen the claim that gains are not due to chance.  
- Strength: gaze features are clearly motivated from psycholinguistics and the graph features are interpretable and reusable.  
- Strength: relevant to cognitive NLP and human-in-the-loop research communities.  
- Weakness: practicality—requires eye-tracking at inference, and feasibility discussion is speculative without a clear deployment pathway.  
- Weakness: modest incremental gains from gaze in CV experiments, with majority improvement from sarcasm/thwarting features; no ablation separating basic vs graph gaze features.  
- Weakness: missing strong modern text-only baselines (e.g., fine-tuned contextual encoders) to judge whether gaze adds value over current neural models.  
- Weakness: potential confounds—gaze collected during annotation may reflect labeling processes; averaging across annotators may hide individual differences and no user-level split used.  
- Weakness: incomplete methodological detail (labeling scheme, preprocessing/scaling, LP feature training, class imbalance handling) and formatting issues; McNemar test only on D1.  
- Questions: how to use the model without gaze at test time—any attempts to predict gaze from text or distill gaze into a gaze-free student model?  
- Questions: provide ablations between basic vs graph gaze features and per-feature group contributions.  
- Questions: compare to fine-tuned contextual encoders (e.g., RoBERTa) and test whether gaze still helps those models.  
- Questions: can you rule out annotation-time gaze confounds by testing natural reading gaze or cross-reader generalization (train/test on different annotators)?  
- Questions: clarify data/implementation details—how gold labels derived, how LP feature trained without label leakage, feature standardization, and controls for length/readability to ensure gaze is not a proxy.
