Summary and contributions
- This paper studies whether cognitive features derived from eye-tracking can improve sentiment polarity classification of short texts, particularly those with higher-level phenomena such as sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectation. The authors engineer a feature set combining: (i) standard sentiment features, (ii) features designed for sarcasm/thwarting, and (iii) gaze features (both simple aggregates and novel graph-derived “gaze-saliency” features), plus readability controls. They evaluate with SVM, NB, and MLP via 10-fold CV on two public eye-tracking datasets (≈1k items each) and present feature-importance analyses and a small held-out evaluation focused on ironic vs non-ironic items.
- Contributions (as I see them):
  1) Empirical evidence that adding gaze-derived features to text-based features yields small but statistically significant gains in F1 for sentiment analysis, with larger gains on texts containing irony/sarcasm.
  2) A catalog of engineered cognitive features for SA, including a gaze-saliency graph with degree-based metrics; and a combined feature-engineering recipe spanning sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, readability, and gaze.
  3) An analysis indicating several gaze features rank highly by chi-squared importance, suggesting they capture useful information beyond textual features.

Strengths
- Tackling a hard slice of SA: The datasets intentionally contain many instances with implicit sentiment, sarcasm, and thwarting—cases where standard lexical/syntactic features often fail. The held-out analysis shows larger gains from gaze on ironic texts (+4.2 F1 over Sn+Sr, +6.1 F1 over Sn), supporting the claim that gaze helps with complex constructs.
- Cross-dataset evaluation with different hardware: The paper evaluates on two public datasets collected with different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000 at 500Hz and Tobii T120 at 120Hz). Despite hardware variability and modest dataset size, the pattern—gains from adding gaze to textual features—holds (albeit small on D1), suggesting some robustness.
- Statistical testing and feature importance: The authors conduct McNemar’s test for the top two systems on D1 (p=0.0001) and for the held-out analysis (p<0.05), and provide a chi-squared ranking where many top features are gaze-based. This strengthens the credibility that gains are not due to chance and that gaze contributes signal.
- Clear, interpretable feature design: The paper carefully motivates gaze features from psycholinguistic literature (fixations, regressions, saccade length) and explains the intuition behind the graph features (e.g., surprisal leading to regressions). This is informative and could be reused in other cognitive-NLP contexts.
- Relevance to cognitive NLP: Bridging eye-movement patterns with NLP objectives is timely and of interest to the CoNLL community exploring cognitive signals and human-in-the-loop resources.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and deployment: The approach requires eye-tracking at inference. The feasibility discussion is speculative (future mobile eye-trackers) and does not offer a realistic pathway for most SA applications today. Without a way to infer or distill gaze from text alone, the method’s utility is limited to settings where per-instance gaze can be collected at test time.
- Modest incremental gains attributable to gaze and limited ablations: On the main CV experiments, the majority of improvement comes from adding sarcasm/thwarting features (Sn→Sn+Sr: +3.1/+7.8 F1), while gaze adds relatively small extra gains (Sn+Sr→Sn+Sr+Gz: +0.6/+1.5 F1). There is no ablation between “basic” vs “graph” gaze features to show which drive the gains, nor an analysis comparing gaze to alternative text-based features that model incongruity/surprisal.
- Missing strong baselines: No comparison to modern text-only neural baselines (e.g., contextual encoders) trained and evaluated on the same datasets. Given that deep models can capture contextual incongruity and negation, it is unclear whether gaze would still help over a strong BERT-like baseline. The “existing SA systems” trained on different domains are not compelling comparisons due to domain mismatch.
- Potential confounds and design concerns: Gaze was collected during annotation for sentiment labels; this may introduce task/label-related gaze patterns (confirmation or decision difficulty) that differ from natural reading. Averaging gaze across annotators may wash out informative individual differences and hides whether performance depends on annotator identity. No user-level split is used (though items are split), and it is unclear whether models depend on annotator-specific gaze idiosyncrasies. The “LP” logistic regression feature trained on external data could introduce unknown overlap/transfer issues.
- Incomplete methodological detail and clarity: Important implementation details are underspecified (e.g., majority labeling scheme, preprocessing and scaling, specifics of Riloff-style bootstrap phrases, training of the LP model, handling of class imbalance). Tables and text have formatting and typographical issues, hindering clarity. Only D1 gets a McNemar test for the top systems.

Questions to authors
- Inference without gaze: How do you envision using this model when eye-tracking is unavailable at test time? Have you tried learning a student model that predicts gaze features from text (or distilling from a gaze-augmented teacher) to achieve gaze-free inference?
- Gaze feature ablations: Which cognitive features contribute most? Please provide ablations between basic gaze features and graph-based features, and per-feature group contributions.
- Strong baselines: How do your results compare to fine-tuned contextual encoders (e.g., RoBERTa) on the same folds without gaze? Does adding gaze to such models still help?
- Confounds of annotation-time gaze: Did you collect or have access to “natural reading” gaze without simultaneous labeling? Can you test cross-reader/cross-session generalization (e.g., train on gaze from one subset of annotators and test on held-out annotators) to rule out annotator-specific artifacts?
- Data/implementation details: How are gold labels derived (majority vote? thresholds)? How is the LP feature trained and guaranteed not to leak label information? Were features standardized per dataset? Did you control for length/readability beyond RED and LEN (e.g., partial correlations) to ensure gaze effects are not proxies for length?

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and relevant exploration of cognitive features for sentiment analysis on difficult phenomena, with some positive evidence that gaze can help. However, the practical limitations, absence of strong baselines, and missing ablations temper the impact. Strengthening the experimental comparisons and clarifying methodology would substantially improve the contribution.
