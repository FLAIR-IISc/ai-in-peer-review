Summary and contributions
- The paper investigates whether cognitive features derived from eye-tracking can improve sentence-level sentiment analysis, especially for linguistically complex phenomena such as sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations. Two existing eye-tracking datasets are used (D1: 994 snippets with high proportion of sarcasm; D2: 843 subjective snippets), with extensive feature engineering: (i) standard sentiment features (lexicon, POS ratios, discourse), (ii) sarcasm/thwarting features (e.g., incongruity phrases, punctuation, polarity subsequences), (iii) gaze features (basic fixation/saccade statistics and graph-based “gaze saliency” features), and (iv) reading-difficulty controls. Classifiers (SVM, NB, MLP) are trained with 10-fold CV, and feature ablations show incremental gains when adding gaze. A held-out subset with many sarcastic examples shows larger improvements for complex texts.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding: Eye-tracking–based cognitive features provide consistent, statistically significant gains over strong text-only feature sets on two datasets, with larger gains for sarcastic/ironic text.
2) Feature design: A reasonably novel set of gaze features, including graph-based saliency measures (weighted degrees, edge density), beyond standard fixation/saccade aggregates.
3) Analysis: Feature importance (chi-squared) and subgroup analysis (irony vs. non-irony), plus a significance test (McNemar) demonstrating that gains are not due to chance.

Strengths
- Well-motivated problem and clear hypothesis: The paper articulates why text-only features struggle with pragmatic phenomena and why gaze (a proxy for cognitive processing) could help. The test sets are indeed challenging (Table 1 shows notable drops for off-the-shelf SA systems).
- Consistent empirical gains across datasets and models: Adding sarcasm/thwarting features to sentiment features yields substantial boosts (e.g., SVM F1: +3.1 on D1, +7.8 on D2), and adding gaze on top yields further improvements (SVM F1: +0.6 on D1, +1.5 on D2; statistically significant on D1 per McNemar). On a held-out sarcasm-heavy subset, the added value of gaze is larger (Irony: 58.2 → 64.3 F1).
- Novel gaze feature engineering: The graph-based “gaze-saliency” features are a creative attempt to capture higher-order relations in gaze behavior, complementing basic fixation/saccade stats.
- Careful acknowledgement of confounds: The authors include reading ease and length features and discuss sources of error (e.g., hardware limitations, aggregation loss), showing awareness of potential artifacts.
- Use of public datasets and interpretable features: The datasets are available; the feature definitions are explicitly described, aiding reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Questionable practicality at inference time: The approach requires eye-tracking at test time, which is not realistic for most SA applications. The feasibility section is speculative (mobile eye trackers) and not validated. Without a pathway to deploy (e.g., distilling gaze into a text-only student model), impact is limited.
- Missing strong text-only baselines: The paper explicitly excludes deep learning methods (CNN/RNN/transformers) despite acknowledging their state-of-the-art performance. To substantiate the contribution, comparisons to modern text-only baselines on the same splits are necessary; otherwise, the incremental gains from gaze may vanish relative to stronger text models.
- Small absolute improvements from gaze on full datasets: While statistically significant, the added F1 from gaze over Sn+Sr is modest (+0.6 and +1.5). The case for gaze is stronger on sarcasm-only subsets; more detailed breakdowns (e.g., per-phenomenon ablations, confidence intervals) would strengthen the claim that gaze meaningfully targets pragmatic difficulty beyond general difficulty proxies.
- Potential methodological ambiguities and confounds:
  • Normalization across readers: Gaze features are averaged across annotators; no subject-level normalization (e.g., z-scores per reader) is reported to control for reading speed or individual variability.
  • Data processing in CV: It is unclear whether dimensionality reduction (PCA for unigrams/incongruity phrases) is fit only on training folds. Leakage here would inflate results.
  • Saliency graph construction details: The weighting schemes and handling of multiple fixations/saccades per edge are described, but not enough to fully replicate; also unclear how punctuation/skips are treated as nodes/edges.
  • Statistical testing: Significance is reported only for one comparison on D1; broader testing (both datasets, multiple classifiers) and confidence intervals would be more convincing.
- Clarity/Presentation issues: Several typos and formatting glitches (e.g., table inconsistencies, section numbering), and a few confusing entries (Table 2 “Sn+Gz” row for D2 appears inconsistent) hinder readability and verification.

Questions to authors
- Practical deployment: Do you envision a setting where sentiment must be predicted for a text independent of any reader? If so, how would you use gaze at test time? Could you train a text-only model to imitate gaze-enhanced predictions (knowledge distillation) and report its performance?
- Reader normalization: Did you perform per-participant normalization (e.g., within-subject z-scoring of fixation durations, saccade lengths) before averaging to mitigate individual variability? If not, can you report whether normalization changes the results?
- Cross-validation hygiene: For features involving PCA (NGRAM_PCA, IMPLICIT_PCA), were PCA components fit only on the training folds and then applied to the test folds? Please clarify to rule out train–test leakage.
- Feature ablations: Among gaze features, which subsets contribute most? Do the gains persist if you remove RED and LEN, or tightly control for length (e.g., matched-length subsets)?
- Stronger baselines: Can you include competitive deep models (e.g., biLSTM, CNN, transformer fine-tuning) trained on the same folds to quantify the incremental benefit of gaze relative to current SA practice?
- Subject effects: Given that gaze is averaged over multiple annotators per snippet, can you evaluate a setting with a single reader’s gaze at test time to assess robustness in low-redundancy scenarios?

Overall, the paper presents a novel and interesting empirical result—that eye-tracking features can measurably aid sentiment classification on pragmatically challenging texts—but its practical impact is limited without a gaze-free inference path and stronger baselines. Addressing the methodological and deployment questions above would substantially strengthen the submission.
