Summary and claimed contributions
- This paper investigates augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking. Using two publicly available gaze datasets (movie/tweet snippets, with substantial presence of sarcasm/irony), the authors engineer (i) standard sentiment features, (ii) features for sarcasm/thwarting, (iii) gaze features (basic and graph-based), and (iv) readability features, and evaluate NB/SVM/MLP with 10-fold CV.
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) Empirical finding: Adding gaze-derived features to text features can yield statistically significant improvements in binary sentiment classification, with stronger gains on instances involving complex phenomena (sarcasm/irony). The held-out “complex constructs” analysis (Irony: 58.2 → 64.3 F with Sn→Sn+Sr+Gz) is the most compelling evidence.
  2) Feature design: A set of gaze features (including novel gaze-saliency graph features) that are interpretable and linked to psycholinguistic evidence (fixations, regressions, saccade length).
  3) Cross-dataset validation across different eye-tracker hardware (Eyelink-1000 vs Tobii T120), suggesting some robustness of the gaze signal.

Strengths
- Addresses a hard slice of SA: The datasets are shown to be challenging for standard systems (Table 1), with many sarcastic/ironic items. The targeted gains on the ironic subset (Table 4) support the claim that gaze helps where textual cues are weak or incongruous. This is a meaningful contribution because sarcasm/irony remain difficult for SA.
- Novelty in combining gaze with SA: Prior gaze work in NLP has focused on annotation complexity, WSD, or sarcasm understandability; applying gaze to downstream SA classification and proposing graph-derived gaze features is new and interesting.
- Careful feature reasoning linked to psycholinguistics: The mapping from first-fixation duration, regressions, saccade length, and skip rates to lexical/syntactic/semantic difficulty is well motivated, with citations. The RSF/LREG features show thoughtfulness about surprisal/incongruity.
- Some statistical hygiene: They perform McNemar tests (albeit limited) and a chi-squared feature importance analysis to show gaze features rank highly, lending credence that gaze contributes distinct signal beyond text.
- Cross-device datasets: Demonstrating gains on datasets collected with different eye-trackers and sampling rates mitigates concerns about device-specific artifacts.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and deployment: The method requires gaze at inference time. Despite the feasibility section, the use of mobile eye-tracking for large-scale sentiment classification remains speculative, with unresolved privacy/consent and reliability issues. The paper lacks a training-only gaze scenario (e.g., using gaze to learn surrogate signals or student models) that could make deployment realistic.
- Baselines and comparisons: No comparison against strong modern text-only baselines (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN or transformers). The rationale that “our approach is feature-based” is not sufficient; state-of-the-art SA is deep and may already handle some incongruity via context. Without such baselines, the incremental value of gaze is unclear. Moreover, even within feature-based methods, results show most of the overall gains come from sarcasm/thwarting features; the additional boost from gaze on full datasets is modest (0.6–1.5 F), making the case for gaze less compelling without stronger baselines.
- Experimental details and clarity gaps: Several critical details are missing/unclear, e.g., exact label aggregation (majority vote?), per-sentence vs per-annotator feature construction and whether cross-validation folds prevent annotator-specific leakage, normalization across different sampling rates/devices, precise hyperparameters for MLP, PCA training protocol, and construction of “implicit incongruity” features. Table 3 (feature ranking) is referenced but not present here. The writing contains typos and occasional grammatical issues that impede clarity.
- Potential confounds: Because the gaze features and labels come from the same readers, gaze may correlate with annotation difficulty rather than ground-truth sentiment. Averaging gaze over annotators may wash out informative idiosyncrasies, but also risks encoding aggregate confusion. The paper partially controls for length/readability, but other text difficulty confounds may remain. Additionally, gains from gaze alone are weak (Table 2), and significance is only reported for one setting; more thorough statistical testing (including dataset 2 and ablations controlling for length/readability) would strengthen claims.
- Generalization and scope: The work is limited to short texts with controlled eye-tracker conditions. It is unclear how well the approach generalizes to longer documents, multilingual settings, or out-of-domain content, nor how sensitive results are to the proportion of sarcasm/irony.

Questions to authors
- Do you require gaze at inference time? Have you explored training-time-only use of gaze (e.g., learning a student model that predicts gaze-informed targets from text alone) to make deployment feasible?
- How are labels aggregated across annotators? Are folds stratified to avoid annotator leakage (e.g., ensuring a sentence’s gaze from reader r does not appear in both train and test via per-reader splits)? Please clarify per-sentence vs per-reader feature construction and fold design.
- How are gaze features normalized across devices (500 Hz Eyelink vs 120 Hz Tobii), and across readers (e.g., fatigue, calibration, baseline reading speed)? Any per-reader z-scoring?
- Could you compare against at least one competitive deep text-only baseline (e.g., a transformer fine-tuned on your datasets), and against a strong sarcasm detector used as a feature? This would help quantify the unique contribution of gaze.
- Can you provide full ablation results controlling for length/readability (e.g., removing LEN/RED), and significance tests for all key improvements on both datasets?
- What is the error breakdown by phenomenon beyond “irony/non-irony” (e.g., negation scope, implicit sentiment, thwarting), and where does gaze help/hurt most?

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and novel direction with suggestive evidence that gaze features help especially on sarcastic/ironic texts. However, practicality, missing strong baselines, and clarity concerns limit the current impact. Strengthening experimental rigor and addressing deployment would markedly improve the submission.
