Summary and contributions
This paper augments standard text-based sentiment analysis (SA) features with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye movements (fixations, saccades, regressions), plus a small set of readability proxies. Using two public gaze-annotated datasets (~1k instances each), it reports modest but statistically significant F1 gains when adding gaze features on top of text features, and larger gains on a held-out subset with sarcasm/irony.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical demonstration that aggregated eye-tracking features provide incremental benefit for SA beyond strong text-only features, with larger gains on irony/sarcasm-heavy inputs.
2) A concrete set of gaze features (basic and graph-based saliency metrics) and a feature-engineering recipe that integrates them with sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features.
3) An evaluation showing that simple readability controls are insufficient to account for the observed gains, suggesting gaze carries information not captured by standard textual proxies.

Strengths
- Clear empirical signal on hard cases: On the irony/sarcasm subset of the held-out data, adding gaze to text features improves F1 by 6.1% (relative), larger than for non-irony. This aligns with the hypothesis that gaze reflects higher-level processing (e.g., surprisal, regressions) pertinent to figurative language.
- Carefully engineered feature design and rationale: The basic gaze features (FDUR, REG, SKIP, RSF, LREG) and saliency-graph features have plausible psycholinguistic motivation, and the paper explains the intuitions. The inclusion of readability and length controls is a thoughtful step to reduce confounding.
- Use of public datasets and multiple classifiers: Both datasets are available, and results are shown across NB, linear SVM, and MLP, with consistent patterns. Reporting of a random baseline helps contextualize F1 values.
- Incremental gains beyond strong text-only features: On both datasets, most of the improvement comes from adding sarcasm/thwarting features (Sn→Sn+Sr), but gaze still yields an additional 0.6–1.5 F1 points (Sn+Sr→Sn+Sr+Gz) with significance tested for D1. This suggests gaze adds complementary signal.
- Feature importance analysis: A chi-squared ranking reportedly places many gaze features among the top-20 predictors on both datasets, supporting the claim that gaze features are informative (though the actual ranked list is not shown in the paper excerpt).

Weaknesses
- Limited external validity and practicality at inference: The main experiments assume gaze is available for test-time inputs. While the paper discusses potential mobile eye-tracking, it does not demonstrate feasibility. Crucially, there is no analysis of performance as a function of the number of readers, device quality, or noisy gaze; models trained on averages from 5–7 readers may not translate to single-reader or in-the-wild use.
- Modest overall gains attributable to gaze on pooled data: The additional improvement from gaze over strong text-only features is small (0.6–1.5 F1 points). While statistically significant in one case, the practical impact for general SA may be limited unless the deployment setting specifically targets figurative/complex language with available gaze.
- Confounds and missing controls: D1 and D2 use different eye-trackers (500Hz vs 120Hz) and participant protocols. The paper does not control for hardware effects, participant identity, or sentence-by-sentence differences in annotation task (e.g., gaze while deciding labels). There is no leave-one-subject-out analysis, no cross-device normalization, and no cross-dataset generalization (train on D1, test on D2 and vice versa).
- Baselines and comparisons are not state-of-the-art: The paper explicitly excludes stronger neural text-only baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN/transformer fine-tuning), which weakens the claim that gaze provides value beyond what modern textual models capture. Without these comparisons, the incremental benefit may be overstated relative to current practice.
- Statistical testing and reporting issues: The text alternates between “accuracy” and “F-score.” McNemar’s test is mentioned for F-score differences but typically applies to paired accuracy/disagreement counts; the methodology should be clarified. The feature-importance table is referenced but not shown. Classwise metrics and variance across folds/readers are not reported.
- Reproducibility details are thin: Preprocessing (AoI mapping, saccade detection parameters), saliency-graph construction choices (“may not be unique” nodes), and MLP architecture/hyperparameters are insufficiently specified for replication.

Questions to authors
- Inference feasibility: How many readers are needed at test time to realize most of the gains? Please provide performance vs number of readers (1, 2, 3, …) and device sampling rate/noise. Have you tried single-reader inference?
- Cross-subject/device robustness: Do results hold under leave-one-subject-out CV? Can you normalize features to mitigate 500Hz vs 120Hz differences and report cross-dataset generalization (train on D1, test on D2; and the reverse)?
- Statistical testing: How exactly was McNemar’s test applied for F1 differences? Please report paired disagreement counts or use bootstrap or approximate randomization over F1; also correct for multiple comparisons if applicable.
- Gaze feature ablation: Which gaze features matter most? Please provide ablations separating basic vs saliency-graph features and per-feature-group contributions on both datasets and on the irony subset.
- Sarcasm/thwarting features: For LP and IMPLICIT_PCA, what are the training data and possible overlaps with your evaluation sets? Any domain adaptation steps?
- Reader-task effects: Were eye movements recorded while annotators were labeling sentiment? Could the labeling task itself induce behaviors that would not occur during natural reading? Any evidence from a passive-reading condition?
- Metric clarity: Please standardize on F1 and report macro/micro F1, per-class precision/recall, and variance across folds.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add stronger text-only baselines (e.g., fine-tuned transformer on your texts or on SST/Twitter, optionally with sarcasm pretraining) and show that gaze offers complementary gains.
- Evaluate practical settings: single-reader, low-frequency webcam eye-tracking, and noisy gaze. Provide calibration-free or minimal-calibration variants of features and show performance degradation curves.
- Provide cross-subject and cross-dataset evaluations, and normalize gaze features for hardware differences. Consider subject-level random effects or domain-adversarial training to control confounds.
- Expand analyses: detailed ablations of gaze features, learning curves versus training size, and error typology (e.g., specific sarcasm types benefiting most).
- Release code and feature-extraction pipelines (including saccade detection thresholds and graph construction) to facilitate reproducibility.
- Discuss ethics and privacy more concretely: consent, storage, anonymization of gaze streams, and potential misuse; outline a privacy-preserving deployment plan.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and timely idea with suggestive evidence that gaze carries complementary signal for difficult SA cases. However, the current evaluation does not yet establish strong generalization or practicality, and comparisons to modern text-only baselines are missing. I encourage the authors to strengthen the empirical evidence along the lines above.
