- Summary: Paper tests whether eye-tracking (gaze) features improve sentiment analysis on sarcasm/irony-heavy text, reporting small but consistent F1 gains when adding gaze features to sentiment+sarcasm features on two eye-tracking datasets.

- Contribution 1: Empirical evidence that gaze-derived features give incremental SA performance gains, especially on ironic/sarcastic cases.

- Contribution 2: A concrete set of gaze features (fixation/saccade statistics and graph-based saliency measures) with feature-importance analysis showing many gaze features rank highly.

- Contribution 3: Evaluation on two public eye-tracking datasets collected with different hardware, showing consistent qualitative patterns across sensors.

- Strength: Focus on hard SA phenomena (sarcasm/irony) and demonstration that sarcasm features help and gaze adds further gains, particularly on ironic instances.

- Strength: Gaze features are psycholinguistically motivated (fixations, regressions, saccade length) and include a novel graph representation; top features align with cognitive load/surprisal intuition.

- Strength: Cross-dataset consistency: results (Sn < Sn+Sr < Sn+Sr+Gz) hold across datasets (500Hz vs 120Hz) and classifiers (NB/SVM/MLP).

- Strength: Use of public datasets and multiple classifiers provides reproducibility and basic baseline variety.

- Weakness: Requires gaze at test time; feasibility discussion is speculative and no “train with gaze, test without” (LUPI/distillation) scenarios are explored.

- Weakness: Baselines are weak relative to modern neural SA (CNN/RNN/transformers); only a small MLP is used as a neural baseline and off-the-shelf comparisons are not directly comparable.

- Weakness: Modest absolute gains from gaze (0.6–1.5 F1) without strong in-domain text-only baselines, leaving added value unclear.

- Weakness: Possible experimental leakage—unclear whether PCA/feature selection/scaling were fit within CV folds; LP feature training details and dataset overlap are unspecified.

- Weakness: No leave-one-reader-out or per-subject controls; averaging gaze over annotators may mask subject-specific effects; no cross-dataset generalization tests.

- Weakness: Gz bundles gaze and readability features, conflating cognitive signals with text difficulty; unclear feature definitions hinder reproducibility (ED formula, PSDH/PSDS).

- Weakness: Limited statistical reporting—only one McNemar test reported; missing significance/CIs for D2 and held-out subset and no multiple-comparison corrections.

- Question: Do you require gaze at inference, and can you present a privileged-information setup where training uses gaze but deployment does not?

- Question: Was PCA/scaling/feature selection fitted only on training folds to avoid leakage—please detail the CV pipeline?

- Question: How are multiple readers’ gaze aggregated, and have you tried leave-one-reader-out or per-reader normalization to check robustness?

- Question: Can you ablate gaze-only vs readability-only within Gz and control for sentence length when assessing gaze gains?

- Question: Can you add stronger text-only baselines (CNN/BiLSTM/transformer) and test whether Sr and Gz still provide improvements?

- Question: Please report statistical tests and confidence intervals for D2 and the held-out subset, and correct for multiple comparisons if applicable.

- Question: Provide details on LP feature training data/splits and confirm no overlap with D1/D2.

- Overall evaluation: Interesting idea with some supporting evidence, but practical relevance, modest gains over strong text-only baselines, and methodological gaps limit impact; stronger baselines, rigorous CV, clear ablations, and a deployable LUPI-style setup would improve significance.
