Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes augmenting standard text-based sentiment analysis (SA) with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking during reading. The authors engineer (i) traditional sentiment features, (ii) sarcasm/irony/thwarting features, and (iii) gaze-derived features, including both simple gaze statistics and novel graph-based “gaze-saliency” features. Using two public datasets that include eye-tracking (one with high sampling, one with low sampling), they report that gaze features provide additional gains over text-only models, with larger gains on texts containing irony/sarcasm.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Introducing and operationalizing gaze-derived cognitive features (including a novel saliency-graph representation) for SA, and showing consistent but modest improvements over strong text-only feature sets across two datasets and multiple classifiers.
2) Empirical evidence that gaze features are particularly beneficial on “complex” sentiment instances (irony/sarcasm), with a larger relative F1 improvement on such cases.
3) A feature-importance analysis suggesting several gaze features rank highly, indicating they capture complementary signals to textual features.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and cross-disciplinary grounding: The idea to incorporate cognitive signals into SA is original for this task, well-motivated by psycholinguistics (fixations, saccades, regressions reflect processing difficulty, surprisal, reanalysis). The proposed saliency-graph features add a structured layer that plausibly captures cross-token interactions beyond simple counts.
- Consistent empirical gains across settings: On both datasets and multiple classifiers, adding gaze to sentiment+sarcasm features improves F1 (e.g., SVM: D1 72.9 → 73.5; D2 70.3 → 71.8), and on a held-out subset with irony/sarcasm, the relative gains are larger (e.g., 60.1 → 64.3 vs. 75.9 → 77.6 on non-irony). Using both a high-quality and a low-quality eye tracker suggests some robustness to sensor quality.
- Careful feature engineering and analysis: The feature set spans lexical, syntactic, and pragmatic cues (negation, discourse connectors, incongruity, flip counts), and the chi-squared ranking indicates gaze signals are often among the top features (10/20 in D1; 7/20 in D2). This supports the claim that gaze adds complementary information beyond text.
- Dataset curation and error/feasibility discussions: The authors purposefully evaluate on data rich in sarcasm/irony and discuss error sources and feasibility challenges, acknowledging sensor limits and proposing potential avenues for broader data collection.

Weaknesses
- Practicality at test time is unclear: The approach requires eye-tracking during inference. In most SA deployments (e.g., classifying large volumes of reviews), we do not have gaze data from readers for each item, let alone multiple readers whose signals are averaged. The proposed “mobile eye-tracking at scale” scenario is speculative and does not address fundamental mismatches: (a) how many readers per item are needed; (b) whether a single reader’s gaze suffices; (c) how to classify items before any reader has read them; (d) cost/latency/privacy trade-offs. Without a viable test-time pathway, the contribution risks being of limited practical value.
- Evaluation design and baselines: The strongest textual baselines omit modern neural SA models (e.g., CNN/LSTM/transformer-based), despite acknowledging their performance. The “existing SA systems” in Table 1 are trained on different corpora and tested on these datasets, which conflates domain mismatch with model weakness and does not constitute a fair SOTA comparison. Given the modest incremental gains from gaze over strong text+sarcasm features (+0.6 to +1.5 F1), it is vital to show competitiveness vs. standard neural models trained and evaluated in-domain.
- Possible methodological leakage and insufficient detail: It is not clear whether dimensionality reduction (e.g., PCA for NGRAM_PCA and IMPLICIT_PCA) is performed within each training fold only; if done on the full dataset, it leaks test information. Similarly, the logistic regression “lexical polarity” feature uses an external dataset—clarify that there is no overlap. The held-out set is “randomly derived from Dataset-1”; more detail is needed to ensure no contamination. Hyperparameters are mostly defaults; nested CV or tuning could change conclusions.
- Limited analysis of gaze signal usage: The method averages gaze features across multiple annotators per sentence, potentially masking inter-reader variability and making the approach dependent on having many readers. There is no sensitivity analysis (performance vs. number of readers), no per-reader robustness, and no ablation of simple vs. graph-based gaze features to quantify where the gains come from. Also, since annotators are reading while labeling sentiment, their gaze may reflect task-induced strategies; generalization to ordinary readers is unclear.
- Missing comparisons to non-gaze cognitive proxies: It would be illuminating to compare against text-derived proxies for processing difficulty (e.g., LM surprisal/perplexity, masked LM loss) and neural attention/ saliency signals. If such proxies close much of the gap, the added complexity and privacy concerns of eye-tracking may not be justified.

Questions to authors
- Test-time use: How do you envision deploying the model when no gaze is available for a new item? Can you report performance when training with gaze but inferring without it (e.g., via co-training/multi-view or teacher-student distillation from gaze to text-only)?
- Readers per item: What is performance as a function of the number of readers? Does a single reader’s gaze suffice? Please provide a sensitivity curve.
- Methodology: Was PCA fit only on training folds? Were any features computed using statistics from the full dataset? How were hyperparameters selected?
- Saliency-graph ablation: Which subset(s) of gaze features contribute most? Do simple gaze statistics (e.g., regressions, first fixation duration) capture most of the gain, or are graph features essential?
- Baselines: Can you compare against in-domain neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN/Transformer fine-tuned on these datasets) and against LM-based surprisal features?

Additional comments
- Clarify inconsistencies in Table 2 (e.g., Sn+Gz numbers for D2 appear misaligned/typo). Include Table 3 (feature rankings) in the paper or supplement.
- Discuss privacy/ethics and accessibility implications of collecting gaze at scale, and strategies for anonymization/consent.
- Minor clarity/typos exist throughout; a careful proofreading would help.

Recommendation
The paper raises an interesting, timely direction—leveraging cognitive signals to tackle nuanced sentiment. However, the current evaluation and deployment story are not yet convincing for CoNLL. I recommend rejection in its current form. To strengthen the work: (1) provide fair neural baselines; (2) add rigorous, leakage-free methodology details; (3) analyze single-reader and reader-count sensitivity; (4) ablate gaze feature subsets; (5) explore training-with-gaze, test-without-gaze strategies and compare to LM-based cognitive proxies. These steps would better quantify the value and feasibility of cognitive features for SA.
