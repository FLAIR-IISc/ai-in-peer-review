Summary and perceived contributions
- Introduces cognitive (eye-tracking) features for sentiment analysis, combined with standard lexical/syntactic/sarcasm features, and reports small but statistically significant gains on two public gaze datasets via 10-fold cross-validation.
- Proposes “gaze-saliency graph” features (degree-based measures with various edge weights) as a way to capture complex reading behavior potentially associated with sarcasm/irony.
- Provides a focused analysis suggesting that gaze features help more on “complex constructs” (irony/sarcasm) than on simpler cases, and a chi-squared feature ranking indicating several gaze features among the top predictors.

Strengths
- Novel use of gaze for SA on public datasets: Using two existing eye-tracking datasets with sentiment labels reflects a non-trivial data collection effort (by prior work) and allows reproducibility of the main comparisons on those corpora.
- Clear hypothesis and feature engineering: The paper articulates plausible psycholinguistic motivations for basic gaze measures (fixations, regressions, saccades) and derives a graph-based representation that is methodically described.
- Statistically tested improvements: The paper reports McNemar tests to establish significance (at least for the top two configurations on D1), and shows a consistent trend that Sn+Sr+Gz outperforms Sn+Sr.
- Targeted analysis on difficult phenomena: The held-out subset analysis (irony/sarcasm vs. non-irony) aligns with the paper’s motivation; larger gains on complex constructs are consistent with the cognitive rationale.
- Feature importance analysis: The chi-squared ranking showing many gaze features in the top-20 provides some interpretability and suggests the cognitive signal carries complementary information.

Weaknesses
- Questionable practicality and external validity: The approach assumes access to test-time gaze data. The “feasibility” section is speculative; no real deployment scenario, latency/cost analysis, or empirical evidence from commodity eye-trackers is provided. Without test-time gaze, the reported gains are not actionable for most SA applications.
- Inadequate baselines and tuning: Strong text-only baselines are missing. The authors explicitly omit modern deep models (CNN/RNN/transformers), but even classic SVM/linear models are under-tuned (“default Weka parameters”) and limited feature ablations are presented. Reported gains attributable to gaze are small (+0.6% and +1.5% absolute over Sn+Sr), and could evaporate against stronger text-only systems. The cross-domain baseline (trained on Pang+Sentiment140, tested on D1/D2) does not substitute for competitive in-domain baselines.
- Methodological confounds and limited rigor in evaluation: (a) Gaze was recorded while annotators were labeling sentiment, potentially entangling task-induced strategies with label signal; the model may learn annotation behavior, not general reading behavior. (b) 10-fold CV on the same dataset risks subtle leakage if any preprocessing (e.g., PCA) was fit on the full data; this is not clarified. (c) Aggregating per-sentence gaze across readers discards subject variance; no subject-wise CV to test generalization to unseen readers. (d) The “held-out” set is sampled from D1; no true out-of-domain evaluation. (e) No calibration of effect sizes via confidence intervals; only one significance test reported.
- Inconsistencies/clarity issues in results: Table 2 shows anomalies (e.g., Sn+Gz for D2 with SVM is worse than Gz alone and identical across P/R/F, suggesting errors). It is unclear how many features are used per category after PCA; whether feature scaling/normalization was applied; and whether sentence-length effects are fully controlled (beyond RED, LEN). The large claimed overall gains (e.g., 9.3%) largely stem from adding sarcasm/thwarting features; the incremental value of gaze is modest.
- Reproducibility and comparison scope: Critical implementation details are missing (hyperparameters, PCA fitting per fold, exact construction and normalization of graph features, thresholds/lists for sarcasm features). No code/models are shared. The claim of being “first of its kind” for SA discounts nearby work using gaze for related annotation-complexity/subjectivity tasks; the boundary of novelty could be more carefully situated.

Questions to authors
- Do you compute PCA, feature scaling, and any feature selection strictly within each training fold, and then apply to the test fold? Please clarify the CV protocol to rule out leakage.
- Are folds constructed to ensure reader independence (i.e., sentences read by specific annotators don’t allow a model to indirectly learn annotator-specific patterns)? Did you test generalization to unseen readers?
- How do results change with a tuned linear SVM (grid over C) or with stronger text-only baselines (e.g., n-gram+char features, fastText, or a modest CNN/LSTM)? What is the incremental gain of gaze on top of a stronger baseline?
- Table 2 has irregularities (e.g., D2 SVM for Sn+Gz). Could you verify and, if needed, correct the reported numbers? Please also report standard deviations or confidence intervals.
- For the “LP” sarcasm-related feature trained via logistic regression on Joshi et al. (2015), is there any overlap with D1/D2? How is its training controlled to avoid leakage into CV?
- Can you provide an ablation within gaze features (basic vs. graph-based) to quantify where the gains come from? Also, how sensitive are graph features to sentence length?
- What is the concrete deployment setting you envision where test-time gaze is available? Can you provide a small-scale study using a commodity eye-tracker to substantiate feasibility?

Overall, while the idea of leveraging gaze is interesting and the analysis points in the right direction, the modest gains, lack of competitive baselines, and limited practical path to deployment make the current empirical evidence insufficient. Strengthening the evaluation, clarifying methodology, and demonstrating utility in a realistic test-time scenario would substantially improve the case.
