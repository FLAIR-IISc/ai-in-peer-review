Summary and contributions
- The paper investigates whether cognitive features derived from eye-tracking can improve sentence-level sentiment analysis, especially for texts with higher-level phenomena such as sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations. The authors engineer a set of basic and graph-based gaze features and combine them with standard sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting textual features.
- Empirical contribution: On two public eye-tracking datasets (≈1k snippets each), adding gaze features to textual features yields modest but statistically significant gains in F1, with larger gains on a held-out subset containing sarcasm/irony. They also analyze feature importance, showing gaze features rank highly.
- Methodological contribution: Introduction of “gaze-saliency graphs” and derived degree-based features as a way to encode complex gaze patterns; discussion of feasibility and limitations.

Strengths
- Novel signal for SA: Using reader gaze as features for sentiment classification is an interesting and relatively unexplored angle in SA (despite prior work using gaze for related tasks). The paper provides evidence that cognitive signals can complement textual features, particularly for challenging pragmatic phenomena. This is a meaningful conceptual contribution.
- Empirical improvements on hard cases: The gains on the sarcastic/ironic subset (e.g., +6.1% relative F1 for complex texts) are stronger than on simpler texts, supporting the hypothesis that gaze can help with implicit sentiment and incongruity. This aligns with psycholinguistic expectations (e.g., regressions/saccade patterns).
- Feature ablation and significance tests: The paper presents ablations across (Sn), (Sn+Sr), and the addition of gaze features, with McNemar testing indicating significance even for small deltas. The chi-square feature ranking further shows a substantial share of top features are gaze-based, strengthening interpretability claims.
- Use of public datasets: Building on public eye-tracking resources improves reproducibility potential compared to custom, closed datasets. The two datasets also differ in hardware (500Hz vs 120Hz), adding some robustness to the claim that the effect is not device-specific.
- Clear articulation of psycholinguistic motivation: The mapping from fixation, regressions, and saccade length to linguistic difficulty is well-grounded in literature, lending plausibility to the feature design.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and external validity: The approach critically depends on eye-tracking at inference time. The feasibility section is speculative (mobile eye trackers, user consent), and there is no evaluation under realistic deployment constraints (e.g., single reader, low-quality/noisy home devices, or predicting gaze from text as a proxy). Without a practical path, the impact for mainstream SA is limited.
- Modest effect sizes overall and limited baselines: The improvements from adding gaze to already augmented textual features are small on average (+0.6 to +1.5 F1), with the strongest case coming from a held-out subset. Moreover, comparisons omit modern text-only baselines (e.g., strong neural models available contemporaneously, tuned feature-based SVMs with richer n-grams/character features). The justification for excluding deep baselines is weak given their relevance to SA; this undermines the strength of the empirical case.
- Potential train–test leakage and insufficient procedural detail: Several features are learned/constructed (e.g., IMPLICIT PCA patterns, PCA to 500 dimensions, lexical polarity via a supervised model). It is unclear whether these were fit strictly on training folds or using the full dataset, which could inflate cross-validation results. Details on preprocessing, parameter settings for the gaze-graph construction, and normalization across readers/devices are sparse.
- Confounds in data and task framing: Eye movements were collected during annotation, which may reflect task-specific search/verification strategies rather than naturalistic reading. The models may partially learn “annotation difficulty” rather than sentiment. Additionally, dataset 2 filters out objective instances, potentially biasing class separability. No cross-dataset generalization (train on D1, test on D2) is reported to assess robustness.
- Limited analysis of what gaze features add beyond sarcasm features: While feature rankings are presented, there is little qualitative or quantitative analysis isolating which specific gaze features drive gains, how they interact with sarcasm/thwarting features, or whether simpler gaze summaries would suffice. The benefits of the proposed graph features versus basic gaze features are not convincingly established.

Questions to authors
- At inference time, what is the intended source of gaze? Single reader? Aggregated multiple readers? How sensitive is performance to the number of readers and device quality? Please provide results with single-reader features and with downsampled/noisy gaze to model realistic deployment.
- How were learned features (PCA components, implicit incongruity patterns, lexical polarity model) fit within cross-validation? Were these recomputed on training folds only? Please clarify to rule out leakage.
- Can you report cross-dataset transfer (train on D1, test on D2 and vice versa) for text-only vs text+gaze to assess generalization and robustness to hardware differences?
- Do basic gaze features alone deliver most of the gains, or are the saliency-graph features necessary? Please include an ablation splitting basic vs graph-based gaze features.
- Could you include a stronger text-only baseline (e.g., a contemporary neural model or a tuned linear classifier with richer n-gram/char features), and compare the incremental benefit of gaze over that?

Overall assessment
The paper presents an interesting and novel application of gaze to sentiment analysis and provides suggestive empirical evidence that cognitive signals help with sarcasm/irony. However, the practical applicability is unclear, effect sizes are modest relative to text-only baselines, and important methodological details and stronger comparisons are missing. I lean toward rejection in its current form, but I encourage the authors to address leakage risks, add stronger baselines and transfer tests, and clarify a realistic deployment pathway (or explore learning to predict gaze from text) to strengthen the contribution.
