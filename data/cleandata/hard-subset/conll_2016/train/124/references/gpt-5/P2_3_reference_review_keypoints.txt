- Paper investigates whether eye-tracking–derived cognitive features improve sentence-level sentiment analysis, especially for sarcasm/irony, using two datasets (D1, D2) and extensive feature engineering.
- Main contribution 1: Empirical finding that gaze features give consistent, statistically significant gains over strong text-only features, with larger gains on sarcastic/ironic text.
- Main contribution 2: Novel gaze feature design, including graph-based saliency measures beyond standard fixation/saccade aggregates.
- Main contribution 3: Analysis with feature importance, subgroup (irony vs. non-irony) breakdowns, and a McNemar significance test.
- Strength: Well-motivated hypothesis linking gaze as a proxy for cognitive processing to help pragmatic phenomena where text-only features struggle.
- Strength: Consistent empirical gains across datasets and classifiers, and larger improvements on a sarcasm-heavy held-out subset.
- Strength: Creative graph-based gaze-saliency features complement basic gaze statistics.
- Strength: Authors acknowledge confounds and include reading-ease/length controls; use public datasets and interpretable features aiding reproducibility.
- Weakness: Practicality concern — requiring eye-tracking at inference time is unrealistic for most applications and feasibility arguments are speculative.
- Weakness: Missing comparisons to modern deep learning text-only baselines (CNN/RNN/transformers), undermining the claim of gaze’s incremental value.
- Weakness: Absolute F1 gains from gaze on full datasets are small (+0.6, +1.5), stronger only on sarcasm subsets; more detailed breakdowns/confidence intervals needed.
- Weakness: Methodological ambiguities — no reported per-reader normalization, unclear PCA CV handling, and incomplete saliency graph construction details.
- Weakness: Limited statistical testing reported (only one McNemar test on D1); broader tests and confidence intervals are needed.
- Weakness: Presentation issues — typos, table inconsistencies, and confusing entries hinder readability and verification.
- Question: How would the authors deploy sentiment prediction without reader gaze at test time — could knowledge distillation produce a text-only student model?
- Question: Was per-participant normalization (e.g., within-subject z-scoring) applied to gaze features before averaging, and does normalization change results?
- Question: Were PCA components for NGRAM_PCA/IMPLICIT_PCA fit only on training folds to avoid leakage?
- Question: Which gaze feature subsets contribute most — do gains persist after removing RED and LEN or controlling tightly for length?
- Question: Can the authors add competitive deep models (biLSTM/CNN/transformer fine-tuning) on the same splits to quantify gaze’s incremental benefit?
- Question: How robust are results to low-redundancy scenarios — e.g., using a single reader’s gaze at test time?
