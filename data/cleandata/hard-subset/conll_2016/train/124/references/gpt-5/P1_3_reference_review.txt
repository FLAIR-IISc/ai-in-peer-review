Summary and contributions
This paper augments standard text-based sentiment analysis (SA) with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking during annotation. Using two public datasets with gaze recordings, the authors engineer a suite of gaze features (basic fixation/saccade/regression statistics and novel saliency-graph features) and show modest gains in F1 over text-only systems, with larger gains on sarcasm/irony-heavy instances.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) Empirical finding: Human gaze features can modestly improve SA performance, and the gains are larger on linguistically complex (sarcastic/ironic) texts. Across two datasets, adding gaze to strong text features improves SVM F1 by +0.6 (72.9→73.5) and +1.5 (70.3→71.8) points; on a held-out subset of “complex” texts, the gain is +4.2 (60.1→64.3).
2) Feature design: A comprehensive, reusable set of gaze-derived features, including novel gaze-saliency graph features (weighted degrees with fixation/saccade-based weights) in addition to standard fixation/saccade/skip/regression aggregates.
3) Cross-disciplinary evaluation: A controlled study across two datasets collected with different eye-trackers and readers, plus feature-importance analysis showing multiple gaze features among the top predictors, suggesting cognitive signals capture nuances beyond text.

Strengths
- Addresses a hard slice of SA: The paper targets semantic/pragmatic phenomena (sarcasm, irony, thwarting) where purely textual features often struggle. The held-out analysis explicitly shows larger benefits on such instances: +6.1% relative F1 on “Irony” versus +2.1% on “Non-Irony.”
- Clear ablation story: Results are reported for Uni, Sn (sentiment features), Sn+Sr (sarcasm/thwarting), Gz, Sn+Gz, and Sn+Sr+Gz across three classifiers and two datasets. This makes the incremental value of gaze features visible and consistent (albeit modest) when added on top of strong textual features.
- Novel gaze features: The saliency graph construction and degree-based metrics tied to fixation/saccade properties are original within SA and plausibly connected to psycholinguistic theory (e.g., regressions and surprisal).
- Public data and statistical tests: Experiments use two public datasets with different tracking hardware. The authors conduct McNemar tests and chi-square feature-ranking; in D1, 10 of top-20 features are gaze-based, supporting the claim that gaze carries complementary signal.
- Attention to confounds: Inclusion of readability and length features helps control for generic reading-difficulty effects that could otherwise explain gaze differences.

Weaknesses
- Practicality at inference time: The method requires gaze data at test time. The paper’s feasibility discussion (future mobile eye-tracking) is speculative, and no realistic deployment scenario (e.g., 1-reader, in-the-wild noise, missing data) is evaluated. Current results average 5–7 readers per snippet; it is unclear whether comparable gains hold with a single reader or weaker devices.
- Modest overall gains and weak standalone gaze: On both datasets, adding gaze to a strong Sn+Sr baseline yields small absolute F1 improvements (+0.6, +1.5). Gaze-only models underperform substantially (e.g., SVM F1 53.4/48.9), indicating gaze is beneficial mainly as a minor complement. For many applications, such small gains may not justify the cost/complexity of collecting gaze.
- Missing comparisons to contemporary neural baselines: The study excludes competitive deep learning SA models (CNN/RNN/transformers) on the grounds that the approach is feature-based. Given the maturity of neural SA (including sarcasm-aware variants), the incremental value of gaze versus modern text-only baselines remains unclear.
- Potential methodological ambiguities and inconsistencies:
  - Table 2 has anomalies (e.g., SVM Sn+Gz on D2 equals 48.9, identical to Gz-only), suggesting possible reporting or implementation issues.
  - “Lexical polarity (LP)” is trained via supervised logistic regression using Joshi et al. (2015) data; potential data leakage or domain overlap is not discussed.
  - Device heterogeneity (Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120) and sampling-rate effects: how are gaze features normalized across devices/participants? Without normalization details, results may conflate device-specific artifacts.
  - Cross-validation splits are sentence-based; the averaging of per-reader features within sentences may mask inter-reader variance. It is unclear if folds control for reader effects.
- Limited qualitative/error analysis: The error analysis is high-level (hardware/tool limitations), with no concrete examples of where gaze helps or fails, and no per-phenomenon breakdown beyond the single held-out subset. This weakens interpretability and the causal link between specific gaze patterns and specific linguistic phenomena.

Questions to authors
- Inference scenario: What is the minimal gaze signal needed at test time to realize gains (one reader vs. averaged multiple, single pass vs. full reading)? Can you report performance with single-reader features and with simulated low-sampling-rate noise?
- Normalization: How are gaze measures normalized across readers/sessions/devices (e.g., z-scoring per reader, per-session baselines)? Do results change if normalization is varied?
- LP feature training: On what data is the “Lexical polarity (LP)” classifier trained? Is there any overlap with D1/D2 domains or instances? Can you ablate LP to isolate its effect?
- Robustness and significance: Are McNemar tests performed on per-instance predictions aggregated across CV folds? Can you report confidence intervals over repeated CV runs or different random seeds?
- Table 2 inconsistencies: Please verify the D2 SVM scores for Sn+Gz. If correct, why does adding Sn to Gz not improve over Gz-only for SVM on D2?
- Reader variance: How consistent are gaze-derived predictions across annotators? Do certain gaze features show higher inter-reader reliability? Any analysis by reading speed?
- Comparison to neural baselines: Can you include a modern transformer-based SA baseline (with and without sarcasm-focused augmentation) to contextualize the size of the gaze contribution?
- Deployment: If gaze is unavailable, could you learn a text-only student model via knowledge distillation from a gaze-augmented teacher? Any preliminary evidence?
