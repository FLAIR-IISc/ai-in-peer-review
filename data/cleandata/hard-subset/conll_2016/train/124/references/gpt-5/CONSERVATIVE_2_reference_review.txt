Summary and contributions
This paper augments standard text-based sentiment analysis with “cognitive features” derived from eye-movement recordings of human annotators. Using two small public datasets with gaze data (≈1k items each), the authors engineer basic and graph-based gaze features and combine them with common sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features. They report modest F-score gains in 10-fold CV, and somewhat larger relative gains on a held-out subset rich in sarcasm/irony.

As I see it, the contributions are:
1) Empirical exploration of gaze-based features for sentiment polarity classification, with evidence that they yield incremental gains over text-only features, especially for sarcasm/irony-heavy items.
2) A set of engineered gaze features (including “gaze-saliency graphs”) and a feature-importance analysis suggesting some gaze features are predictive.
3) A small-scale study contrasting high- vs low-quality eye-trackers across two datasets.

Strengths
- Addresses a genuinely hard aspect of sentiment analysis: implicit sentiment, irony, and thwarting. The held-out analysis showing larger relative gains for irony-rich texts (Table 4: +4.2 F absolute vs Sn+Sr, +6.1% relative) is the most compelling part of the paper.
- Methodological clarity on feature construction. The paper enumerates sentiment, sarcasm, and gaze features, and gives reasonable psycholinguistic motivations for several gaze measures (e.g., regressions, saccade lengths).
- Multi-dataset evaluation with different eye-tracker hardware, suggesting some robustness of the approach across acquisition conditions. The chi-squared ranking also indicates gaze features appear among the top predictors.
- Statistical testing is attempted (McNemar), and the authors analyze feature importance and error sources, which indicates an awareness of limitations.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and deployment: The approach assumes access to per-item gaze during inference. The proposed “mobile eye-tracker” scenario is speculative and does not address core issues (instrument availability, data quality, calibration, latency, privacy/consent, and per-item cost). The reported gains over strong text-only features are modest (e.g., +0.6 to +1.5 F over Sn+Sr in Table 2), which is hard to justify given the substantial overhead of collecting gaze. A thorough cost–benefit analysis is missing.
- Baselines and rigor: The paper does not compare against strong modern text-only baselines. Excluding contemporary neural models (e.g., CNN/RNN, and more importantly, pretrained transformers) limits the relevance of the claimed improvements. Even within feature-based methods, character n-grams, contextual sentiment shifters, and better negation/irony handling could strengthen baselines. The “existing SA systems” in Table 1 are trained on different corpora and thus are not a fair benchmark for these datasets.
- Dataset size and risk of overfitting: Both datasets are small (≈1k items), and the gaze feature space is nontrivial. Many Weka defaults are used without principled tuning or nested CV. The use of PCA on unigrams mitigates sparsity, but the overall setup invites overfitting, particularly for the engineered gaze-graph features. Ablations isolating basic vs graph-based gaze features are not presented.
- Confounds and generalizability: Gaze was collected while annotators performed sentiment labeling. Cognitive signals may reflect task difficulty, disagreement, or annotator strategy rather than sentiment per se. No analysis controls for inter-annotator agreement, item difficulty, or label prevalence. It is unclear whether the model would generalize to gaze collected during natural reading by non-annotators, or to a single reader’s gaze at test time (the features are averaged over 5–7 annotators).
- Statistical analysis clarity: The significance test is described for a small difference (0.5% F on D1) without detailing the paired comparison protocol (e.g., per-instance contingency for McNemar across folds). Given the CV setup and small deltas, a more careful paired-bootstrap or approximate randomization test on per-item predictions would be preferable.

Questions to authors
- Test-time assumptions: What exact test-time scenario do you assume? Single-reader gaze for a new item, or multiple readers? Please report performance as a function of the number of readers (1…7) to quantify practicality. Also, how does downsampling/noising the gaze (to emulate mobile devices) affect results?
- Strong text-only baselines: Can you provide comparisons to stronger baselines, e.g., character n-grams with linear SVM, and a pretrained transformer fine-tuned on these datasets (with standard tricks for small data)? Without this, it’s hard to assess the added value of gaze.
- Ablations within gaze features: What is the incremental gain of basic gaze features vs graph-based features? Which specific gaze features drive improvements on irony items? A per-group ablation and per-item error reduction analysis would help.
- Confounds: How do gains vary with item difficulty (e.g., stratifying by Fleiss’ kappa or annotator disagreement)? Are improvements still present on high-agreement items? This would separate “difficulty” effects from genuine sentiment signal.
- Statistical testing: Please clarify how you conducted McNemar tests across CV folds and provide paired tests on per-item predictions for the key comparisons (Sn+Sr vs Sn+Sr+Gz).
- Ethics and privacy: If deployment requires capturing users’ gaze while reading reviews, how will consent, privacy, and data protection be handled? Is any personally identifiable biometric information stored?

Overall, this is an interesting exploratory study connecting psycholinguistic signals to sentiment analysis, but the practicality is weak and the empirical validation lacks strong baselines and ablations. I would require substantial additional evaluation and clearer test-time assumptions before recommending acceptance.
