- Proposes augmenting text-based sentiment analysis with gaze-derived cognitive features (fixations, saccades, regressions, skips) and a novel gaze-saliency graph; evaluated on two public eye-tracking datasets.
- Primary contribution: empirical study showing modest overall F1 gains from gaze features, with larger gains on sarcasm/irony (absolute F1 +4.2, relative +6.1% on a subset); consistent across datasets and classifiers.
- Secondary contribution: a publicly specified gaze feature set including graph-derived degree statistics linking psycholinguistic phenomena to predictive signals.
- Strong psycholinguistic motivation: maps reading behaviors (regressions, fixations) to linguistic difficulty with supporting citations.
- Novel feature design: gaze-saliency graph and degree-based summaries go beyond standard per-word/per-sentence gaze aggregates.
- Empirical signal on challenging phenomena: largest gains on sarcasm/irony subset and many gaze features rank highly in chi-squared analysis.
- Sensible ablation study: staged ablations show sarcasm/thwarting features boost text baseline and gaze adds further improvement.
- Appropriate statistical testing: use of McNemar tests for small SVM improvements strengthens claims.
- Practicality concerns: requires gaze at test time from multiple readers; feasibility discussion is speculative and lacks quantification of readers needed or single-reader performance.
- Missing strong text-only baselines: no comparisons to contemporary neural models (CNN/RNN/transformers), so the claimed SOTA is unsubstantiated.
- Potential confounds in gaze data: different eye-trackers (500Hz vs 120Hz) and protocols without described normalization or per-device analyses could affect robustness.
- Aggregation issues: averaging across readers may wash out individual signals or instead capture label difficulty rather than sentiment-specific cues.
- Methodological underspecification: unclear bootstrapping sources for implicit incongruity phrases, PCA choices (500 components), SVM/NN hyperparameters, preprocessing, majority-vote labeling, and fold/held-out construction.
- Hard-to-isolate gaze contribution: “Gz” mixes readability and gaze features, making it difficult to attribute gains specifically to gaze.
- Limited analysis of gaze impact: no per-feature ablations, no qualitative error analysis showing where gaze corrected text-only mistakes.
- Question: how many readers per instance are used at test time and how does performance scale with reader count or single-reader input?
- Question: how were gaze features normalized across devices, screen setups, and participants; were resampling or standardizations applied; can results be reported per device?
- Question: can you ablate readability-only vs gaze-only vs combined to isolate gaze’s unique contribution?
- Question: can you compare to strong neural text-only baselines (CNN/RNN/transformer) and recent sarcasm detectors, then add gaze on top?
- Question: from which corpora were implicit incongruity patterns bootstrapped, and could there be data leakage from evaluation sets?
- Question: how was the 294-instance held-out subset constructed and are annotators disjoint between training and held-out to avoid reader-specific leakage?
- Question: which specific gaze features drive gains on the sarcasm subset—can you provide per-feature ablations or SHAP-style importances?
- Question: have you tried multi-view or distillation approaches that use gaze during training but not at inference to improve practicality?
- Question: how will you address ethics, privacy, consent, and potential demographic biases in gaze behavior for real-world deployment?
