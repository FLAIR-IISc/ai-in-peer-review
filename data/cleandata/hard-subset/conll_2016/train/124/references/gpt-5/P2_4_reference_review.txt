Summary
This paper investigates whether cognitive signals from eye-tracking can improve sentiment analysis (SA), especially for linguistically complex phenomena like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations. The authors engineer a large suite of features: standard sentiment features (Sn), sarcasm/thwarting features (Sr), and gaze-derived cognitive features (Gz) including statistics over fixations/saccades and graph-based “gaze-saliency” measures, plus readability controls. On two public eye-tracking datasets (994 and 843 instances after filtering), they run 10-fold CV with NB/SVM/MLP and report that adding Sr to Sn improves F1 substantially, and adding Gz to Sn+Sr yields additional gains (0.6–1.5 F1). A held-out subset enriched for irony shows larger relative gains (≈6% absolute).

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that gaze-derived features provide incremental performance gains for SA and are particularly helpful on cases involving irony/sarcasm. This is the primary contribution.
2) A concrete set of gaze features for SA, including graph-based saliency measures, and an analysis indicating several gaze features rank highly via chi-squared importance.
3) An evaluation on two public eye-tracking datasets with different hardware settings, suggesting robustness across recording conditions.

Strengths
- Targeting hard SA phenomena: The paper focuses on well-known failure modes (sarcasm/irony), documents that off-the-shelf SA systems perform poorly on these datasets, and shows that Sr features help substantially, with Gz adding further gains. The held-out analysis (ironical vs non-ironical) strengthens the claim that gaze features are most useful where semantics/pragmatics are challenging.
- Psycholinguistically grounded features: The chosen gaze features are motivated by established reading research (e.g., fixations, regressions, saccade length) and are complemented by a novel graph representation. The feature-importance analysis (top-20 features include many gaze features in both datasets) aligns with the intuition that gaze captures relevant cognitive load/surprisal.
- Cross-dataset consistency: Results are reported on two datasets collected with different eye-trackers (500Hz vs. 120Hz), and the qualitative pattern—Sn < Sn+Sr < Sn+Sr+Gz—holds across classifiers. This supports some robustness of the approach to different sensors.
- Public data and reasonable baselines: The use of public datasets is a plus for reproducibility. Comparing multiple classifiers (NB/SVM/MLP) provides a modest sanity check beyond a single model.

Weaknesses
- Practicality at inference time: The method requires gaze at test time. The feasibility section is speculative (mobile eye trackers) and does not address near-term deployment. Crucially, the paper does not explore “train with gaze, test without gaze” paradigms (e.g., learning using privileged information, teacher–student distillation, gaze-supervised attention). Without a realistic inference scenario, the impact on mainstream SA is limited.
- Baseline strength and comparability: Modern SA systems routinely use neural models (CNN/RNN/transformers). The paper defers these, yet claims state-of-the-art improvement. The only neural baseline is a small MLP over engineered features. Moreover, the comparison to off-the-shelf systems in Table 1 is not directly comparable to the main 10-fold CV setting (trained on different corpora vs. in-domain CV). The incremental gain of Gz over Sn+Sr (0.6–1.5 F1) is modest; without strong text-only baselines under the same CV, the added value of gaze is unclear.
- Experimental rigor and potential leakage: It is unclear whether PCA and any feature selection/normalization were fitted within each CV fold. Fitting on the full dataset would leak test information. Similarly, the “LP” feature is learned via supervised logistic regression from an external dataset—details on training and whether any overlap exists with D1/D2 are lacking. No leave-one-reader-out or per-subject controls are reported; averaging gaze over annotators may mask subject effects. No cross-dataset generalization (train on D1, test on D2) is presented.
- Ablation clarity: Gz appears to bundle gaze and readability features; this conflates cognitive signals with simple text length/difficulty. A clean ablation separating gaze-only vs readability-only is needed. Also, several feature definitions are unclear or inconsistent (e.g., ED formula notation, PSDH/PSDS descriptions), making exact reproduction harder.
- Statistical reporting: Only one McNemar test is reported (for D1) despite small absolute gains. Confidence intervals or additional significance tests (D2, held-out subset) would strengthen claims. No analysis of calibration or error types beyond a brief qualitative note.

Questions to Authors
- Test-time assumptions: Do you require gaze at inference? If not, can you present a “privileged information” setup where models are trained with gaze but deployed without it, and quantify the retained gains?
- Data processing within CV: Was PCA (and any scaling/feature selection) fit only on training folds? Please detail the pipeline to exclude leakage.
- Gaze aggregation and subject effects: How are multiple readers’ gaze features aggregated? Have you tried leave-one-reader-out or per-reader normalization to ensure robustness across readers?
- Ablations: Can you separate “gaze-only” from “readability-only” features within Gz, and report their individual contributions? Also control for sentence length explicitly when assessing gaze gains.
- Stronger baselines: Can you include competitive text-only baselines (e.g., CNN/BiLSTM, or a small transformer fine-tuned on these datasets) and then add Sr and Gz to those to test whether gaze still adds value?
- Statistical significance: Please report statistical tests (and ideally confidence intervals) for D2 and the held-out subset, and correct for multiple comparisons if applicable.
- LP feature training: Provide details on training data/splits for the logistic regression used to produce LP, and confirm there's no overlap/contamination with D1/D2.

Overall evaluation
The idea of leveraging gaze for SA, particularly on sarcastic/ironic text, is interesting and supported by some evidence. However, the practical relevance (gaze at inference), the modest incremental gains over strong text-only features, and gaps in experimental rigor/baselines limit the impact in its current form. Strengthening baselines, clarifying the evaluation pipeline, and demonstrating a deployable setting (e.g., LUPI) would substantially improve the paper’s significance.
