Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes augmenting standard text-based sentiment analysis (SA) features with “cognitive features” derived from eye-movement behavior (fixations, saccades, regressions) recorded while human annotators read and label short texts. It introduces both simple gaze statistics and graph-based “gaze-saliency” features.
- Experiments on two publicly available eye-tracking datasets (∼1k instances each, with irony/sarcasm present) show that adding gaze features to sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features yields modest absolute F-score gains (≈0.6–1.5 points over text + sarcasm features; ≈3–9 points over basic text features depending on dataset), with larger relative gains for an ironic subset. Feature importance analysis highlights several gaze features among the top-ranked.
- The paper argues for feasibility via emerging mobile eye-tracking and discusses limitations.

My view of the primary contributions, in order:
1) Empirical evidence that aggregated eye-tracking features can improve SA performance on challenging, irony/sarcasm-heavy short texts, beyond engineered text features. This is novel within SA and supported across two datasets and multiple classifiers.
2) A concrete, reusable inventory of gaze-derived features (including a saliency-graph construction) and an ablation demonstrating their marginal utility and significance.
3) An analysis showing larger benefits on a held-out irony subset, supporting the hypothesis that gaze captures higher-level processing difficulty useful for nuanced sentiment.

Strengths
- Novel use of gaze for SA with careful feature design: Prior gaze work in NLP has focused on WSD, annotation difficulty, and sarcasm understandability. Using gaze as predictive signal for SA outputs is original and methodologically interesting. The feature set thoughtfully spans basic fixation/saccade stats and structured graph properties, motivated by psycholinguistic literature (e.g., regressions and surprisal).
- Empirical validation on two distinct datasets and hardware: Results replicate across a high-end (Eyelink-1000) and a lower-end (Tobii T120) tracker, strengthening the claim that gaze signals are robust to acquisition quality. The gains are consistent across NB/SVM/MLP, with SVM strongest.
- Focus on hard phenomena (irony/sarcasm): The paper goes beyond headline accuracy and targets texts that are known to break lexical cues. The held-out irony subset shows a material absolute F-score improvement (Sn+Sr: 60.1 to Sn+Sr+Gz: 64.3), aligning with the stated hypothesis.
- Statistical analyses and feature significance: The McNemar test supports that small average gains are unlikely to be due to chance on D1, and chi-squared rankings surface multiple gaze features among the top predictors, lending interpretability.
- Public data and reproducibility prospects: Using public datasets and well-known toolkits (Weka/LibSVM, NLTK, MPQA, SentiWordNet) simplifies reproduction given access to gaze traces.

Weaknesses
- Practicality at inference time is unresolved: The approach requires eye-tracking at test time. The feasibility section is speculative; consumer-grade gaze collection is not yet pervasive, and privacy/consent constraints are nontrivial. Without an alternative (e.g., predicting gaze features from text), deployment is unclear. This significantly limits real-world impact for general SA.
- Weakness of baselines and comparisons: The work does not compare against stronger modern text-only baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN/Tree-LSTM or contemporary transformer-based fine-tuning), even if only to establish an upper bound on text-only performance. The “existing SA systems” trained on different corpora and evaluated as-is on D1/D2 are not a fair state-of-the-art comparison. As a result, it is unclear whether gaze adds value beyond what strong text models can already capture.
- Modest absolute gains and unclear effect sizes: Over the strongest text-only configuration (Sn+Sr), gaze adds only +0.6 F1 on D1 and +1.5 on D2 with SVM. While statistically significant on D1, the practical significance is borderline, especially weighed against the cost/complexity of eye-tracking. The irony subset shows larger gains, but that analysis is limited to a held-out split derived from D1, not an external dataset.
- Potential confounds and task-induced gaze: Gaze was recorded while annotators performed sentiment labeling, which may induce task-specific attention patterns and difficulty signals that correlate with subjective uncertainty rather than inherent linguistic properties. The paper aggregates across annotators, which may wash out individual patterns but could also encode annotator disagreement. No analysis disentangles these factors or examines inter-annotator variance effects.
- Experimental clarity and completeness: Some feature definitions (saliency graph weighted degrees F1H/F2H/PSH etc.) are difficult to parse and partly redundant/confusing. Important details are missing: how gold labels are derived (majority vote?), exact hyperparameters for the MLP, feature dimensionalities after PCA, and whether any normalization is applied. No cross-dataset generalization (train on D1, test on D2 and vice versa), which would strengthen robustness claims. The “Uni” versus “Sn” feature sets are slightly confusing given NGRAM_PCA is part of “Sentiment features.”

Questions to authors
- Inference scenario: Do you envision gaze features being available at test time? If not, can you train a student model to predict (or distill) gaze-informed signals from text alone, or learn a text-to-gaze predictor to approximate your features?
- Label derivation and confounds: How are gold labels obtained (majority vote, adjudication)? Do you observe a correlation between gaze features and annotator disagreement/entropy? Could gains be driven by difficulty of labeling rather than sentiment per se?
- Cross-dataset transfer: What happens if you train on D1 and test on D2 (and vice versa), with and without gaze? This would probe robustness to domain and device differences.
- Stronger baselines: Can you compare against a competitive text-only baseline (e.g., a BiLSTM/CNN with pretrained embeddings or a transformer fine-tuned on your data) and report whether gaze still adds gains?
- Feature ablation: Which individual gaze features contribute most when added to Sn+Sr? Are gains concentrated in regression-based features (e.g., RSF/LREG) tied to surprisal?
- Ethics and privacy: How do you envisage handling consent, storage, and privacy of gaze data in real applications?

Overall evaluation: The work is original and well-motivated, with careful experiments on suitable datasets and interesting analyses. However, limited practical applicability, modest gains over text features, and lack of strong baselines weaken the case. I lean toward reject unless the authors can (i) demonstrate improvements over modern text-only models and/or (ii) present a viable path to approximate gaze at inference.
