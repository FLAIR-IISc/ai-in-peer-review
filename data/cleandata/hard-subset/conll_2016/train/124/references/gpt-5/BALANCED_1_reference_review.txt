Summary and contributions
This paper augments standard sentiment analysis (SA) features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking while annotators read short texts. The authors engineer a set of basic and graph-based gaze features and combine them with sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting-oriented textual features. They evaluate on two public gaze–sentiment datasets (∼1k instances each) and report consistent F-score gains, with larger improvements on texts containing irony/sarcasm. The paper also offers a feasibility discussion of eye-tracking at inference time.

As I see them, the main contributions (strongest first) are:
1) Empirical finding: Cognitive (gaze) features improve SA performance, especially on complex constructs (irony/sarcasm), across two datasets and multiple classifiers. A held-out subset analysis shows larger relative gains for ironic texts.
2) Feature design: Introduction of gaze-saliency graph features and a curated set of basic gaze statistics for SA; plus a combined feature suite that includes sarcasm/thwarting indicators.
3) Analysis: Feature importance via chi-squared ranking and limited significance testing (McNemar), suggesting gaze features are among top predictors.

Strengths
- Clear empirical utility of gaze features on hard cases. Across both datasets, adding gaze to sentiment+sarcasm features yields additional F1 gains (0.6–1.6 points), and on a held-out subset the relative improvement is larger for irony (6.1% vs 2.1% for non-irony). This supports the hypothesis that cognitive signals capture nuances poorly handled by surface text features.
- Novel gaze-saliency graph features. Modeling fixations/saccades as a graph and deriving degree-based statistics is an interesting and plausible way to encode surprisal and re-reading patterns beyond coarse counts. The feature-importance analysis indicates several gaze features rank highly.
- Evaluation on two datasets with different eye-tracking hardware. The method shows gains on both high- and low-sampling-rate devices, suggesting some robustness to sensor quality. The datasets target challenging phenomena (implicit sentiment, irony), which are appropriate to test the proposed idea.
- Reasonable baselining and ablations within their feature-based paradigm. The comparison among Sn, Sn+Sr, and Sn+Sr+Gz isolates the incremental contribution of sarcasm-related features and then gaze features. The authors also verify that these datasets are non-trivial for off-the-shelf analyzers trained elsewhere.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and generalization across readers are insufficiently addressed. The approach requires gaze at inference. While the paper discusses feasibility of mobile eye-tracking, there is no empirical evaluation of subject-independence (e.g., train on some readers’ gaze, test on unseen readers) or of performance with a single reader’s gaze per instance. Features are averaged over multiple annotators per instance, which is unrealistic at test time and may mask variability. This is the most serious limitation.
- Missing comparisons to strong modern text-only baselines. The paper explicitly sets aside deep learning, but current SA state of the art uses pretrained encoders (e.g., CNN/BiLSTM with pretrained embeddings, or transformer-based fine-tuning). The reported gains over Sn+Sr (≤1.6 F1) might vanish against a modern baseline. Without these comparisons, the practical value of adding gaze remains unclear.
- Methodological details and potential confounds. Important details are missing or unclear: how labels are aggregated; whether PCA and LP/IMPLICIT models were fit strictly within folds (to prevent leakage); how device differences were normalized; per-fold variance; and detailed hyperparameter selection. Sarcasm features and the LP feature are trained on external data—possible overlap or domain leakage is not ruled out. Moreover, gaze features might correlate with annotation difficulty rather than sentiment per se.
- Small datasets and limited statistical testing. With ∼1k instances per dataset and a large feature set, overfitting is a concern. Only one McNemar test is reported (best vs second-best on D1); no confidence intervals or variance across folds are shown; and significance vs Sn+Sr on D2 is not reported. The robustness of small absolute gains is hard to assess.
- Reproducibility and clarity. Several components depend on in-house resources (discourse connectors, gaze-graph construction details), and preprocessing/parameterization (AOI mapping, saccade thresholds, fixation definitions) are not fully specified. No code release is indicated.

Questions to authors
- Labels and aggregation: How are gold labels derived from multiple annotators (majority vote? thresholds?) for D1 and D2? Are items with low agreement treated differently?
- Reader generalization: What is performance when using a single reader’s gaze per instance? Have you tried leave-one-reader-out (train on some readers, test on unseen readers) or leave-device-out setups?
- Data leakage: Were PCA, the LP classifier, and the IMPLICIT (Riloff-style) patterns fit exclusively on the training folds in each CV split? Please detail the pipeline to ensure no test information leaks into features.
- Statistical robustness: Can you report per-fold means/standard deviations and significance of Sn+Sr+Gz vs Sn+Sr on both datasets? Also, how sensitive are results to the choice of PCA dimensionality?
- Hardware normalization: How do you normalize gaze features across devices (500Hz vs 120Hz) and participants (reading speed)? Any calibration or z-scoring per reader before aggregation?
- Baselines: Can you compare to at least one modern text-only baseline (e.g., CNN/BiLSTM with pretrained embeddings; or fine-tuned transformer on these datasets)? Even if not the main focus, this is important for context.
- Deployment: Who provides gaze at inference time in your envisioned applications? Have you quantified the marginal benefit vs cost when only one reader’s gaze is available?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Include a leave-one-reader-out experiment and a single-reader setting; report how many readers are needed to realize gains.
- Evaluate cross-domain generalization (train on D1, test on D2 and vice versa) with and without gaze to assess robustness.
- Provide ablations within gaze: basic vs graph features; which specific gaze features drive gains on irony?
- Clarify and, if possible, release the feature extraction code (gaze graph construction, AOI mapping, thresholds) and lists (discourse connectors), and document the CV pipeline to ensure reproducibility.
- Add at least one modern neural text baseline. If gaze still helps, that strengthens the claim substantially.
- Discuss ethical considerations around gaze collection, consent, storage, and potential demographic biases affecting gaze patterns.

Overall assessment
Interesting and timely idea with promising results on challenging cases, but important methodological and practical gaps remain. I lean toward rejection in its current form; addressing the reader-generalization, stronger baselines, and reproducibility concerns would significantly strengthen the submission.
