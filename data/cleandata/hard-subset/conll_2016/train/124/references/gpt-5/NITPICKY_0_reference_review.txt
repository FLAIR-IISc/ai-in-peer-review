Summary and claimed contributions
This paper augments text-based sentiment analysis with “cognitive features” derived from eye-movement data (fixations, saccades, regressions), arguing that gaze correlates with cognitive processing and can help with nuanced phenomena (sarcasm, irony, thwarting). Using two existing eye-tracking datasets (D1, D2), the authors engineer a large feature set: standard sentiment features (Sn), sarcasm/thwarting features (Sr), and gaze features (Gz), and evaluate with NB/SVM/MLP under 10-fold CV. They report that (i) adding Sr to Sn improves F1 substantially, and (ii) adding Gz on top of Sn+Sr yields small but statistically significant gains. A held-out subset analysis suggests larger gains from Gz on sarcastic texts.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A feature-based exploration of incorporating eye-tracking-derived features into supervised sentiment classification, including basic and graph-derived gaze features.
2) An empirical analysis showing small but consistent F1 gains from gaze features (0.6–1.5 absolute) on two datasets, with larger gains on sarcastic/ironic instances.
3) An extensive (though not always clearly specified) feature engineering and ablation study combining sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, and gaze features.

Strengths
- Novel signal for SA: Using gaze as an auxiliary signal for sentiment classification—especially for sarcasm/irony—is an interesting and underexplored angle in NLP sentiment work. The paper draws on psycholinguistic findings and operationalizes them in measurable features (fixation durations, regressions, saliency graphs).
- Empirical breadth and ablations: The study covers two datasets collected with different eye-trackers and contains ablations (Uni vs Sn vs Sn+Sr vs adding Gz). The gains from Sr are clear, and the incremental gains from Gz are consistent across datasets and classifiers.
- Focus on difficult phenomena: The held-out analysis by complexity (irony vs non-irony) aligns with the hypothesis that gaze helps more where textual cues are subtle; the reported gains on sarcastic items are larger than on non-ironic items.
- Attempt at statistical testing and feature analysis: The authors perform McNemar tests and chi-squared feature ranking (though with caveats below), and discuss the relative importance of gaze features, suggesting they are informative.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and external validity: The approach requires eye-tracking at inference time. The paper does not demonstrate any text-only predictor that can benefit from gaze at training time (e.g., distillation/multi-view learning) to be deployable without gaze. The feasibility section is speculative (mobile eye-tracking, user consent) and does not address real-world constraints (latency, privacy, calibration, battery, noise). As presented, the method is unlikely to be usable in standard SA pipelines.
- Methodological and statistical concerns:
  - The reported improvement attributable to gaze is small (SVM: +0.6 F1 on D1; +1.5 on D2) compared to the much larger gains from Sr. The paper’s headline improvement (“3.6% and 9.3%”) conflates Sr and Gz; the unique contribution of Gz is marginal.
  - Misuse of McNemar test: McNemar assesses paired differences in errors (typically accuracy), not “differences in F-scores.” Reporting p=0.0001 for a 0.5 F1 difference without discordant-pair counts is questionable.
  - No subject-disjoint evaluation: Gaze features are averaged over annotators, but it is unclear whether CV folds are disjoint by subject. Without subject-independent splits, models may overfit annotator-specific gaze idiosyncrasies, inflating performance.
  - Device heterogeneity: D1 uses EyeLink-1000 (500Hz), D2 uses Tobii T120 (120Hz). There is no description of normalization/calibration or per-subject z-scoring. Cross-dataset comparability of gaze features is unclear; this may explain weak performance of Gz-only baselines and questions robustness.
- Potential confounds and leakage risks:
  - Labels vs gaze: The same annotators provide labels and gaze. Gaze may correlate with annotation difficulty rather than sentiment per se. The paper does not analyze whether gaze still helps when controlling for LEN/RED (beyond including those features) via matched analyses or partial correlations.
  - The “Lexical polarity (LP)” feature is produced by a supervised model trained on external data; overlap with D1/D2 or domain leakage is not addressed. Details of the bootstrapped patterns (Riloff) and “in-house” resources are insufficient for reproducibility.
- Reporting and completeness issues:
  - Table 3 (feature ranking) is referenced but not included. Figures are referenced but not shown. Several equations and symbols are malformed (e.g., edge density denominator).
  - Critical implementation details are missing: preprocessing/normalization of gaze across devices/subjects, handling of missing fixations, tokenization and “normalized tweets,” hyperparameters beyond “Weka defaults,” class weighting, F1 definition (macro/micro), and variance across folds.
  - The definition of some gaze-graph features mixes units (characters vs words for amplitudes/positions), and source vs destination features appear duplicative, raising collinearity concerns; no feature selection beyond PCA for NGRAM/IMPLICIT is described.
- Weak baselines by current standards: Excluding modern text-only baselines (e.g., fastText, strong SVMs with character n-grams, pre-trained transformers fine-tuned on small data, or domain-adapted sarcasm detectors) limits the conclusions. Even a simple contextual embedding with linear classifier might outperform Sn+Sr, potentially narrowing or eliminating the marginal benefit of gaze.

Questions to authors
1) Inference-time requirement: Is gaze required at test time? If not, can you demonstrate a model that leverages gaze during training (e.g., teacher-student, multi-view) but predicts from text only at inference?
2) Subject/device normalization: Were CV folds disjoint by annotator? How were gaze features normalized across subjects and across devices with different sampling rates? Please report per-subject z-scoring or scaling procedures and results with subject-disjoint splits.
3) Statistical testing: For the McNemar tests, please report the contingency tables (discordant counts) and clarify which metric was tested (accuracy vs F1). If F1 was compared, please use appropriate paired tests (e.g., bootstrap on F1) and provide CIs.
4) Reproducibility: Provide the missing Table 3 and release “in-house” resources (discourse connectors, LP model, bootstrapped patterns) or detail them sufficiently. Clarify tokenization, POS/NER tools/versions, handling of missing gaze data, and all hyperparameters.
5) Leakage risks: Describe the training data for the LP feature and verify there is no overlap with D1/D2. Similarly, confirm that Riloff-style patterns were not induced from test data.
6) Held-out split: How was the held-out subset constructed? Is it disjoint in text and subjects from the CV folds? How was “irony” identified and by whom?

Overall, while the idea of leveraging gaze is interesting, the small incremental gains, practical limitations, methodological ambiguities, and reporting issues prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form.
