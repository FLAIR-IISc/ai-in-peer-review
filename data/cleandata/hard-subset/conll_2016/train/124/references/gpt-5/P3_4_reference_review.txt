Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes augmenting text-based sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking while humans read and label short texts. The authors engineer both basic gaze features (fixations, saccades, regressions, skips) and a novel “gaze-saliency graph” representation with degree-based statistics. They evaluate combinations of sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting features, and gaze features on two public eye-tracking datasets.
- Primary contribution: an empirical study showing that adding gaze-derived features yields modest overall F1 gains over text-only systems, with larger gains on texts containing sarcasm/irony. The effect is consistent across two datasets and multiple classifiers, and supported by significance tests and feature-importance analysis.
- Secondary contribution: a publicly specified feature set for modeling gaze in SA, including graph-derived features that connect psycholinguistic phenomena (surprisal, reanalysis) to predictive signals.

Strengths
- Clear motivation grounded in psycholinguistics. The mapping from reading behavior to linguistic difficulty (e.g., regressions and surprisal; fixations and lexical complexity) is well-motivated and supported by citations. This provides a principled rationale for expecting cognitive features to help on implicit sentiment, sarcasm, and thwarting.
- Novel feature design that goes beyond standard gaze aggregates. The gaze-saliency graph and degree-based summaries are interesting and plausibly capture distributed incongruities typical of sarcasm. This is a step beyond commonly used per-word/per-sentence gaze aggregates.
- Empirical signal on challenging phenomena. Gains are largest on the sarcasm/irony subset (reported absolute F1 +4.2 on a held-out subset; relative +6.1%), aligning with the paper’s thesis. The chi-squared analysis indicates many gaze features rank among the top predictors, suggesting the modality adds non-redundant information.
- Sensible ablations. The staged ablations (Sn → Sn+Sr → Sn+Sr+Gz) show that (i) sarcasm/thwarting features substantially strengthen the text baseline and (ii) gaze features provide further improvements. The authors also check the difficulty of the datasets by testing off-the-shelf SA systems trained on external corpora.
- Statistical testing. The use of McNemar tests to assess the small SVM improvements is appropriate and strengthens the empirical claims.

Weaknesses
- Practicality at inference time. The approach requires gaze at test time, obtained from multiple readers per sentence. The feasibility section is speculative (mobile webcams, user consent) and does not quantify the number of readers needed or performance with single-reader gaze. Without a demonstration of (i) robust single-reader performance, (ii) few-shot gaze, or (iii) a method to distill gaze into a gaze-free model at inference (e.g., multi-view learning/teacher-student), real-world applicability is limited.
- Missing comparisons to stronger text-only baselines. The paper excludes contemporary neural baselines that are standard for SA and sarcasm (CNN/RNN; and more recent transformer-based encoders). The “state-of-the-art” claim is unsubstantiated without comparison to such models on the same splits. Since the gaze gains over Sn+Sr are modest (+0.6 to +1.5 F1), it is unclear whether a modern text model would close or exceed the gap without gaze.
- Potential confounds and insufficient normalization/details for gaze. The two datasets use different eye-trackers (500Hz vs 120Hz) and different annotation protocols, but the paper does not describe normalization across devices, calibration differences, or reading settings. Aggregating features across readers may wash out informative individual variation; conversely, it may encode label difficulty rather than sentiment signal per se. Without per-device normalization and per-reader analyses, the robustness of the reported gains is uncertain.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility. Key details are underspecified: how implicit incongruity phrases are bootstrapped and from which corpora; how unigrams and sarcasm phrase features are PCA-reduced and why to 500 components; SVM/NN hyperparameters and tuning; pre-processing steps; exact majority-vote labels; and whether folds control for potential leakage across annotators. The held-out subset selection process is not fully specified. The combined definition of “Gz” includes readability features, making it hard to isolate the contribution of gaze alone.
- Limited analysis of what gaze buys. Beyond the chi-squared ranking, there is no per-feature ablation to identify which gaze features drive gains, nor error typology showing clear qualitative cases where gaze corrected text-only errors. Given the modest average improvements, deeper analysis is needed to justify the added complexity and data requirements.

Questions to authors
- Inference requirements: How many readers per instance are used to compute gaze features at test time? What is the performance with a single reader, and how does F1 scale with the number of readers?
- Device normalization: How are gaze features normalized across devices (500Hz vs 120Hz), screen setups, and participants? Did you re-sample or standardize fixations/saccade measures? Can you report results per device to assess sensitivity?
- Gaze vs readability: Since “Gz” includes readability features, can you report an ablation of readability-only vs gaze-only vs combined to isolate the unique contribution of gaze?
- Strong baselines: Can you compare against a competitive neural text-only baseline (e.g., a strong CNN/RNN or transformer fine-tuned on the same data) and a recent sarcasm detector, and then add gaze on top?
- Data leakage and bootstrapping: From which corpora were the implicit incongruity patterns bootstrapped? Were these patterns learned exclusively from external data, or could there be leakage from the evaluation sets?
- Held-out subset: How was the 294-instance held-out set constructed (random disjoint split from Dataset 1)? Are annotators for the held-out set disjoint from those in training folds to avoid reader-specific leakage?
- Per-feature impact: Which gaze features contribute most to the gains in the sarcasm subset? Can you provide per-feature ablations or SHAP-style importance?
- Gaze-free deployment: Have you tried multi-view or distillation approaches that use gaze during training but not at inference? Even preliminary results would greatly strengthen practicality claims.
- Ethics and privacy: How do you plan to address user privacy, consent, and potential demographic biases in gaze behavior if deploying at scale?
