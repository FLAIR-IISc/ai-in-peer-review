Summary and contributions
This paper augments sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye movements while annotating sentiment. It defines both basic gaze statistics and a novel gaze-saliency graph, and shows that adding these features to conventional sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features yields improvements on two gaze-annotated datasets, with larger gains on sarcasm/irony-heavy text.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Introducing cognitive (eye-tracking) features for sentiment analysis, including a new graph-based representation of gaze (gaze-saliency graphs) with degree-based features.
2) Empirical evidence, across two datasets and multiple classifiers, that gaze features significantly help, especially for texts with higher-level phenomena (sarcasm, irony, thwarting), along with feature-importance analyses and a held-out evaluation focusing on complex constructs.
3) A concrete feature engineering blueprint that bridges psycholinguistic insights (fixations, saccades, regressions) with NLP, potentially useful beyond SA.

Strengths
- Bold, novel direction: Using eye-tracking as a cognitive signal for SA is original and timely. The gaze-saliency graph is a creative representation that may inspire follow-up work in cognitive-NLP. This paper clearly pushes the problem framing rather than incrementally tuning standard pipelines.
- Gains on hard phenomena: The strongest empirical result is the larger improvement on sarcasm/irony cases (6.1% F-score gain on the held-out subset when adding gaze), aligning with the stated motivation that cognitive signals help where textual cues are subtle. This is precisely where conventional SA struggles.
- Multiple datasets and consistency: Results are shown on two public datasets collected with different trackers (500Hz Eyelink vs 120Hz Tobii), multiple classifiers, and several feature combinations. Improvements from Sn to Sn+Sr to Sn+Sr+Gz are consistent, with statistical significance reported (McNemar).
- Feature significance analysis: The chi-squared ranking showing many gaze features among top predictors supports the claim that gaze adds genuine signal rather than noise, and the specific basic/graph features are well motivated by prior psycholinguistic findings.
- Careful feature design: Dimensionality controls (PCA) and a diverse set of sentiment/sarcasm features provide a reasonable non-gaze baseline to which the added value of gaze is compared.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and inference-time dependence on gaze: The approach requires eye-tracking at inference time, which today is atypical for most SA applications (batch analysis of tweets/reviews). The feasibility section is forward-looking but speculative; practical deployments will be limited to user-in-the-loop settings (e.g., e-commerce UI with consented tracking). This narrows impact unless a proxy for gaze can be learned/predicted without hardware.
- Dataset scale and generalization: Both datasets are small (~1K snippets each), designed for sarcasm understandability/annotation complexity, and results are from 10-fold CV on the same data used to derive features. There is no out-of-domain test to assess generalization, and the improvements from adding gaze to strong text features are modest in the main tables (0.6–1.5% F-score over Sn+Sr). Risk of overfitting remains despite PCA.
- Confounds from annotation-time gaze: Gaze was collected while annotators produced sentiment labels. The cognitive signal may partly reflect labeling strategy or decision effort rather than text-inherent properties. A stronger control would collect reading-only gaze without a sentiment labeling task, or analyze whether gaze features predictive of label persist across different annotation tasks.
- Baseline comparability: While the internal ablations are fair, the comparison to external SA systems trained on other corpora (Table 1) is not directly comparable to the in-domain CV results. Also, excluding modern neural baselines limits positioning versus current SA practice; even a simple BiLSTM/CNN with pre-trained embeddings on these folds would help calibrate the gains.
- Reproducibility details: Some resources (e.g., in-house discourse connector list) and preprocessing choices are not fully specified; code and feature extraction scripts are not promised for release. Averaging gaze features over annotators may wash out meaningful individual differences; analyzing cross-reader generalization is left as future work.

Questions to authors
- Inference-time use: In what concrete deployment do you envision having gaze available at prediction time? Could you report a scenario where only text is available at test time (e.g., train with gaze+text, test with text-only via multi-view or teacher–student training)?
- Cross-reader generalization: If you train on gaze from subset of readers and test on disjoint readers, how do results change? Similarly, does training on one dataset’s readers transfer to the other?
- Gaze-only vs text-only diagnostics: Gaze-only features underperform text-only, but help when combined. Which specific gaze features contribute most on sarcasm/irony (ablation within Gz)? Are graph features outperforming basic gaze stats?
- Annotation-task confound: Have you considered collecting reading-only gaze (no labeling) for a subset to test whether predictive power holds? Alternatively, can you decorrelate gaze features from annotator agreement/difficulty to ensure they aren’t simply proxies for “hard to label”?
- Neural baselines: Can you include a simple neural text baseline (e.g., CNN/BiLSTM with pre-trained embeddings) under the same CV, and optionally a fusion approach that injects gaze features?
- Release plan: Will you release code for feature extraction (including the discourse connector list) and splits to facilitate replication?

Overall, despite practicality limitations and modest gains over strong text features on the full sets, the conceptual leap—bringing cognitive gaze signals into SA with a new graph representation—and the compelling improvements on sarcasm/irony make this a novel and promising direction. I lean toward acceptance for its originality and potential to open a new line of work at the cognition–NLP interface.
