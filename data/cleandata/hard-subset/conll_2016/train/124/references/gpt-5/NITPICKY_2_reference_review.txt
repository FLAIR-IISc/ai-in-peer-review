Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper augments standard text-based sentiment analysis (SA) features with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye movements (fixations, saccades, regressions) to better handle complex phenomena such as sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations. Two public eye-tracking datasets are used (Mishra et al., 2016; Joshi et al., 2014).
- The authors propose a set of basic and graph-derived “gaze-saliency” features and show 10-fold CV improvements over text-only features, with larger gains on an ironic subset. They also include a feasibility discussion about obtaining gaze at scale via mobile devices.

Contributions as I see them
1) A comprehensive featureization of gaze for SA, including novel graph-based gaze-saliency features, applied to two publicly available datasets. Primary.
2) An empirical observation that gaze features provide larger gains on texts with irony/sarcasm than on simpler texts. Secondary.
3) A consolidated “enhanced” SA feature set combining sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, readability, and gaze features. Tertiary.

Strengths
- Clear empirical signal that gaze helps on complex cases: On a held-out subset with irony/sarcasm, adding gaze improves F1 by 6.1% vs. 2.1% on non-ironic texts. This matches the hypothesis that cognitive signals capture incongruity/surprisal.
- Methodologically diverse gaze features: The paper goes beyond standard fixation/saccade counts to construct gaze-saliency graphs and degree-based measures (e.g., F1H, PSH/RSH), which is a creative and potentially reusable idea for other cognitive-NLP tasks.
- Use of public eye-tracking datasets: Basing the study on two public corpora enables at least partial reproducibility and independent verification of results.
- Ablations across feature groups: The paper reports results for Sn, Sn+Sr, and Sn+Sr+Gz across three classifiers on two datasets, showing consistent incremental gains and a reasonable best model (linear SVM).
- Statistical testing: The authors attempt to assess significance (McNemar) and provide a feature-importance analysis (chi-squared), which is preferable to purely descriptive gains.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and task validity: The approach requires eye-tracking at inference time. The feasibility section is speculative and does not address core deployment realities (latency, per-user calibration, privacy/consent, cost, user diversity). Critically, the gaze signals here are from annotators performing sentiment labeling, not naïve readers in natural conditions, which is a major confound (annotation-task-induced gaze patterns may correlate with labels). Without a clear application where gaze is available at prediction time, the task formulation is of limited external validity.
- Evaluation design and baselines: The “existing SA systems” used as comparators are trained on out-of-domain data (Pang & Lee, Sentiment140) and are largely lexicon + classical ML baselines. Modern, strong text-only neural baselines are excluded by design; yet the paper nonetheless claims improvement over “state of the art,” which is not substantiated. Moreover, the MLP is under-specified and apparently untuned; default WEKA parameters are insufficient for a fair neural baseline.
- Potential data leakage and methodological ambiguities: PCA for dimensionality reduction (e.g., NGRAM_PCA, IMPLICIT_PCA) may have been fit on the full dataset; it is not stated whether PCA is fit within training folds only. Similarly, construction of “LP” (logistic regression sentence polarity) includes training data provenance that is unclear (risk of overlap with target items). The held-out experiment is “randomly derived from Dataset-1” but training protocol and separation from prior CV folds are not specified. These issues can inflate reported gains.
- Cross-dataset comparability and hardware confounds: Dataset 1 (EyeLink 500Hz) and Dataset 2 (Tobii 120Hz) differ substantially in sampling rate and noise characteristics. The paper aggregates raw eye metrics without normalization/calibration across devices/participants, making comparisons and learned weights hard to interpret; improvements on D2 are smaller and some gaze-only rows underperform text-only.
- Incomplete reporting and reproducibility: Key implementation details are missing or vague: tokenization/normalization for mixed genres; exact definitions of graph edge weights (confusing “source/destination” weighted degrees); aggregation across annotators (mean? median? per-subject CV?); classifier hyperparameters and feature scaling; construction of the “implicit incongruity” phrases; how SentiWordNet scores are aggregated; how discourse connectors are curated; whether NE and POS taggers are adapted for tweets. No code/resources are promised, reducing reproducibility.
- Statistical analysis limitations: McNemar is referred to as testing F-score differences; typically it tests paired error counts (accuracy), not F1. The significance testing across multiple feature sets and datasets lacks multiple-comparison control. No confidence intervals are reported.
- Writing, formatting, and references: Numerous grammatical errors and typos (e.g., “an our features”), inconsistent notation (e.g., characters vs words for saccade amplitude), broken citations/placeholders (e.g., “Joshi et al.,? 2013”, “\Q2011\E”), table inconsistencies (e.g., repeated “48.9 48.9 48.9” in Table 2), figure references without clear rendering. These issues impede comprehension and indicate insufficient polishing.

Questions to authors
- Inference-time assumption: In what concrete application will gaze be available for unseen texts at prediction time? Will a single reader’s gaze suffice, or do you rely on multi-reader aggregation? Please quantify performance when using a single reader’s gaze only.
- PCA and preprocessing: Was PCA fit separately within each training fold and then applied to the held-out fold? Were all text features standardized per fold? Please clarify to rule out leakage.
- Annotator confound: Since gaze is recorded during annotation, could annotation effort correlate with label difficulty and bias the model? Did you collect or analyze gaze from readers not asked to label sentiment?
- Hardware normalization: How did you normalize gaze features across devices (500Hz vs 120Hz), subjects, and sessions? Were fixation detection parameters/device noise accounted for?
- Baselines: Can you include strong text-only baselines on the same folds (e.g., BoW/SVM with tuned C; BiLSTM/CNN; RoBERTa fine-tuned) to quantify the incremental value of gaze against modern methods?
- Held-out protocol: How exactly was the held-out subset constructed and how was training performed for that experiment? Is there any overlap with training used in earlier CV?
- Gaze-saliency graph: Please formalize the edge-weight definitions, and clarify how F1H/F2H/PSH/RSH are computed. Are self-loops included? Do you average per subject or build a union graph?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and exact feature extraction pipelines, including the “in-house” discourse connector list and the implicit incongruity bootstrapping resources?

Overall, the idea is interesting and the empirical signal is suggestive, but substantial methodological, evaluation, and presentation issues limit the paper’s current suitability.
