Summary and contributions
This paper investigates augmenting traditional sentiment analysis (SA) with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movements. Using two public gaze datasets that contain many instances with sarcasm/irony, the authors engineer (1) standard sentiment features, (2) features aimed at sarcasm/thwarting, and (3) a suite of gaze-based features, including “gaze-saliency graph” features. With SVM/NB/MLP classifiers, they report consistent gains when adding gaze features, especially on sarcastic/ironic text. They also provide a feature-importance analysis and a brief feasibility discussion about mobile eye-trackers.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence that gaze-derived cognitive features improve SA accuracy, with larger gains on complex constructs like sarcasm/irony, across two datasets and different eye-tracking hardware.
2) A set of gaze features, including graph-based “gaze-saliency” features, and an analysis suggesting these features are among the most informative.
3) An evaluation protocol showing that off-the-shelf text-only SA systems underperform on these challenging datasets, motivating the approach.

Strengths
- Addresses a challenging aspect of SA (sarcasm/irony): The paper focuses on cases where text-only features often fail. Results on a held-out subset show larger relative gains for sarcastic/ironic cases (6.1%) than for simpler cases (2.1%), supporting the motivation.

- Cross-dataset, cross-hardware validation: The approach is tested on two public datasets collected with different eye-trackers (500Hz Eyelink-1000 and 120Hz Tobii T120). Gains are consistent across both, which strengthens the claim that the signal is robust to hardware quality differences.

- Careful feature engineering and interpretability: The gaze feature taxonomy (basic and graph-based) is clearly described and psycholinguistically motivated (fixations, saccades, regressions, skipping). The chi-squared ranking shows many gaze features among the top predictors, supporting the interpretability/utility of the cognitive signals.

- Clear baseline motivation: The authors first show that common SA systems struggle on these datasets (Table 1), which makes the subsequent gains more meaningful.

- Some statistical care: The authors perform McNemar tests to compare top configurations and a feature-importance analysis, which is welcome (though see weaknesses below for caveats).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior gaze work in sentiment-related tasks: Prior work (e.g., Mishra et al., 2014) already explored eye-tracking for sentiment detection and subjectivity extraction. The claim of being “first of its kind” for SA feels overstated. The novelty here is mainly specific feature designs (e.g., gaze-saliency graphs) and a more thorough SA evaluation on sarcasm/irony, but this should be framed more precisely and positioned against Mishra et al. (2014).

- Practicality/deployability is weakly supported: The method requires gaze at inference time. The discussion of mobile eye-trackers is speculative and does not address critical issues (coverage, calibration, latency, privacy/consent). Without a realistic deployment scenario or an approach to approximate gaze from text (e.g., saliency prediction or distillation), the impact for real-world SA is limited.

- Baselines are not sufficiently competitive by today’s standards: The paper explicitly avoids comparing against strong text-only models (e.g., CNN/RNN or modern pre-trained encoders). Even if the approach is feature-based, a fair assessment requires comparing against the best text-only systems available. It remains unclear whether gaze still helps over strong neural baselines fine-tuned on the same data, especially for sarcasm/irony.

- Potential methodological risks and missing details:
  - Possible leakage in PCA/feature construction across folds is not ruled out. PCA (and other statistics) must be fit on the training fold only and then applied to the test fold. This needs to be clarified.
  - Aggregation across multiple annotators per sentence may conflate annotator-specific strategies and sentiment decisions; the paper does not measure generalization to unseen readers. A leave-one-participant-out (LOPO) analysis would better test whether the model generalizes to new readers rather than overfitting to shared annotator behavior.
  - Some feature definitions (gaze graph weights and degree variants) are hard to follow and could be better formalized.

- Inconsistencies/clarity issues in tables and feature groups:
  - Table 2 contains surprising identical SVM numbers for Gz and Sn+Gz on Dataset 2, suggesting a possible reporting error.
  - “Unigram” appears both under “Sentiment features” and as a separate “Uni” baseline, leading to confusion about what “Sn” includes. Please clarify.
  - Minor discrepancies in reported improvements (3.6% vs 3.7%).

- Statistical testing and reporting: A 0.5% difference yielding p = 0.0001 with McNemar on ~1k instances warrants more detail (e.g., number of discordant pairs, multiple-comparison corrections). Confidence intervals for F1 would help.

Questions to authors
1) How is PCA fit during cross-validation? Is PCA estimated on training folds only? Please detail the full CV pipeline to rule out leakage.

2) What does “Sn” include relative to “Uni”? Since unigrams are listed under sentiment features (4.1), why is “Uni” also separate? Please provide a clear mapping from feature groups in Section 4 to the rows in Table 2.

3) Can you provide a leave-one-participant-out setup or training on readers A–B and testing on reader C to assess generalization to unseen readers? How do results change when averaging over fewer readers (e.g., 1–2 vs 5–7)?

4) Could you compare against a strong text-only neural baseline (e.g., CNN/LSTM or a pre-trained encoder) trained and evaluated on the same folds? Do gaze features still offer gains?

5) Table 2 appears to have duplicated SVM scores for Dataset 2 (Gz vs Sn+Gz). Is this a typo? Please double-check all numbers.

6) How much of the gain is attributable to sarcasm/thwarting features vs gaze? Please provide ablations and per-phenomenon breakdowns beyond the small held-out subset.

7) Can you clarify the gaze-saliency graph weights and degree computations with formal definitions and perhaps an example? Some of F1H/F1S/F2H/F2S/PSH/etc. are difficult to interpret.

8) Ethical considerations: How do you plan to handle consent, privacy, and potential demographic biases in gaze collection at scale?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen baselines with modern text-only models; report whether gaze still helps and by how much.
- Add reader generalization experiments (LOPO), sensitivity to number/quality of readers, and robustness to lower sampling rates/noisier devices.
- Tighten the methodology (no leakage), clarify feature groups, and correct table issues; add confidence intervals and detailed McNemar counts.
- Deepen the error analysis with concrete examples where gaze flips the prediction and why.
- Explore “gaze-free” deployment via learning to predict gaze/saliency from text and using it as auxiliary supervision or through knowledge distillation.

Overall recommendation: Reject in current form. The idea is interesting and the empirical gains on hard cases are promising, but the novelty over prior gaze-for-sentiment work is limited, the practicality is not convincingly addressed, and the evaluation lacks strong modern baselines and reader-generalization analyses. With the above additions and clarifications, this work could become a stronger contribution.
