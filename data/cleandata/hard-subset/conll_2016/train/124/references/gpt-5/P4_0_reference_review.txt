Summary and main contributions
The paper investigates whether cognitive features derived from human eye-movement during reading can improve sentiment analysis (SA), especially on text with complex phenomena such as sarcasm, irony, and thwarting. Using two public eye-tracking datasets (≈1k snippets each), the authors engineer a feature set comprising standard sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting features, and gaze-based features (both basic aggregation measures and new “gaze-saliency graph” statistics). They evaluate several classifiers under different feature combinations via 10-fold cross-validation and report modest, statistically significant gains when adding gaze features, with larger improvements on a held-out subset containing sarcasm/irony.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Introduction and operationalization of gaze-derived features for SA, including a novel gaze-saliency graph with weighted-degree features.
2) Empirical evidence that gaze features yield statistically significant improvements over strong text-only feature sets, with larger gains for irony/sarcasm-heavy instances.
3) Feature significance analysis indicating multiple gaze features among the top predictors, and a discussion of feasibility considerations for obtaining gaze at scale.

Strengths
- Clear feature engineering and comprehensive description. The paper documents sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, and cognitive feature families with sufficient detail for replication, including dimensionality reduction choices (e.g., PCA to 500 components) and psycholinguistic motivations for gaze features (e.g., fixations, regressions, saccade length).
- Consistent empirical gains, especially on complex constructs. Adding sarcasm/thwarting features to sentiment features improves F1 by 3.1 and 7.8 points on D1 and D2, respectively; adding gaze features further yields +0.6 and +1.5 points (total +3.7 and +9.3). On a held-out subset, gaze adds +4.2 F1 for irony-containing text versus +1.7 for non-irony, aligning with the hypothesis that cognitive evidence helps with nuanced phenomena.
- Statistical validation and feature analysis. The authors use McNemar tests to assess significance and perform chi-squared feature ranking, where 7–10 of the top 20 features are gaze-based across datasets. This supports the claim that gaze features contribute beyond redundancy with text features.
- Use of two independent datasets and hardware. The improvement persists across differing eye-trackers (SR Research Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120) and data sources (reviews, tweets), suggesting some robustness of the approach to signal quality and domain.
- Interpretability of cognitive signals. The proposed features (e.g., regressions, skip rates, saliency graph degrees) are interpretable and grounded in established psycholinguistic findings, aiding analysis and future extensions.

Weaknesses
- Practicality at inference time is unclear. The approach requires gaze data at test time. The paper’s feasibility section posits mobile eye tracking as a future solution, but does not demonstrate an operational pipeline where SA can be performed on arbitrary new texts without human readers. This limits applicability relative to standard SA, which operates solely on text.
- Modest incremental gains and limited baselines. Absolute gains from gaze are small on D1 (+0.6 F1) and moderate on D2 (+1.5 F1), after already adding sarcasm/thwarting features. Gaze-only features underperform text-only for SVM. The paper excludes contemporary deep learning baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN/transformers) that often constitute the state of the art, weakening the claim of improving over the best available text-only methods.
- Possible confounds and limited ablations. While LEN and readability are included with gaze features, the paper does not isolate how much of the improvement comes from gaze beyond length/readability and other simple correlates. Additional ablations (e.g., Sn + Sr + readability vs. Sn + Sr + Gz) would clarify unique contributions of gaze. Also, the small datasets (≈1k) and use of default hyperparameters raise overfitting concerns despite PCA.
- Potential circularity with annotation setting. Gaze was collected while annotators labeled the same snippets used for training/evaluation. This may entangle gaze patterns with the labeling task itself (e.g., longer scrutiny when uncertain or when sentiment is hard to decide), rather than reflecting how a naive reader would process sentiment. The paper averages across annotators but does not report analyses such as leave-one-annotator-out or cross-annotator generalization to mitigate this concern.
- Limited external validation and deployment scenario. All evaluations require eye-tracking for the instances evaluated; no experiments show transfer to texts without gaze (e.g., training with gaze and inferring surrogates at test time). The “feasibility” discussion is speculative and lacks empirical support about sensor availability, privacy, or noise in real-world usage.

Questions to authors
- Inference-time requirements: Do you envision SA being run only when fresh gaze data is available (e.g., from each reader), or can models trained with gaze generalize to perform SA on text alone? Have you explored learning surrogate models that predict gaze features from text to avoid requiring sensors at test time?
- Annotation dependency: Were the gaze traces collected during an explicit labeling task for sentiment? If so, can you report results when using gaze from readers not asked to label (pure reading) to separate reading behavior from the act of annotation?
- Cross-reader generalization: How robust are the improvements when training on gaze aggregated from some participants and testing on gaze from unseen participants (leave-one-participant-out)? Do results hold when using a single reader’s gaze rather than averaged signals?
- Ablations controlling for readability and length: Can you provide experiments comparing Sn + Sr + readability/length vs. Sn + Sr + Gz to quantify the unique benefit from gaze?
- Stronger baselines: Can you compare against modern text-only baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN or transformer-based models) to contextualize the magnitude of gains attributable to gaze?
- Statistical testing details: For the 0.5–0.6 F1 differences on D1, please report the contingency tables or exact N used in McNemar tests to support p = 0.0001.

Overall, the paper presents a clearly described and technically sound exploration of gaze-based features for SA, with indications that cognitive signals help on linguistically complex cases. The main limitations are the practicality of obtaining gaze at test time, modest incremental gains over strong text-only features, and missing comparisons to state-of-the-art text-only baselines.
