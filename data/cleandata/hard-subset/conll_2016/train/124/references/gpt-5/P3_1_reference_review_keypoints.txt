- Paper augments sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking, showing small but significant gains and larger gains on sarcasm/irony subsets.
- Engineers two families of gaze features: aggregate measures (fixations, saccades, regressions, skips) and novel graph-based gaze-saliency features.
- Evaluates on two public gaze datasets collected with different hardware and participant pools; reports significance tests and feature-importance analysis.
- Contribution: empirical finding that aggregate gaze features incrementally improve sentiment polarity classification, especially for sarcasm/irony.
- Contribution: systematic feature design including novel weighted-degree graph saliency measures for classification.
- Contribution: cross-dataset evaluation with targeted held-out subset and analysis showing gaze features often rank highly among predictors.
- Strength: tackles a recognized gap—pragmatic phenomena like sarcasm are poorly handled by lexical/syntactic features, and cognitive signals may help.
- Strength: evidence across datasets collected with different eye-trackers suggests some robustness to device differences.
- Strength: targeted analysis shows larger relative gains on a sarcasm-heavy held-out set (+6.1% F).
- Strength: feature-importance results indicate gaze features contribute informative signal beyond text features.
- Strength: clear methodological definitions for a broad set of gaze features, aiding reproducibility.
- Weakness: practical deployability is unclear—method assumes multi-reader eye-tracking at inference, which is impractical for most SA applications.
- Weakness: gains from gaze are modest (+0.6 to +1.5 F for SVM); gaze-only models perform poorly; most improvement comes from sarcasm/thwarting text features.
- Weakness: missing comparisons to contemporary SOTA text models (CNN/RNN/fastText/embeddings), making it hard to judge true added value of gaze.
- Weakness: evaluation design risks overfitting (small datasets, high-dimensional features, default hyperparameters, ad hoc PCA) and lacks nested CV or regularization tuning.
- Weakness: “difficulty” check conflates domain shift with task difficulty by training on different-domain corpora and testing on D1/D2.
- Weakness: potential confounds—gaze correlates with reading difficulty and length; controls are insufficient (no matched-length subsets or regression/mixed-effects controls).
- Weakness: no participant-level analysis—features are averaged across readers and cross-participant generalization or reader-specific leakage is not assessed.
- Weakness: gaze-saliency graph definition is under-specified (token vs. position, edge weighting/multiplicity, normalization, top-2 degree choice).
- Weakness: feature-importance table and per-phenomenon error breakdowns are insufficiently detailed (no per-class metrics for sarcastic vs. non-sarcastic cases).
- Questions for authors: report inference assumptions—how many readers at test time, performance with a single reader, and performance as a function of readers per item.
- Questions: perform cross-participant validation (e.g., leave-one-reader-out) to test reader-specific effects; compare to tuned strong text-only baselines; report hyperparameter tuning; assess robustness controlling for length/frequency/readability; formalize saliency graph construction.
