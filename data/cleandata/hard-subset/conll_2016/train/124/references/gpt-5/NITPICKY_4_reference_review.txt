Summary and contributions
This paper augments standard sentiment analysis (SA) features with “cognitive” features derived from eye-tracking recordings collected while annotators label short texts. The authors engineer (i) conventional SA features, (ii) features targeting sarcasm/irony/thwarting, and (iii) gaze-based features, including a proposed gaze-saliency graph. They evaluate on two small eye-tracking datasets rich in sarcasm/irony, reporting modest gains when adding gaze features, especially on a held-out subset of “complex” (ironic/sarcastic) items.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical study showing that gaze-derived features can yield small but measurable gains (0.6–1.5 F1 points over strong textual features including sarcasm cues; larger relative gains on a small “complex” held-out subset).
2) A feature engineering contribution: the “gaze-saliency graph” and several graph-based and aggregate gaze features for SA.
3) A focus on challenging phenomena (sarcasm/irony/thwarting) with an analysis suggesting larger gains on complex cases.

Strengths
- Tackles hard phenomena: The paper targets implicit sentiment, sarcasm, and thwarting—cases where lexical/syntactic signals are often insufficient. Evaluations on two datasets deliberately enriched in such phenomena are appropriate to the stated goal.
- Consistent gains across models/datasets: Adding gaze to textual features generally improves F1 for SVM and NN on both datasets, and the authors test significance (McNemar) for the top two settings on D1. The held-out subset analysis (ironical vs non-ironical) is aligned with the hypothesis and shows larger relative gains on complex texts.
- Extensive feature engineering: The feature set is broad (lexical, syntactic proxies, discourse, sarcasm cues, readability, and multiple gaze-derived features), which allows a fair assessment of the incremental value of gaze beyond strong textual baselines.
- Acknowledges feasibility concerns: The paper explicitly discusses the practical barrier of requiring gaze at inference time and sketches potential application scenarios and hardware trends. Although speculative, it shows awareness of deployment issues.
- Attempts at feature importance: A chi-squared feature ranking (though incompletely reported) suggests several gaze features rank highly, supporting the claim that gaze adds signal orthogonal to text-only features.

Weaknesses
- Questionable practicality and task definition: The system fundamentally requires eye-tracking at test time for each new text, which is at odds with standard SA use-cases (e.g., batch classification of reviews). The “feasibility” discussion is speculative (mobile webcams, consent) and not supported by experiments simulating realistic mobile noise, latency, or missing data. No text-only deployment path is offered (e.g., training student models to mimic gaze), so the practical impact is limited.
- Limited novelty vs prior cognitive-SA work: Prior studies (e.g., Mishra et al., 2014) have already explored gaze for sentiment/subjectivity. The claim of being “first of its kind” is not convincingly substantiated, and there is no direct comparison or ablation against prior gaze-based sentiment features. The novelty seems to rest mainly on the saliency-graph features, which are interesting but under-specified (see below).
- Evaluation design and reporting issues: 
  • The gains over strong textual baselines (Sn+Sr) are small (0.6–1.5 F1), and significance is only reported for D1. Significance for D2 is missing.  
  • Cross-validation is done on the same small datasets used to extract gaze features; no cross-dataset generalization (train on D1, test on D2) nor domain transfer is tested.  
  • The held-out subset experiment is unclear: how is the model trained (on the remainder of D1? same protocol?), and are folds strictly disjoint at the item level?  
  • The comparison to “existing SA systems” trained on 10k+ external texts (Table 1) isn’t directly informative, since those systems lack access to gaze and face domain shift. The main comparison should be within-dataset text-only vs text+gaze (which is done in Table 2), but claims of “state-of-the-art” are not justified without strong modern baselines (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN/Transformer) on the same data.
- Methodological clarity gaps and potential leakage: 
  • PCA and feature normalization: It’s unclear whether PCA and scaling are fit on training folds only. Applying PCA on the full dataset risks leakage.  
  • Label aggregation: D1 and D2 have multiple annotators. What is the gold label (majority? adjudicated?), and is there any filtering by annotator reliability?  
  • Saliency-graph features are ambiguously defined: “Fixation Duration at Left/Source vs Right/Target” use very similar definitions; source/destination weighting is unclear; “RSH/RSS” vs “RSDH/RSDS” appear duplicated; Edge Density formula has a typo; LREG mixes characters vs words; SL is in words while LREG uses characters.  
  • Statistical testing: The paper states a significant difference in “F-scores” via McNemar; McNemar should be applied to paired predictions, not F-scores. The test protocol needs to be specified precisely (e.g., pooled over folds with stratified pairing).
- Writing/formatting/reproducibility issues: Numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., “an our features”), broken reference formatting (“?\ Q2011\E”), inconsistent notation (F˙score), missing Table 3 content, mismatched figure/table captions, and unclear section numbering. Implementation details are insufficient (e.g., handling missing gaze samples, AOI mapping, regression detection, hyperparameters for NN, class weighting). No code or feature extraction scripts promised.

Questions to authors
1) At inference time, how do you envision obtaining gaze for unseen texts in real applications? Could you train a text-only student model to predict the effect of gaze (knowledge distillation) and report its performance?
2) Precisely how are gold labels derived from multiple annotators on D1/D2? Do you filter annotators or weight by reliability?
3) Do PCA and any normalization/feature selection operate within each training fold only? Please confirm and, if not, re-run to avoid leakage.
4) For the held-out subset experiment, what is the training data and protocol? Is the subset completely excluded from training? Are results averaged over multiple random draws?
5) Please provide exact definitions for saliency-graph features (edge weights, degree computations, units) and fix inconsistencies (e.g., characters vs words, source vs destination). Can you share code?
6) Can you report statistical significance on D2 and include confidence intervals? Also provide per-class metrics (precision/recall) for irony vs non-irony splits.
7) How robust are your gaze features to lower sampling rates and missingness? Can you simulate mobile noise and report sensitivity?

Additional comments
- Add a strong modern text-only baseline (e.g., pretrained transformer fine-tuned on your datasets), and compare against it with/without gaze.  
- Consider cross-dataset evaluations (train on D1, test on D2, and vice versa) to test generalization.  
- Clarify the use of the LP feature (supervised LR): what training data is used, and is there any overlap with your evaluation sets?  
- Address ethics and privacy more rigorously (consent, storage, demographic bias).  

Overall, while the idea is intriguing and the engineering is earnest, the limited novelty, small gains, unclear methodology, and practicality concerns outweigh the positives in the current form. I recommend rejection, with potential for a stronger resubmission after methodological clarifications, stronger baselines, and a more realistic deployment story.
