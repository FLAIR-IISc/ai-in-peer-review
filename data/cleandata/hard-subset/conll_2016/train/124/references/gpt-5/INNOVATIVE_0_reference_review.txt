Summary
This paper proposes augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movements. Beyond standard sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting textual features, the authors introduce a set of basic gaze features and a novel gaze-saliency graph representation whose weighted degree statistics serve as complex gaze features. Across two public eye-tracking datasets containing many sarcastic/ironic snippets, the combined feature set improves F1 over text-only baselines; gains are larger on a held-out subset rich in irony/sarcasm.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Introduction of gaze-derived cognitive features, including a saliency-graph formulation, for sentiment analysis, showing consistent improvements, especially on sarcastic/ironic text.
2) Systematic ablation demonstrating the additive value of sarcasm/thwarting features and, on top, gaze features, with significance testing and feature-importance analysis showing several gaze features among top predictors.
3) Empirical evidence that cognitive signals help on linguistically nuanced phenomena (irony/sarcasm) where text-only models struggle, plus an initial discussion of feasibility.

Strengths
- High-level novelty and boldness: Using eye-tracking–based cognitive signals for SA is a fresh, conceptually significant direction for cognitively grounded NLP. The gaze-saliency graph is a particularly creative representation that goes beyond standard aggregate gaze statistics.
- Clear ablations and targeted evaluation: The stepwise gains (Sn → Sn+Sr → Sn+Sr+Gz) are carefully quantified on two datasets. The held-out subset analysis shows larger relative improvements for irony/sarcasm (+6.1% F1), supporting the central hypothesis that gaze captures nuances difficult for text-only features.
- Significance and interpretability: McNemar tests establish significance; chi-squared feature ranking highlights that many top features are gaze-based, lending credibility that cognitive signals contribute, not just noise.
- Multiple data sources and device conditions: Results on datasets collected with both high-end (500Hz) and lower-end (120Hz) eye-trackers suggest some robustness of the approach and improve external validity relative to single-device studies.
- Honest limitations and feasibility discussion: The paper acknowledges hardware and aggregation limitations and discusses potential mobile deployment scenarios, which—though speculative—show awareness of practical barriers.

Weaknesses
- Practicality at inference: The approach requires gaze at test time, and the reported features are averages over 5–7 annotators. It is unclear how well the model performs with a single reader (the likely deployment scenario) or with noisier mobile gaze. Without such analysis, practical impact remains uncertain.
- Baseline strength and positioning: The paper does not compare against strong contemporary text-only neural baselines (e.g., CNN/BiLSTM/Transformer fine-tuned on SA or sarcasm). The claim of “state of the art” on these datasets is therefore not compelling; the incremental value of gaze over modern text models remains unknown.
- Effect size attribution: Most of the overall improvement over unigram/sentiment features stems from sarcasm/thwarting textual features (+3.1% and +7.8%); gaze adds a modest +0.6% and +1.5% on the full sets (albeit larger on irony-only). The narrative should better calibrate claims and analyze when gaze helps the most and which specific gaze features drive the gains.
- Possible task-induced confounds: Eye-tracking during sentiment annotation could encode strategy correlated with labels (e.g., longer regressions for negative judgments). Controls such as free reading vs task reading, cross-reader generalization, or order counterbalancing are not reported, leaving open whether models learn sentiment or annotation behavior.
- Missing experimental details affecting reproducibility: MLP architecture, PCA explained variance, token–AoI alignment for irregular tokens, per-sentence normalization of gaze measures, handling of outlier annotators, and fold splits with respect to readers are not fully specified.

Questions to authors
- Inference setting: How does performance change when using a single reader’s gaze vs the 5–7-reader average? Please report variance across readers and the effect of sampling rate (simulate mobile-quality).
- Cross-reader/domain generalization: Can you train on readers A–D and test on reader E? Train on reviews and test on tweets (and vice versa)? This would help assess robustness and confound risks.
- Stronger baselines: Can you include at least one modern text-only neural baseline and, ideally, a variant augmented with gaze features to show whether gaze still adds beyond today’s best text models?
- Feature robustness: Are gaze-saliency graph statistics length-normalized beyond including LEN/RED? Which specific gaze features contribute most on irony vs non-irony (per-class ablation)?
- Data protocol: Were annotators aware of the sentiment task during reading (likely yes)? If so, can you provide any control evidence that the gains are not driven by task-specific strategies?

Additional comments
- The saliency graph idea is compelling; qualitative case studies mapping surprising regressions/edges to sarcastic cues would be illuminating.
- Releasing the gaze feature extraction code would substantially increase impact and encourage follow-up.

Overall: Despite practicality concerns and missing comparisons to strong neural baselines, the conceptual novelty—cognitively grounded features for SA and the saliency-graph formulation—is significant and worth spotlighting. I am inclined to favor acceptance for its bold direction and potential to inspire a new line of cognitively informed sentiment modeling.
