- Paper augments text-based sentiment analysis with gaze-derived cognitive features (fixations, saccades, regressions, saliency graphs), combining them with sentiment/sarcasm features and evaluating on two datasets/hardware; reports modest but consistent F1 gains, larger on sarcastic/ironic subsets.

- Contribution: Empirical evidence that gaze features provide complementary signal to text for sentiment polarity, especially for complex phenomena like sarcasm, irony, and thwarting.

- Contribution: Feature design that operationalizes gaze into simple aggregates and graph-derived saliency measures, with several gaze features ranking highly by chi-squared.

- Contribution: Demonstration of effect across two datasets with different eye-tracking hardware, showing robustness of small gains despite lower sampling in one dataset.

- Strength: Conceptual novelty linking eye-tracking/psycholinguistics to NLP sentiment analysis, addressing a relatively unexplored application of gaze.

- Strength: Clear empirical improvement on hard phenomena—gaze yields larger gains on sarcastic/ironic subsets (e.g., +4.2 F1 over baseline).

- Strength: Cross-dataset robustness: incremental gains persist across domains and hardware (Eyelink-1000 vs Tobii T120).

- Strength: Thoughtful feature engineering including saliency graphs and degree-based features to model localized processing difficulty and inter-word transitions.

- Strength: Use of statistical checks (McNemar) and chi-squared feature ranking to argue gaze features matter beyond text.

- Weakness: Practicality—reliance on gaze at inference is a major deployment limitation; reported gains may not justify cost/latency/privacy hurdles and training-only uses of gaze are not explored.

- Weakness: Methodological clarity—several feature definitions and naming are confusing, edge-weight computation and normalization across readers/sentences are unclear, and gaze may conflate simple confounds like length or punctuation.

- Weakness: Limited baselines and tuning—missing stronger text-only baselines (char n-grams, tf-idf, embeddings), under-specified neural baseline, Weka defaults used without hyperparameter tuning or variance reporting.

- Weakness: Statistical reporting—McNemar is misapplied relative to F1 claims, no contingency tables shown, and cross-fold variance/confidence intervals are not reported.

- Weakness: Data/reader effects—gaze averaged across annotators who provided labels may entangle annotator behavior with gold labels; no inter-reader variability analysis, leave-one-reader-out tests, or demonstration that a single reader suffices.

- Question: Do authors intend gaze at deployment, or can gaze be used only at training time (e.g., multitask learning or distillation) to yield a text-only deployable model?

- Question: How sensitive are results to the number of readers—please report performance as a function of readers and include leave-one-reader-out experiments for generalization.

- Question: Clarify statistical testing—how was McNemar applied, provide contingency tables, exact n, and report mean±std across CV folds or confidence intervals.

- Question: Add stronger textual baselines—regularized linear models on tf-idf word/char n-grams and simple RNN/CNN baselines to isolate gaze’s unique contribution.

- Question: Provide precise mathematical definitions for saliency graph features (weights, normalization, multiple saccades, self-loops) and ablate basic vs graph-based gaze features.

- Question: Control for confounds—add features for punctuation density, POS distributions, sentence position, and perform partial correlation or mixed-effects analyses to quantify unique variance explained by gaze.
