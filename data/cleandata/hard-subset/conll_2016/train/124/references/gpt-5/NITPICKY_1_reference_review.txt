Summary and claimed contributions
This paper augments text-based sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking during annotation. It defines (i) basic fixation/saccade features and (ii) graph-based “gaze-saliency” features, and combines them with standard sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting textual features. On two public eye-tracking datasets, the authors report modest F-score gains (up to ~1.5 points) over text-only features, with larger relative gains on ironic/sarcastic instances in a small held-out subset.

Contributions as I see them (in order):
1) Empirical evidence that adding simple gaze-derived features yields small but statistically significant improvements over engineered text-only features for sentence-level sentiment classification, with larger gains on irony/sarcasm.
2) A feature set catalog for gaze-based sentiment, including a simple gaze-saliency graph and degree-based descriptors.
3) Error/feasibility discussion linking psycholinguistic signals to NLP tasks.

Strengths
- Bridging psycholinguistics and SA: The paper explores a timely idea—using cognitive signals to complement text, particularly for phenomena (irony, thwarting) known to confound standard features. The held-out analysis indicating bigger gains on ironic items is an informative slice and aligns with the motivation.
- Cross-dataset evidence: Results are shown on two eye-tracking datasets acquired with different hardware (500Hz Eyelink vs 120Hz Tobii), suggesting some robustness to acquisition conditions (though see weaknesses on normalization).
- Clear, modular feature engineering: The paper systematically groups features (sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, gaze, readability) and demonstrates that each block adds value, with the primary improvement coming from sarcasm/thwarting features and a further small gain from gaze.
- Basic statistical checks: The use of McNemar’s test (albeit only on one comparison) and chi-squared feature ranking is a step toward rigor, and the finding that many top-ranked features are gaze-based is consistent with the central claim.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and overstated claims: Prior work has already used eye-tracking for sentiment-related tasks, including sentiment detection and subjectivity extraction (e.g., Mishra et al., 2014). The claim of being “first of its kind” is not substantiated. The graph features are described at a high level and appear to collapse to simple degree statistics; the methodological novelty over prior cognitive-feature use is incremental.
- Methodology and baselines: The main comparison to “existing SA systems” (Table 1) is trained on external corpora with different distributions, while the proposed models are evaluated via within-dataset cross-validation including gaze—this is not a fair, apples-to-apples baseline. Strong text-only baselines (e.g., fine-tuned pretrained encoders) are missing; without them, it is unclear whether gaze still helps when modern textual models are used. Gains reported for SVM are small (e.g., 0.6–1.5 F1) and could plausibly vanish against stronger baselines.
- Ambiguity and reproducibility gaps: Key details are under-specified. Examples: exact construction of gaze-saliency graph (are nodes unique per token? “may not be unique” is confusing), edge-weight definitions for F1H/F2H/PSH/PSDH/RSH/RSDH (the text repeats/contradicts and lacks formulas), normalization across sampling rates (500Hz vs 120Hz), handling of skipped words/missing data, aggregation across annotators, hyperparameters for the MLP, feature scaling, and PCA fit protocol. Held-out protocol is unclear (what is the training set? were hyperparameters tuned on the held-out?).
- Statistical reporting issues: Only one McNemar test (dataset 1, best vs second-best) is reported; no contingency tables or confidence intervals are provided. For dataset 2, no significance testing is shown. Per-class precision/recall and macro vs micro averaging are not clarified; Table 1 has values (e.g., F < both P and R) that are hard to interpret without metric definitions. Several numbers in Table 2 look duplicated or inconsistent (e.g., repeated 48.9s), raising concerns about reporting accuracy.
- Practicality and external validity: The approach requires gaze at inference time; the feasibility section is speculative. No experiment demonstrates a realistic deployment scenario (e.g., passive reading vs annotation gaze), nor a learning-with-privileged-information setup where gaze is used during training only. Gaze collected during labeling may not reflect natural reading, introducing domain shift not addressed here.
- Presentation/formatting quality: Numerous typos and inconsistencies (e.g., “an our features,” “dataset -1,” random TeX artifacts, erratic reference formatting with “? \Q2011\E,” inconsistent acronym use, footnote placement), missing figures/tables (the manuscript includes captions but no figures; Table 3 is referenced but absent). This detracts from clarity and undermines confidence in the results.

Questions to Authors
1) Data splits and leakage: How are folds constructed? Are sentences averaged across the same set of annotators in all folds (participant-level leakage)? Did you try leave-one-subject-out CV to test generalization across readers? For the held-out set from Dataset 1, what exactly is the training set, and were any decisions (feature selection, PCA, thresholds) made using the held-out labels?
2) Metrics and significance: Are P/R/F macro-averaged or micro-averaged? Please provide per-class metrics and the McNemar contingency tables. Report statistical significance for dataset 2 as well, with corrections for multiple comparisons where applicable.
3) Gaze graph definitions: Please provide unambiguous mathematical definitions (node uniqueness, directed/undirected, weighting schemes, degree computation, normalization by length), and justify why “highest/second highest degree” capture information beyond basic fixation/saccade statistics.
4) Normalization across devices: How do you normalize features for different sampling rates and calibration accuracy (500Hz vs 120Hz)? Were fixation durations rescaled? Any per-participant z-scoring?
5) Stronger text-only baselines: Can you report results for modern pretrained models (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa) on the same folds? Do gaze features still add value on top of these?
6) Practical usage: Can you demonstrate a training-only gaze scenario (e.g., LUPI/teacher-student/multiview) where test-time does not require gaze? Any analysis comparing “annotation gaze” to “natural reading” gaze?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The idea is interesting and the empirical signal is suggestive, but the novelty over prior gaze-for-sentiment work is limited; methodological and reporting issues, missing strong baselines, and substantial clarity/formatting problems make the current submission below CoNLL standards. Addressing the questions above, tightening the experimental design, and improving presentation could make this a stronger paper.
