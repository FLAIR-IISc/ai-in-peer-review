Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies sentiment analysis of short texts by augmenting conventional textual features with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movement patterns recorded during annotation. The authors:
1) Propose a suite of gaze-based features (basic fixation/saccade/regression statistics and graph-based “gaze-saliency” measures) and combine them with sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features for supervised classification.
2) Empirically evaluate the effect of adding gaze features on two public eye-tracking datasets, reporting small but statistically significant F1 improvements overall and larger gains on a subset with irony/sarcasm.
3) Provide a feature importance analysis suggesting that gaze features rank among the most informative features.

Main contributions as I see them (strongest first)
1) Introduction and operationalization of gaze-derived features for sentiment classification, including a novel saliency-graph representation. This is the core methodological contribution.
2) Empirical evidence that gaze features improve F1 beyond strong hand-engineered textual features, with clearer benefits on texts containing irony/sarcasm.
3) A systematic feature set and analysis that situates gaze features relative to standard sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features.

Strengths
- Novel use of cognitive signals for SA: While gaze has been explored for related tasks (annotation complexity, sarcasm understandability), using a broad set of eye-tracking features directly for polarity classification is a concrete, implementable addition. The saliency-graph features are especially original.
- Clear empirical decomposition of feature contributions: The paper evaluates Unigrams, Sentiment (Sn), Sn+Sarcasm/Thwarting (Sr), Gaze (Gz), Sn+Gz, and Sn+Sr+Gz across three classifiers and two datasets. This allows attribution: adding Sr to Sn yields sizable gains (+3.1 F1 on D1, +7.8 on D2), and adding Gz on top gives further improvements (+0.6 and +1.5), isolating the marginal utility of gaze.
- Benefits for complex phenomena: On a held-out subset with irony/sarcasm, adding gaze yields a larger relative F1 improvement (6.1% for complex vs. 2.1% for simpler texts), aligning with the hypothesis that cognitive traces capture processing difficulty/incongruity.
- Public datasets and statistical testing: Experiments use publicly available eye-tracking datasets; McNemar tests are reported for key comparisons, and a chi-squared feature ranking indicates that gaze features appear among top features, lending support to the claims.
- Comprehensive feature engineering and documentation: The gaze feature definitions (basic and graph-based) and the sarcasm/thwarting features are described in sufficient detail to be re-implemented, which improves reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited and uneven baselines; unclear “state-of-the-art” comparison: The paper omits comparisons to modern neural baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN/transformer-based SA with pre-trained embeddings) despite acknowledging their strength. Moreover, Table 1 shows an SVM trained on external corpora achieving F1 76.8 on D2, which exceeds the best cross-validated results on D2 with all features (71.8). The claim of “improvement over the state of the art” on the held-out set is not substantiated by a direct, apples-to-apples comparison to state-of-the-art textual models within the same data/splits.
- Modest overall gains from gaze and weak standalone performance: The marginal contribution of gaze beyond strong textual features is small on average (+0.6 and +1.5 F1), and gaze-only features underperform textual baselines (e.g., SVM F1 53.4 on D1, 48.9 on D2). This raises questions about cost-benefit, given the overhead of eye-tracking.
- Evaluation details and potential leakage ambiguities: The 10-fold CV uses gaze aggregated across annotators per sentence, which reduces subject leakage, but it remains unclear whether folds are stratified by sentence only (they seem to be) and whether any per-participant effects remain. For the held-out subset (294 items), the training protocol, use of the remaining data, and whether tuning or feature selection used test information are not specified. Also, macro vs. micro averaging and class imbalance handling are not clearly stated.
- Practicality and deployment assumptions: At inference, the method requires readers’ eye-tracking while reading the target text. The feasibility section is speculative; no experiments demonstrate performance when gaze comes from new, non-annotator readers on-device, or robustness to lower-fidelity/mobile trackers beyond Dataset 2’s Tobii condition. This limits immediate applicability.
- Some feature definitions and preprocessing choices need clarification: Several graph-based feature descriptions are hard to parse (e.g., “Fixation Duration at Right/Target,” “Forward Saccade Word Count of Destination” and how weighted degrees are computed across edges). Details on text preprocessing, feature scaling/normalization, hyperparameters, and PCA choices (why 500 dimensions for unigrams and implicit incongruity) are sparse, impacting reproducibility and interpretability.

Questions to Authors
- Baselines and SoTA: Can you provide comparisons against strong neural baselines (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN with pre-trained embeddings; or transformer-based fine-tuning) trained and evaluated under the same splits, both with and without sarcasm/thwarting features?
- Evaluation protocol: For the held-out set, what is the exact training/validation/test split? Were any parameters or feature selection steps informed by the held-out labels? Please specify macro vs. micro F1 and how class imbalance is handled.
- Gaze aggregation and subject effects: How exactly are per-participant features aggregated per sentence (simple averages or more robust statistics)? Did you attempt subject-wise cross-validation (leave-subjects-out) to ensure no subject-specific patterns influence results?
- Robustness to device/quality: Beyond the reported datasets, did you test sensitivity of gaze features to tracker quality (e.g., subsampling or noise injection) to emulate mobile conditions?
- Feature definitions: Please formalize the gaze-saliency graph features with precise mathematical definitions (edge weights, degree computations, “source” vs. “destination”), and detail preprocessing (e.g., fixation filtering thresholds, saccade classification, AoI mapping).
- Deployment scenario: At inference time, whose gaze is used? If end-user gaze is not available, can you estimate performance using text-only models trained with gaze as auxiliary supervision (e.g., multi-view or distillation)?

Overall, the paper presents a principled and novel cognitive augmentation for sentiment analysis with clear though modest gains, especially on irony/sarcasm. Strengthening baselines, clarifying evaluation, and addressing practical deployment would substantially improve the paper’s impact and technical soundness.
