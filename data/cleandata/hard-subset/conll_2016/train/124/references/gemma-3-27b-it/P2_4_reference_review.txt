## Review for “Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis”

**Summary:**

This paper explores the use of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data of human annotators, to improve sentiment analysis performance. The authors hypothesize that readers' eye-movement patterns reflect cognitive processes relevant to understanding sentiment, particularly in complex cases like sarcasm and irony. They augment traditional sentiment analysis features with these cognitive features and demonstrate improved accuracy on two datasets, reporting significant gains, especially when handling nuanced text.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Feature Engineering:** The primary contribution is the introduction and evaluation of a new set of features derived from eye-tracking data for sentiment analysis. While eye-tracking has been used *in relation* to sentiment annotation, this paper is the first (to the best of my knowledge, and as claimed by the authors) to directly incorporate eye-movement patterns *as features* within a standard sentiment classification pipeline.
2. **Empirical Demonstration of Cognitive Feature Benefit:** The paper provides empirical evidence suggesting that cognitive features enhance sentiment classification accuracy, specifically for datasets containing more complex linguistic constructs like sarcasm and irony. The improvement, while not enormous, is statistically significant and consistent across classifiers.
3. **Dataset Utilization:** The use of two publicly available datasets containing eye-tracking data and focusing on nuanced sentiment (sarcasm, thwarting) provides a good foundation for evaluating the proposed approach. The analysis demonstrating the datasets' inherent difficulty for standard SA methods is also valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty & Potential Impact:**  The core idea of incorporating cognitive features derived from human reading behavior is genuinely novel. If this approach proves robust and scalable, it could represent a significant step forward in sentiment analysis, particularly for handling subtle forms of expression that currently challenge state-of-the-art methods.
2. **Sound Experimental Setup:** The experimental design appears well-considered. The use of 10-fold cross-validation, multiple classifiers (NB, SVM, NN), and a held-out dataset for validation strengthens the reliability of the results. The comparison with baseline systems (NB, SVM, RB) helps establish the value of the proposed features.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The authors thoughtfully combine traditional sentiment features with those related to sarcasm/thwarting and the novel cognitive features. This allows for a clear evaluation of the added value of the eye-tracking data. The detailed explanation of each feature category is helpful.
4. **Statistical Significance:** The authors correctly perform and report statistical significance tests (McNemar test), demonstrating that the observed improvements are not merely due to chance.
5. **Discussion of Feasibility:** The authors proactively address the practicality of collecting eye-tracking data, pointing to the growing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential applications. This demonstrates awareness of the limitations and potential for real-world impact.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Magnitude of Improvement:** While statistically significant, the absolute improvement in F-score (around 3-9% depending on the dataset and feature combination) is not dramatic. This raises the question of whether the added complexity of collecting and processing eye-tracking data is justified by the gains in accuracy. A more thorough cost-benefit analysis would be beneficial.
2. **Data Dependency & Generalizability:** The datasets used are relatively small, and the Fleiss Kappa values (0.62 and 0.68) suggest moderate inter-annotator agreement. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to other datasets and annotation schemes. Investigating the robustness of the approach with different datasets and annotators would be important.
3. **Feature Engineering Complexity:**  The cognitive features, especially those derived from the gaze-saliency graphs, are quite complex and require significant preprocessing. The paper doesn't fully address the computational cost of these features, or how they might scale to very large datasets. A discussion of feature selection or dimensionality reduction techniques would be valuable.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis Detail:** While the authors mention error analysis, the details provided are limited. A more in-depth analysis of the types of errors made by the system with and without cognitive features would provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.  Specifically, *where* are the cognitive features most helpful in correcting errors?
5. **Correlation between annotators**: While the authors use data from multiple annotators, it isn't clear if the cognitive features are consistent across annotators. It would be useful to see how much variance there is in the eye tracking data, and whether averaging the features across annotators loses important information.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed cost-benefit analysis of incorporating cognitive features, considering the data acquisition, preprocessing, and computational costs?
2.  How sensitive are your results to the quality of the eye-tracking data (e.g., sampling rate, tracking accuracy)?  Did you observe any significant differences in performance between the datasets with different eye-tracker quality?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically identifying examples where the cognitive features led to correct classifications that would have been incorrect without them?
4.  Could you discuss the inter-annotator variability in eye-tracking data and how this impacts the effectiveness of the aggregated cognitive features?
5.  Have you experimented with different methods for deriving cognitive features from the eye-tracking data, beyond the ones presented in the paper? (e.g., different graph structures, different statistical aggregations)




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
* The referencing is comprehensive.
* The discussion of related work is appropriate.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a promising and novel approach to sentiment analysis. While the magnitude of improvement is modest and some concerns regarding data dependency and complexity remain, the potential impact of incorporating cognitive features is significant. The paper is well-executed and provides a solid foundation for future research.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
