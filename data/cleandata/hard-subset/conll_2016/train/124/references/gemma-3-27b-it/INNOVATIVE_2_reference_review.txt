This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data during sentiment annotation. The core idea is that readers’ eye-movement patterns reflect underlying cognitive processes related to sentiment perception, and incorporating these patterns can improve sentiment classification, particularly for complex cases like sarcasm and irony.

The primary contribution is the novel exploration of cognitive features, specifically gaze data, within the context of sentiment analysis. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistic studies of sentiment, this paper is the first, to the best of my knowledge, to directly integrate this data into a feature-based sentiment classification system. A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration, through empirical results, that these cognitive features *do* provide a performance boost, particularly when combined with established sentiment features, and notably on datasets containing ironic/sarcastic text. A third contribution is the thoughtful analysis of the gaze data, deriving a range of features beyond simple fixation counts and durations, including graph-based representations of saccadic movements.

**Strengths:**

1. **High Novelty:** The core concept of leveraging cognitive features from eye-tracking for sentiment analysis is genuinely novel and exciting. Most sentiment analysis research focuses on refining linguistic features or deep learning architectures. This work breaks that mold and explores a fundamentally different signal – the human cognitive process itself. This boldness is highly commendable.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides empirical evidence, across two datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reported improvements, while not massive, are statistically significant and consistent across different classifiers. Crucially, the performance gains appear more pronounced on challenging datasets (those with sarcasm and irony), suggesting the cognitive features are capturing something that traditional methods miss.
3. **Feature Engineering Depth:**  The authors don’t simply throw raw eye-tracking data at the problem. They demonstrate a commendable effort in engineering a diverse set of cognitive features, including the innovative gaze-saliency graphs, indicating a deep understanding of the underlying cognitive processes.
4. **Clear Motivation and Justification:** The paper clearly articulates the hypothesis linking eye-movement patterns to sentiment processing. The connection to cognitive science principles (Parasuraman & Rizzo, 2006) strengthens the rationale for the approach.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors address the practical limitations of requiring eye-tracking data and discuss the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios, adding a layer of practicality to the research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Limitations:** The datasets are relatively small (994 and 1059 instances). While the authors acknowledge this, the generalizability of the findings is limited. Larger, more diverse datasets would strengthen the conclusions. The inter-rater agreement (Fleiss Kappa of 0.62 and 0.68) is moderate; higher agreement would increase confidence in the annotation quality.
2. **Methodological Rigor:** While the results are promising, the methodological depth could be improved. The choice of classifiers (NB, SVM, Multi-layer NN) is standard, but a more comprehensive evaluation with state-of-the-art deep learning models (even without incorporating the gaze data) would provide a stronger baseline.  The parameter tuning for the classifiers is mentioned as default Weka values, which may not be optimal.
3. **Feature Redundancy/Interaction:** The paper presents a large number of features, and it’s not entirely clear how these features interact with each other. A more detailed analysis of feature importance and potential redundancy would be beneficial. The Chi-squared tests are a step in the right direction, but a more thorough investigation is needed.
4. **Eye-tracker Discrepancy:** The datasets utilize different eye-trackers with significantly different sampling rates (500Hz vs. 120Hz). This introduces a potential source of noise and inconsistency in the cognitive features.  The impact of this discrepancy isn't fully addressed.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The error analysis section is brief. A more in-depth analysis of the types of errors made by the system, and *why* the cognitive features failed to correct them, would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific parameter tuning performed for the classifiers? Were any optimization techniques employed?
2. How would you characterize the potential impact of the differing eye-tracker sampling rates on the cognitive features and overall results?  Did you consider any normalization or weighting schemes to mitigate this discrepancy?
3. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of feature interactions and potential redundancy? For example, were there any highly correlated features that could be removed without significant performance loss?
4. What are the computational costs associated with extracting and incorporating these cognitive features?  Is this approach scalable to real-time applications?
5. Beyond the datasets used in this study, what other types of text (e.g., social media posts, news articles) do you believe would benefit most from this approach?



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some methodological weaknesses and data limitations, I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The novelty and boldness of the approach are significant. The paper introduces a genuinely new direction for sentiment analysis research, and the empirical results provide encouraging evidence that cognitive features can enhance performance. While further work is needed to address the limitations, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and has the potential to inspire further research in this exciting area. The authors successfully demonstrate that considering the *way* people read and process text, not just the text itself, can lead to improved sentiment understanding. I am willing to overlook the flaws in favour of this strong conceptual advance.
