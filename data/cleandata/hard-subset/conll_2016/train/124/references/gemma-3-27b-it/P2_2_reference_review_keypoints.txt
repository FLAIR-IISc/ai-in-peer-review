- Paper investigates adding eye-tracking derived cognitive features to sentiment models to better handle nuanced language (sarcasm/irony) and reports improved accuracy on two datasets.  
- Novel contribution: first to apply eye-tracking cognitive signals specifically to augment traditional sentiment features.  
- Empirical validation: cognitive features yield consistent F-score improvements across two datasets with statistical significance (McNemar test).  
- Evaluation uses datasets containing sarcasm and irony, making results more relevant to real-world nuanced sentiment.  
- Strong motivation and clear hypothesis linking cognitive processes and eye-movement patterns to sentiment detection.  
- Comprehensive feature set combining traditional sentiment, sarcasm/irony indicators, and novel cognitive features with detailed descriptions.  
- Error analysis and feasibility discussion (including mobile eye-trackers) demonstrate awareness of practical challenges and future directions.  
- Good coverage of related work in sentiment analysis, psycholinguistics, and eye-tracking.  
- Weakness: limited analysis of why specific cognitive features improve performance; deeper investigation of gaze-saliency patterns is needed.  
- Weakness: small dataset sizes (<1000) limit generalizability; scaling to larger, diverse datasets is necessary.  
- Weakness: standard classifier choices (NB, SVM, MLP); lack of exploration of advanced/deep learning models for joint text and eye-tracking representations.  
- Weakness: use of two different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000, Tobii T120) with varying sampling rates introduces potential noise that should be addressed.  
- Asks authors to clarify cognitive processes captured by key gaze features (e.g., fixation duration vs. lexical complexity).  
- Requests sensitivity analysis on gaze-saliency graph parameters (e.g., edge-creation thresholds).  
- Seeks ideas on integrating eye-tracking features with deep learning representation learning.  
- Queries whether differences in eye-tracker quality caused dataset performance discrepancies and how they were mitigated.  
- Additional: paper is well-written, well-referenced, with minor formatting issues.  
- Overall assessment: strong, novel paper recommended for acceptance with minor revisions to deepen cognitive feature analysis and address scalability and eye-tracker variability.
