This paper explores the incorporation of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, into sentiment analysis. The authors hypothesize that readers’ eye-movement patterns during sentiment annotation can provide valuable signals for improving sentiment classification, particularly for nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. They present experiments on two existing datasets, augmenting traditional sentiment features with gaze-based features, and demonstrate improvements in accuracy using SVM, NB, and a neural network.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the *attempt* to bridge the gap between psycholinguistic insights from eye-tracking and the practical task of sentiment analysis. While the idea isn’t entirely novel (related work acknowledges prior efforts), the specific combination of features and the reported performance gains are noteworthy. A secondary contribution is the curated datasets used; while publicly available, their application to evaluating the effectiveness of cognitive features is valuable. Finally, the detailed feature engineering – particularly the gaze-saliency graph representation – represents a significant undertaking.

**Strengths:**

1. **Methodological Rigor in Evaluation:** The authors demonstrate a reasonable experimental setup, employing cross-validation and comparison against baseline systems. Reporting results across multiple classifiers (SVM, NB, NN) strengthens the findings and suggests the improvements aren’t specific to a particular algorithm.
2. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The extensive feature set, encompassing traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting indicators, and numerous eye-tracking metrics, demonstrates a substantial effort to capture relevant linguistic and cognitive information.  The gaze-saliency graph approach, while complex, represents a novel way to represent eye-tracking data for NLP tasks.
3. **Clear Presentation of Results:** The tables presenting classification results are well-organized and easy to interpret. The inclusion of statistical significance testing (McNemar test) adds credibility to the reported improvements.
4. **Focus on Challenging Cases:** The selection of datasets known to pose difficulties for traditional sentiment analysis (containing sarcasm and irony) is appropriate, given the authors' hypothesis. The held-out dataset analysis further isolates the benefit of cognitive features on complex constructs.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Robust Baseline Comparison:** While baselines are included, they are relatively simple (NB, SVM with standard features, a rule-based system). The paper acknowledges more recent, and likely stronger, baselines utilizing deep learning methods (CNNs, RNNs). The lack of comparison against these state-of-the-art techniques severely limits the impact of the reported improvements. Demonstrating superiority over established deep learning approaches is crucial for justifying the added complexity of the cognitive feature approach.
2. **Overly Optimistic Interpretation of Results:** The improvements, while statistically significant, are modest in absolute terms. The authors repeatedly emphasize the “significant improvement,” but the F1-scores remain relatively low, especially for Dataset 1. A more cautious interpretation of the results is warranted.  The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the gains achieved.
3. **Practical Concerns Regarding Data Acquisition:** The core of the approach relies on eye-tracking data, which is expensive and intrusive to collect. While the authors address feasibility in Section 7, the discussion feels superficial.  The reliance on specialized hardware creates a significant barrier to wider adoption.  The discussion of mobile eye-trackers is promising but lacks concrete details about accuracy and reliability in real-world scenarios.
4. **Potential for Confounding Variables:** The eye-tracking data reflects *annotation* behavior, not necessarily inherent cognitive processing during *reading* for understanding. The annotators may be consciously or subconsciously employing different strategies when labeling sentiment compared to naturally reading text. This distinction could introduce biases in the derived cognitive features.
5. **Limited Generalizability:** The experiments are conducted on only two datasets, both relatively small.  It's unclear whether the observed improvements would generalize to other domains or types of text.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How do the results compare against state-of-the-art deep learning models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) fine-tuned for sentiment analysis on the same datasets? Please provide results.
2.  Could you elaborate on the potential biases introduced by collecting eye-tracking data during the *annotation* process, rather than during natural reading? How might these biases affect the interpretation of the cognitive features?
3.  What specific steps were taken to ensure the quality and reliability of the eye-tracking data, particularly given the use of two different eye-trackers with varying sampling rates and accuracy?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the specific parameters used in constructing the gaze-saliency graphs?  Did you perform any sensitivity analysis on these parameters?
5.  What is the computational cost of adding the cognitive features, and how does it impact the scalability of the approach?



**Additional Comments:**

The references are formatted correctly, but the inclusion of abbreviated author lists (e.g., "Agarwal et al., 2011") without full names is a minor stylistic issue. The discussion of related work is adequate but could be more critical, particularly regarding limitations of previous psycholinguistic approaches to sentiment analysis.
