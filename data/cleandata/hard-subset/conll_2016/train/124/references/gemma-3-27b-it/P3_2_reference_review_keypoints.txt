- Paper investigates using eye-tracking–derived cognitive features to improve sentiment analysis, showing gains on two datasets with SVM, NB, and NN and includes feature-significance and complex-vs-simple sentence experiments.
- Main contribution: novel integration of cognitive (eye-movement) features into standard sentiment pipelines as a proof-of-concept.
- Main contribution: careful selection and analysis of two datasets rich in sarcasm, irony, and implicit sentiment.
- Main contribution: detailed feature engineering combining linguistic, sarcasm/thwarting indicators, and novel graph-based gaze features.
- Strength: clearly motivated problem framing linking linguistic nuance to cognitive signals.
- Strength: comprehensive and thoughtfully designed feature set, with interesting graph-based gaze representations.
- Strength: rigorous experimental setup using multiple classifiers, 10-fold cross-validation, a held-out complex set, and statistical significance testing.
- Strength: feature significance analysis (chi-squared) identifies impactful cognitive features.
- Strength: authors discuss practical feasibility and potential for wider applicability with mobile eye-trackers.
- Weakness: limited generalizability due to relatively small datasets (994 and 1059 snippets) and domain bias toward reviews/tweets.
- Weakness: variability in eye-tracking hardware (Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120) and sampling rates is not adequately addressed or normalized.
- Weakness: shallow cognitive interpretation—insufficient linkage of significant eye-movement features to established psycholinguistic theory.
- Weakness: limited benchmarking against modern state-of-the-art deep models (e.g., Transformers/BERT), weakening claims of SOTA improvement.
- Weakness: averaging gaze data across participants may obscure valuable individual-level patterns.
- Question: were any normalizations or sensitivity analyses performed to account for different eye-trackers and sampling rates?
- Question: can the authors provide deeper theoretical explanations linking key gaze features to sarcasm/irony comprehension?
- Question: will the authors test integrating cognitive features with pre-trained models (e.g., BERT) for stronger baselines?
- Question: did the authors attempt retaining or modeling individual-level gaze data rather than averaged features?
- Question: what steps can improve generalizability to domains beyond reviews and tweets?
- Additional comments: writing and references are strong, feature abbreviation table is helpful, and adding gaze-saliency graph visualizations is recommended.
- Overall assessment: solid, novel contribution; recommend Accept with Minor Revisions pending responses to weaknesses and questions.
