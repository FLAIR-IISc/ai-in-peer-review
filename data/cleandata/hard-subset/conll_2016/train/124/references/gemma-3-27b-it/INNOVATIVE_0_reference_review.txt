This paper presents a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional linguistic features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data of human annotators. The central hypothesis – that readers’ eye movements reveal insights into the cognitive processes involved in sentiment perception – is intriguing and, if successful, could significantly advance the field. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating a measurable improvement in sentiment classification accuracy, particularly for nuanced text like ironic or sarcastic statements, through the incorporation of these cognitive features. A secondary contribution is the curated dataset used for evaluation, combining existing resources and focusing on challenging instances of sentiment expression.

**Strengths:**

1. **High Novelty:** The core idea of integrating eye-tracking data into sentiment analysis is genuinely novel. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistics and related NLP tasks, its application to *enhance* sentiment classification, rather than merely study annotation processes, is a bold and potentially impactful direction. This is a significant departure from the standard feature engineering approaches prevalent in the field.
2. **Demonstrated Improvement:** The paper shows consistent accuracy gains across multiple classifiers and datasets when cognitive features are added. The improvement, while not overwhelming, is statistically significant and suggests a real effect. The results on the held-out dataset, highlighting improved performance on complex constructs, is particularly encouraging.
3. **Careful Feature Engineering:** The authors have thoughtfully crafted a range of both traditional and cognitive features, going beyond simple aggregation of eye-tracking metrics to include graph-based representations of gaze patterns. This level of detail demonstrates a deep engagement with the data and a commitment to exploring the full potential of the cognitive signals.
4. **Clear Motivation & Justification:** The introduction effectively articulates the challenges in sentiment analysis and convincingly argues for the potential of cognitive features to address these limitations. The connection between eye movements and underlying cognitive processes is well-established and provides a solid theoretical foundation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The reliance on lab-based eye-tracking data poses a significant practical hurdle. The feasibility section attempts to address this with mobile eye-trackers, but widespread adoption of such technology remains uncertain. The method's efficacy outside of controlled settings is questionable.
2. **Lack of Deep Analysis of Cognitive Features:** While the authors demonstrate *that* cognitive features are helpful, they don’t provide a deeply insightful explanation of *why* specific features contribute to improved performance. The feature significance analysis is presented, but a more nuanced discussion connecting specific eye-movement patterns to sentiment perception mechanisms would be valuable. What, specifically, about regressions or fixation durations is indicative of sarcasm detection?
3. **Methodological Concerns Regarding Feature Aggregation:** The averaging of gaze data across participants to construct graph-based features potentially obscures individual differences in cognitive processing. This simplification might diminish the signal and introduce noise. A more sophisticated approach that accounts for inter-subject variability could be explored.
4. **Incomplete Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The authors acknowledge not comparing to deep learning approaches. While justified by the focus on feature-based methods, dismissing deep learning outright feels somewhat limiting. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss how a deep learning model might *also* incorporate these cognitive features.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific cognitive mechanisms you believe are being captured by the most informative eye-tracking features (e.g., what cognitive process is reflected in increased regression count during sarcastic text processing)?
2. Have you considered alternative methods for aggregating gaze data across participants (e.g., using hierarchical models or clustering techniques) that might preserve individual differences?
3. What are the computational costs associated with extracting and incorporating these cognitive features, and how might this impact the scalability of the approach?
4.  Can you discuss the sensitivity of your results to the quality and sampling rate of the eye-tracking equipment?  Dataset 2 uses a lower quality tracker; does this impact the performance difference observed between datasets?

**Additional Comments:**

The references are comprehensive. The writing is generally clear and concise. The structure of the paper is logical and easy to follow. The inclusion of the error analysis section is a positive sign of critical self-assessment.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the methodological limitations and practical concerns, I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The novelty of the approach, coupled with the demonstrated improvement in performance, outweighs the weaknesses. The work represents a potentially significant step towards a more nuanced and cognitively informed approach to sentiment analysis, and the authors’ willingness to tackle a challenging problem with a bold and innovative idea is commendable. Further research addressing the limitations outlined above will be crucial to realizing the full potential of this promising line of inquiry.
