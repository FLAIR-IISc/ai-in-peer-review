This paper explores the use of cognitive features – derived from eye-tracking data – to enhance sentiment analysis, particularly for nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The core contribution lies in demonstrating a measurable improvement in sentiment classification accuracy by incorporating these features alongside traditional linguistic ones. A secondary, though valuable, contribution is the curated dataset used for evaluation, combining existing resources with a focus on complex sentiment expressions. Finally, the investigation into feature significance, showing cognitive features *do* impact handling of irony/sarcasm, is a positive step.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea – integrating eye-tracking data into sentiment analysis – is genuinely novel. While eye-tracking has been used *around* sentiment analysis (e.g., to study annotation complexity), directly leveraging it as a feature set for *improving* sentiment classification is a significant departure and a bold attempt. I highly value submissions that explore unconventional avenues, and this fits the bill.
2. **Demonstrated Improvement:** The results, while not overwhelmingly large, consistently show accuracy gains across classifiers and datasets when cognitive features are added. This isn’t merely theoretical; the authors provide empirical evidence that the approach works. The statistical significance tests further strengthen this claim.
3. **Focus on Challenging Cases:** Explicitly targeting sarcasm and irony, areas where standard sentiment analysis falters, is commendable. The paper doesn't shy away from tackling difficult problems, which is a positive sign. The held-out dataset results, specifically showing greater improvement on complex examples, reinforce this.
4. **Reasonable Feasibility Discussion:** The authors don’t gloss over the practical challenges (requiring eye-tracking data) but address them thoughtfully, citing advances in mobile eye-tracking technology and potential application scenarios. This demonstrates a realistic understanding of the limitations and potential of their work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Methodological Rigor – Eye-tracking Setup & Generalizability:** While the core idea is compelling, the details of the eye-tracking data collection and preprocessing are somewhat sparse. The use of different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120) across datasets raises concerns about comparability and potential bias. The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough discussion of how these differences were mitigated (or their potential impact) is needed. Also, the generalizability of findings to diverse populations or reading environments is unclear.
2. **Feature Engineering Complexity & Interpretability:** The sheer number of cognitive features, especially those derived from the graph structure, is substantial. While the feature significance analysis helps, it's still difficult to intuitively understand *why* certain gaze patterns are indicative of sentiment. A more streamlined feature set, or a clearer explanation of the underlying cognitive principles driving feature selection, would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The authors acknowledge not comparing to deep learning approaches, citing the feature-based nature of their work. However, a brief discussion of *why* a feature-based approach is still valuable (e.g., interpretability, lower computational cost) or potential avenues for integrating cognitive features into deep learning models would strengthen the paper. While I’m inclined to favor novelty, dismissing relevant approaches entirely feels slightly limiting.
4. **Error Analysis Depth:** The error analysis section is brief. A deeper dive into *where* the system still struggles – specific types of ironic statements, ambiguous phrases – could provide valuable insights for future work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the steps taken to address the differences in eye-tracker hardware (sampling rate, accuracy) between the two datasets? Did you perform any normalization or calibration procedures?
2. The graph-based gaze features seem particularly complex. Can you provide more intuition behind why these features are expected to be predictive of sentiment? Are there specific graph properties that correlate strongly with sarcasm or irony?
3.  Given the increasing prominence of pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa), have you considered exploring ways to incorporate these cognitive features *into* such models, perhaps as additional input embeddings?
4. Can you provide specific examples of sentences where the inclusion of cognitive features led to a correct classification that a baseline system would have missed?



**Additional Comments:** The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow. The related work section is reasonably comprehensive. I appreciate the authors' willingness to explore a new direction in sentiment analysis. While methodological refinements are needed, the novelty and potential impact of this work are significant. I lean towards acceptance.
