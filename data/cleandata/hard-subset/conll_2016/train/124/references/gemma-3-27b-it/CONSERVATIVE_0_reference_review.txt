This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data, hypothesizing that readers' eye movements reflect cognitive processes related to sentiment understanding, particularly for nuanced expressions like sarcasm and irony. The core contribution is the exploration of this novel feature set and demonstration of improved performance on two datasets. A secondary contribution is the creation and use of gaze-saliency graphs to extract more complex cognitive features. Finally, the paper provides a detailed discussion of experimental setup and analysis, including a held-out dataset evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Potentially Valuable Exploration:** The idea of incorporating cognitive features, specifically eye-tracking data, into sentiment analysis is a novel and potentially valuable direction. While the underlying assumption requires careful validation, the exploration itself is commendable. The authors are attempting to move beyond purely linguistic cues, which is a sensible approach given the inherent limitations of traditional methods when dealing with complex sentiment expressions.
2. **Thorough Feature Engineering:** The paper demonstrates a significant effort in designing and implementing a diverse set of features, spanning traditional sentiment analysis components, sarcasm/thwarting related features, and the newly introduced cognitive features. The detailed description of each feature, including justifications for their inclusion, is a strength. The construction of gaze-saliency graphs adds another layer of sophistication.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluated their approach using multiple classifiers (NB, SVM, Multi-layer NN) and employed a 10-fold cross-validation scheme. The inclusion of a held-out dataset, specifically chosen to contain complex sentiment constructs, further strengthens the evaluation. The statistical significance testing adds rigor.
4. **Detailed Dataset Description:** The provided descriptions of Datasets 1 and 2 are adequate, including information on annotation accuracy and inter-rater agreement. The authors also appropriately contextualize the datasets as challenging for existing sentiment analysis systems.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The inclusion of a section discussing the feasibility of the approach, acknowledging the practical challenges of widespread eye-tracking data collection, and proposing potential solutions (e.g., mobile eye-trackers) demonstrates forward thinking.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvement & Limited Practicality:** While the reported performance improvements are statistically significant, the absolute gains are modest. The improvement over strong baselines is not substantial enough to justify the significant overhead of collecting and processing eye-tracking data. The practical deployment of this approach remains a major hurdle. The discussion of mobile eye-trackers is hopeful, but a substantial gap remains between current technology and widespread, unobtrusive data collection.
2. **Lack of Robust Baseline Comparison:** The baseline systems implemented and compared against (NB, SVM, Rule-Based) are relatively simple. The authors acknowledge the existence of more sophisticated deep learning approaches (CNNs, RNNs) but dismiss them without proper comparison. Demonstrating superiority over state-of-the-art deep learning models would have significantly strengthened the paper.
3. **Over-Reliance on Statistical Significance:** The paper heavily emphasizes statistical significance testing. While important, statistical significance alone doesn't guarantee practical significance. A more nuanced discussion of the effect size and real-world impact of the improvements is needed.
4. **Potential for Confounding Factors:** Eye movements are influenced by numerous factors beyond cognitive processing of sentiment, including reading ability, visual fatigue, and individual differences. The paper doesn't adequately address these potential confounding factors or provide sufficient evidence that the observed effects are specifically attributable to sentiment processing. The averaging of gaze data across participants might obscure important individual variations.
5. **Feature Selection Justification:** While the feature descriptions are detailed, the rationale behind *why* certain gaze-based features are expected to be informative is not always fully convincing. More theoretical grounding and justification are needed to support the selection of these features.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Have you considered incorporating attention mechanisms into a deep learning model, utilizing the eye-tracking data to guide the attention weights? This could potentially leverage the benefits of both approaches.
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of individual cognitive features on performance? Which features contribute the most, and are there any interactions between features?
3. How sensitive are the results to the quality and sampling rate of the eye-tracking data? Would the performance gains be maintained with lower-quality, more readily available eye-trackers?
4.  Could you elaborate on how the Fleiss Kappa values for the datasets were used to ensure data quality, and how the relatively low Kappa values may affect the validity of your results?
5. What specific measures were taken to control for potential confounding factors during data collection and analysis (e.g., reading speed, visual fatigue)?
