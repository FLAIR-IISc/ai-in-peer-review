This paper investigates the impact of incorporating cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, into sentiment analysis models. The authors hypothesize that readers’ eye-movement patterns can provide valuable information for resolving linguistic nuances and improving sentiment detection, particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. They evaluate their approach on two publicly available datasets, comparing performance with and without these cognitive features.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the novel exploration of integrating eye-tracking derived cognitive features with traditional sentiment analysis features. While eye-tracking has been used in related areas like annotation complexity measurement, the application to directly enhance sentiment classification accuracy is a noteworthy attempt. A secondary contribution is the creation and analysis of a comprehensive feature set combining textual, sarcasm-related, and cognitive features. The experiments provide empirical evidence supporting the hypothesis that cognitive features can improve sentiment classification, particularly in challenging cases. Finally, the analysis of feature significance provides insights into *which* cognitive features are most informative.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of leveraging cognitive features from eye-tracking for sentiment analysis is genuinely novel. Most existing sentiment analysis approaches rely solely on textual information. This work opens up a new avenue for exploration by incorporating insights into the *process* of how humans interpret sentiment.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors have meticulously designed a rich feature set, encompassing various aspects of textual analysis (sentiment lexicons, POS tags), sarcasm detection, and cognitive processes (fixation duration, saccade length, saliency graph features). This demonstrates a thoughtful and thorough investigation of potential factors influencing sentiment perception.
3. **Empirical Validation & Performance Gains:** The experiments demonstrate statistically significant improvements in sentiment classification accuracy when cognitive features are included, especially for the more challenging dataset containing sarcastic texts. The held-out experiment further supports the claim that cognitive features are beneficial for handling complex linguistic constructs.
4. **Detailed Analysis of Feature Significance:** The feature importance analysis provides valuable insights into which cognitive features are most predictive of sentiment, helping to understand *why* the approach works. Identifying features like regression count and saliency graph metrics as significant is particularly interesting.
5. **Discussion of Feasibility:** The paper proactively addresses the practical concerns surrounding the collection of eye-tracking data, discussing the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios. This demonstrates a pragmatic awareness of the challenges and opportunities associated with the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** The datasets used are relatively small (994 and 1059 instances). This limits the generalizability of the findings and may exaggerate the observed improvements. Larger, more diverse datasets would be needed to confirm the robustness of the approach.
2. **Eye-tracker Quality Variance:** The datasets utilize both a high-quality (Eyelink-1000) and a lower-quality (Tobii T120) eye-tracker. This inconsistency introduces a potential confounding factor, as the precision and reliability of the eye-tracking data may differ significantly between the two datasets. The impact of this difference is not sufficiently discussed.
3. **Aggregation of Eye-Tracking Data:** The authors acknowledge the loss of information when aggregating individual participant data to derive the saliency graph features. Exploring alternative approaches that preserve more individual-level information (e.g., using participant-specific features in the model) could potentially yield further improvements.
4. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Deep Learning Models:** While the authors justify their decision to focus on feature-based approaches, a brief comparison with current state-of-the-art deep learning models (e.g., transformers) would provide a more complete benchmark and highlight the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed method.
5. **Overly Detailed Feature Descriptions:** While the comprehensiveness of the feature set is a strength, the lengthy and detailed descriptions in Section 4 can be overwhelming and detract from the overall flow of the paper. A more concise presentation of the features would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How would the results change if the analysis were performed on the individual-level eye-tracking data, rather than aggregating it to create saliency graphs? Did you experiment with including participant ID as a feature?
2.  Given the varying quality of the eye-trackers used, did you perform any analysis to assess the impact of eye-tracker quality on the results? Could the observed performance differences between the datasets be partially attributable to this factor?
3.  Could you briefly discuss the computational cost associated with extracting and incorporating the cognitive features, particularly the saliency graph features?
4.   Would you expect the benefits of the proposed method to be similar across different languages? Are there any language-specific considerations that might affect the effectiveness of the cognitive features?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a visual illustration of the eye-tracking data and saliency graphs, beyond the example in Figure 1.
*   Consider providing a more intuitive explanation of the saliency graph features and their connection to cognitive processes.
*   The writing style is generally clear, but some sections are overly verbose and could be streamlined.



Overall, this is a promising and well-executed study that explores a novel and potentially impactful approach to sentiment analysis. While the limited dataset size and concerns about eye-tracker quality warrant further investigation, the findings provide compelling evidence for the benefits of incorporating cognitive features. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions outlined above.
