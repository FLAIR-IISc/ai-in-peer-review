This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data of human annotators. The core idea is that readers’ eye-movement patterns reflect cognitive processes related to sentiment understanding, particularly for complex linguistic constructs like irony and sarcasm. The authors present experiments on two datasets, demonstrating improved accuracy with the inclusion of these cognitive features.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that eye-tracking data can provide valuable signals for improving sentiment analysis, particularly in challenging cases. While the use of eye-tracking in NLP isn't entirely novel, applying it specifically to enhance sentiment analysis, especially for nuanced expressions like sarcasm, represents a meaningful extension. A second contribution is the curated dataset utilized, as it contains instances with higher-level nuances and associated eye-tracking data, which could be valuable for further research in this area. Finally, the exploration of both basic and complex gaze features, including the saliency graph representation, provides a reasonably comprehensive investigation into potential cognitive indicators.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Cognitive Features:** The core idea of leveraging cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data to enhance sentiment analysis is novel and promising. This departs from purely text-based approaches and attempts to incorporate a more human-centered perspective.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors explore a wide range of features, combining traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting indicators, and various eye-tracking metrics. This thoroughness is commendable, and the ablation studies (Table 2) clearly demonstrate the contribution of each feature set. The introduction of the saliency graph and associated features is a particularly interesting aspect.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on two datasets demonstrate a statistically significant improvement in accuracy with the inclusion of cognitive features. The held-out experiment (Table 4) further strengthens the claim that these features are particularly helpful for complex cases.
4. **Detailed Feature Analysis:** The chi-squared test-based feature significance analysis (Table 3) provides valuable insight into which eye-tracking features are most predictive of sentiment, supporting the hypothesis that specific cognitive processes are correlated with sentiment understanding.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors proactively address the practicality concerns related to data collection via eye-tracking, discussing the availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios, which strengthens the paper's impact.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The datasets used, while carefully curated, are relatively small (Dataset 1: 994 snippets, Dataset 2: 1059 snippets). This raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to larger and more diverse datasets. The performance gains might be specific to the characteristics of these particular datasets.
2. **Eye-Tracker Quality Variation:** The use of two different eye-trackers (SR-Research Eyelink-1000 and Tobii T120) with differing sampling rates introduces potential noise and inconsistencies in the cognitive features. This isn’t adequately addressed, and could affect the comparability of results across datasets.
3. **Aggregation of Eye-Tracking Data:** The authors acknowledge that averaging eye-tracking data across participants may lead to loss of information. While understandable for simplifying the model, it potentially masks individual variations in cognitive processing that could be valuable. Exploring individual-level models or more sophisticated aggregation techniques might yield further improvements.
4. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Deep Learning Methods:** While the authors mention deep learning approaches, they don't include a comparison with current state-of-the-art deep learning models for sentiment analysis. This limits the assessment of the true improvement offered by their approach.
5. **Potential for Confounding Factors:** The observed improvements with cognitive features could be partially attributable to factors other than cognitive processing, such as variations in reading speed or attention levels among participants. Controlling for these factors would strengthen the conclusions.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the steps taken to mitigate potential biases introduced by the different sampling rates of the two eye-trackers used?
2.  Have you explored any techniques for preserving individual-level information from the eye-tracking data (e.g., participant-specific models or clustering)?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the features derived from the saliency graph? Did you experiment with different graph metrics or edge weighting schemes?
4.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by the system, specifically identifying cases where the cognitive features were particularly helpful or misleading?
5.  Would you consider evaluating your approach on larger, more diverse datasets and comparing it against contemporary deep learning-based sentiment analysis models?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a visual illustration of the saliency graph and how it is constructed from the eye-tracking data.
*   Consider providing more context about the participants involved in the eye-tracking studies (e.g., demographics, linguistic background).
*   The organization of section 4 is slightly cumbersome. Re-ordering the subsections to be more logically sequenced would improve readability.
