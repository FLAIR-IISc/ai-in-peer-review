This paper attempts to improve sentiment analysis (SA) by augmenting traditional linguistic features with “cognitive features” derived from eye-tracking data collected during sentiment annotation. The core claim is that these cognitive features capture nuances in human reading processes related to sentiment, leading to improved accuracy, especially for complex cases like sarcasm and irony. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the exploration of incorporating eye-tracking data into a feature-based sentiment analysis pipeline, alongside a relatively comprehensive feature engineering effort. A secondary contribution is the presentation of two datasets (though previously published) with annotations and eye-tracking data suitable for this type of investigation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Feature Integration:** While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistic studies related to sentiment, its direct integration into a *feature-based* sentiment analysis system, alongside traditional features, represents a novel approach. The attempt to bridge cognitive science and NLP is commendable.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of existing SA features, encompassing lexical, syntactic, and sarcasm/thwarting-related elements. The extensive feature engineering, combined with the cognitive features, provides a rich input to the classifiers.
3. **Demonstrated Improvement:** The results, particularly the improvements observed with the full feature set (including cognitive features), are statistically significant and suggest that the approach holds promise. The improvement on the held-out dataset containing complex constructs further supports the hypothesis.
4. **Dataset Availability:** Providing access to the datasets, even if previously published, is a valuable contribution. The inclusion of eye-tracking data alongside sentiment labels is beneficial for the research community.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors thoughtfully address the practical limitations of requiring eye-tracking data, discussing the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios, indicating a pragmatic awareness of the challenges.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Baseline Depth:** While comparisons to basic SA systems are presented, the absence of comparisons against state-of-the-art deep learning models (as acknowledged by the authors) is a significant omission. This makes it difficult to assess the true magnitude of the improvement and whether the gains justify the complexity of the feature engineering and eye-tracking requirement. Simply stating “our approach is feature based” isn’t sufficient justification for excluding modern approaches.
2. **Overly Granular Feature Engineering:** The sheer number of features (4.6 section is extremely long) raises concerns about overfitting, despite the use of PCA. The justification for *each* feature, particularly within the cognitive feature set, is not always entirely convincing. The “complex gaze features” derived from graph structures appear particularly convoluted and require more robust justification. A more focused feature selection process would have strengthened the work.
3. **Inconsistent Data Quality & Eyetracker Differences:** The datasets utilize different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120) with significantly differing sampling rates (500Hz vs. 120Hz). This introduces potential noise and inconsistencies in the eye-tracking data, which are not adequately addressed. The differing annotation accuracy and inter-rater agreement also raise concerns about the reliability of the labels. A more thorough analysis of the impact of these inconsistencies is needed.
4. **Limited Generalizability of Cognitive Features:** The cognitive features are likely highly dependent on the specific annotation task and the annotators themselves. The assumption that these patterns will generalize to other sentiment analysis scenarios (e.g., product reviews, social media posts) without further validation is questionable. The dependence on the specific phrasing and context of the provided snippets is not sufficiently discussed.
5. **Statistical Significance vs. Practical Significance:** While statistical significance is reported, the *magnitude* of the improvements is often modest. A more thorough discussion of the practical implications of these improvements is needed. Is a small percentage point increase in accuracy worth the added complexity and cost of collecting eye-tracking data?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why were deep learning approaches deliberately excluded from the comparative evaluation? Could you provide a more detailed justification beyond simply stating that your approach is feature-based?
2.  Can you provide a more rigorous justification for the inclusion of *each* cognitive feature? What specific cognitive processes are these features intended to capture, and how do they relate to sentiment analysis?
3.  How did you address the potential confounding effects of the differing eye-tracker quality and sampling rates between the two datasets? Did you perform any analyses to assess the impact of these differences on the results?
4.  What steps were taken to ensure the generalizability of the cognitive features to other sentiment analysis tasks and datasets? Do you anticipate needing to retrain the cognitive feature models for new domains?
5.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically focusing on cases where the system *failed* to correctly classify sentiment despite the inclusion of cognitive features? What types of errors are most common, and what might explain them?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of Table 1 is slightly awkward.
*   The reference list is not consistently formatted.
*   Some sentences are verbose and could be more concise.
*   The writing quality generally needs polishing.



**Recommendation:**

Despite the novelty and potentially valuable contribution of this work, the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning models and the concerns about data quality and feature engineering complexity, lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the idea is interesting, the paper does not convincingly demonstrate a substantial and generalizable improvement over existing techniques, and the methodological concerns raise doubts about the robustness of the findings. A significant revision addressing these issues, including a more comprehensive evaluation and a more focused feature set, would be necessary for reconsideration.
