This paper presents a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional linguistic features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data of human annotators. The core idea—that readers’ eye-movement patterns can reveal insights into nuanced sentiment understanding, particularly for complex cases like sarcasm and irony—is intriguing and potentially valuable.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Integration of Eye-Tracking Data for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the successful integration of cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data into a sentiment analysis pipeline. This is a relatively unexplored area, and the authors demonstrate a measurable performance improvement through this integration.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors have developed a rich feature set, combining traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting-related features, and a novel set of cognitive features. The latter, including both basic gaze measures and more complex graph-based representations of eye movements, are particularly noteworthy.
3. **Empirical Validation & Analysis:** The authors rigorously evaluate their approach on two publicly available datasets, demonstrating statistically significant improvements over baseline systems. The feature significance analysis further supports the hypothesis that cognitive features contribute to better sentiment detection, especially in challenging cases.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Innovation:** The inclusion of cognitive features from eye-tracking data is a truly novel contribution to sentiment analysis. While psycholinguistic studies have used eye-tracking extensively, applying it directly to improve sentiment classification is a fresh perspective.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The experimental design is well-structured, employing standard classifiers (NB, SVM, NN) and a 10-fold cross-validation scheme. The use of two different datasets adds robustness to the findings.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors exhibit a strong understanding of both traditional sentiment analysis features and eye-tracking metrics. The creation of both basic and complex cognitive features demonstrates thoroughness and creativity.
4. **Statistical Significance:** The authors perform statistical significance tests (McNemar test) to support their claims, increasing confidence in the observed improvements.
5. **Clear Explanation of Methodology:** The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the experimental setup, feature engineering process, and results. This allows for reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Practicality Concerns & Scalability:** The reliance on eye-tracking data introduces significant practical limitations. While the authors discuss the potential for mobile eye-trackers, the cost and accessibility of such devices remain a barrier to widespread adoption. The scalability of this approach to large-scale sentiment analysis tasks is questionable.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** While the datasets used are publicly available and contain instances of complex sentiment expressions, their size is relatively small (994 and 1059 snippets). This could limit the generalizability of the results. A more extensive evaluation on larger, more diverse datasets is needed.
3. **Complexity of Cognitive Features:** The graph-based gaze features, while potentially informative, add significant complexity to the model. The justification for these features and their interpretability could be strengthened. It’s not immediately obvious why these specific graph properties are predictive of sentiment.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The error analysis section is brief and doesn't provide a detailed understanding of the types of errors the system makes. A more in-depth analysis, including examples of misclassified instances, would be valuable.
5. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Deep Learning Models:** The authors acknowledge the existence of deep learning-based sentiment analysis models but do not include them in the comparative evaluation.  A comparison against these more recent approaches would provide a better benchmark for the proposed method.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the inter-rater reliability for the eye-tracking data collection process? How was agreement between annotators assessed, and what steps were taken to address discrepancies?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of graph parameters (e.g., edge weighting, graph construction criteria) used to create the gaze-saliency graphs? Have you explored other graph representations?
3.  Given the computational cost of eye-tracking, what strategies could be employed to reduce the amount of eye-tracking data required while still maintaining performance? Could transfer learning be used to leverage eye-tracking data from a smaller set of annotators to improve performance on a larger, unlabeled dataset?
4.  Can you provide specific examples of sentences where the cognitive features are most beneficial in correctly classifying sentiment?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and organized.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of existing research in sentiment analysis and psycholinguistics.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the ethical implications of using eye-tracking data, particularly regarding privacy concerns.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising and innovative approach to sentiment analysis. The integration of cognitive features from eye-tracking data is a significant contribution, and the experimental results demonstrate its potential. While practical limitations and dataset size are concerns, the novelty and thoroughness of the work warrant serious consideration. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent upon addressing the weaknesses outlined above and responding to the questions posed.  Specifically, the authors should more thoroughly address the scalability concerns and provide a more compelling justification for the complexity of the cognitive features. A comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning methods would also strengthen the paper.
