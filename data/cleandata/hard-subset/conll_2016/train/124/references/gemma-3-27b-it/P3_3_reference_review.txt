## Review of "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of cognitive features – derived from eye-tracking data of human annotators – to improve sentiment analysis performance, particularly on texts with nuanced sentiment expressions like sarcasm and irony. The authors hypothesize that readers’ cognitive processes, reflected in their eye movements, can provide valuable signals for sentiment detection. They augment traditional linguistic features with these cognitive features and demonstrate improved accuracy across various classifiers and datasets.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Integration of Cognitive Data:** The primary contribution is the exploration and demonstration of the value of integrating eye-tracking derived features into a traditional sentiment analysis pipeline. This represents a shift towards incorporating psycholinguistic insights into NLP tasks.
2. **Empirical Validation on Challenging Datasets:** The authors utilize datasets specifically curated for containing complex sentiment expressions (sarcasm, irony) which provides a more rigorous evaluation than standard sentiment benchmarks. Showing improvement on these datasets is valuable.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering & Analysis:** The paper provides a comprehensive set of both traditional and novel (cognitive) features, alongside a feature significance analysis, providing insights into *which* cognitive signals are most informative for sentiment detection.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Hypothesis:** The rationale behind incorporating cognitive features is well-explained, grounded in the connection between eye movements and cognitive processes.  The hypothesis that eye-tracking can help resolve linguistic nuances is compelling.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The experimental design, using cross-validation and multiple classifiers, strengthens the validity of the results. The comparison against baseline systems using standard features is crucial for establishing the benefit of the proposed approach.
3. **Significant Results on Complex Cases:**  The improved performance, particularly on datasets containing sarcasm and irony, demonstrates the potential of cognitive features to address a known limitation of traditional sentiment analysis. The held-out dataset experiment further confirms this benefit.
4. **Detailed Feature Exploration:** The extensive feature set, and the analysis showing the importance of gaze-based features, is a valuable contribution. The effort to derive complex gaze features like saliency graphs is commendable.
5. **Consideration of Feasibility:** The discussion regarding the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential applications provides a pragmatic perspective on the real-world applicability of the research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The datasets used, while valuable for their complexity, are relatively small.  It’s unclear how well the observed improvements will generalize to larger, more diverse datasets.  Results on a larger-scale, standard benchmark *with* complex expressions would be valuable.
2. **Eye-tracking Data Quality & Variability:** The paper acknowledges differences in eye-tracker quality between the two datasets. The impact of this variability on the observed results isn’t thoroughly discussed.  Inter-subject variability in eye-tracking data is also a potential concern.  A deeper analysis of how eye-tracking data characteristics (e.g., calibration quality, sampling rate) correlate with performance could be beneficial.
3. **Complexity of Cognitive Features:** While detailed, the cognitive features are complex and potentially difficult to interpret. The saliency graph features, in particular, lack a clear intuitive explanation of *why* they contribute to better performance beyond “capturing complex relationships”.  More in-depth analysis connecting specific eye-movement patterns to sentiment interpretation would strengthen the findings.
4. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Deep Learning Methods:** The authors explicitly exclude deep learning methods from their comparison, citing a feature-based approach. However, modern deep learning models often learn relevant features automatically. A brief discussion of *why* the proposed feature-based approach might still be valuable alongside deep learning (e.g., interpretability, data efficiency) would be helpful.
5. **Statistical Significance Reporting:** While McNemar's test is used, a more comprehensive statistical analysis, including effect sizes, would provide a stronger understanding of the practical significance of the improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential impact of the differing eye-tracker quality between Dataset 1 and Dataset 2 on your results? Did you attempt to control for this difference in any way?
2.  The saliency graph features are complex. Can you provide more concrete examples of specific graph structures and how they relate to the detection of sarcasm or irony?  A visualization of a few example graphs alongside the corresponding text snippets would be helpful.
3.  You mention that feature significance analysis shows the importance of gaze features.  Could you provide more insights into *which* specific gaze features were most consistently important across both datasets and classifiers?
4.  How sensitive is your approach to the number of participants providing eye-tracking data?  Would the results hold if you had fewer annotators per snippet?
5.  Have you explored any dimensionality reduction techniques beyond PCA to simplify the feature space and potentially improve performance?




**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
* The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good overview of the existing literature.
* The acknowledgements section feels a little brief.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and interesting paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of sentiment analysis. The integration of cognitive features is a novel and promising approach, and the results demonstrate its potential. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding generalizability and the complexity of the cognitive features, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
