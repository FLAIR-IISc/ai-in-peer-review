- Paper proposes using eye-tracking–derived cognitive features in sentiment classifiers and reports improved performance, especially for sarcasm/irony, on two datasets.  
- Novel contribution: integrates eye-tracking cognitive features directly into sentiment classification, not just annotation studies.  
- Empirical validation: shows statistically significant gains (notably with SVM) on challenging sarcasm/irony datasets.  
- Feasibility discussion: argues future practicality due to advances in mobile eye-tracking.  
- Clear motivation: well-articulated problem definition focused on user-generated text challenges.  
- Comprehensive feature engineering: combines traditional sentiment, sarcasm/irony, and extensive cognitive features.  
- Statistically significant results: performance gains supported by McNemar tests.  
- Strong focus on complex constructs: targeted handling of sarcasm and irony with held-out experiments.  
- Practical concerns addressed: realistic discussion of data acquisition and mobile adoption.  
- Weakness: superficial analysis of which specific cognitive features drive improvements and why.  
- Weakness: limited classifier exploration—reliance on SVM and no experiments with deep learning/hybrid models.  
- Weakness: dataset size and moderate inter-annotator agreement raise generalizability concerns.  
- Weakness: averaging eye-tracking across participants may lose individual-level signal; alternative aggregations not explored.  
- Q: Were interaction effects between cognitive and traditional features analyzed?  
- Q: Are there notable individual differences in cognitive-feature effectiveness across participants?  
- Q: Did you test alternative aggregation methods for eye-tracking data beyond averaging?  
- Q: Can you provide concrete error analysis examples where cognitive features corrected baseline errors?  
- Q: How do you envision integrating cognitive features into deep learning architectures and what challenges exist?  
- Writing is clear and related work is well-covered; consider adding saliency graph visuals.
