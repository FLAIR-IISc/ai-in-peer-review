This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with “cognitive features” derived from eye-tracking data of human annotators. The core hypothesis is that readers’ eye-movement patterns reflect cognitive processes relevant to sentiment understanding, particularly for nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The authors evaluate their approach on two existing datasets, demonstrating improvements in sentiment classification accuracy compared to baseline methods.

The primary contribution is the exploration of eye-tracking data as a feature source for sentiment analysis, a relatively novel direction. A secondary contribution lies in the curated datasets used, which specifically contain instances with complex linguistic constructs (sarcasm, irony, thwarting), making them suitable for testing the proposed approach. Finally, the feature engineering, while largely standard, benefits from the addition of gaze-derived features and attempts at capturing saliency through graph construction.

**Strengths:**

1. **Potentially Valuable Direction:** The idea of incorporating cognitive features, specifically derived from eye-tracking, into sentiment analysis is intriguing. Traditional methods often struggle with subtleties of language, and tapping into the cognitive processes of human readers *could* offer a pathway to improved performance.
2. **Relevant Datasets:** The choice of datasets (D1 and D2) is a strength. Both datasets contain a significant proportion of instances with sarcasm and irony, presenting a genuine challenge for conventional sentiment analyzers. This selection justifies the focus on cognitive features aimed at handling these nuances.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of existing sentiment analysis features, systematically combining them with novel gaze-based features. The attempt to capture more complex relationships through graph-based representations is also commendable.
4. **Statistically Significant Results:** The reported improvements in F-scores, accompanied by statistical significance tests, provide some initial evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The observation of greater improvements on the more complex instances (Table 4) further strengthens this claim.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Robust Baseline Comparison:** The baselines used (NB, SVM, and a rule-based classifier trained on a different, larger dataset) are not sufficiently competitive. Critically, there is *no* comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning models (e.g., transformers, BERT) which are the current standard for sentiment analysis. This omission makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the reported improvements. A substantial performance gap exists between traditional machine learning methods and deep learning, and claiming improvement *over these* methods is crucial.
2. **Overly Complex Feature Engineering & Interpretability:** The cognitive feature engineering is extensive and somewhat opaque. While the rationale behind many features is provided, the overall complexity hinders interpretability.  It is unclear *which* cognitive features are most crucial for performance and *why*. The addition of graph-based features, while creative, further exacerbates this issue.
3. **Generalizability Concerns:** The study relies on relatively small datasets (994 and 1059 instances). The generalizability of the findings to other domains and text types is questionable.  The inter-rater agreement (Fleiss kappa of 0.62 and 0.68) while acceptable, isn’t particularly strong and raises concerns about the reliability of the sentiment labels themselves.
4. **Practicality Issues:** The requirement for eye-tracking data significantly limits the practical applicability of this approach. While the authors acknowledge this and discuss the potential of mobile eye-trackers, the widespread availability and user acceptance of such technology remain uncertain. The discussion of feasibility feels optimistic rather than grounded in reality.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How do the results compare to state-of-the-art deep learning models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) on the same datasets? Please provide a comparison or a justification for why such a comparison was not conducted.
2.  Can you perform an ablation study to determine the relative importance of different cognitive features? Which features contribute most significantly to the observed performance gains?
3.  The graph-based features seem computationally intensive. What is the computational overhead associated with these features, and how does it impact the scalability of the approach?
4.  The annotation accuracy varies (70-90% and 75-85%). What steps were taken to mitigate the impact of noisy labels on the results?
5.  Could the observed improvements be attributed to the eye-tracking process itself influencing annotation, rather than reflecting inherent cognitive differences in processing sentiment?



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear, but the paper could benefit from more concise explanations of the cognitive features and their underlying rationale. The organization is logical. The reference list is comprehensive.
