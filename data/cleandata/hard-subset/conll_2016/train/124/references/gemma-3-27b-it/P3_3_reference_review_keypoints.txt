- Paper explores using eye-tracking–derived cognitive features to improve sentiment analysis on nuanced text like sarcasm and irony.
- Authors augment traditional linguistic features with cognitive signals and report improved accuracy across classifiers and datasets.

Main contributions:
- Novel integration of cognitive (eye-tracking) data into a sentiment analysis pipeline.
- Empirical validation on datasets curated for complex sentiment (sarcasm, irony).
- Detailed feature engineering and analysis identifying which cognitive signals help sentiment detection.

Strengths:
- Strong, well-motivated hypothesis linking eye movements to linguistic nuance resolution.
- Rigorous experimental setup with cross-validation and multiple classifiers against baselines.
- Notable performance gains on complex-case datasets and held-out experiments.
- Extensive feature set and analysis, including inventive gaze-derived features like saliency graphs.
- Practical discussion of feasibility given growing availability of mobile eye-trackers.

Weaknesses:
- Limited generalizability due to relatively small, specialized datasets.
- Insufficient analysis of eye-tracker quality differences and inter-subject variability effects.
- Cognitive features (especially saliency graphs) are complex and lack clear interpretability.
- No comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning methods or discussion of trade-offs with feature-based approaches.
- Statistical reporting is limited; effect sizes and more comprehensive analyses are missing.

Questions for authors:
- How did differing eye-tracker quality between datasets affect results, and was it controlled for?
- Can you provide concrete examples/visualizations linking saliency graph structures to sarcasm/irony?
- Which specific gaze features were most consistently important across datasets and classifiers?
- How sensitive are results to the number of annotators providing eye-tracking data?
- Were other dimensionality-reduction techniques beyond PCA explored to simplify features?

Additional comments:
- Writing is clear and well-organized.
- Related work coverage is comprehensive.
- Acknowledgements section is brief.

Overall assessment:
- Solid, novel contribution with promising results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address noted weaknesses.
