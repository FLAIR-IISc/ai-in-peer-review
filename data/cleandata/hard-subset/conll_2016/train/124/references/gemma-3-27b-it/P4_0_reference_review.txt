This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional linguistic features with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data during sentiment annotation. The authors hypothesize that readers' eye-movement patterns provide insights into the cognitive processes involved in understanding sentiment, particularly in cases of complex constructs like irony and sarcasm. The work presents experiments on two datasets, demonstrating that incorporating cognitive features leads to improved accuracy over baseline sentiment analysis systems.

The primary contribution of this work is the exploration and demonstration of the utility of cognitive features, specifically those derived from eye-tracking data, in enhancing sentiment analysis performance. While eye-tracking has been used in related NLP tasks, its application to sentiment analysis in this manner – as a direct feature augmentation – appears to be novel. A secondary contribution lies in the curated datasets used for evaluation, which contain instances with higher-level nuances like sarcasm and thwarting, making them suitable for testing the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Finally, the detailed feature engineering, encompassing various textual and cognitive features, and the thorough experimental evaluation with statistical significance testing, add to the robustness of the study.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data to enhance sentiment analysis is innovative. It moves beyond traditional feature engineering by explicitly modeling the cognitive processes of sentiment understanding. The authors convincingly argue that eye-movement patterns can reveal insights into the nuances of sentiment perception, especially in complex cases.
2. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The paper presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation on two datasets, comparing the performance of systems with and without cognitive features. The results consistently show improvements in accuracy, particularly when the complete feature set is used. The statistical significance testing further strengthens these findings. The split evaluation on complex vs. non-complex sentences (Table 4) provides a compelling argument for the benefits of cognitive features in handling challenging sentiment expressions.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors have meticulously crafted a rich feature set encompassing traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting-related features, and a diverse range of cognitive features derived from both basic eye-tracking parameters and the proposed gaze-saliency graphs. This detailed feature engineering demonstrates a thoughtful approach to capturing relevant information from both linguistic and cognitive sources.
4. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates the motivation behind the work. The introduction effectively highlights the challenges in sentiment analysis and positions the proposed approach as a potential solution. The explanations of the datasets, features, and experimental setup are thorough and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Practical Limitations:** The requirement for eye-tracking data poses a significant practical limitation. While the authors discuss the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential applications, the cost and complexity of acquiring this data remain substantial barriers to widespread adoption.
2. **Complexity of Cognitive Features:** The cognitive features, particularly those derived from the gaze-saliency graphs, are relatively complex and may lack interpretability. While they contribute to improved performance, understanding *why* these features are effective remains somewhat opaque. A deeper analysis of the relationship between specific cognitive features and sentiment perception would be valuable.
3. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The authors acknowledge that they did not compare their approach to deep learning methods. Given the recent advances in deep learning for sentiment analysis, a comparison, even a limited one, would provide a more complete understanding of the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach. Even a discussion of why deep learning approaches were excluded would be helpful.
4. **Potential for Participant Bias:** The eye-tracking data is collected from a limited number of annotators. There’s a potential for inherent biases in how individuals interpret and annotate sentiment, which could influence the extracted cognitive features. While the authors mention inter-rater agreement, exploring the variability in eye-movement patterns across different annotators would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of extracting and incorporating the gaze-saliency graph features? Does this add significant overhead to the overall sentiment analysis pipeline?
2. How sensitive are the results to the parameters used in constructing the gaze-saliency graphs (e.g., edge thresholds, weighting schemes)? Have you performed a sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of these features?
3. While you mention the limitations of comparing to deep learning methods, could you discuss how the cognitive features might be integrated into a deep learning framework in future work?
4. What measures were taken to mitigate potential participant biases in the eye-tracking data collection process? Did you observe any significant variations in eye-movement patterns across annotators, and if so, how did you address them?

**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and concise, but some sentences are a bit verbose and could be streamlined.
* The formatting of Table 1 could be improved for better readability.
* The reference list is extensive and well-organized.
