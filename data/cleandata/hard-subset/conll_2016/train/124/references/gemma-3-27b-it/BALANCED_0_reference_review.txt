This paper explores the incorporation of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, into sentiment analysis models. The authors hypothesize that readers’ eye-movement patterns reflect cognitive processes that can improve sentiment detection, particularly for nuanced expressions like sarcasm and irony. They augment traditional sentiment features with these cognitive features and demonstrate performance improvements on two datasets.

The primary contribution of this work is the novel integration of eye-tracking derived cognitive features into a feature-based sentiment analysis pipeline. While eye-tracking has been used in related areas like annotation complexity assessment, its application to directly enhance sentiment classification is a valuable addition. The second contribution lies in the curated datasets used for evaluation. These datasets, specifically chosen for containing complex linguistic constructs, provide a challenging benchmark for assessing the efficacy of the proposed approach. Finally, the extensive feature engineering, encompassing traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting related features, and the novel cognitive features, demonstrates a thorough exploration of potential predictive signals.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The central idea of leveraging cognitive features obtained from eye-tracking data for sentiment analysis is original and potentially impactful. This moves beyond purely linguistic features and attempts to incorporate a psychological dimension into the analysis, offering a potentially richer representation of sentiment expression.
2. **Rigorous Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate a comprehensive feature engineering process, considering a wide range of linguistic and cognitive cues.  The inclusion of features specifically designed to capture sarcasm and irony (e.g., implicit incongruity) alongside eye-tracking metrics strengthens the model's ability to handle complex cases.  The detailed description of each feature category, including the rationale behind their selection, is commendable.
3. **Well-Chosen Datasets:** The use of Datasets 1 and 2, which contain a significant proportion of sarcastic and ironic text, is a strength. The authors appropriately acknowledge that standard sentiment analysis systems struggle with these datasets, justifying the need for an approach that can better handle such nuances. The dataset descriptions are sufficiently detailed, including information on annotation accuracy and inter-rater agreement.
4. **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate improvements in performance with the addition of cognitive features, across both datasets and different classifiers. The statistical significance testing further supports the validity of these findings. The held-out experiment specifically targeting complex constructs provides further evidence of the benefits of the proposed approach.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors proactively address the practical limitations of their approach, particularly the need for eye-tracking data.  The discussion of mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios provides a realistic outlook on the future adoption of this technology.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The datasets used are relatively small (994 and 1059 instances). While appropriate for demonstrating proof-of-concept, the generalizability of the results to larger and more diverse datasets is uncertain. The datasets are also limited in their domain coverage, primarily consisting of reviews and tweets.
2. **Complexity of Cognitive Features:** While the detailed description of the cognitive features is appreciated, their interpretability is limited. The derivation of graph-based features, in particular, is complex and may obscure the underlying reasons for performance improvements. Further analysis is needed to understand which specific cognitive features contribute most to the gains in accuracy.
3. **Data Aggregation Loss:** As acknowledged by the authors, aggregating eye-tracking data across participants and averaging the features can lead to a loss of individual variation and potentially valuable information. Exploring methods to incorporate participant-specific eye-movement patterns could further improve performance.
4. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to baseline systems is somewhat limited. While the authors demonstrate that existing systems struggle with the datasets, a more thorough comparison to recent state-of-the-art deep learning models for sentiment analysis would provide a stronger benchmark for evaluating the proposed approach.  The authors acknowledge the existence of these models but explicitly exclude them from the comparison.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis Detail:** While the authors mention error analysis, the details are sparse. A more detailed analysis of specific error cases (e.g., examples where the model fails to correctly identify sarcasm) would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and potential areas for improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific challenges associated with incorporating participant-specific eye-movement patterns into the model? What methods have you considered or plan to explore to address this?
2.  The paper mentions using a held-out dataset to evaluate performance on complex constructs. Can you provide more details about the selection process for this dataset? Was it manually curated to specifically include sarcastic/ironic examples, or was it a random subset of the original datasets?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the specific parameters used in the eye-tracking data processing (e.g., fixation duration thresholds, saccade velocity thresholds)? Did you perform any sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of the results to these parameters?
4.  You mention that the approach may be useful for other NLP tasks. Could you briefly outline how the cognitive features could be adapted and applied to a different task, such as emotion recognition or text summarization?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of Table 1 could be improved for readability. Consider using a different layout or font size.
*   The reference list appears incomplete; please ensure all cited works are included.
*   A visual representation of the gaze-saliency graph (Figure 2) would benefit from a clearer explanation of the nodes and edges.



Overall, this paper presents a compelling and innovative approach to sentiment analysis. The integration of cognitive features from eye-tracking data is a significant contribution, and the experimental results demonstrate the potential benefits of this approach. While there are some limitations regarding generalizability and interpretability, the authors have conducted a thorough investigation and provided a thoughtful discussion of the challenges and opportunities associated with this research direction.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions raised above.
