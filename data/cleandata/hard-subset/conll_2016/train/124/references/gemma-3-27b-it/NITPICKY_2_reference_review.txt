This paper proposes leveraging cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, to improve sentiment analysis, particularly for nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The core contribution is the augmentation of traditional sentiment analysis features with these gaze-based features, demonstrating performance improvements on two existing datasets. A secondary contribution lies in the exploration of gaze-saliency graphs as a means of encoding cognitive information. A final, less significant contribution is the curated datasets used, though these datasets themselves aren’t novel, their selection for complexity is noted.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The integration of cognitive features, specifically eye-tracking data, into sentiment analysis is a genuinely novel idea. While psycholinguistic studies have used eye-tracking, applying it to enhance sentiment classification represents a fresh perspective.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides empirical evidence, through experiments on two datasets, that the augmented feature set improves sentiment classification accuracy. The reported gains, while not massive, are statistically significant.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors meticulously describe a comprehensive feature set, encompassing traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/irony indicators, cognitive features, and readability metrics. The granularity and thoroughness of this feature engineering are commendable.
4. **Error Analysis & Feasibility Discussion:** The inclusion of an error analysis section and a discussion on the feasibility of the approach demonstrate a thoughtful consideration of the limitations and practical implications of the work. The acknowledgement of the requirement for mobile eye-tracking technology and the consideration of consent issues adds to the paper’s credibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Statistical Significance vs. Practical Impact:** While statistically significant, the reported accuracy improvements (around 3-9%) are relatively modest. The paper lacks a robust discussion of whether these gains justify the substantial overhead of collecting eye-tracking data. A more thorough cost-benefit analysis is needed.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The datasets, while acknowledged as containing nuanced text, are relatively small (under 1000 samples). This limits the generalizability of the findings. The Fleiss Kappa values, while acceptable, are not exceptionally high, raising concerns about the reliability of the sentiment annotations.
3. **Overly Complex Feature Set & Redundancy:** The sheer number of features (over 40) raises concerns about potential redundancy and overfitting, despite the PCA used for dimensionality reduction. A more rigorous feature selection process or ablation study, demonstrating the contribution of *each* cognitive feature, is crucial. The introduction of gaze-saliency graphs adds further complexity without a clear justification for its superior performance.
4. **Lack of Baseline Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The paper compares against relatively simple baselines (NB, SVM, Rule-Based). It does acknowledge deep learning approaches but dismisses them as out-of-scope. A comparison against a more recent and powerful deep learning model specifically designed for sentiment analysis (e.g., BERT-based models) is essential to establish the true value of the proposed approach.
5. **Formatting & Presentation Issues:** The paper contains several minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of abbreviations, awkward phrasing). The table presenting classification results lacks clarity; the column headers are abbreviated and require looking back in the text to understand. Some references are incomplete.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed cost-benefit analysis of using eye-tracking data for sentiment analysis, quantifying the costs of data collection and the value of the accuracy improvement?
2.  What specific feature selection methods were explored, and why were the final features chosen? Can you provide an ablation study demonstrating the individual contribution of each cognitive feature?
3.  How do the performance improvements vary across different types of complex constructs (e.g., sarcasm, irony, thwarted expectations)?
4.  How were the datasets curated to ensure the presence of complex sentiment expressions? What measures were taken to mitigate potential biases in the annotations?
5.  Can you provide a comparison of your approach against a state-of-the-art deep learning model for sentiment analysis?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list should be carefully checked for completeness and consistency.
*   The table formatting should be improved for clarity.
*   Consider a more concise writing style; some sections are unnecessarily verbose.
*   The phrase “to the best of our knowledge” is overused and should be removed unless it’s a truly unique contribution.



**Recommendation:** Reject. While the paper presents an interesting and novel idea, the modest improvements, the complexity of the approach, the lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods, and the numerous minor flaws outweigh the potential benefits. A substantial revision, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, would be necessary for reconsideration.
