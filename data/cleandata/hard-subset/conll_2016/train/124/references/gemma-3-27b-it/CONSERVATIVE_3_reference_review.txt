This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with “cognitive features” derived from eye-tracking data of annotators. The core claim is that these cognitive features, reflecting human reading processes, improve sentiment classification, particularly for nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The authors present experiments on two existing datasets, demonstrating improvements over baseline classifiers.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the *attempt* to integrate cognitive data into a sentiment analysis pipeline. While the idea isn’t entirely novel (related work acknowledges prior psycholinguistic applications of eye-tracking), applying it specifically to enhance sentiment detection, particularly for difficult cases, represents a point of investigation. A secondary contribution is the release of the feature set and experimental results, which may serve as a starting point for future research, though the datasets themselves are publicly available.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Real Challenge:** Sentiment analysis *does* struggle with nuance, sarcasm, and irony. The authors correctly identify these as key limitations of traditional approaches. Attempting to incorporate human cognitive processes to address these limitations is a logical direction.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate a commendable effort in feature engineering, combining established sentiment features with a rich set of eye-tracking derived features, and textual features relating to reading difficulty. This thoroughness, even if the impact of all features isn't equal, is a positive aspect.
3. **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors provide empirical results, showcasing performance gains (albeit modest) through the inclusion of cognitive features. The statistical significance test strengthens these claims. Reporting results on two datasets is also good practice.
4. **Discussion of Feasibility:** The paper thoughtfully considers the practical challenges of acquiring eye-tracking data and proposes potential solutions related to the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers. This demonstrates a realistic understanding of the potential barriers to adoption.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty & Justification for Feature Selection:** The core idea of leveraging cognitive data isn't groundbreaking. The justification for *which* specific eye-tracking metrics were chosen feels somewhat arbitrary. The connection between individual metrics (e.g., edge density of a saliency graph) and the underlying cognitive processes relevant to sarcasm/irony detection is not sufficiently explained. It feels like a “kitchen sink” approach to feature selection, rather than a theoretically driven selection process.
2. **Modest Performance Gains:** While statistically significant, the performance improvements are relatively small. A gain of a few percentage points in F-score, while welcome, does not represent a substantial leap forward in the field. This raises questions about the practical impact of adding the complexity and cost associated with eye-tracking data acquisition.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The comparison to baseline methods (NB, SVM, and a rule-based system) feels weak. The field has moved significantly toward deep learning methods (as the authors acknowledge), and a comparison to a state-of-the-art CNN or RNN model would be crucial to properly assess the value of the proposed approach. Claiming improvement over “the state of the art” without including a comparison against modern deep learning baselines is misleading.
4. **Data Dependence and Generalizability:** The datasets used are relatively small and specific. The performance gains may not generalize to other types of text or domains. The datasets themselves contain relatively high error rates in annotation (Fleiss kappa of 0.62 and 0.68), which could influence results.
5. **Overly Complex Cognitive Features:** The introduction of "gaze-saliency graphs" and associated features seems unnecessarily complex. The added computational burden and interpretability challenges likely outweigh the marginal benefit. A simpler set of cognitive features might have been more effective and easier to analyze.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical motivation behind the selection of each eye-tracking metric as a potential indicator of sentiment or sarcasm?
2. How do the results change when comparing your approach against strong deep learning baselines (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa fine-tuned for sentiment analysis)?
3. Have you explored any feature selection methods to identify the most informative cognitive features, rather than including all of them?
4. How sensitive are your results to the quality of the eye-tracking data (e.g., sampling rate, calibration accuracy)?
5. Can you elaborate on the potential biases introduced by the limited size and specific characteristics of the datasets used?



**Additional Comments:** The reference list appears incomplete. Some cited papers are missing full publication details. The formatting and writing quality are generally acceptable, but some sentences are convoluted and could benefit from simplification.
