This paper proposes to enhance sentiment analysis (SA) by incorporating cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data during sentiment annotation. The core hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns reflect cognitive processes relevant to understanding sentiment, particularly in nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The authors leverage two publicly available datasets with both sentiment labels and eye-tracking data, and demonstrate improvements in classification accuracy by augmenting traditional linguistic features with these novel cognitive features.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are: (1) the novel integration of eye-tracking-derived cognitive features into a sentiment analysis framework; (2) the demonstration of performance improvements, particularly on datasets containing complex linguistic constructs, through this augmentation; and (3) the provision of a curated dataset (combined from Mishra et al., 2016 and Joshi et al., 2014) with both sentiment labels and eye-tracking data, offering a valuable resource for future research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The incorporation of cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data is a genuinely novel approach to sentiment analysis. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistic studies of language processing, its application to directly improve SA performance is a significant contribution. This moves beyond purely textual features and attempts to model the cognitive processes involved in sentiment perception.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide empirical evidence, through extensive experimentation, supporting their hypothesis. The reported improvements in F-scores on both datasets, particularly with the complete feature set, demonstrate the potential of this approach. The statistical significance testing (McNemar test) strengthens these claims.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The paper provides a detailed and well-structured description of the features used, categorized into sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting features, cognitive features, and reading difficulty features. The explanation of the gaze-saliency graph and derived features is particularly commendable, showcasing a thoughtful approach to extracting meaningful information from the eye-tracking data.
4. **Resource Contribution:** The use of publicly available datasets with eye-tracking data is valuable. By demonstrating the effectiveness of this data for SA, the authors encourage further research and facilitate reproducibility. The careful curation and description of these datasets is a strong point.
5. **Analysis of Feature Importance:** The chi-squared test-based feature significance analysis provides insights into which cognitive features are most predictive of sentiment, helping to validate the underlying hypothesis and guide future feature engineering efforts.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Practicality Concerns:** The reliance on eye-tracking data is a significant limitation. While the authors address the potential for mobile eye-trackers to become more accessible, the current requirement for specialized hardware limits the scalability and real-world applicability of the approach. The discussion of user consent is valid but doesn’t fully address the practical challenges.
2. **Complexity and Interpretability:** The large number of features, particularly the gaze-derived features, makes the model complex and potentially difficult to interpret. While the feature engineering is detailed, understanding the specific contribution of each feature to the overall performance can be challenging. A more focused feature selection or dimensionality reduction technique might improve interpretability.
3. **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The comparison to baseline systems is somewhat limited. While the authors demonstrate that the selected datasets pose challenges to existing systems, the baselines used (NB, SVM, and a rule-based classifier) are relatively simple. Comparing against more sophisticated state-of-the-art deep learning models, even if not directly comparable due to the feature-based approach, would provide a stronger benchmark.
4. **Dataset Bias:** The datasets used, while publicly available, may have inherent biases that could influence the results. For example, the datasets were created using different eye-trackers with varying sampling rates. This could potentially introduce noise and affect the generalizability of the findings.
5. **Error Analysis Depth:** The error analysis section is brief and lacks detailed examples of specific errors made by the system. Providing more concrete examples of misclassifications, along with a discussion of the underlying reasons, would strengthen the analysis and offer insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with extracting and incorporating the cognitive features? This would provide a better understanding of the practical feasibility of the approach.
2. How sensitive are the results to the parameters used in the gaze-saliency graph construction? Did you explore different parameter settings, and if so, how did they affect performance?
3. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the system? For example, are there specific types of sarcasm or irony that are particularly difficult to detect?
4. How do the results vary across different participants in the eye-tracking studies? Is there significant inter-subject variability in the effectiveness of the cognitive features?

**Additional Comments:**

* The formatting of Table 1 could be improved for better readability.
* The discussion of related work is somewhat brief, especially regarding recent advances in deep learning for sentiment analysis.
* Consider adding a section discussing the ethical implications of using eye-tracking data, particularly regarding privacy and data security.
