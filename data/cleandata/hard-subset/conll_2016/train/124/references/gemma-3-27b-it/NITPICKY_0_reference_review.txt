## Review

This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with "cognitive features" derived from eye-tracking data, aiming to improve sentiment detection, particularly for nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The authors claim a significant accuracy improvement over state-of-the-art methods and demonstrate that these cognitive features empower sentiment analyzers to handle complex constructs. 

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Exploration of Eye-Tracking Data for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the initial investigation into the utility of eye-movement patterns as features for sentiment classification. While eye-tracking has been used in related areas (annotation complexity, WSD), its application *directly* to sentiment analysis is a novel step.
2. **A Comprehensive Feature Set:** The paper provides a reasonably thorough combination of traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/irony indicators, and the novel cognitive features, offering a solid baseline for future research.
3. **Demonstration of Improvement:** The results show a consistent (though not massive) improvement in accuracy when cognitive features are added, suggesting a promising avenue for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The use of two datasets with varying characteristics (Dataset 1 containing more sarcasm, Dataset 2 more standard reviews) provides a reasonably robust evaluation. The 10-fold cross-validation is a good practice.
2. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors clearly define and justify each feature, both traditional and cognitive. The inclusion of graph-based features derived from eye-tracking is a particularly interesting aspect, demonstrating thoughtful consideration of how to represent the data.
3. **Statistical Significance Testing:** The inclusion of McNemar's test to validate the improvement in results is commendable and strengthens the claim of significant findings.
4. **Thorough Related Work:** The related work section is extensive and provides a good overview of existing sentiment analysis techniques and prior eye-tracking research.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The section addressing the feasibility of the approach, acknowledging the current limitations of eye-tracking technology but highlighting the potential of mobile eye-trackers, is a pragmatic and valuable addition.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvement:** While statistically significant, the magnitude of the improvement (around 3-9%) is relatively small, raising questions about the practical impact of adding eye-tracking data. The cost and complexity of collecting this data may outweigh the benefits for many applications.
2. **Limited Generalizability:** The datasets used are relatively small and specific. The results might not generalize to other domains or types of text. The datasets are also somewhat imbalanced, which may affect performance.
3. **Overly Complex Feature Set:** The sheer number of features (over 60) raises concerns about overfitting and interpretability. While feature selection techniques were used (PCA), a more rigorous feature selection or dimensionality reduction approach could be beneficial. The feature significance analysis (Table 3) is not entirely convincing, as many features have similar chi-squared values.
4. **Eye-Tracker Discrepancy:** The use of two different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120) with significantly different sampling rates raises a potential confounding factor. The authors don't adequately address how this difference might impact the results. The performance discrepancies between the datasets may partially stem from this hardware difference.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis Detail:** The error analysis section is too brief. A more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the system, particularly in cases where cognitive features *failed* to improve performance, would be insightful.
6. **Formatting and Presentation:** There are numerous minor formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of abbreviations, varying font sizes). The table captions are not always clear and complete. The writing style is occasionally verbose and repetitive.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the cost (in terms of data collection and processing) associated with acquiring and utilizing eye-tracking data? How does this cost compare to the accuracy gain achieved?
2. How sensitive are your results to the different sampling rates of the two eye-trackers used? Did you perform any analysis to mitigate the potential impact of this discrepancy?
3. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, specifically focusing on examples where the addition of cognitive features did *not* improve performance? What patterns do you observe in these cases?
4. Have you considered using more advanced feature selection techniques (e.g., recursive feature elimination) to reduce the dimensionality of your feature set?
5. Could you discuss the potential limitations of using averaged gaze data (as in the graph-based features) and explore alternative approaches that might better capture individual differences in eye-movement patterns?



**Additional Comments:**

* The abbreviation “AoI” is used in Figure 1 without being defined in the text.
* The reference list is not formatted consistently (e.g., some entries include “et al.” while others don't).
* Several citations are missing year information (e.g. “Liu and Zhang”).
* The text often uses phrases like "as far as we know" which weaken the claims. It would be preferable to state a comprehensive literature review was conducted.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the novel approach and rigorous experimentation, the limited magnitude of the improvement, the potential generalizability issues, and the numerous minor flaws lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the work is valuable as an initial exploration of eye-tracking data for sentiment analysis, it does not yet present a compelling case for its practical adoption. The authors should address the weaknesses outlined above and conduct more extensive experiments on larger and more diverse datasets before resubmitting. A major revision addressing these concerns is necessary.
