This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data during sentiment annotation. The central hypothesis is that readers' eye-movement patterns reflect cognitive processes relevant to understanding sentiment, especially in nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The authors demonstrate improved accuracy on two datasets by incorporating these cognitive features, alongside sentiment and sarcasm-related features, using SVM, NB, and a multi-layer neural network.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the *novelty of integrating cognitive features—specifically, eye-tracking data—into the sentiment analysis pipeline*. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistic studies related to sentiment, its direct application as features *for* sentiment classification is a significant departure from established approaches.  The paper clearly articulates the rationale for this approach – linking cognitive processes to observable eye movements – and provides a reasonably thorough exploration of relevant features. A second contribution is the curated datasets used, as they are claimed to be particularly challenging for traditional sentiment analyzers due to the presence of irony, sarcasm, and thwarted expectations. The exploration of gaze-saliency graphs, while complex, represents a further attempt to extract meaningful cognitive signals.

**Strengths:**

1. **High Novelty:** The core idea of incorporating cognitive features from eye-tracking data into sentiment analysis is highly innovative.  Most sentiment analysis research focuses on lexical, syntactic, or stylistic features. This work opens a new avenue for exploring the cognitive underpinnings of sentiment understanding.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The authors demonstrate consistent improvements in accuracy across different classifiers and datasets. While the improvements aren't massive, they are statistically significant and support the hypothesis that cognitive features provide valuable information. The held-out experiment further strengthens this finding.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors have gone to considerable lengths to define and extract a rich set of cognitive features, including both basic gaze metrics and more complex features based on gaze-saliency graphs. This demonstrates a thoughtful approach to translating eye-tracking data into usable features.
4. **Clear Motivation & Rationale:** The paper clearly explains *why* eye-tracking data might be relevant to sentiment analysis, grounding the approach in established cognitive science principles. The connection between eye movements and cognitive processing is well-articulated.
5. **Addressing Feasibility:** The authors proactively address the practical challenges of obtaining eye-tracking data, discussing the growing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential applications in real-world scenarios. This shows awareness of the limitations and potential for future work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The datasets used are relatively small and specific.  While the authors argue they are challenging, their limited size raises concerns about the generalizability of the results to other domains or types of text. The reliance on two datasets only makes the conclusions less robust.
2. **Eye-tracker Quality Discrepancy:** The use of different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000 and Tobii T120) with varying sampling rates introduces a potential source of noise and inconsistency in the cognitive features. The difference in quality is acknowledged, but a more thorough discussion of its potential impact is needed.
3. **Feature Interaction Complexity:** The large number of features, including cognitive, sentiment, and sarcasm-related features, makes it difficult to isolate the specific contribution of each feature. While feature significance analysis is presented, a more in-depth analysis of feature interactions would be valuable.
4. **Methodological Rigor (Minor):** While the statistical tests are reported, a more comprehensive error analysis, perhaps categorizing the types of errors made by the system, would enhance the paper. The discussion of error analysis feels somewhat cursory.
5. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper acknowledges the existence of deep learning approaches to sentiment analysis but dismisses them for being feature-independent. While a direct comparison might be beyond the scope, a brief discussion of how the proposed approach *complements* or differs from deep learning methods would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific cognitive processes that you believe are reflected in the gaze-saliency graph features? Which aspects of reading complexity do these features capture?
2.  How sensitive are your results to the parameters used in the construction of the gaze-saliency graphs? Have you explored different parameter settings?
3.  Given the varying quality of the eye-trackers used, did you attempt to normalize the data or account for the differences in sampling rates? If so, how?
4.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by the system (e.g., misclassifying sarcasm, failing to detect subtle sentiment)?
5.  What are your plans for exploring the integration of cognitive features with deep learning models for sentiment analysis?
