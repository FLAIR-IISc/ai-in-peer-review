This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data of annotators. The core idea is that readers’ eye-movement patterns reveal cognitive processes that can improve sentiment detection, particularly for complex linguistic constructs like irony and sarcasm. The authors evaluate their approach on two publicly available datasets and report improvements in accuracy compared to baseline systems.

The primary contribution of this work is the exploration of cognitive features, specifically those derived from eye-tracking data, as a means to enhance sentiment analysis performance, especially for nuanced text. While the idea of incorporating cognitive data into NLP is not entirely novel (as evidenced by the related work section), applying it specifically to sentiment analysis, and demonstrating its effectiveness, constitutes a valuable addition. A secondary contribution lies in the curated datasets used for experimentation, which contain instances with inherent complexities such as sarcasm and irony, making them suitable for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The detailed feature engineering, encompassing both traditional and cognitive aspects, represents a third contribution, offering a comprehensive feature set for sentiment analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Cognitive Feature Integration:** The primary strength is the innovative incorporation of cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data into a sentiment analysis pipeline. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistics and related NLP tasks, its application to sentiment analysis and the demonstrable improvement in performance is a significant contribution. This moves beyond purely lexical or syntactic features.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The authors demonstrate a commendable effort in creating a comprehensive feature set, combining established sentiment analysis features with novel cognitive features. The categorization of features (Sentiment, Sarcasm/Thwarting, Cognitive, Reading Difficulty) provides a clear and organized approach. The inclusion of graph-based features from eye-tracking data represents a particularly interesting extension.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides empirical evidence supporting the benefits of the proposed approach. The results on the two datasets, along with the statistical significance testing, indicate a consistent improvement in accuracy when cognitive features are included. The held-out dataset analysis further strengthens the claim that cognitive features are particularly helpful for complex text.
4. **Detailed Experimentation:** The authors performed extensive experiments with different classifiers (NB, SVM, Multi-layer NN) and feature combinations, providing a thorough evaluation of the proposed method. The ablation study, implicitly presented through the different feature combinations, helps to isolate the contribution of the cognitive features.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors acknowledge the practical challenges related to data acquisition (eye-tracking) and proactively address them by discussing the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios. This demonstrates a realistic understanding of the limitations and future directions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability of Eye-tracking Data:** A key weakness is the potential for variability in eye-tracking data across individuals and contexts. The study relies on data from a limited number of annotators, and it’s unclear how well the derived cognitive features would generalize to a broader population or different types of text. The datasets are limited in size as well.
2. **Complexity and Interpretability:** The introduction of a large number of cognitive features, particularly those derived from the saliency graph, increases the complexity of the model and may hinder interpretability. While the authors attempt to explain the rationale behind these features, it is not always clear how they contribute to the overall performance.
3. **Feature Engineering Dependency:** The performance gains are heavily dependent on the specific feature engineering choices made by the authors. The process of selecting and combining features appears somewhat ad-hoc, and it’s unclear how robust the approach is to different feature engineering strategies.
4. **Lack of Comparison with Deep Learning Models:** The authors explicitly state that they do not compare their approach with deep learning models, which have achieved state-of-the-art results in sentiment analysis. This limits the ability to assess the relative benefits of the proposed cognitive features in the context of current best practices.
5. **Dataset Limitations:** While the datasets contain challenging examples, they are relatively small in size. Larger and more diverse datasets would be needed to further validate the generalizability of the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the number of annotators used for eye-tracking data collection? Would the performance change significantly with a larger or smaller sample size?
2. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the process used to select and combine the cognitive features? Were any feature selection techniques employed?
3. How do the cognitive features interact with the traditional sentiment features? Can you identify any specific feature interactions that are particularly important for performance?
4. Have you experimented with different methods for aggregating eye-tracking data across participants (e.g., using participant-specific models instead of averaging)?
5. Do you have any plans to explore the integration of cognitive features into deep learning models for sentiment analysis?

**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear and well-organized. The related work section is comprehensive. The inclusion of tables and figures enhances the presentation of the results. The reference list is extensive. Consider adding discussion around potential biases in the annotation data and how these might influence the eye-tracking results.
