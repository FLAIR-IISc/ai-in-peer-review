This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data, hypothesizing that readers’ eye-movement patterns can improve sentiment detection, particularly for nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The primary claimed contribution is a novel feature set incorporating cognitive data, and subsequent performance improvements on two datasets. A secondary contribution is demonstrating the relevance of cognitive features for handling complex linguistic constructs.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of integrating eye-tracking data into sentiment analysis is genuinely novel. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistic studies related to language understanding, its application to *improving* sentiment classification accuracy is a valuable exploration.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of existing sentiment analysis features and meticulously combine them with a diverse set of cognitive features, including graph-based representations of gaze data. The attempt to extract meaningful cognitive signals from eye-tracking data is commendable.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides experimental results on two datasets, demonstrating consistent performance improvements when cognitive features are included, particularly with SVM. The inclusion of a held-out dataset to further demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach for complex texts is also a positive aspect.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The feature significance analysis, though not entirely conclusive, provides some initial support for the hypothesis that cognitive features contribute to better handling of irony and sarcasm. The table showcasing top features is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Eye-Tracking Methodology Discussion:** While the authors mention the eye-trackers used, there is a critical lack of detail regarding the experimental setup. Critical parameters like calibration procedures, inter-pupillary distance measurements, data cleaning methods (e.g., blink detection and removal), and subject demographics are absent. The differing quality of the eye-trackers (500Hz vs 120Hz) used for the two datasets is acknowledged but not fully addressed in terms of potential impact on the results.
2. **Overly Complex Feature Set & Dimensionality Reduction Justification:** The feature set is extremely large and complex. The use of PCA for dimensionality reduction is mentioned, but the rationale for choosing a fixed dimensionality of 500 is unclear. A more rigorous feature selection or ablation study would be necessary to determine the true contribution of each cognitive feature and justify their inclusion. Redundancy between features is a significant concern.
3. **Limited Statistical Significance Testing:** The McNemar test is only applied to a subset of the results. A more comprehensive statistical analysis, including paired t-tests or ANOVA, should be performed to rigorously demonstrate the significance of the observed improvements. The p-values for feature significance are not reported, making it difficult to assess the reliability of the analysis.
4. **Dataset Limitations & Generalizability:** The datasets used, while containing instances of sarcasm and irony, are relatively small. The Fleiss kappa values, while acceptable, are not exceptionally high, suggesting potential inconsistencies in the annotations. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to other datasets and real-world scenarios.
5. **Feasibility Discussion Lacks Depth:** The discussion of feasibility relies heavily on the future availability of inexpensive mobile eye-trackers. A more nuanced discussion of the practical challenges, such as data privacy concerns, user burden, and the cost of data collection and annotation, is needed.
6. **Formatting and Typos:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and typos are present throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of abbreviations, missing periods, grammatical errors). These detract from the overall professionalism of the submission. (e.g. “D1 66.15 66 66.15” in Table 1, inconsistent hyphenation).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a detailed description of the eye-tracking experimental setup, including calibration procedures, data cleaning methods, subject demographics, and justifications for the chosen sampling rate.
2.  What criteria were used to select the dimensionality of 500 for PCA? Can you provide an ablation study demonstrating the contribution of different cognitive features?
3.  Provide a more comprehensive statistical analysis of the results, including p-values and effect sizes, to rigorously demonstrate the significance of the observed improvements.
4.  Discuss the potential impact of the differing quality of eye-trackers used for the two datasets on the reported results.
5.  Address the potential privacy concerns associated with collecting eye-tracking data from users in real-world applications.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are not formatted consistently.
*   The section numbering appears to have an error (Section 7 is not referenced in the main text).
*   The discussion of related work could be more focused and critical.




**Recommendation:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents a novel idea and demonstrates some promising results, the significant methodological flaws, lack of rigor in the statistical analysis, dataset limitations, and numerous minor errors outweigh the contributions. The authors need to address the weaknesses outlined above before this work can be considered for publication. A major revision, addressing the methodological concerns and providing more robust empirical evidence, would be required.
