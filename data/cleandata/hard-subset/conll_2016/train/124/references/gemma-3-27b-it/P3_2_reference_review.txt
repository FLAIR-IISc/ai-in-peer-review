## Peer Review: Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis

**Summary of the Paper:**

This paper investigates the incorporation of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data during sentiment annotation, into standard sentiment analysis models. The authors hypothesize that eye-movement patterns reflect underlying cognitive processes that can improve sentiment detection, especially in nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. They augment traditional linguistic features with these cognitive features and demonstrate performance improvements on two publicly available datasets using SVM, Naive Bayes, and a Multi-layer Neural Network. They also present a feature significance analysis and a dedicated experiment focusing on complex vs. simpler sentence structures.

**Main Contributions (as I see them, ordered by significance):**

1. **Novelty in Feature Integration:** The primary contribution is the exploration and demonstration of the value of integrating cognitive features (derived from eye-tracking) into a traditional sentiment analysis pipeline.  This is a relatively unexplored area, and the work provides a proof-of-concept demonstrating potential benefits.
2. **Dataset Utilization & Analysis:** The paper leverages two existing datasets specifically chosen for their complexity (presence of sarcasm, irony, and implicit sentiment). The thorough analysis of these datasets and their inherent challenges adds value.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:**  The authors define a comprehensive feature set, encompassing traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting-related indicators, and a detailed set of eye-tracking-derived features, including novel graph-based representations. This level of detail is commendable and allows for a granular understanding of feature importance.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges in sentiment analysis, particularly those related to linguistic nuance, and convincingly motivates the use of cognitive features as a potential solution.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The extensive feature engineering is a significant strength. The authors thoughtfully consider various aspects of both linguistic and cognitive data, providing a rich representation for the classifiers. The exploration of graph-based gaze features is particularly interesting.
3. **Rigorous Experimental Evaluation:** The paper utilizes multiple classifiers (NB, SVM, NN) and a robust 10-fold cross-validation methodology. The inclusion of a held-out dataset focused on complex constructs adds confidence to the results. The statistical significance testing is appropriate.
4. **Feature Significance Analysis:** The chi-squared test for feature importance provides valuable insights into which cognitive features are most impactful, bolstering the argument for their effectiveness.
5. **Discussion of Feasibility:**  The authors proactively address the practical challenges of acquiring eye-tracking data and discuss potential avenues for wider applicability with the advent of mobile eye-trackers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability & Dataset Bias:** The datasets are relatively small (994 and 1059 snippets) and may not fully represent the diversity of real-world text.  The heavy reliance on reviews and normalized tweets could introduce bias.  Results need to be cautiously extrapolated to other domains.
2. **Eye-Tracking Setup Variability:** The datasets utilize different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120) with different sampling rates. This disparity in data quality could introduce noise and affect the consistency of the cognitive features. The impact of this variability is not adequately discussed.  Were any attempts made to normalize the data or account for differences in tracking accuracy?
3. **Depth of Cognitive Interpretation:** While the paper identifies statistically significant cognitive features, the *interpretation* of *why* these features are effective is somewhat superficial.  The connection between specific eye-movement patterns (e.g., high regression count) and the cognitive processes involved in understanding sarcasm/irony could be explored in greater detail, linking back to established psycholinguistic theory.  Simply stating they “intuitively relate” is insufficient.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper focuses on comparison with relatively simple baseline systems (NB, SVM, Rule-Based).  A more thorough comparison with current state-of-the-art deep learning models (e.g., Transformers, BERT) would provide a stronger benchmark for evaluating the contribution of cognitive features. While the authors acknowledge these approaches, excluding them weakens the claim of "significant improvement over the state of the art."
5. **Averaging of Individual Gaze Data:** As mentioned in the paper itself, averaging gaze data across participants could lose valuable individual-level insights.  Exploring methods for incorporating individual differences in eye-movement patterns could yield further improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Eye-Tracker Variability:** Could you elaborate on the potential impact of using different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000 and Tobii T120) with varying sampling rates on the derived cognitive features? Did you perform any normalization or sensitivity analysis to assess the robustness of your findings to this variability?
2. **Cognitive Interpretation:** Can you provide a more in-depth discussion of the cognitive processes underlying the significant eye-tracking features? How do these features relate to established models of sarcasm/irony comprehension or sentiment processing?
3. **Deep Learning Comparison:**  Would you be willing to experiment with integrating your cognitive features into a pre-trained language model (e.g., BERT) to evaluate its performance against state-of-the-art sentiment analysis systems?
4. **Individual vs. Averaged Data:** Did you explore any techniques for retaining individual-level eye-tracking information instead of relying solely on averaged features? If so, what were the results?
5. **Generalizability Across Domains:**  What steps could be taken to improve the generalizability of your approach to different domains beyond reviews and tweets?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   The references are comprehensive.
*   The inclusion of a table summarizing feature abbreviations is helpful.
*   Consider adding a visualization of the gaze-saliency graphs in the main paper.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and potentially impactful contribution to the field of sentiment analysis. While some limitations exist, the novelty of the approach, the comprehensive feature engineering, and the rigorous experimental evaluation make this paper worthy of acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work and enhance its impact. I recommend **Accept with Minor Revisions**, contingent upon addressing the questions and incorporating the suggested improvements.
