- Proposes augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking to capture readers’ processing signals, especially for sarcasm/irony.  
- Demonstrates improved classification accuracy across classifiers and datasets when adding cognitive features, with statistical significance.  
- Designs a rich set of cognitive features including graph-based saccade representations beyond simple eye-tracking metrics.  
- Well-motivated hypothesis grounded in psycholinguistics that eye movements reflect cognitive processes relevant to sentiment.  
- Combines traditional sentiment, sarcasm-specific, and cognitive features, including reading-difficulty measures, for a multifaceted representation.  
- Robust evaluation using two datasets, multiple classifiers (NB, SVM, NN), statistical tests, and a held-out experiment targeting complex constructs.  
- Clear and detailed presentation of features and experimental setup, aiding replicability.  
- Authors discuss feasibility and practical issues, noting emerging mobile eye-tracking solutions.  
- Datasets are small and domain-specific (reviews, tweets), raising concerns about generalizability to other genres and larger corpora.  
- Uses two different eye-trackers with varying sampling rates; potential hardware-induced variability is not fully addressed.  
- Large feature set with possible redundancy/collinearity; lacks thorough feature correlation or selection analysis.  
- Mechanism by which cognitive features improve detection (which eye patterns indicate sarcasm/irony) remains opaque and under-visualized.  
- No comparison to state-of-the-art deep learning models or clear discussion of complementarity with modern NLP approaches.  
- Asks about dataset representativeness and whether other public datasets were tested for generalizability.  
- Requests details on calibration/standardization, filtering, and smoothing applied to reconcile different eye-tracker data.  
- Seeks concrete examples and visualizations linking specific cognitive features (e.g., regressions, edge density) to sarcasm/irony instances.  
- Inquires whether feature redundancy analysis was performed and what the results were.  
- Questions computational cost and scalability of extracting/incorporating cognitive features (time, memory).  
- Notes the reference list is extensive and well-formatted but suggests a more concise related-work discussion and updating older citations.  
- Recommends clearer initial expansion of abbreviations (e.g., FDUR, FC) for readability.  
- Overall assessment: innovative, well-evaluated paper with practical potential; merits acceptance with minor revisions focusing on generalizability and feature analysis.
