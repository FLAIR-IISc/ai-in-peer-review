## Review for "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data of human annotators, to improve sentiment analysis performance, particularly for nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The authors augment traditional sentiment analysis features with these cognitive features and demonstrate improved accuracy on two existing datasets. They highlight the potential of understanding the *process* of sentiment perception, rather than solely relying on the text itself.

**Main Contributions (as I see them, ordered by strength):**

1. **Novel Exploration of Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the demonstration that eye-tracking data can provide valuable signal for sentiment analysis. While eye-tracking has been used in related areas (annotation complexity, WSD), applying it directly to enhance sentiment *classification* is a novel direction.
2. **Empirical Validation on Challenging Datasets:** The authors use datasets specifically chosen for their complexity (sarcasm, irony), making the results more meaningful than if tested solely on straightforward sentiment examples. The datasets, though existing, are appropriate for the research question.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of both traditional and novel cognitive features, with a thoughtful rationale behind their selection. The development of gaze-saliency graphs is particularly interesting.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional sentiment analysis approaches when dealing with linguistic nuances and provides a compelling motivation for incorporating cognitive factors.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experimental design is well-structured, utilizing cross-validation and comparing performance against baseline systems. The inclusion of statistical significance testing strengthens the claims.
3. **Demonstrated Improvement:**  The results show consistent improvements in F-score by incorporating cognitive features, especially in complex cases. The held-out experiment provides further evidence of the benefits.
4. **Detailed Feature Analysis:** The feature significance analysis, while perhaps not deeply insightful, does provide some support for the idea that cognitive features are indeed contributing to the model’s ability to handle complex constructs.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors thoughtfully address the practical concerns regarding the availability of eye-tracking data and propose plausible scenarios for future implementation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** While the *application* to sentiment analysis is novel, the cognitive features themselves largely rely on established eye-tracking metrics (fixation duration, saccade length, etc.). The saliency graph is the most novel component, but its impact isn’t overwhelmingly large.
2. **Data Limitations:** The datasets, while appropriate for the task, are relatively small. The Fleiss kappa values indicate moderate inter-annotator agreement, which could introduce noise into the data.  A larger, more consistently annotated dataset would strengthen the findings.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study on Gaze Features:** The paper does not perform a detailed ablation study of the gaze features themselves. It would be beneficial to understand *which* specific cognitive features are most impactful, and whether some are redundant or even detrimental.  For example, are all the features derived from the saliency graph truly necessary?
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The authors acknowledge the existence of deep learning approaches for sentiment analysis but do not compare their results against them.  While they justify this decision, a brief comparison (even with limitations) would provide valuable context.
5. **Superficial Error Analysis:** The error analysis section is very brief and doesn't provide much insight into the types of errors the system is making and where it could be improved.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Ablation Study:** Can you provide an ablation study demonstrating the individual contribution of different gaze-based features (e.g., removing the saliency graph features, removing regression-based features)?
2. **Data Analysis:** Could you elaborate on the sources of disagreement among annotators in the datasets? Does the eye-tracking data reveal any patterns related to these disagreements?
3. **Saliency Graph Interpretation:** Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of *why* the saliency graph features are helpful for sentiment analysis? Are there specific graph properties that are strongly correlated with sentiment polarity?
4. **Deep Learning Comparison:** While you acknowledge the existence of deep learning approaches, can you provide a brief discussion of why a comparison was not included, and what challenges would be involved in incorporating gaze data into a deep learning framework?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise, although some sections (e.g., describing the feature set) are quite dense.
*   The referencing is comprehensive.
*   The figures could be improved in terms of clarity and visual appeal.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and interesting paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of sentiment analysis. While the core method isn’t revolutionary, the application of cognitive features to this problem is novel and promising. The experimental results are encouraging, and the authors provide a thoughtful discussion of the limitations and potential future directions. I recommend **acceptance**, pending satisfactory responses to the questions raised above. The weaknesses are addressable through further experimentation and analysis, and the overall contribution warrants publication.
