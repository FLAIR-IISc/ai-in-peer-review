## Review for "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data of human annotators, to improve sentiment analysis performance. The authors hypothesize that readers’ eye-movement patterns contain information about the cognitive processes involved in sentiment understanding, particularly for nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. They augment traditional sentiment analysis features with these cognitive features and demonstrate improved accuracy on two datasets containing challenging text snippets.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Application of Eye-Tracking Data:**  The most significant contribution is the exploration of using eye-tracking data *directly* as features for sentiment analysis. While eye-tracking has been used to *study* sentiment annotation and complexity, this paper takes the step of incorporating the data itself into a classification pipeline.
2. **Demonstrated Improvement on Challenging Datasets:** The paper shows a statistically significant improvement in accuracy, particularly on datasets specifically designed to contain sarcasm and irony, suggesting the cognitive features are most beneficial for complex sentiment expressions.
3. **Feature Importance Analysis:** The analysis identifying which cognitive features are most important provides insight into *how* eye-movement patterns relate to sentiment processing – a valuable contribution to understanding the cognitive underpinnings of sentiment.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Hypothesis:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation for incorporating cognitive features, grounding it in the relationship between eye-movements and cognitive processes. The hypothesis is logical and well-supported by references to psycholinguistic literature.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:**  The authors present a rich feature set, combining traditional linguistic features with a well-defined set of cognitive and reading difficulty features. The inclusion of the graph-based gaze features is particularly interesting and potentially insightful.
3. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The use of two publicly available datasets, cross-validation, and comparison with baseline systems strengthens the validity of the findings. The statistical significance testing is also a positive aspect.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis & Feasibility Discussion:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach and discuss the feasibility of obtaining eye-tracking data in real-world scenarios, which demonstrates a pragmatic understanding of the challenges.
5. **Strong Results:** The observed improvements in performance, especially on datasets containing sarcasm and irony, are compelling and support the core hypothesis.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability/Scalability:** The reliance on having eye-tracking data for each text snippet presents a significant barrier to scalability and real-world deployment. The discussion of mobile eye-trackers is positive, but widespread adoption remains a challenge.
2. **Data Dependency & Annotation Quality:**  The performance is highly dependent on the quality and consistency of both the sentiment annotations *and* the eye-tracking data. While Fleiss Kappa is reported, further analysis of annotator agreement and the potential impact of noisy eye-tracking data would be valuable.  The difference in eye-tracker quality between the two datasets (500Hz vs 120Hz) isn't fully addressed regarding its potential impact.
3. **Feature Redundancy & Intercorrelation:** The feature set is quite large. A more detailed analysis of feature correlation and potential redundancy could lead to a more parsimonious and interpretable model. It's unclear how much each individual cognitive feature contributes *independently* to the improvement, beyond the overall set.
4. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Deep Learning Models:** The authors explicitly exclude deep learning approaches, citing their feature-based approach. However, a brief discussion of *why* this choice was made and a comparison to the performance of recent deep learning models on the same datasets would strengthen the paper’s position.  Even acknowledging the computational cost of adding eye-tracking data to a deep learning framework would be useful.
5. **Limited Depth in Cognitive Interpretation:** While the feature importance analysis is helpful, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the *cognitive interpretations* of the significant features. For example, what does a high regression count specifically indicate about the cognitive processing of sarcastic text?



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Data Quality:** Could you provide more details on the quality control procedures used for both the sentiment annotations and the eye-tracking data? Were there any specific strategies for handling noisy or unreliable eye-tracking data points?
2.  **Feature Correlation:** Have you conducted an analysis of the correlation between the different features, particularly between the cognitive features and the traditional linguistic features? Did this analysis inform any feature selection or dimensionality reduction techniques?
3.  **Dataset Differences:** Given the difference in eye-tracker quality between Dataset 1 and 2, did you investigate whether this impacted the performance of the cognitive features on each dataset?
4. **Explainability:** Can you provide concrete examples illustrating how specific cognitive features (e.g., high regression count) help the model correctly classify sarcastic or ironic text that a traditional model might misclassify?
5. **Future Work:**  You mention exploring deep learning.  What are the biggest challenges you foresee in integrating these cognitive features into a deep learning framework (beyond computational cost)?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The inclusion of a table summarizing the classification results is very helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and interesting paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of sentiment analysis.  The novel application of eye-tracking data as features shows promise, and the demonstrated improvements on challenging datasets are encouraging. While the scalability concerns are legitimate, the authors acknowledge these limitations and provide a thoughtful discussion of potential solutions. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
