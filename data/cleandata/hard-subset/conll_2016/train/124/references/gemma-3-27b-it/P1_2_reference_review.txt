## Peer Review for "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of incorporating cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data of human annotators, into sentiment analysis (SA) systems. The authors argue that these features, reflecting human cognitive processes during sentiment assessment, can improve SA accuracy, particularly in handling complex linguistic constructs like sarcasm and irony. They evaluate their approach on two existing datasets, comparing performance against baseline SA systems and demonstrating a statistically significant improvement using SVM, NB, and a multi-layer neural network. The core contribution lies in exploring a novel feature space for SA, grounding it in human cognitive behavior, and demonstrating its effectiveness.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Feature Engineering:** The primary contribution is the exploration and successful integration of cognitive features (derived from eye-tracking data) into the SA pipeline. This moves beyond traditional linguistic features and attempts to model human sentiment perception.
2. **Empirical Validation on Challenging Datasets:** The paper provides empirical evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of these cognitive features on datasets specifically curated for their complexity (presence of sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations).
3. **Demonstration of Impact on Complex Constructs:** The authors present evidence suggesting that the inclusion of cognitive features specifically improves the performance of sentiment analysis on texts containing sarcasm and irony, supporting their initial hypothesis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** Combining eye-tracking data with sentiment analysis is a relatively unexplored area, and this paper provides a valuable initial exploration. The idea of leveraging cognitive processes for better sentiment understanding is compelling.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors employ a standard experimental setup including cross-validation and comparison with baseline systems, lending credibility to their findings. The use of two different datasets helps to generalize the results.
3. **Statistically Significant Results:** The reported improvements in F-score are statistically significant, lending support to the claim that the cognitive features contribute meaningfully to the performance of the SA systems.
4. **Detailed Feature Description:** The paper provides a comprehensive description of the features used, including both traditional and novel cognitive features, which allows for reproducibility and further research. The inclusion of gaze-saliency graph features is particularly interesting.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors address the practical limitations of their approach (requirement of eye-tracking data) and present a reasonable discussion of how this limitation could be overcome with the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability of Eye-tracking Setup:** The two datasets utilize different eye-trackers (high quality vs. low quality). The differing tracking quality might introduce variability and affect the cognitive features derived. A thorough analysis of how eye-tracker quality influences feature performance is missing.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study Detail:** While the results demonstrate an overall improvement with all features combined, the paper lacks a detailed ablation study showcasing the individual contribution of each cognitive feature (or groups of features). This would help identify which cognitive signals are most impactful.
3. **Feature Redundancy:** The large number of features (both traditional and cognitive) raises concerns about potential redundancy and overfitting, despite the use of PCA. A more rigorous feature selection or dimensionality reduction technique beyond PCA could be explored.
4. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the paper compares against simple baseline systems, it explicitly avoids comparing to recent deep learning approaches (CNNs, RNNs) that have achieved state-of-the-art results in sentiment analysis. This limits the contextualization of their improvement.  A discussion of *why* these approaches were excluded (beyond the feature-based vs. learned features argument) would strengthen the paper.
5. **Subjectivity in Eye-Tracking Interpretation:** Linking specific eye-movement patterns to cognitive processes is inherently subjective. While the authors cite relevant psycholinguistic literature, a more nuanced discussion of potential alternative interpretations of the observed eye-movement data would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Eye-tracker Quality:** How did the different eye-tracker qualities (sampling rate, accuracy) in Dataset 1 and Dataset 2 potentially affect the derived cognitive features, and did you attempt to mitigate any discrepancies caused by these differences?
2. **Ablation Study:** Could you provide a more detailed ablation study showing the individual contribution of different cognitive feature groups (e.g., basic gaze features vs. graph-based features) to the overall performance improvement?
3. **Feature Selection:** What criteria were used to select the 500 principal components during PCA? Could you justify this choice and explore alternative dimensionality reduction techniques?
4. **Deep Learning Comparison:**  Could you elaborate on the rationale for excluding deep learning-based sentiment analysis models from the comparison, and discuss potential challenges or benefits of integrating cognitive features into such models?
5. **Cognitive Interpretation:** Can you discuss potential alternative interpretations of the observed eye-movement patterns that might contribute to the improved performance, beyond the proposed cognitive mechanisms?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly structured.
*   The provided references are relevant and up-to-date.
*   Consider adding a visualization of the gaze-saliency graph for a representative example sentence to better illustrate the feature.
*   Formatting inconsistencies in table captions (e.g. use of "˙" instead of ".") should be addressed.
