This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data of human annotators. The central hypothesis is that readers’ eye-movement patterns reveal insights into the cognitive processes involved in sentiment understanding, particularly for nuanced expressions like sarcasm and irony. The authors demonstrate improvements in sentiment classification accuracy across multiple classifiers and datasets when incorporating these cognitive features.

The primary contribution of this work is the *novel* exploration of using cognitive features – specifically, eye-tracking data – to enhance sentiment analysis. While eye-tracking has been used in related psycholinguistic tasks, its application to directly improve sentiment classification accuracy, especially for complex linguistic constructs, is a genuinely new direction. The second contribution lies in the comprehensive feature engineering, encompassing both established sentiment analysis features *and* a detailed set of gaze-derived metrics, including graph-based features attempting to capture relationships between fixations. Finally, the demonstration of performance gains, particularly on datasets containing sarcasm and irony, provides initial evidence supporting the hypothesis.

**Strengths:**

1. **High Novelty:** The core idea of incorporating cognitive features from eye-tracking into sentiment analysis is highly innovative. This is not a refinement of existing techniques but a fundamentally different approach, potentially opening up new avenues for research. I prioritize novelty highly, and this paper delivers on that front.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The authors haven’t simply thrown in a few eye-tracking metrics; they’ve developed a rich and thoughtfully designed feature set, exploring both basic and complex aspects of gaze behavior. The inclusion of graph-based features derived from saccade patterns demonstrates significant effort.
3. **Demonstrated Improvement:**  The results presented, while not massive, consistently show accuracy improvements with the inclusion of cognitive features, particularly on the more challenging datasets. The statistical significance tests add confidence to these findings.
4. **Relevance to a Core NLP Challenge:** Sentiment analysis of nuanced and complex text, such as sarcastic or ironic expressions, remains a significant challenge. This work directly addresses this challenge, attempting to move beyond purely lexical and syntactic approaches.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors thoughtfully address the practical limitations of eye-tracking data acquisition and suggest potential pathways towards wider applicability with the increasing availability of mobile eye-tracking technology.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size & Generalizability:** The datasets used are relatively small, and while curated to include challenging examples, their size limits the robustness of the conclusions. The performance gains might not generalize to significantly different datasets.
2. **Eye-tracker Variability:** The paper uses data from two different eye-trackers with varying quality (500Hz vs. 120Hz). This introduces potential variability in the data and might influence the observed results. A more rigorous analysis of the impact of eye-tracker quality would be beneficial.
3. **Feature Interpretation & Redundancy:** While the feature set is comprehensive, the individual contribution of each cognitive feature isn't always clearly explained. There’s a risk of redundancy among some of the features, and feature selection/reduction techniques could have been explored further.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Deep Learning Baselines:** While the authors justify not comparing to deep learning approaches, the current state-of-the-art in sentiment analysis is increasingly dominated by deep learning models.  A brief discussion of how this approach *might* integrate with deep learning (as suggested in the conclusion) would strengthen the paper.
5. **Error Analysis Depth:** The error analysis section is brief. A more detailed examination of *where* the model still fails, and *why*, could provide valuable insights for future research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more intuition behind the design choices for the gaze-saliency graph features? What specific cognitive processes are these features intended to capture?
2. How sensitive are the results to the specific parameters used in constructing the gaze-saliency graphs (e.g., edge weighting)?
3.  Given the different eye-tracker qualities used, have you investigated whether performance gains are consistent across both datasets, or are they more pronounced for the higher-quality data?
4.  What steps were taken to ensure the eye-tracking data accurately reflects the annotators' genuine cognitive processes during sentiment assessment, rather than being influenced by the act of being eye-tracked?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper's conclusion hinting at integrating these features into deep learning is interesting. Exploring that direction could be a powerful future research avenue.  The inclusion of a small ablation study showing the individual contributions of feature groups (Sentiment, Sarcasm, Cognitive) would also be valuable.



Overall, despite the limitations, I believe this paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of sentiment analysis. The bold approach of incorporating cognitive features from eye-tracking data is highly promising, and the initial results are encouraging. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The novelty and potential impact of this work outweigh the current limitations.
