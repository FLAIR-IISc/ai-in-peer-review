This paper investigates the use of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, to improve sentiment analysis performance, particularly for nuanced language like sarcasm and irony. The authors augment traditional linguistic features with these cognitive signals and demonstrate performance gains on two publicly available datasets.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration that incorporating eye-tracking derived features can enhance sentiment analysis accuracy, particularly when dealing with complex linguistic constructs like sarcasm and irony. While the idea of using cognitive data for NLP isn’t entirely novel (as evidenced by the related work section), its application to sentiment analysis, and the specific features engineered from eye-movement patterns, represent a valuable addition. A secondary contribution lies in the curated datasets used for evaluation, which contain instances with higher-level nuances (sarcasm, irony, implicit sentiment) making them suitable for testing the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Finally, the detailed feature engineering and analysis, including the exploration of gaze-saliency graphs, is a noteworthy aspect of the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Feature Integration:** The core strength of this paper lies in its innovative integration of cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data into the sentiment analysis pipeline. This is a relatively unexplored area, and the authors convincingly demonstrate the potential of this approach. The use of saliency graphs to represent eye-movement patterns is also a creative contribution.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results presented are compelling. The authors consistently show improvement in F-scores when cognitive features are added to the feature set, across both datasets and different classifiers. The statistical significance tests further strengthen these findings. The separate evaluation on ironic/non-ironic samples in the held-out dataset adds further granularity.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The paper provides a thorough description of the features used, both traditional sentiment features and the newly introduced cognitive features. This level of detail allows for reproducibility and provides a solid foundation for future research. The explanation of the motivation behind each feature is also helpful.
4. **Relevant Datasets:** The use of datasets specifically designed to include sarcasm and irony is a strong point. This choice ensures that the evaluation is focused on the scenarios where the proposed approach is expected to excel.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The inclusion of a section discussing the feasibility of deploying this approach in real-world scenarios, considering the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and the potential for user consent, demonstrates a practical awareness and expands the potential impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability of Eye-Tracking Setup:** A key concern is the variability in eye-tracker quality used across the datasets (high-quality Eyelink-1000 vs. lower-quality Tobii T120). This difference could introduce bias and limit the generalizability of the findings. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed analysis of how the eye-tracker quality affects the derived features would be beneficial.
2. **Averaging of Individual Gaze Data:** The method of averaging gaze data across participants to create graph-based features may lose valuable individual-specific information. While this simplifies the analysis, it potentially discards nuances that could contribute to improved accuracy. This is acknowledged in the error analysis, but a potential solution or further exploration could be included.
3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Deep Learning Models:** The authors explicitly state they do not compare against deep learning models, citing the feature-based nature of their approach. However, given the current dominance of deep learning in sentiment analysis, this omission weakens the paper. While a direct comparison might be challenging, a discussion of how their approach complements or differs from deep learning approaches would be valuable.
4. **Feature Significance Interpretation:** The feature significance analysis in Table 3 indicates several cognitive features are important, but the explanation of *why* these features are important could be strengthened. Connecting the specific eye-movement patterns to cognitive processes (e.g., increased regressions indicating difficulty in processing ironic statements) would enhance the understanding of the results.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors acknowledge the errors committed by the system, the error analysis is relatively brief. A more in-depth analysis of specific error cases, highlighting the types of sentences where the system struggles, could provide valuable insights for future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how the different eye-tracker qualities (Eyelink vs. Tobii) might affect the derived cognitive features, and potentially the overall performance?
2. Have you considered alternative methods for aggregating gaze data, beyond simple averaging, to retain more individual-specific information?
3. How do you envision your approach being integrated with or complementing deep learning-based sentiment analysis models?
4. Can you provide concrete examples of sentences where the inclusion of cognitive features significantly improved the classification accuracy, and explain the underlying cognitive rationale?
5. Could you expand the error analysis to categorize the types of errors made by the system, and identify specific linguistic patterns that are particularly challenging?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The extensive list of references is commendable. The figures and tables are clear and informative. Consider adding a visual example illustrating the gaze-saliency graph for a sentence containing sarcasm or irony to better convey the concept.
