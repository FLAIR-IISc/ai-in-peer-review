This paper investigates the use of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, to improve sentiment analysis performance, particularly in handling complex linguistic constructs like irony and sarcasm. The authors augment traditional sentiment features with gaze-based metrics, arguing that readers’ eye-movement patterns reflect cognitive processes related to sentiment understanding. They evaluate their approach on two publicly available datasets, demonstrating improvements in accuracy over baseline systems.

The main contributions of this work are: (1) the novel incorporation of cognitive features extracted from eye-tracking data into a sentiment analysis pipeline; (2) the demonstration of performance gains, particularly on datasets containing nuanced sentiment expressions (irony, sarcasm); and (3) the detailed feature engineering of both traditional and cognitive features, including the innovative use of gaze-saliency graphs.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of integrating cognitive features from eye-tracking into sentiment analysis is a valuable contribution. While eye-tracking has been used in related areas like annotation complexity and WSD, its application to directly enhance sentiment classification accuracy is novel and potentially impactful. This offers a different perspective compared to primarily feature-based or deep learning approaches.

2. **Empirical Evidence of Improvement:** The paper provides clear experimental results demonstrating performance improvements across multiple classifiers (NB, SVM, Multi-layer NN) on both datasets when cognitive features are included. The statistical significance testing (McNemar test) strengthens the claim of genuine improvements. The specific focus on improvements for complex constructs like irony is well-supported by the held-out dataset experiment.

3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors present a comprehensive set of features, categorized and explained clearly. The inclusion of both basic gaze features and more complex features derived from gaze-saliency graphs demonstrates a thoughtful exploration of the potential of eye-tracking data. The extensive feature significance analysis (Table 3) provides insight into which cognitive features are most informative.

4. **Discussion of Feasibility:** The authors proactively address the practicality of their approach, acknowledging the current limitations of eye-tracking technology and discussing the potential of emerging mobile eye-tracking solutions. They also present compelling scenarios for real-world applications, strengthening the overall value of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Diversity:** The evaluation relies on only two datasets, both focused on short-text snippets (tweets, reviews).  The generalizability of the findings to other text types (e.g., news articles, formal documents) is unclear. The datasets also have relatively small sizes.

2. **Eye-tracker Quality Variance:** The two datasets utilize different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000 and Tobii T120) with significantly different sampling rates. This introduces potential confounding factors, as the quality of eye-tracking data directly influences the reliability of the derived features.  A more detailed discussion of the implications of this disparity is needed.

3. **Feature Aggregation & Loss of Information:** The method of averaging gaze-saliency graph features across participants may result in a loss of individual cognitive patterns. While understandable for scalability, this aggregation could mask potentially valuable personalized insights.  The authors acknowledge this limitation, but further exploration of individual-level analysis might be beneficial.

4. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Deep Learning Methods:** The authors deliberately exclude comparisons to deep learning approaches, citing that their method is feature-based. However, state-of-the-art sentiment analysis systems frequently employ deep learning models.  Acknowledging the limitations of not including such comparisons and discussing potential future work involving integrating cognitive features into deep learning architectures would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the parameters used in constructing the gaze-saliency graphs?  Were different parameter settings explored, and what impact did they have on performance?
2. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing specific features from the gaze-saliency graphs (e.g., highest weighted degree)? Was there a theoretical basis or were these selected empirically?
3. Given the limitations of the datasets, what further experiments would you conduct to assess the generalizability of your findings to other text domains and writing styles?
4.  You mention the potential for integrating cognitive features into deep learning models in the conclusion. Could you provide a more concrete outline of how you envision this integration?



**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
* The related work section provides a good overview of existing research in sentiment analysis and eye-tracking.
*  Consider providing more details about the data preprocessing steps applied to the eye-tracking data (e.g., filtering, outlier removal).
* The visual representation of the gaze-saliency graph (Figure 2) is helpful for understanding the concept.
