- Paper proposes using cognitive features from eye-tracking (fixation duration, regression count, gaze-saliency graphs) to improve sentiment analysis, especially for sarcasm/irony.
- Main contributions: demonstrating cognitive features improve sentiment classification, showing effectiveness on complex constructs, and discussing feasibility of mobile eye-tracking deployment.
- Reported improvements: 6.1% gain on complex texts vs. 2.1% on simple texts; overall claimed improvements of 3.7% and 9.3% on datasets.
- Strength: Novel application of eye-tracking-derived cognitive features to address sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations in sentiment analysis.
- Strength: Well-designed experiments using two targeted datasets (994 and 1059 examples) with statistical significance testing and feature significance analysis.
- Strength: Practical discussion of mobile eye-tracking feasibility and application scenarios (e-commerce, online learning).
- Weakness: Cognitive features themselves are standard psycholinguistic metrics and the paper doesn't justify why these features are specifically suited to sentiment analysis.
- Weakness: No comparison with modern deep learning baselines (CNNs/RNNs), undermining the claim of state-of-the-art improvements.
- Weakness: Insufficient implementation details for reproducibility (gaze-saliency graph construction, edge thresholds, annotator aggregation).
- Question: How does this work fundamentally differ from prior eye-tracking studies (e.g., Joshi et al. 2014, Mishra et al. 2016) in feature extraction and application?
- Question: Can authors provide comparisons against strong deep learning baselines (e.g., CNNs) on the same datasets to contextualize improvements?
- Question: How was PCA dimensionality (500 features) chosen/validated, and how was information loss prevented?
- Question: How do authors address overfitting risk given the small held-out set (294 examples; 131 complex)?
- Additional comment: Provide detailed gaze-saliency graph construction parameters (edge thresholding, graph feature computation) for replication.
- Additional comment: Clarify handling of different eye-tracking hardware (Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120) and differing sampling rates (500Hz vs. 120Hz).
- Additional comment: Include qualitative examples showing how cognitive features differ between sarcastic and non-sarcastic texts to illustrate the connection.
