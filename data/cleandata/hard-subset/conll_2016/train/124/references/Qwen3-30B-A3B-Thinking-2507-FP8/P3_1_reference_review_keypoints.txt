- Empirical demonstration that eye-tracking–derived cognitive features improve sentiment analysis, especially for sarcasm/irony.  
- Curated and adapted two existing datasets rich in complex sentiment expressions for the task.  
- Systematic feature engineering of gaze-based cognitive features that could aid future psycholinguistic NLP work.  
- Strong evidence that cognitive features yield larger performance gains on complex sentiment (6.1% vs 2.1%), with statistical significance.  
- Careful dataset selection and validation showing traditional sentiment analyzers perform poorly on these datasets.  
- Comprehensive feature analysis showing gaze-based features rank highly among top predictive features.  
- Overstated novelty claim: prior work used eye-tracking for sentiment-related tasks, so claiming to be first is misleading.  
- Lack of comparison to deep learning/state-of-the-art sentiment methods makes it hard to judge the practical significance of reported gains.  
- Dataset suitability and eye-tracking quality concerns: differing hardware (500Hz vs 120Hz) and datasets not originally created for polarity classification.  
- Insufficient theoretical justification for why specific eye-tracking metrics should correlate with sentiment processing.  
- Limited discussion of practical feasibility and deployment challenges (privacy, UX, cost) for mobile eye-tracking applications.  
- Request for clarification on how this work differs from Joshi et al. (2014) and Mishra et al. (2016) regarding sentiment polarity classification.  
- Asked to speculate how cognitive features could be integrated into deep learning architectures and potential benefits.  
- Query about how noise from low-quality eye-tracking (Tobii T120) was handled and its impact on derived features.  
- Asked to provide cognitive-science references to justify selection of specific eye-tracking features (e.g., Largest Regression Position).  
- Questioned the practical significance of the reported 3–9% improvement given modern benchmarks and real-world impact.  
- Overall recommendation: weak accept, contingent on clarifying novelty, comparing to state-of-the-art, and providing stronger theoretical and practical justification.
