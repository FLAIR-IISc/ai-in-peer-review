- Proposes adding cognitive features from annotators' eye-tracking to improve sentiment analysis, especially for irony/sarcasm.  
- Introduces cognitive features as a novel feature set for handling complex sentiment phenomena.  
- Reports up to 9.3% accuracy improvement on challenging datasets.  
- Shows cognitive features give ~6.1% improvement specifically for irony/sarcasm cases.  
- Novel use of eye-movement data to tackle complex sentiment phenomena with a plausible theoretical link to cognitive processing.  
- Rigorous experiments on two challenging datasets with statistical significance testing and consistent gains.  
- Discusses practical feasibility (mobile eye-trackers, applications, consent) and real-world considerations.  
- Fails to detail how raw eye-tracking data is processed into features and gaze-saliency graphs, hindering reproducibility.  
- Cognitive features are biased because data were collected while annotators performed sentiment annotation, not during natural reading.  
- Overstates novelty; insufficiently distinguishes from prior work like Mishra et al. (2016) that used gaze data.  
- Lacks theoretical explanation for why specific eye-movement patterns correlate with sentiment phenomena, leaving the method a "black box."  
- Omits comparisons or integration with state-of-the-art deep learning approaches (CNNs/RNNs).  
- How do you mitigate bias from eye-tracking collected during active annotation rather than natural reading?  
- Please provide detailed steps for converting raw eye-tracking data into the gaze-saliency graph and derived features.  
- Clarify differences from Mishra et al. (2016) and justify the "first of its kind" claim.  
- Promising idea with empirical gains, but methodological biases, insufficient technical detail, and weak theoretical grounding significantly limit the paper's contribution.
