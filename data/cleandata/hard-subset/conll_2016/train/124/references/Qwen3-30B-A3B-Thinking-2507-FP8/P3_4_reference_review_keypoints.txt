- Paper proposes using eye-tracking–derived cognitive features to improve sentiment analysis, especially for sarcasm/irony.  
- Introduces a combined feature set: traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/irony features, and engineered cognitive (eye-movement) features.  
- First application of eye-tracking cognitive features to sentiment analysis and complex linguistic constructs.  
- Detailed engineering of cognitive features, including basic gaze metrics and complex graph-based gaze-saliency features.  
- Empirical results show significant accuracy improvements (3.6–9.3%), with larger gains on complex cases and gaze features ranking among top predictors.  
- Novelty and feature engineering are well-motivated and demonstrate deep knowledge of eye-tracking and NLP.  
- Authors chose challenging datasets (sarcasm/irony-heavy), making evaluation more meaningful than standard sentiment datasets.  
- Results are statistically significant and compelling for specialized, difficult sentiment examples.  
- Paper lacks strong theoretical/psychological justification linking specific eye-movement features to sentiment cognition.  
- Practical feasibility is under-addressed: deployment, privacy, data-collection costs, and user adoption of eye-tracking are not adequately discussed.  
- No comparison to modern deep learning baselines (CNNs/RNNs), leaving claims relative to state-of-the-art incomplete or misleading.  
- Dataset sizes are small (994 and 1059 examples), raising overfitting concerns given high-dimensional gaze features.  
- Questions: request for stronger psychological evidence linking features to sentiment processing.  
- Questions: how the approach handles cold-start/new domains without eye-tracking data.  
- Questions: how confident authors are that improvements are not due to overfitting with small datasets.  
- Overall: promising and novel approach with solid engineering and results, but needs stronger theory, feasibility discussion, and deep-learning comparisons.  
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions to address theoretical justification, deployment feasibility, and comparison to modern methods.
