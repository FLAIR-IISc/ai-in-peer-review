

# Peer Review: "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of human annotators to improve sentiment analysis, particularly for complex sentiment expressions like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations. The authors claim to be the first to use cognitive features from eye-tracking for sentiment polarity classification, rather than for measuring annotation complexity or sarcasm understandability as in prior work.

In my assessment, the paper's strongest contribution is the **empirical demonstration that cognitive features from eye-tracking data can improve sentiment analysis for complex constructs**, particularly sarcasm and irony. The second most significant contribution is the **curation and adaptation of two existing datasets** that contain rich examples of complex sentiment expressions. The third contribution, while less substantial, is the **systematic feature engineering of gaze-based cognitive features** that could be useful for future work in psycholinguistic NLP.

## Strengths

1. **Demonstration of Cognitive Features' Value for Complex Sentiment**: The most compelling contribution is the empirical evidence that cognitive features specifically improve performance on complex sentiment expressions (irony/sarcasm) by 6.1% compared to 2.1% on simpler texts (Table 4). This demonstrates that the proposed features aren't just generally useful but actually address a specific challenge in sentiment analysis that traditional methods struggle with. The authors provide a statistically significant comparison (p<0.05) showing this differential improvement.

2. **Careful Dataset Selection and Validation**: The authors wisely selected two datasets (Dataset 1 and 2) that were originally created for related tasks (sarcasm understandability prediction and sentiment annotation complexity prediction) and validated them by showing that traditional sentiment analyzers perform poorly on them (Table 1). This is a strong justification for why these datasets are appropriate for testing their approach, as they contain the very complexity the authors aim to address.

3. **Comprehensive Feature Analysis**: The paper provides a thorough feature significance analysis (Table 3), showing that gaze-based features are among the top-ranked features for both datasets (10 out of top 20 for Dataset 1, 7 out of top 20 for Dataset 2). This goes beyond simple accuracy improvements to provide insight into why the cognitive features are effective, supporting the claim that they capture relevant information for sentiment analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Overstated Novelty Claim**: The paper claims "our approach is the first of its kind to the best of our knowledge," but the related work section cites Joshi et al. (2014) who "develop a method to measure the sentiment annotation complexity using cognitive evidence from eye-tracking" and Mishra et al. (2016) who "predict readers' sarcasm understandability by modeling gaze behavior." The paper is not the first to use eye-tracking for sentiment-related tasks, though it may be the first to use it specifically for sentiment polarity classification. This overstatement of novelty undermines the paper's credibility.

2. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Methods**: The authors explicitly state they don't consider deep learning approaches because their approach is feature-based. However, this is a significant limitation as deep learning approaches (like Maas et al., 2011; dos Santos and Gatti, 2014) have become the standard for sentiment analysis. The paper doesn't compare their approach to these state-of-the-art methods, making it difficult to assess whether their 3-9% improvement is meaningful in the current landscape of sentiment analysis.

3. **Questionable Dataset Suitability and Eye-Tracking Quality**: Dataset 1 uses high-quality SR-Research Eyelink-1000 (500Hz), while Dataset 2 uses low-quality Tobii T120 (120Hz). The paper doesn't address how this difference in hardware quality might affect the cognitive features or results. More importantly, neither dataset was originally created for sentiment polarity classification - they were created for sarcasm understandability prediction and sentiment annotation complexity prediction. This raises questions about whether these datasets are truly appropriate for testing sentiment polarity classification.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Feature Selection**: The paper describes numerous cognitive features (e.g., "Largest Regression Position," "Edge density of the saliency gaze graph") but provides little theoretical justification for why these specific features should correlate with sentiment processing. The connection between cognitive science and these specific eye-tracking metrics for sentiment analysis is not well established in the paper, making it difficult to assess whether these features are meaningful or just statistical artifacts.

5. **Limited Discussion of Practical Feasibility**: While Section 7 discusses the feasibility of mobile eye-tracking, the paper doesn't address the practical challenges of deploying this approach. For example, the authors claim eye-tracking could be integrated into mobile apps for e-commerce, but they don't discuss the privacy implications of collecting eye-tracking data, the user experience impact of requiring eye-tracking, or the cost of implementing this in real-world applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be the first to use cognitive features for sentiment polarity classification, but prior work by Joshi et al. (2014) and Mishra et al. (2016) used eye-tracking for sentiment-related tasks. Could you clarify exactly how your approach differs from these works in terms of the specific application to sentiment polarity classification?

2. You state that your approach doesn't consider deep learning methods because it's feature-based, but this seems like a missed opportunity. Could you speculate on how cognitive features might be integrated into deep learning architectures (e.g., as additional inputs to a CNN or RNN), and what potential benefits this might have?

3. Given that Dataset 2 uses low-quality eye-tracking hardware (Tobii T120, 120Hz), how did the authors account for potential noise in the gaze data, and how might this have affected the cognitive features derived from it?

4. The paper doesn't provide a clear theoretical framework explaining why specific eye-tracking metrics (e.g., "Largest Regression Position") would correlate with sentiment processing. Could you provide more specific cognitive science references that support the selection of these particular features?

5. The improvement of 3-9% reported in the paper is modest. In the context of current sentiment analysis benchmarks (where state-of-the-art models often achieve >85% accuracy on standard datasets), how significant are these improvements, and what would be the practical impact of this level of improvement in real-world applications?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper makes a compelling empirical case that cognitive features from eye-tracking can improve sentiment analysis for complex sentiment expressions like sarcasm. However, the overstated novelty claim, lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods, and questionable dataset suitability significantly undermine the contribution. The paper would be substantially stronger with a more nuanced discussion of related work, comparison to deep learning approaches, and clearer justification for the specific cognitive features selected.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors must address these concerns in the revision, particularly by clarifying the novelty of their approach, comparing to state-of-the-art methods, and providing more theoretical justification for their cognitive feature selection.
