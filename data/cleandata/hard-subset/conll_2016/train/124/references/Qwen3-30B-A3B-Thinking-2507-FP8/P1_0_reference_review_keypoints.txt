1. Introduces a novel set of cognitive features from eye movements (fixations, saccades, regressions, gaze-saliency graphs) to improve sentiment analysis, especially for sarcasm/irony.  
2. Provides empirical evidence of statistically significant accuracy gains (3.7% and 9.3% overall; 6.1% for irony) across two datasets.  
3. Discusses feasibility of using eye-tracking (including mobile devices) for real-world sentiment analysis.  
4. Addresses a key gap in sentiment analysis by targeting complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations.  
5. Delivers strong experimental validation with multiple classifiers, datasets, statistical tests, and a held-out irony/sarcasm evaluation.  
6. Grounds the approach in psycholinguistic theory linking eye-movement patterns to cognitive processing.  
7. Offers a thoughtful practical-feasibility discussion and relevant application scenarios (e-commerce, online learning).  
8. Presents a comprehensive feature set combining traditional sentiment, sarcasm/irony, cognitive, and reading-difficulty features.  
9. Fails to sufficiently justify novelty or clearly differentiate from prior eye-tracking works (e.g., Joshi et al., Mishra et al.).  
10. Under-addresses practical barriers to deployment: hardware cost, user consent, privacy concerns, and processing complexity.  
11. Omits comparisons with modern deep learning approaches or justification for preferring a feature-based approach.  
12. Lacks technical detail in feature extraction (e.g., PCA implementation and parameters), affecting reproducibility.  
13. Does not adequately account for differing eye-tracker quality (500Hz vs 120Hz) and its impact on results.  
14. Request to clarify distinctions from Joshi et al. (2014) and why prior eye-tracking methods weren’t effective for sentiment.  
15. Asks how the authors ensure cognitive features reflect linguistic complexity rather than annotator bias across labelers.  
16. Requests concrete qualitative examples where cognitive features clearly improved sarcasm classification.  
17. Seeks precise rules for constructing gaze-saliency graphs (edge definitions, saccade/duration/distance thresholds).  
18. Asks how authors will handle scalability, privacy, consent, and user resistance when collecting eye-tracking data in real-world settings.
