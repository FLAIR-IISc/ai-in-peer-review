

# Review for "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes using cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of human annotators to improve sentiment analysis, particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. The authors claim their approach is novel and significantly improves accuracy over existing methods by augmenting traditional linguistic features with gaze-based cognitive features.

## Main Contributions Claimed by Authors
1. Introducing cognitive features from eye-movement patterns for sentiment analysis
2. Demonstrating significant improvement in accuracy, especially for complex texts like irony/sarcasm
3. Being the first to use gaze-based cognition for sentiment analysis

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies a potentially interesting connection between eye-tracking and sentiment analysis, which could be valuable for future research. The datasets used (Dataset 1 and Dataset 2) contain examples with complex linguistic features like sarcasm and irony, which are challenging for sentiment analysis.

2. The authors provide a detailed description of the gaze-based features, including both basic gaze features and complex gaze features derived from gaze-saliency graphs, which could be useful for future work in this area.

3. The paper includes a thorough error analysis section discussing limitations of the approach, including hardware limitations of eye-trackers and potential loss of information when aggregating eye-tracking parameters.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Lack of Novelty and Misrepresentation of Related Work
The paper claims "our approach is the first of its kind to the best of our knowledge" (1.4), but the related work section cites Mishra et al. (2016) and Joshi et al. (2014), who have already used eye-tracking for related NLP tasks. The authors fail to clearly differentiate their approach from these previous works. Mishra et al. (2016) specifically used eye-tracking to predict sarcasm understandability, which is directly related to the current work's focus on sarcasm. The paper also fails to cite a number of relevant works that have used eye-tracking for sentiment-related tasks, including "A cognitive study of subjectivity extraction in sentiment annotation" (Mishra et al., 2014), which directly addresses sentiment annotation with eye-tracking. This misrepresentation of related work undermines the paper's claim of novelty.

### 2. Conceptual Confusion Between "Cognitive Features" and "Eye-Tracking Features"
The paper consistently conflates "cognitive features" with the features derived from eye-tracking data. In section 4.3, they state "Cognitive features from eye-movement" but then in section 4.4 they describe "Basic gaze features" and "Complex gaze features" - these are eye-tracking features, not cognitive features. The paper never clearly explains how these eye-tracking features translate to cognitive features, despite claiming that "cognitive processes in the brain are related to eye-movement activities" (Parasuraman and Rizzo, 2006). The authors provide no theoretical grounding for why eye-movement patterns should correlate with cognitive processes relevant to sentiment analysis. This conceptual confusion significantly weakens the paper's foundation.

### 3. Marginal Improvements with Questionable Significance
The paper reports marginal improvements (3.7% and 9.3% for Dataset 1 and 2 respectively), but the evaluation is flawed. The baseline systems (SVM, NB, Rule-based) are trained on data from Pang and Lee (2004) and Sentiment 140, which are from different domains than the test datasets (which contain movie reviews, normalized tweets, and quotes). This domain mismatch likely explains the low baseline performance (F-scores around 60-70%) but isn't addressed. The paper also claims "significant improvement in sentiment polarity detection over the state of the art" but doesn't compare against current state-of-the-art methods for sentiment analysis, especially for sarcasm and irony detection. The improvements are marginal for a method that requires additional data collection (eye-tracking) and complex feature engineering.

### 4. Technical Inconsistencies and Formatting Errors
The paper contains numerous minor errors that suggest a lack of attention to detail:
- Inconsistent notation: "F˙score" instead of "F-score" in multiple places (Tables 1 and 2)
- Inconsistent spacing: "Sn+ Sr+Gz" vs "Sn+Sr+Gz" in text and tables
- Typos: "P˙score" instead of "F-score" in Table 1
- In Table 2, the paper claims "an overall improvement of 3.7% and 9.3% respectively" but the actual improvements are 3.7% for Dataset 1 and 9.3% for Dataset 2, which is inconsistent with the ordering in the text.
- In Table 4, they report "Irony Non-Irony" but don't explain how they defined these categories or how they labeled the examples.
- The reference section has inconsistent citation formats (e.g., "Joshi et al.,? 2014" instead of "Joshi et al., 2014").

### 5. Poorly Justified Held-Out Dataset Evaluation
The held-out dataset in Section 5.1 is described as "randomly derived from Dataset-1" but without details about how the partitioning was done or if it's truly held-out. The paper doesn't provide the exact split ratio or how the 131 irony examples were identified. This undermines the claim that the approach is particularly effective for complex texts. The improvement of 6.1% for irony vs 2.1% for non-irony is marginal and the statistical significance claim (p < 0.05 for McNemar test) is not verified with proper contingency tables.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "our approach is the first of its kind" but cites Mishra et al. (2016) who used eye-tracking for sarcasm understandability prediction. Can you clearly explain how your approach differs significantly from this previous work?

2. You consistently refer to "cognitive features" but describe features derived from eye-tracking data (e.g., "Average First-Fixation Duration per word"). How do these eye-tracking features translate to cognitive features, and what is the theoretical basis for this translation beyond the vague reference to Parasuraman and Rizzo (2006)?

3. In Table 2, you claim "an overall improvement of 3.7% and 9.3% respectively" but the actual improvements are 3.7% for Dataset 1 and 9.3% for Dataset 2. Why is the order reversed in the text?

4. Your held-out dataset in Section 5.1 is described as "randomly derived from Dataset-1" but no details are provided about how the partitioning was done or how irony examples were identified. Please provide these details.

5. Your baseline classifiers are trained on data from Pang and Lee (2004) and Sentiment 140, which are from different domains than your test datasets. How do you account for this domain mismatch in your evaluation?

## Final Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper presents an interesting idea, the numerous methodological flaws, lack of clear novelty, conceptual confusion, and marginal improvements with questionable significance outweigh the potential value of the work. The numerous formatting inconsistencies and technical errors further undermine the paper's credibility. The authors have not convincingly demonstrated that their approach provides meaningful advances over previous work or that the marginal improvements justify the additional complexity of requiring eye-tracking data. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to clarify the theoretical foundation, properly differentiate from related work, and provide a more rigorous evaluation.
