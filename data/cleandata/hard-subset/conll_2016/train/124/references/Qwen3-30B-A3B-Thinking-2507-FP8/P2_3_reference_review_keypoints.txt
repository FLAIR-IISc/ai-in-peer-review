- Proposes using eye-tracking–derived cognitive features for sentiment analysis, especially to handle sarcasm and irony.  
- Introduces a novel cognitive feature set, shows empirical gains (notably 6.1% on complex constructs), and evaluates feasibility for mobile eye-tracking.

- Novel approach targeting a real gap: leverages cognitive signals to address complex sentiment cases.  
- Strong empirical validation across two datasets with statistical significance and feature importance analyses.  
- Practical feasibility discussion linking to mobile eye-tracking and real-world applications.

- No comparison to modern deep learning SOTA (CNNs/transformers), undermining claims of improvement.  
- Insufficient theoretical explanation of why specific eye-movement features capture sentiment complexity.  
- Limited discussion of how differing eye-tracking hardware and annotator populations might bias results.  
- Reproducibility issues: lacks detailed eye-tracking setup, preprocessing, and gaze-saliency graph implementation.

- How would the cognitive features compare or integrate with deep learning models?  
- What cognitive mechanisms justify the link between eye movements and sarcasm/irony detection?  
- How are hardware differences and potential artifacts across datasets controlled for or modeled?  
- How was the held-out dataset curated and how were complex vs. simple constructs defined?

- Overall: promising and novel with strong results for complex sentiment, but needs SOTA comparisons, stronger theoretical justification, hardware/bias analysis, and reproducibility details before acceptance.
