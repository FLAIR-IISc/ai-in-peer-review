- Paper proposes augmenting linguistic features with eye-tracking–derived cognitive features for sentiment analysis, claiming modest accuracy gains, especially for sarcasm/irony.

- Main contribution: provides two sentiment datasets with eye-tracking data (strongest contribution).

- Main contribution: proposes a comprehensive set of cognitive/eye-movement features applied to sentiment analysis.

- Main contribution: shows larger relative performance gains on complex texts (6.1% for irony/sarcasm vs 2.1% for simple texts).

- Strength: novel application of eye-tracking specifically to sentiment analysis and sarcasm/irony detection.

- Strength: comprehensive evaluation across two datasets with different hardware and multiple classifiers, including a held-out complex-case set.

- Strength: empirical evidence that cognitive features help more on linguistically complex sentiment cases.

- Weakness: core idea has limited novelty given prior eye-tracking work (Mishra et al., Joshi et al.); insufficient differentiation from prior methods.

- Weakness: practicality and feasibility are questionable due to hardware limitations, need for consent, and unclear real-world collection methods.

- Weakness: absolute improvements are modest (3–9%), raising doubts about cost–benefit of eye-tracking data collection.

- Weakness: no comparison with state-of-the-art deep learning models, making it hard to assess real-world competitiveness.

- Weakness: insufficient analysis of why specific cognitive/eye-tracking features correlate with sentiment complexity.

- Question: how do your cognitive features specifically compare to those in Mishra et al. (2016) and Joshi et al. (2014)?

- Question: what is the practical significance of the modest accuracy gains for real-world systems?

- Question: why was no comparison made with modern deep learning baselines, and how would cognitive features integrate with them?

- Question: provide concrete examples and limitations of mobile eye-tracking technology that would enable real-world deployment.

- Question: what cognitive theory explains why features like gaze-saliency graph metrics are effective for sarcasm/irony detection?

- Additional: paper should more fully discuss dataset/hardware limitations (e.g., low-quality Tobii T120) and clarify whether features are cognitive vs behavioral.

- Additional: feature significance table shows gaze features ranked high, but the paper lacks explanation of the underlying cognitive mechanisms.

- Additional: claim of being "first of its kind" is questionable given cited prior work and needs a more nuanced positioning.

- Overall assessment: valuable dataset and promising results for complex cases, but limited novelty, practical concerns, modest gains, and missing DL comparisons lead to a recommendation of Weak Reject.
