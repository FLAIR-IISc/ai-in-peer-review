

# Review for "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes using eye-movement patterns (cognitive features) to enhance sentiment analysis, particularly for complex constructs like irony and sarcasm. The authors claim to be the first to use such features for sentiment analysis, introducing a new feature set combining traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/irony features, and cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. They evaluate their approach on two datasets, reporting marginal improvements in accuracy (0.6% and 1.5% for Dataset 1 and 2 respectively) when adding cognitive features to existing sentiment and sarcasm features.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Application of eye-tracking data to sentiment analysis (though not novel as prior work exists)
2. A marginal improvement in performance for complex sentiment analysis tasks (with significant methodological flaws)
3. A detailed description of cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns (with inconsistencies)

## Strengths

1. **Detailed Feature Engineering**: The paper provides a comprehensive description of the cognitive features derived from eye-movement data, including both basic gaze features and complex gaze features based on saliency graphs. This level of detail would be valuable for researchers interested in psycholinguistic approaches to NLP.

2. **Held-Out Dataset Evaluation**: The authors test their approach on a held-out dataset specifically designed to evaluate performance on complex constructs (irony/sarcasm), showing a 6.1% improvement in F-score for these cases. This provides some evidence that cognitive features may help with particularly challenging sentiment analysis cases.

3. **Feasibility Discussion**: The paper thoughtfully addresses practical concerns about implementing the approach, discussing mobile eye-trackers, applicability scenarios, and user consent issues in Section 7. This demonstrates awareness of real-world implementation challenges.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novelty**: The paper claims to be "the first of its kind" to use cognitive features for sentiment analysis, but the related work section cites Joshi et al. (2014) and Mishra et al. (2016) who have already used eye-tracking for sentiment-related tasks. Joshi et al. (2014) specifically "measured sentiment annotation complexity using cognitive evidence from eye-tracking," and Mishra et al. (2016) "predicted readers' sarcasm understandability by modeling gaze behavior." The paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from these prior works, making the novelty claim unfounded.

2. **Marginal Performance Improvements**: The paper reports marginal improvements (0.6% and 1.5%) in F-scores when adding cognitive features to existing feature sets. For Dataset 2, adding cognitive features alone (Gz) actually decreases performance from Sn+Sr (70.3) to Gz (48.9), and only improves when adding all features (Sn+Sr+Gz at 71.8). This suggests cognitive features are not beneficial on their own, and the claimed "significant improvement" is misleading. The paper overstates the contribution of cognitive features by claiming a 9.3% overall improvement when the marginal improvement from adding cognitive features is only 1.5%.

3. **Inadequate Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper states they don't consider deep learning approaches (like Maas et al., 2011; dos Santos and Gatti, 2014) because "our approach is feature based" and "beyond the scope of this work." This is a critical omission, as deep learning methods have become the state-of-the-art for sentiment analysis, and the paper's claimed improvements are not meaningful without comparison to these methods.

4. **Methodological Inconsistencies and Errors**: 
   - The paper claims a 6.1% improvement for complex texts but calculates it as (64.3-60.1)/60.1 = 6.98%, not 6.1% as stated.
   - In Table 2, the feature labels are inconsistent (Gz is defined as "Cognitive features" but the caption says "Gaze features along with features related to reading difficulty").
   - The dataset description is inconsistent (Dataset 1 has 350 sarcastic/ironic out of 994 snippets, but the held-out dataset is described as having 131 complex constructs out of 294 snippets, with no explanation of how the held-out set was created).
   - The paper states "the relative improvement of F-score, when gaze features are included, is 6.1% for complex texts" but doesn't provide the actual F-scores for the comparison (Sn vs. Sn+Gz).

5. **Inadequate Justification for Feature Selection**: The paper claims cognitive features are motivated by "qualitative observations" but doesn't provide any evidence of these observations. For example, the rationale for choosing "Largest Regression Position (LREG)" over other regression-related features is not explained. The paper also fails to justify why certain cognitive features were included while others were excluded.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to be "the first of its kind" to use cognitive features for sentiment analysis, but prior work by Joshi et al. (2014) and Mishra et al. (2016) has already used eye-tracking for sentiment-related tasks. How does your approach specifically differ from these prior works?

2. The paper states that cognitive features alone (Gz) decrease performance on Dataset 2 (from 70.3 to 48.9). Why did the authors choose to include these features in the final model, and what was the rationale for the final feature combination?

3. The paper reports a 6.1% improvement for complex texts but calculates it as (64.3-60.1)/60.1 = 6.98%. Please clarify this discrepancy in your revision.

4. Why did the authors choose not to compare against state-of-the-art deep learning approaches for sentiment analysis, which have become the standard in the field?

5. The paper states that "cognitive features indeed empower sentiment analyzers to handle complex constructs like irony and sarcasm" based on a 6.1% improvement. Given the marginal nature of this improvement, how do you justify this claim in light of the fact that the best performance was achieved with a combination of all features, not just cognitive features?

## Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper has some interesting methodological details about cognitive features, the fundamental flaws in novelty claims, methodological inconsistencies, and marginal performance improvements are substantial. The paper's claims about the significance of cognitive features for sentiment analysis are not supported by the evidence presented. The marginal improvements reported are not meaningful in the context of current sentiment analysis research, and the paper fails to adequately distinguish itself from prior work that has already applied eye-tracking to sentiment-related tasks.

The paper's failure to compare with state-of-the-art deep learning approaches, the inconsistent reporting of results, and the overstatement of cognitive features' contribution make it unsuitable for publication at CoNLL. The marginal improvements (0.6% and 1.5%) are not sufficient to justify the paper's claims, especially when compared to the current state-of-the-art in sentiment analysis.
