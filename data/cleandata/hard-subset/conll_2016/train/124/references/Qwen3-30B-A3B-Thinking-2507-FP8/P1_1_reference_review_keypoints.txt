- Introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis by integrating eye-tracking–derived cognitive features.  
- Shows cognitive features improve detection of complex constructs (sarcasm/irony) with a reported 6.1% gain on a held-out set.  
- Provides comprehensive, systematic feature engineering connecting eye-movement measures to cognitive/linguistic processes.  
- Novel research direction that departs from purely linguistic feature methods and opens new avenues for sentiment analysis.  
- Strong empirical validation across multiple datasets and classifiers with statistical significance testing.  
- Detailed mapping of specific gaze metrics (e.g., regression count, largest regression position) to cognitive difficulties and surprisal.  
- Thoughtful discussion of practical deployment issues (mobile eye-trackers, consent scenarios) showing real-world awareness.  
- Clearly acknowledges limitations such as hardware dependence and possible information loss from gaze averaging.  
- Fails to compare against state-of-the-art deep learning baselines, limiting claims of practical superiority.  
- Overstates novelty by not sufficiently distinguishing from prior eye-tracking work (e.g., Joshi et al., Mishra et al.).  
- Practicality and scalability claims are questionable given privacy, consent, and cost concerns around eye-tracking.  
- Lacks deep analysis of why specific cognitive features help distinguish sarcasm versus other phenomena.  
- Potential overfitting risk: PCA to 500 components is insufficiently detailed and may hurt generalizability.  
- Unclear how differences in eye-tracking hardware (sampling rates, devices) affect extracted features and results.  
- Ambiguity about held-out dataset construction and distribution of complex constructs could bias reported gains.  
- Unclear whether approach generalizes to three-class sentiment tasks (positive/negative/objective) after excluding objective labels.  
- Requests examples: ask for 2–3 sentences where cognitive features enabled correct sarcasm/irony classification.  
- Asks how cognitive features could be integrated into or compared with deep learning architectures.  
- Seeks clarification on cross-device feature robustness given use of multiple eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000, Tobii T120).  
- Overall: well-written and valuable contribution but needs stronger comparisons, deeper analyses, and clearer practical justification.
