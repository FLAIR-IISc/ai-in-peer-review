### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with "cognitive features" derived from the eye-tracking data of human annotators. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, which reflect cognitive processing, can help classifiers better handle complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations. The authors define a set of basic and complex gaze-based features and demonstrate through experiments on two datasets that adding these features to a strong text-based feature set results in a statistically significant, albeit modest, improvement in classification accuracy.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  The introduction of a novel feature set for sentiment analysis based on eye-tracking data.
2.  An empirical study showing that these cognitive features provide a marginal performance gain over a well-constructed baseline of textual features, particularly for sentences containing sarcasm and irony.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of Approach:** The paper explores a genuinely novel direction by attempting to incorporate a new modality (cognitive signals via eye-tracking) into a standard NLP task. Moving beyond purely textual information is an interesting academic exploration and represents a creative line of inquiry.
2.  **Systematic Feature Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough ablation study (Table 2), testing various combinations of feature sets (unigrams, sentiment features, sarcasm features, and gaze features). This experimental design is sound and effectively isolates the contribution of the proposed cognitive features, lending credibility to the claim that these features are indeed the source of the observed performance gain. The use of statistical significance tests adds a necessary layer of rigor.
3.  **Targeted Analysis of Complex Phenomena:** The experiment on a held-out dataset containing ironic and non-ironic texts (Table 4) is a key strength. This analysis provides more compelling evidence for the authors' central hypothesis—that gaze features are particularly useful for detecting complex, non-literal sentiment—than the aggregate results on the full datasets.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Fundamental Lack of Practicality:** The core weakness of this work is its reliance on eye-tracking data being available for every text instance at inference time. This requirement is fundamentally impractical for any real-world sentiment analysis application. The authors' discussion of feasibility in Section 7 is highly speculative and unconvincing. It relies on the future ubiquity of mobile eye-trackers and users' willingness to be constantly monitored, which is far from the current reality. Without a credible path to deployment, the method remains a purely theoretical exercise, and the utility of the contribution is severely limited.
2.  **Insufficient Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The paper explicitly dismisses comparisons to deep learning models (e.g., CNNs, RNNs), which were established as powerful methods for sentiment analysis. The justification that the proposed approach is "feature based" is inadequate. To properly assess the value of a new method, it must be benchmarked against the strongest available alternatives, regardless of their architecture. The provided baselines are reasonable feature-based systems, but their performance relative to the true state-of-the-art is unknown, making it difficult to gauge the significance of the reported improvements.
3.  **Marginal Performance Gains vs. High Cost:** The overall performance improvement gained by adding cognitive features is very small—an F-score increase of 0.6% on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2 over the authors' strongest text-only model. While statistically significant, this minor gain does not justify the immense operational cost and complexity of collecting multi-participant eye-tracking data for every new input. The cost-benefit trade-off is exceptionally poor, which further undermines the practical relevance of the approach.
4.  **Ad-Hoc Feature Engineering:** Some of the proposed cognitive features, particularly the "complex gaze features" derived from the "gaze-saliency graph," appear somewhat ad-hoc. The motivation that they are based on "qualitative observations" is not a rigorous justification. A more principled theoretical grounding for why these specific graph properties should capture sentiment-relevant cognitive signals is needed. Furthermore, the simple averaging of gaze metrics across participants is a crude aggregation method that likely loses significant information contained in individual reading patterns.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The proposed method requires eye-tracking data for new, unseen texts at test time. Could you outline a single, concrete, and realistic application scenario where this requirement is feasible? The scenarios in Section 7 appear to conflate general user-experience eye-tracking with the specific, multi-annotator data collection pipeline your model requires.
2.  Have you considered an alternative application for these cognitive signals? For example, instead of being features for test-time classification, could gaze data be used during the data annotation phase to identify difficult or ambiguous examples (i.e., a form of active learning or curriculum learning)? This seems like a more practical way to leverage the insight that gaze reflects text complexity.
3.  Could you provide a stronger justification for omitting comparisons to contemporary neural network models? Given that these models often achieve state-of-the-art performance by automatically learning features, it is crucial to know if your elaborate feature engineering pipeline offers any advantage over them.
