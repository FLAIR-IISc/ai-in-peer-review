### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis (SA) by incorporating "cognitive features" derived from human eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, recorded as users read and annotate texts, contain signals that can help resolve complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations, which are challenging for traditional feature-based SA systems.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **Novel Feature Engineering:** The primary contribution is the introduction and formalization of a set of cognitive features for a downstream NLP task. The authors design a comprehensive set of features, ranging from basic gaze metrics (e.g., fixation duration, regression counts) to more complex, graph-based representations of gaze patterns (the "gaze-saliency graph"). This is a creative and original way to bridge psycholinguistics and computational linguistics.
2.  **Empirical Validation of Cognitive Signals for SA:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that these cognitive features significantly improve sentiment classification performance, particularly on datasets rich with sarcasm and irony. The authors conduct a careful, ablation-style study showing that adding their proposed features to a strong baseline of linguistic and sentiment features yields statistically significant gains in F1-score on two different datasets.
3.  **Targeted Analysis of Complex Phenomena:** A key contribution is the focused experiment on a held-out dataset (Section 5.1, Table 4), which demonstrates that the performance improvement from cognitive features is substantially larger for ironic/sarcastic texts (a 6.1% relative improvement) than for simpler texts (2.1%). This provides compelling evidence that the features are not just capturing general text difficulty, but are indeed helping with the specific linguistic challenges the authors set out to solve.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Originality:** The core idea of using eye-tracking data as features for sentiment analysis is highly innovative. While eye-tracking has been used to *study* NLP tasks, its direct integration as a feature source in a predictive model is rare and opens up a new and interesting research direction. The proposed "gaze-saliency graph" is a particularly creative method for extracting complex relational information from gaze patterns.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Design:** The evaluation is well-structured and rigorous. The authors use two public datasets, enhancing reproducibility. They systematically compare feature sets, clearly demonstrating the additive value of sarcasm-specific features (Sr) and, subsequently, the cognitive gaze features (Gz). The use of multiple classifiers (SVM, NB, NN) strengthens their claims of generalizability. The statistical significance testing (McNemar test) adds rigor to their reported improvements.
3.  **Strong Supporting Analysis:** The paper goes beyond just reporting final F1-scores. The feature significance analysis, which shows that a large proportion of the top-ranked features are gaze-based, reinforces their importance. Most compellingly, the experiment in Section 5.1 isolates the effect of the features on complex vs. simple texts, providing direct and convincing support for the paper's central hypothesis.
4.  **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. It begins by clearly motivating the problem with a well-organized summary of challenges in SA. The features are described in detail with clear intuitions. The experimental setup and results are presented logically. The inclusion of a dedicated section (Section 7) on the feasibility of the approach shows foresight and a thoughtful consideration of the work's practical limitations.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Major Practicality and Scalability Concerns:** The most significant weakness is the fundamental requirement for eye-tracking data at inference time. The proposed method assumes that for any new text to be classified, one must first collect gaze data from multiple human readers. This makes the system impractical for almost any real-world application of sentiment analysis (e.g., analyzing millions of tweets or product reviews). The authors address this in Section 7, but their arguments rely on future, not-yet-ubiquitous technology (e.g., inexpensive mobile eye-trackers) and optimistic assumptions about user consent. This positions the work as a fascinating but "laboratory-only" proof-of-concept, not a deployable method.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Models:** The authors explicitly state they do not compare against deep learning approaches (e.g., BERT-based classifiers) because their method is feature-based. While this rationale is understandable, it leaves a critical question unanswered: is this feature-based SVM with gaze data better than a fine-tuned transformer model without it? In 2023, large pre-trained language models are the de-facto state of the art for sentiment analysis, and their ability to capture complex context is substantial. Without this comparison, it's impossible to gauge where this method truly stands in the broader landscape of SA performance. The reported baselines in Table 1 seem relatively weak, which may overstate the difficulty of the datasets for modern methods.
3.  **Potential Confounding of Cognitive Signals:** While the analysis in Section 5.1 is strong, there's still a risk that the gaze features are acting as a sophisticated proxy for general "processing difficulty" or "surprisal" rather than sentiment-specific cues. Sarcastic sentences are often syntactically or pragmatically complex, which would naturally elicit longer fixations and more regressions. The paper attempts to control for this with readability and length features, but the rich gaze features may be capturing a much stronger signal of general complexity that happens to correlate well with sarcasm and negative sentiment in these specific datasets.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify the intended application scenario for this method? Is the goal to use gaze data at inference time for live classification (as suggested in Section 7.2), or could these features be used in a different paradigm, for example, to automatically identify and label difficult examples in a large unlabeled corpus to create better training data for a standard (non-gaze-based) classifier?
2.  While a full deep learning implementation may be out of scope, could you speculate on how cognitive features might be integrated with transformer-based models? For instance, could the vector of gaze features be concatenated with the [CLS] token representation before the final classification layer? This could test whether cognitive signals provide information that is orthogonal and complementary to the rich contextual representations learned by models like BERT.
3.  The "gaze-saliency graph" features are quite complex. Could you provide more qualitative intuition or examples of what specific linguistic phenomena these graph-based features (e.g., weighted degree) capture that simpler metrics like total regression count (`REG`) or regressions from the second half to the first (`RSF`) do not?
