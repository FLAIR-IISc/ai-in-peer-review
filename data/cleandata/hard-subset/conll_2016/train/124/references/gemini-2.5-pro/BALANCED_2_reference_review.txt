### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating "cognitive features" derived from the eye-tracking data of human annotators. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns reflect the cognitive processes involved in understanding complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony, and can thus serve as powerful signals for sentiment classification. The authors design a rich set of features, including basic gaze metrics (e.g., fixation duration, regressions) and more complex graph-based features derived from "gaze-saliency graphs." They evaluate their approach on two datasets containing challenging short texts, showing that augmenting traditional textual features with these cognitive features leads to statistically significant improvements in classification accuracy, particularly for texts containing sarcasm or irony.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Feature Set for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the introduction and systematic evaluation of cognitive features from eye-tracking data for sentiment analysis. This represents a genuinely novel direction for feature engineering in this domain, bridging psycholinguistics with a practical NLP task.
2.  **Empirical Validation on Challenging Data:** The paper provides a solid empirical demonstration that these novel features are particularly effective for texts with complex semantic and pragmatic challenges (e.g., sarcasm), a known difficult area for traditional methods. The targeted analysis on a held-out set explicitly separating ironic from non-ironic texts strongly supports this claim.
3.  **Demonstration of Multimodal NLP Potential:** The work serves as a strong proof-of-concept for leveraging multimodal signals (text + human cognitive data) to tackle nuanced language understanding tasks. By showing the utility of gaze data, it encourages the collection and use of such resources for NLP research.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea of using cognitive signals as features for a high-level NLP task is highly innovative and thought-provoking. It opens up a new research direction that could be extended to other challenging NLP problems where subtle interpretation is key, such as detecting deception, understanding metaphors, or identifying fake news. The work successfully makes a case for looking beyond the text itself to the human processes involved in interpreting it.
2.  **Thorough and Methodical Experimentation:** The experimental design is rigorous and well-executed. The authors begin by establishing strong baselines and demonstrating that their chosen datasets are indeed challenging for standard SA systems (Table 1), which effectively motivates the need for their approach. The feature ablation study (Table 2) is systematic and clearly illustrates the incremental benefit of adding sarcasm-related and, subsequently, gaze-based features. The specific analysis on the held-out dataset (Table 4) is particularly compelling, as it directly tests and confirms the hypothesis that cognitive features disproportionately help in classifying complex, ironic texts.
3.  **Well-Motivated and Interpretable Feature Engineering:** The authors do an excellent job of grounding their cognitive feature design in psycholinguistic literature. Each feature (e.g., first-fixation duration, regression count, saccade length) is presented with a clear intuition about what cognitive process it might capture (e.g., lexical processing, syntactic reanalysis). The introduction of the "gaze-saliency graph" is also an interesting method for modeling more complex gaze patterns. This interpretability is a welcome contrast to more black-box approaches.
4.  **Proactive Discussion of Limitations:** The authors thoughtfully address the main practical limitation of their work—the requirement of eye-tracking data—in Section 7 ("Feasibility of our approach"). By discussing the potential of future mobile eye-tracking technology and outlining possible application scenarios, they frame their work as forward-looking research, anticipating and preemptively answering a key question any reviewer or reader would have.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Practicality and Generalizability Concerns:** The most critical weakness is that the proposed method requires eye-tracking data for inference on new, unseen text. This is an extremely restrictive requirement that limits its applicability to controlled lab settings. For any real-world application (e.g., analyzing product reviews on a website), this is infeasible. The discussion in Section 7 is speculative and relies on future technological developments. A more compelling approach would be to use the cognitive data during training to build a better text-only model. For instance, one could train a model to *predict* cognitive features from text, and then use these predicted features at test time. Without such a step, the method remains a compelling academic exercise with limited immediate practical utility.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Modern State-of-the-Art:** The paper explicitly omits comparison with deep learning models (e.g., CNNs, RNNs, or Transformer-based models like BERT), which are the current state-of-the-art for most sentiment analysis benchmarks. While the work is feature-based, this omission makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the performance gains. The absolute improvements from adding gaze features are modest (0.6% F1 on Dataset 1, 1.5% on Dataset 2). It is crucial to know whether the final system outperforms a fine-tuned BERT model, which can capture complex textual patterns without external signals. A comparison against a strong neural baseline is necessary to contextualize the results and understand if the proposed features offer advantages over what can be learned automatically by powerful pre-trained models.
3.  **Potential for Confounding Variables:** The paper attributes gaze pattern variations primarily to sentiment-related complexities like sarcasm. However, eye-movements are also heavily influenced by more general textual properties like lexical frequency, syntactic complexity (e.g., subordinate clauses), and topic familiarity. While the authors include basic readability scores, the analysis does not fully disentangle these effects. It's possible that the gaze features are acting as a proxy for general reading difficulty rather than sentiment-specific complexity. The work would be stronger if it demonstrated that the cognitive features provide discriminative information *beyond* what could be captured by a richer set of text-only complexity features (e.g., parse tree metrics, word frequency statistics).
4.  **Information Loss Through Data Aggregation:** The cognitive features are derived by averaging eye-tracking metrics across all participants for a given text snippet. This approach discards potentially valuable information contained in the *variance* of reading behavior. A high degree of disagreement in gaze patterns across readers for a particular sentence could itself be a powerful signal of ambiguity, irony, or sarcasm. Exploring features based on the distribution (e.g., variance, entropy) of gaze metrics across readers, rather than just the mean, could potentially yield further improvements.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The primary hurdle for your method's adoption is the need for eye-tracking data at inference time. Have you considered a multi-stage approach where you first train a model to predict the cognitive features from textual features alone, and then use these *predicted* cognitive features in your final sentiment classifier? This would result in a model that is deployable on raw text but still benefits from the insights gained from the gaze data during its training phase.
2.  To better situate your work's performance, could you provide results for a strong, standard neural baseline (e.g., a fine-tuned BERT-base model) on your two datasets? This would help clarify the absolute performance of your best system in the context of the current state-of-the-art and better quantify the value added by the gaze features.
3.  Your current method aggregates gaze data by averaging across participants. Did you investigate using the variance or other distributional statistics of gaze metrics across participants as features? It seems plausible that high variance in reading behavior could be a strong indicator of a text being pragmatically complex or open to multiple interpretations.
