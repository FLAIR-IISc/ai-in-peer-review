### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating "cognitive features" derived from the eye-tracking data of human readers. The central hypothesis is that human gaze patterns, which reflect cognitive processing, can provide valuable signals for deciphering complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations, which are challenging for traditional text-based models. The authors engineer a set of features from raw gaze data—including basic metrics like fixation duration and regressions, as well as more complex features from a proposed "gaze-saliency graph"—and show that augmenting a standard feature-based sentiment classifier with these cognitive features improves performance, especially on texts containing such complex constructs.

From my perspective, the contributions are:
1.  **A New Paradigm for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary and most significant contribution is the conceptual leap of using psycholinguistic signals (via eye-tracking) as a direct input for a core NLP task. This is a bold and exciting new direction that moves beyond purely textual information to ground the analysis in the human process of language understanding. It opens up a new avenue of research for tackling the notoriously difficult pragmatic and semantic layers of language.
2.  **A Concrete Feature Set for Gaze-Informed NLP:** The paper provides a well-motivated set of engineered cognitive features. The creation of the "gaze-saliency graph" is a particularly creative method for extracting higher-order information from reading patterns, offering a useful blueprint for future work in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Boldness:** The core idea is highly innovative and refreshing. In a field often characterized by incremental improvements on existing architectures, this work proposes an entirely new modality of information. By attempting to capture the cognitive "fingerprint" of understanding complex sentiment, the authors are tackling a fundamental challenge in NLP from a genuinely new angle. This is exactly the kind of high-risk, high-reward research that can push the field in new directions.
2.  **Compelling Evidence for the Central Hypothesis:** The analysis on the held-out dataset (Table 4) provides strong support for the paper's main claim. The finding that cognitive features provide a substantially larger boost in F-score for ironic texts (+6.1%) than for simpler texts (+2.1%) is a key result. It compellingly argues that these features are not just capturing generic reading difficulty, but are indeed sensitive to the specific cognitive load associated with resolving non-literal meaning.
3.  **Thoughtful Problem Formulation and Analysis:** The authors do a good job of situating their work. They clearly demonstrate that the chosen datasets are challenging for standard sentiment analyzers (Table 1), motivating the need for their novel approach. The feature significance analysis (Table 3), which shows a high prevalence of gaze-based features among the most informative ones, further strengthens the case for their contribution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The "Deployment Problem":** The most glaring weakness is the method's reliance on eye-tracking data at inference time. This presents a massive, immediate barrier to any practical application. The authors' discussion of future mobile eye-trackers (Section 7) is speculative and forward-looking, but it does not address the fundamental impracticality of the current pipeline. The work would be far more impactful if it explored methods to make the approach viable without the hardware dependency at test time, for instance, by training a model to *predict* gaze features from text alone. As it stands, the method is confined to a lab setting.
2.  **Modest Overall Performance Gains:** While the novelty is high, the absolute F-score improvements from adding the final set of gaze features (Gz) are quite small: 0.6% on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2 over the best text-only model. While my review philosophy prioritizes novelty, these small gains suggest that the current feature set may have a low signal-to-noise ratio or that its benefits are limited to a specific subset of the data (as shown in the held-out experiment). This tempers the excitement about the immediate effectiveness of the proposed features.
3.  **Limited Model Sophistication:** The experiments rely on classic machine learning models (SVM, NB, MLP) and explicitly exclude modern deep learning architectures. While the contribution lies in the features, not the model, this is a significant omission. It is an open question whether these cognitive signals would provide a complementary benefit to powerful pre-trained language models (like BERT), or if their signal would be rendered redundant by the sophisticated representations learned by such models. A lack of comparison to more current baselines makes it hard to gauge the true state-of-the-art potential of this approach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The requirement of an eye-tracker at inference time is a major practical limitation. Have you considered or experimented with a two-stage "distillation" approach? For example, one could first use the text and gaze data to train a model that *predicts* cognitive features from text alone. The output of this model (predicted gaze features) could then be used in the final sentiment classifier, thus removing the dependency on eye-tracking hardware for deployment.
2.  The cognitive features are created by averaging gaze metrics across all human annotators for a given text snippet. This seems to discard potentially rich information contained in the variance of reading patterns. High variance across readers could itself be a powerful signal of ambiguity, controversy, or sarcasm. Did you explore features that capture this inter-annotator disagreement (e.g., the standard deviation of first-fixation duration)?
3.  The analysis in Table 4 strongly suggests the features are helpful for irony. However, is it possible that these features are primarily capturing a more general notion of "syntactic or semantic surprise" rather than something specific to sentiment? For example, a syntactically complex but sentiment-neutral sentence might also elicit long regressions. Could you elaborate on why you believe these gaze patterns are uniquely indicative of complex *sentiment* processing, beyond just being markers of general processing difficulty that isn't captured by your baseline readability features?
