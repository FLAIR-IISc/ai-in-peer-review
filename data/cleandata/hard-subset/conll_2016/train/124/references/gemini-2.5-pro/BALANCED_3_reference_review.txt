**Summary of the Paper**
This paper proposes a novel approach to improve sentiment analysis (SA) by incorporating "cognitive features" derived from the eye-movement patterns of human readers. The central hypothesis is that the cognitive processes involved in understanding complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony are reflected in gaze patterns, such as fixations, saccades, and regressions. The authors create an enhanced feature set that combines traditional textual features (e.g., n-grams, sentiment lexicons), features designed for sarcasm and thwarting, and a new suite of basic and complex gaze-based features. They evaluate this approach on two publicly available datasets containing eye-tracking data for sentiment-annotated texts. Experiments using SVM, Naïve Bayes, and Neural Network classifiers show that the inclusion of cognitive features leads to statistically significant improvements in SA performance, particularly for texts containing complex constructs like irony.

**Contributions**
1.  **Novel Feature Set for NLP:** The primary contribution is the introduction and validation of eye-tracking data as a source of features for a downstream NLP task. By operationalizing gaze patterns (e.g., regression counts, fixation durations, graph-based saliency metrics) into features for a machine learning model, the work opens a novel direction for integrating psycholinguistic signals into computational models to tackle semantic and pragmatic challenges.
2.  **Improved Handling of Complex Sentiment:** The paper provides strong evidence that these cognitive features are particularly effective for identifying sentiment in texts with complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. The dedicated experiment on a held-out dataset, which shows a much larger performance gain on ironic texts (6.1%) compared to non-ironic ones (2.1%), is a significant contribution that validates the authors' core hypothesis.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Novelty and Interdisciplinary Value:** The core idea of leveraging cognitive signals from eye-tracking for sentiment analysis is highly innovative. It bridges the gap between computational linguistics and psycholinguistics in a practical, task-oriented manner. This approach presents a new way to capture the subtle, non-lexical cues that are crucial for understanding complex language but are often missed by traditional text-based models. This line of research could inspire similar work in other NLP tasks that involve deep semantic understanding, such as humor detection, stance detection, or abusive language detection.

2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is strong. The authors use two distinct datasets collected with different eye-trackers (one high-quality, one low-quality), demonstrating a degree of robustness. They test their feature sets across three different classifiers and perform statistical significance testing to validate their results. The feature significance analysis (Table 3) and, most importantly, the targeted evaluation on a held-out set of ironic vs. non-ironic texts (Table 4) provide compelling and direct evidence for the utility of the proposed features where they are most needed.

3.  **Well-Motivated and Clearly Presented:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work by clearly outlining the lexical, syntactic, and semantic challenges in sentiment analysis. The feature engineering is extensive and well-explained, categorizing features logically and providing intuitions for why each cognitive feature might be useful (e.g., linking long regressions from the second half to the first half of a sentence with the processing of incongruity in sarcasm). The paper is well-structured and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Significant Practicality and Scalability Concerns:** The most critical weakness of this work is its feasibility outside a laboratory setting. The proposed method requires eye-tracking data to be collected for the specific text for which sentiment is to be predicted. This is a major bottleneck for any real-world application. How would one classify the sentiment of a new tweet or a product review at scale? The authors' discussion in Section 7 on the future availability of mobile eye-trackers is speculative and does not address the fundamental chicken-and-egg problem: you need the gaze data to make a prediction, but you can't get that data without having someone read the text first. This limits the current method to post-hoc analysis rather than a deployable, predictive system.

2.  **Potential Confounding Variables in Gaze Features:** Eye movements are influenced by numerous factors beyond the processing of sentiment, including lexical frequency, syntactic complexity, word length, and reader fatigue. While the authors include some features to control for reading difficulty (e.g., Flesch score, sentence length), it is not entirely clear if the model has truly isolated the cognitive signals for sentiment from those for general processing difficulty. For instance, sarcastic sentences are often more complexly constructed. The model's improvement could stem from learning to identify "cognitively difficult" sentences, which happen to correlate with sarcasm in the provided datasets, rather than learning signals specific to sentiment processing itself. A more detailed analysis to disentangle these effects would strengthen the claims.

3.  **Complexity of Feature Engineering:** The paper relies on a large set of hand-crafted features, including complex graph-based gaze features. While effective, this extensive feature engineering is somewhat contrary to the current trend towards end-to-end models that learn feature representations automatically. The authors acknowledge this by stating that deep learning is out of scope, but the comparison is still relevant. Furthermore, while the chi-squared analysis shows the importance of gaze features as a group, a more granular ablation study on the cognitive features themselves (e.g., basic vs. graph-based) would have provided deeper insight into which specific cognitive phenomena are most predictive.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you clarify the intended deployment scenario for this system? Is the goal to build a model that requires new eye-tracking data for every inference on a new text? Or is there a pathway to a model that could, for example, first predict gaze patterns from text alone and then use those *predicted* gaze features for the final sentiment classification, thereby removing the dependency on live eye-tracking for inference?

2.  The features are derived by averaging gaze data across multiple participants. This likely smooths over valuable individual differences in reading strategies. Have you explored alternative aggregation methods or models that can leverage per-participant data to see if more signal can be extracted before averaging?

3.  The complex graph-based features (e.g., highest and second-highest weighted degrees) are motivated by "qualitative observations." Could you provide a more concrete linguistic or psycholinguistic intuition for what a feature like "Second largest weighted degree of the saliency graph considering the number of regressive saccades" (RSS) might be capturing in the context of processing sarcastic or ironic language?
