**Summary of the paper**
This paper proposes to improve sentiment analysis, particularly for texts with complex constructs like sarcasm and irony, by incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The authors introduce a novel feature set combining traditional sentiment and sarcasm-related textual features with a variety of basic and complex "gaze features" extracted from the eye-movement patterns of human annotators. They evaluate their approach on two publicly available datasets, showing that the inclusion of cognitive features provides a statistically significant, albeit modest, improvement in classification performance over textual features alone. The authors claim their main contributions are (1) the novel augmentation of SA systems with cognitive features from eye-tracking data, (2) a detailed feature set including complex graph-based gaze features, and (3) an analysis showing these features are particularly effective for handling sarcasm and irony.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novelty of the Core Idea:** The primary strength of this work is the novel application of cognitive signals from eye-tracking to the task of sentiment analysis. Connecting psycholinguistic phenomena (via gaze patterns) to computational challenges like sarcasm detection is an interesting and promising research direction. The work provides a solid proof-of-concept that such signals contain relevant information.
2.  **Targeted Analysis of Complex Phenomena:** The paper's hypothesis is that cognitive features are most useful for nuanced linguistic phenomena that are difficult to capture with surface-level text features. The experiment on the held-out dataset (Section 5.1, Table 4), which shows a much larger performance gain on ironic texts (6.1%) compared to non-ironic texts (2.1%), provides strong evidence for this claim and is a highlight of the paper.
3.  **Reproducibility:** The use of two publicly available datasets is commendable and facilitates the verification and extension of this work by other researchers.

**Weaknesses**
Despite the interesting premise, the paper is marred by significant methodological weaknesses and a general lack of rigor in its execution and presentation.

1.  **Inadequate Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The authors explicitly dismiss deep learning-based approaches, stating "we do not consider these approaches for our current experimentation" because their method is feature-based. This is not a valid justification. To claim effectiveness, a new method must be compared against strong, contemporary baselines, regardless of their architecture. The baselines used (NB, SVM, RB with traditional features) are weak by modern standards. Without comparison to even a simple CNN or LSTM-based model, the true contribution and performance of the proposed features remain unclear.
2.  **Fundamental Mismatch between Method and Application:** The experimental setup relies on aggregating gaze features by *averaging across multiple annotators* (5-7 per text snippet). However, the feasibility and application scenario proposed in Section 7 envisions a real-time system using gaze data from a *single user* on a mobile device. There is a critical disconnect here. It is not demonstrated, or even discussed, whether features from a single, potentially non-expert user would be stable or informative enough to yield similar results. The entire premise of the practical application is therefore unsubstantiated by the experiments.
3.  **Severe Lack of Clarity and Missing Details:**
    *   The description of the "complex gaze features" in Section 4.5 is nearly incomprehensible. The definition of features like F1H ("Largest weighted degree...of the saliency graph considering the fixation duration on the word of node i of edge Eij as edge weight") is confusingly worded. It is unclear if these are properties of a node or the graph, and how edge weights and node properties are combined. This section is critical as it describes a novel contribution, but it is not explained well enough to be understood or reproduced.
    *   Key experimental details are missing. The architecture of the Multi-layer NN is not described. The authors state that default hyperparameters were used, which is not rigorous; tuning on a development set is standard practice.
    *   Table 3, which is referenced for the feature significance analysis, is missing from the paper entirely.
4.  **Questionable Experimental Results:** In Table 2, for Dataset 2, adding gaze features (`Gz`) to sentiment features (`Sn`) causes the SVM's F-score to plummet from 62.5 to 48.9. This is a massive, counter-intuitive drop that strongly suggests an error in the experiment or reporting. The authors do not acknowledge or explain this bizarre result.
5.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The paper suffers from a startling number of minor, but collectively significant, errors that undermine its credibility.
    *   The list of references is exceptionally sloppy, riddled with inconsistent formatting, extraneous question marks (`et al\.,?`), and leftover LaTeX commands (`\Q`, `\E`).
    *   There are typos and formatting errors in the main text (e.g., "F˙score" in Table 1, a missing Section 6 in the introduction's outline).
    *   The caption for Figure 2 is improperly formatted and contains a strange hyphenation ("mis-conception").
    *   The citation for Maas et al. (2011) is used to support two different, contradictory claims (WordNet-based vs. Deep Learning approach) in Sections 1.1 and 2.

In summary, while the core idea is promising, the paper in its current form does not meet the standards for publication at a top-tier conference. The methodological flaws are substantial, the presentation is careless, and the experimental results contain unexplained and suspicious artifacts.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Can you please clarify the exact procedure for calculating the "complex gaze features" from Section 4.5? For example, for feature F1H, what is the precise mathematical definition?
2.  Could you please explain the dramatic drop in performance for the SVM classifier on Dataset 2 when gaze features are added to sentiment features (`Sn` vs. `Sn+Gz` in Table 2)? This result seems anomalous and warrants an explanation.
3.  Given the significant performance of modern neural networks on sentiment analysis, could you justify why they were not included as a stronger baseline? Even if your contribution is feature-based, the value of those features is best measured by their ability to improve upon a strong baseline.
4.  How do you propose to bridge the gap between your experimental methodology (averaging features over multiple annotators in an offline setting) and your proposed application (using features from a single user in a real-time setting)? What evidence do you have that single-user gaze data is sufficiently reliable for this task?
