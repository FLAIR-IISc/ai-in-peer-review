**Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the use of cognitive features, derived from human eye-tracking data, to improve sentiment analysis of short texts. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, which reflect cognitive processing, can provide signals to help classify challenging texts containing sarcasm, irony, and other nuances that are difficult to capture with purely text-based features. The authors introduce a feature set including basic gaze metrics (fixation duration, regressions) and more complex features derived from a novel "gaze-saliency graph." They conduct experiments on two existing datasets that contain both sentiment labels and corresponding eye-tracking data, showing that a model combining traditional linguistic features with these new cognitive features outperforms models using linguistic features alone.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1.  An exploratory study demonstrating a correlation between eye-movement patterns and the sentiment polarity of complex texts, particularly those containing irony.
2.  The introduction of a novel, albeit specialized, set of "cognitive features" for sentiment classification, including some derived from a graph-based representation of gaze patterns.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel Problem Formulation:** The paper explores a novel, interdisciplinary direction by attempting to link psycholinguistic phenomena (via eye-tracking) to a core NLP task. This is an interesting, thought-provoking idea that challenges the conventional text-only paradigm of sentiment analysis.
2.  **Clear Problem Motivation:** The authors correctly identify a persistent weakness in sentiment analysis systems: the difficulty of handling non-literal language like sarcasm and irony. The motivation to find alternative signals to address this challenge is well-founded.
3.  **Systematic Internal Ablation Study:** The experiments are methodical in their comparison of different feature sets (Sentiment, Sarcasm, Gaze). The authors systematically add feature groups and report performance, conduct statistical significance tests, and perform a feature importance analysis, which lends some internal validity to their claims about the utility of the proposed features within their experimental framework. The targeted experiment on a held-out set of ironic vs. non-ironic texts provides specific evidence for their central hypothesis.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Fundamental Impracticality and Lack of Generalizability:** The most significant weakness of this work is that the proposed method is fundamentally impractical for any real-world application of sentiment analysis. The approach requires eye-tracking data to be collected for every single text instance at inference time. This is not a model that can be trained and then deployed on new text from the web; it is a system that analyzes a text *and* its associated reading patterns. This redefines the task into a niche laboratory problem, not a general NLP solution. The discussion in Section 7 regarding the future availability of mobile eye-trackers is highly speculative and does not address the immediate and profound limitations of the approach. For a contribution to be valuable to the NLP community, it must either be applicable to standard task formulations or convincingly argue for a new, viable paradigm. This paper does neither.

2.  **Lack of Competitive Baselines:** The paper fails to compare its approach against established, state-of-the-art methods for sentiment analysis. The authors explicitly set aside comparisons to deep learning models, which currently represent the state of the art. A feature-engineered SVM is a weak baseline in 2024. To demonstrate that cognitive features provide truly new, valuable information, it is essential to show that they can improve upon a powerful model that is already adept at capturing textual nuance, such as a fine-tuned BERT or RoBERTa. Without this comparison, it is highly plausible that the observed gains are simply compensating for the deficiencies of the underlying text-based model, and that a strong language model could achieve similar or better performance using text alone.

3.  **Confounding Variables:** The paper claims that cognitive features help detect complex constructs like irony and sarcasm. However, the analysis does not sufficiently disentangle these specific phenomena from more general effects like processing difficulty, semantic ambiguity, or syntactic complexity. Eye-movement patterns such as increased fixations or regressions are well-known correlates of general reading difficulty. It is possible that the model is not learning to detect "sarcasm" per se, but rather "sentences that cause a reader to slow down or re-read," which are merely correlated with sarcasm in the specific datasets used. The inclusion of Flesch scores is a minimal attempt to control for this, but a more thorough analysis is needed to substantiate the claims.

4.  **Limited Scale of Experiments:** The conclusions are drawn from two small, specialized datasets (~1000 instances each). While these datasets are unique, their size limits the reliability and generalizability of the findings. The reported performance gains from adding gaze features are modest in absolute terms (e.g., a 0.6% F1-score increase on Dataset 1 for the best model). Such small gains on small datasets require more extensive validation before they can be considered conclusive evidence of a method's effectiveness.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The core premise of your method requires eye-tracking data for a given text to classify its sentiment. Can you describe a single, non-speculative, real-world scenario where sentiment analysis is needed and this type of data would be available at scale for inference? How does your approach offer a path toward a deployable system, rather than just a laboratory finding?
2.  The exclusion of modern deep learning baselines is a critical omission. A proper evaluation of the value of cognitive features requires demonstrating their benefit on top of a strong baseline. Could you justify why a comparison to a fine-tuned transformer-based model (e.g., BERT) was not performed? How can you be confident that your gaze features capture information that such a model cannot already infer from the text alone?
3.  How do you propose to disentangle cognitive signals for sarcasm from those for general reading difficulty? For instance, would your model perform well on a dataset containing difficult, but non-sarcastic, technical sentences mixed with simple, sarcastic sentences? Your feature analysis suggests gaze features are important, but this may be because they are a proxy for text complexity, which is a useful feature in itself.
