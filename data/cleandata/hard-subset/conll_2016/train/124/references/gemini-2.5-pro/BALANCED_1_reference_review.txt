### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis (SA) by augmenting traditional text-based features with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that gaze patterns captured while users read and annotate text can provide crucial signals for deciphering complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony, where purely textual analysis often fails. The authors design a comprehensive set of "gaze features"—ranging from basic metrics like fixation duration and regression counts to more complex ones derived from a proposed "gaze-saliency graph." Through experiments on two challenging datasets containing such complex texts, they demonstrate that their combined feature set significantly improves SA accuracy over strong feature-based baselines.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **Novel Feature Engineering from Cognitive Signals:** The primary contribution is the introduction and systematic engineering of a rich set of features from eye-tracking data for a core NLP task. This provides a novel and well-motivated bridge between psycholinguistic evidence and computational sentiment analysis, with the "gaze-saliency graph" being a particularly creative method for structuring this data.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation for Complex Language:** The paper provides compelling evidence that cognitive features are most impactful when classifying linguistically complex texts. The targeted experiment showing a significantly larger performance boost on ironic versus non-ironic sentences is a key result that validates the authors' core hypothesis.
3.  **Creation of a Strong Hybrid Benchmark:** By combining features from general SA, sarcasm detection, and cognitive science, the authors establish a new and powerful feature-based system for these specific datasets, offering a strong benchmark for future research at the intersection of NLP and cognitive science.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and Innovative Core Idea:** The paper is built on a strong, intuitive premise: that the way we read reflects our cognitive processing and can reveal nuances of meaning missed by text-only models. The introduction effectively outlines the limitations of existing SA approaches and presents the use of cognitive signals as a plausible solution. This cross-disciplinary approach is a significant strength.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experimental Design:** The evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors use two distinct datasets to improve the generalizability of their findings. They first establish the difficulty of these datasets for standard methods (Table 1), justifying the need for their approach. The ablation studies (Table 2) clearly and incrementally show the value of adding sarcasm-specific and then gaze-based features.
3.  **Targeted Analysis Supporting the Central Claim:** The analysis in Section 5.1 and Table 4 is a major strength. By isolating ironic/sarcastic texts and showing that the performance gain from gaze features is substantially higher for this subset (6.1% improvement vs. 2.1% for simpler texts), the authors provide direct and powerful evidence for their main hypothesis. This moves beyond a general accuracy improvement to show *why* the features are useful.
4.  **Consideration of Practical Implications:** The authors proactively address the most obvious criticism of their work—its practical feasibility—in Section 7. While speculative, this discussion on emerging mobile eye-tracking technology demonstrates foresight and a mature consideration of the research's potential long-term impact, which strengthens the paper.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Practicality and Unclear Deployment Scenario:** The most significant weakness is the reliance on having eye-tracking data available at inference time. For any new text to be classified, it must first be read by a human whose gaze is being tracked. This makes the system inapplicable for the vast majority of real-world SA tasks (e.g., analyzing a stream of tweets). The paper frames the work as a practical system, but it is more fundamentally a scientific study demonstrating the information content of gaze data. The paper would be stronger if it clarified this distinction and discussed how the *insights* from this work could be used, for example, to train a model that predicts gaze patterns from text alone.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Modern State-of-the-Art Models:** The paper explicitly omits comparisons to deep learning models like BERT, which are the current state-of-the-art for sentiment analysis. While the work is feature-based, this omission makes it difficult to assess the true value of the cognitive features. A fine-tuned BERT model may well outperform the text-only baseline (Sn+Sr) and could potentially rival the final model with gaze features. The crucial scientific question is whether these engineered cognitive features provide information that a large pre-trained language model *cannot* capture from text. Without this comparison, the paper's contribution is benchmarked against an outdated class of models.
3.  **Potential Confounding Between Textual and Gaze Features:** The analysis does not explore the correlation between the textual and cognitive feature sets. It is highly likely that textual properties (e.g., lexical complexity, syntactic ambiguity, presence of incongruous phrases) that are captured by the textual features directly cause the gaze patterns (e.g., longer fixations, more regressions) captured by the cognitive features. The observed improvement could stem from the gaze features being a more direct or less noisy signal of this underlying textual difficulty, rather than providing entirely new, orthogonal information. An analysis of feature correlation would help disentangle this and strengthen the claim that gaze data offers a unique source of information.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the intended deployment scenario for this system? Is the goal to classify text for which gaze data will always be available (e.g., within a specific user-testing application), or have you considered methods to generalize this model to work on text alone at inference time, perhaps by training a model to predict gaze features from text?
2.  While I understand the focus on a feature-engineering approach, the lack of comparison to transformer-based models like BERT makes it hard to contextualize the results within the current state of NLP. Could you comment on how you believe your cognitive features would perform in conjunction with such models (e.g., as additional input to a classification head)? Do you hypothesize that gaze data captures information that is truly beyond the capabilities of large language models?
3.  Have you performed any analysis on the redundancy or correlation between your text-based feature sets (Sn, Sr) and the gaze-based features (Gz)? How can you be certain that the strong performance of gaze features does not simply stem from them being a better proxy for textual complexity that is already partially captured by the text features?
