### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis (SA) by incorporating "cognitive features" derived from human eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, which reflect cognitive processing, can provide valuable signals for detecting sentiment, especially in linguistically complex texts containing sarcasm, irony, or thwarted expectations. The authors define a comprehensive feature set combining traditional SA features, sarcasm-specific features, and a novel suite of basic and complex gaze-based features (e.g., fixation durations, regressions, graph-based metrics from a "gaze-saliency graph"). They evaluate their approach on two publicly available datasets, demonstrating that augmenting a feature-based classifier with cognitive features yields statistically significant improvements in F1-score, with the most pronounced gains observed on texts containing irony and sarcasm.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Novel Feature Set for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the introduction and validation of cognitive features from eye-tracking data as a new source of information for sentiment analysis. This bridges the gap between psycholinguistic research on reading and a core NLP task, offering a genuinely new perspective on feature engineering.
2.  **Targeted Empirical Validation:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that these cognitive features are not just adding noise but are particularly effective at resolving sentiment in complex cases like sarcasm. The experiment on the held-out dataset (Table 4), which explicitly separates ironic from non-ironic texts, is a key piece of analysis that convincingly supports the paper's core hypothesis.
3.  **Methodology for Gaze Feature Extraction:** The authors propose a structured way to extract features from raw gaze data, including the novel "gaze-saliency graph." This provides a useful methodology that could be adapted for other NLP tasks where understanding cognitive load is relevant.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Strong Motivation:** The core idea of using cognitive signals from eye-tracking to aid a downstream NLP task is highly innovative and well-motivated. The introduction does an excellent job of outlining the limitations of purely text-based SA and building a convincing case for why cognitive signals like re-reading (regressions) and processing difficulty (fixations) should correlate with linguistic phenomena like incongruity in sarcastic statements.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is sound. The authors test their method on two different datasets, use multiple standard classifiers, and perform statistical significance testing. The inclusion of an ablation-style study showing the incremental benefit of each feature set (Sn, Sn+Sr, Sn+Sr+Gz) is clear and effective. The targeted analysis in Section 5.1, showing a 6.1% F-score improvement on ironic texts versus 2.1% on simpler ones, provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of the proposed features where they are needed most.
3.  **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The paper presents a rich set of cognitive features, moving beyond simple aggregates like average fixation duration. The "basic" features (like RSF - regressions from second to first half) are well-justified for capturing surprisal in incongruous sentences. The "complex" graph-based features represent a creative attempt to model more intricate reading patterns, even if their individual contributions are not fully teased apart.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The motivation, methodology, and results are presented logically. The authors are also commendably forthright about the practical limitations of their approach and dedicate a section (Section 7) to discussing its potential feasibility, which shows a thoughtful consideration of the work's context.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant Practicality and Scalability Concerns:** The most substantial weakness is the method's reliance on specialized eye-tracking data. For any given text, this approach requires collecting gaze data from multiple human readers to generate the features needed for classification. This makes it impractical for almost any real-world application of sentiment analysis, which typically operates on massive, un-annotated text streams. While the authors' discussion of future mobile eye-trackers (Section 7) is interesting, it remains speculative and does not address the fundamental bottleneck of needing human-in-the-loop data collection *at prediction time*. The paper would be stronger if it framed the work as an *analysis* tool for understanding sentiment complexity rather than a practical SA system.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Modern Baselines:** The paper explicitly sets aside comparisons with deep learning models. In the current NLP landscape, this is a significant omission. State-of-the-art sentiment analysis relies on large pre-trained models (e.g., BERT-based classifiers). While the feature-based approach allows for clear interpretability, its performance gains are difficult to contextualize without knowing how a strong neural baseline performs on these specific datasets. The improvements (e.g., 0.6% F-score on Dataset 1) might be marginal compared to what a fine-tuned transformer model could achieve with text alone.
3.  **Information Loss Through Feature Aggregation:** The cognitive features are derived by averaging the gaze metrics across all participants for a given text snippet. This approach smooths the data but potentially discards a crucial signal: inter-reader variance. A text that elicits highly varied reading patterns (e.g., some readers breeze through it while others regress heavily) is likely to be ambiguous or sarcastic. Using the variance or other statistical properties of the gaze metrics across readers as features could provide a more powerful signal than the mean alone.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the comparison to state-of-the-art models: While a full deep learning implementation might be out of scope, could you elaborate on how you envision these cognitive features being integrated into a modern neural architecture? For example, could gaze features be used as auxiliary inputs to a transformer model, or could a multi-modal approach be used to fuse textual and cognitive representations?
2.  Regarding feature aggregation: Have you experimented with using the variance (or standard deviation) of gaze metrics across participants as features, in addition to the mean? My hypothesis is that high variance in metrics like regression count or fixation duration could be a strong indicator of controversial or ambiguous content, which often overlaps with sarcasm.
3.  The performance gain on Dataset 2 (+9.3%) is substantially larger than on Dataset 1 (+3.7%). However, Dataset 2 was collected with a lower-quality eye-tracker (120Hz vs 500Hz). This seems counter-intuitive, as one might expect richer data to yield better features. Do you have a hypothesis for this difference? Is it due to the composition of the datasets (e.g., different types or proportions of complex texts), or could the feature extraction method be unexpectedly robust to lower sampling rates?
