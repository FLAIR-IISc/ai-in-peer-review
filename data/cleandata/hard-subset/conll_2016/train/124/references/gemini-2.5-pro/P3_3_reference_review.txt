### Summary & Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis (SA) by incorporating cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, which reflect cognitive processing, can help models better handle complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations that are challenging for traditional text-based methods. The authors define a rich set of cognitive features, including basic gaze metrics (fixations, saccades, regressions) and more complex graph-based features. They conduct experiments on two datasets containing eye-tracking data, showing that augmenting a strong set of linguistic features (for both general sentiment and sarcasm) with these cognitive features yields statistically significant improvements in classification accuracy, particularly for texts containing irony.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Novel Feature Set and Interdisciplinary Approach:** The primary contribution is the pioneering use of eye-tracking data as features for sentiment analysis. This is a highly original idea that bridges psycholinguistics and computational linguistics, opening a new avenue for research that moves beyond purely textual signals to model the human interpretation process.
2.  **Targeted Improvement on Complex Constructs:** The paper provides a compelling empirical demonstration that cognitive features are not just a weak proxy for textual features, but offer specific advantages in classifying difficult cases like sarcasm and irony. The experiment on the held-out dataset, showing a much larger performance gain on ironic texts (6.1%) than on simpler texts (2.1%), strongly supports the authors' core claim.
3.  **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors present a comprehensive set of engineered cognitive features, ranging from simple aggregated statistics to innovative graph-based representations (the "gaze-saliency graph"). This feature set is well-motivated and could serve as a valuable starting point for future work in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Impact Potential:** The paper's core idea is innovative and thought-provoking. While NLP has long been inspired by cognitive science, the direct integration of cognitive processing signals (like gaze) into a mainstream task model is rare and exciting. It pushes the community to think about data modalities beyond text and has the potential to inspire similar work in other challenging NLP tasks where human interpretation is key (e.g., ambiguity resolution, argument mining, hate speech detection).
2.  **Strong Motivation and Problem Framing:** The introduction provides an excellent and clear summary of the key challenges in sentiment analysis. It effectively frames the problem, positioning cognitive signals as a targeted solution for the semantic and pragmatic difficulties (sarcasm, implicit sentiment) where traditional methods often fail. This framing makes the motivation for the work exceptionally clear and compelling.
3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is sound. The authors use two separate datasets, multiple classifier types, and perform systematic feature ablation studies. The inclusion of a baseline that already incorporates sarcasm-specific features (Sn+Sr) makes the final comparison fairer and the observed gains more meaningful. The feature significance analysis and statistical tests further strengthen the validity of the results. The dedicated experiment on ironic vs. non-ironic texts is a particularly strong piece of evidence for the efficacy of their approach.
4.  **Proactive Discussion of Practicality:** The authors wisely anticipate and address the most significant limitation of their work: the feasibility of obtaining gaze data at scale. Section 7, which discusses the advent of mobile eye-trackers, frames the work as foundational research for next-generation applications. This foresight adds maturity to the paper and helps contextualize its contribution appropriately.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Practicality and Scalability:** Despite the thoughtful discussion in Section 7, the reliance on eye-tracking data for feature extraction *at inference time* remains the most significant weakness. The proposed scenario of using mobile eye-trackers is still speculative and faces enormous privacy, consent, and technical hurdles. The paper would be substantially stronger if it explored methods to remove this dependency at deployment, for instance, by training a model to *predict* gaze features from text alone, thereby using the cognitive data as a privileged information signal during training only.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The authors explicitly exclude comparisons to deep learning models, arguing their approach is feature-based. In the current NLP landscape, this is a major omission. State-of-the-art SA heavily relies on large pre-trained transformer models (e.g., BERT), which have shown remarkable ability to capture complex contextual nuances. Without this comparison, it's impossible to know if the proposed method offers a genuine advantage over current SOTA or if modern neural architectures can already implicitly learn the patterns that cause human cognitive load. The claim of improving over "the state of the art" is therefore not fully supported.
3.  **Modest Overall Performance Gains:** While statistically significant, the absolute improvements from adding gaze features (Gz) on top of the already strong text-based baseline (Sn+Sr) are quite small for the full datasets: a 0.6% F1-score increase on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2. This raises questions about the cost-benefit ratio of collecting such complex and expensive data. While the 6.1% gain on the specific subset of ironic texts is more impressive, it's important to recognize this applies to a narrower portion of the overall problem.
4.  **Potential for Confounding Variables:** The paper attributes gaze patterns primarily to the processing of sentiment and sarcasm. However, gaze is well-known to be affected by more general psycholinguistic properties like lexical frequency, syntactic complexity, and semantic surprisal. The "Reading Difficulty" features (Flesch score, length) are a good first step, but a more rigorous analysis would be needed to disentangle these effects. It is plausible that a more sophisticated set of *text-only* psycholinguistic features could capture some of the same variance as the gaze features, which would challenge the claim that eye-tracking is essential.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The primary hurdle for your approach is the need for eye-tracking hardware at inference time. Have you considered a multi-stage "predict-then-use" framework? For example, one could first train a model to predict gaze features from text, and then use these *predicted* features in the final sentiment classifier. This would allow you to leverage the rich cognitive signal during training while resulting in a final model that only requires text as input.
2.  You explicitly set aside deep learning models. However, their ability to capture context is paramount. Could you speculate on how your cognitive insights could be integrated with modern transformer architectures? For example, could gaze data be used as an auxiliary training objective to guide the model's attention mechanisms to focus on words that humans find important or difficult to process?
3.  The error analysis mentions that averaging features across participants might cause information loss. Have you explored models that treat individual participant data as separate instances, or perhaps models that learn to weight participant data based on their annotation accuracy or consistency? This might extract a stronger signal from the noisy gaze data.
