### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis (SA) by incorporating cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, which reflect cognitive processing, can provide valuable signals for disambiguating complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations, where traditional text-based features often fail.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **Novel Feature Set:** The primary contribution is the introduction and validation of cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data for a downstream NLP classification task. This is a creative and underexplored method that successfully bridges psycholinguistic insights with a practical NLP problem. The design of these features, ranging from basic statistics (e.g., fixation duration, regression count) to more complex graph-based representations ("gaze-saliency graphs"), is a significant methodological contribution.
2.  **Strong Empirical Evidence for Complex Cases:** The paper provides compelling evidence that cognitive features are particularly effective for correctly classifying texts containing sarcasm and irony. The experiment on the held-out dataset (Section 5.1), which shows a 6.1% F-score improvement on ironic texts versus a 2.1% improvement on non-ironic texts, is a strong piece of analysis that directly supports the paper's core claim.
3.  **Thorough Feature Engineering and Ablation Study:** The authors systematically build and evaluate their model, starting with traditional sentiment features, adding specialized sarcasm features, and finally incorporating their proposed cognitive features. This careful ablation study clearly demonstrates the incremental value of each feature set across two different datasets and multiple classifiers.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Interdisciplinary Appeal:** The core idea of using gaze data as features for sentiment classification is highly original. While eye-tracking has been used to *study* NLP tasks, its use as a direct input signal for a predictive model is new and exciting. This approach opens up a new avenue for research and has strong interdisciplinary appeal, connecting computational linguistics with cognitive science.
2.  **Rigorous and Convincing Experimental Design:** The evaluation is thorough and well-executed. The authors use two distinct datasets with different eye-trackers, test multiple standard classifiers (SVM, NB, NN), and perform significance testing (McNemar test) to validate their results. The feature importance analysis (Table 3) and, most notably, the targeted experiment on ironic vs. non-ironic text (Table 4) provide robust support for their hypothesis. The initial analysis in Section 3.3, showing that existing SA systems struggle with these datasets, effectively motivates the need for a new approach.
3.  **Honest and Insightful Discussion of Limitations:** The inclusion of Section 7 ("Feasibility of our approach") is a major strength. The authors proactively address the most obvious and critical limitation of their work: the requirement of eye-tracking data at inference time. Their discussion of emerging mobile eye-tracking technology and potential application scenarios is thoughtful and realistic. This demonstrates a mature understanding of their work's practical implications and avoids overstating its immediate applicability.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-established in the introduction, the features are described in detail, and the results are presented logically. This makes the paper's novel contributions accessible and understandable.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Practical Scalability and Generalizability:** This is the most significant weakness, despite the authors' candid discussion in Section 7. The proposed method fundamentally requires gaze data for every new instance to be classified. This makes it unsuitable for the vast majority of real-world SA applications (e.g., analyzing historical tweets, product reviews on a website). The reliance on future, widespread, and consented mobile eye-tracking is speculative. The paper is therefore more of a proof-of-concept and an analysis of what is *possible* with such data, rather than a proposal for a readily deployable system. The contribution is more scientific than engineering.
2.  **Lack of Comparison with Strong Contemporary Baselines:** The authors explicitly exclude deep learning models, stating their approach is feature-based. In the current NLP landscape, this is a notable omission. A fine-tuned BERT-based classifier is the standard baseline for most text classification tasks and is known to be very effective at capturing contextual nuances without explicit feature engineering. Comparing their best model against a strong text-only neural baseline on these datasets is crucial for contextualizing their performance. While their model shows a significant *relative* improvement from adding gaze features, it is unclear if the final model's *absolute* performance is competitive with the current state-of-the-art that uses only text.
3.  **Potential for Confounding Variables:** The eye-tracking data was collected while human participants were performing the sentiment annotation task. It is plausible that the gaze patterns reflect the *cognitive difficulty of the annotation task* rather than the underlying sentiment itself. Sarcastic sentences are inherently more difficult to annotate, which would likely lead to more regressions, longer fixations, etc. The model might be learning to predict "annotation difficulty," which happens to be highly correlated with sarcasm and negative sentiment in these particular datasets. While this correlation is precisely what the authors leverage, it's a subtle distinction that clouds whether the features are capturing sentiment directly or just task difficulty.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the generalizability issue: Do you envision this methodology primarily as an analysis tool to understand how humans process complex sentiment, or as a practical classification system? If the latter, could you elaborate on a scenario where collecting real-time gaze data for every inference is feasible beyond the examples in Section 7? For instance, could a model be trained to *predict* gaze features from text alone, thereby removing the need for an eye-tracker at inference time?
2.  Could you comment on why a strong, text-only deep learning baseline (e.g., fine-tuned RoBERTa) was not included in the comparison? Such a model would provide a crucial data point to understand whether the complex feature engineering pipeline (including gaze data) provides information beyond what a large pre-trained model can capture from text alone.
3.  How do you disentangle the effects of sentiment from the effects of general reading/annotation difficulty? Since sarcastic texts are both affectively complex and cognitively demanding to process, are the gaze features truly capturing sentiment-specific signals, or are they proxies for cognitive load that happens to correlate with the labels in your datasets?
