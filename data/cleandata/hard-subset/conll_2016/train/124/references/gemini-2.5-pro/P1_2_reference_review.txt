### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis (SA) by augmenting traditional text-based features with "cognitive features" derived from the eye-movement patterns of human annotators. The central hypothesis is that cognitive signals, as captured by eye-tracking, can help models better understand complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations, which are challenging for standard SA systems. The authors engineer a comprehensive feature set including sentiment features, sarcasm-specific features, and a variety of basic and complex gaze-based features. They evaluate their approach on two publicly available datasets containing eye-tracking data, demonstrating that the inclusion of cognitive features provides a statistically significant improvement in classification accuracy over models using only textual features. The improvement is shown to be particularly pronounced for texts containing complex constructs like irony.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Approach to Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the novel idea of incorporating cognitive signals (from eye-tracking) as features for a downstream NLP task. This opens up a new, interesting, and potentially fruitful direction for research, moving beyond purely text-based analysis to leverage human processing signals.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation of the Core Hypothesis:** The paper provides compelling evidence that cognitive features are beneficial, especially for challenging cases. The experiment on a held-out set of ironic vs. non-ironic texts (Table 4) is particularly strong, showing a much larger performance gain on the complex examples. This directly supports the authors' central claim.
3.  **A Comprehensive Set of Engineered Cognitive Features:** The authors propose a detailed set of cognitive features, ranging from simple aggregated statistics (e.g., average fixation duration, regression count) to more complex graph-based features derived from a "gaze-saliency graph." This provides a solid foundation for future work in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core concept is highly original. While eye-tracking has been used to *study* NLP phenomena, using it as a direct input feature for a classification task like sentiment analysis is a fresh perspective. It could inspire similar work in other NLP tasks where subtle human interpretation is key, such as detecting hate speech, analyzing figurative language, or assessing text quality.
2.  **Rigorous and Well-Designed Experiments:** The experimental setup is sound. The authors use multiple classifiers (SVM, NB, NN), test on two different datasets, and perform careful ablation studies (Table 2) to isolate the contribution of each feature group (Sentiment, Sarcasm, Gaze). The inclusion of statistical significance testing (McNemar test) adds to the credibility of the results. The targeted analysis on the ironic/non-ironic subset is a particularly strong piece of evidence.
3.  **Reproducibility and Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The methodology, especially the feature engineering, is described in sufficient detail. Crucially, the authors use publicly available datasets, which is a major strength that allows the community to verify their results and build directly upon their work.
4.  **Thoughtful Discussion of Limitations:** The authors proactively address the most obvious and critical limitation of their work—its practical feasibility—in Section 7. While their proposed solutions are speculative, acknowledging this major hurdle and discussing potential future scenarios (e.g., ubiquitous mobile eye-trackers) shows a mature understanding of their work's position in the field.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Severe Practicality Concerns:** This is the most significant weakness. The proposed method requires eye-tracking data not just for training, but for *inference* on new, unseen text. This makes it unusable in virtually any real-world application today. The feasibility section relies on future, hypothetical technology. A more grounded discussion could have explored alternative paradigms, for example, using gaze data as a form of weak supervision to train a better *text-only* model, which would be deployable. As it stands, the method is a fascinating academic proof-of-concept with no clear path to practical application.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Modern Baselines:** The paper explicitly sets aside deep learning models, framing the work as feature-based. However, in the current NLP landscape, even simple neural models (e.g., BiLSTM with attention, fine-tuned BERT) are the de-facto standard for strong baselines. The baselines used in Table 1 and the unigram features in Table 2 represent an older generation of SA systems. Without comparison to a modern neural baseline, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed model's absolute performance is competitive. It is possible that a standard BERT-based classifier, using text alone, could outperform the authors' final system, which would challenge the necessity of the complex cognitive features.
3.  **Modest Performance Gains on Full Datasets:** While statistically significant, the overall F-score improvements from adding gaze features (`Gz`) to the best text-only model (`Sn+Sr`) are relatively small: 0.6% on Dataset 1 (72.9 to 73.5) and 1.5% on Dataset 2 (70.3 to 71.8). The paper introduces a large and complex set of gaze features, including the novel gaze-saliency graphs. It is unclear if this complexity is justified for such a modest gain. The analysis could be strengthened by showing which specific gaze features provide the most benefit.
4.  **Potential Confounding between Text Difficulty and Sentiment Nuance:** The authors include features for reading difficulty (e.g., Flesch score, sentence length) to control for confounds. However, it's possible that the cognitive features (e.g., regression count, fixation duration) are primarily capturing general processing difficulty rather than sentiment-specific nuances like sarcasm. Complex, convoluted, or poorly written sentences are likely to elicit more regressions and longer fixations, and may also happen to be more frequently negative. The paper could do more to disentangle these effects to prove that the gaze features are truly capturing signals related to sarcasm and irony, and not just acting as a proxy for sentence complexity.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the major practical limitation of requiring an eye-tracker at inference time: Have you considered an alternative framework where gaze data is used only during training? For example, could gaze patterns be used to predict an intermediate representation or a set of auxiliary labels (e.g., `is_sarcastic`, `has_thwarting`), which a text-only model would then learn to predict and use for the final sentiment classification? This would allow the knowledge from the cognitive signals to be "distilled" into a model that is practically deployable.
2.  Could you elaborate on the decision to exclude modern neural baselines? Given that models like BERT have shown remarkable ability to capture complex linguistic context, comparing your system against a fine-tuned BERT model on these datasets would provide a crucial point of reference for the community and help to contextualize the absolute performance of your approach.
3.  The feature set includes many basic and complex gaze features. Could you provide a more detailed feature importance analysis? Specifically, how much of the performance gain comes from the simple, intuitive features (e.g., `FDUR`, `FC`, `REG`) versus the more novel and complex gaze-saliency graph features (e.g., `ED`, `F1H`, etc.)? This would help clarify whether the added complexity of the graph-based approach is necessary.
