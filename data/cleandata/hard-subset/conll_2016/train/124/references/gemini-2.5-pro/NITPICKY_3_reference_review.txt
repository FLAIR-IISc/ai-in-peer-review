### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes to improve sentiment analysis (SA), particularly for texts with complex constructs like sarcasm and irony, by augmenting traditional linguistic features with cognitive features derived from the eye-tracking data of human annotators. The authors introduce a set of basic and complex gaze-based features, including some derived from a novel "gaze-saliency graph." They demonstrate that their combined feature set improves classification accuracy on two different datasets over baselines that use only textual features.

As I see them, the contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical demonstration that features derived from eye-tracking data can improve sentiment classification performance, with the most significant gains observed on texts containing irony and sarcasm.
2.  The introduction of a specific set of cognitive features for this task, including metrics derived from a proposed graph-based representation of gaze patterns.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of leveraging cognitive signals from eye-tracking to aid in a high-level NLP task like sentiment analysis is novel and interesting. The intuition that complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm induce a different cognitive load, which is reflected in gaze patterns, is well-motivated.
2.  **Targeted Evaluation:** The experiment on the held-out dataset (Section 5.1), which explicitly separates performance on ironic vs. non-ironic texts, provides strong evidence for the paper's central hypothesis. The reported 6.1% F-score improvement on complex texts is a compelling result.
3.  **Thoroughness in Feature Engineering:** The authors are comprehensive in their feature design, drawing from standard sentiment literature, sarcasm detection literature, and proposing their own set of gaze-based features.

### Weaknesses

Despite the novelty, the paper is undermined by a substantial number of methodological and presentational flaws.

1.  **Fundamental Practicality Issues:** The proposed method requires eye-tracking data to be collected for every text instance that needs to be classified. This is a prohibitive limitation for any practical application. The authors' discussion of feasibility in Section 7, which relies on the future availability of ubiquitous mobile eye-trackers, does not address the core problem: one cannot classify a new tweet or product review without first assembling a group of people to read it under an eye-tracker. This makes the system a tool for analysis, not a deployable SA classifier, a crucial distinction that is not made clear.

2.  **Unexplained and Contradictory Results:** Table 2 contains results that directly contradict the paper's narrative. For Dataset 2, adding gaze features (Gz) to the sentiment features (Sn) causes a catastrophic drop in performance for both Naïve Bayes (50.7 F-score to 48.5) and SVM (62.5 to 48.9). This is a massive decrease that is neither mentioned nor explained in the text. The authors selectively focus only on the final `Sn+Sr+Gz` combination, where the `Sr` features seem to mask this negative effect. This omission is concerning and suggests that the utility of the Gz features is not robust and is highly dependent on the presence of other specific features.

3.  **Confounding Variables in Data Collection:** The eye-tracking data was collected while participants were actively performing a sentiment annotation task. Their gaze patterns may therefore reflect the cognitive processes of *labeling* (e.g., re-reading to confirm a choice, looking for specific clues) rather than natural *comprehension*. This is a critical confound that threatens the validity of the features. The study should have used gaze data from participants who were simply reading for comprehension to isolate the effect of the text itself. Furthermore, the two datasets use eye-trackers of vastly different quality (500Hz vs. 120Hz), another major variable whose impact is never discussed.

4.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor:**
    *   **Feature Justification:** Many feature choices lack rigorous justification. For example, the "complex gaze features" are motivated by "qualitative observations," and the choice of PCA dimensionality (500) is presented without any analysis or tuning.
    *   **Feature Aggregation:** Gaze features are averaged across all participants for a given sentence. This is a crude aggregation method that discards rich individual-level information and may obscure the signal in noise. This is noted as a limitation in the error analysis but is a significant design choice that warrants more justification.
    *   **Dismissal of SoTA Baselines:** The authors explicitly exclude deep learning models, which represent the state of the art for many SA tasks. The justification that "our approach is feature based" is insufficient; a comparison is necessary to properly contextualize the performance of the proposed model.

5.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish that detracts from its credibility.
    *   The introduction's roadmap omits Section 6.
    *   There are numerous typos (e.g., "F˙score" in Table 1 caption).
    *   The bibliography is in an unacceptable state. It is riddled with formatting errors, inconsistent styles, and extraneous characters (e.g., `et al\.,?`, `\Q2009\E`). This level of carelessness is highly unprofessional and suggests the paper was not carefully prepared.

Given the fundamental impracticality of the proposed method for real-world classification, the unexplained negative results in the ablation study, and the large number of minor but significant methodological and formatting flaws, I cannot recommend this paper for acceptance.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please explain the severe performance degradation observed in Table 2 when gaze features (Gz) are combined with sentiment features (Sn), particularly for SVM on Dataset 2 where the F-score drops from 62.5 to 48.9? How does this align with your central claim about the utility of gaze features?
2.  Beyond the prospect of future hardware, can you describe a realistic, practical deployment scenario for your system where the need to collect eye-tracking data for every new text instance is not a prohibitive bottleneck?
3.  How do you disentangle the cognitive signals related to text comprehension from those related to the explicit task of sentiment annotation in your gaze data? Could the gaze patterns you model be artifacts of the labeling task itself?
4.  The reference list contains extensive and systematic formatting errors. This suggests a significant lack of attention to detail. Can you confirm this will be fully rectified?
