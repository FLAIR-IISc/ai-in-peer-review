### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of cognitive features, derived from human eye-tracking data, to enhance sentiment analysis (SA) of short texts. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns reflect the cognitive processes involved in comprehending complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony, and can thus serve as valuable signals for an SA classifier. The authors introduce a set of novel cognitive features, including basic gaze metrics (e.g., fixation duration, regression counts) and more complex features derived from a "gaze-saliency graph." These features are combined with traditional linguistic features for sentiment, sarcasm, and thwarting. The proposed method is evaluated on two publicly available datasets containing sentiment labels and associated eye-tracking data. Experiments using SVM, Naive Bayes, and Neural Network classifiers show that the inclusion of cognitive features yields a statistically significant, albeit modest, improvement in classification performance, particularly for texts containing sarcasm or irony.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **Novel Feature Engineering from Cognitive Data:** The primary contribution is the introduction and evaluation of a novel set of features for sentiment analysis derived directly from human eye-tracking data. The conceptualization of "basic" and "complex" (graph-based) gaze features is a creative approach to quantifying reading patterns for a downstream NLP task.
2.  **Empirical Demonstration of Cognitive-Linguistic Synergy:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that cognitive features can complement a robust set of linguistic features to improve SA performance. The analysis on a held-out dataset, which shows a more pronounced improvement on ironic texts compared to non-ironic ones, effectively supports the authors' hypothesis that these features are most useful for tackling complex semantic and pragmatic challenges.
3.  **Justification of Task Difficulty and Experimental Rigor:** The authors convincingly demonstrate that the chosen datasets are challenging for standard SA systems, thereby motivating the need for their proposed approach. The experimental design is methodologically sound, employing standard cross-validation, multiple classifiers, and statistical significance testing to validate the results.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Interdisciplinary Contribution:** The core idea of leveraging eye-tracking data as features for a mainstream NLP task is highly innovative. It creates a valuable bridge between the fields of psycholinguistics and computational linguistics, potentially opening a new avenue of research where cognitive signals are used to augment text-based models for various NLP tasks. This novelty is a significant argument for acceptance.
2.  **Thorough Feature Analysis:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of cognitive features and provides a detailed analysis of their impact. The feature significance analysis (Table 3), which shows that numerous gaze-based features rank among the most informative, and the targeted experiment on ironic versus non-ironic texts (Table 4) provide compelling evidence for the utility of the proposed features. This analysis successfully demonstrates that the cognitive features capture information beyond what is available in the text alone, especially for the linguistically complex cases the paper aims to address.
3.  **Sound and Reproducible Experimental Setup:** The methodology is clear and scientifically rigorous. The use of public datasets, standard classifiers (SVM, NB, NN), and proper evaluation protocols (10-fold cross-validation, McNemar's test for significance) ensures the work is verifiable and reproducible. The authors establish a strong baseline by first combining standard sentiment features with sarcasm-specific features before introducing their novel cognitive features, allowing for a clear assessment of the incremental benefit.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Practicality and Generalizability Concerns:** The most critical weakness is the method's reliance on the availability of eye-tracking data during inference. A trained model cannot classify the sentiment of a new, arbitrary text without first collecting eye-tracking data from human readers for that specific text. This requirement severely limits the practical applicability of the proposed system in any real-world scenario. The discussion in Section 7 regarding the future availability of mobile eye-trackers is speculative and does not address the fundamental limitation for current and near-future use cases.
2.  **Modest Absolute Performance Gains:** While the improvements are shown to be statistically significant, the absolute gains in F-score from adding the gaze features (Gz) on top of a strong linguistic baseline (Sn+Sr) are small: 0.6% for Dataset 1 and 1.5% for Dataset 2. Given the significant cost and effort required to collect eye-tracking data, these marginal improvements raise questions about the cost-benefit trade-off of the approach.
3.  **Confounding of Cognitive and Textual Features:** The "Gz" feature set, as described in the caption of Table 2, includes both the novel cognitive features from eye-tracking and standard textual features related to reading difficulty (Flesch score, sentence length). These readability features are purely text-based and could have been included in the linguistic baseline. By bundling them with the eye-tracking features, the paper makes it impossible to isolate the true contribution of the cognitive signals alone. The reported improvement attributed to "Gz" may be partially due to these simple text features rather than the novel cognitive ones.
4.  **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The authors explicitly exclude deep learning models from their comparison. In recent years, sentiment analysis has been dominated by large pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT). While the paper's focus is on feature engineering, the absence of any comparison to modern, strong baselines makes it difficult to assess the overall effectiveness and positioning of the proposed method within the current state of the art. It is plausible that powerful neural models already capture some of the subtle cues that the gaze features are designed to detect.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The primary limitation of this work is its reliance on gaze data at inference time. Have you considered a two-stage approach where one model learns to *predict* gaze features from text alone, and a second model (as presented in the paper) uses these predicted gaze features for sentiment analysis? Such an approach could decouple the method from the need for live eye-tracking and vastly improve its practical utility.
2.  In your experiments (Table 2), the "Gz" feature group includes both eye-tracking features and text-based readability features (LEN, RED). Could you provide results from an ablation study that separates the contribution of the purely cognitive (eye-tracking) features from the contribution of these text-based readability features? This would help clarify the exact performance gain attributable to the novel cognitive signals.
3.  The cognitive features are derived by averaging gaze data across multiple participants. This approach discards information about inter-annotator variance. Have you explored using the variance or other statistics of gaze patterns across readers as a feature itself? High variance in reading patterns for a given text might be a strong signal of its ambiguity or complexity.
