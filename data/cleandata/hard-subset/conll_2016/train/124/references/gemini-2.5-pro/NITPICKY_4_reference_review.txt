### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper investigates the utility of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, for the task of sentiment analysis in short texts. The authors hypothesize that eye-movement patterns of human annotators can provide signals to help disambiguate complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony, which are challenging for traditional feature-based models. They propose an extensive set of cognitive features, categorized into basic and complex graph-based features, and augment them with standard sentiment and sarcasm-specific textual features. The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data can provide a statistically significant, albeit small, improvement in sentiment classification accuracy over a strong baseline of textual features.
2.  A targeted analysis on a held-out set showing that these cognitive features are particularly effective for texts containing complex constructs like irony and sarcasm, where the performance gain is more pronounced.
3.  The introduction of a novel set of graph-based "gaze-saliency" features, attempting to model more complex interactions in reading patterns.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty of the Core Idea:** The central premise of using cognitive signals from eye-tracking to augment NLP models for a subjective task like sentiment analysis is highly innovative. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistics and for annotation studies, its direct application as a feature source for a downstream classification task, particularly for tackling nuances like sarcasm, is a novel and valuable research direction.

2.  **Targeted Experimental Design:** The analysis in Section 5.1 is a key strength. By separating the held-out test set into "Irony" and "Non-Irony" subsets, the authors provide compelling evidence for their central hypothesis: that cognitive features are most beneficial for texts where sentiment is not expressed straightforwardly. The 6.1% F-score improvement on ironic texts, compared to a 2.1% improvement on simpler texts, strongly supports their claims.

3.  **Use of Public Datasets:** The experiments are conducted on two publicly available datasets. This is commendable as it enhances the transparency and reproducibility of the work, allowing other researchers to build upon or challenge their findings. The initial analysis in Section 3.3, which establishes these datasets as challenging for standard SA systems, also effectively motivates the need for the proposed approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Methodological Opacity and Potential Errors:** The experimental methodology lacks critical details and contains results that are highly suspect.
    *   The "Multi-layered Neural Network" is not described at all; there is no mention of its architecture (layers, nodes), activation functions, or optimization algorithm. This makes a portion of the results completely unreproducible.
    *   Hyperparameter tuning is dismissed by stating that "default values given in Weka" were used. This is not a rigorous approach and can lead to suboptimal and unreliable comparisons between models.
    *   In Table 2, the SVM performance on Dataset 2 for the "Sn+Gz" feature set drops to a 48.9 F-score from a 62.5 F-score for the "Sn" set. A 13.6-point drop upon adding new features is alarming and suggests a potential implementation error or a critical negative interaction that is left entirely unanalyzed and unexplained. Such a result severely undermines confidence in the reported findings.

2.  **Vague Feature Definitions and Lack of Ablation:** While the feature engineering is extensive, the "complex gaze features" derived from the saliency graph are poorly defined. The description of how weighted degrees are calculated is ambiguous (e.g., "considering the fixation duration on the word of node i of edge Eij as edge weight"). A formal mathematical definition is required. Furthermore, the paper introduces a large suite of cognitive features (`Gz`) but treats them as a monolithic block. There is no ablation study to determine which of these features (e.g., basic vs. complex, regression-based vs. fixation-based) are actually contributing to the performance gain. This makes it difficult to understand the source of the improvement.

3.  **Impracticality and Speculative Justification:** The approach relies on having eye-tracking data for the text at inference time, which is a severe limitation for any practical application. The authors dedicate an entire section (Section 7) to arguing for the future feasibility of this approach. This section is highly speculative, referencing patents and future "promising situations," and reads more like a project proposal than a scientific paper. It distracts from the research contributions and fails to adequately address the profound and immediate impracticality of the method.

4.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper is marred by a substantial number of formatting and typographical errors, which detracts from its professionalism and readability.
    *   The introduction's roadmap omits a discussion of Section 6 (Error Analysis).
    *   Table 1 contains the non-standard notation "F˙score".
    *   The References section is egregiously formatted. It is riddled with stray characters (`et al\.,?`), inconsistent formatting styles, random question marks, and LaTeX-like commands (`\Q2009\E`), rendering it nearly unreadable and demonstrating a significant lack of care in the paper's preparation. Such sloppiness is unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier conference.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a complete and precise description of the "Multi-layered NN" architecture and training procedure used in your experiments?
2.  Can you explain the severe performance degradation observed for the SVM classifier on Dataset 2 when gaze features were added to sentiment features (Sn: 62.5 F-score vs. Sn+Gz: 48.9 F-score)? Was this a typo, or is there a technical reason for this drastic drop?
3.  Could you provide a formal, mathematical definition for the calculation of the "complex gaze features," specifically the various weighted degree measures derived from the saliency graph?
4.  The two datasets used were collected with eye-trackers of substantially different quality (500Hz vs. 120Hz). Did you perform any analysis on how tracker quality affects the utility of the cognitive features? One might expect the noisier data from the Tobii T120 to yield less useful features.
