### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis (SA) by incorporating "cognitive features" derived from the eye-movement patterns of human annotators. The central hypothesis is that these gaze patterns, which reflect cognitive processing, can help models better handle linguistically complex texts containing sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations, where purely textual features often fall short. The authors design an extensive feature set combining traditional SA features, sarcasm-specific textual features, and a range of new cognitive features (both basic statistical aggregates and more complex graph-based ones). Through experiments on two challenging, publicly available datasets, they demonstrate that augmenting a strong feature-based classifier with these cognitive features leads to statistically significant improvements in performance, particularly for ironic texts.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Novel Integration of Cognitive Signals for a Core NLP Task:** The primary contribution is the novel and well-executed idea of using eye-tracking data as a direct feature source for sentiment analysis. This provides a new, tangible link between psycholinguistic evidence and a practical NLP application, opening a promising direction for research.
2.  **Strong Empirical Evidence for Handling Linguistic Nuance:** The paper provides compelling evidence that cognitive features are particularly effective for decoding complex pragmatic phenomena. The experiment on the held-out dataset, which shows a substantially larger performance gain for ironic texts (6.1% F1 improvement) compared to simpler texts (2.1% F1 improvement), is a significant finding that strongly supports the paper's core claims.
3.  **Comprehensive Feature Engineering and Analysis:** The authors propose a well-motivated and detailed set of cognitive features, including novel graph-based representations of gaze patterns. The systematic ablation studies and feature importance analysis provide a clear understanding of the value added by each feature category.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Potential Impact:** The paper's core concept is highly innovative. While eye-tracking has been used to study NLP phenomena, its direct use as a feature in a predictive model for a task like sentiment analysis is rare and exciting. This work could inspire a new line of research in "cognitively-informed NLP" where signals from human processing are used to enrich models. The approach is particularly relevant for tasks that involve subjective and nuanced interpretation, which remain challenging for current models.
2.  **Rigorous Experimental Methodology:** The evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors use two distinct datasets, multiple standard classifiers (NB, SVM, NN), and perform systematic feature ablation studies. The inclusion of a targeted experiment on ironic vs. non-ironic text (Table 4) is a major strength, as it directly tests and validates the central hypothesis. The use of statistical significance tests (McNemar) adds rigor to the reported performance gains.
3.  **Well-Written and Clearly Motivated:** The paper is exceptionally clear and well-structured. The introduction does an excellent job of outlining the specific lexical, syntactic, and pragmatic challenges in SA that motivate the need for a new source of information. The proposed features are explained intuitively, and the feasibility discussion in Section 7 shows that the authors have carefully considered the practical limitations of their work, which is commendable.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Practicality and Scalability Concerns:** The most substantial weakness is the fundamental reliance on eye-tracking data for feature extraction. This makes the approach impractical for any real-world, large-scale application, as it requires every text snippet to be read by multiple human subjects under controlled lab conditions. While the authors' discussion of future mobile eye-trackers is interesting, it remains speculative. At present, the method is a proof-of-concept confined to a research setting and cannot be deployed as a general-purpose SA tool. The model is trained on text+gaze and thus requires gaze data at inference time, which is a critical bottleneck.
2.  **Modest Absolute Performance Gains:** While statistically significant, the performance improvement from adding gaze features over the strong textual baseline (`Sn+Sr`) is quite small on the full datasets: +0.6% F1-score for Dataset 1 and +1.5% for Dataset 2 with the best SVM model. Given the immense cost and effort required to collect eye-tracking data, this raises questions about the cost-benefit trade-off. The more impressive gains on the ironic subset are encouraging, but the overall improvement is marginal.
3.  **Lack of Comparison to Modern Neural Baselines:** The authors explicitly exclude comparisons to deep learning models (e.g., CNN, RNN, Transformers like BERT), stating their approach is feature-based. In the current NLP landscape, this is a major omission. A fine-tuned BERT model would be a much stronger baseline than the feature-based SVM. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess where the proposed model truly stands in relation to the state of the art. A more compelling contribution would have been to show how cognitive features could be integrated into a neural architecture to improve upon a strong BERT baseline.
4.  **Simplistic Aggregation of Gaze Data:** All cognitive features are derived by averaging the gaze metrics across the multiple participants who read each text. This simple aggregation may obscure valuable information contained in the variance or distribution of reading patterns among individuals. It is plausible that disagreement or high variance in gaze among readers is itself a strong signal of ambiguity or sarcasm. The paper fails to explore or justify the choice of averaging over other, potentially more informative aggregation strategies.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given the critical limitation of requiring gaze data at inference time, have you considered a two-stage approach to make the system more practical? For example, could a model first be trained to *predict* gaze features from text alone, and these predicted features could then be used by your sentiment classifier? This would decouple the model from the eye-tracker at deployment.
2.  The results in Table 4 are very compelling but only show F-score improvements. Could you provide the full precision, recall, and F-score values for both the "Irony" and "Non-Irony" subsets in that experiment? This would provide a clearer picture of the absolute performance and the difficulty of the task on that specific data split.
3.  Could you elaborate on the choice to average gaze features across participants? Have you explored any other aggregation methods (e.g., using variance, min/max, or creating separate feature vectors per reader) and how did they perform in comparison? It seems that variability in reading patterns could be a very powerful feature in itself.
