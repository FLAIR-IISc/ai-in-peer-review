### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to improve sentiment analysis (SA), particularly for texts with complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony, by incorporating cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, which reflect cognitive processing, can provide signals that are absent in the text alone, thereby helping classifiers handle nuances that traditional features miss. The authors engineer a rich set of cognitive features from eye-tracking data, including basic gaze metrics (fixation duration, regressions) and more complex ones derived from a proposed "gaze-saliency graph". They conduct experiments on two publicly available datasets containing short texts paired with eye-tracking data. Through a series of ablation studies, they demonstrate that augmenting a strong set of textual sentiment and sarcasm features with their proposed cognitive features leads to statistically significant improvements in classification accuracy, with the gains being most pronounced on sarcastic and ironic texts.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A Novel Methodology for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the novel idea of using signals from human cognitive processing (via eye-tracking) as direct features for a downstream NLP task. This is a creative and well-motivated approach to tackling some of the hardest challenges in SA.
2.  **Strong Empirical Evidence for Handling Complex Texts:** The paper provides compelling evidence, especially through its targeted experiment on ironic vs. non-ironic text, that cognitive features are particularly effective for detecting sentiment in linguistically complex cases where textual cues can be misleading.
3.  **A New Set of Cognitive Features:** The authors introduce a set of engineered features, including those based on a novel gaze-saliency graph representation, which could potentially be adopted by other researchers working at the intersection of NLP and psycholinguistics.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Creativity:** The core concept of leveraging eye-tracking data as a feature source for SA is highly original. It represents a significant departure from conventional text-only approaches and creatively bridges the gap between computational linguistics and cognitive science in a practical, task-oriented manner.
2.  **Strong and Clear Motivation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work. It clearly outlines the limitations of existing SA techniques when faced with semantic and pragmatic challenges like sarcasm and thwarted expectations. The argument that cognitive signals might help overcome these limitations is logical and well-articulated.
3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experimentation:** The experimental design is a key strength. The authors validate their approach on two distinct datasets, enhancing the credibility of their findings. The systematic feature ablation study (Sentiment -> +Sarcasm -> +Gaze) clearly isolates and demonstrates the incremental value of each feature set. The inclusion of statistical significance tests adds rigor to the reported results. The analysis in Table 4, which shows a 6.1% F-score improvement on ironic texts versus a 2.1% improvement on simpler texts, provides powerful and direct support for the central hypothesis.
4.  **Proactive Discussion of Limitations:** The authors thoughtfully address the main practical limitation of their work in Section 7 ("Feasibility of our approach"). By discussing emerging technologies like mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios, they show a mature understanding of their method's place in the broader landscape and proactively counter the most obvious criticism. This candor strengthens the paper.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Fundamental Practicality Concerns:** The most significant weakness is the requirement of eye-tracking data at inference time. The proposed model classifies a `(text, gaze_data)` pair, not just text. This fundamentally changes the task from standard SA to a form of SA with a "human-in-the-loop," severely limiting its real-world applicability. For any new piece of text, one would need to recruit human subjects to read it while being eye-tracked. While the discussion in Section 7 is appreciated, it remains speculative and doesn't fully resolve the immediate and substantial barrier to deployment.
2.  **Weak Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The paper explicitly avoids comparison with deep learning models, arguing that its approach is feature-based. In the current landscape, this is a major omission. Powerful pre-trained language models (like BERT) have shown remarkable ability to capture complex context and are the de facto state of the art for many nuance-dependent tasks, including sarcasm detection. Without a comparison to a strong baseline like a fine-tuned BERT on the text-only data, it is difficult to gauge the true significance of the performance gains. The improvements shown are over traditional feature-based SVMs, which are no longer considered a SOTA benchmark.
3.  **Potential for Confounding Variables:** The cognitive features might be capturing general processing difficulty or surprise rather than sentiment-specific signals. For instance, a syntactically complex or semantically ambiguous but non-sarcastic sentence might elicit similar gaze patterns (e.g., more regressions, longer fixations). The authors attempt to control for this with readability and length features, but this may be insufficient. The experiment in Table 4 helps, but doesn't fully disentangle sentiment-related incongruity from other forms of cognitive load.
4.  **Data Aggregation and Size:** The cognitive features are averaged across all participants for a given text snippet. This approach smooths the data but loses potentially critical information about individual differences in comprehension (e.g., some users get the sarcasm, others don't). Furthermore, the datasets are relatively small (~1k instances each), which is understandable given the expense of data collection but raises concerns about the generalizability of the learned feature weights, especially given the large number of engineered features.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the intended deployment scenario? Is the model intended to operate exclusively in settings where live eye-tracking is available (e.g., in a UX lab), or do you envision a two-stage model where gaze features are first *predicted* from text, and then used for the downstream SA task? The latter would resolve the main practicality issue and vastly increase the impact of this work.
2.  Your results show a much larger F-score improvement from gaze features on Dataset 2 (+9.3% overall) compared to Dataset 1 (+3.7%), even though Dataset 1 is described as having more sarcastic content. Do you have any hypotheses to explain this discrepancy? Could it relate to the different eye-trackers used, the nature of the non-sarcastic texts in each dataset, or other experimental artifacts?
3.  Have you considered the potential impact of individual differences? Since gaze features are averaged, the signal from a reader who correctly interprets sarcasm might be diluted by one who does not. Could a model that uses individual participants' gaze data as separate training instances (with the participant's sentiment label) perform better or reveal more about the cognitive process?
