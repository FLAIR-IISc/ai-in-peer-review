### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis (SA) by augmenting traditional text-based features with "cognitive features" derived from the eye-tracking data of human annotators. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns reflect cognitive processes involved in understanding complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony, and can therefore serve as useful signals for a classifier. The authors engineer a set of basic and complex gaze-based features (e.g., fixation duration, regression counts, graph-based metrics from a "gaze-saliency graph") and test their effectiveness on two datasets known to be challenging for standard SA systems.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Novel Feature Set and Modality:** The primary contribution is the introduction and evaluation of eye-tracking data as a feature source for a core NLP task like sentiment analysis. This is a highly novel idea that bridges psycholinguistics and computational linguistics to tackle semantic and pragmatic challenges (e.g., sarcasm) that are notoriously difficult to model from text alone.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation on Challenging Data:** The paper provides a thorough empirical demonstration of the value of these cognitive features. The authors show that their chosen datasets are indeed difficult for standard systems, and then systematically demonstrate through ablation studies that adding cognitive features yields statistically significant performance improvements. The held-out experiment specifically isolating ironic vs. non-ironic texts provides compelling evidence for their core hypothesis.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Potential for a New Research Direction:** The core idea of using gaze data to inform a text classification model is innovative and well-motivated. The introduction does an excellent job of situating the work, highlighting the limitations of purely lexical or syntactic approaches for handling pragmatic phenomena like sarcasm. By introducing a new modality that directly captures a reader's cognitive load and "suprisal," the paper opens up a promising avenue for future research in computational pragmatics and other NLP tasks where subtle understanding is key (e.g., detecting misinformation, humor, or figurative language).

2.  **Rigorous and Convincing Experimental Design:** The evaluation is a major strength. The authors don't just show that their full feature set works well; they carefully build the case for the cognitive features.
    *   They first establish a baseline by showing that standard SA methods struggle on their chosen datasets (Table 1).
    *   The ablation study (Table 2) clearly isolates the contribution of each feature group (Sentiment, Sarcasm, Gaze).
    *   They use statistical significance testing (McNemar test) to confirm that the observed improvements are not due to chance.
    *   Most impressively, the experiment on the held-out dataset (Table 4) directly tests and confirms their central claim: that gaze features provide a disproportionately larger benefit for texts with complex constructs like irony (a 6.1% F-score improvement) compared to simpler texts (2.1% improvement). This is excellent scientific practice.

3.  **Thoughtful Discussion of Limitations and Feasibility:** The authors proactively address the most obvious and critical weakness of their approach: the practical requirement of eye-tracking data. Section 7 ("Feasibility of our approach") is a commendable and necessary part of the paper. While the proposed solutions (e.g., future mobile eye-trackers) are speculative, the fact that the authors acknowledge and grapple with this fundamental limitation adds maturity and credibility to the work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Major Practicality and Scalability Concerns:** While the authors discuss feasibility, the fundamental limitation remains the strongest argument against this work's immediate impact. The proposed method, as presented, requires eye-tracking data not just for training but *for every new instance at inference time*. This makes it unusable for virtually any real-world SA application (e.g., classifying tweets or product reviews in real-time). The paper presents a research "in-the-lab" finding, but its path to practical application is unclear and relies on technological developments that are not yet widespread. The contribution is therefore primarily academic and conceptual at this stage.

2.  **Modest Overall Performance Gains:** While statistically significant, the absolute improvements in F-score from adding gaze features on the full datasets are small: 0.6% on Dataset 1 (from 72.9% to 73.5%) and 1.5% on Dataset 2 (from 70.3% to 71.8%). One could argue that the significant cost and complexity of collecting and processing eye-tracking data are not justified by such modest gains. The more impressive 6.1% gain is on a specific subset of the data, but the overall utility lift is less pronounced.

3.  **Potential Confounding with Textual Complexity:** The paper hypothesizes that gaze features capture high-level semantic nuances like incongruity. However, it's also possible they are simply a very effective proxy for general textual difficulty (e.g., rare words, complex syntax) which is already correlated with sarcasm. While the authors include Flesch Readability and sentence length as control features (a good step), a more in-depth analysis of the relationship between gaze features and other text-based complexity metrics would strengthen their claims. For example, how much of the variance in regression count is already explained by purely textual features like the presence of negators, sentiment flips, or rare words? The feature importance analysis in Table 3 shows gaze features rank highly, but doesn't disentangle this correlation.

4.  **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The authors explicitly state they do not compare against deep learning models because their method is feature-based. While this is a reasonable scoping decision, it leaves an important question unanswered. Modern large language models (e.g., BERT-based classifiers) are known to be quite effective at capturing complex contextual relationships. It is possible that such a model, trained on sufficient data, could achieve similar or better performance without needing an entirely new data modality, thereby rendering this approach less necessary.

### Questions to Authors

1.  To clarify the critical point from Weakness #1: Does your proposed classification pipeline require new eye-tracking data for each new sentence at test time? If so, have you considered an alternative framing where gaze data is used to create a "gold standard" training set, which is then used to train a text-only model that learns to predict sentiment *without* gaze features at inference time? Or perhaps a multi-task model that learns to predict gaze features from text as an auxiliary task?

2.  Regarding the small overall performance gains (Weakness #2), could you elaborate on the cost-benefit trade-off? In what specific, high-value applications do you believe the significant overhead of eye-tracking would be justified by the observed 0.6-1.5% F1 improvement on a general dataset?

3.  Could you provide any further analysis on the unique signal captured by gaze features (Weakness #3)? For instance, can you show examples of sentences that a text-only model (Sn+Sr) gets wrong but the full model (Sn+Sr+Gz) gets right, and link the success to a specific gaze pattern (e.g., a long regression from a surprising phrase) that isn't captured by simple text complexity?
