**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis (SA) for short, user-generated texts by augmenting traditional linguistic features with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, recorded while annotators read and label texts, provide valuable signals for identifying complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony, which are challenging for text-only models. The authors engineer a comprehensive set of features from gaze data—ranging from basic metrics like fixation duration and regressions to more complex features derived from a novel "gaze-saliency graph"—and demonstrate through experiments on two datasets that their inclusion significantly improves classification accuracy over strong feature-based baselines.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **Novel Integration of Cognitive Signals into an NLP task:** The primary contribution is the pioneering use of eye-tracking data as features for sentiment classification. This work provides a well-executed bridge between psycholinguistics and computational linguistics, demonstrating that quantifiable traces of human cognitive processes can be effectively leveraged to solve a difficult NLP problem.
2.  **Strong Empirical Evidence for Handling Linguistic Nuance:** The paper presents compelling evidence that cognitive features are particularly effective for detecting sentiment in complex texts. The ablation study on a held-out set of ironic vs. non-ironic texts (Table 4), showing a much larger performance gain for the ironic subset, is a significant result that validates the authors' core hypothesis.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea is highly innovative and refreshing. While eye-tracking has been used to study NLP phenomena, its direct use as a feature source for a predictive task like SA is new. This opens a promising research direction for incorporating human cognitive signals into computational models, potentially impacting other areas like emotion detection, fake news detection, or readability assessment.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is sound. The use of two distinct datasets (with different eye-trackers), multiple classifiers, systematic feature ablation, and statistical significance testing provides a robust validation of the results. The feature importance analysis (Table 3) and the dedicated experiment on complex vs. simple constructs (Table 4) are particularly effective in supporting the paper's claims.
3.  **Well-Motivated Feature Engineering:** The cognitive features are not arbitrary but are grounded in psycholinguistic research. The authors provide clear intuitions for why features like first-fixation duration (lexical complexity), regressions (reanalysis), and long-distance regressions (surprisal from incongruity) should be relevant. This theoretical grounding elevates the work beyond a simple feature-stacking exercise.
4.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The problem is well-defined, the related work is appropriately situated, the features are explained in detail, and the experimental results are presented lucidly.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Major Practicality and Scalability Concerns:** The most significant weakness is the method's reliance on eye-tracking data for every instance at inference time. This makes the system infeasible for almost any real-world application (e.g., analyzing tweets or product reviews in real time). While Section 7 discusses the future potential of mobile eye-trackers, this is speculative and does not address the fundamental limitation of the current approach. The work would be much stronger if it explored a paradigm where the model learns from gaze data during training but can make predictions at test time using text alone (e.g., via multi-task learning to predict gaze patterns from text). As it stands, the method is more of an academic proof-of-concept than a practical solution.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Neural Models:** The authors explicitly decline to compare their system against deep learning models, which is a major omission. By the time of this work, neural networks (LSTMs, CNNs, and early transformers) were the state-of-the-art for sentiment analysis. Comparing only against other feature-based models (SVM, NB) and a rule-based system provides an incomplete picture of the method's true performance. Without a comparison to a strong, text-only neural baseline on the same datasets, it is difficult to gauge whether the addition of complex gaze data is truly advancing the state-of-the-art or simply catching up to what text-only neural models can already achieve.
3.  **Confounding Factors in Gaze Data:** The paper assumes that gaze patterns reflect the cognitive difficulty of *comprehending* the text. However, since the data was collected during an *annotation* task, the gaze patterns could equally reflect the difficulty of *assigning a label*. A sentence could be easy to understand but difficult to label (e.g., neutral or mixed sentiment), potentially generating gaze patterns similar to those for a sarcastic sentence. The work does not attempt to disentangle the cognitive load of comprehension from the load of decision-making, which is a key confound.
4.  **Information Loss from Feature Aggregation:** The cognitive features are created by averaging the metrics across all human annotators for a given sentence. This approach discards rich information contained in the variance and distribution of gaze patterns across different readers. High disagreement among readers' gaze patterns could itself be a powerful feature indicating ambiguity or sarcasm. The authors briefly mention this as a limitation but do not explore alternative modeling approaches that could leverage individual-level data.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given the significant practical limitation of requiring gaze data at inference time, have you considered alternative frameworks? For instance, could a model be trained to predict gaze features from text as an auxiliary task, allowing it to internalize the "cognitive signal" during training while only requiring text for deployment?
2.  To better situate your results within the broader field of sentiment analysis, could you provide performance numbers for a standard, text-only neural baseline (e.g., a BiLSTM or a fine-tuned BERT model) on your two datasets? This would provide a crucial point of comparison for understanding the true value added by the cognitive features.
3.  The gaze features are averaged across annotators. What is the typical variance of these features for a given text snippet? Have you explored using this inter-annotator variance itself as a feature, on the hypothesis that more complex or ambiguous texts would elicit more varied reading patterns?
