### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis (SA) by incorporating "cognitive features" derived from human eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, which reflect cognitive processing, can provide valuable signals for classifying challenging texts that contain sarcasm, irony, or other complex linguistic constructs. The authors engineer a comprehensive feature set including basic gaze statistics (fixations, saccades, regressions) and more complex features derived from a novel "gaze-saliency graph". Through experiments on two publicly available eye-tracking datasets, they demonstrate that augmenting a strong set of linguistic features (for both general sentiment and sarcasm) with their proposed cognitive features leads to statistically significant improvements in classification accuracy, particularly for ironic texts.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  **Novel Integration of Cognitive Signals:** The primary contribution is the novel and well-executed integration of eye-tracking data as features for a core NLP task. While eye-tracking has been used to *study* language processing, this work is a strong proof-of-concept showing it can be used to *improve* a predictive model, offering a new potential source of information for NLP.
2.  **Creative Feature Engineering:** The authors propose a thoughtful set of cognitive features, moving beyond simple statistics to introduce the "gaze-saliency graph." This graph-based representation is an innovative method for capturing the complex, non-linear reading patterns associated with processing difficult text.
3.  **Targeted Empirical Validation:** The paper provides compelling evidence for its central hypothesis through a well-designed experiment (Section 5.1) that isolates the performance gain on ironic vs. non-ironic texts. This analysis convincingly demonstrates that the cognitive features are not just adding noise but are particularly helpful for the exact types of complex language they are intended to address.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Originality:** The paper's core idea is highly original and opens up a new research direction at the intersection of psycholinguistics and computational linguistics. By demonstrating that the cognitive effort involved in reading is a useful signal for sentiment classification, the work challenges the community to think beyond purely text-based features and models.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experiments:** The experimental methodology is sound. The authors use two distinct datasets, multiple classifier types, and a systematic ablation study to isolate the contribution of each feature set. The use of statistical significance tests adds credibility to their claims. Establishing a strong baseline by first adding sarcasm-specific features (Sn+Sr) before introducing the gaze features (Gz) is a particularly strong point, as it shows the cognitive features provide value even beyond dedicated linguistic features for irony.
3.  **Strong Motivation and Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, with a clear and logical structure. The introduction effectively motivates the work by outlining the lexical, syntactic, and semantic challenges in SA. The justification for each cognitive feature is well-grounded in psycholinguistic literature, making the feature engineering choices transparent and convincing.
4.  **Valuable Analysis:** The feature significance analysis in Table 3, which shows that gaze-based features are consistently ranked among the most important, provides quantitative support for the paper's claims. Furthermore, the targeted analysis on the held-out dataset of ironic vs. non-ironic texts is a key strength, providing direct and powerful evidence that the cognitive features are most impactful where they are needed most.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Fundamental Practicality Concerns:** The most significant weakness is the method's reliance on eye-tracking data at test time. For any new piece of text, the model requires a set of humans to first read it while being eye-tracked to generate the necessary features for classification. This makes the system impractical for virtually all real-world SA applications (e.g., classifying a stream of tweets or product reviews). While Section 7 speculates about a future with ubiquitous mobile eye-trackers, this does not address the fundamental limitation in the current paradigm. The paper should more explicitly frame itself as an *analytical* work demonstrating the information value of cognitive signals, rather than as a practical, deployable system.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Modern Neural Baselines:** The paper explicitly dismisses deep learning models, which is a major omission. State-of-the-art SA and sarcasm detection systems rely on large pre-trained transformers (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa). These models are known to capture complex linguistic patterns from text alone. Without a comparison to such a baseline, it is impossible to know whether the proposed cognitive features provide information that is truly complementary or simply redundant with what a powerful neural model can already infer. A key unanswered question is: does a BERT-based classifier make the same errors that the gaze-enhanced model corrects?
3.  **Suboptimal Aggregation of Gaze Data:** The gaze features are derived by averaging measurements across multiple human annotators. This approach conflates the reading patterns of annotators who may have correctly understood the text's sentiment/sarcasm with those who did not. This averaging process likely discards valuable information about what constitutes a "successful" reading pattern versus an "unsuccessful" one. A more sophisticated aggregation method, perhaps weighting by annotator agreement or clustering different reading patterns, could yield a stronger signal.
4.  **Limited Scale and Generalizability:** The experiments are conducted on two relatively small datasets (<1000 instances each). While this is a common constraint for resource-intensive data like eye-tracking, it raises questions about how well the specific feature weights and performance gains would generalize to larger, more diverse datasets. Furthermore, the use of PCA to reduce dimensionality to 500 on such a small dataset is concerning and may not be a robust approach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the intended application scenario? Given that classifying a new text requires collecting new eye-tracking data for it, is this work better framed as a scientific investigation into the "information gap" of text-only models, rather than a proposal for a practical SA system?
2.  The exclusion of deep learning baselines is a critical point. Could you elaborate on the reasoning for this? A comparison against a fine-tuned BERT model would be crucial for contextualizing the contribution of cognitive features. Is it possible that the gains you observe are on phenomena that large language models already handle well?
3.  Regarding the aggregation of gaze data across annotators: did you consider alternative methods to simple averaging? For example, have you explored modeling individual annotator data or separating the gaze patterns of annotators who correctly identified the sentiment from those who did not, to potentially extract a cleaner signal for "correct" cognitive processing?
