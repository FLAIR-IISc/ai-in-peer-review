### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the utility of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, for the task of sentiment analysis. The authors hypothesize that eye-gaze patterns from human annotators can provide signals to help disambiguate complex linguistic phenomena such as sarcasm and irony. They propose a set of "basic" and "complex" gaze features, the latter derived from a novel "gaze-saliency graph" structure. These features are augmented to traditional sentiment and sarcasm-related textual features. Experiments on two datasets using SVM, Naive Bayes, and a neural network show that the inclusion of cognitive features yields a statistically significant, albeit small, improvement in classification performance, with a more pronounced effect on texts containing irony.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1.  The primary contribution is the novel exploration of using cognitive signals from eye-tracking as features for sentiment analysis. This bridges psycholinguistics and NLP in a practical application.
2.  The paper introduces a detailed set of engineered cognitive features, including a graph-based representation, which could serve as a baseline for future work in this area.
3.  The targeted analysis on a held-out set provides evidence that these cognitive features are most impactful on the specific problem they are intended to solve—texts with complex constructs like sarcasm.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of leveraging real-time cognitive processing signals (via eye-tracking) to inform a computational model is highly innovative and timely. It opens up a new and interesting avenue of research that could have implications for other NLP tasks where subtle human interpretation is key.
2.  **Thorough Feature Engineering:** The authors have clearly put significant effort into designing a comprehensive suite of cognitive features. The distinction between "basic" gaze metrics and the more complex graph-based features is well-motivated. This detailed feature set provides a strong foundation for this nascent research direction.
3.  **Targeted Experimental Design:** The use of datasets known to be rich in sarcasm and irony is a strong choice that directly tests the paper's central hypothesis. Furthermore, the experiment on the held-out dataset (Section 5.1), which explicitly separates complex and simple texts, provides the most compelling evidence in the paper, demonstrating that the gaze features disproportionately benefit the classification of ironic statements.
4.  **Acknowledging Practical Limitations:** The authors proactively address the obvious feasibility concerns of their approach in Section 7. While the arguments are speculative, it is commendable that they acknowledge this major limitation and propose potential future scenarios where such data collection could become practical.

### Weaknesses

Despite the novelty of the idea, the paper is riddled with a substantial number of minor-to-moderate flaws in methodology, experimental interpretation, and presentation. These collective issues undermine the work's contribution and prevent a confident recommendation for acceptance.

1.  **Lack of Methodological Clarity and Rigor:**
    *   The "gaze-saliency graph" and its associated features (Section 4.5) are poorly explained. The definitions are ambiguous and borderline incomprehensible. For example, "Fixation Duration at Left/Source (F1H, F1S) i.e. Largest weighted degree ... considering the fixation duration on the word of node i of edge Eij as edge weight." How can the fixation duration on a single node `i` be an edge weight `Eij`? This critical part of the feature design is not clear enough for replication.
    *   The description of the Multi-layered Neural Network is completely inadequate. Stating it was implemented using Weka with default hyperparameters is insufficient for a scientific publication. The architecture (layers, nodes, activation functions, etc.) must be specified.
    *   The choice of 500 principal components for `NGRAM_PCA` and `IMPLICIT_PCA` is arbitrary and unjustified. Was this value tuned on a development set? Without this information, it appears to be a "magic number."
    . The dismissal of deep learning baselines in Section 2 with the reasoning that "our approach is feature based" is not a valid scientific argument. The goal should be to compare against the state of the art, regardless of the underlying methodology. This omission weakens the claim of performance improvement.

2.  **Superficial Interpretation of Results:**
    *   The overall performance gain from the cognitive features is marginal (0.6% F1 on Dataset 1, 1.5% on Dataset 2). While the authors show statistical significance, they do not discuss the practical significance, especially when weighed against the extreme cost and difficulty of acquiring the necessary eye-tracking data.
    *   Critically, the authors fail to discuss or even mention a major negative result in Table 2. For Dataset 2, the `Sn+Gz` feature set performs catastrophically worse than `Sn` alone when using an SVM (48.9 F1 vs. 62.5 F1). This suggests that cognitive features can significantly *harm* performance under certain conditions, a finding that directly challenges the paper's central narrative. This omission is a serious flaw.

3.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   **Reference List:** The bibliography is in a state of disarray. It is plagued by formatting inconsistencies, spurious question marks (e.g., "Agarwal et al\.,?"), and typesetting artifacts (e.g., "\Q2009\E"). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a conference submission.
    *   **Structural Errors:** The paper's outline, provided in Section 1.4, omits Section 6 ("Error Analysis"), jumping from Section 5 to 7. Section 6 itself appears in the paper without a numbered title. This indicates a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   **Minor Errors:** There are numerous grammatical errors and typos (e.g., "an our features" in Section 4, "discuss on the feasibility" in Section 1.4). Table 1 contains a formatting inconsistency ("F˙score" in the caption vs. "F" in the table header). Captions are not sufficiently detailed; for example, Figure 1 mentions "Areas of Interest (AoI)" but the concept is not central to the paper's methodology and is not explained further.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a much clearer, unambiguous mathematical definition of the edge weights and the subsequent weighted degree calculations for your "gaze-saliency graph" features in Section 4.5?
2.  Could you please explain the severe performance degradation observed for the `Sn+Gz` feature set with the SVM on Dataset 2 (Table 2)? Why do you believe the gaze features were so detrimental in this specific case, and why was this result omitted from your analysis in the main text?
3.  What was the architecture of the Multi-layer NN used in your experiments? Please specify the number of layers, nodes per layer, activation functions, and optimizer used.
