### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of cognitive features, derived from human eye-tracking data, to improve sentiment analysis (SA) for short texts. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, which reflect cognitive processing, can provide valuable signals for detecting complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony that challenge traditional text-based models. The authors propose an extensive feature set that combines standard sentiment and sarcasm-related textual features with a novel collection of gaze-based features. These cognitive features include basic metrics (e.g., fixation duration, regression counts) and more complex ones derived from a proposed "gaze-saliency graph". The efficacy of this augmented feature set is evaluated on two publicly available datasets that contain both text and corresponding eye-tracking data. The results, obtained using several standard classifiers, show that incorporating cognitive features leads to statistically significant improvements in SA performance, with the most pronounced gains observed on texts containing sarcasm and irony.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **Introduction of Novel Cognitive Features for SA:** The main contribution is the design and application of a novel class of features for sentiment analysis based on human eye-movement data. This includes both simple gaze statistics and more complex features derived from a graph-based representation of reading patterns, providing a new source of information for the task.
2.  **Empirical Demonstration of Efficacy for Complex Language:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that these cognitive features are particularly effective for improving the classification of texts with non-literal or complex sentiment, such as sarcasm and irony. This is a significant finding, as this sub-problem remains a major challenge in sentiment analysis.
3.  **Analysis and Application of Psycholinguistic Datasets:** The work effectively leverages two existing datasets containing eye-tracking data for a core NLP task. By first benchmarking standard SA systems on this data, the authors demonstrate its challenging nature and validate its suitability for testing their hypothesis.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Sound Motivation:** The core idea of integrating cognitive signals from eye-tracking into a semantic NLP task is highly novel and represents a creative, interdisciplinary approach. The hypothesis that the cognitive effort required to process complex language like sarcasm will be reflected in gaze patterns is well-motivated by psycholinguistic research, providing a solid conceptual foundation for the experimental work.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is robust. The authors validate their approach on two distinct datasets, which were collected using different eye-tracking hardware (high vs. low sampling rate), enhancing the generalizability of the findings. The systematic ablation of feature sets (Sentiment, Sarcasm, Gaze) clearly isolates and quantifies the contribution of the proposed cognitive features. The use of multiple classifiers and statistical significance testing (McNemar test) adds rigor to the reported results.
3.  **Targeted Analysis of Sarcasm/Irony:** A key strength is the experiment on a held-out dataset partitioned into ironic and non-ironic texts (Table 4). This analysis provides compelling evidence that the performance gain from cognitive features is substantially larger for the more complex ironic texts (a 6.1% F-score improvement) than for simpler, literal texts (2.1%). This directly supports the central claim of the paper and highlights the practical value of the features for a notoriously difficult NLP problem.
4.  **Clarity and Structure:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The features are described in detail, and the experiments are presented logically. The authors also include a thoughtful discussion on the practical feasibility of their approach, proactively addressing the most obvious limitation of their work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Practicality and Generalizability:** The most significant weakness is the model's dependency on eye-tracking data at inference time. This requirement makes the proposed system inapplicable to the vast majority of real-world scenarios where text exists without corresponding gaze data. While the authors discuss the future potential of mobile eye-trackers, this remains speculative and does not address the method's immediate utility. The model, as presented, cannot be used to classify a new piece of text without first conducting a user study to collect gaze data for it.
2.  **Exclusion of State-of-the-Art Comparisons:** The paper explicitly declines to compare its feature-based approach with modern deep learning models, which are the state-of-the-art for sentiment analysis. Without this comparison, it is difficult to gauge the absolute performance of the system. A powerful neural network might learn to identify cues for sarcasm from text alone as effectively as, or better than, the proposed model does with the aid of gaze features. This omission makes it hard to determine if the proposed method advances the state-of-the-art in terms of overall performance, or if it primarily serves as a proof-of-concept for the utility of cognitive data.
3.  **Potential for Confounding Factors:** While the authors include reading difficulty metrics as control features, the analysis could more deeply investigate the potential for confounds. Eye-movement patterns are influenced by many factors besides semantic complexity, including lexical frequency, syntactic complexity, and sentence length. The feature significance analysis is useful, but a more detailed correlational analysis between the proposed gaze features and various textual properties would help to disentangle whether the features are truly capturing high-level sentiment nuances or are acting as proxies for lower-level reading difficulty.
4.  **Information Loss from Feature Aggregation:** The cognitive features are derived by averaging gaze metrics across multiple human participants for each text snippet. This aggregation potentially masks significant and informative variance in reading patterns among individuals. For instance, high variance in gaze metrics for a sentence could itself be a strong signal of ambiguity. The paper does not explore this possibility or justify why averaging is the optimal approach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The proposed method requires gaze data for inference, limiting its practical application. Have you considered an alternative framework where gaze data is used only during *training*? For instance, gaze features could serve as an auxiliary supervision signal in a multi-task learning setup to help a text-only model better identify linguistically complex regions, thereby improving its performance at test time without needing gaze data.
2.  The cognitive features are averaged across all participants. Did you analyze the variance of gaze patterns across participants for the same text? Could this variance itself be a useful feature, perhaps indicating that a text is ambiguous or its sentiment is controversial?
3.  The text states that a significant number of the top-ranked features in your chi-squared analysis are gaze-based. Could you provide more detail on which *specific* cognitive features were most consistently predictive across both datasets (e.g., were regression-based features like `REG` and `RSF` more influential than fixation-based ones like `FDUR`)? This would offer valuable insight into which specific cognitive processes are most tightly linked to the perception of complex sentiment.
