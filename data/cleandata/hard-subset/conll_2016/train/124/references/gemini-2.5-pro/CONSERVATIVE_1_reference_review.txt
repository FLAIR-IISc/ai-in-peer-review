### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with "cognitive features" derived from the eye-tracking data of human annotators. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns (fixations, saccades, regressions) reflect the cognitive effort required to process complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony, and can therefore provide a useful signal for classification. The authors engineer a set of gaze-based features, combine them with standard sentiment and sarcasm features, and demonstrate improved classification accuracy using SVM, Naïve Bayes, and a Multi-layer NN on two datasets.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that features extracted from eye-tracking data can be used to improve sentiment classification performance. The paper shows a statistically significant, though modest, improvement over a baseline of strong textual features.
2.  The introduction of a novel feature set derived from gaze patterns, including both basic statistical measures (e.g., fixation duration, regression count) and more complex features derived from a proposed "gaze-saliency graph."
3.  A targeted analysis on a held-out dataset suggesting that these cognitive features are particularly beneficial for classifying texts containing complex constructs like irony and sarcasm, lending support to the authors' core hypothesis.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of the Explored Modality:** The paper explores an unconventional and interesting direction by introducing a new modality (human gaze data) to a well-established NLP task. Connecting psycholinguistic signals to computational models for sentiment analysis is an intellectually stimulating idea that could open new avenues of research.

2.  **Sound Experimental Procedure:** Within the scope of the experiments conducted, the methodology is largely sound. The authors use standard machine learning classifiers, perform 10-fold cross-validation, and report results using standard metrics (Precision, Recall, F-score). The inclusion of a statistical significance test (McNemar) to validate the observed improvements is a good practice that adds credibility to their findings.

3.  **Targeted Analysis of Feature Utility:** The experiment in Section 5.1, which evaluates the model's performance separately on ironic and non-ironic texts, is a key strength. It provides specific evidence for the authors' central claim that cognitive features are most useful for tackling complex linguistic phenomena, rather than just providing a general, marginal boost.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Fundamental Lack of Practicality and Generalizability:** The most significant weakness of this work is the fundamental infeasibility of the proposed method for any real-world application. The model requires eye-tracking data as an input feature *at inference time*. This implies that to classify the sentiment of a new piece of text, one must first have a group of people read it while their eye movements are recorded with specialized hardware. This is a prohibitive constraint that renders the method unusable outside of a laboratory setting. The discussion in Section 7 ("Feasibility of our approach") is highly speculative and relies on future technological advancements that are far from guaranteed or widespread. A research paper should propose a method that is, at a minimum, plausibly deployable, or should frame its contribution as a purely analytical one. This paper presents its method as a practical classifier, which it is not.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The paper compares its enhanced feature set against baselines using only traditional, non-neural features (unigrams, lexicon counts, etc.). The authors explicitly state they do not consider deep learning approaches (Section 2), which even at the time of this work were established as powerful baselines for sentiment analysis. A convincing argument for the utility of a novel, complex feature set requires demonstrating its superiority over the strongest available methods that rely on text alone. Without a comparison to strong neural baselines (e.g., CNNs or LSTMs on word embeddings), it is impossible to know if the small performance gains from gaze data could have been more easily achieved by simply using a more powerful text-based model. The cognitive signal might be redundant if a strong enough language model is used.

3.  **Potential for Confounding Variables:** The paper hypothesizes that gaze features capture sentiment-related nuances. However, it is equally plausible that these features are simply proxies for general reading difficulty, lexical complexity, or syntactic ambiguity. While the authors include Flesch Readability scores as a control, this is a coarse measure. Complex phenomena like sarcasm often correlate with general processing difficulty. The paper does not adequately disentangle whether the gaze features are capturing a sentiment-specific signal or a more general "this text is difficult/surprising" signal, which a powerful text-based model might also learn to detect.

4.  **Marginal Performance Gains:** While statistically significant, the absolute improvements from adding gaze features (Gz) to the combined sentiment and sarcasm features (Sn+Sr) are quite small: a 0.6% F-score increase on Dataset 1 and a 1.5% increase on Dataset 2 for the best classifier (SVM). Given the extraordinary cost and complexity of acquiring the gaze data, this small gain raises serious questions about the cost-benefit tradeoff of the proposed approach.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the intended deployment scenario for this model? Is the contribution purely an analytical finding (i.e., demonstrating a correlation between gaze and sentiment), or do you envision a practical system? If the latter, how do you propose to overcome the seemingly insurmountable requirement of obtaining multi-user eye-tracking data for every new text instance that needs to be classified?

2.  The decision to exclude deep learning models from your comparisons is a major limitation. Could you provide a stronger justification for this? Without comparing against state-of-the-art text-only models, the claim that cognitive features provide a uniquely valuable signal remains unproven. How can we be sure that a standard BiLSTM or CNN model would not outperform your best model (Sn+Sr+Gz) using text alone?

3.  How have you controlled for the possibility that your cognitive features are simply capturing general linguistic surprisal or processing difficulty, rather than sentiment-specific constructs? Could the same improvements be achieved by using a text-based measure of surprisal from a language model as a feature?
