**Summary of Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution of this work is the novel introduction of cognitive features, derived from human eye-tracking data, as a signal for sentiment analysis. This represents a bold and original attempt to bridge the gap between computational linguistics and psycholinguistics, moving beyond purely textual information to incorporate a proxy for real-time human cognitive processing.
2.  The paper provides strong empirical evidence that these cognitive features are particularly effective for correctly classifying sentiment in linguistically complex texts that contain sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations—areas where traditional text-based models notoriously struggle.

**Strengths**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and a New Research Direction:** In a field often focused on incremental improvements to existing architectures, this paper stands out for its conceptual creativity. The idea of using eye-movement patterns as features for a classic NLP task is genuinely new and thought-provoking. It challenges the community to think beyond the text itself and consider how signals from human language processing can inform our models. This work has the potential to seed a new and exciting subfield of research.
2.  **Compelling and Well-Designed Experiments:** The authors do an excellent job of empirically validating their core hypothesis. The experiments are clear, and the ablation studies systematically demonstrate the value of each feature set. The analysis on the held-out dataset (Table 4) is particularly insightful, providing convincing proof that the gaze-based features disproportionately benefit the most challenging cases (a 6.1% F-score improvement for ironic texts vs. 2.1% for non-ironic). This targeted analysis elevates the work from a simple demonstration of feature utility to a more nuanced scientific finding.
3.  **High Potential for Broader Impact:** Although the method is tested on sentiment analysis, its underlying principle is applicable to a wide range of NLP tasks that require deep pragmatic and semantic understanding. One could easily imagine similar cognitive features being useful for tasks like abusive language detection, argument mining, question answering, and text summarization. The paper successfully opens the door to these future explorations, giving it a significance that extends beyond its immediate application.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant Practicality Limitations:** The most glaring issue is the feasibility of the approach in any real-world scenario. The model requires eye-tracking data to be collected for every new text instance at inference time, which is currently impractical on a large scale. The authors’ discussion of future mobile eye-trackers (Section 7) is appreciated but remains speculative and does not resolve the immediate and fundamental limitation of the proposed system. While this does not diminish the scientific contribution of the paper as a proof-of-concept, it is a major barrier to its application.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The decision to exclude deep learning models from the comparison is a notable weakness. While the work is presented as a feature-engineering approach, the central claim is that cognitive features help with complex linguistic phenomena. It is crucial to know if a powerful, pre-trained language model (e.g., a fine-tuned BERT or RoBERTa) could learn to capture these same nuances from text alone, potentially rendering the expensive and impractical data collection unnecessary. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the true necessity of the cognitive features relative to the current state-of-the-art.
3.  **Somewhat Ad-Hoc Feature Design:** While the basic gaze features are well-motivated by psycholinguistic literature, the more complex graph-based features feel less principled. The use of "largest and second largest weighted degree" metrics seems to be the result of exploratory data analysis rather than being derived from a clear theoretical basis. A stronger justification for why these specific graph properties are expected to capture sentiment-related processing would improve the work's rigor.

**Overall Assessment and Recommendation**

This is an exciting, high-risk, high-reward paper that introduces a genuinely novel idea to the field. The core contribution—using cognitive signals from eye-tracking to aid sentiment analysis—is both creative and compellingly demonstrated. The authors show convincingly that these features are not just redundant signals but provide crucial information for disambiguating complex cases like sarcasm, which remain a major hurdle for text-only systems.

The weaknesses, particularly the practical infeasibility and the lack of SOTA baselines, are significant. In a more methodologically conservative venue, these might be grounds for rejection. However, for a conference like CoNLL that should value innovation, the sheer novelty and potential impact of the core idea outweigh these limitations. This paper presents a fascinating new direction and a wealth of intriguing findings that are sure to spark discussion and inspire follow-up work. It is precisely the kind of bold, thought-provoking research that our community needs.

I strongly recommend acceptance.
