**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper introduces a novel method for sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that the cognitive processes involved in understanding complex linguistic phenomena, such as sarcasm and irony, are reflected in readers' eye-movement patterns, and that these patterns can be leveraged to improve classification accuracy. The authors design a suite of cognitive features, ranging from basic gaze metrics (fixation duration, regressions) to more complex measures derived from a proposed "gaze-saliency graph." They test their approach on two datasets containing sentiment-annotated texts with corresponding eye-tracking data, demonstrating that the inclusion of cognitive features yields significant improvements, particularly on texts with complex sentimental constructs.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A new paradigm for feature engineering:** The primary contribution is the conceptual leap of using direct cognitive signals from human readers as features for an NLP task. This is a bold and creative approach that opens up a new research direction at the intersection of computational linguistics and cognitive science, moving beyond using text as the sole source of information.
2.  **A targeted solution for a known problem:** The paper provides compelling evidence that this new source of information is particularly effective for tackling sarcasm and irony—long-standing challenges for sentiment analysis systems. The targeted analysis showing a much larger performance gain on complex texts is a significant finding.
3.  **Introduction of novel feature representations:** The paper proposes concrete ways to operationalize gaze data, notably through the "gaze-saliency graph," which provides a structured way to represent the complex interplay of fixations and saccades across a sentence.

**Strengths**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Vision:** The core idea is exceptionally novel and forward-thinking. In a field often focused on incremental improvements to existing architectures, this work proposes an entirely new modality of information. By directly modeling the human reading process, the authors are tackling the problem from a fundamentally different and exciting angle. This willingness to explore high-risk, high-reward ideas is commendable and pushes the boundaries of the field.
2.  **Strong Empirical Evidence for the Core Claim:** While the overall F-score improvements are modest, the analysis in Table 4 is extremely persuasive. Demonstrating that the cognitive features provide a ~3x greater boost for ironic texts compared to non-ironic texts provides strong support for the central hypothesis. This is not just another feature that helps a little everywhere; it is a feature that specifically helps where traditional methods fail most, which is a much stronger result.
3.  **Well-Motivated Feature Design:** The cognitive features are not arbitrary but are grounded in psycholinguistic literature and intuitive hypotheses about the reading process. For example, the "Regression from second half to first half" (RSF) feature is a clever operationalization of the "surprise" effect often encountered at the punchline of a sarcastic sentence. This thoughtful feature engineering adds to the work's credibility.
4.  **Proactive and Honest Discussion of Limitations:** The authors directly confront the obvious and significant practical limitation of their method in the "Feasibility" section. By discussing potential future scenarios involving mobile eye-trackers, they show foresight and an understanding of their work's place in the broader research landscape. This transparency strengthens the paper.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant Practicality Concerns:** The most glaring weakness is the method's reliance on eye-tracking data for inference, which renders it impractical for almost any real-world application today. The proposed future with ubiquitous mobile eye-trackers is speculative. As it stands, the method is a compelling proof-of-concept, but its utility is confined to a research setting. This is a major hurdle that limits the immediate impact of the work, even if its conceptual contribution is high.
2.  **Exclusion of State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The paper explicitly omits comparisons to deep learning models (e.g., RNNs, Transformers), which are the standard for sentiment analysis. While the work is feature-based, the lack of engagement with the current state-of-the-art makes it difficult to assess the true power of the cognitive signal. It is unclear whether these features offer information that is orthogonal and complementary to what a large pre-trained model might learn, or if their effect would be diminished in a stronger baseline system.
3.  **Potential for Confounding Variables:** The paper attempts to control for general reading difficulty using features like sentence length and readability scores. However, it's plausible that the cognitive features are capturing a more general "cognitive load" signal that is correlated with, but not specific to, sentiment. For instance, syntactically ambiguous or thematically complex sentences might elicit similar gaze patterns. A more thorough analysis to disentangle sentiment-specific difficulty from other forms of processing difficulty would make the claims more robust.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Your method relies on averaging gaze data across participants. This discards potentially rich information about individual differences and inter-annotator disagreement. Have you explored using the variance of gaze metrics across users as a feature, or perhaps a multi-view learning approach where each user's gaze data provides a separate "view" of the instance?
2.  The cost of collecting gaze data at test time is a major bottleneck. Have you considered a two-stage approach where you first train a model to *predict* the cognitive features from the text alone, and then use these predicted features for the final sentiment classification? This could potentially transfer the cognitive signal into a text-only model, making the approach far more scalable.
3.  The two datasets use eye-trackers with very different sampling rates (500Hz vs. 120Hz). Did you observe any differences in the utility or stability of the gaze features between the two datasets that might be attributable to hardware quality? This seems crucial for your argument about the future feasibility of using low-fidelity mobile cameras.
