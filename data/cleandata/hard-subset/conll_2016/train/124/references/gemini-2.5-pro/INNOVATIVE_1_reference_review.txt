### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data. The authors hypothesize that eye-movement patterns, which reflect cognitive processing, can provide crucial signals for deciphering complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations, where purely textual analysis often fails. They engineer a set of "gaze features"—ranging from basic statistics like fixation duration and regression counts to more complex metrics derived from a novel "gaze-saliency graph"—and show that their inclusion leads to significant performance gains on two datasets known to be challenging for standard sentiment classifiers.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A New Paradigm for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the bold conceptual leap of incorporating real-time cognitive signals (via eye-tracking) directly into a computational linguistics task. This moves beyond analyzing just the text itself to analyzing the *human process of interpreting* the text, opening a fascinating and underexplored avenue for NLP research.
2.  **Targeted Improvement on a Hard Problem:** The work provides compelling evidence that this new source of information is not just redundant but is particularly effective for the most difficult cases of sentiment analysis (i.e., sarcasm and irony). The targeted evaluation on a held-out set of complex texts demonstrates the unique value of the cognitive signals where they are needed most.
3.  **Novel Feature Engineering:** The paper introduces a creative feature engineering approach, most notably the "gaze-saliency graph," which attempts to model the structural dynamics of a reader's attention. This is a thoughtful attempt to extract more complex relational information from raw gaze data.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Vision:** This is precisely the kind of high-risk, high-reward research that our community needs more of. The authors are not making an incremental improvement to an existing model; they are proposing a fundamentally new type of input signal for a classic NLP task. The idea of bridging the gap between psycholinguistic observation and machine learning features is both exciting and visionary. The paper's value lies not just in its results but in the new direction it charts for the field.
2.  **Strong Proof of Concept:** While the overall performance gains may seem modest in absolute terms, they serve as a powerful proof of concept. The fact that adding gaze features yields a consistent and statistically significant improvement across multiple datasets and classifiers is a strong validation of the central hypothesis. The feature importance analysis, showing that 7-10 of the top 20 features are gaze-based, provides clear evidence that these signals are not spurious.
3.  **Well-Motivated and Focused Evaluation:** The authors intelligently frame their contribution as a solution to a well-known, difficult sub-problem in sentiment analysis. The experiment in Section 5.1, which isolates complex ironic/sarcastic texts and shows a much larger F-score improvement (6.1%), is the most compelling result in the paper. It convincingly demonstrates that the cognitive features are indeed capturing information related to the processing of linguistic nuance that text-based features miss.
4.  **Commendable Honesty about Feasibility:** The authors proactively and honestly address the elephant in the room: the practical infeasibility of requiring eye-tracking data for inference (Section 7). While their proposed solutions are speculative, their acknowledgment and discussion of this critical limitation is a sign of mature research. For a paper introducing a novel concept, demonstrating its potential is the primary goal, and this paper succeeds in doing so.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Major Practicality and Scalability Hurdles:** The most significant weakness is the method's reliance on specialized hardware and data collection for every text snippet at inference time. This currently confines the approach to a laboratory setting and presents a massive barrier to real-world application. While the paper discusses future technologies, this remains a speculative solution to a very real and immediate limitation. However, for a paper valued on its novelty, I see this as an acceptable trade-off for exploring a new frontier.
2.  **Exclusion of Modern Deep Learning Baselines:** The authors' decision to exclude comparisons to deep learning models, while justified by their focus on feature engineering, makes the work feel slightly disconnected from the current state of the art in sentiment analysis. It is unclear how these engineered features would stack up against or complement the representations learned by a transformer-based model. A simple experiment concatenating the cognitive feature vector to a sentence embedding from a pre-trained model could have provided a valuable data point and better contextualized the contribution.
3.  **Simple Aggregation of Gaze Data:** The cognitive features are derived by averaging the gaze data across multiple human annotators. This approach, while straightforward, potentially discards rich information present in the variance of reading patterns. High disagreement among readers (e.g., some fly through a sentence while others regress heavily) could itself be a powerful feature indicative of ambiguity, sarcasm, or controversy.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The averaging of gaze features across participants seems like a missed opportunity. Have you considered modeling the variance or other moments of the distribution of gaze metrics across annotators as features? Intuitively, high variance in fixation time on a particular word might signal that it is a point of contention or ambiguity, which seems highly relevant for detecting sarcasm.
2.  The "gaze-saliency graphs" are an innovative idea. The current features are based on node degree and density. Did you explore more complex graph-theoretic properties (e.g., centrality measures, community detection, path analysis) that might capture more sophisticated reading strategies, such as identifying key "anchor" words that readers repeatedly return to?
3.  Regarding the feasibility gap, have you considered a knowledge distillation approach as a possible path forward? One could potentially use the collected gaze data to train a "teacher" model (as you have done) and then use its outputs to train a "student" model that operates on text alone, attempting to predict the "cognitive signature" of a sentence without needing an eye-tracker at inference time. Do you see this as a viable direction?
