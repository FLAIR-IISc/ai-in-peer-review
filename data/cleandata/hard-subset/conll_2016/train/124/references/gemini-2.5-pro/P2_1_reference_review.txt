### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the utility of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, for the task of sentiment analysis in short texts. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns of human readers can provide valuable signals for detecting complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony, which are challenging for traditional text-based models. The authors propose an enhanced feature set that combines standard sentiment and sarcasm-related textual features with a novel set of gaze-based features. These cognitive features include basic statistics (e.g., fixation duration, regression counts) and more complex measures derived from a proposed "gaze-saliency graph." Through experiments on two datasets containing challenging texts, the authors demonstrate that augmenting a feature-based classifier with these cognitive features yields statistically significant improvements in performance, especially on texts containing irony and sarcasm.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Novel Integration of Cognitive Signals for a Core NLP Task:** The primary contribution is the novel idea of using eye-tracking data not just for analyzing linguistic phenomena, but as a direct feature source to improve a downstream NLP application like sentiment analysis. This bridges psycholinguistic research with practical NLP in a compelling way.
2.  **A Comprehensive Set of Gaze-Based Features:** The paper introduces a well-motivated set of cognitive features, ranging from established metrics like fixation duration and regressions to more innovative graph-based features derived from their "gaze-saliency graph." This provides a concrete, engineered feature set that future work can build upon.
3.  **Targeted Empirical Validation:** The authors provide strong evidence for their hypothesis through a carefully designed experiment on a held-out set of ironic vs. non-ironic texts. This analysis demonstrates that the performance gain from cognitive features is most pronounced on the complex examples they are designed to address.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Originality:** The core idea is highly innovative. While eye-tracking has been used to study reading and annotation processes, its use as a feature for a classification model is a fresh and exciting direction for the field. It opens up a new potential modality for understanding and modeling language.
2.  **Thorough and Methodical Evaluation:** The experimental design is sound. The authors systematically show the incremental benefit of each feature group (Sentiment -> +Sarcasm -> +Gaze), which clearly isolates the contribution of the proposed cognitive features. The use of a held-out set to specifically test performance on complex vs. simple texts (Table 4) is a particularly strong piece of analysis that directly supports their main claim. The inclusion of statistical significance testing further strengthens their conclusions.
3.  **Well-Motivated Feature Engineering:** The cognitive features are not arbitrary; they are grounded in psycholinguistic literature. For instance, the authors correctly link regressions (REG, RSF) to reanalysis and processing difficulty, and first-fixation duration (FDUR) to lexical complexity. This thoughtful feature design adds to the paper's credibility.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors use publicly available datasets and provide sufficient detail about their feature extraction and experimental setup, which is commendable and facilitates reproducibility.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Major Practicality and Feasibility Concerns:** The biggest weakness of this work is its practical applicability. The proposed method requires eye-tracking data *at inference time* for any new text to be classified. This is a significant bottleneck that severely limits its use in any real-world scenario. The "Feasibility" section (Section 7) acknowledges this but relies on speculative future technology (ubiquitous mobile eye-trackers) and user consent, which are not current realities. The paper would be much stronger if it proposed a method to *predict* gaze features from text, thereby decoupling the model from the need for an eye-tracker at prediction time. As it stands, the work is more of a diagnostic analysis of what information is *contained* in gaze data, rather than a deployable system.
2.  **Limited Comparison to Modern Baselines:** The authors explicitly state they do not compare against deep learning approaches, justifying this by the feature-based nature of their work. However, in the current NLP landscape, this is a significant omission. State-of-the-art sentiment analysis relies heavily on pre-trained transformer models (e.g., BERT). Without a comparison, it is impossible to know if the proposed feature-based model, even with cognitive features, outperforms a standard fine-tuned BERT model on these challenging datasets. It's plausible that large language models implicitly learn to handle some of the nuances that the gaze features are designed to capture, making the practical benefit of this complex data collection pipeline unclear.
3.  **Modest Overall Performance Gains:** While statistically significant, the absolute improvements on the full datasets are quite small: an F-score increase of 0.6% on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2 when adding gaze features to the best textual model (Sn+Sr). The impressive 6.1% improvement is on a smaller, curated subset of "complex" texts. This raises questions about the cost-benefit trade-off of the proposed approach. The effort of collecting and processing eye-tracking data for a modest general improvement may not be justified.
4.  **Information Loss Through Feature Aggregation:** The cognitive features are created by averaging the eye-movement data from multiple annotators for each text snippet. This approach risks losing crucial information. For example, the gaze patterns of a reader who understands the sarcasm might be very different from one who does not. Averaging these patterns could result in a noisy, less discriminative feature. The authors briefly mention this in the Error Analysis, but it is a methodological limitation of the current approach.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The primary concern is the method's reliance on eye-tracking data at inference time. Have you considered a two-stage approach where you first train a model to predict salient gaze features (e.g., regression count, average fixation duration) from text alone, and then use these *predicted* features in your final sentiment classifier? This would create a practical system that leverages the insights from cognitive data without the hardware dependency during prediction.
2.  Could you provide more context for your results by comparing your best-performing model (Sn+Sr+Gz) against a standard fine-tuned transformer-based baseline like BERT-base on your two datasets? This would help the community understand where this novel approach stands in relation to current state-of-the-art methods, especially for handling sarcasm and irony.
3.  Regarding the aggregation of features across annotators: did you perform any analysis on the variance of gaze patterns among participants for the same text? Does higher variance in gaze behavior correlate with texts that are more ambiguous or difficult to classify? Exploring individual-level patterns, rather than just the average, could be a fruitful direction.
