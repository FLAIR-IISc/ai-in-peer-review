### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis (SA) by incorporating "cognitive features" derived from the eye-movement patterns of human annotators. The central hypothesis is that these gaze-based features can help machine learning models better understand and classify texts containing complex linguistic phenomena such as sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations, which pose significant challenges for systems relying solely on textual features. The authors engineer a comprehensive feature set combining traditional sentiment and sarcasm features with a suite of new basic and graph-based cognitive features. They test their approach on two publicly available datasets containing short texts with associated eye-tracking data, demonstrating that augmenting a feature-based SVM classifier with cognitive features yields statistically significant improvements in classification F-score. A targeted experiment on a held-out dataset further shows that these gains are most pronounced for texts containing irony and sarcasm.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Novelty of Cognitive Features for SA:** The primary contribution is the pioneering use of eye-tracking data as a direct signal for the task of sentiment analysis. The work convincingly demonstrates that cognitive processing signals, as captured by gaze patterns, are a rich source of information that is complementary to standard textual features, particularly for identifying nuanced and non-literal sentiment.
2.  **Specialized Analysis of Complex Language:** A key contribution is the empirical validation that cognitive features are especially effective for difficult cases of SA. The analysis in Section 5.1, which isolates ironic/sarcastic texts, provides strong evidence that gaze patterns capture the "surprise" or re-analysis inherent in processing such language, directly addressing a well-known weakness of traditional SA systems.
3.  **Feature Engineering of Gaze Data:** The paper contributes a well-motivated set of cognitive features. Beyond simple metrics like fixation duration, the introduction of more complex features, such as the count of regressions from the second half to the first half of a sentence (RSF) and the graph-based "gaze-saliency" features, represents a thoughtful attempt to model specific cognitive processes relevant to understanding sentiment.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The paper's core idea is highly innovative and opens a new research direction for computational linguistics. By bridging psycholinguistics and NLP, it shows a promising path for integrating human cognitive signals to solve challenging language understanding tasks. The potential impact is significant, suggesting that how humans process text can be as important as the text itself.
2.  **Rigorous and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough. The authors validate their claims on two separate datasets collected with different eye-trackers, test their features with three different classifiers (SVM, NB, NN), and perform ablation studies to clearly isolate the contribution of each feature set. The use of statistical significance testing (McNemar test) adds rigor to their claims of improvement.
3.  **Strong Supporting Analysis:** The experiment on the held-out set (Table 4) is a particularly strong piece of evidence. By separating complex (ironic) and simple (non-ironic) texts, the authors directly test and confirm their central hypothesis: that cognitive features disproportionately help in cases where textual cues are ambiguous or misleading. The 6.1% F-score improvement on ironic texts is a compelling result.
4.  **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the need for cognitive features by outlining the lexical, syntactic, and semantic challenges in SA. Each proposed cognitive feature is accompanied by a clear, intuitive justification grounded in psycholinguistic theory (e.g., linking regressions to syntactic/semantic reanalysis).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Practicality and Applicability:** The method's primary weakness is its reliance on eye-tracking data at prediction time. The approach is not practical for real-world, large-scale sentiment analysis, where such data is unavailable for new texts. The discussion in Section 7 acknowledges this but relies on speculative future technology (ubiquitous mobile eye-trackers). A more critical discussion is needed. The paper frames this as a classification method, but it is perhaps better viewed as a data analysis technique for creating better training data or a method for building models that could then be distilled into a text-only model. The lack of a clear path to deployment for a text-only SA system is a major limitation.
2.  **Baselines Are Not State-of-the-Art:** The authors compare their method against feature-based classifiers (SVM, NB) which, while standard, are not state-of-the-art. The paper explicitly mentions but dismisses deep learning models (CNNs, RNNs), which were already strong performers for SA at the time of this work. The claim of improving over the "state of the art" (Section 1.4) is therefore not substantiated. A stronger evaluation would have been to show that cognitive features can augment a powerful deep learning baseline, demonstrating their value is orthogonal to the choice of model architecture.
3.  **Potential for Confounding Variables:** While the authors control for basic reading difficulty (Flesch score, sentence length), the performance gains attributed to cognitive features might be partially explained by other confounding textual properties. Gaze patterns are known to be sensitive to factors like syntactic complexity, lexical frequency, and semantic ambiguity, all of which may also correlate with sentiment polarity. It is not entirely clear if the gaze features are truly capturing high-level pragmatic reasoning (like sarcasm detection) or if they are simply a more effective proxy for general textual complexity than the simple baseline features used.
4.  **Aggregation of Participant Data:** The gaze features for a given text are derived by averaging the metrics across all participants. This approach discards potentially valuable information about individual differences and inter-annotator disagreement. For texts where readers have very different gaze patterns (perhaps indicating ambiguity), this variance is lost. A method that models individual readings or the distribution of gaze behaviors might yield a more robust signal.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you clarify the intended deployment scenario for a model trained with cognitive features? Given that eye-tracking data is not available for texts "in the wild," is the goal to use these features only during training? If so, how would the final model make predictions on new text without access to gaze data? Have you considered a multi-task learning setup where one task is to predict gaze features from text, which are then used for the main SA task?
2.  The performance gain on Dataset 2 (+9.3% F-score with all features) was substantially larger than on Dataset 1 (+3.7%). However, Dataset 2 was collected using a lower-fidelity eye-tracker (120Hz vs. 500Hz). This seems counter-intuitive, as one might expect higher-quality data to be more useful. Do you have a hypothesis that could explain this result?
3.  Your feature significance analysis is mentioned but the results (i.e., the table) are not included. Could you elaborate on which specific gaze features were most predictive? For instance, were features related to regressions (e.g., REG, RSF) more strongly correlated with ironic texts compared to features related to simple fixations (e.g., FDUR, FC)? Such an analysis could provide more direct evidence for the cognitive processes you hypothesize are at play.
