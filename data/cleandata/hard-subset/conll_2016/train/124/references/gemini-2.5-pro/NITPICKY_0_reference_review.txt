### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, to enhance sentiment analysis (SA) of short texts. The authors hypothesize that eye-movement patterns of human readers can help capture complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony, which are challenging for traditional feature-based models. The core contribution is an extensive feature engineering study that combines standard sentiment and sarcasm features with a newly proposed set of "cognitive features" based on gaze patterns. The authors demonstrate through experiments on two datasets that augmenting a model with these cognitive features results in a statistically significant, albeit modest, improvement in classification performance, particularly for texts containing sarcasm and irony. A secondary contribution is the exploration of various gaze-based features, including basic statistics and more complex ones derived from a proposed "gaze-saliency graph".

### Strengths

1.  **Novel Research Direction:** The primary strength of this work is its novel premise. Integrating cognitive signals from eye-tracking into a mainstream NLP task like sentiment analysis is an interesting and underexplored area. The core hypothesis that cognitive processing difficulties, reflected in gaze, can serve as a proxy for linguistic complexity (like sarcasm) is well-motivated and compelling.
2.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments. They use two distinct datasets collected with different eye-trackers, test three different classifiers (NB, SVM, NN), and systematically evaluate the contribution of different feature subsets (Sentiment, Sarcasm, Gaze). This ablation-style study provides a clear picture of how each feature group impacts performance.
3.  **Targeted Analysis of Complex Phenomena:** The experiment in Section 5.1 on a held-out dataset, separating "irony" from "non-irony" instances, is a significant strength. It provides direct evidence for the central claim that cognitive features are most beneficial for handling complex linguistic constructs. The reported 6.1% F-score improvement on ironic texts is a much stronger result than the marginal gains seen in the overall dataset.

### Weaknesses

Despite the novel idea, the paper is marred by a substantial number of minor and major flaws in its methodology, presentation, and experimental rigor. These issues collectively undermine the credibility of the results and conclusions.

1.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper demonstrates a significant lack of attention to detail.
    *   **Section Numbering:** There is a critical structural error in the manuscript. The introduction (Section 1.4) states that Section 7 will discuss feasibility and Section 8 is the conclusion. However, the paper presents Section 7 ("Feasibility") and then Section 6 ("Error Analysis"), followed by Section 8 ("Conclusion"). This is a glaring organizational mistake.
    *   **Formatting Errors:** There are numerous typos and formatting inconsistencies. For instance, "F˙score" in Table 1 and "NGRAM˙PCA" in Section 4.1 contain stray diacritics. The formula for the number of possible links in a graph in Section 4.5 is incorrectly written as `(|V |×|V |−1|)/2)`. Notation for datasets is inconsistent (`Dataset 1` vs. `Dataset-1`).
    *   **References:** The reference section is exceptionally poorly formatted and appears to be an unedited export from a reference manager. It is littered with errors such as stray question marks (e.g., "Agarwal et al\.,?"), inconsistent use of "et al.", misplaced commas, and formatting tags (e.g., `\Q2009\E`). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a conference submission.

2.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor:**
    *   **No Hyperparameter Tuning:** The authors explicitly state that "Several classifier hyperparameters are kept to the default values given in Weka." This is a major methodological weakness. Without proper hyperparameter tuning for each feature combination, the reported results are likely suboptimal and the comparisons between feature sets may not be valid.
    *   **Inappropriate Statistical Test:** The authors use the McNemar test to assess the statistical significance of differences in F-scores (Section 5). The McNemar test is designed to compare the predictions of two classifiers on a contingency table of their agreements/disagreements, not to compare aggregate performance metrics like F-score. A paired t-test over the cross-validation folds or a bootstrap test would have been appropriate. This misuse of a statistical test calls the significance claims into question.
    *   **Arbitrary Parameter Choices:** Key parameters seem to have been chosen without justification. For instance, the dimensionality for PCA reduction is set to 500 for both n-grams and incongruity features. Given the small dataset sizes (<1000 instances), 500 is still a very large dimension, and no rationale is provided for this specific number.

3.  **Clarity and Justification of Features:**
    *   The "complex gaze features" derived from the "gaze-saliency graph" (Section 4.5) are poorly defined. The paper claims the graph is undirected but then defines features based on "Left/Source" and "Right/Target" (e.g., F1H, F2H), which implies directionality. The description of features as "Largest weighted degree and second largest weighted degree" is ambiguous. It is unclear what these features represent physically.
    *   The motivation for these complex features is weak, stated as being "motivated by our qualitative observations from the gaze data." This is not a rigorous basis for feature design.

4.  **Limited Practical Significance:** While the authors demonstrate statistical significance, the practical significance of the results is questionable. The performance gain on Dataset 1 from adding gaze features is only 0.6% F-score (72.9% to 73.5%). Given the immense cost, complexity, and intrusiveness of collecting eye-tracking data, such a marginal improvement seems insufficient to justify the approach. The feasibility discussion in Section 7 is highly speculative and relies on future technological advancements that are not yet reality.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the precise definitions of the "complex gaze features" in Section 4.5? Is the gaze-saliency graph directed or undirected? How exactly are the "largest weighted degree" features computed and what is their intuition?
2.  Can you justify the use of the McNemar test on an aggregate metric like F-score? Have you considered using a more appropriate statistical test, such as a paired t-test over the cross-validation folds, to validate your significance claims?
3.  Why were classifier hyperparameters not tuned? It is standard practice and its omission may mean that the reported improvements are simply an artifact of how well the default settings happen to suit a particular feature set.
4.  The paper contains a major structural error where Section 6 follows Section 7. Could you please address this and the numerous formatting and referencing errors throughout the manuscript?
