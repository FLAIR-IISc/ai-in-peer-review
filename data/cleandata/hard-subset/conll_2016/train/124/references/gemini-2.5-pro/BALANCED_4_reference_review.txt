**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis (SA) by augmenting traditional text-based features with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that gaze patterns, such as fixations and saccades, reflect the cognitive processes involved in understanding complex linguistic phenomena and can thus help resolve ambiguities that challenge standard SA models. The authors engineer a comprehensive set of "basic" and graph-based "complex" gaze features from two existing datasets containing eye-tracking data for sentiment-annotated texts. Through a series of experiments using SVM, Naive Bayes, and Neural Network classifiers, they demonstrate that their combined feature set significantly outperforms models that rely on textual features alone. The performance gains are shown to be particularly pronounced for texts containing complex constructs like sarcasm and irony.

**Main Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is the novel and effective application of cognitive features from eye-tracking to the task of sentiment analysis. The paper provides compelling evidence (especially in the held-out experiment in Table 4) that gaze patterns serve as a powerful signal for disambiguating complex, non-literal language like sarcasm, an area where purely text-based models often struggle.
2.  A secondary contribution is the development of a rich set of engineered gaze features. Beyond using standard psycholinguistic metrics, the authors propose a novel "gaze-saliency graph" to derive more complex relational features, providing a methodological contribution for future work in this interdisciplinary area.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Strong Motivation:** The idea of incorporating cognitive signals into a core NLP task like sentiment analysis is highly original and well-motivated. The introduction does an excellent job of outlining the limitations of text-only approaches, particularly for pragmatic challenges like sarcasm, which convincingly sets the stage for exploring an alternative information source like gaze data.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is a key strength. The authors validate their approach on two distinct datasets collected with different eye-trackers, increasing the robustness of their findings. They perform detailed ablation studies (Table 2) to clearly isolate the contribution of each feature set, and the use of statistical significance testing adds rigor to their claims of improvement.
3.  **Compelling Analysis of Complex Texts:** The targeted experiment on a held-out set of ironic vs. non-ironic texts (Section 5.1) provides the strongest evidence for the paper's central hypothesis. The demonstration that gaze features yield a much larger performance gain on ironic texts (6.1% F-score increase) compared to simpler texts (2.1% increase) is a very powerful and persuasive result that directly validates the utility of the proposed method for its intended purpose.
4.  **Proactive Discussion of Limitations:** The authors should be commended for dedicating a section (Section 7) to the practical feasibility of their approach. While the requirement of eye-tracking data is a major hurdle, their acknowledgment of this limitation and discussion of potential future scenarios (e.g., ubiquitous mobile eye-trackers) shows a mature and realistic perspective on the work's current standing and future potential.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Fundamental Practicality and Applicability Concerns:** This is the most significant weakness. The proposed method requires eye-tracking data for every instance at inference time, which severely limits its real-world applicability to a few niche scenarios. The paper frames the work as a general improvement for sentiment analysis, but it cannot be directly compared to or replace standard text-only models. The contribution is more accurately a proof-of-concept demonstrating the value of cognitive signals, rather than a practical, deployable system. The framing could be more precise about this distinction. A more practical research direction, not explored here, would be to use gaze data to improve a model that *only requires text at inference*.
2.  **Potential Confounding between Cognitive and Readability Signals:** The authors group basic text readability features (Flesch score, sentence length) together with the gaze features into a single `Gz` set. However, many basic gaze metrics (e.g., fixation count, saccade length) are known to be highly correlated with text complexity and length. This experimental design makes it difficult to disentangle the source of the performance gain. Is it because gaze patterns are capturing nuanced sentiment processing, or are they largely just a proxy for reading difficulty, which itself correlates with sarcasm? An ablation study that separates readability features from gaze features (e.g., `Sn+Sr` vs. `Sn+Sr+Readability` vs. `Sn+Sr+Readability+Gaze`) would be necessary to make a stronger claim.
3.  **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art Models:** The authors explicitly decline to compare their feature-based system to contemporary deep learning models. While their reasoning is noted, this is a major omission. By the time of this work, neural networks were setting new state-of-the-art results in sentiment analysis. Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess the true impact of the performance gains. The key question is not just whether gaze features can improve a classic SVM model, but whether the information they provide is orthogonal and complementary to what a powerful neural model can learn from text alone.
4.  **Ambiguity in Feature Aggregation:** Gaze data from multiple participants is aggregated by averaging the computed features. This approach is simple but potentially suboptimal, as it discards valuable information about inter-annotator disagreement. High variance in gaze patterns across readers for a given text is likely a very strong signal of ambiguity, sarcasm, or controversy. Exploring this variance as a feature itself, or using other aggregation methods (e.g., min/max, distributions), could potentially yield further improvements and provide deeper insights.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given the primary weakness of requiring gaze data at inference time, can you elaborate on a more concrete research path to leverage these cognitive signals to build a model that relies solely on text at deployment? For instance, have you considered a multi-task learning setup where one task is SA and another is predicting gaze patterns from text, using the second task to regularize the text representations learned for the first?
2.  To address the confounding of gaze signals and readability, could you provide results for a baseline that includes your sentiment and sarcasm features plus the text readability features (`Sn+Sr+RED+LEN`)? This would help clarify how much of the gain from the `Gz` set is uniquely attributable to the cognitive processing patterns captured by eye-tracking.
3.  Could you please clarify the definition of the edge weights used for the complex graph-based features? For example, for feature `F1H`, is the weight of an edge between words `i` and `j` based on the fixation on `i`, on `j`, or a function of both? A more formal definition would improve the clarity and reproducibility of these novel features.
