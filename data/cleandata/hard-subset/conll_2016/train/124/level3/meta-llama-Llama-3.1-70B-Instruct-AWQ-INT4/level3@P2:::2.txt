**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of human annotators. The authors argue that traditional sentiment analysis methods often struggle to capture nuances in user-generated text, particularly at the lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic levels. To address this, they introduce a new feature set that combines traditional sentiment features with cognitive features extracted from eye-tracking data. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on two publicly available datasets, achieving significant improvements in sentiment polarity detection over state-of-the-art methods.

**Contributions**

1. **Introduction of cognitive features for sentiment analysis**: The authors propose a novel feature set that leverages eye-movement patterns to capture cognitive processes involved in sentiment analysis. This is a significant contribution, as it offers a new perspective on tackling the challenges of sentiment analysis.
2. **Development of a comprehensive feature set**: The authors provide a detailed description of their feature set, which includes traditional sentiment features, sarcasm and thwarting-related features, and cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. This comprehensive feature set is a valuable contribution to the field.
3. **Experimental evaluation on challenging datasets**: The authors evaluate their approach on two publicly available datasets that contain nuanced text with implicit sentiment, sarcasm, and thwarting. Their results demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach in handling complex constructs.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to sentiment analysis**: The authors' use of eye-tracking features as cognitive proxies for sentiment analysis is an innovative idea that offers a new perspective on tackling the challenges of sentiment analysis.
2. **Clear and helpful discussion and comparison of feature sets**: The authors provide a detailed description of their feature set and compare it to traditional sentiment analysis methods. This clarity is helpful in understanding the strengths and limitations of their approach.
3. **Feasibility of the approach is appropriately addressed**: The authors discuss the feasibility of their approach, including the availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential applications in e-commerce and online learning.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Datasets conflate domains**: The authors use datasets that conflate movie reviews and tweets, which may affect the training and interpretability of their results. It would be beneficial to separate domains to improve the robustness of their approach.
2. **Results look low relative to Pang & Lee state-of-the-art**: The authors' results, while significant, appear lower than the state-of-the-art results reported by Pang and Lee. It would be helpful to discuss this discrepancy and provide more context.
3. **Clarification needed on overlapping snippets**: The authors should clarify whether there are overlapping snippets between training and test sets in datasets 1 and 2, even if they are from the same sources.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more information on the preprocessing steps used to handle the eye-tracking data, such as filtering out noise or handling missing values?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of domain conflation in your datasets, and what steps can be taken to improve the robustness of your approach?
3. Can you provide more context on the discrepancy between your results and the state-of-the-art results reported by Pang and Lee?
