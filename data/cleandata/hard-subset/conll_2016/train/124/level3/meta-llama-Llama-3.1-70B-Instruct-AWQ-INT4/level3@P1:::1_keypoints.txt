- Proposes using eye-movement (gaze) cognitive features from human annotators for sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection, claimed as first such use in NLP.  
- Augments traditional sentiment features with gaze-derived cognitive features to handle lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic nuances.  
- Uses two public datasets containing sarcasm and irony to evaluate the approach.  
- Evaluates multiple classifiers and feature combinations to measure impact of cognitive features.  
- Reports a significant improvement in sentiment polarity detection with the enhanced feature set, especially on sarcastic/ironic text.  
- Conducts feature significance analysis indicating cognitive features help capture complex constructs like sarcasm and irony.  
- Paper is well-written, clear, and accessible.  
- The idea of incorporating cognitive/gaze features is innovative and could inspire further NLP work.  
- Evaluation is thorough across different methods and feature subsets.  
- Motivation for why cognitive features should aid polarity detection is weak and lacks theoretical justification.  
- The observed improvement is marginal, particularly versus the Sn+Sr feature combination.  
- The feasibility discussion (Section 7.2 example) is unconvincing and does not demonstrate clear practical benefits.  
- Reviewer asks for clearer explanation of which cognitive processes the gaze features capture and why they help polarity detection.  
- Reviewer requests plans to address marginal gains with additional experiments or analyses.  
- Reviewer asks for concrete use-cases or integration strategies showing where this approach would be especially useful in existing NLP systems.
