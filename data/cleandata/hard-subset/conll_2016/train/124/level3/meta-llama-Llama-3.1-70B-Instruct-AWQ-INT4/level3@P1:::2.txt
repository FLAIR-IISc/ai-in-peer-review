**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by leveraging cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of readers. The authors argue that traditional sentiment analysis methods often struggle with nuanced texts that exhibit subtleties at lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic levels. To address this, they introduce cognitive features that capture the eye-movement behavior of human annotators while they read and annotate short texts with sentiment labels. The authors demonstrate that their approach improves the performance of polarity detection by a maximum of 3.7% and 9.3% on two datasets, over systems that use only traditional features.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Introduction of cognitive features**: The authors propose a new type of feature that captures the eye-movement behavior of human annotators while they read and annotate short texts with sentiment labels. This is a novel contribution to the field of sentiment analysis.
2. **Improved performance on nuanced texts**: The authors demonstrate that their approach improves the performance of polarity detection on texts that exhibit subtleties at lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic levels.
3. **Feasibility of the approach**: The authors address the feasibility of their approach in Section 7, discussing the availability of mobile eye-trackers and the potential for non-intrusive data collection.

**Strengths**

1. **Innovative approach**: The authors' use of cognitive features is a fresh and innovative approach to sentiment analysis.
2. **Clear discussion and comparison of feature sets**: The authors provide a clear and helpful discussion and comparison of feature sets, making it easy to understand the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.
3. **Feasibility of the approach**: The authors' discussion of the feasibility of their approach is thorough and well-reasoned.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Low performance on some datasets**: The authors' results in Table 2 are lower than the state-of-the-art results reported by Pang and Lee. However, this may be due to the differences in datasets and experimental setup.
2. **Potential overlap between training and test sets**: The authors do not clarify whether there are overlapping snippets between training and test sets in datasets 1 and 2. This could potentially affect the validity of their results.
3. **Excessive use of bold**: The authors' use of bold text is distracting and could be reduced to improve the readability of the paper.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you clarify whether there are overlapping snippets between training and test sets in datasets 1 and 2?
2. How do you plan to address the potential issue of overlapping snippets between training and test sets?
3. Can you provide more details on how you plan to collect eye-tracking data from mobile devices in a non-intrusive manner?
4. How do you plan to evaluate the performance of your approach on different domains (e.g., movie reviews, tweets, etc.)?
