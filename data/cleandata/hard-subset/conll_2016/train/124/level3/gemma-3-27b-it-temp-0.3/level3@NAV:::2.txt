## Review of "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

This paper explores an interesting and novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The core idea – that readers’ eye movements reflect cognitive processing related to sentiment – is promising and potentially valuable for tackling the nuances of sentiment expression, particularly in short-form text. The authors clearly articulate the challenges in sentiment analysis and position their work as a means to address these limitations.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the demonstration of the *feasibility* of integrating eye-tracking data into a sentiment analysis pipeline and showing a measurable, albeit modest, performance improvement. The detailed discussion and comparison of the various feature sets (sentiment, sarcasm, cognitive, and reading difficulty) is a strong point, providing a clear understanding of the authors’ methodology. The inclusion of a held-out dataset to assess performance on complex constructs is also commendable.

A key strength is the appropriate addressing of feasibility concerns in Section 7, acknowledging the practical challenges of obtaining eye-tracking data and discussing potential avenues for wider adoption with the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers. The feature significance analysis (Table 3) provides further support for the value of the cognitive features.

However, the performance gains (Table 1 & 2) appear relatively low, particularly when compared to the Pang & Lee state-of-the-art results. While the authors rightly point out the differences in data and task complexity, the magnitude of the improvement warrants further investigation. A significant weakness is the conflation of domains (movie reviews and tweets) within the datasets. Separating these domains during training and evaluation would likely improve both model performance and interpretability, allowing for a clearer understanding of the generalizability of the findings. 

Furthermore, clarification is needed regarding potential overlap between the training and test sets. Even if sourced from different platforms, snippets could be similar, potentially inflating performance metrics. The excessive use of **bold** text throughout the paper is distracting and should be reduced.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more detail on the specific overlap analysis conducted (or planned) to ensure the independence of the training and test sets in Datasets 1 and 2?
2.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for averaging gaze data across participants when constructing the graph-based features? Are there plans to explore individual-level models or account for inter-subject variability?
3.  Given the relatively modest performance gains, what specific types of complex linguistic constructs (e.g., specific forms of sarcasm, irony, or implicit sentiment) benefit *most* from the inclusion of cognitive features?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference list is extensive but could benefit from more consistent formatting.
