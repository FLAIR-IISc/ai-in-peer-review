- Paper augments sentence-level sentiment classification with cognitive features from readers’ eye movements and evaluates on two gaze-annotated datasets.  
- Main empirical contribution: eye-tracking features yield small but statistically significant gains, especially on irony/sarcasm items.  
- Feature contribution: proposes basic gaze measures (fixations, regressions, saccade length) and a novel gaze-saliency graph with graph-based features.  
- Analysis contribution: chi-squared feature ranking and breakdowns showing several gaze features among top predictors and larger gains on complex items.  
- Writing and organization are clear, with careful dataset/feature/experiment descriptions and transparent ablation studies.  
- Cross-disciplinary idea is interesting and timely: cognitive signals may help with pragmatic phenomena like irony and thwarting.  
- Novel feature engineering: gaze-saliency graph and highest-degree weighted features are creative and potentially useful beyond this task.  
- Uses multiple datasets, different eye-trackers, and significance testing (McNemar) to support results.  
- Diagnostic analyses show gaze features provide complementary signal to textual features.  
- Practical feasibility is limited: approach assumes access to eye-tracking at test time with unclear deployment scenarios (readers required, latency, consent, device availability).  
- Gains are marginal over strong textual features (≈+0.6% and +1.5% F), and gaze-only features underperform text; largest gains come from sarcasm/thwarting features.  
- Motivation weak: paper does not convincingly link specific gaze phenomena causally to sentiment polarity rather than general processing difficulty.  
- Missing stronger textual baselines and comparisons to contemporary neural text-only models; some experiments use mismatched training data.  
- Hyperparameter tuning is limited (mostly defaults, no nested CV), raising concerns about robustness.  
- Validity/generalization threats: small datasets (~1k items), gaze recorded during annotation (task-induced patterns), averaging across annotators may hide variability, and held-out subsets are from same dataset.  
- Requests clarification on test-time requirements: how many readers/gaze data per sentence are needed and whether gains hold with single/noisy readers.  
- Requests deployment quantification: realistic throughput, consent rate, device coverage, latency, and cost compared to text-only models.  
- Requests stronger baselines: add text-only models (SVM, logistic TF-IDF, CNN/RNN) trained/evaluated on same folds to test incremental value of gaze.  
- Asks for controls for task-induced gaze patterns and replication with free reading or unrelated tasks.  
- Asks robustness checks: sensitivity to PCA dimension, feature selection, hyperparameters, and cross-domain evaluation (train/test across datasets).  
- Requests per-class metrics and confusion matrices to see which classes benefit from gaze features.  
- Suggests scoping the “first of its kind” claim and citing prior related eye-tracking work.  
- Recommends repositioning as an analysis paper if deployment is out of scope and releasing code/feature scripts for reproducibility.  
- Overall: intriguing and well-presented work but marginal gains, practicality issues, and limited baselines weaken the contribution.
