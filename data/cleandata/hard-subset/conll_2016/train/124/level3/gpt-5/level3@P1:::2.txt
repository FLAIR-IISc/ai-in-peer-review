Summary
This paper augments standard sentiment analysis (SA) features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking. Using two eye-tracking datasets containing short texts with substantial semantic/pragmatic complexity (sarcasm, irony, thwarting), the authors evaluate several feature groupings (sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting features, gaze features, and combinations) with SVM, Naive Bayes, and a small neural network. The combined feature set yields modest but statistically significant F-score gains over text-only features, with larger relative gains on texts containing complex constructs. The paper also provides a feature-importance analysis and discusses feasibility of obtaining gaze signals at scale.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that adding eye-tracking-based cognitive features to strong text-only feature sets improves SA performance, especially for irony/sarcasm cases. The incremental gain of gaze over sentiment+sarcasm features is small in absolute terms (0.6–1.5 F1), but consistent and statistically significant; on a held-out subset with complex constructs the relative gain is larger (≈6.1%).
2) A clear feature ablation comparing traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting features, and gaze features, plus a chi-square ranking that highlights which gaze features are most informative.
3) A pragmatic discussion of feasibility and deployment scenarios for obtaining gaze data, acknowledging current limitations.

Strengths
- Novel cross-disciplinary angle with concrete benefits: Leveraging eye movements as cognitive proxies is an original and well-motivated idea for tackling subtle sentiment phenomena (sarcasm, thwarting) that are difficult for purely textual models. The held-out analysis showing larger gains for complex texts directly targets the paper’s hypothesis.
- Solid and transparent feature study: The paper is unusually clear in defining feature groups (Sn, Sr, Gz), reporting results for multiple classifiers, and performing statistical significance testing (McNemar) and feature-importance analysis. This structure makes the empirical claims easy to evaluate.
- Sensible baselines and sanity checks: The preliminary check showing that off-the-shelf SA systems perform relatively poorly on these datasets supports the claim that the data are challenging and appropriate for testing the proposed approach.
- Feasibility discussion: Section 7 proactively addresses concerns about practicality, surveying hardware trends, potential application contexts, and consent issues. This helps position the work beyond proof-of-concept.
- Methodological care with dimensionality: The use of PCA to reduce sparse lexical features acknowledges overfitting risks in small data settings.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and scalability remain the main bottleneck: While Section 7 is thoughtful, the central limitation is that the proposed model requires gaze at inference time. The paper would benefit from a “student–teacher” or distillation variant that transfers gaze-informed signals into a text-only model to enable deployment without eye-tracking.
- Modest absolute gains from gaze over strong text features: On both datasets, the incremental improvement from adding gaze to Sn+Sr is 0.6–1.5 F1. Although significant, this is small and should be contextualized more carefully. Gaze-only features also underperform substantially, indicating they are complementary but weak on their own.
- Domain conflation in datasets: Mixing movie reviews and tweets blurs domain effects. This complicates interpretation of the absolute F-scores (which are low versus Pang & Lee movie-review benchmarks, but more in line with Twitter). Reporting per-domain results or training/testing without cross-domain mixing would improve interpretability and fairness of comparisons.
- Clarity on data overlap and splits: It is unclear whether there is any overlap (verbatim or near-duplicate snippets) between the training corpora used to construct some features (e.g., LP from Joshi et al., bootstrapped phrase lists) and D1/D2; and whether folds avoid leakage tied to annotators or snippets. A leave-one-reader-out and leave-one-item-out evaluation would strengthen claims.
- Novelty claim may be overstated: Prior work has used gaze in related sentiment/subjectivity tasks (e.g., Mishra et al., 2014). The paper should clarify how the present contribution differs (e.g., first to operationalize gaze features directly in SA classification and to show gains on irony/sarcasm) and temper “first-of-its-kind” language.
- Heterogeneous hardware and normalization: D1 and D2 use different eye-trackers (500Hz vs. 120Hz). Although features are normalized to some extent (e.g., per-word ratios), the paper should document normalization/calibration across devices and participants, and analyze whether improvements persist after stronger normalization.

Questions to authors
- Data leakage: Are any snippets or near-duplicates shared between the corpora used to learn auxiliary components (e.g., LP, bootstrapped incongruity patterns) and the evaluation datasets (D1/D2)? Please detail measures taken to prevent overlap.
- Splitting protocol: Were cross-validation folds constructed to avoid participant leakage (e.g., leave-one-reader-out) or to ensure that the same sentence cannot appear in both training and test via different participants’ gaze? How would results change under leave-one-reader-out?
- Domain effects: Can you report per-domain (tweets vs. reviews vs. quotes) results, and train/test without cross-domain mixing? Do gaze gains vary by domain?
- Device normalization: How were gaze features normalized across different eye-trackers and sampling rates? Any per-device ablation?
- Deployment without gaze: Have you tried training a text-only model with gaze-informed auxiliary targets (e.g., multi-task learning or distillation) so that inference does not require eye-tracking?
- Feature extraction details: For the gaze-saliency graphs, please clarify edge-weight definitions, handling of multiple fixations/saccades between word pairs, and whether punctuation and stopwords are nodes. Will you release feature extraction code?

Additional comments
- The bold formatting in tables/feature lists is overused and distracts from content; consider lighter emphasis.
- Given space, adding confidence intervals across CV folds and per-class (pos/neg) metrics would help assess robustness.
- Minor editorial: fix typos (“present an our features”) and ensure consistent notation for feature labels.
