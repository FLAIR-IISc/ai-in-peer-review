- Eye-tracking features modestly improve sentiment analysis, with clearer benefits on irony/sarcasm cases.
- Several gaze-derived features rank highly by chi-squared, indicating complementary signal to textual cues.
- Ablation shows sarcasm features contribute most, with gaze providing a smaller incremental gain.
- Novel cross-disciplinary idea: cognitive signals to complement text for SA and figurative language tasks.
- Clear writing and organization with transparent feature definitions, ablations, and significance testing.
- Gaze features yield notable benefit on hard irony cases (≈+4.2 F1 on held-out subset).
- Thorough catalog and analysis of basic and graph-based gaze features.
- Weak motivation for why gaze should encode sentiment polarity beyond reading difficulty and confounds.
- Marginal average gains (0.6–1.5 F1) may not justify eye-tracking cost and complexity.
- Practicality concerns: deployment scenarios are speculative and lack discussion of privacy, latency, and cost-benefit.
- Missing comparisons to modern neural baselines (e.g., pretrained transformers), weakening the claims.
- Potential task-induced bias: gaze recorded during labeling may reflect annotator strategy, risking leakage/circularity.
- Can authors provide a mechanistic link between specific gaze patterns and polarity and disentangle difficulty vs. sentiment?
- Were the same annotators’ labels and gaze used for both feature extraction and gold labels; any subject-level leakage across folds?
- Could gaze be used only at training time (e.g., distillation or surrogate predictors) to avoid requiring eye-tracking at inference?
- How do results compare against strong neural baselines on the same splits?
