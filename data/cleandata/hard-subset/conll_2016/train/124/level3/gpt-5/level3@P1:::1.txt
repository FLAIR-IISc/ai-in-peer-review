Summary
The paper augments traditional sentiment analysis (SA) features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking while readers annotate sentiment. It proposes a set of basic gaze features (fixation/saccade statistics, regressions, skips) and more complex graph-based “gaze-saliency” features. Evaluations on two eye-tracking datasets show that adding sarcasm/thwarting features to standard sentiment features substantially improves performance, and adding gaze features yields additional, statistically significant but modest gains. A held-out subset with many ironic/sarcastic examples shows larger gains from gaze features, and feature-importance analyses indicate several gaze features rank highly.

Main contributions (as I see them, strongest first)
1) Empirical evidence that gaze-derived features can improve SA, especially on texts with complex phenomena (irony/sarcasm), with significant though small additive gains over strong textual features.
2) A concrete, reusable inventory of gaze features for SA, including a novel saliency-graph representation that captures complex eye-movement patterns.
3) Feature significance and targeted held-out analysis suggesting which gaze features matter and where they help (irony/sarcasm vs. simpler cases).

Strengths
- Clear problem framing and presentation: The paper is well written, easy to follow, and situates the work against both SA and psycholinguistic literature. Implementation details (classifiers, features, datasets, significance tests) are sufficiently reported to assess the results.
- Interesting cross-disciplinary idea: Leveraging cognitive signals to capture lexical, syntactic, and pragmatic difficulty that textual features miss is a compelling angle that can inspire work across NLP tasks involving implicit sentiment, irony, and pragmatics.
- Evidence of benefit on complex constructs: The held-out set shows a larger relative improvement for irony/sarcasm (6.1%) than for non-irony (2.1%), aligning with the hypothesis that gaze may help where textual cues are subtle or contradictory. This is supported by statistical tests on that subset.
- Feature importance analysis: Chi-squared ranking shows many gaze features among the top 20 predictors on both datasets, suggesting cognitive signals are informative rather than incidental. The specific gaze features (e.g., regressions, saliency-graph degrees) are plausibly connected to processing difficulty and surprisal.

Weaknesses
- Weak motivation beyond reading difficulty: The paper does not convincingly argue why eye movements should specifically help polarity detection (vs. just general reading complexity). Many gaze features (fixation duration, regressions, skips) correlate with readability and processing load; without stronger theory or controls, it is unclear whether gaze is capturing sentiment-relevant cues or just general difficulty. The modest additive gains (0.6 and 1.5 F1 over Sn+Sr) reinforce the need for a clearer causal story.
- Marginal practical impact of gaze features: The majority of the improvement comes from adding sarcasm/thwarting textual features (3.1% and 7.8%); gaze adds relatively small increments. The paper highlights statistical significance but does not discuss effect sizes or practical utility. An application would need consistent, material gains to justify the cost and complexity of collecting gaze at inference time.
- Feasibility arguments are unconvincing: Section 7 speculates about mobile eye tracking and e-commerce use cases, but provides no demonstration that noisy, low-cost, real-world gaze is sufficient for classification, nor a cost-benefit or privacy/consent analysis. The proposal to “watch how readers read reviews” to infer sentiment raises clear ethical and UX concerns, and the paper does not address variance across devices, calibration, or user behavior outside a controlled lab.
- Limited experimental validation and ablations: 
  - No analysis of the unique contribution of complex saliency-graph features over basic gaze features.
  - No control experiments disentangling gaze effects from readability (e.g., partialing out RED/LEN, or comparing to a stronger readability baseline).
  - No cross-participant generalization tests (e.g., leave-one-reader-out), which would be important for real-world use.
  - No cross-device/domain generalization (train on Eyelink, test on Tobii, or train on one dataset and test on the other).
  - Significance is shown for one dataset and the held-out subset; it is unclear if gains on the second main dataset are robust across folds.
- Novelty is incremental: Prior work has used eye-tracking for SA-related tasks (annotation complexity, subjectivity extraction). Applying gaze to SA classification is a reasonable step forward, but the “first-of-its-kind” claim should be tempered given the small gains and the reliance on known gaze correlates of processing difficulty.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide stronger theoretical or empirical motivation that connects specific gaze patterns to sentiment polarity (not just difficulty), e.g., by controlling for readability and length (partial correlations, matched-pairs design)?
- Which gaze features actually drive the gains? Please include an ablation separating basic vs. saliency-graph features, and report the incremental benefit of each block over Sn and over Sn+Sr.
- How robust are the gains across readers and devices? Please report leave-one-reader-out results and cross-device/cross-dataset generalization (train on D1, test on D2, and vice versa).
- Are the gains on Dataset 2 statistically significant via McNemar (or another paired test)? Please report p-values across folds for all main comparisons.
- What is the cost-benefit at inference time? Can you show performance as a function of the number of readers (e.g., 1 vs. 5–7) and with lower sampling rates/noisy gaze to simulate mobile conditions?
- Could similar improvements be achieved using purely textual proxies for processing difficulty (e.g., surprisal from a language model, syntactic complexity, discourse features)? A comparison would help isolate the unique value of gaze.

Overall, the paper presents a thought-provoking direction with careful feature engineering and some supporting evidence, but the core motivation and the practical utility of gaze at inference time require stronger justification and more thorough experimentation.
