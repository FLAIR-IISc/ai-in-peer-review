Summary
The paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with eye-tracking–based cognitive features to better handle lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic phenomena (notably irony/sarcasm). Using two eye-tracking datasets with sentence-level polarity labels, the authors design basic and graph-based gaze features, combine them with standard sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features, and evaluate multiple classifiers via 10-fold cross-validation. They report absolute F-score gains up to 3.7% (D1) and 9.3% (D2) over text-only features, and demonstrate larger gains on an irony-heavy held-out set. Section 7 discusses feasibility of obtaining gaze at scale via mobile eye-tracking.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Demonstration that eye-tracking features provide statistically significant, additive gains for sentence-level sentiment classification, especially on complex constructs (e.g., +6.1 F1 for irony cases on held-out data).
2) A clear and thorough comparison and ablation of feature families (sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, gaze), including feature-importance analysis that highlights gaze features among the top-ranked signals.
3) Introduction of gaze-saliency graph features that encode fixations/saccades structurally and complement simpler aggregated gaze statistics.

Strengths
- Novel and interesting idea: leveraging eye-tracking as a cognitive proxy. The paper is among the first to translate psycholinguistic evidence (fixations, regressions, scanpaths) into operational features for sentiment. The hypothesis is well-motivated and supported by measurable gains.
- Careful feature engineering and analysis. The paper clearly separates feature families, shows incremental gains (Sn → Sn+Sr → Sn+Sr+Gz), and provides chi-squared rankings where many gaze features rank highly (10/20 for D1; 7/20 for D2). This helps interpretability and future reuse.
- Empirical evidence on challenging data. The datasets explicitly contain irony/sarcasm/thwarting, which degrade standard text-only performance (Table 1). The held-out experiment further shows gaze helps most where textual cues are weakest (irony +6.1% vs non-irony +2.1%).
- Feasibility addressed. Section 7 gives a realistic discussion of deployment scenarios and emerging mobile eye-tracking, acknowledging current limitations while outlining paths to practicality.
- Methodological transparency for the most part. Classifiers, features, and training data sources are described; statistical tests (McNemar) are used to assess significance.

Weaknesses
- Domain conflation and limited generalization analysis. Both datasets mix movie reviews and tweets, and the external baselines train on mixed Pang & Lee + Sentiment140. This blurs conclusions: improvements may vary by domain. Per-domain results or domain-controlled splits are needed to understand when gaze helps most and to improve interpretability.
- Comparisons to prior state of the art are not fully contextualized. Table 2 F-scores (∼72–73 on D1; ∼71–72 on D2 for SVM) look low relative to classic movie-review benchmarks (e.g., Pang & Lee on longer reviews), but are more in line with Twitter sentence-level results. Please situate results by dataset type/length and label balance, and, if possible, include stronger text-only baselines (e.g., recent neural models circa 2015–2016) to calibrate the incremental value of gaze.
- Potential data leakage/overlap concerns. It is unclear whether snippets overlap between D1 and D2 (both aggregate from reviews and normalized tweets), or between these datasets and the 10,662-item external training corpus used for the baselines in Table 1. Overlap would inflate performance or confound conclusions. Similarly, PCA for unigram features must be fit on training folds only; this is not stated.
- Feasibility remains speculative at inference time. The current evaluation requires gaze features at test time; Section 7 proposes plausible applications, but it would strengthen the work to explore learning with eye-tracking as privileged information (train-time only) and test-time text-only predictions, or to quantify the trade-off: labeling cost (gaze collection) vs F1 gains.
- Hardware and annotation confounds. D1 uses a 500Hz Eyelink; D2 uses a 120Hz Tobii. Gains from gaze differ across datasets (0.6 vs 1.5 F1). Please analyze how sampling rate and annotation protocol affect gaze features, and whether results hold when normalizing gaze features across hardware.

Questions to Authors
- Are there any overlapping or near-duplicate snippets between Dataset 1 and Dataset 2, or between these datasets and the Pang & Lee/Sentiment140 corpora used to train the baselines in Table 1? How did you deduplicate and ensure no leakage?
- For 10-fold cross-validation, was PCA for unigram features fitted only on the training folds and then applied to the test fold? Same question for any feature normalization.
- How are folds constructed relative to readers? Since gaze features are averaged across participants per sentence, this likely avoids per-reader leakage, but please confirm.
- Can you report per-domain (movie vs tweet) results and gains for Sn, Sn+Sr, and Sn+Sr+Gz? Do gaze features help more in one domain?
- Please clarify averaging for F-score (macro vs micro, class balance) and provide class-wise F1 to assess performance on the minority class.
- Can you include an ablation over gaze feature groups (basic vs graph-based) to quantify their separate contributions?
- Have you tried cross-dataset evaluation (train on D1, test on D2 and vice versa), given that both datasets include gaze, to assess robustness across hardware/domains?

Additional Comments
- The discussion of feature sets is clear and helpful; keep it. Section 7 appropriately addresses feasibility; consider adding a brief cost-benefit analysis or a learning-with-privileged-information variant.
- Table 2 results are low compared to classic movie-review benchmarks but reasonable for Twitter sentence-level tasks; please contextualize accordingly.
- Minor style: boldface is overused (e.g., in feature-importance tables); consider reducing to improve readability.
- Releasing code and feature-extraction scripts (including saliency-graph construction) would aid reproducibility.
