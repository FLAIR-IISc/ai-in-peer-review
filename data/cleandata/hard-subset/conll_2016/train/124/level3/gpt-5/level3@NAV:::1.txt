Summary and contributions
This paper augments standard textual features for sentiment analysis (SA) and sarcasm detection with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movements. On two public datasets, adding gaze features yields small but statistically significant F1 gains over strong feature-based baselines, and larger gains on an irony-heavy held-out subset. Contributions (as I see them):
1) An empirical study showing that eye-tracking features can modestly improve SA, with clearer benefits on irony/sarcasm cases.
2) A careful feature analysis indicating several gaze-derived features rank highly by chi-squared, suggesting complementary signal to textual cues.
3) An ablation that quantifies where improvements come from (sarcasm features contribute most; gaze adds a smaller incremental gain).

Strengths
- Cross-disciplinary idea with potential: Using cognitive signals to complement text features is novel in SA and could catalyze similar investigations for other NLP tasks (e.g., figurative language).
- Clear writing and organization: The paper is easy to follow, with transparent feature definitions, ablations, and significance testing (McNemar).
- Evidence of benefit on hard cases: On the held-out set, gaze features help more for irony (+4.2 F1 over Sn+Sr), aligning with the hypothesis that cognitive signals capture processing difficulty related to pragmatic nuances.
- Thorough feature catalog and analysis: Both basic and graph-based gaze features are well-motivated and inspected via chi-squared ranking, offering insights for future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited motivation for polarity detection: Beyond general “reading difficulty,” the paper does not convincingly articulate why eye-movement patterns should encode sentiment polarity per se, as opposed to confounds like length and readability (partially controlled but not fully disentangled).
- Marginal average gains: Relative to Sn+Sr, adding gaze yields only 0.6–1.5 F1 on the main datasets; the cost and complexity of eye-tracking may not be justified.
- Practicality/feasibility is unconvincing: The Section 7 scenarios are speculative and do not demonstrate net utility in real deployments, nor address privacy, latency, or cost-benefit trade-offs.
- Missing comparisons to modern baselines: Excluding competitive neural SA models limits the strength of claims; current SOTA may already surpass reported gains without gaze.
- Potential task-induced bias: Gaze was recorded during sentiment labeling, which may reflect annotator strategy rather than general reading behavior; the risk of leakage or circularity is not analyzed.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a mechanistic account linking specific gaze patterns to polarity (beyond difficulty), and controls that disentangle difficulty vs. sentiment?
- Were the same annotators’ labels and gaze used for both feature extraction and gold labels? Any subject-level leakage across folds?
- Could gaze be used only at training time (e.g., distillation or surrogate predictors) to avoid requiring eye-tracking at inference?
- How do results compare against strong neural baselines (e.g., pretrained transformers) on the same splits?
