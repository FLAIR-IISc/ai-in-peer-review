- Paper augments sentiment features with eye-tracking–based cognitive features (basic + graph-based) and evaluates on two sentence-level polarity datasets with sarcasm/irony.  
- Reports absolute F-score gains up to 3.7% (D1) and 9.3% (D2) over text-only; larger gains on an irony-heavy held-out set (+6.1 F1 for irony).  

Main contributions:
- Shows eye-tracking features give statistically significant, additive gains for sentence-level sentiment, especially on complex constructs.  
- Provides thorough feature-family comparison and ablation with feature-importance analysis highlighting gaze signals.  
- Introduces gaze-saliency graph features encoding fixations/saccades structurally to complement aggregated gaze stats.  

Strengths:
- Novel translation of psycholinguistic eye-tracking evidence into operational sentiment features with measurable gains.  
- Careful feature engineering, clear incremental evaluation (Sn → Sn+Sr → Sn+Sr+Gz) and chi-squared rankings for interpretability.  
- Empirical focus on irony/sarcasm data where text-only models struggle; held-out test shows gaze helps most on irony.  
- Feasibility discussion about mobile eye-tracking and deployment scenarios.  
- Reasonable methodological transparency and use of statistical tests (McNemar) for significance.  

Weaknesses:
- Datasets mix movie reviews and tweets, risking domain conflation; per-domain results or controlled splits are needed for generalization claims.  
- Comparisons to prior state of the art are not fully contextualized; stronger modern text-only baselines (e.g., recent neural models) would better calibrate gaze’s incremental value.  
- Possible data leakage/overlap between datasets and external corpora and unclear PCA/normalization protocol across folds.  
- Feasibility at inference is speculative since current evaluation requires gaze at test time; should explore training-with-privileged-information or quantify cost vs F1 trade-offs.  
- Hardware/annotation confounds (500Hz Eyelink vs 120Hz Tobii) may affect gaze gains; need analysis and normalization across devices.  

Questions for authors:
- Any overlapping or near-duplicate snippets across D1, D2, and external corpora? How was deduplication handled?  
- Was PCA for unigram features and any normalization fitted only on training folds per CV split?  
- How were folds constructed relative to readers, given gaze averaging across participants?  
- Can you report per-domain (movie vs tweet) results and gains for Sn, Sn+Sr, and Sn+Sr+Gz?  
- What averaging was used for F-score (macro vs micro) and can you provide class-wise F1?  
- Please provide an ablation separating basic gaze vs graph-based gaze contributions.  
- Have you tried cross-dataset evaluation (train on D1/test on D2 and vice versa) to assess robustness?
