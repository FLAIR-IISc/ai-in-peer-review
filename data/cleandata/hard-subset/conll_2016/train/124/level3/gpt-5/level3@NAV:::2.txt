Summary and Contributions
This paper augments text-based sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking, including both basic fixation/saccade statistics and graph-based “gaze-saliency” features. The authors evaluate on two eye-tracking datasets containing nuanced phenomena (sarcasm, irony, thwarting), conduct detailed ablations, and discuss feasibility of deployment via mobile eye-tracking.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) Empirical demonstration that gaze-derived features provide complementary signal for sentiment classification, with clearer gains on texts involving complex pragmatic phenomena.
2) A careful, interpretable feature-set comparison (Sn, Sr, Gz) and significance analysis that clarifies when and how cognitive features help.
3) A feasibility analysis (Section 7) that, while preliminary, thoughtfully scopes how eye-tracking could be collected in practical applications.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty with practical impact: Using eye-tracking as a cognitive proxy for sentiment is an interesting, well-motivated idea that targets well-known limitations of purely textual features on sarcasm/irony.
- Thorough ablations and interpretability: The Sn vs Sn+Sr vs Sn+Sr+Gz comparisons are clear and helpful; feature-importance analysis and the held-out “complex constructs” subset convincingly show larger relative gains where expected (e.g., +6.1% relative F on irony).
- Feasibility discussion: Section 7 appropriately addresses practicality, user consent, and potential mobile sensing routes, which is essential for a method relying on gaze data.
- Methodological clarity: The paper clearly defines basic and graph-based gaze features and compares multiple classifiers; the presentation of categories (sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, gaze, readability) aids reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Domain conflation: Datasets mix movie reviews and tweets; separating domains for training/evaluation would likely improve both performance and interpretability, and help quantify domain sensitivity of gaze features.
- Modest absolute gains from gaze overall: In Table 2, Gz adds 0.6–1.5 F1 over Sn+Sr; while meaningful, the cost-benefit should be discussed more explicitly.
- Baseline context: Table 2 F1 appears low relative to Pang & Lee-style movie-review SOTA but is more reasonable for Twitter; clearer positioning against domain-appropriate baselines (including modern neural models) would strengthen claims.
- Potential data overlap: It is unclear whether any snippets overlap between training and test sources (for baselines) or across D1/D2 folds; leakage would confound conclusions.
- Minor presentation: Excessive use of bold is distracting.

Questions to Authors
- Do datasets 1 and 2 contain any snippets overlapping with the Pang & Lee or Sentiment140 sources used for baseline training, or across train/test folds? Please clarify deduplication.
- Can you report results with domain-separated training/testing (movie-only vs tweet-only) and cross-domain transfer?
- How robust are gaze features to lower sampling rates and mobile noise? Can you provide per-device or per-reader variability analyses?
