- Paper augments sentiment polarity classification with cognitive features from readers’ eye movements and a graph-based gaze-saliency representation.  
- Experiments use two existing eye-tracking datasets and evaluate SVM, Naive Bayes, and a simple neural network.  
- Best performance when combining sentiment (Sn), sarcasm/thwarting (Sr), and gaze (Gz) features.  
- Held-out analysis suggests gaze features help more on irony/sarcasm cases than on simpler ones.  
- Authors discuss feasibility of mobile eye-tracking for deployment.  
- Empirical contribution: small but consistent gains from adding eye-movement features over text-only models on two datasets.  
- Feature contribution: a reusable set of cognitive/gaze features including a gaze-saliency graph and degree-based aggregations.  
- Strength: paper is clearly written with well-described problem framing, feature definitions, and experimental protocol.  
- Strength: cross-disciplinary idea that could inspire follow-up work linking psycholinguistics and sentiment analysis.  
- Strength: systematic ablations, feature-importance analysis, and an irony-focused held-out subset showing larger relative gains.  
- Strength: several gaze features rank highly, indicating non-trivial correlation between reading behavior and nuanced polarity expressions.  
- Weakness: weak theoretical motivation linking gaze/processing difficulty specifically to polarity valence rather than general complexity.  
- Weakness: practical gains are marginal (e.g., +0.6 to +1.5 F1), Gz alone performs poorly, and most lift may come from Sr features.  
- Weakness: statistical-significance claims (e.g., 0.5% gain at p=0.0001) need exact discordant counts, effect sizes, and multiple-comparison corrections.  
- Weakness: deployment discussion is speculative and omits costs, privacy/consent, device noise, reader variability, and scalability concerns.  
- Weakness: validity threats from recording gaze during annotation (task-specific signals), aggregation masking reader variability, small/different-quality datasets, and counterintuitive results favoring lower-quality tracker data.  
- Weakness: novelty is overstated given prior eye-tracking work in sentiment/subjectivity; should be positioned as incremental with a new feature set.  
- Rebuttal request: clarify novelty relative to Mishra et al. (2014) and similar prior work.  
- Rebuttal request: provide stricter generalization tests (leave-one-reader-out, leave-one-domain-out) to assess robustness of gains.  
- Rebuttal request: test stronger controls for reading difficulty (LM-based surprisal, syntactic depth, richer readability proxies) to see if Gz increment persists.  
- Rebuttal request: provide per-phenomenon breakdowns (sarcasm, irony, thwarting, implicit sentiment) and confusion matrices to support claims about complex constructs.  
- Rebuttal request: clarify deployment path, required number of readers per item for stable gains, and consider training models to predict gaze from text to avoid needing gaze at inference.  
- Rebuttal request: report exact McNemar discordant counts and apply multiple-comparison corrections across settings.  
- Additional comment: compare against stronger text-only baselines (tuned linear models, contemporary neural baselines).  
- Additional comment: releasing code and feature extraction scripts would strengthen reproducibility and impact.
