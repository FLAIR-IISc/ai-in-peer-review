Summary and claimed contributions
The paper augments traditional sentiment analysis (SA) features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking during human sentiment annotation. On two public datasets rich in sarcasm/irony and other higher-level phenomena, the authors show statistically significant F-score gains from adding gaze-based features, with larger relative improvements on text containing complex constructs. They provide feature ablations and a feature-importance analysis, and discuss feasibility of obtaining gaze data at scale.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that cognitive (eye-tracking) features provide complementary signal for SA, especially on irony/sarcasm-laden text. The incremental gains over strong text-only feature sets are small-to-moderate overall (0.6–1.5 F1 on the full sets) but substantially larger on a held-out subset of complex cases (6.1 F1).
2) A clear and systematic comparison of feature families (sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, gaze), with ablations and significance testing, plus chi-squared feature ranking showing many gaze features among the top-20.
3) An explicit feasibility discussion around acquiring gaze data via consumer-grade devices, along with cross-device evidence (datasets collected with high- and low-quality trackers), which strengthens the case for practical relevance.

Strengths
- Novel cognitive signal for SA with targeted benefit on hard cases. The paper is, to my knowledge, the first to demonstrate that gaze features improve polarity classification for texts rich in sarcasm/irony, with statistically significant improvements on both full datasets and a sarcasm-heavy held-out subset (Table 4: +6.1 F1 on complex constructs).
- Thorough ablation and significance analysis. The progressive gains from Sn → Sn+Sr → Sn+Sr+Gz are clearly documented (Table 2), and the McNemar tests provide statistical support for the best-vs-second-best comparison. The chi-squared feature rankings (Table 3) further corroborate the utility of specific gaze features.
- Cross-hardware generality. The two datasets use different trackers (500Hz Eyelink vs 120Hz Tobii), yet gaze features contribute on both, suggesting robustness to hardware quality.
- Feasibility addressed. Section 7 directly engages with practicality (mobile eye trackers, consent scenarios, application settings), which is important for a method requiring auxiliary signals at inference time.
- Clear exposition of the feature space. The categorization (sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, gaze, readability) and detailed definitions (including gaze-saliency graphs) make the approach replicable in principle and help interpret the gains.

Weaknesses
- Domain conflation and limited analysis by domain. Training/testing mixes movie reviews and tweets. Domain effects are known to be large in SA; without explicit domain separation or per-domain reporting, it is hard to interpret where gaze features help most. A per-domain breakdown and/or domain-controlled experiments would strengthen claims.
- Comparisons to stronger text-only baselines are limited. Reported text-only results in Table 2 look low relative to classic movie-review benchmarks (e.g., Pang & Lee datasets) and do not include modern baselines (e.g., strong linear models with more comprehensive n-grams/embeddings or neural models). Since the authors focus on feature engineering, a fairer and stronger text-only baseline on these datasets would clarify the true incremental value of gaze.
- Risk of leakage/overlap and unclear data hygiene. It is not fully clear whether any snippets (or near-duplicates) overlap between the training sources used for the “existing SA systems” (Pang & Lee 2004; Sentiment140) and the two target datasets, given similar domains. Any overlap could bias conclusions about difficulty and relative gains.
- Small absolute gains on full datasets and Gz-alone underperformance. Most of the full-dataset improvement comes from sarcasm/thwarting features (Sr); the gaze features add 0.6–1.5 F1 on top of Sn+Sr. Gz alone is weak (often below Sn), suggesting gaze is complementary but not strong independently. The paper would benefit from a deeper error analysis isolating which phenomena the gaze features uniquely capture beyond Sr.
- Generalization and deployment assumptions. The method assumes access to multiple readers’ gaze traces (averaged across annotators). It is unclear how performance changes with one reader at inference time, or with noisy consumer-grade signals in-the-wild. Without such analysis, the feasibility section remains somewhat speculative.

Questions to authors
- Data overlap: Can you confirm there are no overlapping or near-duplicate snippets between your “existing SA systems” training data (Pang & Lee; Sentiment140) and D1/D2? How did you check for overlap?
- Domain analysis: Can you report results separated by domain (tweets vs reviews) and by presence/absence of sarcasm/irony for each domain?
- PCA and preprocessing hygiene: Were PCA and any normalization/feature selection performed strictly within each training fold to avoid test leakage? Please clarify the pipeline per fold.
- Annotator aggregation: How many readers per instance contribute gaze? What is the performance as a function of the number of readers (1, 2, …, N)? Do the gains persist with a single gaze trace?
- Hardware sensitivity: Can you provide an analysis of performance when restricting to the low-quality Tobii data vs high-quality Eyelink data, holding other factors constant?
- Stronger baselines: Can you compare against a stronger text-only baseline (e.g., linear models with richer n-grams/character n-grams/embeddings, or a lightweight CNN) to validate the incremental value of gaze?
- Efficiency: What is the runtime/feature extraction overhead per instance, and how would this scale in a practical setting?

Additional comments
- The paper is clearly written overall, but the heavy use of bold font in tables/sections is visually distracting; consider more restrained emphasis.
- If possible, release code for feature extraction (especially gaze-saliency graph construction) to facilitate reproducibility.
- A per-phenomenon error analysis (negation, irony, thwarting) showing cases where gaze helps vs hurts would improve interpretability.
