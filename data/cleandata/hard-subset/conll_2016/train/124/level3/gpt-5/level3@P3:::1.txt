Summary
This paper augments standard text features for sentiment polarity classification with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movement patterns. Using two existing eye-tracking datasets, the authors engineer a set of “basic” gaze features and propose a graph-based “gaze-saliency” representation, and evaluate several feature combinations with SVM, Naive Bayes, and a simple neural network. The best results are obtained when combining sentiment features (Sn), sarcasm/thwarting features (Sr), and gaze features (Gz). The paper also reports a held-out analysis suggesting gaze features help more on irony/sarcasm cases than on simpler ones, and discusses feasibility via mobile eye tracking.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical finding: Adding eye-movement features yields small but consistent gains over text-only models for sentiment polarity classification on two datasets, with somewhat larger gains reported on a held-out subset rich in irony/sarcasm.
- Feature design: A collection of cognitive features (including a gaze-saliency graph and degree-based aggregations) that can be reused in future work investigating cognitive signals for NLP.

Strengths
- Clarity and organization: The paper is well written and easy to follow. The problem framing, feature definitions, and experimental protocol are clearly described, making the work reproducible in spirit.
- Interesting idea with cross-disciplinary appeal: Bringing psycholinguistic evidence into SA is thought-provoking and could spur follow-up work, e.g., using predicted gaze/surprisal or multi-task models tying text saliency to downstream labels.
- Systematic ablations and analysis: The authors compare Sn, Sn+Sr, Gz alone, and combined settings across multiple classifiers. They also include a feature-importance analysis and a held-out subset focused on complex phenomena (irony), where the relative improvements are larger (e.g., +4.2 F1 absolute from Sn+Sr to Sn+Sr+Gz in the irony subset).
- Evidence that gaze captures some useful signal: Several gaze features appear among top-ranked features, suggesting non-trivial correlation between reading behavior and aspects of difficult, nuanced polarity expressions.

Weaknesses
- Weak motivation for polarity prediction: Beyond the reasonable hypothesis that gaze reflects processing difficulty (lexical/syntactic/pragmatic), the paper does not articulate a convincing mechanism for how such difficulty translates specifically into positive vs negative polarity. Many of the gaze features resemble reading-ease proxies; the work would benefit from tighter theoretical or empirical grounding that links specific gaze patterns to polarity valence rather than to presence of complexity or figurative language.
- Marginal practical gains from gaze over strong text features: The incremental improvement from Sn+Sr to Sn+Sr+Gz is small (SVM: +0.6 F1 on D1; +1.5 on D2). Given the cost and complexity of collecting gaze, these gains may not justify the added modality. Moreover, Gz alone performs poorly, and most of the lift seems attributable to Sr features. The statistical significance claim for a 0.5% absolute gain (p=0.0001) would be more convincing with exact discordant counts and effect sizes; multiple-comparison corrections are not discussed.
- Questionable feasibility and limited applicability: Section 7’s deployment discussion is speculative. The concrete example (tracking every reader of product reviews on a platform like Amazon) lacks a compelling cost–benefit case, does not address privacy/consent, noise on commodity devices, reader variability, or inference latency at scale. As presented, the method requires fresh gaze for each new text instance, which severely limits practical use.
- Threats to validity in data and protocol: Gaze was recorded while annotators assigned sentiment labels, potentially entangling reading with decision processes and creating task-specific signals that may not generalize to ordinary reading. Aggregating across readers may mask inter-reader variability; no leave-one-reader-out or per-reader generalization is reported. Datasets are small, domain-limited, and differ in eye-tracker quality; yet the larger incremental gain appears on the lower-quality tracker dataset, which is counterintuitive and merits analysis.
- Novelty claim is overstated: The paper cites prior work leveraging eye-tracking for sentiment-related tasks (e.g., sentiment detection/subjectivity extraction, sarcasm understandability). While applying gaze features directly to polarity classification is a reasonable step, the “first of its kind” claim should be toned down and positioned as an incremental empirical study with a new feature set rather than a first-ever use of cognitive signals in SA/NLP.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Please clarify the novelty vis-à-vis Mishra et al. (2014) and related work using eye-tracking for sentiment/subjectivity. What exactly is new here beyond task framing and feature design?
- Can you provide a stricter validation protocol that assesses generalization across readers (e.g., leave-one-reader-out) and across items (e.g., leave-one-domain-out), and report whether gains persist?
- How do the results change after stronger controls for reading difficulty, such as including LM-based surprisal, syntactic depth, and richer text-only readability proxies? Does the Gz increment remain when these are added?
- Could you report per-phenomenon breakdowns (sarcasm, irony, thwarting, implicit sentiment) and confusion matrices to substantiate the claim that gaze especially helps complex constructs?
- What is the intended deployment path? How many readers’ gaze would be needed per item at test time for stable gains? Have you considered training a model to predict gaze features from text (so that at inference time no gaze is needed) and using those predicted features?
- Please provide exact McNemar discordant counts and apply multiple-comparison corrections across the many feature/classifier settings.

Additional comments
- Consider comparing against stronger text-only baselines (e.g., tuned linear models with n-grams/char-ngrams and regularization, or contemporary neural baselines). The current baselines may understate what can be achieved without gaze.
- Releasing code and feature extraction scripts would strengthen the contribution.
