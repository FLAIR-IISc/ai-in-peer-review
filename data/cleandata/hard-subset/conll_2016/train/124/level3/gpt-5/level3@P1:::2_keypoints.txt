- Paper augments sentiment analysis with eye-tracking (gaze) features and shows modest but significant F-score gains, especially on texts with sarcasm/irony/thwarting.
- Evaluation uses two eye-tracking datasets and compares feature groups (sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, gaze, combinations) across SVM, NB, and a small neural net.
- Combined feature sets outperform text-only baselines; larger relative gains observed on held-out complex-construct subset (~6.1%).
- Contribution: empirical evidence that gaze features improve SA performance, with small absolute gains (0.6–1.5 F1) over strong text features.
- Contribution: clear feature ablation and chi-square ranking identifying the most informative gaze features.
- Contribution: pragmatic discussion of feasibility and deployment scenarios for collecting gaze data at scale.
- Strength: novel cross-disciplinary idea—using eye movements as cognitive proxies for subtle sentiment phenomena.
- Strength: transparent, well-structured feature study with multiple classifiers and statistical significance testing.
- Strength: sensible baselines and sanity checks showing datasets are challenging for off-the-shelf SA systems.
- Strength: thoughtful feasibility section addressing hardware trends, application contexts, and consent issues.
- Strength: methodological care (PCA) to mitigate overfitting from sparse lexical features.
- Weakness: practicality/scalability—model requires gaze at inference time; no distillation/student–teacher alternative presented.
- Weakness: modest absolute improvements from gaze; gaze-only features perform poorly alone and act mainly as complementary signals.
- Weakness: datasets conflate domains (movie reviews + tweets), complicating interpretation of absolute F-scores and comparisons.
- Weakness: unclear potential data leakage between auxiliary corpora and evaluation sets; need for leave-one-reader/item-out analyses.
- Weakness: novelty claim may be overstated given prior gaze work; paper should clarify distinctions from past studies.
- Weakness: heterogeneous eye-tracker hardware (500Hz vs 120Hz) requires clearer normalization and calibration analysis.
- Question: Are there overlaps or near-duplicates between auxiliary corpora (e.g., LP, bootstrapped lists) and D1/D2, and what was done to prevent leakage?
- Question: Were CV folds constructed to avoid participant/snippet leakage (leave-one-reader/item-out), and how do results change under such splits?
- Question: Can authors report per-domain results (tweets vs. reviews vs. quotes) and train/test without cross-domain mixing to assess domain effects?
- Question: How were gaze features normalized across devices and sampling rates; was any per-device ablation performed?
- Question: Have the authors tried transferring gaze information into text-only models (multi-task/distillation) to enable deployment without eye-tracking?
- Question: Please clarify gaze-saliency graph construction (edge weights, multiple fixations/saccades handling, node treatment of punctuation/stopwords) and whether code will be released.
- Additional: Reduce overuse of bold formatting; add CV confidence intervals and per-class metrics for robustness; fix minor typos and notation inconsistencies.
