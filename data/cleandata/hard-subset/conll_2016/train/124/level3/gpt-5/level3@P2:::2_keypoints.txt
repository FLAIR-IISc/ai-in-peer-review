- Paper augments sentiment analysis with eye-tracking (gaze) features and reports statistically significant F1 gains, especially on sarcasm/irony-rich text.
- Contribution: empirical evidence that cognitive (gaze) features complement text features, with small-to-moderate overall gains (0.6–1.5 F1) and large gains (6.1 F1) on complex cases.
- Contribution: systematic comparison and ablation of feature families (sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, gaze), with significance testing and chi-squared ranking showing many gaze features in the top-20.
- Contribution: feasibility discussion of acquiring gaze data at scale and cross-device evidence using both high- and low-quality eye trackers.
- Strength: novel cognitive signal for SA, first demonstration of gaze improving polarity classification on sarcasm/irony, with significant improvements on full datasets and a sarcasm-heavy subset.
- Strength: thorough ablation and statistical analysis (progressive feature additions, McNemar tests, chi-squared rankings) supporting the reported gains.
- Strength: cross-hardware generality — gaze features help on datasets collected with both high-end (500Hz) and lower-end (120Hz) trackers.
- Strength: authors directly address practicality (mobile trackers, consent, application scenarios) strengthening real-world relevance.
- Strength: clear exposition and categorization of features (sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, gaze, readability) and detailed feature definitions enabling replicability.
- Weakness: domain conflation — mixing movie reviews and tweets without per-domain analysis makes it hard to interpret where gaze helps most.
- Weakness: limited comparisons to stronger text-only baselines (modern linear/neural models or richer n-grams/embeddings), so incremental value of gaze is unclear.
- Weakness: possible data leakage/overlap with existing SA training data (Pang & Lee; Sentiment140) not fully ruled out.
- Weakness: small absolute gains on full datasets and gaze-alone underperforms text features; gaze appears complementary but not strong independently.
- Weakness: unclear generalization and deployment assumptions — reliance on multiple readers’ averaged gaze traces and uncertain behavior with single/noisy consumer-grade traces.
- Question: confirm there are no overlapping or near-duplicate snippets between existing SA training data and D1/D2, and describe how overlap was checked.
- Question: provide results separated by domain (tweets vs reviews) and by presence/absence of sarcasm/irony per domain.
- Question: clarify whether PCA/normalization/feature selection were performed strictly within each training fold to avoid test leakage.
- Question: report how many readers contribute gaze per instance and performance as a function of reader count (1,2,…,N); do gains persist with a single trace?
- Question: analyze performance restricting to low-quality Tobii data vs high-quality Eyelink data while holding other factors constant.
- Question: compare against stronger text-only baselines (richer n-grams, character n-grams, embeddings, lightweight CNN) to validate gaze’s incremental value.
- Question: report runtime/feature-extraction overhead per instance and scaling implications for practical deployment.
- Additional: writing is clear but heavy use of bold in tables is distracting; recommend more restrained emphasis.
- Additional: release code for feature extraction (especially gaze-saliency graph construction) to facilitate reproducibility.
- Additional: include per-phenomenon error analysis (negation, irony, thwarting) showing cases where gaze helps vs hurts for better interpretability.
