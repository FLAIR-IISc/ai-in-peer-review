Summary and Contributions
This paper augments standard textual features for sentence-level sentiment polarity classification with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movement patterns. The authors evaluate on two publicly available gaze-annotated datasets that include many instances with irony/sarcasm and report small but statistically significant gains when adding gaze features to sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features. They also introduce a gaze-saliency graph and conduct feature-importance analyses.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding: Adding eye-tracking-derived cognitive features yields small (but statistically significant) gains over strong textual feature sets on two datasets and especially on a held-out subset with irony/sarcasm.
2) Feature design: A set of gaze features, including “basic” measures (fixation counts, regressions, saccade length) and “complex” graph-based features (gaze-saliency graph degrees).
3) Analysis: Chi-squared feature ranking showing several gaze features among the top features, and a breakdown showing larger gains on complex (irony/sarcasm) items.

Strengths
- Clear writing and organization: The paper is easy to follow, with a careful description of datasets, features, and experimental protocol. The ablations (Sn, Sr, Gz; combinations) make the empirical story transparent.
- Interesting, cross-disciplinary idea: Leveraging cognitive signals to address semantic/pragmatic phenomena (irony, thwarting) is timely and could inspire related work in NLP that struggles with implicit sentiment and pragmatic nuances.
- Novel feature engineering: The gaze-saliency graph and highest-degree weighted features are a creative way to summarize gaze behavior beyond standard fixation/saccade counts, potentially useful to other tasks.
- Multiple datasets and significance testing: Results are reported on two datasets recorded with different eye-trackers, with McNemar tests to establish significance when differences are small. The held-out subset analysis for complex items strengthens the claim that gaze features help with harder phenomena.
- Diagnostic analyses: The chi-squared ranking shows several gaze features among the top predictors, providing evidence that they capture signal complementary to textual features.

Weaknesses
- Limited practical impact and feasibility: The approach assumes access to eye-tracking at test time. The application scenarios in Section 7 are speculative and do not demonstrate a realistic path to deployment (e.g., required number of readers per sentence, latency, consent, device availability, noise). As a result, the utility for operational sentiment analysis is unclear.
- Marginal gains over strong textual features: The incremental improvements from adding gaze to Sn+Sr are modest (≈+0.6% and +1.5% absolute F on D1 and D2). While statistically significant, these are small, and Gz alone underperforms text features. The largest jump comes from adding sarcasm/thwarting features (3.1% and 7.8%), not from gaze.
- Motivation is underdeveloped: Beyond correlates of reading difficulty, the paper does not convincingly connect specific gaze phenomena to sentiment polarity (as opposed to general processing difficulty). The causal story for why polarity (not just complexity or incongruity) should be better captured via gaze remains weak.
- Baselines and comparability: Stronger textual baselines are missing. No comparison to contemporary neural text-only models (even lightweight CNN/RNN/Tree models) trained and evaluated on the same folds. The “existing SA systems” table uses mismatched training data, making conclusions about dataset difficulty and relative performance hard to interpret. Hyperparameters are mostly defaults, and no nested CV is used.
- Threats to validity and generalization: The datasets are small (≈1k items) relative to the number and diversity of features, increasing overfitting risk. Gaze was collected during explicit sentiment annotation, which may induce task-specific reading strategies not present in natural reading. Averaging gaze over annotators may wash out informative differences and hides the number of readers needed at test time. The held-out subset is sampled from the same dataset, limiting claims about domain robustness.

Questions to Authors
- Test-time requirements: How many readers and how much gaze data per sentence are required to reach the reported gains? Do the gains hold with a single reader, or with low-frequency/noisy gaze typical of mobile devices?
- Deployment: Can you quantify a realistic deployment scenario (throughput, consent rate, device coverage) that would make this preferable to stronger text-only models? What is the expected end-to-end latency and cost?
- Baselines: Can you add stronger text-only baselines trained and evaluated on the same folds (e.g., linear SVM with character and word n-grams, logistic regression with TF-IDF, simple CNN/RNN, or a recursive model), and report whether gaze still adds significant gains on top of them?
- Confounds and controls: Given that gaze was recorded during sentiment annotation, how do you control for task-induced attention patterns? Can you replicate with free reading before labeling or with unrelated tasks?
- Robustness: How sensitive are results to the PCA dimension, feature selection thresholds, and classifier hyperparameters (with proper tuning/nested CV)? Do the gains persist under cross-domain evaluation (train on D1, test on D2 and vice versa)?
- Per-class effects: Please report precision/recall by class and confusion matrices to understand whether gaze helps mainly with negative/ironic cases or reduces specific error types (e.g., irony-as-positive vs negative).

Additional Comments
- The “first of its kind” claim should be carefully scoped in light of prior eye-tracking work on sentiment-related tasks (e.g., Mishra et al., 2014). Clarify what is truly novel (direct use of gaze features for polarity classification).
- Consider repositioning as an analysis paper: if deployment is out of scope, frame the contribution as demonstrating that cognitive signals contain complementary information for pragmatic phenomena and guiding future multimodal models.
- Releasing code and feature extraction scripts would improve reproducibility.

Overall, the idea is intriguing and the paper is well presented, but the marginal gains, weak practical feasibility, and limited baselines temper the strength of the contribution.
