- Summary: Augments sentiment analysis with eye-tracking-derived cognitive features (basic gaze stats + saliency-graph), showing modest but statistically significant gains over textual features, larger on ironic/sarcastic texts.

- Contribution 1: Empirical evidence that gaze features can improve sentiment classification, especially for irony/sarcasm, with small additive gains over strong textual baselines.

- Contribution 2: A reusable inventory of gaze features for SA, including a novel saliency-graph representation.

- Contribution 3: Feature-importance and held-out analyses indicating which gaze features matter and that benefits concentrate on complex phenomena (irony/sarcasm).

- Strength: Clear framing and reporting—well written with sufficient implementation and evaluation details.

- Strength: Cross-disciplinary idea—cognitive signals capture processing difficulty and pragmatic cues text features may miss.

- Strength: Evidence that gains are larger on complex constructs (6.1% for irony vs. 2.1% for non-irony) supported by statistical tests.

- Strength: Feature importance analysis shows many gaze features rank highly, linking them plausibly to processing difficulty.

- Weakness: Motivation unclear—gaze may only reflect general reading difficulty rather than sentiment-specific signals.

- Weakness: Practical impact marginal—most gains come from sarcasm/thwarting textual features; gaze adds only small increments.

- Weakness: Feasibility/ethics under-argued—mobile/noisy gaze, calibration, privacy, and UX concerns not addressed.

- Weakness: Limited experimental validation—no ablation isolating saliency-graph vs basic gaze, no controls for readability/length, no leave-one-reader-out tests, and no cross-device/dataset generalization.

- Weakness: Unclear robustness—significance shown for one dataset and held-out subset but second dataset/folds robustness is not fully reported.

- Weakness: Novelty is incremental given prior eye-tracking work on related tasks.

- Question: Can you show gaze patterns relate to sentiment polarity specifically (controls for readability/length, partial correlations, matched pairs)?

- Question: Which gaze features drive gains—provide ablations separating basic vs. saliency-graph and incremental benefits over Sn and Sn+Sr?

- Question: How robust are gains across readers/devices—report leave-one-reader-out and cross-device/dataset generalization?

- Question: Are gains on Dataset 2 statistically significant across folds (e.g., McNemar p-values)?

- Question: What is the inference-time cost-benefit—performance vs number of readers and with noisy/low-sampling gaze?

- Question: Could textual proxies for processing difficulty (LM surprisal, syntactic/discourse features) achieve similar improvements, isolating gaze’s unique value?
