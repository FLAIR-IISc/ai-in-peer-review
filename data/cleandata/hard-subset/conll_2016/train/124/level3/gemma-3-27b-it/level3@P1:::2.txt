This paper explores augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The core idea – leveraging how readers process text to improve sentiment detection – is novel and potentially impactful. The authors address a challenging problem, acknowledging the limitations of traditional methods in handling nuanced language, sarcasm, and irony. They systematically introduce and evaluate a comprehensive feature set, including sentiment features, sarcasm-related features, cognitive features from eye-movements, and features related to reading difficulty. The discussion regarding the feasibility of applying this approach, given the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers, is well-considered and strengthens the paper.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the exploration and demonstration of the value of incorporating cognitive features, specifically those derived from eye-tracking, into sentiment analysis. While eye-tracking has been used in related psycholinguistic tasks, its application to enhancing sentiment classification, particularly in comparison to traditional feature sets, is a significant step. A second, noteworthy contribution is the detailed feature engineering process. The authors don't simply add eye-tracking data; they thoughtfully create a range of features, including basic gaze metrics and complex graph-based representations of eye-movement patterns, attempting to capture different aspects of cognitive processing. Finally, the paper’s careful error analysis and thoughtful consideration of practical limitations represent a valuable contribution to the field.

Several strengths support the acceptance of this submission. First, the experimental setup is robust. The authors utilize two datasets, demonstrating the generalizability of their approach. They compare different classifiers and perform feature significance analysis, providing insights into which cognitive features are most impactful. The results presented in Table 2 show a consistent improvement in F-score when cognitive features are added to the baseline models, with a maximum improvement of 9.3% on Dataset 2, a statistically significant result. This suggests that eye-tracking data does indeed provide valuable information for sentiment analysis.

Second, the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the related work. They thoroughly review existing approaches in sentiment analysis and psycholinguistics, positioning their work appropriately within the existing literature. The discussion of prior work using eye-tracking for related tasks (like sarcasm understandability) is helpful in contextualizing their contribution. 

Third, the analysis of the held-out dataset (Table 4) convincingly demonstrates the added value of cognitive features for complex text, showing a greater relative improvement on ironic/sarcastic snippets compared to simpler text. This validates the hypothesis that cognitive features are particularly useful in handling nuances that are difficult for traditional methods to capture.

Despite these strengths, several weaknesses warrant attention. The absolute F-scores achieved (Table 2) appear relatively low, particularly for Dataset 1, when compared to established benchmarks in sentiment analysis like those reported by Pang and Lee (2004). While the authors acknowledge the challenges posed by the datasets, a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art methods on standard datasets would strengthen the claims of improvement.

A significant concern is the potential for domain conflation within the datasets. The combination of movie reviews and tweets in both datasets could introduce noise and reduce the effectiveness of the models. Different domains often exhibit distinct linguistic characteristics and sentiment expressions, and training on a mixed dataset might hinder the model's ability to generalize effectively. Separating the datasets by domain would allow for a more controlled evaluation and a better understanding of the approach's performance in different contexts.

Finally, the authors need to clarify the potential for overlap between the training and test sets. Even though the data is collected from publicly available sources (e.g., Sentiment140), there is a possibility that some snippets may be present in both the training and test datasets, leading to an overestimation of performance. Addressing this concern is crucial for establishing the reliability of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more direct comparison of your approach to state-of-the-art sentiment analysis methods on standard benchmark datasets (e.g., those used in the SemEval competitions)?
2.  Is there any possibility of overlap between the snippets used in the training and test sets for Datasets 1 and 2? If so, how was this addressed?
3. Would you consider evaluating your approach on domain-separated datasets (e.g., a dataset consisting solely of movie reviews and a separate dataset consisting solely of tweets) to assess its generalizability?
4.  While the discussion of feature importance is valuable, could you provide more detailed insights into *which* specific cognitive features contribute most to the performance improvement? Perhaps a visualization of feature weights?

**Additional Comments:**

The excessive use of bold text throughout the paper is distracting and should be reduced for improved readability. Consider using italics or a consistent style for emphasizing key terms.
