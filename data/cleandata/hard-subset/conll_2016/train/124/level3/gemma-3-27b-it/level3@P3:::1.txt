## Review for “Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis”

This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of annotators. The authors claim this is the first such use in NLP, leading to modest improvements in sentiment and sarcasm detection on two datasets. While the paper is well-written and easy to follow, the contributions and their significance require careful consideration.

**Summary:**

The paper tackles the challenging problem of sentiment analysis, specifically addressing the nuances present in user-generated short texts. The authors hypothesize that incorporating cognitive features – derived from how readers’ eyes move while reading text – can improve sentiment classification performance. They extract features from eye-tracking data (fixation duration, saccade length, regressions, etc.) and combine them with traditional sentiment analysis features (lexical, syntactic, and existing sarcasm/thwarting indicators).  Experiments on two datasets demonstrate a small improvement in F-score (up to 3.7% and 9.3%) when cognitive features are added.  The authors also present a feasibility discussion, suggesting the potential of using mobile eye-trackers for broader data collection.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Exploration of Eye-Movement Data for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution lies in the novel *application* of eye-tracking data to the task of sentiment analysis. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistic studies of language processing, its integration into a mainstream NLP task like sentiment analysis is a worthwhile exploration.
2. **Creation of a Feature Set:** The authors have constructed a comprehensive feature set combining traditional sentiment features with a range of cognitive features, including both basic gaze metrics and more complex graph-based features derived from saccade patterns. This provides a useful resource for future research.
3. **Initial Empirical Investigation:** The paper provides an initial empirical assessment of the potential benefits of incorporating cognitive features.  Although the performance gains are modest, the experiments offer a starting point for further investigation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to understand. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined.  The organization is logical and facilitates comprehension.
2. **Novelty of Approach:** The application of eye-tracking data to sentiment analysis is a genuinely novel idea.  It represents a step towards incorporating cognitive science principles into NLP models.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate considerable effort in designing and implementing a diverse set of features, encompassing both linguistic and cognitive aspects. The attempt to capture more nuanced reading behavior through graph-based features is particularly commendable.
4. **Thorough Experimentation:** The experiments were conducted on two datasets with established baselines, providing a reasonable evaluation framework. The statistical significance testing adds credibility to the reported results.
5. **Well-Motivated Research Question:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of sentiment analysis and positions cognitive features as a potential solution for addressing linguistic nuances that traditional methods struggle with.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Weak Motivation for Cognitive Features:** The rationale for *why* cognitive features should improve sentiment analysis is underdeveloped. While the authors state a hypothesis about relating brain processes to eye movements, the connection remains largely intuitive.  A stronger theoretical grounding, perhaps drawing more heavily on cognitive psychology, would strengthen the argument.  Simply stating that cognitive features help with "complex constructs" is insufficient.
2. **Marginal Performance Improvement:** The observed improvement in F-score (3.7% and 9.3%) is relatively small, especially considering the added complexity of collecting and processing eye-tracking data.  This raises questions about the practical utility of the approach. The difference between Sn+Sr and Sn+Sr+Gz is particularly small.
3. **Unconvincing Feasibility Discussion:** The discussion of feasibility (Section 7) feels speculative and lacks concrete evidence. The claim that inexpensive mobile eye-trackers will make widespread data collection feasible is optimistic, and the example scenario (e-commerce reviews) is not convincingly demonstrated.  The current state of mobile eye-tracking technology and user consent remain significant hurdles.
4. **Limited Analysis of Feature Importance:** While the paper includes feature significance analysis, the results aren't deeply explored. A more granular analysis of which specific cognitive features are most predictive and why would be valuable.
5. **Dataset Specificity:** The datasets used are relatively small and may not be representative of all types of sentiment-bearing text. The generalization ability of the approach remains uncertain.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the cognitive theory underpinning your hypothesis that eye-movement patterns are indicative of sentiment processing? Specifically, what cognitive processes are you attempting to capture with each of your cognitive features?
2.  The improvement on Dataset 2 (9.3%) is more significant than on Dataset 1 (3.7%). Can you explain why the cognitive features might be more effective on this particular dataset? Are there inherent differences between the datasets that contribute to this variation?
3.  Given the marginal performance gains and the complexity of data collection, what specific applications or scenarios do you envision where this approach would be most valuable?
4.  Regarding the graph-based features, could you provide more insight into the rationale behind using weighted degree as a feature? What does the magnitude of the weighted degree represent in the context of sentiment analysis?
5.  Could you elaborate on the challenges encountered during eye-tracking data collection and pre-processing? Were there any significant issues with data quality or participant variability that affected your results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are well-cited and relevant to the topic.
*   Consider discussing the potential impact of individual differences in reading habits and eye-movement patterns on the results.
*   Exploring different machine learning algorithms and hyperparameter tuning could potentially yield better performance.
