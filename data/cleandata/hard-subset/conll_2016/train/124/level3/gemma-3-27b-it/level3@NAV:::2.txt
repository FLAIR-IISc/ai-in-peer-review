## Review of "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

This paper explores an interesting and novel approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data alongside traditional linguistic features. The core idea – that readers’ eye movements reflect cognitive processes related to sentiment understanding – is promising and potentially valuable, especially for nuanced text like sarcasm and irony. The authors demonstrate a measurable improvement in F-score (up to 9.3%) by augmenting existing feature sets with these cognitive signals.

The paper’s main contribution lies in demonstrating the *feasibility* of integrating eye-tracking data into a sentiment analysis pipeline and showing its beneficial impact on performance. A second, valuable contribution is the thorough comparison of different feature sets, allowing for a clear understanding of which cognitive features are most informative. The inclusion of a held-out dataset (Section 7) to assess performance on complex constructs further strengthens the work.

**Strengths:**

* **Novelty of Approach:** Combining eye-tracking data with sentiment analysis is a relatively unexplored area, making this work stand out.
* **Comprehensive Feature Comparison:** The authors provide a well-structured and informative discussion of various feature sets, detailing their impact on performance. Table 2 clearly illustrates the improvements gained by adding cognitive features.
* **Feasibility Assessment:** Section 7 directly addresses practical concerns regarding the implementation of this approach, discussing the growing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential applications.
* **Appropriate Evaluation:** The use of two datasets and a held-out evaluation set demonstrates a reasonable effort to validate the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Dataset Conflation:** The datasets used (D1 & D2) combine movie reviews and tweets, potentially introducing domain-specific biases and hindering generalizability. Separating these domains during training and evaluation would likely improve both performance and interpretability.
* **Performance Relative to SOTA:** The reported F-scores in Table 1, while improved with the added features, appear relatively low compared to state-of-the-art sentiment analysis methods like Pang & Lee (2008). This is partially addressed by the authors when discussing Twitter data, but further clarification is needed.
* **Data Overlap Concerns:** The paper needs to explicitly address the possibility of overlapping snippets between the training and test sets, even if originating from different sources. Clarifying this is crucial for ensuring a fair evaluation of the model's generalization ability.
* **Excessive Bold Text:** The frequent use of bold formatting is distracting and detracts from the readability of the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more detail on the exact preprocessing steps applied to the eye-tracking data, and the rationale behind those choices?
2. How would you expect the results to change if the training and testing data were separated by domain (movie reviews vs. tweets)?
3. Can you definitively confirm whether there is any overlap between the training and test sets, even at the snippet level?
