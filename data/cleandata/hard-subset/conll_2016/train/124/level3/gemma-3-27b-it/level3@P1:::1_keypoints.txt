- Proposes augmenting traditional sentiment features with cognitive features from readers’ eye movements.  
- Hypothesizes eye-movement patterns reveal cognitive signals helpful for complex sentiment constructs like sarcasm and irony.  
- Core contribution: introduces eye-tracking data as a novel feature source for sentiment classification, showing modest improvements over baselines.  
- Secondary contribution: provides a comprehensive dataset combining sentiment/sarcasm corpora with eye-tracking data.  
- Offers a preliminary discussion on feasibility of real-world integration of eye-tracking features.  

Strengths:
- Novelty: integrating eye-tracking cognitive features into NLP/sentiment analysis is a valuable new direction.  
- Thorough feature engineering: combines lexical, syntactic, semantic, sarcasm-targeted, and graph-based eye-tracking features with clear organization.  
- Rigorous evaluation: uses two public datasets, 10-fold CV, held-out tests, baselines, and statistical significance testing (McNemar).  

Weaknesses:
- Modest gains: improvements (max 9.3% F-score) are statistically significant but practically limited given added complexity.  
- Weak theoretical justification: insufficient grounding for why specific gaze features correlate with sentiment processing.  
- Feasibility concerns: deployment scenario (e.g., mobile eye-trackers) and privacy/technical challenges are under-addressed and unconvincing.  

Questions for authors:
- Please clarify theoretical basis linking chosen eye-movement features to cognitive models of sentiment processing.  
- What is the computational cost of extracting/incorporating cognitive features versus traditional approaches?  
- Why are gains larger on Dataset 2—what dataset characteristics explain this difference?  
- Have you tried dimensionality reduction or feature selection to isolate the most impactful cognitive features?  

Additional comments:
- Table 3 should highlight top cognitive features specifically, not just overall top features.  
- Discuss limitations from individual differences in eye movements (reading speed, visual acuity) and their effects.  
- References are extensive and well organized.
