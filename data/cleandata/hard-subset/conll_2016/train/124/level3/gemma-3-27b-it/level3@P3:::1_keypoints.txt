- Proposes augmenting traditional sentiment features with cognitive features from annotators' eye-movement patterns for sentiment and sarcasm detection.
- Experiments on two datasets show modest F-score improvements when adding cognitive features (up to 3.7% and 9.3%).
- Novelty: first application of eye-tracking data to a mainstream NLP task like sentiment analysis.
- Built a comprehensive feature set combining lexical/syntactic features with basic gaze metrics and graph-based saccade features.
- Provides an initial empirical assessment demonstrating potential benefits of cognitive features.
- Well-written, clearly explained methodology and well-defined experimental setup.
- Approach is novel and helps bridge cognitive science and NLP.
- Considerable effort in feature engineering, with notable inclusion of graph-based reading-behavior features.
- Experiments on two datasets with baselines and statistical significance testing lend credibility.
- Research question is well-motivated, targeting linguistic nuances in short user-generated texts.
- Motivation for why cognitive features should improve sentiment analysis is underdeveloped and needs stronger cognitive-theory grounding.
- Performance gains are marginal relative to the cost/complexity of collecting and processing eye-tracking data.
- Feasibility discussion about mobile eye-trackers is speculative and lacks concrete evidence.
- Analysis of which cognitive features drive performance is limited and needs deeper exploration.
- Datasets are relatively small and may not generalize to broader sentiment-bearing texts.
- Asks authors to clarify the cognitive theory linking eye movements to sentiment processing and feature-level mappings.
- Requests explanation for dataset-specific performance differences and real-world application scenarios where this approach is valuable.
- Suggests discussing data collection challenges, participant variability, individual reading differences, and exploring more ML algorithms/hyperparameter tuning.
