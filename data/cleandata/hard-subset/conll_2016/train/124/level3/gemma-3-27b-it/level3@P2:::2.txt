This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data, hypothesizing that readers’ eye-movement patterns reflect cognitive processes relevant to understanding nuanced sentiment. The authors demonstrate improved performance on two datasets, achieving up to a 9.3% F-score increase over systems using only traditional features. The primary contribution lies in the novel integration of cognitive features, specifically those derived from eye-tracking, into a sentiment analysis pipeline. A secondary contribution is the thorough feature analysis, demonstrating the significance of these cognitive features, particularly in handling complex constructs like irony and sarcasm. Finally, the authors proactively address the feasibility concerns of applying this approach, considering the increasing availability of mobile eye-tracking technology.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea of incorporating cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, into sentiment analysis is genuinely novel. While psycholinguistic approaches to NLP are gaining traction, this work represents a direct attempt to leverage real-time cognitive processes during sentiment understanding. This has the potential to significantly improve sentiment analysis accuracy, particularly for nuanced or complex text where traditional methods struggle.

2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering and Analysis:** The authors present a well-defined and comprehensive feature set, clearly categorizing features into sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting-related, cognitive, and reading difficulty components.  The detailed explanation of each feature is valuable, and the feature significance analysis (Table 3) convincingly demonstrates the utility of the cognitive features, showing that gaze-based features consistently rank highly in importance.

3. **Proactive Feasibility Discussion:** Section 7 directly addresses a critical limitation of this approach: the requirement for eye-tracking data. The authors realistically discuss the growing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios, acknowledging the challenges while presenting a plausible path towards practical implementation. The discussion of user consent is also well-considered.

4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors utilize two datasets and multiple classifiers (SVM, NB, Multi-layer NN) to comprehensively evaluate their approach. The 10-fold cross-validation and statistical significance testing (McNemar test) enhance the robustness of their findings. The held-out dataset analysis (Table 4) provides further evidence supporting the effectiveness of the cognitive features, particularly in complex cases.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dataset Composition & Generalizability:** The datasets used (Dataset 1 and Dataset 2) combine movie reviews and tweets. These domains are known to exhibit different linguistic characteristics and sentiment expression patterns. This conflation could limit the generalizability of the results and make it difficult to isolate the true impact of the cognitive features. Separating the datasets by domain would provide a more controlled evaluation and potentially reveal domain-specific benefits of the approach.

2. **Relatively Low Baseline Performance:** The baseline F-scores reported in Table 1 appear relatively low, especially for Dataset 1. While the authors acknowledge the complexity of the datasets, these scores raise concerns about whether the improvements achieved with the cognitive features are substantial enough given the initial performance. Further comparison with more recent and sophisticated state-of-the-art methods (beyond Pang & Lee) would strengthen the evaluation.

3. **Potential Data Contamination:**  A crucial point needing clarification is the potential for overlap between the text snippets used in the training data (from Pang & Lee and Sentiment140) and the snippets present in Datasets 1 and 2. Even if the data sources are different (e.g., movie reviews vs. tweets), there’s a possibility of identical or highly similar snippets appearing in both datasets, leading to overly optimistic performance estimates.

4. **Presentation & Redundancy:**  The excessive use of bold text throughout the paper is distracting and detracts from readability. Furthermore, some explanations and justifications are repetitive, potentially lengthening the paper unnecessarily. A more concise and streamlined presentation would enhance the overall clarity.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you clarify whether there is any overlap in text snippets between the training data used to train the baseline classifiers (Pang & Lee, Sentiment140) and the text snippets present in Datasets 1 and 2? If so, please quantify the extent of this overlap.

2.  Would separating the datasets by domain (movie reviews vs. tweets) significantly alter the reported results? If so, please provide the results for each domain separately.

3.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the cognitive features were aggregated across participants in the saliency graph construction (Section 4.5)? What considerations were taken to account for individual differences in eye-movement patterns?

**Additional Comments:**

- Consider providing ablation studies to further isolate the contribution of individual cognitive features.
- The abbreviation “Gz” for “Gaze features” is unconventional and could be replaced with a more descriptive abbreviation.
- While the discussion of feasibility is good, a brief consideration of data privacy implications related to eye-tracking data collection would be beneficial.
