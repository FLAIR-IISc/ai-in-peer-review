- Novel idea: augment sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking to leverage reader processing signals.  
- Addresses a challenging problem by targeting nuanced language, sarcasm, and irony where traditional methods struggle.  
- Introduces and evaluates a comprehensive feature set: sentiment, sarcasm-related, cognitive eye-movement, and reading-difficulty features.  
- Practical feasibility discussed, noting increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers strengthens applicability.  
- Primary contribution: demonstrating that eye-tracking–derived cognitive features can improve sentiment classification.  
- Secondary contribution: careful feature engineering, including basic gaze metrics and graph-based eye-movement representations.  
- Provides careful error analysis and thoughtful discussion of practical limitations.  
- Strong experimental setup: two datasets, multiple classifiers, and feature significance analysis for generalizability.  
- Empirical result: consistent F-score improvement when adding cognitive features, up to 9.3% on Dataset 2 (statistically significant).  
- Good literature review and contextualization of prior eye-tracking work in psycholinguistics and sarcasm detection.  
- Held-out dataset analysis shows larger relative gains on ironic/sarcastic snippets, supporting the hypothesis about cognitive features.  
- Weakness: absolute F-scores are relatively low (especially Dataset 1) compared to established benchmarks, limiting claims of overall performance.  
- Concern about domain conflation: mixing movie reviews and tweets may introduce noise and reduce generalizability.  
- Concern about possible overlap between training and test snippets (e.g., from Sentiment140), which could inflate performance.  
- Requested comparisons: run and report results against state-of-the-art methods on standard benchmarks (e.g., SemEval).  
- Requested clarification: confirm and address any training/test overlap for Datasets 1 and 2.  
- Requested experiment: evaluate approach on domain-separated datasets (e.g., only movie reviews vs. only tweets) to assess generalizability.  
- Requested analysis: provide more detailed insight/visualization of which specific cognitive features drive improvements.
