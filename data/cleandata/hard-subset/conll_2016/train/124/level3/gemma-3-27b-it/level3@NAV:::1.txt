## Review for "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

This paper explores the novel idea of augmenting traditional sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The authors claim this is the first work to utilize eye-movement patterns for sentiment and sarcasm detection in NLP, and demonstrate modest improvements in F-score (up to 9.3%) on two datasets. While the writing is clear and easy to follow, and the core idea is intriguing with potential for inspiring future work, the contribution feels incremental given the limited gains and questionable feasibility.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the exploration of integrating eye-tracking data into a standard sentiment analysis pipeline, and the creation of a dataset of cognitive features alongside existing sentiment benchmarks.  The comprehensive feature engineering, encompassing traditional, sarcasm-related, cognitive, and readability features, is a notable effort. The paper effectively surveys related work in both sentiment analysis and the use of eye-tracking in psycholinguistics, providing useful context for the proposed approach.

However, the motivation for *why* cognitive features should improve sentiment analysis beyond simply easing reading difficulty is weak. The connection between specific eye-movement patterns (e.g., regressions, fixation durations) and nuanced understanding of sentiment, particularly sarcasm, isn't convincingly established. The paper asserts a relationship based on cognitive processes but lacks a strong theoretical grounding for this connection beyond general principles. 

Critically, the improvement gained from adding cognitive features (Sn+Sr+Gz vs. Sn+Sr) is marginal.  While statistically significant, a 0.6-1.5% increase in F-score doesn't represent a substantial leap forward, particularly considering the additional complexity of acquiring and processing eye-tracking data. Furthermore, the feasibility discussion, especially the example in Section 7.2, relies heavily on future technologies (inexpensive mobile eye-trackers) and optimistic assumptions about user consent, making it unconvincing in its current form. The argument doesn’t clearly demonstrate a clear benefit that justifies the complexity of data acquisition.



**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** Exploring the use of cognitive features from eye-tracking in sentiment analysis is a fresh direction.
*   **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors consider a wide range of features, providing a solid baseline for comparison.
*   **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is well-written and easy to understand.
*   **Thorough Related Work:** A good overview of existing research in sentiment analysis and eye-tracking is provided.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Weak Motivation:** The theoretical link between eye-movement patterns and sentiment understanding is not well-established.
*   **Marginal Improvement:** The performance gains from adding cognitive features are relatively small.
*   **Questionable Feasibility:** The proposed application scenario relies on future technologies and optimistic assumptions.
*   **Limited Error Analysis:**  A more detailed analysis of error types would provide valuable insights.




**Questions to Authors:**

*   Can you elaborate on the specific cognitive processes you hypothesize are driving the observed improvements with the addition of eye-tracking features?
*   How sensitive are the results to the quality/accuracy of the eye-tracking data? Would lower-quality, more readily available eye-trackers significantly impact performance?
*   Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by the system with and without cognitive features?
