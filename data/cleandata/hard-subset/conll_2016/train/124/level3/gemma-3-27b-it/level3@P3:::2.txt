This paper introduces an interesting approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional feature sets with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The core idea – that readers' eye movements can reveal nuances in sentiment perception – is novel and potentially valuable for improving sentiment detection, particularly in complex cases like sarcasm and irony. The authors provide a comprehensive feature set, including both established sentiment analysis features and novel gaze-based features, and demonstrate improvements in F-score on two datasets.

**Contributions:**

1. **Integration of Eye-Tracking Data for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the demonstration of the feasibility and potential benefits of incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-tracking into a sentiment analysis pipeline. This opens up a new avenue for capturing subtle cues related to sentiment understanding.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors present a well-defined and diverse feature set, carefully categorized and explained. The inclusion of both traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting-related features, and a detailed set of gaze-based features provides a strong foundation for comparison and analysis.
3. **Initial Empirical Validation:** The paper provides empirical evidence, through experiments on two datasets, that the augmented feature set can lead to improved sentiment classification performance, particularly for complex linguistic constructs.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The exploration of eye-tracking features as proxies for cognitive processes in sentiment analysis is a genuinely novel contribution. While eye-tracking has been used in related areas like annotation complexity, its direct application to sentiment classification is a significant step forward.
2. **Detailed Feature Set and Comparison:** The authors thoroughly describe their feature engineering process, presenting a wide range of features and clearly delineating their categories. The comparative analysis of feature combinations allows for a nuanced understanding of which features contribute most to performance improvements. The inclusion of PCA for dimensionality reduction is also a good practice.
3. **Appropriate Feasibility Discussion:** The authors acknowledge the practical limitations of their approach (requiring eye-tracking data) and proactively address the feasibility issue by discussing the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios. This demonstrates a realistic understanding of the challenges and opportunities.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis:** The inclusion of an error analysis section, even if brief, shows awareness of the limitations and potential areas for future improvement. Recognizing that aggregating individual eye-tracking patterns may lead to information loss is a valuable insight.
5. **Statistical Significance Testing:** The use of McNemar's test to confirm the significance of the improvements in F-score adds rigor to the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Domain Conflation in Datasets:** A significant weakness is the mixing of movie reviews and tweets within the datasets. These domains have distinct linguistic characteristics and sentiment expressions. Combining them may hinder model generalization and make it difficult to interpret the results. Separating the datasets into domain-specific subsets would substantially improve the validity and interpretability of the findings.
2. **Relatively Low Baseline Performance:** The F-scores reported for the baseline systems (Table 1) are relatively low, particularly for Dataset 1. While the authors acknowledge the complexity of the datasets, the low baselines raise questions about the comparability of their results with state-of-the-art sentiment analysis techniques. Comparing against stronger baselines (e.g., pre-trained language models fine-tuned for sentiment) would strengthen the paper.
3. **Overlapping Snippets Concern:** The paper does not explicitly address the possibility of overlapping snippets between the training and test sets within Datasets 1 and 2, even if sourced from different origins.  Such overlap could lead to inflated performance estimates.  Clarification on this point is crucial.
4. **Excessive Use of Bold Text:** The extensive use of bold text throughout the paper is distracting and detracts from readability. Reducing the reliance on bolding would improve the overall presentation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Overlap:** Can you confirm whether there is any overlap of text snippets (even partial overlap) between the training and test sets in Datasets 1 and 2? If overlap exists, how might this have affected the reported results?
2. **Domain Separation:** Have you considered training and evaluating separate models for movie reviews and tweets? If so, what were the results? If not, do you plan to explore this in future work?
3. **Baseline Comparison:**  Could you provide a comparison of your best performing model against stronger baseline methods, such as pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) fine-tuned for sentiment analysis on the same datasets?
4. **Individual Gaze Patterns:**  You mention that aggregating individual eye-tracking patterns might cause information loss. Have you explored methods for preserving or leveraging individual-level gaze data, such as using participant-specific features or multi-instance learning?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation (e.g., P, R, F) in Table 2 could be clarified within the table caption.
* Consider removing unnecessary abbreviations within the text to improve readability.
* The figures could be improved with clearer labels and annotations.
