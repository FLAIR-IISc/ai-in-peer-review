- Proposes integrating eye-tracking–derived cognitive features with linguistic features for sentiment analysis, showing up to 9.3% F-score improvement.  
- Demonstrates feasibility of using eye-tracking signals in a sentiment pipeline and identifies which cognitive features are most informative.  
- Provides a thorough comparison of feature sets with clear performance gains illustrated (Table 2).  
- Includes a held-out dataset evaluation to test performance on complex constructs like sarcasm/irony.  
- Novelty: the approach is relatively unexplored and potentially valuable for nuanced sentiment detection.  
- Practical feasibility discussed (Section 7), including growing availability of mobile eye-trackers and applications.  
- Evaluation uses two datasets plus a held-out set, showing reasonable validation effort.  
- Dataset conflation: combining movie reviews and tweets may introduce domain bias and hurt generalizability.  
- Reported F-scores, though improved, appear low relative to state-of-the-art methods and need clarification.  
- Possible overlap between training and test snippets not explicitly addressed, raising generalization concerns.  
- Presentation issue: excessive use of bold text is distracting and reduces readability.  
- Questions for authors: specify eye-tracking preprocessing and rationale; indicate expected effects of separating domains for training/testing; confirm any snippet-level overlap between train and test sets.
