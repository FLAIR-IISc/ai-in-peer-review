- Summary: Proposes augmenting linguistic features with eye-tracking-derived cognitive features for sentiment analysis, showing modest but statistically significant F-score gains on two complex datasets.  
- Contribution 1: Novel proposal and validation of eye-tracking data as cognitive features for sentiment analysis, expanding feature engineering beyond text.  
- Contribution 2: Analysis indicating cognitive features particularly help classify complex constructs like sarcasm.  
- Strength 1: Innovative interdisciplinary idea leveraging cognitive proxies to address subtle sentiment challenges.  
- Strength 2: Clear ablation studies and feature-set comparisons that show incremental performance gains (Table 2).  
- Strength 3: Thoughtful discussion on real-world feasibility and potential for scalable mobile eye-tracking data collection.  
- Weakness 1: Datasets mix domains (movie reviews, tweets, quotes), risking domain-confounding effects and limiting interpretability; per-domain analysis needed.  
- Weakness 2: Absolute performance is relatively low compared to SOTA on benchmarks; lacks direct comparison to sarcasm- or nuance-focused baselines.  
- Weakness 3: Experimental setup may permit data leakage—unclear if training corpora overlap with eye-tracking test snippets.  
- Question to authors: Please clarify whether any specific text-snippet overlap exists between the large training corpora (e.g., Pang & Lee, Sentiment140) and the two test datasets.
