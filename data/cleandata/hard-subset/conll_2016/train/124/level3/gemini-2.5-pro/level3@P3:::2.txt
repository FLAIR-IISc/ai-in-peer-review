### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with "cognitive features" derived from readers' eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, such as fixations and regressions, serve as proxies for the cognitive effort required to understand nuanced or complex text, like sarcasm and irony. The authors test this hypothesis by building classifiers using various feature sets (standard sentiment features, sarcasm-specific features, and gaze-based features) on two datasets containing movie reviews and tweets. Their results show that the full feature set, including cognitive features, improves sentiment classification F-score by up to 3.7% on one dataset and 9.3% on the other, compared to using only text-based features. The paper also includes a feature importance analysis and an experiment on a held-out set to demonstrate that the gaze features are particularly beneficial for classifying complex, ironic texts.

### Main Contributions

1.  **Introduction of Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the novel proposal and application of eye-tracking data as a source of features for sentiment analysis. This bridges the gap between psycholinguistics and computational linguistics, offering a new modality to tackle challenging aspects of language understanding like sarcasm and implicit sentiment, which are often difficult to capture from text alone.
2.  **Systematic Evaluation of Feature Impact:** The paper provides a clear empirical demonstration that cognitive features, when combined with well-established textual features, lead to significant performance gains. The analysis in Table 4, which shows a much larger improvement on ironic vs. non-ironic text (6.1% vs 2.1%), provides strong evidence for the authors' core hypothesis that these features help model the processing of complex linguistic constructs.

### Strengths

1.  **Innovative and Well-Motivated Approach:** The core idea of using eye-tracking data as a proxy for cognitive processing is highly innovative and well-motivated. The authors correctly identify that traditional NLP features struggle with subtleties like sarcasm and thwarted expectations. Linking these linguistic challenges to measurable cognitive signals (e.g., increased regressions when a reader encounters an incongruous phrase) is a compelling and promising research direction.
2.  **Clear Experimental Design and Feature Analysis:** The paper presents a well-structured experimental setup that systematically evaluates the contribution of different feature groups. The incremental addition of sentiment (Sn), sarcasm (Sr), and gaze (Gz) features in Table 2 clearly illustrates the performance benefit at each step. Furthermore, the feature significance analysis (Table 3) provides concrete evidence that gaze features are among the most informative predictors, reinforcing their value.
3.  **Proactive Discussion of Practical Feasibility:** The authors wisely include Section 7 to address the most obvious critique of this work: its real-world applicability. By discussing the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and outlining potential application scenarios (e.g., in e-commerce review analysis), they demonstrate foresight and ground their research in a plausible future context. This discussion substantially strengthens the paper by preempting a major concern.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Conflation of Data Domains:** The datasets used (Dataset 1 and 2) are described as a mix of movie reviews, tweets, and quotes. These domains are linguistically distinct; tweets are characterized by brevity, informal language, and hashtags, while movie reviews are typically longer and more structured. Combining them into a single dataset for training and evaluation muddles the interpretation of the results. It is unclear whether the cognitive features are more beneficial for one domain over the other. A separate analysis on each domain would provide much richer insights and could lead to stronger, more specialized models.
2.  **Unclear Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The performance reported in Table 2 (e.g., a top F-score of 73.5 on Dataset 1 and 71.8 on Dataset 2) appears low when compared to modern sentiment analysis results on standard benchmarks like the Pang & Lee movie review dataset. While the authors demonstrate in Table 1 that their datasets are challenging for baseline systems, the context is still not fully clear. A more direct comparison with recent, strong baselines on these specific datasets would better situate the contribution. The current baselines seem somewhat dated.
3.  **Potential for Data Overlap between Training and Test Sets:** In Section 3.3, the baseline classifiers are trained on snippets from Pang and Lee (2004) and Sentiment140. The test datasets (Dataset 1 and 2) are also sourced from movie reviews and tweets, implying the same original sources. The authors must clarify whether there is any possibility of exact snippet overlap between the training corpus for the baselines and the test sets used in the main experiments. Any overlap would compromise the validity of the baseline results presented in Table 1 and the subsequent claims of improvement.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the datasets, could you please clarify whether there is any exact instance-level overlap between the training set used to build the baseline systems in Table 1 and the snippets contained in Dataset 1 and Dataset 2?
2.  Given that the datasets combine distinct domains (movie reviews and tweets), have you considered performing an analysis where you evaluate the models on each domain separately? Such a study could reveal interesting differences in how cognitive features contribute to understanding sentiment in short, informal texts versus longer, more formal reviews.

### Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. A minor stylistic point: the excessive use of bolding for feature names and other terms throughout the text can be distracting and makes the document harder to read. It would be more effective to use bolding more sparingly for emphasis.
