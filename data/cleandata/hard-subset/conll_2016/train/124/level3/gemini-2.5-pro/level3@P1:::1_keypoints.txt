- Proposes augmenting text-based sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking (fixations, saccades, regressions, gaze-saliency graph) to capture nuances like sarcasm and irony.  
- Evaluates the approach on two sentiment-annotated short-text datasets and reports improved classification, especially for complex constructs.

- Introduces a novel, systematic feature set derived from human gaze behavior, including basic statistics and graph-based properties.  
- Empirically shows cognitive features are especially beneficial for classifying ironic/sarcastic texts, suggesting gaze captures re-analysis or surprise signals.

- High novelty and potential to inspire follow-up work applying cognitive signals to other NLP tasks requiring subtle human interpretation.  
- Thorough feature engineering and analysis, including feature significance and targeted experiments comparing complex vs. simple texts.  
- Clear, well-structured presentation situating the work in related literature and explaining design choices.

- Overall performance gains from gaze features are marginal relative to the cost and effort of collecting eye-tracking data (only ~0.6–1.5% additional improvement).  
- Weak theoretical justification linking specific eye-movement patterns to sentiment polarity; features may reflect cognitive effort rather than polarity.  
- Feasibility and deployment scenario are unconvincing—real-time gaze collection (e.g., watching readers on e-commerce sites) is impractical and often redundant.

- How do the proposed cognitive features theoretically map to sentiment polarity rather than general processing difficulty?  
- Have you analyzed whether gaze features simply correlate with the sarcasm/textual features and thus add little unique signal?  
- Is the intended use of gaze data at inference time for each sample, or as a training/annotation aid to build better text-only models for deployment?
