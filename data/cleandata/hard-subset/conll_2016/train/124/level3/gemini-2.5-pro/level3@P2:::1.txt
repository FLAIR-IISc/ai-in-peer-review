### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with "cognitive features" derived from the eye-movement patterns of human readers. The authors hypothesize that gaze patterns can capture signals related to the cognitive processing of complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony, which are challenging for standard sentiment analysis models. They experiment on two datasets containing user-generated text, showing that adding cognitive features to a strong baseline of sentiment and sarcasm-related textual features yields a statistically significant, albeit small, improvement in classification F-score.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Novelty of using gaze data for sentiment analysis:** The primary contribution is the introduction and evaluation of eye-tracking data as a feature source for a high-level NLP task. This is an interesting, interdisciplinary approach that brings a new modality to a well-studied problem and could inspire new research directions.
2.  **Empirical analysis of cognitive features:** The paper provides a detailed empirical study showing that these features can provide a measurable performance lift, particularly for texts containing complex constructs like sarcasm and irony, over a strong feature-based baseline.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea of using cognitive signals from eye-tracking to improve sentiment analysis is highly novel and thought-provoking. It moves beyond purely textual information to incorporate data about the human comprehension process. This approach has the potential to inspire similar investigations in other challenging NLP tasks where text alone is insufficient, such as emotion detection, summarization, or question answering.
2.  **Thorough Experimental Design:** The authors conduct a solid empirical evaluation. They use two distinct datasets, multiple classifiers, and systematically ablate feature sets (Unigram, Sentiment, Sarcasm, Gaze) to clearly demonstrate the incremental contribution of each component. The inclusion of an experiment on a held-out dataset specifically comparing performance on ironic vs. non-ironic text (Table 4) is a strong piece of analysis that directly supports their central hypothesis.
3.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The problem motivation is well-established, the feature engineering is described in detail, and the results are presented clearly, making the work highly accessible and the authors' claims easy to evaluate.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Marginal Performance Gains:** The central claim of the paper rests on the improvement offered by cognitive features, but this improvement is marginal. For the best-performing SVM classifier, adding gaze features (Gz) to the already strong combination of sentiment and sarcasm features (Sn+Sr) results in an F-score increase of only 0.6% on Dataset 1 (from 72.9% to 73.5%) and 1.5% on Dataset 2 (from 70.3% to 71.8%). While this is reported as statistically significant, the practical significance is questionable, especially considering the immense cost and difficulty of collecting eye-tracking data.
2.  **Weak Theoretical Grounding and Confounding Factors:** The paper's motivation for why specific gaze patterns signal sentiment nuances is not deeply established beyond intuition. Many of the features, such as increased fixation duration, saccade length, and regression counts, are well-known correlates of general cognitive load and reading difficulty (e.g., due to complex syntax or low-frequency words). The paper fails to adequately disentangle whether the model is learning signals specific to sarcasm/sentiment processing or simply learning that harder-to-read sentences are more likely to be negative or sarcastic in these particular datasets. The connection needs to be more rigorously established.
3.  **Serious Practicality and Scalability Concerns:** The proposed method requires eye-tracking data for any new text that needs to be classified, which is a major bottleneck. The "Feasibility" section (Section 7) is highly speculative, relying on the future mass-availability of mobile eye-trackers. The proposed application scenario—classifying an online review by observing how other people read it—seems impractical and does not scale. This raises significant questions about the utility of the approach outside of a controlled laboratory setting, positioning it more as an academic curiosity than a deployable solution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The results on the held-out set (Table 4) suggest that gaze features are most helpful for "complex constructs" like irony. Could you provide a more focused feature analysis (similar to Table 3) on just the ironic subset of the data? It would be illuminating to see which specific cognitive features are most discriminative for these cases.
2.  How can you be confident that the gaze features are capturing sentiment-related cognitive load rather than simply acting as a proxy for general reading difficulty? For instance, do the performance gains from gaze features hold if you control for readability scores (e.g., by analyzing subsets of sentences with similar Flesch Readability Ease scores)?
3.  Could you please clarify the intended deployment pipeline for this system? For every new text to be classified (e.g., a new product review), is it necessary to first collect gaze data from a panel of readers? If so, this would severely limit scalability. Or do you envision a model that learns to *predict* gaze features from text alone, which could then be used for the downstream task?
