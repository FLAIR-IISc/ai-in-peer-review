**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach to improve sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection by augmenting traditional text-based features with cognitive features derived from readers' eye-movement data. The authors hypothesize that eye-gaze patterns, which reflect cognitive processing, can help capture the linguistic nuances present in complex texts like sarcastic reviews. They define a set of basic (e.g., fixation duration, regression count) and complex graph-based gaze features. Experiments are conducted on two datasets containing challenging texts using SVM, Naïve Bayes, and Neural Network classifiers. The results show that adding cognitive features to a strong baseline of sentiment and sarcasm features yields a small but statistically significant improvement in classification performance, particularly on texts containing sarcasm and irony.

**Contributions**

1.  **Novelty of Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution of this work is the novel idea of using eye-movement data as a source of features for a core NLP task. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first attempt to systematically extract and apply cognitive features from eye-tracking for sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection, opening a new, interdisciplinary research direction.
2.  **Empirical Demonstration of Benefit for Complex Texts:** The paper provides an empirical validation of its hypothesis on two datasets rich in sarcasm and irony. The analysis on a held-out set (Table 4) specifically demonstrates that the performance gain from cognitive features is more pronounced for complex, ironic texts than for simpler, non-ironic ones, providing targeted evidence for the utility of this approach.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Potential Impact:** The central idea is creative and thought-provoking. By bridging psycholinguistics and NLP, this work has the potential to inspire a new class of models that leverage cognitive signals for other challenging NLP tasks that involve deep understanding, such as abusive language detection, summarization, and question answering. It encourages the community to think beyond purely textual data.
2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the motivation, the different feature sets (sentiment, sarcasm, and gaze), the experimental setup, and the results. This high level of clarity makes the novel concepts accessible and the research easy to appreciate and build upon.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a careful and robust evaluation. They use two distinct datasets, multiple classifiers, and perform ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each feature set. The inclusion of a feature significance analysis (Table 3) and a targeted experiment on ironic vs. non-ironic text (Table 4) strengthens the empirical evidence and provides valuable insights into where and why the proposed features are helpful.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Weak Theoretical Motivation:** The paper's motivation for why specific gaze features should correlate with sentiment polarity is underdeveloped. While it's intuitive that complex or surprising text (like sarcasm) requires more cognitive processing (leading to more regressions, longer fixations, etc.), the paper fails to establish a clear theoretical link between these processing patterns and sentiment *valence* (positive vs. negative). Many of these gaze features are known indicators of general processing difficulty or surprise, which can occur in both positive and negative contexts (e.g., "This movie was surprisingly brilliant" vs. "This movie was surprisingly awful"). The paper conflates the detection of linguistic complexity with the detection of sentiment polarity, and the connection between the two remains unclear.
2.  **Marginal Performance Improvement:** The practical contribution of the cognitive features is minimal. According to Table 2, for the best-performing SVM classifier on Dataset 1, the F-score improves from 72.9% (Sn+Sr) to 73.5% (Sn+Sr+Gz), a gain of only 0.6%. On Dataset 2, the gain is 1.5% (from 70.3% to 71.8%). While statistically significant, these gains are very small in absolute terms. Given the immense practical difficulty and cost of collecting eye-tracking data at scale, such a marginal improvement makes the approach seem more like an academic curiosity than a practical solution for real-world sentiment analysis systems.
3.  **Unconvincing Feasibility and Applicability:** The discussion on the feasibility of the approach in Section 7 is highly speculative and unpersuasive. It hinges on the future mass availability of cheap, accurate mobile eye-trackers, which is far from reality. The proposed application scenario (classifying an Amazon review by observing how users read it) is ill-defined and impractical. It implies that a review can only be classified *after* a sufficient number of users have already read it while being monitored, which is a reactive, limited, and likely slow process. This section fails to address significant hurdles like user privacy, consent at scale, and the calibration/accuracy of consumer-grade trackers.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the theoretical link between your proposed cognitive features and sentiment polarity, as distinct from general processing difficulty? Is there psycholinguistic evidence to suggest that, for example, negative sarcasm elicits systematically different gaze patterns than positive but equally surprising statements?
2.  The performance gains are quite small. Considering the high cost and complexity of acquiring eye-tracking data, could you outline a realistic, high-value application where this marginal improvement would justify deploying such a data-intensive system over a state-of-the-art text-only model?
3.  Your error analysis notes that averaging features across participants may lead to information loss. Have you considered alternative modeling approaches, such as multi-view learning or treating each participant's gaze pattern as a separate training instance, to better exploit the rich, individualized information in the raw gaze data?
