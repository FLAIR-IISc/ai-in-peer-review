- Proposes augmenting text features with eye-tracking derived cognitive features to improve sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection.  
- Defines basic (fixation duration, regression count) and complex graph-based gaze features.  
- Evaluates on two sarcasm-rich datasets using SVM, Naïve Bayes, and Neural Network classifiers.  
- Reports small but statistically significant performance gains from adding cognitive features, especially on ironic/sarcastic texts.  
- Contribution: first systematic use of eye-movement cognitive features for sentiment and sarcasm tasks.  
- Contribution: empirical evidence showing larger gains on complex/ironic texts (held-out analysis).  
- Strength: highly novel, interdisciplinary idea with potential to inspire cognitive-signal augmented NLP.  
- Strength: paper is clearly written and well-structured, making methods and results accessible.  
- Strength: robust experimental setup with two datasets, multiple classifiers, ablation and feature significance analyses.  
- Weakness: limited theoretical motivation linking specific gaze patterns to sentiment valence rather than general processing difficulty.  
- Weakness: marginal absolute performance improvements (e.g., +0.6% and +1.5% F-score), questioning practical value given data collection costs.  
- Weakness: feasibility and applicability discussion is speculative and overlooks privacy, consent, calibration, and practicality issues.  
- Question: please clarify theoretical/psycholinguistic evidence that gaze features correlate with sentiment polarity distinct from processing difficulty.  
- Question: given small gains and high data cost, what realistic, high-value applications justify deploying this approach over text-only models?  
- Question: have you considered alternative modeling (multi-view learning or per-participant instances) to avoid information loss from averaging gaze features?
