This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional linguistic features with "cognitive features" derived from the eye-movement patterns of human readers. The central hypothesis is that the cognitive effort required to understand complex or nuanced text, such as sarcasm, is reflected in gaze patterns (e.g., regressions, fixation durations), and that these patterns can be used as features to improve classification. The authors test this by combining sentiment features, sarcasm-specific features, and a set of basic and complex gaze features. Using SVM, Naïve Bayes, and Neural Network classifiers on two datasets containing challenging texts, they demonstrate that the full feature set outperforms models using only linguistic features, with particularly strong gains on texts containing irony and sarcasm.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction and validation of eye-tracking data as a feature source for a core NLP task like sentiment analysis. This is a novel and creative approach that bridges psycholinguistics and computational linguistics, opening up a new modality of information for tasks where understanding authorial intent and reader interpretation is key. A secondary contribution is the detailed feature engineering and analysis, which not only demonstrates the efficacy of the cognitive features but also provides insight into which specific reading behaviors (e.g., regressions, saccade lengths) are most correlated with complex sentiment. The experiment on a held-out set that explicitly separates simple and complex texts provides strong evidence for this.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of the Core Idea:** The main strength of this paper is its novel proposal to use cognitive signals from eye-tracking to improve sentiment analysis. This moves beyond the purely text-based paradigm that dominates the field. By treating gaze patterns as a proxy for the cognitive load associated with interpreting nuanced language, the authors tap into a rich, orthogonal information source. This is a promising research direction, not just for sentiment analysis, but for other NLP tasks requiring deep semantic and pragmatic understanding.

2.  **Thorough Experimental Design and Analysis:** The authors conduct a well-structured set of experiments to validate their hypothesis. The ablation study presented in Table 2 clearly demonstrates the incremental value of adding sarcasm-specific features and then gaze-based features. The feature significance analysis provides evidence that the cognitive features are indeed informative. Most convincingly, the experiment on the held-out dataset (Table 4) directly tests the core claim—that gaze features are particularly helpful for complex constructs like irony—and the results strongly support it, showing a much larger performance gain on ironic texts than on non-ironic ones.

3.  **Proactive Discussion of Practical Feasibility:** A significant potential critique of this work is its reliance on specialized eye-tracking hardware, which limits its real-world applicability. The authors wisely and directly address this in Section 7 ("Feasibility of our approach"). By discussing the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and plausible application scenarios, they successfully frame the work not as an impractical academic exercise, but as a forward-looking approach that may become widely viable as technology evolves. This demonstrates foresight and strengthens the paper's overall argument.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Conflation of Domains in Datasets:** The datasets used for experimentation mix snippets from multiple genres, including movie reviews, tweets, and quotes. These domains have vastly different linguistic characteristics (e.g., length, formality, use of hashtags/emoticons) and may elicit different reading behaviors independent of sentiment. By training and evaluating on this mixed-domain data, the models may be learning genre-specific patterns rather than pure sentiment-related cognitive signals. This introduces a potential confounding variable that complicates the interpretation of the results. The work would be stronger if the analysis were performed separately for each domain to show whether the contribution of cognitive features is consistent across different text types.

2.  **Limited Context for Absolute Performance:** While the *relative* improvement from adding cognitive features is clearly demonstrated, the absolute F-scores of the best-performing models (~73.5% on D1, ~71.8% on D2) are modest. The authors rightly show in Table 1 that these are challenging datasets for standard classifiers. However, the comparison could be strengthened by benchmarking against more powerful, contemporary (for 2016) non-cognitive baselines on these exact datasets and splits. The current baselines rely on fairly standard feature engineering; a stronger baseline could provide a more realistic measure of the cognitive features' added value.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify if there is any potential overlap between the snippets in Datasets 1 and 2 and the 10,662 snippets used to train the baseline classifiers in Section 3.3? Since both sets of data draw from similar sources (e.g., Pang and Lee movie reviews, tweets), ensuring a strict separation between the data used to establish the baseline performance and the data used for the main cross-validation experiments is critical to the validity of the comparison in Table 1.
