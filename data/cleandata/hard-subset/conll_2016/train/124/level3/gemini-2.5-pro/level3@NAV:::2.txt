**Summary**

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional linguistic feature sets with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The authors hypothesize that eye-movement patterns, serving as proxies for cognitive processing, can help models better handle nuanced linguistic constructs such as sarcasm and irony. They test this by adding a suite of gaze-based features to sentiment and sarcasm classifiers, demonstrating a modest but statistically significant F-score improvement on two datasets specifically chosen for their complexity.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is the novel proposal and validation of using eye-tracking data as a source of cognitive features for sentiment analysis. This opens a new direction for feature engineering in a well-established task, moving beyond purely textual information.
2.  The paper provides a valuable analysis demonstrating that these cognitive features are particularly beneficial for classifying texts with complex constructs like sarcasm, where traditional methods often fall short.

**Strengths**

1.  The core idea of leveraging cognitive proxies for a linguistic task is highly innovative and interesting. It represents a creative, interdisciplinary approach to tackling the subtle challenges of sentiment analysis that plague purely text-based systems.
2.  The discussion and ablation studies comparing the various feature sets (Sentiment, Sarcasm, Gaze) are clear and well-executed. The incremental performance gains shown in Table 2 effectively support the authors' central claim.
3.  The authors proactively address the potential critique of their method's real-world viability. Section 7 provides a thoughtful discussion on the feasibility of collecting gaze data at scale with emerging mobile eye-tracking technology, grounding the research in future applicability.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The most significant methodological weakness is the conflation of text from different domains (movie reviews, tweets, quotes) within the datasets. These domains have vastly different linguistic styles and distributions. Training and evaluating on a mixed corpus may mask domain-specific effects and limit the interpretability of the results. The study would be strengthened by separating the analysis by domain.
2.  While the improvement is statistically significant, the absolute performance results in Table 2 are relatively low compared to state-of-the-art systems on standard benchmarks. Although the datasets are admittedly challenging, a more direct comparison to baselines specifically designed for sarcasm or nuanced sentiment would better contextualize the contribution.
3.  The experimental setup requires a crucial clarification regarding potential data leakage. It is unclear if there might be overlapping text snippets between the external corpora used for training the baseline systems (Section 3.3) and the eye-tracking datasets used for testing.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you clarify whether you ensured there was no overlap in the specific text snippets used in the large training corpus (from Pang & Lee, Sentiment140) and those present in your two test datasets? Any overlap would compromise the validity of the baseline comparisons.
