**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with "cognitive features" derived from the eye-movement patterns of human readers. The central hypothesis is that cognitive signals, as captured by eye-tracking, can help models better understand linguistic nuances like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations, which are challenging for systems relying solely on textual cues. The authors engineer a set of basic and complex gaze-based features (e.g., fixation duration, regression counts, graph-based metrics) and integrate them into SVM, Naïve Bayes, and Neural Network classifiers. They evaluate their approach on two datasets containing a mix of reviews and tweets, demonstrating that the inclusion of cognitive features improves F1-score by up to 9.3% over a baseline with only sentiment features, and shows particular effectiveness on texts with complex ironic or sarcastic constructs.

**Main Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution of this work is the novel application of eye-tracking data as a direct feature source for the task of sentiment analysis. This introduces a new, cognitively-grounded signal to a classic NLP problem and demonstrates its potential to capture subtleties beyond the reach of standard textual features.
2.  The paper provides a detailed empirical validation of this approach. Through systematic feature ablation studies and a targeted analysis on a held-out set of complex vs. simple texts, the authors provide convincing evidence that cognitive features are not just a proxy for reading difficulty but specifically aid in the classification of nuanced language like sarcasm.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Interesting Research Direction:** The core idea of leveraging cognitive signals from eye-tracking to improve a downstream NLP task is highly innovative. It bridges the gap between psycholinguistics and computational linguistics in a practical, feature-driven way. This work opens up a compelling new direction for research where human processing signals can be used to enrich machine understanding of language.
2.  **Thorough and Clear Experimental Analysis:** The authors conduct a well-structured set of experiments. The incremental addition of feature sets (Sentiment, Sarcasm, Gaze) in Table 2 clearly isolates and demonstrates the contribution of each component. Furthermore, the experiment on a held-out dataset (Table 4) directly tests the central hypothesis—that gaze features are particularly helpful for complex constructs like irony—and provides strong supporting evidence. The feature significance analysis in Table 3 further reinforces the importance of the proposed cognitive features.
3.  **Proactive Discussion of Practicality:** A major and obvious question regarding this work is its real-world feasibility. The authors address this head-on in Section 7, discussing the potential of emerging mobile eye-tracking technology. While the approach is not immediately scalable, this discussion shows foresight and helps frame the work as a forward-looking exploration of what may become possible, rather than ignoring a critical limitation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Conflation of Data Domains:** The datasets used for evaluation are a mixture of movie reviews, tweets, and quotes. These domains have vastly different stylistic and structural properties (e.g., length, formality, use of hashtags). By training and testing on a conflated dataset, the results become difficult to interpret. It is unclear whether the cognitive features are more beneficial for short, informal tweets or longer, more structured reviews. A domain-separated analysis would provide much stronger and more fine-grained insights into the utility of the proposed features.
2.  **Modest Absolute Performance Gains over Strong Baseline:** While the improvements are shown to be statistically significant, the absolute F-score gain from adding gaze features (Gz) on top of the already strong sentiment and sarcasm feature set (Sn+Sr) is relatively small: 0.6% for Dataset 1 and 1.5% for Dataset 2 with the best SVM classifier. Given the extremely high cost and complexity of collecting eye-tracking data, this raises questions about the cost-benefit trade-off for practical applications.
3.  **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The authors explicitly state they do not compare against deep learning models, which represent the state-of-the-art for many sentiment analysis tasks. While this choice is justified by the feature-engineering focus of the paper, it makes it difficult to assess the overall competitiveness of the proposed method. The reported F-scores (around 72-73%) are reasonable for difficult, nuanced datasets but their standing relative to modern neural architectures remains unknown.
4.  **Minor Presentation Issue:** The excessive use of bold formatting in the feature significance analysis (Table 3) and elsewhere is distracting and makes the tables more difficult to read.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a performance breakdown by the original data source (e.g., movie reviews vs. tweets) within your datasets? This would be very helpful in understanding which linguistic styles benefit most from the inclusion of cognitive features.
2.  The baseline systems in Section 3.3 are trained on external corpora (Pang & Lee, Sentiment140) and tested on Datasets 1 and 2. The main experiments in Section 5, however, use 10-fold cross-validation within Datasets 1 and 2. Could you clarify if there is any overlap between the snippets in the external corpora and those in your two datasets? This is important for ensuring that the difficulty of the datasets (as established by the baseline performance) is not overestimated.
