- Proposes augmenting sentiment analysis with "cognitive features" from readers' eye-movement patterns to capture cognitive effort for nuanced text (e.g., sarcasm).  
- Hypothesis: gaze patterns (regressions, fixation durations) reflect difficulty interpreting complex sentiment and can improve classification.  
- Method: combine sentiment, sarcasm-specific, basic and complex gaze features; evaluate with SVM, Naïve Bayes, and neural nets on two challenging datasets.  
- Result: full feature set outperforms linguistic-only models, with larger gains on texts containing irony and sarcasm.  
- Primary contribution: novel use and validation of eye-tracking data as a feature source for a core NLP task.  
- Secondary contribution: detailed feature engineering and analysis identifying which reading behaviors (e.g., regressions, saccade lengths) correlate with complex sentiment.  
- Strength — Novelty: introduces a promising, orthogonal cognitive signal modality for NLP tasks requiring pragmatic understanding.  
- Strength — Experimental design: well-structured experiments, ablation study, and held-out test showing cognitive features help especially on ironic texts.  
- Strength — Practical feasibility discussion: addresses concerns about eye-tracker availability and outlines plausible future application scenarios.  
- Weakness — Mixed-domain datasets: combining movie reviews, tweets, and quotes may confound genre-specific reading behaviors with sentiment-related cognitive signals.  
- Weakness — Limited absolute performance context: relative gains shown, but absolute F-scores are modest and stronger contemporary non-cognitive baselines are needed.  
- Question for authors: clarify whether there is any overlap between the study datasets and the 10,662 snippets used to train baseline classifiers to ensure strict data separation.
