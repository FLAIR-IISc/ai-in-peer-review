- Introduces using eye-tracking–derived "cognitive features" to augment text features for sentiment analysis, targeting sarcasm/irony.  
- Tests classifiers with text, sarcasm-specific, and gaze features on two datasets (movie reviews + tweets).  
- Adding gaze features yields up to 3.7% and 9.3% F-score improvements on the two datasets, with larger gains on ironic text (6.1% vs 2.1%).  
- Main contribution: novel application of eye-tracking data, bridging psycholinguistics and NLP for subtle language understanding.  
- Main contribution: systematic empirical evaluation showing cognitive features improve performance and rank highly in feature importance.  
- Strength: highly innovative and well-motivated linking of eye movements (fixations/regressions) to cognitive processing of complex text.  
- Strength: clear experimental design with incremental feature ablation and informative feature-significance analysis.  
- Strength: proactive discussion of real-world feasibility (mobile eye-trackers) and plausible application scenarios.  
- Weakness: conflates distinct domains (tweets vs movie reviews), which obscures domain-specific benefits of cognitive features.  
- Weakness: unclear comparison to modern state-of-the-art; reported F-scores seem low and baselines may be outdated.  
- Weakness: possible overlap between baseline training corpora and test snippets could invalidate baseline comparisons—needs clarification.  
- Question: please confirm whether any exact instance-level overlap exists between baseline training data and Dataset 1/2.  
- Question: have you evaluated models separately by domain (tweets vs reviews) to assess domain-specific contributions of gaze features?  
- Additional comment: paper is well-written but overuse of bolding is distracting; use bold sparingly.
