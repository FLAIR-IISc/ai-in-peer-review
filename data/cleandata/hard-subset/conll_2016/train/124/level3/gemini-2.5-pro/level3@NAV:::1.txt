**Summary of the paper:**
This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection by augmenting traditional text-based features with "cognitive features" derived from the eye-movement patterns of human readers. The authors hypothesize that gaze patterns can capture cognitive processes related to understanding complex linguistic nuances. They combine standard sentiment features, sarcasm-specific features, and a comprehensive set of gaze-based features, showing that the combined feature set achieves a statistically significant improvement in classification F-score on two public datasets.

The main contribution of this work is the novel proposal to use eye-movement data as a direct feature source for a core NLP task. This introduces a new modality of information that is distinct from purely textual analysis and could inspire new research directions in multimodal NLP.

**Strengths:**
1.  The primary strength is the novelty of the core idea. Applying cognitive signals from eye-tracking as features for sentiment analysis is an original concept that bridges psycholinguistics and computational linguistics in a practical way, potentially inspiring work on other NLP tasks.
2.  The paper is very well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining their feature sets, experimental setup, and results, which facilitates reproducibility and understanding.
3.  The work demonstrates that cognitive features offer the most benefit for classifying complex texts containing sarcasm and irony. The held-out experiment shows a more substantial 6.1% F-score improvement on ironic texts, highlighting a promising niche for this approach.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The main weakness is the marginal overall performance gain. The improvement from adding cognitive features over a strong textual baseline (Sn+Sr) is very small (0.6% to 1.5% F-score). While statistically significant, this minor boost questions the practical value of the expensive and complex data collection process required for eye-tracking.
2.  The motivation for why specific gaze features should capture sentiment polarity, beyond general reading difficulty, is not well-established. The paper lacks a strong theoretical link, and the analysis does not clearly disentangle signals related to sentiment from those related to general syntactic or lexical complexity.
3.  The feasibility discussion in Section 7 is unconvincing. The envisioned application of using ubiquitous mobile eye-trackers to analyze online reviews is highly speculative and fails to address substantial practical hurdles regarding privacy, user consent, and scalability. The marginal performance gain does not justify the proposed real-world implementation.
