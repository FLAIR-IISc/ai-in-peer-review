- Summary: Proposes augmenting text-based sentiment analysis with eye-tracking-derived cognitive features (fixations, regressions, graph metrics) and shows up to 9.3% F1 improvement across two mixed datasets using SVM, Naïve Bayes, and neural classifiers, especially on ironic/sarcastic texts.  
- Contribution 1: Introduces eye-tracking data as novel, cognitively grounded features for sentiment analysis.  
- Contribution 2: Provides empirical validation including ablation studies and held-out tests showing gaze features aid detection of nuanced language beyond reading difficulty.  
- Strength 1: Highly novel interdisciplinary idea linking psycholinguistics and NLP, opening a new research direction.  
- Strength 2: Thorough, clear experiments that isolate feature contributions and analyze performance on complex vs. simple texts.  
- Strength 3: Proactive discussion of practical feasibility and future mobile eye-tracking applicability.  
- Weakness 1: Mixed-domain datasets (reviews, tweets, quotes) confound interpretation; domain-separated analysis is needed.  
- Weakness 2: Absolute F1 gains over strong baselines are modest (0.6%–1.5% in best cases), raising cost–benefit concerns given eye-tracking expense.  
- Weakness 3: No comparison to contemporary deep learning state-of-the-art, leaving competitiveness unclear.  
- Weakness 4: Presentation issue—overuse of bolding in tables hurts readability.  
- Question 1: Please provide performance breakdowns by original data source (movie reviews vs. tweets).  
- Question 2: Clarify whether external corpora used for baselines overlap with snippets in Datasets 1 and 2 to ensure baseline difficulty is not overstated.
