### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that the eye-movement patterns of human readers can capture subtle linguistic nuances, such as sarcasm and irony, which are often challenging for systems relying solely on textual information. The authors engineer a set of cognitive features based on fixations, saccades, and regressions, including more complex features derived from a "gaze-saliency graph." They evaluate their enhanced feature set on two datasets containing sentiment-annotated short texts, demonstrating that the inclusion of cognitive features improves classification performance, particularly on texts with complex constructs.

### Contributions

1.  **Novel Feature Set for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution of this work is the introduction and evaluation of a new class of features for sentiment analysis derived from human cognitive processing, specifically eye-tracking data. The paper presents a systematic approach to engineering these features, categorizing them into basic gaze statistics (e.g., fixation duration, regression count) and more complex graph-based properties. This represents a novel direction for feature-based sentiment analysis.

2.  **Empirical Analysis of Cognitive Features for Complex Text:** The paper provides an empirical demonstration that cognitive features are particularly effective for correctly classifying texts containing complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony. Through a targeted experiment on a held-out dataset, the authors show that the performance gain from cognitive features is substantially larger for ironic texts than for simpler, non-ironic ones. This analysis lends support to the hypothesis that gaze patterns capture signals of "surprise" or re-analysis that are characteristic of such language.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea of using cognitive signals as direct features for a high-level NLP task is highly innovative and thought-provoking. While eye-tracking is a staple of psycholinguistic research, its application as a feature source to improve a machine learning classifier is underexplored. This work could inspire a new line of research in other NLP tasks where subtle human interpretation is key, such as detecting fake news, analyzing figurative language, or assessing text quality.

2.  **Thorough Feature Engineering and Analysis:** The authors do not simply use raw gaze data but instead conduct a thorough exploration of feature engineering. They propose a diverse range of features, from simple statistics to novel graph-based features derived from "gaze-saliency graphs." This detailed feature design is a valuable component of the work. Furthermore, the inclusion of a feature significance analysis (Table 3) and a targeted experiment on complex vs. simple texts (Table 4) adds depth to the results and helps validate the authors' claims about where these features are most useful.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The motivation, methodology, and experiments are explained logically, making the work accessible and its contributions understandable. The authors do a good job of situating their work within the existing literature and explaining the intuition behind their proposed features.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Marginal Performance Improvement and High Cost:** The primary weakness is that the performance improvement gained by adding the proposed cognitive features is marginal, especially when considering the high cost and effort of collecting eye-tracking data. Looking at the results for the best-performing SVM classifier in Table 2, the addition of sarcasm features (Sn+Sr) over the sentiment baseline (Sn) provides a substantial F-score boost (from 69.8 to 72.9 on Dataset 1, and from 62.5 to 70.3 on Dataset 2). However, the subsequent addition of the gaze features (Sn+Sr+Gz) only provides a further increase of 0.6% on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2. While this gain is reported as statistically significant, it raises serious questions about the practical utility and cost-benefit trade-off of the proposed method.

2.  **Weak Theoretical Link between Gaze and Sentiment Polarity:** The paper's motivation for why specific eye-movement patterns should correlate with sentiment *polarity* (positive vs. negative) is not well-established. The authors plausibly argue that these features capture processing difficulty associated with constructs like sarcasm. However, it is not clear why, for example, a higher regression count or a specific graph edge density would systematically indicate a negative sentiment over a positive one, or vice-versa. Many of the features seem to be proxies for "cognitive effort" or "surprise," which are not inherently tied to polarity. A sentence can be syntactically complex and positive (e.g., a flowery, elaborate compliment) or complex and negative (e.g., sarcasm). The paper would be much stronger if it provided a clearer theoretical or empirical justification for the direct link between the proposed features and the final classification target of positive/negative sentiment.

3.  **Unconvincing Feasibility and Application Scenario:** The discussion in Section 7 regarding the feasibility of the approach is unconvincing. The application scenario described in Section 7.2—where a system watches readers on an e-commerce site to determine review polarity—is particularly problematic. If a human is already reading the review to provide the gaze data, they have already interpreted its sentiment, making the automatic system redundant for that instance. A more compelling use case would be to leverage gaze data from a small panel of readers to help train a more robust *text-only* model that could then be deployed at scale without needing gaze data at inference time. The current framing suggests a real-time requirement for gaze data that seems impractical and does not offer a clear advantage over the human-in-the-loop.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the link between the proposed cognitive features and sentiment *polarity*? Is there an underlying assumption that negative sentiments (or specific types like sarcasm) are inherently more "cognitively complex" to process than positive ones? Or are the features primarily capturing sarcasm, which in your datasets happens to be mostly negative, and would thus not generalize to other forms of complex but positive sentiment?

2.  Given the substantial improvement from adding sarcasm-specific textual features (Sr) and the much smaller subsequent improvement from adding gaze features (Gz), have you analyzed the correlation between the most predictive gaze features and the sarcasm features? Is it possible that the gaze features are simply a noisy proxy for the same linguistic phenomena already captured more directly by the textual sarcasm features?

3.  Regarding the feasibility, could you clarify the intended application scenario? Is the primary goal to use gaze data at inference time for every new sample (as the Amazon example seems to suggest), or is it to use gaze data during a training or data annotation phase to build better text-only models or create higher-quality labeled datasets?
