- Paper proposes augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-movement patterns, claiming first use in NLP for sentiment and sarcasm detection.  
- Authors report marginal F-score gains (3.7% and 9.3% on two datasets) when adding cognitive features.  
- Main contributions: novel integration of eye-tracking cognitive features into sentiment analysis.  
- Main contributions: cognitive features particularly help complex constructs like sarcasm/irony (6.1% improvement on held-out data).  
- Main contributions: presents a well-structured experimental evaluation framework.  
- Strength: Exceptionally well-written and easy-to-follow interdisciplinary paper.  
- Strength: Leveraging cognitive features is an interesting idea with potential for other nuanced NLP tasks.  
- Strength: Feature significance analysis suggests cognitive features help specifically with complex sentiment constructs.  
- Weakness: Weak theoretical motivation linking cognitive/eye-movement features to sentiment polarity beyond generic “reading ease.”  
- Weakness: Reported performance gains over strong baseline are marginal (only 0.6–1.5% over Sn+Sr), questioning practical value.  
- Weakness: Feasibility discussion, especially mobile eye-tracking example, is unconvincing and omits issues like consent, privacy, cost, and real-world integration.  
- Overall: Conceptually interesting but falls short on practical utility and fails to sufficiently justify novelty or impact for the NLP community.  
- Questions for authors: How do you justify the small 0.6–1.5% gain given the complexity of collecting/processing eye-tracking data?  
- Questions for authors: What specific cognitive mechanisms link eye-movement patterns to sentiment polarity beyond general reading difficulty?
