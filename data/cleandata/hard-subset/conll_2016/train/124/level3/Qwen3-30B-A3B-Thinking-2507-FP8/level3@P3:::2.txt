

# Review for "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

## Summary
This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of readers. The authors argue that eye-tracking data can serve as cognitive proxies for capturing linguistic nuances, particularly for complex sentiment constructs like sarcasm and irony. They introduce a comprehensive feature set combining traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/irony features, and cognitive features from eye-tracking data, demonstrating improved performance over traditional approaches.

## Main Contributions
1. Introduction of cognitive features from eye-tracking data as novel proxies for sentiment analysis, addressing challenges at lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic levels.
2. Comprehensive feature set design that combines traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/irony features, and cognitive features, with rigorous evaluation across multiple classifiers and datasets.
3. Demonstration that cognitive features significantly improve performance specifically for complex sentiment constructs (irony/sarcasm), with 6.1% relative improvement on held-out data.

## Strengths
1. **Novel Cognitive Approach**: The core idea of using eye-tracking as a cognitive proxy for sentiment analysis is genuinely novel and well-motivated. The authors provide strong theoretical grounding in psycholinguistics (citing Parasuraman & Rizzo, 2006) and clearly explain how eye-movement patterns correlate with cognitive processing of sentiment nuances. This represents a meaningful departure from conventional linguistic feature-based approaches.

2. **Clear Feature Comparison and Analysis**: The paper excels in systematically comparing different feature combinations and providing statistical significance analysis. The feature significance analysis (Table 3) showing that gaze-based features dominate the top-ranked features for both datasets provides strong evidence for the value of cognitive features. The held-out dataset experiment (Table 4) specifically demonstrating 6.1% improvement for complex constructs is particularly compelling.

3. **Thoughtful Feasibility Discussion**: Section 7 provides a realistic and forward-looking assessment of the practical feasibility of the approach. The discussion of mobile eye-trackers, applicability scenarios, and user consent considerations is well-considered and addresses potential criticisms regarding implementation challenges.

## Weaknesses
1. **Domain Conflation in Datasets**: The paper uses datasets that conflate movie reviews and tweets (Datasets 1 and 2), which is problematic. Sentiment analysis performance can be highly domain-dependent, and combining domains likely reduces the effectiveness of the feature set. Separating these domains would improve both training effectiveness and interpretability, and the authors should address this limitation.

2. **Performance Contextualization**: The results in Table 2 appear modest compared to the Pang & Lee (2004) state-of-the-art, which typically achieves 80-85% F-score on movie reviews. The authors should better contextualize their results by explicitly comparing to Twitter-specific sentiment analysis benchmarks (which typically have lower performance) and explaining why their approach is more reasonable in this context.

3. **Potential Data Leakage Concerns**: The paper does not clarify whether there are overlapping snippets between training and test sets in Datasets 1 and 2 (even if from the same sources). Given the small size of the datasets (994 and 1059 snippets), this is a critical issue that could inflate performance metrics. The authors should explicitly confirm whether data leakage is possible.

4. **Formatting Issue**: The excessive use of bold formatting throughout the paper is distracting and unprofessional, detracting from the content presentation.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you confirm whether there are any overlapping snippets between training and test sets in Datasets 1 and 2, even if from the same source? If so, please clarify how this was handled.

2. The results in Table 2 show modest improvements compared to Pang & Lee (2004). How do you explain this relative performance, and why is it more reasonable when compared to Twitter data specifically? Please provide a more detailed comparison to Twitter-specific sentiment analysis benchmarks.

3. Would you consider re-evaluating your approach by separating the movie review and tweet domains in your datasets to improve training effectiveness and interpretability? How might this affect your conclusions about cognitive features?
