

This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns, claiming to be the first to do so in NLP. The main contributions are: (1) a comprehensive feature set combining traditional sentiment features with cognitive features from eye-tracking data, (2) empirical evidence demonstrating marginal improvements in sentiment classification performance (particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm), and (3) a detailed analysis showing cognitive features are particularly effective for text with linguistic nuances.

Strengths: The paper presents a well-structured and clearly written approach to sentiment analysis, making it accessible to readers. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the challenges in sentiment analysis at lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic levels, establishing a strong foundation for their work. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, including multiple classifiers, two distinct datasets, and a held-out dataset specifically designed to test performance on complex sentiment constructs. The feature significance analysis (Table 3) convincingly demonstrates that cognitive features are among the top-ranked features for both datasets, with 10 out of top 20 features being gaze-based for Dataset 1. The paper's most significant strength is its demonstration that cognitive features specifically improve performance on complex sentiment constructs (6.1% improvement on irony vs. 2.1% on simple texts), suggesting these features capture nuances beyond traditional linguistic features.

Weaknesses: The primary weakness is the weak motivation for why cognitive features should improve sentiment analysis beyond reading ease. The authors suggest cognitive processes relate to eye-movement activities, but fail to provide a convincing theoretical link between specific eye-movement patterns and sentiment processing. The marginal performance gains (0.6% and 1.5% improvement when adding cognitive features to the best baseline) are not compelling for such a novel approach, especially when compared to state-of-the-art deep learning methods that could potentially achieve larger improvements. The feasibility discussion (Section 7.2) is particularly unconvincing, as the e-commerce example fails to demonstrate clear benefits that outweigh the practical challenges of implementing eye-tracking in real-world applications. The authors do not adequately address whether the cognitive features could be approximated through other means, making the requirement for eye-tracking data seem unnecessarily restrictive. The paper also fails to compare against recent deep learning approaches that automatically learn features from text, which would provide a more meaningful benchmark for their feature-based approach.

Questions to Authors: 
1. How would you address the weak theoretical connection between eye-movement patterns and sentiment processing? Could you provide specific examples of how particular eye-movement patterns relate to specific sentiment phenomena (e.g., sarcasm, implicit sentiment)?
2. Given the marginal performance gains (only 0.6% and 1.5% improvement when adding cognitive features), how do you justify the additional complexity and data requirements of collecting eye-tracking data compared to standard sentiment analysis approaches?
3. Why didn't you compare against deep learning approaches that have achieved state-of-the-art results in sentiment analysis, such as the CNN-based methods by Maas et al. (2011) or RNN-based methods by dos Santos and Gatti (2014)?

The paper presents an interesting idea with potential for future work, but the current implementation lacks sufficient justification for the claimed benefits. The marginal improvements and weak theoretical foundation undermine the significance of the contribution. While the feature set is comprehensive and the evaluation thorough, the paper would benefit from a stronger theoretical motivation, more compelling performance gains, and a more convincing feasibility discussion. The authors should also consider whether their cognitive features could be learned from other sources without requiring eye-tracking data, which would significantly enhance the practical applicability of their approach.
