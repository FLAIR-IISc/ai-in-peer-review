1. Proposes augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive (eye-tracking) features alongside traditional features.  
2. Reports empirical gains from cognitive features, notably a 6.1% improvement on held-out complex-construct data.  
3. Shows cognitive features frequently rank among top features in feature significance analysis.

4. Clear, well-structured experiments with appropriate baselines, multiple classifiers, two datasets, and statistical significance testing (McNemar’s test, p=0.0001).  
5. Convincing feature significance results (chi-squared) showing cognitive features appear among top features for both datasets.  
6. Novel and potentially influential idea that bridges cognitive science and NLP, with applicability beyond sentiment analysis.

7. Weak theoretical motivation for why eye-movement patterns should correlate with sentiment nuances; explanation reduced to "reading ease."  
8. Marginal gains specifically attributable to cognitive features (0.6–1.5% F-score), raising concerns about cost-benefit given eye-tracking complexity.  
9. Unconvincing feasibility and adoption discussion (e.g., unrealistic examples and unsupported claims about mobile eye-tracker uptake).

10. How does this work differ fundamentally from Mishra et al. (2016) using eye-tracking for sarcasm understandability?  
11. What are the estimated computational and data-collection costs of using eye-tracking versus standard sentiment methods given marginal gains?  
12. Can you provide error analysis showing which types of sarcasm/irony benefit most from cognitive features?  
13. How do you address that derived features (FDUR, FC, SL) may reflect general reading difficulty rather than sentiment-specific signals?
