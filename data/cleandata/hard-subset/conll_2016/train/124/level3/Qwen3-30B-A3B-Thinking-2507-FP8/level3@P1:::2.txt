

This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of human readers. The authors argue that traditional linguistic features struggle with complex sentiment constructs like sarcasm and irony, and that eye-tracking data can provide additional cognitive insights to improve performance. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) introducing cognitive features from eye-movement patterns as a novel approach to sentiment analysis, particularly for complex constructs; (2) demonstrating significant performance improvements (3.7% and 9.3% F-score increases) over traditional feature sets on two datasets; and (3) providing detailed evidence that cognitive features specifically benefit sentiment analysis of complex constructs like sarcasm (6.1% improvement on held-out irony data).

Strengths:
1. The paper makes a compelling case for cognitive features as a solution to the well-documented challenges of sentiment analysis for complex constructs. The authors clearly articulate why traditional features fail for sarcasm and irony (e.g., "sentiment expressed implicitly," "presence of sarcasm and other forms of irony"), and provide strong evidence that their cognitive features specifically address these challenges, with 6.1% improvement on irony-containing texts versus only 2.1% on simpler texts.

2. The experimental design is robust, including statistical significance testing (McNemar test, p=0.0001), feature significance analysis showing 10/20 top features for Dataset 1 are gaze-based, and careful analysis of the held-out dataset specifically for complex constructs. The paper also thoroughly addresses feasibility concerns in Section 7, discussing mobile eye-trackers, application scenarios, and user consent, which is crucial for real-world adoption.

3. The paper provides a comprehensive feature set that carefully integrates traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/irony features, and cognitive features, demonstrating the additive value of each component (e.g., adding sarcasm features increases accuracy by 3.1% for Dataset 1, then adding cognitive features provides another 0.6% improvement).

Weaknesses:
1. The datasets (D1 and D2) conflate domains (movie reviews and tweets), which creates significant domain confusion. The paper's results could be substantially improved and more interpretable if the authors separated these domains, as sentiment polarity and linguistic patterns differ significantly between movie reviews and social media text. This domain conflation is a major limitation for generalizability.

2. The performance results (Table 2) appear relatively low compared to state-of-the-art sentiment analysis systems. While the authors note this is for Twitter data, the F-scores of 69.6 for SVM on Dataset 1 are substantially lower than what would be expected from modern sentiment analysis systems on similar data (e.g., Pang & Lee 2004 achieves ~70-80% F1 on similar Twitter data). This suggests potential issues with the baseline comparison or dataset characteristics.

3. There's a significant risk of data leakage between training and test sets. The training data is drawn from Pang & Lee (2004) and Sentiment 140, while the test datasets (D1 and D2) contain snippets from similar sources (e.g., "reviews, normalized-tweets and quotes"). The authors should clarify whether there are overlapping snippets between training and test sets, as this would invalidate the results.

4. The use of a low-quality eye-tracker (Tobii T120, 120Hz sampling) for Dataset 2 may compromise the reliability of the cognitive features. The paper notes Dataset 2 uses "low quality" eye-tracking, yet reports significant improvements with cognitive features on this dataset. The authors should address how the lower-quality data affects the cognitive feature extraction.

Questions to Authors:
1. Are there overlapping snippets between the training data (Pang & Lee 2004 and Sentiment 140) and the test datasets (D1 and D2), even if from similar sources? If so, how were they handled?

2. Given the domain conflation in the datasets (movie reviews and tweets), how would the authors recommend handling domain-specific sentiment analysis in practice, and how would they separate the effects of domain versus cognitive features?
