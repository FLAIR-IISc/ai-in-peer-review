- Proposal: Augment traditional sentiment-analysis features with cognitive features derived from readers' eye-movement patterns to better capture linguistic nuances like sarcasm and irony.  
- Contribution 1: Introduces eye-tracking-based cognitive features as novel proxies addressing lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic sentiment challenges.  
- Contribution 2: Presents a comprehensive feature set combining traditional, sarcasm/irony, and cognitive features with evaluation across multiple classifiers and datasets.  
- Contribution 3: Shows cognitive features improve performance on complex constructs (irony/sarcasm), reporting a 6.1% relative gain on held-out data.  
- Strength 1: The cognitive/eye-tracking approach is novel, well-motivated, and grounded in psycholinguistics linking gaze patterns to sentiment processing.  
- Strength 2: Strong experimental analysis with systematic feature comparisons and statistical significance; gaze-based features rank highly in feature importance.  
- Strength 3: Thoughtful practical feasibility discussion covering mobile eye-trackers, application scenarios, and user consent considerations.  
- Weakness 1: Datasets conflate movie reviews and tweets, risking domain mismatch that can hurt training effectiveness and interpretability.  
- Weakness 2: Reported results seem modest relative to Pang & Lee (2004) movie-review benchmarks and need contextualization against Twitter-specific baselines.  
- Weakness 3: Possible data leakage not addressed—overlap between training and test snippets could inflate performance given small dataset sizes.  
- Weakness 4: Excessive bold formatting throughout the paper is distracting and unprofessional.  
- Question 1: Are there any overlapping snippets between training and test sets in Datasets 1 and 2, and how was this handled?  
- Question 2: Please explain the modest results versus Pang & Lee (2004) and provide comparisons to Twitter-specific sentiment benchmarks.  
- Question 3: Would separating movie reviews and tweets for re-evaluation improve training effectiveness and change conclusions about cognitive features?
