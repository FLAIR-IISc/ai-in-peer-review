- Proposes augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-movement patterns and provides experiments on two datasets (994 and 1059 snippets).
- Claims to be the first use of cognitive (gaze) features in NLP for sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection.
- Contribution: releases a novel eye-tracking dataset for sentiment annotation (though originally collected for other purposes).
- Contribution: demonstrates marginal performance improvements when adding cognitive features to traditional systems.
- Contribution: explores gaze-based features as a potential direction for cognitive-informed NLP research.
- Strength: paper is well-structured and clearly written, with a logical flow and accessible explanations.
- Strength: dataset collection is valuable and could support future research on cognitive aspects of language processing.
- Strength: conceptual direction of leveraging cognitive features for NLP is interesting and potentially inspiring for other tasks.
- Weakness: motivation is weak—no convincing theoretical basis that eye movements specifically capture sentiment rather than general reading difficulty.
- Weakness: performance gains are minimal (0.6% and 1.5% for the two datasets; 0.5% F-score difference is practically negligible).
- Weakness: feasibility discussion for real-world deployment of eye-tracking is unconvincing and lacks evidence on user consent, cost, accuracy, or adoption.
- Question: how reliable are the claimed gains on complex constructs given only 131 complex examples in the held-out set?
- Question: no concrete examples or evidence of how cognitive features would transfer to other NLP tasks like emotion analysis or summarization.
- Question: feature significance shows only half of top features are gaze-based—how do the authors explain strong Sn+Sr performance without gaze and the marginal added benefit of gaze features?
