- Proposes using cognitive features from eye-movement patterns to improve sentiment analysis, especially for sarcasm and irony.  
- Reports significant performance gains: +3.7% and +9.3% F-score over traditional feature sets on two datasets.  
- Shows cognitive features give targeted benefit on complex constructs (6.1% improvement on held-out irony data).

- Argues cognitive features address failure modes of traditional features for sarcasm/irony, with empirical evidence separating complex vs. simple text gains.  
- Uses robust experimental design with statistical significance testing, feature importance analysis, and held-out evaluation for complex constructs.  
- Presents a comprehensive feature set integrating traditional, sarcasm/irony, and cognitive features, showing additive improvements.

- Datasets conflate domains (movie reviews and tweets), causing domain confusion that limits generalizability and interpretability.  
- Overall performance appears low relative to state-of-the-art baselines, raising concerns about baseline choices or dataset characteristics.  
- Potential risk of data leakage between training and test sets due to overlapping sources (Pang & Lee 2004, Sentiment140)—needs clarification.  
- Reliability of cognitive features may be compromised by low-quality eye-tracking hardware (Tobii T120, 120Hz) used for Dataset 2.

- Are there overlapping snippets between the training data and test datasets, and how were overlaps handled?  
- How should practitioners handle domain-specific sentiment differences, and how can domain effects be separated from cognitive feature benefits?
