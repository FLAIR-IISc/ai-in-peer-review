- Augments sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking to capture sarcasm, irony, and implicit sentiment.
- Experiments on two datasets show F-score gains of 3.7% and 9.3% when adding gaze features.
- Proposes diverse gaze features including fixation durations, regression counts, and graph-based saliency.
- Chi-squared significance analysis ranks gaze-based features among the most informative for complex constructs.
- Novel contribution: first known integration of eye-tracking data into sentiment analysis.
- Empirically rigorous: evaluates multiple classifiers, feature combinations, and reports statistical significance.
- Specifically targets hard cases like sarcasm and thwarted expectations that traditional features struggle with.
- Discusses practical applications and the potential of mobile eye-tracking for e-commerce and online reviews.
- Scalability concern: dependence on eye-tracking data limits real-world practicality; mobile solutions remain speculative.
- Over-reliance on handcrafted features may hinder generalization compared to end-to-end deep learning approaches.
- Datasets are relatively small and not diverse enough, limiting broad generalizability.
- Error analysis is shallow and does not provide actionable remedies.
- Question: how can gaze-based features be integrated where eye-tracking data is unavailable—can proxies or synthetic features be used?
- Question: have the authors tried combining gaze features with deep models (e.g., attention) to improve scalability and generalization?
- Question: how robust are gaze features to noisy or lower-quality eye-tracking devices?
- Overall: novel and promising work that introduces a new dimension to SA but faces practical scalability and generalizability limitations.
