## Review of "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

This paper explores augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The core idea is that readers’ eye-movement patterns reveal cognitive processes related to sentiment understanding, particularly for nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The authors demonstrate improvements in F-score (up to 9.3%) on two datasets by incorporating these cognitive features alongside standard linguistic features, using SVM and Naive Bayes classifiers.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the *novel application of eye-tracking data to enhance sentiment analysis*. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistic studies of sentiment, this paper is the first, to the authors’ knowledge, to directly integrate these cognitive features into a statistical sentiment classification pipeline. A second, supporting contribution is the *demonstration of the effectiveness of these cognitive features*, particularly in handling complex constructs like sarcasm. The feature significance analysis and results on a held-out dataset support this claim. Finally, the *creation of a comprehensive feature set* combining traditional, sarcasm-related, cognitive, and readability features is a valuable contribution, providing a strong baseline for future research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty:** The integration of eye-tracking data into a standard sentiment analysis framework is a genuinely novel approach. This opens up a new avenue for incorporating cognitive insights into NLP tasks.
2. **Empirical Results:** The reported performance improvements, particularly the 9.3% increase on Dataset 2, are significant and demonstrate the potential of the proposed method. The statistical significance testing further strengthens these findings.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors have thoughtfully curated a diverse feature set, covering various aspects of text and cognitive processing. This allows for a more nuanced analysis of sentiment.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The feature significance analysis provides valuable insights into which cognitive features are most informative for sentiment detection, aiding in understanding the underlying mechanisms.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors proactively address the practical challenges of obtaining eye-tracking data, discussing the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Dependency:** The method relies heavily on the availability of eye-tracking data, which is expensive and time-consuming to collect. The generalizability of the findings to scenarios without eye-tracking data is limited.
2. **Complexity of Cognitive Features:** The derivation of cognitive features, particularly the graph-based features, is complex and may not be easily replicable. Further clarification on the implementation details would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Classifier Exploration:** While SVM and Naive Bayes are standard baselines, exploring more advanced classifiers (e.g., deep learning models) could potentially yield further improvements and reveal the limitations of the proposed feature set.
4. **Dataset Size:** The datasets used are relatively small, which may limit the statistical power of the results. Larger datasets would provide more robust evidence for the effectiveness of the method.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the specific implementation of the gaze-saliency graph construction and the rationale behind choosing weighted degree as the feature?
2. How sensitive are the results to the quality of the eye-tracking data (e.g., sampling rate, calibration accuracy)?
3. Have you considered any methods for reducing the dimensionality of the cognitive feature space, given the potentially high number of features?
