- Proposes augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking to capture readers' cognitive processes, especially for sarcasm/irony.  
- Demonstrates up to 9.3% F-score improvement on two datasets by incorporating gaze-based features.  
- Introduces a diverse set of gaze-derived features, including novel graph-based features, integrated into a standard sentiment pipeline.

- Clear motivation that cognitive features can address nuanced sentiment challenges like sarcasm and irony.  
- Combines traditional sentiment features with a rich, well-described set of eye-tracking and reading-difficulty features.  
- Uses rigorous experimental setup: multiple classifiers (NB, SVM, NN), 10-fold CV, and statistical significance testing.  
- Includes a held-out dataset focused on irony/sarcasm to show when cognitive features are most helpful.  
- Provides error analysis, discusses feasibility and limitations, and considers future directions (e.g., mobile eye-trackers).  
- Establishes strong baselines and shows datasets are challenging for existing systems.

- Datasets are relatively small (994 and 843 instances), raising concerns about generalizability.  
- Two datasets use different eye-tracker qualities (500Hz vs 120Hz), a potential confound not fully investigated.  
- Averaging gaze data across participants may lose individual-level cognitive signals.  
- Lacks comparison to or integration discussion with deep learning approaches that dominate sentiment analysis.  
- Gaze-saliency graph description lacks sufficient detail and intuitive examples.

- How sensitive are results to specific eye-tracking features; were feature selection or ablation studies performed?  
- What is the impact of differing eye-tracker qualities (500Hz vs 120Hz) and can normalization mitigate it?  
- Which specific sentiment phenomena (irony, sarcasm, negation) benefit most from cognitive features; can examples be provided?  
- What is the computational cost of extracting gaze-based features and the scalability for real-world use?

- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, with comprehensive related work and references.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations and practical challenges candidly.  
- Empirical results are convincing despite dataset and sensor-quality limitations.

- Overall recommendation: strong paper; accept with minor revisions addressing questions and concerns.
