- Core idea: augment traditional sentiment features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking to improve polarity detection, especially for nuanced text like sarcasm.
- Empirical result: incorporating cognitive features yields up to 9.3% F-score improvement on two datasets.
- Primary contribution: novel integration of eye-tracking data into a feature-based sentiment analysis pipeline.
- Feature engineering: careful design including basic gaze metrics and a graph-based representation of saccade patterns, justified by cognitive principles.
- Evaluation focus: validated on datasets containing sarcasm and irony, addressing constructs that challenge standard sentiment models.
- Strength: thorough related work review, well-defined experimental setup, clear presentation of results.
- Strength: use of statistical significance testing increases credibility.
- Strength: practical feasibility discussion (data acquisition challenges, potential mobile eye-tracker applications) and inclusion of a held-out dataset for complex constructs.
- Weakness: limited dataset sizes raise concerns about generalizability.
- Weakness: insufficient detail on the annotation process and potential annotator biases despite mentioning inter-rater agreement.
- Weakness: lack of a granular analysis identifying which specific cognitive features drive performance gains.
- Weakness: brief mention of future deep-learning work but no discussion of why a feature-based approach was chosen over deep learning.
- Question 1: provide more details about the annotation process, annotator instructions, and quality-control steps.
- Question 2: provide a more granular feature-importance analysis to identify the most impactful cognitive features.
- Question 3: given small datasets, consider whether data augmentation or transfer learning were explored to improve model robustness.
