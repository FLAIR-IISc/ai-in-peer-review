- Novel integration of eye-tracking–derived cognitive features with traditional textual features for sentiment analysis.  
- Empirical validation showing up to 9.3% F-score improvement on two datasets, including one with irony/sarcasm.  
- Feature-significance analysis identifies which cognitive features are most impactful.  

- Clear motivation targeting challenges of nuanced sentiment (sarcasm/irony).  
- Rigorous experiments (SVM/NB/MLP, 10-fold CV, held-out complex dataset).  
- Reported improvements are statistically significant.  
- Comprehensive feature set combining textual, sarcasm/thwarting, and cognitive features.  
- Thoughtful discussion of feasibility and increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers.  

- Derived cognitive features (e.g., gaze-saliency graph) are complex and not fully explained.  
- Limited generalizability due to reliance on two specific datasets with possible biases.  
- Different eye-tracker hardware used across datasets; impact not analyzed.  
- Averaging gaze data across participants may discard useful individual differences.  
- No comparison with or integration into state-of-the-art deep learning approaches.  

- Please clarify the rationale for gaze-saliency graph features (e.g., edge density → semantic complexity).  
- How sensitive are results to the choice and quality of eye-tracker hardware?  
- Were alternative gaze-aggregation methods (hierarchical models, clustering) explored?  
- How can cognitive features be incorporated into deep learning models and what challenges exist?  
- What are the ethical considerations for collecting and using eye-tracking data in practice?  

- Include visualizations of gaze-saliency graphs tied to example texts.  
- Reference list is extensive and well-maintained.  
- Writing is clear but could be streamlined in places.
