This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The core idea is that readers’ eye-movement patterns reveal information about the cognitive processes involved in understanding sentiment, particularly in nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The authors demonstrate performance improvements (up to 9.3% F-score) on two datasets by incorporating these cognitive features.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Feature Integration:** The most significant contribution is the exploration and integration of eye-tracking derived cognitive features into a standard sentiment analysis pipeline. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistic studies of sentiment, its direct application to *improve* sentiment classification accuracy is novel. The diverse set of gaze-based features, including graph-based features, is a strong point.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The authors provide solid empirical evidence that the inclusion of cognitive features consistently improves performance across different classifiers (NB, SVM, NN) and datasets. The statistically significant improvements reported are a key strength.
3. **Held-out Dataset Analysis:** The analysis on a held-out dataset specifically targeting complex constructs (irony/sarcasm) provides further support for the hypothesis that cognitive features are particularly beneficial in handling such challenging cases. This provides insight into *when* these features are most helpful.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of sentiment analysis, particularly in dealing with nuances like sarcasm, and motivates the use of cognitive features as a potential solution.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The authors combine traditional sentiment features with a rich set of eye-tracking-derived features, and features relating to reading difficulty. This comprehensive approach allows for a thorough evaluation of the contribution of cognitive features. The features are well-described and justified, demonstrating a good understanding of both NLP and psycholinguistics.
3. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The use of multiple classifiers, 10-fold cross-validation, and statistical significance tests strengthens the validity of the results. The inclusion of a held-out dataset for focused analysis is a particularly strong point.
4. **Error Analysis and Discussion of Feasibility:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach and discuss the practical challenges and potential future directions, including the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers. This demonstrates critical thinking and a realistic assessment of the work's impact.
5. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** Demonstrating that the datasets *are* challenging for existing systems (Table 1) is crucial, and the authors effectively establish this.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dataset Size:** The datasets used are relatively small (Dataset 1: 994 instances, Dataset 2: 843 instances). While the authors justify their choice, the limited size raises concerns about the generalizability of the results. Larger datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Eye-tracker Quality Disparity:** The use of two datasets with different quality eye-trackers (500Hz vs 120Hz) is a potential confounding factor. The impact of this difference on the extracted cognitive features is not fully addressed. This should be investigated to understand if results differ between the datasets.
3. **Feature Averaging:** The authors acknowledge that averaging gaze data across participants may lead to information loss. While practical, this simplification could mask important individual differences in cognitive processing. Exploring alternative methods for incorporating individual-level data could be beneficial.
4. **Limited Comparison to Deep Learning Methods:** The authors explicitly exclude deep learning methods from their comparison, citing their feature-based approach. However, given the state-of-the-art performance of deep learning in sentiment analysis, a brief discussion of how their approach might complement or be integrated with these methods would be valuable.
5. **Graph Feature Detail:** The description of the gaze-saliency graphs and derived features could be more detailed. It’s not immediately clear how the graph structure specifically captures relevant cognitive information. More examples and intuitive explanations would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of features extracted from the eye-tracking data? Did you perform any feature selection or ablation studies to identify the most important cognitive features?
2.  Can you elaborate on the potential impact of the different eye-tracker qualities (500Hz vs. 120Hz) on the observed results? Have you considered any normalization techniques to mitigate this difference?
3.  Beyond the average performance improvements, are there specific *types* of sentiment expressions (e.g., irony, sarcasm, negation) where the cognitive features demonstrate the largest gains? Can you provide examples?
4.   How computationally expensive is the extraction of the gaze-based features, and what are the scalability implications for real-world applications?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of existing research in sentiment analysis and eye-tracking.
*   The references are comprehensive.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to sentiment analysis. The integration of cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data is a valuable contribution, and the empirical results are convincing. While the dataset size and eye-tracker quality are potential limitations, the authors address these concerns and provide a thorough analysis of their work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions raised above.
