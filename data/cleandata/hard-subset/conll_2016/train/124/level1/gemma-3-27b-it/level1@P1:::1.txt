This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The core idea is that readers’ eye-movement patterns reveal cognitive processes that can improve sentiment detection, especially for nuanced language like sarcasm and irony. The authors demonstrate improvements in F-score (up to 9.3%) on two datasets using this combined feature set and present feature significance analysis to support their claim.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Integration of Eye-Tracking Data for Sentiment Analysis:** The most significant contribution is the novel application of cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns to the task of sentiment analysis. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistic studies of sentiment and annotation complexity, this work is among the first to directly integrate this data into a statistical sentiment classifier.
2. **Demonstrated Performance Improvement:** The paper empirically demonstrates that incorporating cognitive features leads to a measurable improvement in sentiment classification accuracy, particularly on datasets containing sarcastic or ironic content. The reported gains (up to 9.3%) are substantial and warrant attention.
3. **Feature Significance Analysis:** The inclusion of feature significance analysis lends credibility to the claim that the cognitive features are actively contributing to the performance improvement, rather than just adding noise. Highlighting which gaze-based features are most impactful is valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The central idea of leveraging cognitive features for sentiment analysis is genuinely novel and represents a potentially valuable direction for research. It moves beyond purely textual features and incorporates information about the *process* of reading and understanding sentiment.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides empirical evidence, through experiments on two datasets, that supports the authors’ hypothesis. The reported F-score improvements, while not overwhelming, are statistically significant and demonstrate the feasibility of the approach.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors have thoughtfully engineered a comprehensive set of features, encompassing traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/irony indicators, cognitive features, and features related to reading difficulty.  This demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature and a careful consideration of potential factors influencing sentiment detection.
4. **Held-Out Dataset Evaluation:** The use of a held-out dataset to specifically evaluate the performance on complex constructs (irony) further strengthens the validity of the findings and demonstrates the method’s targeted effectiveness.
5. **Feasibility Discussion:** The authors proactively address the practicality concerns related to data acquisition (eye-tracking), acknowledging the challenges and presenting arguments regarding the increasing availability of mobile eye-tracking technology.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size and Domain Specificity:** The datasets used (994 and 1059 samples) are relatively small, and both focus on relatively informal text (tweets and reviews). This limits the generalizability of the findings. It’s unclear how well this approach would perform on different text domains or more formal writing styles.
2. **Eye-Tracker Quality Disparity:** The use of two different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000 and Tobii T120) with varying sampling rates introduces a potential source of variability and may affect the comparability of results. The authors should better address the potential impact of this difference.
3. **Averaging of Gaze Data:** The aggregation of gaze data across participants during graph construction (as described in Section 4.6) might obscure important individual differences in eye-movement patterns. Averaging can smooth out nuanced cognitive responses.  Exploring alternative methods for incorporating individual-level gaze data could be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Deep Learning Comparison:** While the authors explicitly state they don’t compare against deep learning approaches, this is a significant omission. State-of-the-art sentiment analysis often relies on deep learning models, and a comparison (even brief) would provide valuable context for evaluating the contribution of the cognitive features.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The error analysis section is brief and doesn’t provide sufficient insight into *why* the system makes errors. A more detailed analysis of misclassified examples, particularly those involving sarcasm and irony, could reveal areas for improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of eye-tracker? Did you perform any analysis to assess the impact of using different quality eye-tracking devices?
2.  Could you elaborate on why you chose to average gaze data during graph construction, and what alternatives were considered?  Would it be possible to incorporate individual-level gaze data into the model?
3.  Given the success of deep learning in sentiment analysis, could you briefly discuss how cognitive features might be integrated into a deep learning framework (e.g., as additional input features)?
4.  Can you provide more specific examples of misclassified instances, particularly those involving sarcasm or irony, and explain why the system failed to correctly classify them?
5.  What measures were taken to ensure the representativeness of the participants whose eye-tracking data was collected, and how might participant demographics influence the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of the relevant literature.
*   Figure 1 is helpful for illustrating the eye-tracking data and the concept of fixations and saccades.
*   Consider providing links to the datasets used in the paper.
*   The abbreviation “Gz” for “gaze features” is not intuitive and could be improved.
