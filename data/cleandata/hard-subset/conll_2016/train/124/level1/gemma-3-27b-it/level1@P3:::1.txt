This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The core idea is that readers’ eye-movement patterns reflect cognitive processing, and incorporating this information can improve sentiment detection, particularly for nuanced cases like sarcasm and irony. The authors demonstrate performance improvements on two datasets, achieving up to 9.3% F-score gain over baseline systems.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the novel exploration of integrating eye-tracking data with traditional features for sentiment analysis. While eye-tracking has been used in related areas like annotation complexity measurement, this is, to the best of my knowledge, the first attempt to directly incorporate it into a sentiment classification pipeline. The detailed feature engineering, encompassing both basic and complex gaze-based features, is also a significant aspect of this work. Finally, the empirical validation on two datasets, along with feature significance analysis and a held-out experiment, provides evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The central idea of leveraging cognitive features from eye-tracking for sentiment analysis is genuinely novel. Sentiment analysis often struggles with subtleties like sarcasm, and incorporating cognitive data offers a promising avenue for addressing these challenges. The reported improvements, while not enormous, are statistically significant and suggest a viable direction for future research.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate considerable effort in defining and extracting a rich set of features, going beyond basic eye-tracking metrics to incorporate graph-based representations of gaze patterns. This detailed feature engineering is crucial for effectively capturing the cognitive information and translating it into usable signals for the classifiers. The inclusion of reading difficulty features is a smart move to control for confounding factors.
3. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper presents a reasonably thorough evaluation. The use of two datasets, along with a held-out test set specifically chosen for complex constructs, strengthens the validity of the findings. The feature significance analysis provides insights into which cognitive features are most informative. The statistical significance testing (McNemar test) further supports the claim of improvement.
4. **Addressing a Real-World Challenge:** The authors acknowledge the practical limitations of requiring eye-tracking data and discuss the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios. This demonstrates an awareness of the broader context and a forward-looking perspective. The discussion of user consent is also responsible.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability and Scalability:** The biggest weakness is the inherent dependency on eye-tracking data, which significantly limits the scalability and generalizability of the approach. While the authors discuss the potential for mobile eye-trackers, the cost and intrusiveness of even those devices remain barriers to widespread adoption. The fact that the performance gains are moderate further exacerbates this concern.
2. **Feature Complexity and Interpretability:** The large number of features, particularly the complex gaze-based features derived from graph representations, makes the model less interpretable. It's difficult to understand *why* certain eye-movement patterns are predictive of sentiment, hindering the development of more targeted and theoretically grounded approaches. A more focused feature selection or dimensionality reduction technique might be beneficial.
3. **Baseline Comparison:** While the authors compare against baseline systems, these baselines are somewhat dated and do not represent the state-of-the-art in sentiment analysis.  Recent deep learning approaches (e.g., transformers) often achieve significantly higher accuracy. Comparing against these more powerful baselines would provide a more realistic assessment of the contribution of the cognitive features. It's stated the authors chose not to compare against these deep learning approaches, but the rationale is weak.
4. **Dataset Characteristics & Annotation Quality:** While the authors mention Fleiss kappa scores, it would be useful to have a more detailed discussion of the inter-annotator agreement within each dataset and potential biases in the annotations themselves. The datasets are also relatively small, which may limit the statistical power of the results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed justification for not comparing your approach against current state-of-the-art deep learning models for sentiment analysis? What specific challenges did you encounter when attempting to integrate your features with these models?
2. The description of the graph-based gaze features is somewhat abstract. Could you provide a visual example illustrating the construction of the gaze-saliency graph and explain how the weighted degrees are calculated?
3. Given the limitations of eye-tracking data, have you considered exploring alternative methods for approximating cognitive processes, such as using linguistic cues that correlate with cognitive effort (e.g., sentence complexity, ambiguity)?
4. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the errors made by your system? Are there specific types of sentences or sentiment expressions where the cognitive features are particularly helpful or unhelpful?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper could benefit from a more concise and focused introduction.
* The organization of Section 4 (Enhanced Feature Set) is a bit cumbersome. It might be more readable to present the features in a more streamlined manner.
* Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of eye-tracking technology itself (e.g., calibration errors, participant fatigue) and how these factors might have affected the results.
