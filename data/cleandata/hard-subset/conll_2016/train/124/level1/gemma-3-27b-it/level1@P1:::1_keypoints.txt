- Novel integration of eye-tracking–derived cognitive features into sentiment analysis to capture reader processing of sarcasm and irony.
- Empirical improvement in F-score (up to 9.3%) on two datasets when combining cognitive and traditional features.
- Feature significance analysis shows gaze-based features meaningfully contribute to performance gains.
- Approach is novel and moves beyond purely textual features by leveraging reading-process information.
- Experiments provide statistically significant empirical validation of the method on two datasets.
- Comprehensive feature engineering covering textual, sarcasm/irony, cognitive, and reading-difficulty features.
- Use of a held-out dataset to specifically evaluate performance on irony strengthens validity.
- Authors discuss feasibility and practical concerns around eye-tracking data acquisition and mobile eye-tracking availability.
- Datasets are small (994 and 1059 samples) and domain-specific (tweets/reviews), limiting generalizability.
- Use of two different eye-trackers with varying quality introduces potential variability not fully addressed.
- Averaging gaze data across participants may obscure individual differences in eye-movement patterns.
- No comparison to deep learning baselines, leaving unclear how cognitive features stack up against SOTA models.
- Error analysis is limited and lacks detailed insights into misclassifications, especially for sarcasm/irony.
- Question: How sensitive are results to choice and quality of eye-tracker equipment?
- Question: Why was gaze data averaged in graph construction and were individual-level alternatives considered?
- Question: How could cognitive features be integrated into deep learning frameworks as additional inputs?
- Question: Can authors provide specific misclassified examples (esp. sarcasm/irony) and explanations for failures?
- Question: What were participant demographics and how was representativeness ensured for gaze data?
- Paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
- Related work section gives a good overview of relevant literature.
- Figure 1 effectively illustrates eye-tracking concepts like fixations and saccades.
- Consider providing links to the datasets used for reproducibility.
- The abbreviation “Gz” for gaze features is unintuitive and should be improved.
