## Review of "Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis"

This paper explores the augmentation of traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The core idea is that readers’ eye-movement patterns during sentiment annotation can provide valuable signals for improving polarity detection, particularly for nuanced text like sarcasm. The authors demonstrate performance gains (up to 9.3% F-score improvement) on two datasets by incorporating these cognitive features alongside standard linguistic features.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the **novel integration of eye-tracking data into a feature-based sentiment analysis pipeline.** While eye-tracking has been used in related areas like annotation complexity assessment, this paper represents a significant step towards directly leveraging cognitive processes for improved sentiment classification. A second notable contribution is the **careful feature engineering from eye-tracking data**, including both basic gaze metrics and a more complex graph-based representation of saccade patterns. The authors clearly justify their feature choices based on cognitive principles. Finally, the **empirical validation on datasets containing sarcasm and irony** is a strong point, as these constructs are known to challenge traditional sentiment analysis techniques.

Strengths of the paper include a thorough exploration of related work, a well-defined experimental setup, and a clear presentation of results. The statistical significance testing adds credibility to the findings. The discussion of feasibility, acknowledging the challenges of data acquisition and potential applications with mobile eye-trackers, demonstrates practical awareness. The inclusion of a held-out dataset specifically to test performance on complex constructs is a strong validation step.

However, the paper has some weaknesses. The **limited size of the datasets** is a concern, potentially hindering the generalizability of the results. While the authors mention inter-rater agreement, more details on the annotation process and potential biases would be beneficial. Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a **more in-depth analysis of *which* cognitive features are most impactful**. While feature significance is mentioned, a more granular analysis could reveal specific eye-movement patterns that are indicative of sentiment. Finally, the conclusion acknowledges future work exploring deep learning approaches, but a brief discussion of why feature-based methods were chosen *over* deep learning would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the annotation process used to create the datasets, including instructions given to annotators and steps taken to ensure annotation quality?
2.  Could you provide a more granular analysis of feature importance, identifying which specific cognitive features contribute most significantly to the performance gains?
3.  Given the limitations in dataset size, have you considered techniques like data augmentation or transfer learning to improve the robustness of your model?
