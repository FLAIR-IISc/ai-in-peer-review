This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The core idea is that readers’ eye-movement patterns reveal cognitive processes related to sentiment understanding, particularly for nuanced expressions like sarcasm and irony. The authors demonstrate improvements in F-score (up to 9.3%) on two datasets by incorporating these cognitive features into standard classification models.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Feature Integration:** The primary contribution is the innovative integration of eye-tracking derived cognitive features with traditional textual features for sentiment analysis. While eye-tracking has been applied to related tasks, its application to augment *standard* sentiment classification is novel.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a solid empirical validation of the proposed approach, demonstrating statistically significant performance gains on two datasets, including one specifically designed to contain ironic and sarcastic content.
3. **Feature Significance Analysis:** The feature significance analysis provides insights into which cognitive features are most impactful, reinforcing the idea that eye-movement data captures relevant cognitive processes.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of sentiment analysis, particularly the difficulties associated with nuanced language constructs. The motivation for leveraging cognitive features is well-explained.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The experiments employ standard classification algorithms (SVM, NB, Multi-layer NN), a 10-fold cross-validation scheme, and a held-out dataset for further validation. The inclusion of a held-out dataset specifically designed with complex linguistic constructs is a major strength.
3. **Statistically Significant Results:** The observed improvements in F-score are statistically significant, suggesting that the cognitive features contribute genuinely to the performance of the sentiment analysis system.
4. **Comprehensive Feature Set:** The combination of traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting features, and cognitive features is well-considered and provides a rich representation of the input text.
5. **Discussion of Feasibility:** The authors thoughtfully address the practical limitations of requiring eye-tracking data and discuss potential avenues for making the approach more feasible, such as the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Complexity of Cognitive Features:** While novel, the derived cognitive features (especially those based on the gaze-saliency graph) are complex and potentially difficult to interpret. The rationale behind some features isn't fully explained, making it hard to understand *why* they are effective.
2. **Limited Generalizability:** The study uses two specific datasets. While one is designed for irony/sarcasm, the generalizability of the findings to other domains or types of text is uncertain. The reliance on datasets with inherent biases may limit broader application.
3. **Eye-tracker Differences:** The two datasets employ different eye-trackers (high-quality vs. low-quality). The authors don’t explicitly address how this difference might influence the results. It's unclear if the observed improvements are consistent across different hardware configurations.
4. **Averaging of Gaze Data:** The method of averaging gaze data across participants when constructing the gaze-saliency graphs may lead to a loss of valuable individual-level information. Considering individual differences in reading patterns could potentially improve performance.
5. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Deep Learning Models:** The authors explicitly exclude deep learning approaches. While understandable given the feature-based nature of their work, a brief discussion of how cognitive features *might* be incorporated into deep learning models would strengthen the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detailed explanations for the rationale behind the gaze-saliency graph features (e.g., why is edge density expected to correlate with semantic complexity)?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of eye-tracker? Did you investigate the potential impact of using different eye-tracking hardware on the performance of the system?
3.  Did you explore alternative methods for aggregating gaze data across participants (e.g., using hierarchical models or clustering)? If so, what were the results?
4.  What are your thoughts on incorporating these cognitive features into a deep learning framework? Do you foresee any significant challenges in doing so?
5.  Can you elaborate on the ethical considerations of collecting and using eye-tracking data, especially in a real-world application scenario?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from visualizations illustrating the gaze-saliency graphs and how they relate to specific text examples.
*   The reference list is extensive and generally well-maintained.
*   The writing is clear and concise, although some sections could be streamlined for better readability.
