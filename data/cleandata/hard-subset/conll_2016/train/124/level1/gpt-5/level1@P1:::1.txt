Summary
This paper augments standard sentiment analysis (SA) features with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movement patterns. Using two publicly available eye-tracking datasets containing short texts rich in sarcasm/irony and other complex phenomena, the authors engineer: (i) traditional sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features, (ii) basic gaze features (fixations, saccades, regressions, skips), (iii) graph-based gaze-saliency features, and (iv) readability controls. With SVM, NB, and MLP classifiers in 10-fold cross-validation, they report that adding sarcasm/thwarting features substantially improves over plain sentiment features, and further adding gaze features yields additional gains (SVM F-score: +0.6 on D1 and +1.5 on D2 on top of Sn+Sr). On a held-out subset rich in irony, gaze features yield larger gains. Feature ranking suggests multiple gaze features are among the most informative.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that eye-tracking-derived cognitive features provide statistically significant improvements for SA, especially for texts with complex constructs (sarcasm/irony).
2) Introduction of gaze-saliency graph features and a diverse set of gaze-based indicators tailored to sentiment-related phenomena.
3) A careful evaluation highlighting the relative impact of sarcasm/thwarting features vs. gaze features and a held-out analysis for complex cases.

Strengths
- Novel use of cognitive evidence for SA. The idea to leverage gaze patterns to capture semantic/pragmatic incongruity, regressions, and processing difficulty addresses known limitations of text-only features for sarcasm/irony and implicit sentiment.
- Solid empirical methodology and significance testing. The paper evaluates multiple classifiers, performs McNemar tests, and includes a held-out analysis that isolates complex constructs, where the impact of gaze is most pronounced (e.g., +4.2 F absolute on the irony subset).
- Thorough feature design and controls. The feature set spans standard sentiment cues, sarcasm-specific features, gaze features (basic and graph-based), and readability controls (length, Flesch score), reducing confounds from general reading difficulty. The gaze-saliency graph is a creative way to summarize scanpath structure.
- Informative feature analysis. Chi-squared ranking shows several gaze features among top predictors on both datasets, supporting the claim that gaze adds complementary signal beyond text features.
- Use of public datasets and clear problem framing. The datasets are challenging and previously used in related cognitive-SA work, anchoring this study in a reproducible setting.

Weaknesses
- Practicality/Deployment gap. The method requires eye-tracking at inference time. The proposed feasibility via mobile webcams is speculative and does not address the need for multiple readers per text (as used here) or calibration/consent/logistics. The primary SA use case typically requires predicting sentiment prior to any reading event.
- Modest incremental gains from gaze on top of strong text features. While statistically significant, the additional improvements from gaze in the main CV are small (+0.6% and +1.5% F), with most of the overall gain attributable to sarcasm/thwarting features. The benefit is larger on irony-heavy data, but the general utility appears limited in typical settings.
- Potential confounds and circularity. Gaze was recorded during sentiment annotation; patterns may reflect annotation strategy or decision difficulty rather than properties strictly necessary to infer sentiment from text. Without controls (e.g., gaze collected during neutral reading tasks, analysis conditioned on annotator agreement), it is hard to attribute gains to text-intrinsic sentiment cues rather than annotator effort.
- Limited baselines and ablations. The text-only baselines exclude stronger 2015–2016 models (e.g., CNN/RNN character/word models, tree-structured nets) that often handle polarity flips and compositionality. The paper lacks ablations within the gaze features (basic vs. graph-based vs. specific regressions) to pinpoint which cognitive signals matter most, and no leave-one-user/device-out setup to assess robustness across readers and hardware (Eyelink-1000 vs. Tobii T120).
- Aggregation choices and normalization. Averaging gaze features across readers collapses individual variability and might hide model sensitivity to user traits. The paper provides limited detail on feature normalization across users/devices (e.g., per-user z-scoring of durations/saccade lengths), which is crucial for generalization.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Inference-time assumption: Do you envision using gaze from a single reader, multiple readers, or pre-aggregated templates? How would the model operate when only one reader’s gaze is available (or none)?
- Normalization: How are fixation durations, saccade lengths, and regression counts normalized across participants and devices? Are features standardized per user/session to reduce inter-subject/hardware variance?
- Robustness across readers: Can you report a leave-one-reader-out experiment (train on aggregated gaze excluding one reader, test on that reader) or per-reader training/testing to show the model does not overfit to specific annotators?
- Ablations within gaze features: Which gaze features drive the gains? Please provide ablation results separating basic vs. graph-based features and key indicators (e.g., REG, RSF, LREG).
- Stronger text-only baselines: Can you compare against a contemporary deep learning baseline (e.g., CNN or RNN with character/word embeddings) and/or a state-of-the-art sarcasm-aware SA model trained text-only, to contextualize the incremental value of gaze?
- Data leakage risks: For the LP (lexical polarity) and IMPLICIT features, confirm there is no overlap between resources used to train those components and the evaluation splits. Also, clarify whether any label information from annotators (beyond the final label) could indirectly influence gaze-derived features.
- Generalization: Have you tested cross-dataset transfer (train on D1, test on D2 and vice versa), especially for gaze features, to assess domain/device robustness?

Additional comments (non-urgent)
- Minor typos and table formatting issues should be fixed for clarity.
- Consider exploring gaze-supervised representation learning or multi-view/distillation approaches where gaze is used only during training to improve text-only models, addressing deployment constraints.
