- Paper augments sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking, engineering sentiment/sarcasm, basic gaze, graph-based gaze-saliency, and readability features on two public datasets; adding sarcasm features yields big gains and gaze features give further small but significant improvements (e.g., +0.6 F on D1, +1.5 F on D2), larger on an irony subset.  
- Contribution: provides empirical evidence that eye-tracking improves SA for complex constructs like sarcasm/irony.  
- Contribution: introduces gaze-saliency graph features and a diverse set of gaze-based sentiment indicators.  
- Contribution: performs careful evaluation separating the impact of sarcasm/thwarting vs. gaze features with held-out analysis for complex cases.  
- Strength: novel use of cognitive signals to capture semantic/pragmatic incongruity and processing difficulty for sarcasm/implicit sentiment.  
- Strength: solid empirical methodology with multiple classifiers, significance testing, and a held-out irony-focused analysis.  
- Strength: thorough feature design and controls (sentiment, sarcasm, gaze, readability) and creative gaze-saliency graph.  
- Strength: informative feature analysis showing several gaze features among top predictors and use of public, challenging datasets.  
- Weakness: impractical deployment requirement of eye-tracking at inference and speculative webcam feasibility, with unaddressed need for multiple readers, calibration, consent, and pre-reading prediction use cases.  
- Weakness: modest incremental gains from gaze over strong text features in CV (most gain from sarcasm features), questioning general utility outside irony-heavy texts.  
- Weakness: potential confound that gaze was recorded during annotation so signals may reflect annotator effort/strategy rather than text-intrinsic cues.  
- Weakness: limited baselines and ablations—omits stronger DL/text-only baselines (CNN/RNN/etc.), lacks ablations between gaze subtypes and leave-one-user/device-out robustness tests.  
- Weakness: aggregation and normalization concerns—averaging across readers may hide variability and paper gives limited detail on per-user/device normalization.  
- Question: do you expect inference-time gaze from single reader, multiple readers, or pre-aggregated templates, and how would the model work with one/no reader?  
- Question: how are fixation durations, saccade lengths, and regression counts normalized across participants and devices (per-user/session standardization)?  
- Question: can you report leave-one-reader-out or per-reader experiments to show robustness to annotator-specific patterns?  
- Question: provide ablation results separating basic vs. graph-based gaze features and identify which specific gaze indicators drive gains (e.g., REG, RSF, LREG).  
- Question: can you compare against contemporary deep learning text-only baselines and state-of-the-art sarcasm-aware SA models to contextualize gaze value?  
- Question: confirm no data leakage between resources used for LP/IMPLICIT features and evaluation splits, and clarify whether annotator metadata influenced gaze features.  
- Question: have you tested cross-dataset transfer (train on D1/test on D2 and vice versa) to assess domain/device robustness for gaze features?  
- Additional: fix minor typos and table formatting for clarity.  
- Additional: suggest exploring gaze-supervised representation learning or using gaze only during training (distillation) to address deployment constraints.
