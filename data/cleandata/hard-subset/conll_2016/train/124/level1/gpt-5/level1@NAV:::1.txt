Summary: The paper augments text-based sentiment analysis with cognitive features extracted from readers’ eye movements. Using two eye-tracked datasets containing many nuanced instances (sarcasm, irony, thwarting), the authors design basic and graph-based gaze features and combine them with standard sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features. Across 10-fold CV, they report F-score gains up to 3.7% and 9.3% over traditional features on two datasets, and a 6.1% relative gain on a held-out subset of ironic texts. Feature significance analyses suggest several gaze features are among the top predictors.

Contributions (as I see them):
- Empirical evidence that gaze-derived cognitive signals improve sentiment classification on linguistically complex short texts, with the largest relative benefit on ironic/sarcastic cases.
- A novel, interpretable gaze feature set, including saliency-graph measures that capture saccade structure and regressions, and an analysis showing their importance.
- A careful ablation and significance-testing regimen demonstrating incremental value beyond sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting textual features.

Strengths:
- Targeting hard cases: The held-out analysis shows larger gains on irony/sarcasm (+6.1% F for ironic vs +2.1% for non-ironic), supporting the central claim that cognitive cues help where textual cues underperform.
- Interpretable, theory-motivated features: Fixations, regressions, and saccade-based graph degrees are grounded in psycholinguistics, aiding explainability; chi-squared ranks place multiple gaze features among top predictors.
- Sound ablations and statistics: Clear stepwise gains (Sn → Sn+Sr → Sn+Sr+Gz) and McNemar tests bolster the robustness of conclusions.
- Device diversity: Results hold across datasets collected with different eye-trackers (Eyelink-1000 vs Tobii T120), hinting at some robustness to sensor quality.

Weaknesses:
- Practicality and deployment: The method requires gaze at inference time; proposed mobile eye-tracking scenarios are speculative and not empirically validated. No exploration of training-time-only use (e.g., distillation) or proxy modeling.
- Limited baselines: Absent comparisons to strong modern text-only baselines (e.g., CNN/RNN/transformers for SA, sarcasm detection), leaving open whether gaze still helps when text models are stronger.
- Dataset scale and generalization: Small sample sizes (<1.1k instances each) and within-dataset CV; no cross-domain/leave-device/leave-subject generalization, and the “held-out” set is a subset of Dataset 1, risking overlap in distributional quirks.
- Possible confounds: Different hardware and annotation protocols per dataset; gains could partly reflect artifacts. Details on hyperparameter tuning and PCA choices are sparse.

Questions to Authors:
- Can you report results with stronger text-only baselines (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN or transformer fine-tunes) and on cross-device/leave-user splits?
- Can gaze be used only at training time (e.g., multitask or knowledge distillation) to yield deployable text-only models?
- How sensitive are results to the construction of saliency graphs (e.g., weighting schemes) and to gaze aggregation across annotators?
