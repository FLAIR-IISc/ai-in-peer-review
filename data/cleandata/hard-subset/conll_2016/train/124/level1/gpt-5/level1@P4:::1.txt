Summary and main contributions
- The paper augments feature-based sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye movement (fixations, saccades, regressions) recorded during sentiment annotation. It evaluates the impact of adding these features—alongside traditional sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting features, and readability—on two publicly available eye-tracking datasets via standard classifiers.
- Claimed improvements include up to 3.7% and 9.3% absolute F-score gains over a traditional feature set on two datasets, and larger gains on a held-out subset containing sarcasm/irony. A feature importance analysis suggests gaze features rank among the most informative.
- As I see it, the primary contributions are:
  1) Introduction and systematic evaluation of gaze-based cognitive features for sentiment polarity detection, including novel graph-based “gaze-saliency” features.
  2) Empirical evidence that gaze features particularly help on sentences with complex pragmatic phenomena (sarcasm/irony), beyond gains from sarcasm/thwarting textual features.
  3) Feature significance analysis indicating several gaze features rank highly, suggesting they capture information not covered by textual features.

Strengths
- Clear empirical finding that gaze features add predictive signal, especially for complex constructs. On the held-out subset, adding gaze to sentiment+sarcasm features improves F-score from 60.1 to 64.3 for irony (absolute +4.2), and from 75.9 to 77.6 for non-irony. On the full datasets, adding gaze to sentiment+sarcasm features yields additional +0.6 (D1) and +1.5 (D2) F1 improvements, with a McNemar test indicating statistical significance for the strongest settings.
- Novel gaze-saliency graph features and a comprehensive gaze feature set. Beyond basic fixation/saccade statistics, the paper proposes graph-derived degree measures weighted by different gaze signals and an edge density metric, expanding the methodological toolkit for leveraging eye-tracking in NLP.
- Sensible control for confounds from reading difficulty. The inclusion of readability (Flesch score) and sentence length features helps mitigate the concern that gains are merely due to general reading difficulty rather than sentiment-related cognitive effects.
- Thorough comparison across classifiers and feature ablations. Results are reported for SVM, Naive Bayes, and a simple neural network, with multiple feature combinations (unigram, sentiment only, sentiment+sarcasm, gaze-only, combined). This helps attribute where gains come from: the largest boosts come from adding sarcasm/thwarting features, with further gains from gaze.
- Use of two publicly available eye-tracking datasets with differing hardware quality and content types. This adds some robustness to the finding that gaze features contribute under varied recording conditions (500 Hz vs 120 Hz trackers; reviews and tweets; presence of irony/sarcasm).

Weaknesses
- Limited practical applicability at inference time. The approach assumes availability of gaze data for the target text at prediction time. While the paper discusses future mobile eye-tracking scenarios, the current method is not deployable in standard offline SA settings where only text is available. The feasibility section is speculative and does not quantify data collection costs, noise, or privacy/consent constraints.
- Modest incremental gains from gaze over strong textual features. The additional F1 from gaze on top of sentiment+sarcasm features is small on the main datasets (+0.6 and +1.5 absolute), with the majority of total gains driven by adding sarcasm/thwarting features. The impact is more noticeable on the “complex” held-out subset, but the subset is relatively small (131 complex examples), and only drawn from Dataset 1.
- Limited external validity and generalization tests. Experiments are 10-fold cross-validation within each dataset; there is no cross-dataset transfer (e.g., train on D1, test on D2) or domain transfer beyond the curated datasets. Given the small size (∼1K sentences each) and specialized nature (texts read under annotation conditions), generalization to broader SA tasks remains unclear.
- Incomplete methodological clarity and reproducibility details. Some gaze feature definitions are ambiguous or duplicative (e.g., F1H/F2H descriptions), and it is not entirely clear how PCA was trained/applied across folds for unigram/implicit incongruity features. The construction of sarcasm/thwarting features (e.g., bootstrapped phrase sets) lacks corpus and parameter details. It is also unclear whether subject-level effects were normalized prior to aggregation and whether any per-subject leakage could occur across folds (though sentences appear unique per fold).
- Baselines omit strong contemporary models. The paper explicitly excludes deep learning methods despite their prominence (even in 2014–2016) and does not compare with stronger feature-based baselines exploiting syntactic dependencies or more advanced lexicon+rule systems beyond those already included. This limits the strength of the empirical claim that gaze features provide additive value over the best available text-only systems.

Questions to the authors
- Inference-time setting: In what concrete application scenarios do you expect gaze to be available at prediction time? Do you envision real-time per-user sentiment classification while a user reads, or training with gaze but testing without? If the latter, can you show results for models trained with gaze but tested without (e.g., via teacher–student, multi-view, or privileged information paradigms)?
- Cross-dataset generalization: Can you report train-on-D1/test-on-D2 and vice versa to assess robustness? Similarly, can you train on one hardware setting (500 Hz) and test on the other (120 Hz) to evaluate sensitivity to tracker quality?
- Feature definitions and preprocessing: Please clarify the precise computation for the graph-based features (F1H/F2H, PSDH/PSDS, RSDH/RSDS), how edge weights are aggregated, and whether features were normalized per sentence length and per subject before averaging. Also, specify how PCA was fit within cross-validation to avoid train–test leakage.
- Subject variability: Did you analyze inter-participant variability and its impact on features? Is there an advantage to modeling per-reader features rather than averaging, or to using mixed-effects models?
- Stronger baselines: Can you add comparisons to a dependency-based feature model and to a contemporary CNN/RNN baseline to better contextualize the incremental value of gaze?

Additional comments
- Please specify whether F-scores are macro or micro for the binary classification, and provide confidence intervals alongside significance tests.
- There are minor typos/format inconsistencies; tightening definitions and aligning feature labels across text and tables would aid clarity.
