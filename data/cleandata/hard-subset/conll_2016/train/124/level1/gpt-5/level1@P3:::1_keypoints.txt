- Empirical claim: Eye-tracking–derived features yield consistent, statistically significant improvements for sentence-level sentiment detection, especially on sarcastic/ironic sentences and other “hard” items.  
- Novel features: Introduction of gaze-saliency graphs and degree-based gaze summaries grounded in psycholinguistic theory.  
- Baseline contribution: A strong unified text-only baseline (sentiment + sarcasm/thwarting features) clarifies the incremental value of gaze.  

- Psycholinguistic motivation: Feature design maps reading behaviors (fixations, regressions, etc.) to linguistic processing difficulty in a well-argued way.  
- Creative modeling: Gaze-saliency graphs capture distributed processing and word/phrase interactions.  
- Robustness: Consistent F-score improvements across two datasets collected with different eye-trackers, with larger gains on irony/sarcasm.  
- Strong baselines and feature analysis: Text-only baselines are competitive and chi-squared ranking places multiple gaze features among top predictors.  
- Statistical testing: Use of McNemar tests to assess significance of small performance deltas.  

- Practicality concerns: Method requires eye-tracking at inference with no deployment-ready training-only or distillation approach, limiting real-world applicability.  
- Small incremental gains: With strong sarcasm features included, overall F improvements are modest (+0.6 to +1.5), raising cost–benefit questions.  
- Evaluation confounds: Unclear whether PCA and other preprocessing were fit only on training folds, risking leakage.  
- Subject coupling: Gaze features computed from annotators who also labeled data; no leave-one-subject-out results to test generalization across readers.  
- Held-out split issues: “Held-out” set is randomly derived from the same dataset, so not truly out-of-domain.  
- Missing contemporary baselines: No comparison to strong neural/text-only models (CNNs, RNNs, char n-gram models), so added value of gaze versus modern learners is unclear.  
- Reporting gaps: Unspecified whether F is macro or micro, no confidence intervals, and incomplete normalization details across participants/hardware; some gaze-only configs underperform baselines but aren’t discussed.  
- Limited ablation and error analysis: No systematic ablation to identify which gaze features drive gains or link gaze signals to specific error reductions.  

- CV/preprocessing question: Were PCA, scaling, and learned components fit exclusively on training folds within each CV split?  
- Subject generalization question: Can you report leave-one-subject-out or leave-some-subjects-out CV to test gaze generalization across readers?  
- Metrics question: Are reported F-scores macro or micro; please report accuracy, macro-F, and confidence intervals given class imbalance.  
- Gaze normalization question: How were fixation durations/counts normalized across annotators and different sampling rates (500Hz vs 120Hz); were features z-scored per subject?  
- Stronger baselines question: Can you add bi-LSTM/CNN and char n-gram SVM baselines to test whether gaze still adds value?  
- Training-only gaze question: Can you try multi-view/co-training or distillation where gaze is used only at training time to produce a deployable text-only model?
