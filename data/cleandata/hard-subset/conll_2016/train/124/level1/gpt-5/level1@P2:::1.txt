Summary and contributions
The paper augments standard text-based sentiment features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking during annotation, arguing that gaze encodes processing difficulty and surprisal associated with nuanced sentiment (sarcasm, thwarting). The authors evaluate on two public datasets with eye-tracking, use SVM/NB/MLP with 10-fold cross-validation, and report F-score gains up to 3.7% and 9.3% over text-only features, with larger gains on irony-heavy instances.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that gaze-derived cognitive features provide statistically significant, albeit modest, improvements for sentiment polarity detection on short texts rich in sarcasm/irony. The incremental gain over strong text features (Sn+Sr) is 0.6% (72.9 to 73.5 F on D1) and 1.5% (70.3 to 71.8 F on D2), with p = 0.0001 on D1 and p < 0.05 on held-out irony vs non-irony analysis.
2) A concrete feature set that operationalizes psycholinguistic insights: basic gaze features (fixations, saccades, regressions) and graph-based saliency features, plus sarcasm/thwarting textual features, together improving over sentiment-only features by 3.1% (D1) and 7.8% (D2).
3) Diagnostic analyses: feature significance showing many gaze features among top-ranked (10/20 for D1; 7/20 for D2), and a held-out subset demonstrating larger gains on complex constructs (6.1% boost for irony vs 2.1% for non-irony).

Strengths
1) Novelty and conceptual significance. Using eye-tracking to augment sentiment analysis is original for SA (the authors situate it vs prior gaze work in annotation complexity and sarcasm understandability). The approach is well-motivated by psycholinguistic findings (e.g., regressions for reanalysis, fixation duration for lexical difficulty), and the feature design reflects these theories.
2) Empirical support across datasets and analyses. Improvements are observed on two independent gaze datasets with different eye-trackers (500 Hz Eyelink vs 120 Hz Tobii) and reader cohorts, suggesting robustness to hardware/data variance. Statistical testing (McNemar) supports significance for small but consistent gains; feature-ranking corroborates the utility of gaze signals.
3) Targeted benefit on hard cases. The held-out analysis (294 items; 131 irony) shows that gaze features help most where textual cues are weak or incongruous (64.3 vs 60.1 F for irony when adding gaze), matching the paper’s central hypothesis about cognitive signals capturing pragmatic complexity.
4) Clear and reproducible feature definitions. The paper provides concrete, implementable descriptions for sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, and gaze features (including PCA dimensionality caps), enabling replication given access to the datasets. The gaze-saliency graph formulation is clear and extensible.
5) Sensible baselines and ablations. The authors compare unigrams vs broader sentiment features vs sarcasm/thwarting features, then add gaze; this decomposition makes the incremental contribution of each block transparent.

Weaknesses
1) Limited practicality at inference time. The method requires eye-tracking at test time. The discussion of mobile eye-trackers is speculative; no experiment explores using predicted gaze, distillation to text-only models, or cost-benefit tradeoffs. As-is, the approach has constrained applicability, which undermines impact for most SA scenarios.
2) Possible task-induced and dataset confounds. Gaze is collected while annotators perform sentiment labeling, which risks encoding task strategies rather than natural reading. This could inflate gains for the same task distribution and reduce ecological validity. Additionally, D1 contains many sarcastic items, potentially aligning the gains with a particular stylistic distribution.
3) Modest incremental gains from gaze over strong text features. The largest contributions come from adding sarcasm/thwarting features to sentiment features (3.1% and 7.8%), while gaze yields only +0.6% (D1) and +1.5% (D2) on top. Gaze-only features underperform text baselines (e.g., SVM Gz 53.4/48.9 F vs Sn 69.8/62.5), suggesting limited standalone utility and raising questions about cost-effectiveness.
4) Missing comparisons to stronger text-only models. The paper explicitly omits deep learning baselines (CNN/RNN) that, by 2016, were competitive for short-text SA. Without such comparisons, it is unclear whether gaze still helps when text modeling is stronger. Even a re-implementation of a standard CNN for sentence sentiment would contextualize the gains.
5) Insufficient control for reader/hardware variability and confounds. It is unclear whether gaze features were normalized per participant (e.g., z-scoring by reader) to control idiosyncrasies, or how differing sampling rates (500 vs 120 Hz) were accounted for. No leave-one-reader-out evaluation is reported, so generalization to unseen readers is unknown. Length/readability are included, but additional controls (length-matched analyses, partial correlations) would strengthen causal claims.

Questions to authors
- Do you normalize gaze features per participant before aggregation? How do you control for inter-reader variability and differing hardware sampling rates?
- Can you report leave-one-reader-out and leave-one-item-type-out (e.g., sarcasm vs non-sarcasm) results to assess generalization to unseen readers and distributions?
- How much of the gaze signal remains predictive after controlling tightly for length and readability (e.g., matched subsets or regression-based residualization)?
- Could you compare against a strong text-only neural baseline (e.g., CNN or simple bi-LSTM) to test whether gaze still provides additive gains?
- What deployment path do you envision without eye-tracking at inference? Have you tried training a text-only student model via distillation from a gaze-augmented teacher, or using predicted gaze from a saliency model?

Overall assessment
The paper is original and thought-provoking, with careful feature engineering and informative analyses demonstrating that eye-tracking can help sentiment classification, especially for irony. However, practicality concerns, modest incremental gains, and limited comparisons to stronger text-only baselines temper the impact. Strengthening the evaluation with reader-robustness controls and a deployment-minded pathway (e.g., gaze distillation or predicted gaze) would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
