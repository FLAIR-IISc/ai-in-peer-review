- Summary: Paper augments sentiment features with eye-tracking–derived cognitive features and reports modest F-score gains on two gaze datasets, especially for irony-heavy instances.

- Contribution 1: Empirical evidence that gaze features yield statistically significant but modest improvements for sentiment detection (e.g., +0.6% on D1, +1.5% on D2 over strong text features).

- Contribution 2: A concrete, implementable feature set combining basic gaze measures, graph-based saliency features, and sarcasm/thwarting textual features that improve over sentiment-only baselines.

- Contribution 3: Diagnostic analyses showing many gaze features rank highly and larger gains on complex constructs (notably irony vs non-irony).

- Strength 1: Novel and well-motivated idea linking psycholinguistic theory (fixations, regressions) to sentiment analysis.

- Strength 2: Empirical support across two independent datasets with different eye-trackers and cohorts, with statistical tests confirming small consistent gains.

- Strength 3: Targeted benefit on hard cases: gaze features help more on irony/thwarting examples where textual cues are weak.

- Strength 4: Clear, reproducible feature definitions (including PCA caps) and a transparent feature engineering pipeline.

- Strength 5: Sensible baselines and ablation-style comparisons showing incremental contributions of feature blocks.

- Weakness 1: Limited practicality — approach requires eye-tracking at test time and lacks explored deployment paths (predicted gaze, distillation).

- Weakness 2: Possible task- and dataset-induced confounds since gaze was collected during annotation and datasets may be skewed (e.g., many sarcastic items).

- Weakness 3: Modest incremental gains from gaze over strong text features; gaze-only models perform poorly versus text baselines, raising cost-effectiveness concerns.

- Weakness 4: Missing comparisons to stronger text-only models (e.g., CNN/RNN), leaving unclear whether gaze adds value over modern neural baselines.

- Weakness 5: Insufficient control for reader/hardware variability and no leave-one-reader-out evaluation to assess generalization across readers and sampling rates.

- Question 1: Are gaze features normalized per participant and how are inter-reader/hardware differences handled?

- Question 2: Can you report leave-one-reader-out and leave-one-item-type-out results to show generalization?

- Question 3: How much gaze signal remains predictive after tightly controlling for length and readability (matched or residualized analyses)?

- Question 4: Can you compare against a strong text-only neural baseline (e.g., CNN or bi-LSTM) to contextualize gains?

- Question 5: What deployment path do you envision without real-time eye-tracking — have you tried distillation or predicted gaze approaches?

- Overall assessment: Original and thought-provoking work with careful analyses showing gaze helps for irony, but impact is tempered by practicality concerns, modest gains, and missing stronger baselines and reader-robustness evaluations.
