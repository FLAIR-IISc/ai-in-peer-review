Summary and contributions
This paper augments traditional text-based features for sentence-level sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movement patterns. The authors design both basic gaze features (fixations, saccades, regressions, skips) and novel graph-based gaze features (gaze-saliency graphs), and evaluate on two public eye-tracking datasets rich in sarcasm/irony. They report statistically significant F-score gains over text-only baselines and show larger gains on a held-out subset containing complex constructs.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence that eye-tracking–derived features yield consistent, statistically significant improvements for sentiment polarity detection, particularly for sentences with sarcasm/irony. This includes a useful analysis showing larger gains on “hard” items.
2) A novel feature design for gaze signals, including gaze-saliency graphs and degree-based summaries, grounded in psycholinguistic findings and tailored to SA.
3) A strong, unified text-only baseline that combines standard sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features, clarifying where gaze adds value beyond well-engineered textual features.

Strengths
- Clear, psycholinguistically motivated feature design: The mapping from reading behavior to potential sources of linguistic difficulty is well argued (e.g., first-fixation duration for lexical complexity, regressions for reanalysis, long cross-sentence regressions for incongruity in sarcasm). The gaze-saliency graph and degree-based features are a creative way to capture distributed processing and interaction between words/phrases.

- Consistent improvements and targeted analysis: Across two datasets (different domains and eye-trackers), adding gaze to strong text features improves SVM F-score by +0.6 (D1) and +1.5 (D2). While modest overall, the held-out analysis shows larger gains on irony/sarcasm (+6.1% vs +2.1% for non-irony), directly supporting the hypothesis that gaze helps with nuanced phenomena traditional text features miss.

- Strong text-only baselines and ablations: The paper doesn’t rely on a weak strawman; the sarcasm/thwarting feature set (Sn+Sr) already yields substantial gains over unigrams (+3.1 on D1, +7.8 on D2), which makes the incremental gain from gaze more credible. The chi-squared feature ranking further shows multiple gaze features among the top features on both datasets.

- Use of public datasets with different hardware: Evaluations on SR Research (500Hz) and Tobii (120Hz) datasets mitigate concerns that the results hinge on a particular device or collection protocol. Within-dataset CV avoids cross-device confounds while demonstrating robustness to different tracking regimes.

- Statistical testing: The use of McNemar tests to establish significance for small deltas is appropriate and missing from many feature-comparison papers; this strengthens the empirical claims.

Weaknesses
- Practicality and deployment story remain weak: The method requires eye-tracking at inference time. The paper’s feasibility section is speculative (mobile eye-tracking, opt-in), without an accompanying learning setup that uses gaze only at training time (e.g., multi-view, student–teacher, distillation) and deploys text-only at test time. Given that most SA use-cases cannot collect gaze at scale, the practical impact is limited. Moreover, once strong sarcasm features are included, the incremental gains from gaze are small (+0.6 to +1.5 F), which further narrows the real-world trade-off.

- Potential evaluation confounds and missing controls: 
  - Cross-validation protocol is unclear regarding preprocessing leakage. PCA dimensionality reduction for unigrams and implicit incongruity is mentioned, but it is not stated whether PCA is fit only on training folds. Fitting PCA on the full dataset would leak information.
  - The gaze features are computed from annotators who also provide labels. A leave-one-subject-out (LOSO) evaluation would better test whether gaze patterns generalize across readers and avoid coupling idiosyncratic reader behavior with labels.
  - The “held-out” set is “randomly derived from Dataset-1,” so it is not truly out-of-domain; it’s unclear whether decisions about features/hyperparameters were influenced by the same data distribution.

- Baselines do not include contemporary neural text-only models: By 2014–2016, CNNs/RNNs for sentence sentiment (e.g., Santos & Gatti 2014; recursive models) and strong linear models with character n-grams were standard. The comparison intentionally omits these, but then claims state-of-the-art improvements over “traditional features.” Without such baselines, it is hard to gauge whether gaze still adds value over stronger text-only learners that often capture long-range dependencies and subtle cues.

- Reporting clarity and metrics: It is not specified whether F-scores are macro- or micro-averaged, which matters due to class imbalance (e.g., D1). Confidence intervals are absent. Some gaze-only configurations underperform random class-frequency baselines in F, but this is not discussed. Normalization details (e.g., how durations are normalized across participants and sentences; handling variability in sampling rates) are not fully specified.

- Limited ablation on gaze features: Beyond a ranked list, there is no systematic ablation to identify which gaze features (e.g., REG, RSF, LREG vs ED, degree-based features) drive gains, nor an error analysis tying specific gaze signals to specific error reductions (e.g., thwarting vs implicit sentiment). This makes it harder to transfer insights.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Were PCA, feature scaling, and any learned components (e.g., IMPLICIT_PCA, NGRAM_PCA) fit exclusively on training folds within each CV split? If not, please re-run with proper isolation.

- Subject generalization: Can you report a leave-one-subject-out (or leave-some-subjects-out) CV to assess whether gaze features generalize across readers, not just sentences?

- Metrics: Are the reported F-scores macro- or micro-averaged? Given class imbalance, please report both accuracy and macro-F, with confidence intervals.

- Gaze normalization: How did you normalize fixation durations and counts across annotators and hardware (500Hz vs 120Hz)? Were features z-scored per subject before aggregation?

- Stronger text baselines: Can you add a bi-LSTM/CNN baseline and a linear SVM with character n-grams? Do gaze features still add significant gains over those?

- Training-only gaze: Can you attempt a co-training/multi-view or distillation setup where gaze supervises a text-only model, to address deployment constraints?

Overall, this is a creative and well-motivated use of cognitive signals for SA with promising, statistically supported gains on hard cases. Addressing evaluation rigor and deployment feasibility would substantially strengthen the contribution.
