

# Peer Review: Leveraging Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of human readers. The authors claim that these cognitive features improve sentiment classification, particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. Demonstrating that cognitive features from eye-tracking data significantly improve sentiment analysis for complex linguistic constructs (irony/sarcasm), with a 6.1% F-score improvement on held-out complex cases.
2. Developing a comprehensive feature set that integrates traditional sentiment features, sarcasm/irony features, and novel gaze-based features.
3. Providing a practical feasibility analysis for mobile eye-tracking in real-world sentiment analysis applications.

## Strengths

1. **Compelling evidence for cognitive features' effectiveness on complex cases**: The held-out dataset experiment specifically targeting irony/sarcasm (Table 4) provides strong evidence that cognitive features are particularly valuable for the most challenging sentiment analysis cases. The 6.1% relative improvement for complex constructs versus 2.1% for simple texts (both statistically significant) strongly supports the authors' hypothesis that cognitive features help handle linguistic nuances.

2. **Robust statistical validation**: The authors provide rigorous statistical validation with McNemar tests (p=0.0001 for Dataset 1) and feature significance analysis (Table 3), demonstrating that gaze features are among the top-ranked features. This goes beyond simple accuracy comparisons and provides evidence that the cognitive features are meaningfully contributing to classification.

3. **Practical feasibility discussion**: Section 7 thoughtfully addresses key practical barriers (hardware availability, consent, applicability scenarios) and provides concrete examples of how mobile eye-tracking could be integrated into existing applications. This elevates the work beyond theoretical novelty to practical consideration.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art deep learning approaches**: The authors explicitly state they don't consider deep learning methods, but this is a significant oversight given that deep learning approaches (e.g., Maas et al. 2011, dos Santos and Gatti 2014) have become the standard for sentiment analysis. Without comparison to these methods, the claimed 9.3% improvement is difficult to contextualize in current NLP practice.

2. **Questionable ecological validity of eye-tracking data**: The eye-tracking data comes from annotators who are specifically reading the text for sentiment annotation, not general readers. This creates a potential bias where the cognitive features may reflect annotation behavior rather than natural reading patterns, limiting the generalizability of the findings.

3. **Insufficient discussion of ethical and privacy implications**: The paper completely omits discussion of critical ethical concerns around continuous eye-tracking data collection, which would be a major barrier to real-world adoption. Without addressing privacy concerns, consent mechanisms, and data security, the feasibility discussion is incomplete.

4. **Scalability concerns unaddressed**: The authors acknowledge that eye-tracking requires specialized hardware, yet don't provide any discussion of the cost or practicality of deploying this at scale. For sentiment analysis to be useful in real-world applications, it would need to be applicable to millions of users, not just a small subset with specialized equipment.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you address the potential bias introduced by using annotators' eye movements rather than general readers' eye movements? Could you provide a pilot study comparing these two groups?

2. Given the growing dominance of deep learning approaches in sentiment analysis, how would your cognitive features integrate with or complement these methods? Would they be applicable to end-to-end neural architectures?

3. What specific privacy safeguards would you propose for collecting and storing eye-tracking data in real-world applications, and how would you obtain meaningful user consent for this sensitive biometric data?

4. How would you handle the significant cost barrier of eye-tracking hardware, particularly for applications targeting low-resource settings where sentiment analysis is often most needed?
