- Cognitive features from eye-tracking improve sentiment classification for complex constructs (6.1% F-score gain on held-out irony/sarcasm cases).  
- Developed a comprehensive feature set combining traditional sentiment, sarcasm/irony, and novel gaze-based features.  
- Provides a practical feasibility analysis for mobile eye-tracking integration in real-world applications.  

- Strong evidence that cognitive features are especially effective on complex cases (6.1% vs 2.1% improvement; statistically significant).  
- Robust statistical validation (McNemar tests, p=0.0001 for Dataset 1) and feature significance analysis showing gaze features rank highly.  
- Thoughtful discussion of practical barriers (hardware, consent, application scenarios) for mobile eye-tracking deployment.  

- No comparison with state-of-the-art deep learning approaches, making reported improvements hard to contextualize.  
- Eye-tracking data collected from annotators may bias features toward annotation behavior rather than natural reading, threatening ecological validity.  
- Paper omits discussion of ethical and privacy implications of continuous eye-tracking data collection and storage.  
- Scalability concerns unaddressed—no cost or practicality analysis for deploying eye-tracking at large scale.  

- How will you address potential bias from annotators’ eye movements versus general readers’ eye movements (pilot comparison)?  
- How would cognitive features integrate with or complement end-to-end deep learning sentiment models?  
- What specific privacy safeguards and consent mechanisms would you propose for collecting/storing eye-tracking biometric data?  
- How would you overcome the cost barrier of eye-tracking hardware, especially for low-resource application settings?
