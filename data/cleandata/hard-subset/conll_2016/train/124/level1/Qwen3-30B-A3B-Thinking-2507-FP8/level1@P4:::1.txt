

Summary: This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of readers. The authors claim these cognitive features address challenges at lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic levels in sentiment analysis. They evaluate their approach on two datasets with eye-tracking data, reporting improvements of up to 3.7% and 9.3% in F-score over traditional feature sets.

Main Contributions:
1. A novel feature set incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns into sentiment analysis
2. Experimental validation showing significant improvements for complex sentiment expressions (irony/sarcasm)
3. Feature significance analysis demonstrating the relevance of cognitive features for nuanced sentiment analysis

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a well-motivated approach to addressing a real challenge in sentiment analysis. The authors clearly articulate limitations of current approaches across multiple linguistic levels, making a compelling case for why cognitive features might help with nuanced sentiment expressions.

2. The experimental design is sound with comprehensive evaluation across multiple classifiers (SVM, Naive Bayes, Neural Network) and two datasets. The authors provide statistical significance testing (McNemar test) to validate their improvements, strengthening the technical soundness of their claims.

3. The held-out dataset analysis specifically targeting complex sentiment expressions (irony/sarcasm) is a significant strength. The 6.1% improvement for complex texts versus 2.1% for simple texts provides compelling evidence that cognitive features are particularly valuable for the most challenging sentiment cases.

Weaknesses:
1. The novelty claim is questionable. The paper cites previous work by Joshi et al. (2014) and Mishra et al. (2016) that also uses eye-tracking for sentiment-related tasks. The paper fails to sufficiently differentiate their approach from these prior works or explain how their cognitive features represent a meaningful advancement. The related work section does not adequately address this gap.

2. The paper lacks sufficient methodological detail on how cognitive features are derived from eye-tracking data. While they describe various gaze features, they don't provide enough detail on how these features are computed from raw eye-tracking data, making the approach difficult to reproduce. The graph-based features are particularly opaque.

3. The feasibility discussion (section 7) is insufficient. While the authors mention mobile eye-trackers, they don't provide evidence that these would be practical for large-scale sentiment analysis. The paper doesn't address critical challenges like hardware costs, user consent requirements, and data privacy concerns, which are significant barriers to real-world implementation.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does your cognitive feature set differ from the gaze features used by Mishra et al. (2016) in their sarcasm understandability prediction task? What specific improvements does your approach offer?

2. Please provide more detail on how the cognitive features are computed from raw eye-tracking data, especially the graph-based features. This is essential for reproducibility.

3. Given the significant practical challenges of eye-tracking for sentiment analysis at scale, what specific real-world applications would justify the implementation cost of your approach?

Overall, while the paper presents an interesting approach with promising results for handling complex sentiment expressions, it suffers from questionable novelty claims and insufficient methodological detail. The core idea has potential, but the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this approach offers meaningful advantages over previous work or that it's practically viable for large-scale applications. The strength of the contribution is limited by the lack of clear differentiation from prior work and insufficient detail on feature derivation.
