**Summary:**
This paper proposes to improve sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional textual features with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data. The core hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, such as fixations and regressions, can capture the cognitive load associated with processing complex linguistic nuances like sarcasm and irony. The authors introduce a novel set of gaze-based features and show that their inclusion leads to statistically significant F-score improvements on two datasets, with the most pronounced gains observed for texts containing complex constructs.

**Contributions:**
1.  The primary contribution is the novel application of cognitive signals from eye-tracking data as features for sentiment analysis. This is an innovative, interdisciplinary approach that bridges psycholinguistics and NLP, demonstrating that non-textual data reflecting human cognitive processes can help resolve ambiguities where purely textual features are insufficient.
2.  The second key contribution is a thorough empirical validation showing that these cognitive features provide a measurable performance boost, particularly for the challenging sub-task of classifying sarcastic and ironic texts. The held-out experiment in Section 5.1 provides compelling evidence for this specific benefit.

**Strengths:**
1.  **High Novelty:** The fundamental idea of using cognitive signals to inform a computational model for an NLP task is highly original and opens up a new and promising research direction.
2.  **Strong Experimental Design:** The authors conduct a rigorous evaluation across two datasets. The use of multiple classifiers, detailed feature ablation studies (Table 2), statistical significance testing, and a targeted analysis on complex vs. simple texts (Table 4) provides convincing support for their claims.
3.  **Well-Motivated Feature Engineering:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of cognitive features, from basic gaze metrics to more complex graph-based features, that are well-grounded in and motivated by established principles from psycholinguistics.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Practicality and Scalability:** The method’s reliance on eye-tracking data is a major weakness. This data is expensive and difficult to collect at scale, which severely limits the model's applicability to new texts at inference time, as it would require a new round of human annotation with specialized hardware.
2.  **Modest Performance Gains:** While statistically significant, the absolute improvement in F-score from adding gaze features is relatively small (0.6% on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2 over the strong Sn+Sr baseline). The high cost and effort of data collection may not justify these gains for general-purpose sentiment analysis.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Given the practical limitations of requiring an eye-tracker at inference time, have you considered a two-stage approach? For instance, could a model be trained to first *predict* gaze features from text alone, and these predicted features then be used for the final sentiment classification? This could dramatically improve the method's real-world usability.
