- Proposes augmenting text features with eye-tracking–derived cognitive features for sentiment analysis.
- Hypothesis: eye-movement patterns reflect cognitive effort and help detect nuanced language like sarcasm and irony.
- Engineers "gaze features" from two datasets and shows improved classifier performance, especially on complex pragmatic cases.
- Contribution: introduces a novel paradigm of using cognitive/eye-tracking features as predictive signals for NLP tasks.
- Contribution: provides empirical evidence that gaze features particularly improve detection of sarcasm/irony (larger F-score gains).
- Strength: high novelty and potential impact by bridging psycholinguistics and computational linguistics.
- Strength: thorough experimental design—two datasets, multiple classifiers, significance testing, and feature importance analysis.
- Strength: strong targeted analysis showing a 6.1% F-score improvement on ironic texts vs 2.1% on simpler texts.
- Weakness: major practicality and scalability issues—requires eye-tracking at inference, limiting real-world deployment.
- Weakness: potential confound with general reading difficulty—gaze signals may reflect textual complexity rather than sentiment-specific processing.
- Weakness: excludes comparison to deep learning state-of-the-art and lacks discussion of integrating gaze features with neural models.
- Question: can gaze features be used as supervisory signals (e.g., for annotation, curriculum learning) given inference-time constraints?
- Question: request for further analysis to disentangle effects of sarcasm processing from general textual complexity/readability.
- Question: did the authors explore using inter-reader variance or other distributional gaze statistics as signals of ambiguity or sarcasm?
