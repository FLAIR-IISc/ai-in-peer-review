**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes augmenting standard feature sets for sentiment analysis with a novel class of "cognitive features" derived from the eye-movement patterns of human readers. The central hypothesis is that gaze patterns, such as fixations and regressions, reflect the cognitive effort required to understand nuanced and complex text, including sarcasm and irony. The authors engineer a set of basic and complex gaze features (derived from a proposed "gaze-saliency graph") from two existing datasets that contain short texts with sentiment labels and corresponding eye-tracking data. They conduct experiments using SVM, Naïve Bayes, and Neural Network classifiers, showing that the inclusion of these cognitive features improves sentiment polarity classification F-score by up to 3.7% and 9.3% on the two datasets, respectively. A feature significance analysis and an experiment on a held-out set of ironic vs. non-ironic texts are presented to demonstrate that the proposed features are particularly effective for handling linguistically complex constructs.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **Introduction of Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the novel application of features derived from human eye-tracking data to the task of sentiment classification. This represents a new direction for the field, moving beyond purely text-based signals to incorporate psycholinguistic indicators of human text processing.
2.  **Empirical Validation on Complex Language:** The paper provides a well-structured empirical study showing that these cognitive features provide a significant performance boost, particularly for texts containing complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm and irony, which are known challenges for traditional sentiment analysis systems.
3.  **Comprehensive Feature Engineering from Gaze Data:** The work details a thorough feature engineering process, proposing both direct statistical aggregations of gaze metrics (e.g., fixation duration, regression count) and more complex graph-based features derived from a novel "gaze-saliency graph" representation.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of using eye-tracking data as a feature source for a downstream NLP task is highly original. It effectively bridges the fields of computational linguistics and psycholinguistics, exploring a new modality of information for a well-established problem. This approach has the potential to inspire new research directions in multimodal and cognitively-informed NLP.
2.  **Sound Experimental Methodology:** The experimental setup is logical and well-designed. The authors first establish the difficulty of the datasets for standard methods, then systematically perform feature ablation studies to isolate the impact of their proposed cognitive features. The use of multiple classifiers strengthens the conclusion that the performance gain is attributable to the features themselves. The targeted experiment on a held-out set of ironic vs. non-ironic sentences provides compelling evidence for the paper's central hypothesis.
3.  **Thorough Analysis and Presentation:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized. The features are described in sufficient detail to be understood, and the feature significance analysis (Table 3) transparently shows that the new cognitive features are among the most discriminative. The authors also include a thoughtful discussion on the real-world feasibility of their approach (Section 7), proactively addressing a key potential criticism regarding the requirement of specialized data.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Practicality and Generalizability:** The method's primary weakness is its reliance on eye-tracking data for inference. This makes the trained models inapplicable to standard text corpora or real-world scenarios where such data is unavailable. While the authors discuss future availability of mobile eye-trackers, this remains speculative. The contribution is therefore more of a proof-of-concept in a controlled setting rather than a readily deployable solution for sentiment analysis.
2.  **Small-Scale Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted on two relatively small datasets (fewer than 1000 instances each). While these datasets are unique, conclusions drawn from such small-scale experiments may not generalize to larger, more diverse data distributions. The performance gains, while statistically significant on this data, need to be validated on a larger scale to be considered robust.
3.  **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The authors explicitly decline to compare their feature-based method to contemporary deep learning models, which were already producing state-of-the-art results for sentiment analysis in 2016. By restricting comparisons to traditional feature-based classifiers, the paper does not establish whether the information captured by cognitive features is orthogonal to or subsumed by what powerful neural architectures can learn from text alone. The absolute F-scores of the best-performing text-only systems are also moderate, which may inflate the relative impact of the new features.
4.  **Confounding Variables in Feature Aggregation:** The cognitive features are created by averaging gaze metrics across several participants. This aggregation process may obscure important inter-annotator variations that could signal ambiguity. Furthermore, while the authors control for basic text readability, it remains unclear to what extent the cognitive features are simply a more complex proxy for textual difficulty (which could potentially be captured by other advanced textual features) rather than being specific to the cognitive load of sentiment processing.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The results for Dataset 2 in Table 2 present a puzzling outcome. For the SVM classifier, adding gaze features to the basic sentiment features (`Sn+Gz`) causes a drastic drop in F-score from 62.5% to 48.9%. However, adding gaze features to the more comprehensive `Sn+Sr` set results in an improvement (from 70.3% to 71.8%). Could you provide an explanation for this apparent contradiction? Why would gaze features be detrimental in one combination but beneficial in another on the same dataset and with the same classifier?
2.  The central challenge to deployment is the need for gaze data at inference time. Have you considered methods to mitigate this dependency? For instance, could a model be trained in a multi-task framework to predict sentiment and *also* predict the cognitive feature values from text alone? Such an approach might enable the model to learn text representations that implicitly encode cognitive load, thereby making the final sentiment classifier applicable to text without accompanying gaze data.
