- Summary: Proposes augmenting text-based sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking, hypothesizing gaze patterns capture cognitive load for nuances like sarcasm/irony; inclusion of novel gaze features yields statistically significant F-score gains on two datasets, especially for complex texts.

- Contribution 1: Novel application of eye-tracking cognitive signals as features for sentiment analysis, bridging psycholinguistics and NLP.

- Contribution 2: Thorough empirical validation showing measurable performance boosts, particularly for sarcastic/ironic texts, with compelling held-out experiments.

- Strength 1: High novelty—introduces a new interdisciplinary research direction using cognitive signals.

- Strength 2: Strong experimental design—multiple datasets and classifiers, ablation studies, significance testing, and targeted analyses.

- Strength 3: Well-motivated feature engineering—comprehensive gaze-based features grounded in psycholinguistic principles.

- Weakness 1: Practicality and scalability concerns—eye-tracking data is costly and hard to collect at inference time, limiting applicability.

- Weakness 2: Modest absolute gains—the F-score improvements are small (0.6% and 1.5% over strong baseline), possibly insufficient given data-collection costs.

- Question: Have you considered a two-stage approach where gaze features are predicted from text and those predicted features are used for final sentiment classification to avoid needing eye-trackers at inference?
