- Summary: Augments traditional linguistic features with eye-tracking–derived cognitive features for sentiment analysis, yielding up to 9.3% F-score gain and larger improvements on ironic/sarcastic texts.

- Contribution 1: Novel integration of eye-tracking (cognitive) signals as direct features for sentiment classification.

- Contribution 2: Empirical evidence that cognitive features particularly help resolve hard pragmatic cases (sarcasm/irony).

- Contribution 3: A comprehensive engineered set of gaze-based features, including fixation, saccade, regression metrics and gaze-saliency graph properties.

- Strength 1: High novelty and potential impact by bridging psycholinguistics and NLP, offering an orthogonal signal for difficult phenomena.

- Strength 2: Strong, well-designed experiments across two datasets, multiple classifiers, and ablation studies, plus a held-out test contrasting simple vs. complex texts.

- Strength 3: Clear motivation and feature-significance analysis showing gaze features are informative, not redundant.

- Strength 4: Thoughtful discussion of practicality and future scenarios (e.g., mobile eye-trackers) acknowledging limitations.

- Weakness 1: Major practicality and scalability concerns due to reliance on specialized eye-tracking hardware, limiting real-world applicability.

- Weakness 2: No comparison with contemporary state-of-the-art deep learning models (CNNs/RNNs), leaving unclear how gains compare or integrate with neural approaches.

- Weakness 3: Potential confound that gaze features reflect general reading difficulty rather than sentiment-specific processes; more controlled comparisons are needed.

- Weakness 4: Information loss from averaging gaze features across participants, possibly discarding useful individual-difference signals.

- Question 1: Why does Dataset 2 (lower-quality 120Hz tracker) show a much larger gain (+9.3%) than Dataset 1 (+3.7%)—could dataset composition or annotator counts explain this?

- Question 2: Why were neural sentiment models not compared or integrated—do authors believe cognitive features capture signals inaccessible to models like LSTMs?

- Question 3: Did authors try non-averaging approaches (treating each text–user pair separately and aggregating predictions) to preserve individual gaze patterns?
