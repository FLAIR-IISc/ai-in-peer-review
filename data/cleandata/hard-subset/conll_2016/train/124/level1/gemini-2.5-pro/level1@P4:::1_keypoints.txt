- Summary: Introduces cognitive features from human eye-tracking (fixations, regressions, gaze-saliency graph) to augment sentiment analysis; shows up to 3.7% and 9.3% F-score gains on two datasets and improved handling of sarcasm/irony.

- Contribution 1: Novel use of eye-tracking–derived cognitive features for sentiment classification, bringing psycholinguistic signals into NLP.

- Contribution 2: Empirical evidence that cognitive features improve performance, especially on linguistically complex texts (sarcasm/irony).

- Contribution 3: Detailed feature engineering including basic gaze statistics and graph-based gaze-saliency features.

- Strength 1: Highly original multimodal idea bridging computational linguistics and psycholinguistics with potential to inspire new research.

- Strength 2: Sound experimental design with dataset difficulty analysis, ablation studies, multiple classifiers, and targeted ironic vs. non-ironic evaluation.

- Strength 3: Clear writing and transparent feature significance analysis; authors discuss real-world feasibility proactively.

- Weakness 1: Limited practicality because reliance on eye-tracking data makes models inapplicable to standard corpora and real-world settings without such data.

- Weakness 2: Small-scale evaluation (two datasets <1000 instances each) limits generalizability and robustness of the results.

- Weakness 3: No comparison to strong contemporary deep learning baselines, leaving unclear whether cognitive features add information beyond powerful text models.

- Weakness 4: Aggregating gaze metrics across participants may mask inter-annotator variation and could merely reflect textual difficulty rather than sentiment-specific cognitive load.

- Question 1: Why do gaze features drastically hurt SVM performance when added to basic sentiment features on Dataset 2 (62.5% → 48.9%) but help when added to a richer feature set (70.3% → 71.8%)?

- Question 2: Have you considered learning or predicting cognitive features from text (e.g., via multitask learning) to remove the requirement for gaze data at inference time?
