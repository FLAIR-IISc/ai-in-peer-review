- Summary: Augments text-based sentiment analysis with eye-tracking–derived cognitive features, yielding modest but statistically significant F-score improvements on two datasets, especially for sarcastic/ironic texts.

- Contribution: Novel interdisciplinary idea of using human cognitive (gaze) signals as features for NLP sentiment classification.

- Contribution: Empirical validation showing larger performance gains on complex (ironic/sarcastic) texts via held-out tests.

- Contribution: Introduces new gaze-based features, including aggregated fixation/regression stats and a gaze-saliency graph.

- Strength: High novelty—first to directly use eye-tracking signals as model inputs for supervised NLP tasks.

- Strength: Thorough experimental design with two datasets, multiple classifiers, ablation studies, held-out tests, and statistical significance checks.

- Strength: Proactive discussion of practicality and long-term prospects for mobile eye-tracking deployment.

- Weakness: Limited practicality/scalability—requires gaze data at inference, restricting real-world applicability.

- Weakness: Modest performance gains (0.6–1.5% F1) versus high cost/time/equipment required for eye-tracking data.

- Weakness: Potential confounding factors—averaging gaze across readers may conflate sentiment signals with reading ability, fatigue, or text complexity.

- Weakness: No comparison with contemporary deep models (e.g., BERT) or discussion of integration with neural architectures.

- Question: Clarify deployment—does the model require eye-tracking for every new instance, or can it operate on text-only inputs after training?

- Question: Have you considered using gaze patterns only from annotators who correctly identify sentiment to reduce noise?

- Question: Given the small gains, in which real-world scenarios is the cost-benefit trade-off of collecting eye-tracking data justified?
