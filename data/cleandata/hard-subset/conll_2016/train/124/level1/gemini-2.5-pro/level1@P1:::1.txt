### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with "cognitive features" derived from the eye-movement patterns of human readers. The central hypothesis is that the cognitive effort required to understand nuanced sentiment, such as sarcasm or irony, is reflected in gaze patterns (e.g., fixations, regressions). By quantifying these patterns and using them as features in a machine learning model, the authors aim to improve classification accuracy, particularly for linguistically complex texts. The method is evaluated on two publicly available datasets containing sentiment-annotated texts and corresponding eye-tracking data. The results show that the inclusion of cognitive features provides a statistically significant, albeit modest, improvement in F-score over a strong baseline that already includes features for sentiment and sarcasm.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Interdisciplinary Approach:** The primary contribution is the novel idea of using cognitive signals from human readers (via eye-tracking) as a feature source for a downstream NLP task. This is a creative and promising direction that bridges psycholinguistics with computational linguistics, suggesting that signals from human processing can help models tackle complex semantic and pragmatic phenomena that are difficult to capture from text alone.
2.  **Targeted Empirical Validation:** A key contribution is the empirical validation showing that these cognitive features are most impactful for texts containing complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. The experiment on a held-out set, which demonstrates a substantially larger performance gain on ironic texts compared to simpler ones, provides strong evidence for the authors' core hypothesis.
3.  **Introduction of New Gaze-based Features:** The paper proposes a set of cognitive features, including both basic aggregated statistics (e.g., fixation duration, regression count) and more complex features derived from a novel "gaze-saliency graph." This provides a useful feature engineering framework for future work in this area.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Originality:** The core idea of "leveraging cognitive features" for sentiment analysis is highly original and thought-provoking. While eye-tracking is well-established in psycholinguistics, its direct application as a feature source to improve a supervised NLP classification task is new. It opens up an interesting new research avenue for using human-in-the-loop signals not just for annotation, but as part of the model's input itself.
2.  **Thorough Experimental Design:** The evaluation is well-structured and convincing. The authors use two different datasets, multiple classifiers, and perform a careful ablation study (Table 2) that clearly isolates the contribution of each feature set (sentiment, sarcasm, and gaze). The inclusion of a held-out test (Table 4) to specifically measure performance on complex vs. simple texts is a major strength that directly supports their claims. The use of statistical significance tests adds rigor to the reported results.
3.  **Proactive Discussion of Practicality:** The authors anticipate the most significant criticism—the practical feasibility of requiring eye-tracking data—and address it head-on in Section 7. While the vision of ubiquitous mobile eye-tracking is futuristic, this discussion is valuable. It shows that the authors have considered the real-world implications of their work and provides a plausible, long-term path toward making such an approach viable.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Practicality and Scalability:** The central weakness of the proposed method is its reliance on eye-tracking data for inference. The paper is unclear about the intended deployment scenario. As presented, the model requires gaze features for every new instance it needs to classify, which restricts its use to post-hoc analysis of existing eye-tracked corpora. It cannot function as a general-purpose sentiment analyzer for new, unseen text (e.g., a tweet from a live feed) for which no gaze data exists. This fundamentally limits the applicability of the system in its current form to a very niche research setting.
2.  **Modest Performance Gains vs. High Data Cost:** While the improvements are statistically significant, the absolute gains in F-score from adding gaze features are quite small: 0.6% on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2 for the best-performing SVM model. Given the immense cost, time, and specialized equipment required to collect eye-tracking data, this raises a serious question about the cost-benefit trade-off. It is unclear if this marginal improvement justifies the massive overhead compared to alternative approaches, such as collecting more text-only data or developing more sophisticated text-based models.
3.  **Potential Confounding Factors in Gaze Features:** The cognitive features are derived by averaging gaze data across multiple participants. This process aggregates signals but also risks conflating sentiment-related processing difficulty with other factors. For instance, gaze patterns can be influenced by general reading ability, reader fatigue, or simple textual properties like the use of rare words or complex syntax not directly related to sentiment. While the inclusion of readability features is a good step towards controlling for this, it is difficult to be certain that the captured signal is uniquely about sentiment nuance rather than general cognitive load.
4.  **Lack of Comparison with Modern Architectures:** The authors explicitly exclude comparisons with deep learning models. While their feature-based approach makes this a difficult comparison, it sidesteps the question of where this work fits within the current state of the art. Powerful pre-trained models like BERT have shown remarkable ability to capture subtle linguistic nuances from text alone. A discussion of how this cognitive feature approach compares conceptually, or how it could potentially be integrated into neural architectures (e.g., as an auxiliary signal), would have significantly strengthened the paper's positioning and impact.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the intended application and deployment scenario for your system? Does the model require eye-tracking data to be collected for every new text instance at inference time? If so, this seems to limit its application to research contexts. If not, how do you envision applying a model trained with gaze features to a text-only input?
2.  The features are averaged across all annotators for a given text. This treats all readers equally. Have you considered whether the gaze patterns of annotators who *correctly* identify the sentiment (especially for difficult cases like sarcasm) differ from those who do not? It seems this could be a stronger, less noisy signal to leverage.
3.  Given the modest performance gains (0.6-1.5% F1) relative to the high cost of data acquisition, could you elaborate on the practical value proposition of this approach? In what specific, real-world scenarios would this cost-benefit trade-off be justified over investing in larger text-only datasets or more advanced text-based models?
