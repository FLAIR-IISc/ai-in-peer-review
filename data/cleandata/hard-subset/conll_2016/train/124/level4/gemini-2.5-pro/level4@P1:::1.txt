### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection by augmenting traditional text-based features with a set of "cognitive features" derived from the eye-movement patterns of human readers. The central hypothesis is that the cognitive effort required to understand nuanced or complex text, such as sarcastic sentences, is reflected in gaze patterns (e.g., fixations, regressions) and can be leveraged to improve classification performance. The authors introduce a range of basic and graph-based gaze features, combine them with standard sentiment and sarcasm features, and evaluate their model on two datasets. They report F-score improvements when the cognitive features are included, particularly for texts containing complex constructs like irony.

The main contribution of this work is the introduction and systematic evaluation of cognitive features from eye-tracking data for the task of sentiment analysis. This is a creative approach that opens a new, interdisciplinary line of inquiry for tackling complex semantic and pragmatic phenomena in natural language processing.

### Strengths
1.  **High Novelty and Potential for Impact:** The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. To my knowledge, this is the first work to systematically incorporate features derived from eye-tracking data into a mainstream NLP task like sentiment analysis. This is a creative and thought-provoking approach that has the potential to inspire similar investigations in other complex NLP problems where human cognitive effort might be a useful signal (e.g., ambiguity resolution, text summarization, or machine translation evaluation).

2.  **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is well-conducted. The authors test their approach on two different datasets using multiple standard classifiers. They responsibly compare their full feature set against strong baselines that include not just sentiment features (Sn) but also sarcasm-specific features (Sr). The inclusion of a feature significance analysis (Table 3) and a targeted experiment on a held-out dataset to isolate the impact on complex vs. simple texts (Table 4) provides compelling evidence supporting their claims.

3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors do an excellent job of motivating the problem and explaining the various feature sets, including the novel and complex gaze-based features. This clarity makes the methodology easy to understand and the work potentially reproducible.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Insufficient Justification for Polarity Detection:** The conceptual link between the proposed cognitive features and the specific task of determining sentiment *polarity* (positive vs. negative) is not sufficiently justified. While it is intuitive that complex constructs like sarcasm might require more cognitive processing (e.g., more regressions, longer fixations), these signals seem more indicative of the *presence* of linguistic complexity or reading difficulty, rather than the resulting polarity itself. The paper does not convincingly explain how these gaze patterns would differ, for instance, between a sarcastically positive statement and a sarcastically negative one, assuming all other factors are equal. The features appear to signal "something complex is happening here" but not necessarily "the final polarity is positive."

2.  **Marginal Performance Gains:** The performance improvements attributed to the cognitive features are quite small, which raises questions about their practical utility. According to the main results in Table 2, adding gaze features (Gz) to the strong baseline of sentiment and sarcasm features (Sn+Sr) improves the F-score by only 0.6% on Dataset 1 (from 72.9% to 73.5% for the SVM) and 1.5% on Dataset 2 (from 70.3% to 71.8%). While the authors show these gains are statistically significant, the modest effect size may not justify the significant overhead and complexity of collecting eye-tracking data at scale.

3.  **Unconvincing Feasibility Argument:** The discussion on the practical feasibility of the approach (Section 7) is speculative and not persuasive. The reliance on eye-tracking data presents a major practical bottleneck for any real-world application. The proposed scenario in Section 7.2, where an e-commerce platform would "watch how readers read the review" using mobile eye-trackers, seems unrealistic in the near future due to significant technical hurdles, as well as formidable privacy and user consent challenges. This limitation positions the work as a primarily academic exploration rather than a readily deployable solution, which tempers its potential impact.
