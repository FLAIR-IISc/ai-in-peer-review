- Proposes augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking (fixation duration, regressions) and shows statistically significant F-score gains, especially for sarcasm/irony.

- Contribution: introduces a novel class of gaze-derived features for sentiment analysis that incorporate human cognitive processing signals.

- Contribution: provides empirical evidence that cognitive features help classify linguistically nuanced texts (sarcasm/irony), with larger gains on an ironic held-out set.

- Strength: highly novel idea bridging psycholinguistics and computational linguistics, suggesting reading behavior can complement text signals.

- Strength: thorough feature engineering and analysis, including basic and complex gaze features, a gaze-saliency graph, ablation studies, significance tests, and feature importance analysis.

- Strength: proactively discusses practical feasibility and scalability of eye-tracking (mobile/inexpensive trackers) and potential application scenarios.

- Weakness: evaluation mixes disparate text domains (movie reviews, tweets, quotes) without domain-specific analysis, making reported F-scores hard to interpret.

- Weakness: unclear training protocol for data-dependent textual features (NGRAM_PCA, IMPLICIT_PCA) in 10-fold CV—risk of data leakage if not learned per fold.

- Weakness: limited comparison to stronger baselines (no deep models, not compared to published competitive feature-based systems); absolute gains are modest (0.6% and 1.5%).

- Question: please justify combining different text domains and provide domain-specific results to show cognitive feature effectiveness per text type.

- Question: clarify whether NGRAM_PCA and IMPLICIT_PCA were learned exclusively from training folds in each CV split or from the entire dataset.

- Question: confirm that baseline training data (Pang & Lee, Sentiment140) and the test sets (Dataset 1 and 2) are completely disjoint.
