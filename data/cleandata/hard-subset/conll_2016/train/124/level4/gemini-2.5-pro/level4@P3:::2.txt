### Summary

This paper proposes augmenting traditional feature sets for sentiment analysis with "cognitive features" derived from the eye-movement patterns of human readers. The central hypothesis is that eye-tracking data, such as fixation duration and regressions, can serve as a proxy for the cognitive effort required to understand complex linguistic constructs like sarcasm and irony. The authors define a set of basic and complex gaze-based features, integrate them into a feature-based classification framework, and evaluate their approach on two datasets containing nuanced short texts. The results show that the inclusion of cognitive features provides a statistically significant improvement in F-score over systems using only textual features, particularly for sentences containing sarcasm or irony.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Feature Set for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution of this work is the introduction and evaluation of a novel class of features for sentiment analysis derived from eye-tracking data. This represents an innovative approach that moves beyond purely textual information to incorporate signals related to human cognitive processing during reading.

2.  **Empirical Evidence for Handling Linguistic Nuance:** The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that these cognitive features are particularly effective for correctly classifying texts with complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. The targeted experiment on a held-out dataset (Table 4) successfully demonstrates that the performance gain from gaze features is substantially larger for ironic texts than for simpler ones.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Interesting Research Direction:** The core idea of using eye-tracking data as a signal for cognitive load in a sentiment analysis task is highly innovative. It bridges the gap between psycholinguistics and computational linguistics, opening up a promising avenue for research. By showing that how a person reads a sentence can be as informative as what the sentence contains, the work challenges conventional text-only approaches and encourages the exploration of multi-modal signals for NLP tasks.

2.  **Thorough Feature Engineering and Analysis:** The authors conduct a detailed and systematic evaluation. They carefully design a wide range of cognitive features, from basic statistics (fixation count, regression count) to more complex ones derived from a novel "gaze-saliency graph." The experiments methodically compare the performance of different feature combinations (Sentiment, Sentiment+Sarcasm, Sentiment+Sarcasm+Gaze), providing a clear ablation study that isolates the contribution of each feature set. The inclusion of statistical significance tests and a feature importance analysis further strengthens the empirical claims.

3.  **Proactive Discussion of Practical Feasibility:** A commendable aspect of the paper is Section 7, which directly addresses the most obvious practical limitation of the approach: the requirement of eye-tracking data. By discussing the increasing availability of inexpensive, mobile eye-trackers and providing potential application scenarios, the authors make a convincing case for the future relevance and scalability of their method. This foresight strengthens the paper's potential impact beyond a purely academic contribution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Conflated Datasets in Evaluation:** A significant weakness of the experimental design is the use of datasets that mix different domains (movie reviews, tweets, quotes) without separate analysis. The linguistic characteristics and baseline performance for sentiment analysis differ substantially between formal reviews and informal tweets. By combining them, the reported F-scores in Table 2 are difficult to interpret and compare with established results in the literature. For instance, an F-score of 73.5% on movie reviews would be relatively low compared to the state-of-the-art, whereas for Twitter data, it could be quite strong. A more insightful evaluation would involve training and testing on domain-specific subsets to demonstrate the effectiveness of the features in different contexts.

2.  **Ambiguity in Training Protocol for Textual Features:** The description of how textual features are derived within the 10-fold cross-validation setup is unclear. For instance, features like `NGRAM_PCA` and `IMPLICIT_PCA` are dependent on a vocabulary or set of phrases learned from a "training corpus." In a cross-validation setting, it is crucial to clarify whether these features are learned anew from the 9 training folds in each split to avoid any data leakage from the test fold. A clearer explanation of this process is needed to ensure the reproducibility and validity of the results.

3.  **Limited Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The paper explicitly sets aside deep learning models, which were already prominent for sentiment analysis in 2016. While the focus is on a feature-engineering approach, the baselines used for comparison (`Sn` and `Sn+Sr`) are constructed by the authors themselves. The paper would be more convincing if it demonstrated improvement over other strong, published feature-based systems on these specific datasets. The absolute F-score improvements from adding gaze features are modest (0.6% on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2), and benchmarking against more competitive baselines would provide better context for the significance of this contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a justification for combining different text domains (reviews, tweets) in your datasets? Would it be possible to report domain-specific results from your experiments to show how the cognitive features perform on each text type individually?

2.  In your 10-fold cross-validation experiments (Section 5), how were the data-dependent textual features, specifically `NGRAM_PCA` and `IMPLICIT_PCA`, generated? Were the principal components and bootstrapped phrases learned exclusively from the training portion of each fold, or were they learned once from the entire dataset?

3.  Regarding the baseline systems in Section 3.3, could you please confirm that the training data (from Pang & Lee and Sentiment140) and the test sets (Dataset 1 and Dataset 2) are completely disjoint, even if they draw from the same original sources?
