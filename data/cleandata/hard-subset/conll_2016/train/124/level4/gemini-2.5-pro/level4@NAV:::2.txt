This paper introduces eye-tracking data as a form of cognitive feature for sentiment analysis. The core idea of using eye-tracking metrics as a proxy for cognitive load in this context is both interesting and novel.

The discussion of the features and the comparative analysis of various feature sets are particularly clear and insightful. Furthermore, the inclusion of Section 7, which addresses the practical feasibility of the proposed approach, is a commendable strength of the work.

The evaluation could be strengthened by not mixing disparate domains within the datasets, such as the movie review corpus and the Twitter corpus. Training and testing on domain-specific data might improve predictive performance for each respective domain and would certainly make the results more interpretable. As it stands, the results in Table 2 appear somewhat low when compared to the state-of-the-art for the Pang and Lee dataset, but they are considerably more competitive when viewed in the context of Twitter sentiment analysis, which suggests a domain-mixing effect.

For clarification regarding Section 3.3: can the authors confirm that there are no overlapping instances between the training and testing sets for datasets 1 and 2, even though they originate from the same sources (i.e., Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140)?

Minor suggestion: The frequent use of bold formatting in the text can be distracting.
