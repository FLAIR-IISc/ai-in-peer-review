This paper introduces an interesting approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating eye-tracking features as a proxy for cognitive load. The core idea is well-motivated, and the exploration of cognitive features in this context is a novel contribution.

The discussion and comparison of the proposed feature sets are clear and insightful. A particular strength of the paper is the inclusion of Section 7, which addresses the practical feasibility of the approach. This thoughtful consideration of real-world applicability is a commendable and valuable addition.

A primary concern regarding the experimental setup is the conflation of different domains (movie reviews and tweets) within the datasets. The evaluation could be significantly strengthened by training and testing on domain-specific datasets separately. This would likely provide a clearer picture of the model's performance within each domain and might also lead to improved predictive accuracy by preventing negative transfer between disparate text styles.

Furthermore, separating the domains would make the results easier to interpret and compare with existing work. As it stands, the performance reported in Table 2 is difficult to contextualize; it appears somewhat low when compared to state-of-the-art results on the Pang and Lee movie review dataset, yet seems more competitive when compared to typical benchmarks on Twitter data. A domain-specific evaluation would allow for a more direct and meaningful comparison with established results in the literature.

A point of clarification regarding Section 3.3: Could the authors confirm that there are no overlapping text snippets between the training and testing sets for datasets 1 and 2, even though they are drawn from the same original sources (e.g., Pang & Lee, Sentiment140)?

Minor comment: The extensive use of bold formatting throughout the text can be distracting and may hinder readability.
