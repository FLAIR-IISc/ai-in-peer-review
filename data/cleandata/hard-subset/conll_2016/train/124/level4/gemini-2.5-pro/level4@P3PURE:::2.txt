This paper introduces the novel idea of using eye-tracking features, as a proxy for cognitive load, for sentiment analysis. The core idea is interesting and well-motivated.

The paper's strengths include a clear discussion and comparison of the proposed feature sets. Furthermore, the authors are to be commended for addressing the practical feasibility of the approach in Section 7.

A primary concern with the evaluation is the conflation of different domains (e.g., movie reviews and tweets) within the datasets. This mixed-domain setup may hinder performance, as training on one domain (e.g., tweets) might not be optimal for predicting sentiment in another (e.g., movie reviews), and vice-versa. Evaluating on single-domain datasets would likely lead to more interpretable results. This issue also complicates the comparison to prior work; for instance, the results reported in Table 2 appear low when compared to state-of-the-art systems on the Pang and Lee movie review dataset, but are more competitive when viewed in the context of Twitter sentiment analysis benchmarks.

A clarifying question regarding Section 3.3: could the authors confirm that there are no overlapping text snippets between the training and testing splits for datasets 1 and 2, given that they are drawn from the same original sources (Pang & Lee, Sentiment140)?

Minor point: The extensive use of bold formatting was occasionally distracting.
