This paper presents a novel approach to sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection by incorporating cognitive features derived from the eye-movement patterns of human annotators. The authors posit this as the first work to leverage such features within the NLP community for these tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making the methodology and experiments easy to comprehend.
2.  The core idea of using cognitive signals is highly innovative and holds significant potential to inspire new lines of research in other NLP domains beyond sentiment analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  The primary weakness lies in the motivation. While the authors effectively collect cognitive data, the theoretical link between these features and the tasks of sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection is not sufficiently justified. For instance, eye-tracking features like gaze duration or fixation counts might plausibly correlate with textual complexity or reading difficulty. However, the paper does not provide a compelling argument for why these specific signals should be indicative of sentiment polarity or sarcasm. A more thorough justification connecting cognitive processing effort to the perception of sentiment is needed.

2.  The empirical results show only marginal performance gains from the inclusion of cognitive features. The comparison between the model with syntactic and semantic features (Sn+Sr) and the model that adds gaze-based features (Sn+Sr+Gz) reveals a very modest improvement. This limited impact raises questions about the practical utility of the proposed features, especially considering the significant overhead required for data collection.

3.  The discussion on the practical feasibility of this approach (Section 7) is not persuasive. The data collection process, which requires specialized eye-tracking equipment for every annotator, presents a significant barrier to scalability and adoption in real-world applications. The hypothetical scenario described in Section 7.2, intended to illustrate a potential application, is also unconvincing and does not adequately address how this technique could be feasibly deployed at scale or why it would be preferable to other methods in that context.
