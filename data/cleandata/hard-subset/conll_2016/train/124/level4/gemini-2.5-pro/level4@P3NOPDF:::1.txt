This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection by incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data. The core idea is to capture the eye-movement patterns of human annotators as they read and label texts, and then use these patterns as features for classification models. The authors claim this is the first work to integrate such cognitive signals into mainstream NLP tasks.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The primary strength of this work lies in its innovative and thought-provoking central idea. The introduction of cognitive features, specifically eye-movement patterns, is a creative direction for the field. This approach has the potential to inspire a new line of research, encouraging the NLP community to explore how human cognitive processes can inform and enhance computational models for various tasks beyond sentiment analysis.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The proposed methodology, data collection process, and experimental setup are presented in a manner that is easy to follow and understand.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Insufficient Motivation:** While the idea of using cognitive features is intriguing, the paper's motivation for their specific application to sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection requires stronger justification. The link between eye-movement patterns and cognitive load or reading ease is intuitive. However, the theoretical connection between these features and the detection of sentiment polarity is not as clearly articulated. The authors could significantly strengthen the paper by providing a more detailed explanation or citing relevant psycholinguistic literature that supports the hypothesis that gaze patterns are reliable indicators of a reader's sentiment assessment, rather than just their processing difficulty.

2.  **Marginal Empirical Improvement:** The empirical results, while showing some improvement, are not entirely convincing of the new features' efficacy. The performance gain observed when adding the gaze features (Gz) to the baseline sentiment and syntax features (Sn+Sr) is marginal. This modest improvement raises questions about the practical utility and the signal-to-noise ratio of the proposed cognitive features, especially when weighed against the significant overhead and cost required to collect eye-tracking data on a large scale.

3.  **Unconvincing Feasibility Argument:** The discussion on the practical feasibility and real-world application of this approach (Section 7) is underdeveloped. The challenges of scalability and the niche applicability of this technique are not fully addressed. For instance, the scenario described in Section 7.2 is not compelling; it is difficult to see how collecting eye-tracking data would be practical or provide a significant advantage over existing methods in that context. A more grounded discussion of potential use cases or a path toward approximating these features without direct eye-tracking would make the paper's contribution more impactful.

In summary, the paper presents a highly original and interesting research direction. However, its contribution is currently limited by a weakly justified link between the features and the task, marginal performance gains, and an unconvincing argument for its practical application.
