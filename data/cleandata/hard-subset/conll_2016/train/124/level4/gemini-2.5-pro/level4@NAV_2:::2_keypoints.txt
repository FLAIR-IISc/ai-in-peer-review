- Paper proposes augmenting sentiment analysis with cognitive features from readers' eye-tracking to better handle nuanced constructs like sarcasm and irony.  
- Authors introduce a novel set of gaze-derived features (fixation durations, regression counts, gaze-saliency graph) combined with standard textual and sarcasm-specific features.  
- Empirical results on two challenging short-text datasets show improved classification, with the largest gains on sarcastic/ironic examples (held-out analysis/Table 4).  
- Strength: Novel idea to use eye-tracking as a cognitive signal for NLP, opening a new multimodal research direction.  
- Strength: Thorough feature engineering and systematic evaluation of feature combinations, with feature significance and targeted ironic vs. non-ironic analyses.  
- Strength: Practical feasibility discussion recognizing limited eye-tracking availability and citing mobile eye-tracking trends.  
- Weakness: Use of mixed-domain datasets (movie reviews, tweets, quotes) and baselines trained on different domains creates confounding variables and hampers interpretation.  
- Weakness: Reported F-scores seem low versus state-of-the-art on standard benchmarks; mixed-domain evaluation makes it hard to validate the authors' explanation.  
- Weakness: Minor presentation issues—excessive bold formatting reduces readability.  
- Question for authors: Please clarify whether there is any overlap between snippets used to train baseline NB/SVM classifiers (from Pang & Lee and Sentiment140) and the test snippets in Dataset 1 and Dataset 2.
