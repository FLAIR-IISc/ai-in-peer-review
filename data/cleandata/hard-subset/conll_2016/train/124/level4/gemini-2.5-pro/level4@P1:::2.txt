### Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach for sentiment analysis by augmenting standard linguistic features with cognitive features derived from human eye-tracking data. The central hypothesis is that eye-movement patterns, which reflect cognitive processing and reading difficulty, can provide valuable signals for identifying the sentiment of texts, particularly those with complex linguistic constructs like sarcasm and irony. The authors engineer a set of "gaze features" from two existing datasets and show that their inclusion improves F-scores for polarity detection by up to 9.3% over a baseline using only traditional textual features. An analysis on a held-out set further suggests that these cognitive features are especially beneficial for classifying challenging, ironic texts.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Feature Set for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the introduction and evaluation of a novel set of cognitive features derived from readers' eye-movement patterns. The authors propose a range of features, from basic gaze statistics (e.g., fixation duration, regression counts) to more complex graph-based metrics derived from "gaze-saliency graphs."
2.  **Empirical Evidence for Handling Complex Texts:** The paper provides empirical evidence that these cognitive features are particularly effective for handling challenging linguistic phenomena such as sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations, where purely textual features often struggle. The experiment on the held-out dataset (Table 4) clearly demonstrates this point.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Compelling Idea:** The central idea of using eye-tracking data as a proxy for cognitive load in sentiment analysis is innovative and compelling. It opens a promising new research direction that bridges psycholinguistics with computational sentiment analysis, moving beyond purely surface-level text features.
2.  **Clear Feature Engineering and Analysis:** The paper provides a clear and detailed discussion of its feature engineering in Section 4. The breakdown of features into sentiment, sarcasm-related, basic gaze, and complex gaze categories is logical and helpful. The ablation study presented in Table 2 effectively demonstrates the incremental value of each feature set, strengthening the authors' claims.
3.  **Proactive Discussion of Feasibility:** The authors are to be commended for proactively addressing the practical feasibility and deployment scenarios of their approach in Section 7. Discussing the advent of mobile eye-trackers and the potential for user consent shows foresight and strengthens the paper's potential impact beyond a purely academic experiment.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Conflation of Domains in Evaluation Datasets:** The main weakness of the evaluation lies in the composition of the datasets. Both Dataset 1 and Dataset 2 are described as collections of snippets from different domains (reviews, tweets, quotes). This conflation of genres raises two significant concerns:
    *   **Difficulty in Interpreting Results:** It makes the reported performance difficult to interpret and compare to prior work. For instance, the F-scores in Table 2 might seem low when compared to state-of-the-art results on standard movie review datasets (e.g., Pang and Lee), but could be considered quite strong for noisier Twitter data. Without a domain-specific breakdown, it is hard to properly situate the model's performance.
    *   **Potential for Negative Transfer:** Training and evaluating on mixed-domain data may obscure the true effectiveness of the features. It is possible that a model trained exclusively on movie reviews would perform better on a movie review test set (and vice-versa for tweets) than a model trained on a mixed corpus. A more controlled experimental setup with separate evaluations for each domain would provide a clearer picture of the features' benefits and generalizability.

### Questions to Authors

1.  In Section 3.3, the baseline classifiers are trained on a large corpus of 10,662 snippets from standard datasets (Pang and Lee, Sentiment140). Could you please confirm that there is no overlap between the instances in this training set and the instances used in the eye-tracking test sets (Dataset 1 and Dataset 2), which are also mentioned to be drawn from similar sources?
