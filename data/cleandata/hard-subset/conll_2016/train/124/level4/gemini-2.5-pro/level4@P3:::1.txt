### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional linguistic features with cognitive features derived from the eye-movement patterns of human readers. The central hypothesis is that cognitive signals, as captured by eye-tracking, can help models better understand complex linguistic phenomena like sarcasm, irony, and thwarted expectations, which are challenging for systems relying solely on textual features. The authors introduce a comprehensive feature set, including basic gaze metrics (e.g., fixation duration, regressions) and more complex graph-based features derived from gaze-saliency graphs. They evaluate their enhanced feature set on two datasets containing nuanced texts, demonstrating improved F-scores over baselines that use only sentiment and sarcasm-related features.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Introduction of Cognitive Features for Sentiment Analysis:** The primary contribution is the novel idea of incorporating features from eye-tracking data into a standard feature-based sentiment classification pipeline. This introduces a new modality of information (psycholinguistic signals) to a well-established NLP task.
2.  **Demonstration of Efficacy on Complex Texts:** A key finding is that the inclusion of cognitive features provides a more significant performance boost when classifying texts with complex constructs like sarcasm and irony, as shown in the held-out dataset experiment. This provides strong evidence for the authors' core hypothesis.
3.  **A Comprehensive Feature Set and Analysis:** The paper presents a detailed set of engineered cognitive features, ranging from simple counts to novel graph-based metrics. The feature significance analysis further validates that these new features are indeed informative for the classification task.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Potential for Impact:** The core idea of leveraging cognitive data from readers to improve NLP models is highly innovative and thought-provoking. It bridges the gap between computational linguistics and psycholinguistics in a practical, task-oriented manner. This work could inspire a new line of research where cognitive signals are used to enrich models for other challenging NLP tasks such as ambiguity resolution, text summarization, or argument mining.
2.  **Strong Empirical Evidence for Handling Linguistic Nuances:** The paper's strongest result is the experiment on the held-out dataset (Table 4), which explicitly separates simple texts from those containing irony/sarcasm. The demonstration of a 4.2 point absolute F-score improvement on complex texts (from 60.1% to 64.3%) compared to a 1.7 point improvement on simpler texts provides convincing evidence that the cognitive features are not just capturing noise but are genuinely helpful for the specific challenges the authors set out to address.
3.  **Thorough Feature Engineering and Evaluation:** The authors have clearly put significant effort into designing their feature set. They go beyond simple gaze metrics to propose novel graph-based features. The experimental setup is solid, comparing the augmented feature set against strong baselines that already include both standard sentiment features (Sn) and specialized sarcasm features (Sr). The inclusion of feature significance analysis (Table 3) and statistical significance testing strengthens the validity of their claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Questionable Practicality and Scalability:** The most significant weakness of the proposed method is its reliance on eye-tracking data for inference. The current framework seems to require that every text to be classified must first be read by a panel of human subjects whose eye movements are recorded. This is prohibitively expensive and slow for any real-world application. The discussion in Section 7 on the feasibility of using future mobile eye-trackers is speculative and does not resolve the fundamental pipeline issue. For instance, the scenario in 7.2, where a system watches readers to classify an existing Amazon review, is unclear: does it classify the review for each new reader, or does it aggregate data to arrive at a single classification? This core logistical problem makes the approach impractical outside of a research lab setting.
2.  **Marginal Overall Performance Gains:** While the improvement on complex texts is notable, the overall improvement on the full datasets is quite small. On Dataset 1, adding gaze features (Gz) to the strong Sn+Sr baseline only lifts the F-score from 72.9% to 73.5% (+0.6%). On Dataset 2, the gain is from 70.3% to 71.8% (+1.5%). Given the immense overhead required to collect the gaze data, this modest overall improvement raises questions about the cost-benefit trade-off of the approach for general-purpose sentiment analysis.
3.  **Potential Conflation of Sentiment Cues with General Reading Difficulty:** The motivation for *why* eye-tracking helps with sentiment polarity is not fully established. Eye-movement patterns are well-known indicators of cognitive load and reading difficulty. The features may be capturing general linguistic complexity (e.g., unusual syntax, low-frequency words) rather than signals specific to sentiment or sarcasm. While the authors include readability features to account for this, the paper does not conclusively disentangle these factors. It remains possible that the gaze features are simply a more powerful proxy for text complexity, which happens to correlate with non-literal language, rather than a direct signal of the sentiment itself.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the intended real-world deployment scenario for this model? Is the intention to use eye-tracking to create richer training datasets, or does the model require new eye-tracking data for every new instance it needs to classify at test time? The latter seems to be implied but is highly impractical.
2.  The cognitive features seem strongly tied to cognitive load or "surprisal." Could you comment on how these features differ from computational proxies for surprisal, such as the perplexity of a text under a powerful language model? Have you considered experiments to determine if the signal captured by gaze data is distinct from what could be captured by such purely text-based methods?
3.  The cognitive features are created by averaging gaze data across all participants for a given text. This approach might obscure important information, such as disagreement among readers (e.g., some get the sarcasm, others don't). Have you explored modeling individual reader variations or using the variance in gaze patterns across readers as a feature itself?
