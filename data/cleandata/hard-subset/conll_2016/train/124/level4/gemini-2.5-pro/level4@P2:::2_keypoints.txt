- Proposal: Augment sentiment analysis with cognitive features from eye-tracking to capture subtle constructs like sarcasm and irony.  
- Contribution: Novel use of eye-tracking as a cognitive signal modality for sentiment classification, motivated by capturing comprehension effort beyond text.  
- Contribution: Comprehensive feature engineering including basic gaze metrics and graph-based gaze-saliency features, with ablation and significance analyses showing complementary value.  
- Contribution: Empirical evidence that cognitive/gaze features disproportionately help texts with complex constructs (sarcasm/irony), supported by held-out experiments.  
- Strength: Creative, well-motivated approach that addresses limitations of purely lexical/syntactic features for pragmatic nuances.  
- Strength: Thorough experimental design evaluating feature sets and feature importance, plus targeted experiments on ironic vs. non-ironic texts.  
- Strength: Practical feasibility discussion acknowledging eye-tracking deployment challenges and pointing to emerging mobile solutions and application scenarios.  
- Weakness: Evaluation conflates domains (movie reviews and tweets), making results hard to interpret and suggesting a need for domain-separated experiments or domain-specific models.  
- Weakness: Baseline clarity issue — baselines trained on external datasets vs. main experiments' cross-validation complicates direct comparison; a stronger, comparable feature-based baseline is needed.  
- Question: Clarify whether the baseline training data (Pang & Lee, Sentiment140) overlapped with test instances in Datasets 1 and 2.
