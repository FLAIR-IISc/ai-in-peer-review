### Summary of the paper and its main contributions:

This paper proposes augmenting feature sets for sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from readers' eye-movement patterns. The central hypothesis is that cognitive signals captured by eye-tracking can help models better handle nuanced and complex linguistic constructs, such as sarcasm and irony, which are challenging for systems relying solely on traditional textual features. The authors introduce a range of gaze-based features, combine them with standard sentiment and sarcasm-specific textual features, and demonstrate improved classification performance on two datasets containing challenging short texts.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that incorporating cognitive features from eye-tracking data significantly improves sentiment classification, particularly for texts with complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. The analysis on a held-out dataset (Table 4) provides strong evidence that the performance gain is most pronounced for these challenging cases.
2.  The introduction and detailed analysis of a novel set of features derived from eye-tracking data. These range from basic gaze metrics (e.g., fixation duration, regression counts) to more complex features derived from a proposed "gaze-saliency graph," providing a new resource and direction for feature engineering in this domain.

### Strengths:

1.  **Novelty of the Core Idea:** The primary strength of this paper is the novel proposal to use eye-tracking data as a proxy for cognitive load to improve sentiment analysis. This moves beyond traditional linguistic features and introduces a new, well-motivated modality of information. The approach is creative and has the potential to inspire further research at the intersection of NLP and cognitive science.
2.  **Thorough Feature Engineering and Analysis:** The authors do an excellent job of defining and motivating a comprehensive set of cognitive features (Section 4.3). The experimental design, which systematically evaluates different feature combinations (Table 2), clearly demonstrates the incremental value of adding sarcasm-related features and then the proposed gaze features. The feature significance analysis (Table 3) and the targeted experiment on ironic vs. non-ironic text (Table 4) provide convincing support for the paper's central claims.
3.  **Consideration of Practical Feasibility:** The inclusion of Section 7, which discusses the practical feasibility of the proposed approach, is a commendable strength. By addressing the obvious limitation that eye-tracking data is not widely available, the authors show foresight and ground their research in real-world applicability, citing the increasing prevalence of mobile eye-tracking technology.

### Weaknesses:

1.  **Confounding Experimental Variables due to Mixed-Domain Datasets:** A significant weakness is the use of datasets that conflate multiple domains (e.g., movie reviews, tweets, quotes). The datasets used for evaluation (Dataset 1 and 2) are heterogeneous, and the baseline systems in Section 3.3 are trained on yet another mix of domains. This makes the results difficult to interpret. It is unclear whether performance changes are due to the efficacy of the features or domain adaptation effects. A cleaner experimental design, such as training and testing within a single domain (e.g., only movie reviews or only tweets), would allow for a more direct assessment of the features' impact and easier comparison to established state-of-the-art results in those specific domains.
2.  **Difficulty in Comparing Performance to State-of-the-Art:** Related to the first point, the reported F-scores (e.g., maxing out at 73.5% on D1 and 71.8% on D2) appear low compared to state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks like the Pang and Lee movie review dataset. While the authors argue this is because their datasets are inherently more challenging, the mixed-domain nature of the evaluation makes this claim hard to verify. Without a clearer comparison point, the overall significance of the performance improvement is somewhat diminished.
3.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The presentation could be slightly improved. The frequent use of bold formatting for emphasis throughout the text is somewhat distracting and hinders readability.
