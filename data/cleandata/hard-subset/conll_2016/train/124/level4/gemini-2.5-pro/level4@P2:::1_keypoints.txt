- Summary: Proposes augmenting text-based sentiment/sarcasm detection with cognitive features from readers' eye movements; collects eye-tracking data, extracts gaze features, combines with standard features, and reports improved F-scores on two datasets using SVM/NB/NN.

- Contribution — Novel Feature Engineering: Introduces and formalizes gaze-derived cognitive features for sentiment analysis, showing human cognitive signals can be quantified and integrated into ML models.

- Contribution — Experimental Validation: Provides empirical results on two datasets demonstrating statistically significant performance gains, especially for irony/sarcasm cases.

- Strength — High Novelty and Potential Impact: Applying eye-tracking to mainstream NLP is original and could open new interdisciplinary research directions for subtle language understanding.

- Strength — Clear Presentation and Methodology: Paper is well-written with transparent feature descriptions and experimental setup.

- Strength — Strong Baseline and Analysis: Compares to a robust text+sarcasm baseline and includes feature significance and complexity-targeted analyses supporting the claims.

- Weakness — Insufficient Motivation for Polarity Detection: Paper fails to convincingly justify how gaze patterns causally map to positive vs. negative sentiment rather than general reading difficulty.

- Weakness — Marginal Performance Gain vs. Data Cost: Improvements are small (0.6% and 1.5% F-score) while eye-tracking data collection is costly, questioning practical benefit.

- Weakness — Unconvincing Feasibility and Applicability: Deployment scenarios are speculative and do not address how to scale or apply the method to existing corpora without live readers.

- Question 1: Please clarify the theoretical/psycholinguistic link between specific gaze features (e.g., regressions, saccades) and sentiment polarity versus general complexity.

- Question 2: How do you justify the eye-tracking overhead given modest gains, and could modern text-only methods (e.g., BERT) achieve similar or better improvements?

- Question 3: Is the approach dependent on a live reader at inference, and how can it be generalized to classify large, pre-existing text corpora without gaze data?
