### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes to improve sentiment analysis, particularly for texts with complex constructs like sarcasm and irony, by augmenting standard linguistic features with cognitive features derived from readers' eye-movement patterns. The central hypothesis is that eye-tracking data can serve as a proxy for the cognitive effort involved in comprehending nuanced sentiment, thereby providing signals that are not available in the text alone. The authors introduce a range of "gaze features," including basic statistics (e.g., fixation duration, regression counts) and more complex graph-based features derived from gaze-saliency graphs. They conduct experiments on two datasets containing challenging short texts, demonstrating that their combined feature set outperforms models using only traditional sentiment and sarcasm features.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Novel Application of Cognitive Features:** The primary contribution is the novel idea of using eye-tracking data as a source of features for sentiment classification. This introduces a new modality to a traditionally text-focused task and provides a well-motivated approach for capturing the subtleties of human language comprehension that are often missed by purely text-based models.
2.  **Comprehensive Feature Engineering and Analysis:** The paper introduces a detailed set of cognitive features, from basic gaze metrics to more complex graph-based representations. The ablation studies (Table 2) and feature significance analysis (Table 3) provide a clear and compelling demonstration of the utility of these new features, showing their complementary value to existing linguistic features.
3.  **Empirical Evidence for Handling Complex Texts:** A significant finding, supported by the held-out experiment (Table 4), is that the proposed cognitive features provide a disproportionately larger benefit for texts containing complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. This validates the initial hypothesis and highlights a specific area where this approach is most impactful.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Approach:** The core idea of leveraging cognitive signals from eye-tracking is both creative and well-grounded. The authors rightly point out that lexical and syntactic features often fail to capture higher-level pragmatic and semantic nuances, especially in user-generated content. Using eye-movement patterns as a proxy for cognitive load (e.g., confusion, re-analysis) is an intuitive and promising direction for addressing this gap.

2.  **Thorough Experimental Analysis:** The paper's experimental design is a clear strength. The authors systematically evaluate the impact of different feature sets (Sentiment, Sarcasm, Gaze) and their combinations. The inclusion of a feature significance analysis (Table 3) helps to demystify which gaze features are most informative. Furthermore, the targeted experiment on the held-out ironic vs. non-ironic dataset (Section 5.1) provides strong, focused evidence supporting the central claim that these features are particularly useful for complex texts.

3.  **Consideration of Practical Feasibility:** I commend the authors for proactively addressing the practical limitations of their approach in Section 7. While requiring eye-tracking data is a major hurdle for widespread adoption, the discussion on emerging mobile eye-tracking technology and potential application scenarios shows foresight and a realistic perspective. This strengthens the paper by acknowledging current limitations while outlining a plausible path forward.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Conflation of Data Domains in Evaluation:** My main concern with the evaluation is the mixing of data from different domains (movie reviews and tweets) within the datasets. These domains have vastly different characteristics in terms of language, style, and length. Consequently, the reported performance metrics in Table 2 are difficult to interpret and compare against established benchmarks. For instance, an F-score of ~73% on movie reviews might be considered low, whereas the same score on tweet sentiment analysis could be quite competitive. Separating the experiments by domain would likely yield clearer, more interpretable results and could potentially lead to better performance by training domain-specific models.

2.  **Clarity of Baselines:** The baseline systems presented in Section 3.3 are trained on a large, external dataset, while the main experiments in Section 5 use 10-fold cross-validation on the target datasets. This makes a direct comparison of the improvements difficult. While the ablation study in Table 2 is effective at showing the value of the new features relative to the authors' own feature subsets, the paper would be stronger if it compared its best model against a state-of-the-art, feature-based sentiment analysis system evaluated under the exact same cross-validation setup. The paper dismisses deep learning models, which is reasonable given the feature-engineering focus, but a stronger feature-based baseline would provide better context for the reported gains.

### Questions to Authors

1.  In the experiment described in Section 3.3, you train baseline classifiers on standard datasets from Pang and Lee (2004) and Sentiment140 and then test on Dataset 1 and Dataset 2. Since your datasets are also composed of movie reviews and tweets, could you please clarify whether you ensured there were no overlapping instances between the training set used for these baselines and the test instances in Datasets 1 and 2?
