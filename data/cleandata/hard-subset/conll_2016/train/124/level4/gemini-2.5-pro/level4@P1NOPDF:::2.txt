This paper introduces eye-tracking features for sentiment analysis, proposing them as a proxy for cognitive load. The core idea is interesting and novel.

The discussion on the features and the comparison of different feature sets are clear and very helpful. I also appreciate the inclusion of Section 7, which addresses the practical feasibility of the approach.

My main concern relates to the evaluation methodology. By conflating data from different domains (movie reviews and tweets) into single training and testing sets, the results become difficult to interpret. This experimental design has two potential drawbacks. First, performance on a single domain (e.g., movie reviews) might be improved by training solely on in-domain data rather than on a mixed-domain corpus. Second, and more importantly, this setup complicates comparison with prior work. For instance, the results in Table 2 appear low when compared to state-of-the-art results on the Pang and Lee dataset, but seem more competitive when compared to typical results on Twitter data. A domain-specific evaluation would provide a clearer picture of the contribution and allow for a more direct comparison with established benchmarks.

I have one clarifying question regarding the data splits described in Section 3.3: Can the authors confirm that there are no overlapping text snippets between the training and testing sets for datasets 1 and 2, given that they are drawn from the same original sources (Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140)?

Minor point: I found the extensive use of bold formatting to be somewhat distracting.
