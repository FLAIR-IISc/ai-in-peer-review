This paper introduces eye-tracking features, as a proxy for cognitive load, for the task of sentiment analysis. The central idea is novel and well-motivated.

The discussion of the proposed features and the comparative analysis of different feature sets are clear and insightful. Additionally, a strength of the paper is the inclusion of Section 7, which addresses the practical feasibility of the approach.

A potential weakness in the evaluation lies in the conflation of different domains within the datasets, specifically the movie review corpus and the tweet corpus. Separating these domains could improve predictive performance, as training exclusively on in-domain data (e.g., movie reviews) might yield better results for that domain. Furthermore, this conflation makes the results harder to interpret. For instance, the performance reported in Table 2 appears low compared to the state-of-the-art for the Pang and Lee movie review dataset, but more competitive when compared to typical results on Twitter data. An in-domain evaluation would clarify the method's performance in each context.

A point for clarification regarding Section 3.3: Is it correct to assume that there are no overlapping text snippets between the training and testing splits for datasets 1 and 2, even though they draw from the same original sources (Pang & Lee, Sentiment140)?

Minor point: The excessive use of bold formatting throughout the paper can be distracting.
