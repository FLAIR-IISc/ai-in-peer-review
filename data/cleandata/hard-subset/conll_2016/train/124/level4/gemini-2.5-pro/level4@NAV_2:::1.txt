**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis by augmenting traditional text-based features with cognitive features derived from readers' eye-movement patterns. The core hypothesis is that cognitive signals, captured via eye-tracking, can help models better interpret linguistically complex texts containing nuances like sarcasm and irony. The authors demonstrate that incorporating these gaze-based features improves the F-score of sentiment polarity detection on two separate datasets.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The introduction of a new modality—cognitive features from eye-tracking data—to the task of sentiment analysis. This is a novel concept that bridges psycholinguistics and computational linguistics to address a challenging NLP problem.
2.  An empirical demonstration that these cognitive features, when combined with standard linguistic features, yield performance improvements. The evaluation is particularly notable for showing that the gains are more pronounced for texts containing complex constructs like sarcasm, as evidenced by the analysis on a held-out dataset.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Potential Impact:** The central idea of leveraging cognitive signals for sentiment analysis is highly innovative and thought-provoking. This work pioneers a promising new research direction that could inspire similar approaches in other NLP tasks that involve resolving ambiguity and understanding subtle linguistic phenomena (e.g., emotion detection, argument mining, and summarization).
2.  **Thorough Experimental Design:** The evaluation is well-conducted and comprehensive. The authors validate their method on two distinct datasets using multiple classifiers (SVM, Naive Bayes, NN). The inclusion of ablation studies (Table 2), a feature significance analysis (Table 3), and a targeted experiment on ironic vs. non-ironic text (Table 4) provides strong and multifaceted support for their claims.
3.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors effectively motivate the problem by detailing lexical, syntactic, and semantic challenges in sentiment analysis. The methodology, including the detailed descriptions of the 25+ engineered features, is presented with sufficient clarity to allow for understanding and potential replication.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Performance Gain:** While the improvements are shown to be statistically significant, the practical impact of adding cognitive features is marginal. According to Table 2, the F-score gain from adding gaze features (Gz) to the strong baseline of sentiment and sarcasm features (Sn+Sr) is only 0.6% for Dataset 1 and 1.5% for Dataset 2. This modest increase raises questions about the cost-benefit trade-off, given the significant data collection effort required for eye-tracking.
2.  **Weakened Link Between Gaze and Polarity:** The motivation for how specific gaze patterns correlate with sentiment polarity (positive vs. negative), as opposed to simply signaling processing difficulty, could be stronger. The paper intuitively links gaze features to the presence of complex constructs like sarcasm, but the mechanism by which they help distinguish between a positive and a negative sentiment in general cases is not fully elaborated. For example, it is unclear why a text eliciting a high regression count would be more likely to be negative than positive.
3.  **Concerns about Practical Feasibility:** The real-world applicability of this method is questionable. The approach is fundamentally dependent on the availability of eye-tracking data for the text being analyzed. The authors' discussion on feasibility in Section 7 relies on future, speculative advancements like ubiquitous, low-cost mobile eye-trackers. This dependency severely limits the method's scalability and generalizability to new domains or large-scale, real-time applications where such data collection is not viable.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more intuition or analysis on how specific gaze features help differentiate between positive and negative sentiment, especially in non-sarcastic texts? For instance, do positive and negative expressions systematically elicit different reading patterns (e.g., fixation durations, saccade lengths) that your model is capturing?
2.  The practical barrier of requiring gaze data is significant. Have you considered a transfer learning approach where a model is first trained with both textual and gaze features, and then the knowledge is distilled into a text-only model for deployment? Could a model trained with gaze data on one dataset generalize to a new dataset using only textual features at inference time?
