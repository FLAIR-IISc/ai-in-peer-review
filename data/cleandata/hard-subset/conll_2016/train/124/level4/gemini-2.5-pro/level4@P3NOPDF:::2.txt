This paper introduces the use of eye-tracking features, proposed as a proxy for cognitive processing, to improve sentiment analysis. The central hypothesis is that cognitive load, as measured by gaze patterns, can provide valuable signals for determining the sentiment of a text.

**Strengths**

The core idea of leveraging eye-tracking data as a cognitive signal for sentiment analysis is both novel and intriguing. The paper makes a compelling case for exploring features beyond the text itself.

The authors provide a clear and well-structured discussion of the proposed features and a helpful comparison of different feature sets. This analysis effectively demonstrates the contribution of the new cognitive features.

A notable strength of the paper is the inclusion of Section 7, which addresses the practical feasibility of this approach. Discussing the path toward wider application is a valuable and often-overlooked aspect of this type of research.

**Areas for Improvement**

The primary concern with the current evaluation lies in the experimental design, specifically the conflation of different domains within the datasets. The datasets combine texts from distinct sources, such as movie reviews (Pang & Lee) and tweets (Sentiment 140). This design choice raises two main issues:

1.  **Performance Masking:** It is plausible that domain-specific characteristics significantly influence both the linguistic content and the cognitive signals (gaze patterns). Training and testing on a mix of these domains may hinder the model's ability to learn domain-specific patterns, potentially depressing performance. A more controlled experiment, such as training and testing exclusively on movie reviews, might yield stronger results for that specific domain, and vice-versa for tweets.

2.  **Difficulty in Interpretation:** This domain-mixing also complicates the interpretation of the results. As noted, the performance reported in Table 2 appears low when compared to state-of-the-art models on the Pang & Lee movie review corpus alone, but seems more competitive in the context of Twitter sentiment analysis. Without a domain-separated evaluation, it is difficult to accurately assess the method's effectiveness relative to established benchmarks in either domain.

I would strongly recommend that the authors consider conducting additional experiments on domain-pure datasets. This would provide a clearer picture of the features' effectiveness and allow for a more direct comparison with prior work.

**Clarifying Questions**

Could the authors please clarify the data splitting procedure in Section 3.3? Specifically, while the paper creates two datasets from the same sources (Pang & Lee, Sentiment 140), it is crucial to confirm that there are no overlapping text snippets between the final training and test splits used in the experiments.

**Minor Comments**

The use of bold formatting throughout the paper is occasionally excessive and can be distracting to the reader. A more sparing application might improve readability.
