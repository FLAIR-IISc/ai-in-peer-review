**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection by augmenting traditional text-based features with cognitive features derived from readers' eye-movement patterns. The core hypothesis is that the cognitive processes involved in understanding complex or nuanced sentiment (such as irony and sarcasm) are reflected in gaze behavior, such as fixations, saccades, and regressions. The authors collect eye-tracking data from human annotators reading short texts and extract a set of "gaze features." These are combined with standard sentiment features and features specifically designed for sarcasm detection. The proposed feature set is evaluated on two datasets using SVM, Naïve Bayes, and Neural Network classifiers, showing that the inclusion of cognitive features improves F-scores over a strong text-only baseline.

**Contributions**

1.  **Novel Feature Engineering:** The primary contribution of this work is the introduction and formulation of cognitive features from eye-tracking data for a mainstream NLP task. The paper successfully demonstrates that signals from human cognitive processes can be quantified and integrated into a feature-based machine learning model for sentiment analysis. This presents a novel research direction that could potentially be explored in other areas of NLP where textual cues alone are insufficient.
2.  **Experimental Validation:** The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation on two datasets containing nuanced and complex texts. They demonstrate that their augmented feature set achieves statistically significant performance gains, particularly in identifying sentiment in texts containing complex constructs like irony and sarcasm, as shown in the held-out experiment (Table 4).

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea of leveraging cognitive data from human readers is highly original and thought-provoking. While eye-tracking is established in psycholinguistics, its application as a feature source for sentiment classification is new and could inspire a new line of research. Exploring the intersection of cognitive science and NLP could open up new avenues for tackling tasks that require understanding subtle human communication, such as humor, deception, or emotion detection.
2.  **Clear Presentation and Methodology:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining the different feature categories, from traditional sentiment features to the more complex gaze-saliency graph features. The experimental setup is transparent, and the results are presented clearly, allowing the reader to easily assess the contribution of each feature set.
3.  **Strong Baseline and Analysis:** The authors compare their full model against a robust baseline that includes not only standard sentiment features (Sn) but also features specifically designed for sarcasm and thwarting (Sr). This makes the subsequent improvements from adding cognitive features (Gz) more credible. The feature significance analysis (Table 3) and the targeted experiment on complex vs. simple texts (Table 4) provide convincing evidence that the cognitive features are indeed capturing signals relevant to linguistic complexity.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Motivation for Polarity Detection:** The paper's motivation for how cognitive features directly predict sentiment *polarity* (positive vs. negative) is underdeveloped. While it is intuitive that features like regression counts or longer fixation durations correlate with textual complexity, confusion, or "surprisal" (as seen in sarcasm), the link between these signals and a specific positive or negative valence is not clearly justified. For example, why would a higher regression count necessarily indicate a negative sentiment rather than just a confusingly worded positive one? The paper demonstrates a correlation but does not provide a strong theoretical grounding for a causal link between these specific gaze patterns and sentiment polarity itself.
2.  **Marginal Performance Gain for High Data Acquisition Cost:** The reported performance improvements from adding cognitive features are statistically significant but practically modest. When adding gaze features (Gz) to the strong baseline of sentiment and sarcasm features (Sn+Sr), the F-score improves by only 0.6% on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2 (Table 2). Given the immense cost, complexity, and logistical challenges of collecting high-quality eye-tracking data for every piece of text to be classified, this small gain raises questions about the cost-benefit trade-off of the proposed approach.
3.  **Unconvincing Feasibility and Applicability Scenarios:** The discussion in Section 7 regarding the practical feasibility of this approach is speculative and does not address fundamental limitations. The proposed scenario in Section 7.2—analyzing a review's sentiment by tracking a user's eyes as they read it—seems impractical and convoluted. Most real-world sentiment analysis tasks involve classifying vast amounts of pre-existing, static text where no concurrent reader exists. The paper fails to propose a viable method for applying this technique at scale to existing datasets (e.g., all of Twitter or Amazon reviews), which is the primary use case for automated sentiment analysis.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the theoretical link between specific gaze features (e.g., regression count, saccade length) and the *polarity* of a sentiment, as distinct from their established connection to general reading difficulty or textual complexity? Are there psycholinguistic theories suggesting that, for instance, negative sarcastic sentences elicit different gaze patterns than positive ironic ones?
2.  Given the modest F-score improvements when adding gaze features (0.6% on Dataset 1 and 1.5% on Dataset 2 over the Sn+Sr baseline), how do you justify the significant overhead of collecting eye-tracking data? Could similar or greater gains be achieved by further improving the text-only model, for instance, by using more advanced contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT-based models, which have since become standard) instead of traditional features?
3.  The applicability scenario in Section 7.2 implies real-time classification of a text *as it is being read*. Could you clarify this use case? More importantly, how do you envision this method being applied to the more common task of classifying a large, pre-existing corpus of text for which no gaze data is available? Does the approach require a "live reader" for every inference, or is there a path to generalizing the model to work on text alone after being trained with cognitive data?
