- Proposes augmenting sentiment and sarcasm classification with "cognitive features" from eye-tracking, hypothesizing gaze patterns reflect processing effort useful for detection.  
- Introduces basic and graph-based gaze features, combines them with standard sentiment/sarcasm features, and reports F-score improvements on two datasets, especially for irony/complex text.  
- Main contribution: first systematic use of eye-tracking–derived cognitive features for sentiment analysis, opening interdisciplinary research directions.  
- Strength: high novelty and potential impact—creative, likely to inspire similar work on other complex NLP problems.  
- Strength: thorough experimental evaluation—two datasets, multiple classifiers, strong baselines, feature significance analysis, and targeted held-out tests.  
- Strength: clear presentation and reproducible methodology with well-explained feature sets.  
- Weakness: insufficient justification that gaze-derived cognitive features directly indicate sentiment polarity rather than just reading difficulty or complexity.  
- Weakness: marginal performance gains (e.g., +0.6% and +1.5% F-score) that may not justify the overhead of collecting eye-tracking data.  
- Weakness: unconvincing feasibility argument—practical deployment via eye-tracking is speculative and faces technical, privacy, and consent challenges, limiting real-world applicability.
