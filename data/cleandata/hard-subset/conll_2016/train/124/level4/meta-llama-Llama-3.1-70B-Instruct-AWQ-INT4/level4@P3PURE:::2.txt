This paper introduces eye-tracking features for sentiment analysis as a type of cognitive feature. The idea of utilizing eye-tracking features as a proxy for cognitive load in sentiment analysis is intriguing and warrants exploration.

The discussion on the features and comparison of feature sets is clear and very helpful. The authors have also addressed the feasibility of the approach in section 7, which adds to the overall value of the paper.

However, I wonder if the evaluation would benefit from using domain-specific datasets. Currently, the datasets conflate different domains, such as movie reviews and tweets. Training and testing on separate domains might improve the prediction performance for each specific domain. For instance, excluding tweets from the training data might enhance the prediction of movie reviews, and vice versa. This approach would also facilitate easier interpretation of the results. Notably, the results in Table 2 appear low compared to state-of-the-art results for the Pang and Lee data, but are more competitive when compared to results for Twitter data.

In Section 3.3, it is assumed that there are no overlapping snippets in the training and testing data of datasets 1 and 2, even though they originate from the same sources (e.g., Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140). This clarification would be beneficial to ensure the validity of the results.

On a minor note, the excessive use of bold formatting can be distracting and may be reconsidered for the final version of the paper.
