This paper introduces eye-tracking features for sentiment analysis as a type of cognitive feature, which I find to be an intriguing concept. The idea of leveraging eye-tracking features as a proxy for cognitive load in sentiment analysis is particularly noteworthy.

The discussion on the features and comparison of feature sets is clear and very helpful, providing valuable insights into the approach. Additionally, the authors' consideration of the feasibility of the approach in Section 7 is a welcome inclusion.

However, I wonder if the evaluation would benefit from using domain-specific datasets, rather than combining different domains, such as the movie review corpus and the tweet corpus. This is because including tweets in the training data may negatively impact the prediction performance for movie reviews, and vice versa. Furthermore, using domain-specific datasets would facilitate easier interpretation of the results. For instance, the results in Table 2 appear relatively low compared to state-of-the-art results for the Pang and Lee data, but are more comparable to results for Twitter data.

Regarding Section 3.3, I would like to confirm that there are no overlapping snippets in the training and testing data of datasets 1 and 2, despite being sourced from the same origins (e.g., Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140).

On a minor note, the excessive use of bold font can be distracting, although this may be a matter of personal preference.
