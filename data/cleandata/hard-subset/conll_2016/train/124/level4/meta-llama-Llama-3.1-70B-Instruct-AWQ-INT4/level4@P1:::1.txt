This paper proposes a very interesting idea of using cognitive features for sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection. More specifically, the eye-movement patterns of human annotators are recorded to derive a new set of features. The authors claim that this is the first work to include cognitive features into the NLP community.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper addresses the task of sentiment analysis (SA) for user-generated short texts and sentences. The authors argue that SA is a challenging problem due to the complexities at various levels, including lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic levels. To address these challenges, the authors propose to augment traditional features used for SA and sarcasm detection with cognitive features derived from the eye-movement patterns of readers. The authors claim that their approach is the first of its kind to incorporate cognitive features into the NLP community.

The main contributions of this paper are:

1. The introduction of cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of human annotators for sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection.
2. The demonstration of the effectiveness of these cognitive features in improving the performance of SA systems, particularly for texts with complex constructs like irony and sarcasm.

**Strengths**

1. The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a wide range of readers.
2. The idea of using cognitive features for sentiment analysis is very interesting and may inspire research in other NLP tasks.

**Weaknesses**

1. The motivation for using cognitive features for sentiment analysis is not very well justified. While the authors argue that these features may help reflect the reading ease, it is not clear why they are helpful in detecting sentiment polarities.
2. The improvement in performance after considering cognitive features is marginal, particularly when comparing the results of Sn+Sr+Gz with Sn+Sr.
3. Although the authors discuss the feasibility of their approach in Section 7, the example provided in Section 7.2 is not convincing, and it is not clear why this technique is helpful in such a scenario.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more justification for why cognitive features are helpful in detecting sentiment polarities?
2. How do you plan to address the marginal improvement in performance after considering cognitive features?
3. Can you provide more examples or scenarios where your approach is feasible and useful?
