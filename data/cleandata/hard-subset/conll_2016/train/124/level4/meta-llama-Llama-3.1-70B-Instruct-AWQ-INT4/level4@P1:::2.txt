This paper proposes an innovative approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating eye-tracking features as a type of cognitive feature. The idea of using eye-tracking data as a proxy for cognitive load in sentiment analysis is intriguing and has the potential to improve the accuracy of sentiment classification.

The discussion on the features and comparison of feature sets is clear and very helpful. The authors have done a thorough job in explaining the different types of features used, including sentiment features, sarcasm and irony-related features, cognitive features from eye-movement, and features related to reading difficulty. The experimental setup and results are also well-presented, making it easy to follow and understand the methodology and findings.

I appreciate that the authors have addressed the feasibility of their approach in section 7. This is a crucial aspect of the paper, as it discusses the potential applications and limitations of using eye-tracking features in sentiment analysis.

However, I have some concerns regarding the datasets used in the experiments. The datasets appear to conflate different domains, such as movie reviews and tweets. This might affect the prediction accuracy, as the model may not be able to generalize well across different domains. It would be helpful to evaluate the performance of the model on separate datasets for movie reviews and tweets to see if the results improve. Additionally, this would make the results easier to interpret, as the performance of the model on different domains would be more comparable.

I also have a minor question regarding the training and testing data in Section 3.3. Are there any overlapping snippets in the training data and testing data of datasets 1 and 2, even if they come from the same sources (e.g., Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140)?

Finally, I found some of the extra use of bold formatting to be distracting. However, this is a minor issue and does not affect the overall clarity of the paper.

Main Contributions:

1. Introduction of eye-tracking features as a type of cognitive feature for sentiment analysis.
2. Comprehensive comparison of feature sets, including sentiment features, sarcasm and irony-related features, cognitive features from eye-movement, and features related to reading difficulty.
3. Addressing the feasibility of the approach in section 7.

Strengths:

1. Innovative approach to sentiment analysis using eye-tracking features.
2. Clear and thorough discussion on features and experimental setup.
3. Addressing the feasibility of the approach.

Weaknesses:

1. Datasets conflate different domains, which might affect prediction accuracy.
2. No separate evaluation of model performance on different domains.
3. Minor issue with distracting bold formatting.

Questions to Authors:

1. Have you considered evaluating the performance of the model on separate datasets for movie reviews and tweets?
2. Are there any overlapping snippets in the training data and testing data of datasets 1 and 2, even if they come from the same sources?
