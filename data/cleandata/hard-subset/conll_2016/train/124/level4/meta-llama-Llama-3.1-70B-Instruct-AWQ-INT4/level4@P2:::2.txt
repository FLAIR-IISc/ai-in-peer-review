This paper introduces eye-tracking features for sentiment analysis as a type of cognitive feature. The idea of using eye-tracking features as a proxy for cognitive load in sentiment analysis is interesting and has potential.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of human annotators. The authors argue that traditional sentiment analysis methods often struggle with nuanced text, such as sarcasm and implicit sentiment. To address this, they augment traditional features with cognitive features obtained from eye-tracking data. The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach using two publicly available datasets.

**Main Contributions**

1. Introduction of cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns for sentiment analysis.
2. A comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach using two publicly available datasets.
3. A comparison of the performance of different feature sets, including traditional sentiment features, sarcasm and thwarting features, and cognitive features.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to sentiment analysis.
2. The discussion on features and comparison of feature sets is clear and helpful.
3. The authors address the feasibility of the approach in Section 7, which is a crucial aspect of the paper.

**Weaknesses**

1. The datasets used in the evaluation conflate different domains (e.g., movie reviews and tweets), which might affect the results.
2. The results in Table 2 seem low compared to state-of-the-art results for the Pang and Lee data, but look better when compared to results for Twitter data.
3. It is unclear whether there are overlapping snippets in the training data and testing data of datasets 1 and 2, even if they come from the same sources (e.g., Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140).

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you clarify whether there are overlapping snippets in the training data and testing data of datasets 1 and 2?
2. How do you think the results would change if the datasets were domain-specific (e.g., only movie reviews or only tweets)?
3. Can you provide more details on the eye-tracking equipment and software used to collect the data?

**Additional Comments**

Some of the extra use of bold in the paper is distracting. It would be helpful to use bold only when necessary to emphasize important points.
