This paper introduces eye-tracking features as a type of cognitive feature for sentiment analysis, a concept that I find intriguing. The idea of utilizing eye-tracking features as a proxy for cognitive load in sentiment analysis is innovative and warrants exploration.

The discussion on features and the comparison of feature sets are clear and helpful, providing valuable insights into the approach. I also appreciate that the authors address the feasibility of their method in Section 7, which adds to the overall credibility of the paper.

However, I think the evaluation could be improved if the datasets were not conflated across different domains. For instance, combining the movie review corpus and the tweet corpus may not be ideal. Training on a dataset that includes both movie reviews and tweets might not be the most effective way to predict sentiment for either domain. By separating the datasets, the authors may achieve better results for each individual domain, and the outcomes would be easier to interpret. As it stands, the results in Table 2 seem low compared to state-of-the-art results for the Pang and Lee data, but they appear more favorable when compared to results for Twitter data.

I have a minor clarification question regarding Section 3.3: are there no overlapping snippets in the training and testing data of datasets 1 and 2, even though they originate from the same sources (e.g., Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140)?

On a minor note, the extra use of bold font in some sections can be distracting and may not be necessary.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting idea and provides a clear discussion on the features and approach. With some adjustments to the evaluation methodology and minor revisions to the formatting, the paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field of sentiment analysis.
