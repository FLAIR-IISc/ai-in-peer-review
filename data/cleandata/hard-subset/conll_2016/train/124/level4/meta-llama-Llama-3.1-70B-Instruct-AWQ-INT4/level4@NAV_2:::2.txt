This paper proposes an innovative approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating eye-tracking features as a type of cognitive feature. The idea of utilizing eye-tracking data as a proxy for cognitive load in sentiment analysis is intriguing and warrants further exploration.

The discussion on feature engineering and comparison of different feature sets is clear and well-structured, providing valuable insights into the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Additionally, the authors' consideration of the feasibility of their method in Section 7 is a welcome addition, as it acknowledges potential limitations and future directions.

However, the evaluation could be strengthened by using more homogeneous datasets. Currently, the datasets appear to conflate different domains, such as movie reviews and tweets. Training and testing on separate domains might improve the prediction accuracy for each specific domain. Furthermore, this would facilitate more interpretable results, as the performance metrics in Table 2 seem relatively low compared to state-of-the-art results on the Pang and Lee dataset, but more competitive when compared to Twitter data.

A minor clarification is needed in Section 3.3: are there any overlapping snippets between the training data and testing data of datasets 1 and 2, even though they originate from the same sources (e.g., Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140)?

Lastly, some of the bold formatting is distracting and could be reconsidered for better readability.

Main Contributions:
1. Introduction of eye-tracking features as a type of cognitive feature for sentiment analysis.
2. Clear and structured discussion on feature engineering and comparison of different feature sets.
3. Consideration of the feasibility of the proposed approach in Section 7.

Strengths:
1. Innovative approach to sentiment analysis using eye-tracking features.
2. Well-structured discussion on feature engineering and comparison of feature sets.
3. Acknowledgement of potential limitations and future directions.

Weaknesses:
1. Evaluation could be strengthened by using more homogeneous datasets.
2. Minor formatting issues, such as distracting bold formatting.
3. Clarification needed in Section 3.3 regarding overlapping snippets between training and testing data.

Questions to Authors:
1. How do you plan to address the issue of dataset homogeneity in future work?
2. Can you provide more details on the potential applications of eye-tracking features in sentiment analysis beyond the current scope of the paper?
3. How do you envision the integration of eye-tracking data with other modalities (e.g., text, speech) for multimodal sentiment analysis?
