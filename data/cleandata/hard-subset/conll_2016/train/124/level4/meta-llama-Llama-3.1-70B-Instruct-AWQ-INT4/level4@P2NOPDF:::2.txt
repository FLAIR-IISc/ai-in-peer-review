This paper proposes the incorporation of eye-tracking features as a type of cognitive feature for sentiment analysis, an idea that I find intriguing. The discussion on the features and comparison of feature sets is clear and very helpful, providing valuable insights into the approach. Additionally, the authors address the feasibility of their method in Section 7, which strengthens the overall contribution of the paper.

However, I suggest that the evaluation could be improved by using domain-specific datasets. Currently, the datasets appear to conflate different domains, such as movie reviews and tweets. Training and testing on separate, domain-specific datasets might enhance the prediction performance for each domain. For instance, excluding tweets from the training data might improve the prediction of movie reviews, and vice versa. This would also facilitate the interpretation of the results, as the performance metrics would be more comparable to state-of-the-art results for each specific domain. Notably, the results in Table 2 seem relatively low compared to the state-of-the-art results for the Pang and Lee data, but they appear more favorable when compared to results for Twitter data.

I would like to clarify one point regarding the dataset construction. In Section 3.3, it is stated that there are no overlapping snippets in the training and testing data for datasets 1 and 2. Could the authors confirm this, even though the datasets originate from the same sources (e.g., Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140)?

On a minor note, the excessive use of bold formatting can be distracting and may be reconsidered for the sake of readability.
