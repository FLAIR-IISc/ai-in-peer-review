- Proposes using eye-tracking features as cognitive signals for sentiment analysis — an innovative and promising idea.
- Feature engineering and comparison of different feature sets are clearly explained and well-structured.
- Section 7’s discussion on feasibility acknowledges limitations and suggests future directions.
- Evaluation is weak due to heterogeneous datasets (mixing domains like movie reviews and tweets) that may confound results.
- Training/testing on separate, more homogeneous domains could improve accuracy and interpretability.
- Reported performance is low compared to Pang & Lee benchmarks but more competitive on Twitter data, indicating domain effects.
- Clarification needed in Section 3.3: possible overlap of snippets between training and testing data for datasets 1 and 2.
- Minor formatting issues (distracting bold text) should be cleaned up for readability.
- Main contributions summarized: introduction of eye-tracking features, clear feature-engineering analysis, and feasibility discussion.
- Strengths: innovative approach, strong feature engineering discussion, and honest consideration of limitations.
- Weaknesses: dataset heterogeneity in evaluation, minor formatting problems, and unclear dataset overlap.
- Questions for authors: plans to address dataset homogeneity, broader applications of eye-tracking features, and integration with other modalities (text, speech).
