Summary
The paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis (SA) features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data collected while human annotators read and label short texts. The authors design basic gaze features (fixations, saccades, regressions) and more complex graph-based gaze-saliency features, and combine them with sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, and readability features. Using SVM, Naïve Bayes, and a multi-layer perceptron on two public datasets rich in irony and implicit sentiment, they report overall F-score gains of up to 3.7% and 9.3% over systems using only traditional features. They further show larger gains for texts containing irony/sarcasm and perform significance and feature-importance analyses.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that gaze-derived cognitive features provide a small but statistically significant incremental benefit for polarity classification, especially on texts with sarcasm/irony.
2) A feature design that operationalizes eye-movement signals at both basic (fixation/saccade/regression) and graph-based levels, along with a feature-importance analysis showing many gaze features among the top ranks.
3) A careful evaluation on challenging datasets where standard SA systems underperform, including significance testing and a held-out subset analysis focused on complex constructs.

Strengths
- Targeting hard cases where SA typically fails: The datasets contain substantial amounts of sarcasm, irony, and implicit sentiment. Baseline systems trained on standard corpora achieve modest F-scores (e.g., Table 1), which justifies the focus on cognitive cues. The held-out analysis further shows that gaze features help more for irony (relative +6.1% F-score) than for non-irony (+2.1%).
- Clear, reproducible feature engineering and analysis: The paper clearly enumerates traditional, sarcasm/thwarting, readability, and gaze features. The gaze features are well-motivated by psycholinguistic findings (e.g., regressions for reanalysis). The chi-squared ranking indicates that gaze features frequently appear among the top features, lending interpretability to the observed gains.
- Sound experimental methodology: Multiple classifiers are evaluated, with SVM consistently strongest. Results are reported via 10-fold cross-validation on two datasets collected with different eye-trackers, and key differences are tested for statistical significance (McNemar). Dimensionality control (e.g., PCA) is sensibly applied to mitigate overfitting risks on modest data.
- Insightful focus on complex constructs: Augmenting sentiment features with sarcasm/thwarting features provides sizable gains, and adding gaze features yields further, albeit smaller, improvements. This aligns with the intuition that purely textual cues sometimes miss pragmatic nuances and that cognitive signals can capture processing difficulty and surprisal.
- Timely and potentially influential idea: Bringing cognitive signals into SA could inspire similar approaches in other tasks (e.g., subjectivity, emotion, or discourse phenomena) where implicit cues dominate.

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical justification linking gaze to polarity (not just difficulty): While eye-movement patterns plausibly capture processing difficulty and incongruity, the paper provides limited explanation of why these signals should correlate specifically with positive vs. negative polarity, independent of sarcasm/irony presence or general reading difficulty. The results suggest most of the gain comes from handling complex constructs rather than polarity per se.
- Marginal incremental gains from gaze features: On top of sentiment + sarcasm/thwarting features, adding gaze yields small improvements (e.g., +0.6% and +1.5% absolute). Although statistically significant, the practical impact may be limited given the cost and complexity of collecting gaze data.
- Questionable practicality at inference time: The method assumes gaze data are available for the texts to be classified. The feasibility section envisions mobile eye-tracking and ecommerce applications, but the pipeline remains unclear: will end-users’ gaze be recorded for every review to determine polarity? This raises adoption, privacy, and latency concerns, and the paper does not show a realistic deployment scenario or a student-modeling approach that predicts gaze features from text.
- Missing comparisons to strong neural baselines: Contemporary deep learning methods (e.g., CNNs/RNNs) are acknowledged but omitted. Without such comparisons, it is difficult to situate the contribution: would gaze still help over strong neural baselines, especially those modeling context and compositionality that are known to aid irony/sarcasm handling?
- Possible confounds and normalization issues: The two datasets use different eye-trackers (500Hz vs. 120Hz). The paper does not detail normalization across hardware or readers beyond averaging, nor does it assess robustness to participant variability (e.g., leave-one-participant-out). Readability controls (RED, LEN) help but may not fully disentangle reading difficulty from sentiment signals.

Questions to the authors
- Inference-time setting: How do you envision using the model when gaze is unavailable? Could you train a student model to predict gaze features from text, then use predicted gaze at test time?
- Normalization and robustness: How are gaze features normalized for differences in hardware sampling rates and individual reading speed? Have you tested leave-one-participant-out or cross-device robustness?
- Scope of gains: Can you provide per-phenomenon breakdowns beyond irony (e.g., implicit sentiment without irony, thwarted expectations) to pinpoint where gaze helps most?
- Stronger baselines: Can you compare against a contemporary neural baseline and/or augment such a model with gaze features to assess orthogonality?
- Practical scenarios: In Section 7.2, please clarify the end-to-end workflow for an ecommerce use case. Who provides gaze data, when, and at what scale, and how is consent/privacy handled?

Overall evaluation
The paper presents a novel and carefully executed exploration of gaze-based features for sentiment analysis, with statistically significant but modest gains overall and more meaningful improvements on sarcastic/ironic texts. The main concerns are practical deployability, the small incremental improvements over strong textual features, and the lack of comparison to neural baselines. I encourage the authors to strengthen the motivation linking gaze to polarity, clarify the intended inference-time setup, and provide a comparison to stronger baselines or a student-teacher approach that leverages gaze during training but not at test time.
