Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper augments standard sentiment analysis (SA) features with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movement patterns. Across two datasets containing nuanced phenomena (e.g., sarcasm, irony), the authors report F-score gains, conduct feature significance analysis, and evaluate on a held-out set with complex constructs.
- Primary contribution: Empirical evidence that gaze-derived cognitive features add predictive power for SA, particularly on texts with sarcasm/irony or implicit sentiment.
- Secondary contributions: A clear feature engineering study with ablations across sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, and gaze features; and a discussion of feasibility and potential deployment via mobile eye-tracking.

Strengths
- Novel angle: Using eye-tracking as a proxy for cognitive processing to enhance SA is interesting and well-motivated, especially for phenomena that are hard to capture with text-only features.
- Careful feature analysis: The paper clearly explains the feature sets and provides comparative evaluations (including significance tests and a held-out analysis focused on complex constructs). This makes the contribution easy to assess.
- Practicality addressed: The feasibility section (Section 7) anticipates deployment concerns and discusses avenues for obtaining gaze data at scale, which strengthens the paper’s case beyond pure accuracy gains.

Weaknesses
- Domain conflation complicates interpretation: Mixing movie reviews and tweets in training/evaluation makes the results harder to interpret. It is plausible that performance on one domain could be affected by the presence of another in training, and vice versa. Domain-specific training/testing (or reporting per-domain results) would likely clarify where the gains come from and may improve accuracy within each domain.
- Comparative context: The Table 2 numbers look low if compared against standard results on the Pang and Lee movie review dataset alone, but they look more reasonable relative to Twitter benchmarks. Without a clear domain separation, it is difficult to assess how competitive the approach is versus domain-specific baselines.
- Clarity on data overlap: Section 3.3 mentions training on Pang & Lee and Sentiment140 and testing on Datasets 1 and 2 sourced partly from similar origins. It is crucial to explicitly confirm there is no snippet overlap between training data and the test sets to avoid leakage.

Questions to the authors
- Can you confirm that there is no overlap of snippets (exact or near-duplicate) between the training data used for the baselines (from Pang & Lee and Sentiment140) and Datasets 1 and 2?
- Have you tried domain-specific splits (e.g., training and testing only on movie reviews, and separately only on tweets) and/or reported per-domain performance? If so, how do the gaze features affect each domain independently?
- Would a per-domain ablation (Sn vs. Sn+Sr vs. Sn+Sr+Gz) change the relative gains, especially for sarcasm-heavy subsets?

Minor comments
- The extra use of boldface in places is somewhat distracting; reducing it would improve readability.
