- Paper augments text-based sentiment features with eye-movement (gaze) features and reports F-score improvements on two eye-tracking datasets.
- Gains are larger for sentences with irony/sarcasm; feature-importance analysis shows cognitive features rank highly.

- Primary contribution: empirical demonstration that gaze-derived features improve sentiment classification, especially on complex inputs like sarcasm.
- Secondary contribution: a well-engineered set of gaze features (basic and graph-based) with clear ablation experiments.
- Tertiary contribution: feature-importance analysis and a feasibility discussion for using gaze in NLP applications.

- Clear feature design and ablation that isolates the impact of sentiment, sarcasm, and gaze feature groups.
- Demonstrated improvements on challenging data with implicit sentiment and sarcasm, including held-out ironic subsets.
- Statistical rigor via McNemar significance tests and held-out evaluations focused on complex constructs.
- Feasibility discussion acknowledges deployment challenges (mobile eye-tracking, consent) and outlines plausible scenarios.
- Timely and relevant work combining psycholinguistic signals with NLP tasks.

- Dataset heterogeneity (mixing tweets and reviews) complicates interpretation and likely depresses absolute performance; per-domain results are needed.
- Baseline design uses cross-domain classifiers, which may understate baseline performance; stronger in-domain and SOTA text-only baselines are needed.
- Practicality and scalability concerns: unclear how performance degrades with fewer readers, single-reader signals, or consumer-grade hardware.
- Potential leakage/reader effects: unclear whether cross-validation folds are reader-disjoint; need leave-one-reader-out tests to avoid overfitting to readers.
- Limited analysis of gaze-only performance and interactions; deeper ablation of gaze subfeatures and interactions with text features is missing.

- Are there any overlaps between Pang & Lee / Sentiment140 training data and Datasets 1/2, and are cross-validation folds disjoint by reader?
- Please report results separately for reviews vs. tweets and for sarcastic/ironic vs. non-ironic across all experiments.
- How does performance vary with number of readers aggregated (e.g., 1, 3, 5, 7) and with simulated noisier/consumer-grade eye-tracking?
- Which gaze subgroups (basic vs. graph-based vs. reading-difficulty controls) drive the gains when added to text+sarcasm features?
- Does the method generalize across datasets/domains (e.g., train on one eye-tracking dataset and test on another)?

- Minor editorial cleanup needed (consistent bolding/phrasing) and clarification of some feature nomenclature (e.g., PSDH/PSDS vs. PSH/PSS).
