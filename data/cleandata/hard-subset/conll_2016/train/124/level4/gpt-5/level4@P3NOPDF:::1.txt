This paper explores an intriguing direction: augmenting sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection with cognitive features derived from human eye-movement patterns. The authors record annotators’ gaze behavior and use it to construct additional features, claiming novelty for bringing such cognitive signals into NLP for these tasks. The paper is clearly written and easy to follow, and the idea is thought-provoking with potential to inspire cross-disciplinary work.

Strengths
- Clarity and organization: The paper is generally well structured and readable.
- Novel perspective: Incorporating eye-tracking features into sentiment and sarcasm modeling is an interesting idea that could stimulate research in other NLP applications.

Weaknesses
- Motivation for sentiment: The paper does not convincingly justify why eye-movement features should help detect sentiment polarity. While gaze may reflect reading difficulty or surprise, the causal or correlational link to positive/negative sentiment is not clearly articulated. Without a stronger theoretical or empirical grounding, the use of cognitive features for sentiment risks seeming ad hoc.
- Marginal gains: The reported improvement when adding gaze (Sn+Sr+Gz) over text-only baselines (Sn+Sr) appears small. It is unclear whether the gains are statistically significant, consistent across datasets or classes, or practically meaningful given the cost of collecting gaze data.
- Feasibility and use cases: The discussion in Section 7 does not fully address practical deployment. The example in Section 7.2, in particular, does not make it evident why gaze would help in that scenario beyond what robust text-based models can already capture. The argument that gaze provides unique, necessary signal remains unconvincing.

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen the motivation: Provide concrete hypotheses linking specific gaze phenomena to sentiment and sarcasm. For example, do negative sentiments elicit longer fixation durations on sentiment-bearing tokens, more regressions around negators, or distinctive patterns around intensifiers? Are sarcasm cues associated with increased regressions when expectations are violated? Cite relevant cognitive psychology/psycholinguistics literature to ground these hypotheses.
- Deepen the analysis: 
  - Report statistical significance tests for all improvements and include effect sizes.
  - Provide ablations and feature-importance analyses to identify which gaze features contribute most.
  - Include per-class and per-example error analyses to show where gaze helps (e.g., negation-heavy sentences, subtle sarcasm).
  - If sarcasm benefits more than sentiment polarity, make that distinction explicit.
- Clarify cost-benefit and scalability: Discuss the practical constraints of collecting eye-tracking data at scale (equipment, participant variability, calibration), and compare the marginal gains to those from cheaper alternatives (e.g., adding more labeled text data or leveraging stronger text encoders). Address domain transfer: do gaze features generalize across readers, demographics, devices, and genres?
- Revisit Section 7 and 7.2:
  - Make the feasibility argument concrete with quantitative estimates (collection time, cost per instance, expected performance lift).
  - For the Section 7.2 example, show a detailed case study where text-only models fail but gaze resolves the ambiguity, and explain the mechanism (which gaze features changed the decision and why).
  - Consider hybrid or proxy approaches (e.g., limited gaze for model calibration; predicting gaze from text and using predicted features) and discuss trade-offs.
- Experimental clarity: Clearly define Sn, Sr, and Gz; ensure fair comparisons with identical training data and model capacity. If possible, add baselines with modern text-only architectures to contextualize the incremental value of gaze.
- Reproducibility and ethics: If feasible, release code and a sanitized portion of the gaze-annotated data. Include a brief discussion of privacy/consent considerations when collecting cognitive signals.

Overall assessment
The paper is well presented and introduces a creative idea with potential impact, particularly for sarcasm detection and other pragmatically complex phenomena. However, the current version does not sufficiently justify the use of gaze for sentiment polarity, shows only marginal empirical gains without strong analysis, and leaves key feasibility questions unanswered. With a stronger theoretical motivation, deeper empirical evidence (including significance, ablations, and targeted case studies), and a more convincing deployment story, the contribution would be much more compelling.
