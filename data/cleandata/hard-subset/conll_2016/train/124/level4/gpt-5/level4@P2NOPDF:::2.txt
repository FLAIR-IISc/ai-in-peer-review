This paper proposes incorporating eye-tracking features as cognitive signals for sentiment analysis. Using gaze data as a proxy for cognitive load is a compelling and timely idea. The presentation of the feature sets and their comparisons is clear and informative, and I appreciate that the authors discuss feasibility in Section 7.

I have concerns about the evaluation design, particularly the mixing of domains (e.g., combining movie reviews and tweets). Separating domains during training and testing could improve performance within each domain and make the results easier to interpret. For instance, reporting in-domain results (train/test on movie reviews; train/test on tweets) alongside cross-domain results would clarify the contributions of the proposed features and provide a fairer comparison to relevant baselines in each setting.

Relatedly, the results in Table 2 seem low relative to state-of-the-art performance on the Pang and Lee dataset, but they appear more competitive when compared with typical results on Twitter data. Presenting domain-specific baselines and results would help contextualize these numbers and prevent misleading comparisons across inherently different datasets.

A clarification question on Section 3.3: can you confirm that there is no overlap between training and test snippets for datasets 1 and 2, even when they originate from the same sources (e.g., Pang & Lee and Sentiment140)? Ensuring strict deduplication and preventing leakage is important, especially when working with snippet-level data derived from shared corpora.

Minor style comment: the frequent use of boldface is somewhat distracting and could be reduced to improve readability.

Overall, this is an interesting contribution that explores a novel cognitive signal for sentiment analysis. With clearer domain-separated evaluations and explicit confirmation of data partitioning practices, the paper would be significantly strengthened.
