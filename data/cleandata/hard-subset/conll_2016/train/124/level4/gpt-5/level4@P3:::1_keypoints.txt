- Summary: Proposes augmenting sentiment analysis with gaze-derived cognitive features (basic and graph-based) and reports up to 3.7–9.3% F-score gains on ironic/implicit-sentiment datasets.

- Contribution 1: Empirical evidence that eye-tracking features yield a small but statistically significant improvement in polarity classification, especially for sarcasm/irony.

- Contribution 2: Design and operationalization of basic (fixation/saccade/regression) and graph-based gaze-saliency features, with feature-importance analysis showing many gaze features ranked highly.

- Contribution 3: Careful evaluation on challenging datasets rich in irony/implicit sentiment, including significance testing and held-out analyses focused on complex constructs.

- Strength: Targets hard SA cases (sarcasm/irony) where baseline systems perform poorly, and shows larger relative gains for ironic texts.

- Strength: Clear, reproducible feature engineering motivated by psycholinguistics, with chi-squared rankings highlighting gaze features’ importance.

- Strength: Sound experimental methodology—multiple classifiers, 10-fold CV on two datasets, significance testing, and dimensionality control (PCA).

- Strength: Insightful focus showing sarcasm/thwarting features help substantially and gaze features provide additional incremental benefits.

- Strength: Timely idea that cognitive signals could inspire improvements in other implicit-cue tasks (subjectivity, emotion, discourse).

- Weakness: Limited theoretical justification linking gaze patterns directly to sentiment polarity beyond indicating processing difficulty.

- Weakness: Marginal absolute gains from gaze when added to sentiment + sarcasm features (e.g., +0.6%–1.5%), which may limit practical impact given data collection costs.

- Weakness: Practicality concerns—method assumes gaze availability at inference, raising deployment, privacy, and latency issues without a clear pipeline.

- Weakness: Missing comparisons to strong neural baselines (CNNs/RNNs) to establish whether gaze adds value over contemporary deep models.

- Weakness: Potential confounds and normalization issues across different eye-trackers and participants; robustness (e.g., leave-one-participant-out) not fully assessed.

- Question: How would the model be used when gaze is unavailable—can a student model predict gaze features from text for inference?

- Question: How are gaze features normalized across hardware sampling rates and individual reading speeds; any cross-device or leave-one-participant-out tests?

- Question: Can you provide finer-grained per-phenomenon breakdowns beyond irony (e.g., implicit sentiment without irony, thwarted expectations)?

- Question: Can you compare against contemporary neural baselines and/or augment them with gaze to test orthogonality?

- Question: Please clarify the end-to-end workflow and consent/privacy handling for the proposed ecommerce/mobile eye-tracking deployment scenario.

- Overall evaluation: Novel, well-executed exploration showing statistically significant but modest gains—particularly useful for sarcasm/irony—while practical deployability, marginal improvements, and lack of neural comparisons remain concerns.
