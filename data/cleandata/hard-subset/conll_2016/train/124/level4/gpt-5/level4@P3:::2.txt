Summary
This paper augments standard text-based sentiment features with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movement patterns. Using two publicly available eye-tracking datasets, the authors show that adding gaze-based features to sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features yields improvements in F-score (up to 3.7% and 9.3% on the two datasets). A held-out analysis suggests larger gains for sentences containing irony/sarcasm than for relatively simple sentences. The paper also discusses feasibility considerations for obtaining gaze data at scale and provides a feature-importance analysis that highlights the contribution of cognitive features.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: An empirical demonstration that gaze-derived cognitive features improve sentiment polarity classification, with stronger effects on linguistically complex phenomena such as irony and sarcasm.
- Secondary: A carefully engineered set of gaze features, including both basic measures (fixations, saccades, regressions) and graph-based “gaze-saliency” features, along with a clear ablation comparing feature groups.
- Tertiary: An analysis of feature importance and a feasibility discussion that frames practical considerations for future applications of gaze in NLP.

Strengths
- Clear feature design and ablation: The paper thoughtfully defines gaze features and organizes experiments to isolate the impact of sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting features, and gaze features. The ablation shows that gaze adds measurable value over strong text-only feature sets, and the feature-importance analysis (chi-squared ranking) indicates that many top-ranked features are gaze-based.
- Improvements on challenging data: The datasets include many instances with implicit sentiment, sarcasm, and thwarting—cases that routinely degrade text-only performance. The reported gains (up to 3.7% and 9.3% F-score) and the larger relative improvement on the held-out ironic subset (6.1%) support the claim that cognitive signals are particularly helpful on complex inputs where surface cues are weak.
- Statistical rigor: The use of McNemar tests to establish significance and a held-out evaluation focused on complex constructs strengthen the empirical claims beyond simple cross-validation averages.
- Useful feasibility discussion: The paper does not ignore the obvious deployment challenge (access to gaze), and it outlines plausible scenarios (e.g., mobile eye-tracking) and user-consent considerations. While not conclusive, this section shows awareness of practical constraints.
- Relevance and timeliness: Bringing cognitive signals to sentiment analysis is compelling and aligns with a growing interest in combining psycholinguistic evidence with NLP models.

Weaknesses
- Dataset heterogeneity and interpretability: Results pool tweets and reviews, which differ substantially in style, length, and lexical distribution. This makes absolute performance hard to interpret and invites confounds. For example, SOTA on Pang & Lee reviews is typically much higher than the results in Table 2; mixing with Twitter likely depresses scores. Reporting per-domain performance (reviews vs. tweets) and per-phenomenon performance (irony vs. non-irony) throughout would clarify the effect sizes.
- Baseline design and domain mismatch: Section 3.3 uses classifiers trained on Pang & Lee and Sentiment140 to “test” the difficulty of Datasets 1 and 2, but this cross-domain setup may underestimate baseline performance and overstate dataset difficulty. Stronger, in-domain baselines (trained and tuned on the same domain distributions) would be a fairer point of comparison. Additionally, comparing against a competitive text-only SOTA (e.g., tuned linear models with regularization or modern neural baselines) on the same splits would better contextualize gains from gaze.
- Practicality and scalability: The approach averages gaze features over multiple annotators per snippet, using high-quality lab eye-trackers in one dataset and a lower-quality device in the other. It remains unclear how performance degrades with fewer readers, consumer-grade hardware, or single-reader signals—conditions that are far more likely in practice. The feasibility discussion is welcome but speculative; empirical sensitivity analyses are needed.
- Potential leakage and reader effects: Because gaze features reflect readers’ processing of the same text they label, care is needed to ensure there is no inadvertent leakage (e.g., reusing the same readers across folds). It is not stated whether cross-validation partitions are reader-disjoint. A leave-one-reader-out design would test generalization across readers and avoid overfitting to individual gaze idiosyncrasies.
- Limited analysis of “Gz only” and feature interactions: Gaze features alone underperform text features, and gains appear only in combination. This is reasonable, but a deeper analysis of which gaze subfeatures drive improvements (basic vs. graph-based) and how they interact with specific text features (e.g., negation, discourse markers) would strengthen the causal story.

Questions to Authors
- Data splits and overlap: In Section 3.3, can you confirm there is no overlap in snippets between the training data used for the baseline classifiers (Pang & Lee, Sentiment140) and Datasets 1 and 2? Also, are the cross-validation folds for Datasets 1 and 2 disjoint by reader, not just by sentence?
- Domain-wise reporting: Can you report results separately for reviews vs. tweets (and for sarcastic/ironic vs. non-ironic) across all experiments in Table 2? This would clarify where gaze helps most.
- Sensitivity to number and quality of readers: How does performance vary with the number of readers aggregated (e.g., 1, 3, 5, 7)? Can you simulate lower sampling rates or noisier calibration to approximate consumer devices?
- Feature ablation within gaze: Which gaze subgroups (basic vs. graph-based vs. reading-difficulty controls) contribute most to the gains when added to Sn+Sr?
- Generalization beyond these datasets: Have you tested transfer to a new dataset with gaze (or to a new domain), possibly via training on one eye-tracking dataset and testing on the other?

Additional Comments
- The paper would benefit from minor editorial cleanup (some inconsistent bolding and phrasing). Clarify a few feature definitions (e.g., PSDH/PSDS vs. PSH/PSS nomenclature) and ensure consistent notation.
