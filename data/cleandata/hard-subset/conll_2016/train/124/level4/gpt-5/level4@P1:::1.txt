Summary and contributions
This paper augments traditional sentiment analysis (SA) and sarcasm detection features with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movement patterns. The authors extract both basic and graph-based gaze features (e.g., fixations, regressions, saccade properties, and saliency graph degrees) and evaluate them on two public datasets containing short texts with complex phenomena such as sarcasm and thwarted expectations. They report modest but statistically significant gains when adding gaze features to sentiment and sarcasm-related features, and show larger relative gains on texts with complex constructs. They also discuss feature significance, error sources, and feasibility.

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) An empirical investigation showing that gaze-derived cognitive features provide small but consistent improvements over strong text-only feature sets for polarity detection, particularly on complex constructs (overall gains up to 3.7% and 9.3% on two datasets; +6.1% relative on a held-out subset with irony).
2) A feature design contribution introducing gaze-saliency graph features and a comprehensive set of gaze-based indicators for SA.
3) A targeted evaluation on challenging datasets with nuanced phenomena, including feature significance analysis and a discussion of practical feasibility.

Strengths
1) Interesting and potentially impactful idea: Incorporating cognitive signals is novel for sentiment polarity classification and could inspire work in other NLP tasks where textual cues are subtle (e.g., sarcasm, implicit sentiment).
2) Clear writing and organization: The paper is generally well structured and easy to follow, with a thorough presentation of features and datasets.
3) Solid experimental setup: The authors test multiple classifiers and feature combinations on two public datasets and provide held-out analysis focused on complex constructs. They perform a McNemar test to verify statistical significance and include a chi-squared feature importance analysis.
4) Relevance to challenging cases: The reported improvements are higher on texts with irony/sarcasm than on simpler cases, aligning with the stated goal of handling nuanced expressions where standard lexical features are insufficient.

Weaknesses
1) Motivation for polarity classification is underdeveloped: The link between gaze metrics and sentiment polarity is not well justified. Many gaze measures (e.g., regressions, fixation durations) primarily reflect processing difficulty, ambiguity, or incongruity, not sentiment per se. The paper would benefit from stronger theoretical or empirical grounding that connects specific gaze patterns to polarity cues beyond general reading difficulty or sarcasm-related surprisal.
2) Marginal incremental gains from cognitive features: Compared to Sn+Sr, adding gaze features yields relatively small additional improvements (e.g., +0.6% and +1.5% F-score). While statistically significant, the effect sizes are modest, particularly given the practical cost and complexity of collecting gaze data. Moreover, gaze-only features underperform text-based features, suggesting limited standalone predictive power.
3) Practical feasibility remains unconvincing: Although Section 7 discusses mobile eye tracking and potential applications (e.g., e-commerce reviews), the example in Section 7.2 does not convincingly justify real-world utility. It is unclear how systems would reliably obtain and calibrate gaze data at scale, ensure user consent, handle device variability and noise, or aggregate multiple readers’ signals for a single review. The deployment argument needs more concrete evidence and a clearer pathway from research to practice.

Questions to authors
1) Mechanistic link: Can you provide deeper motivation or evidence connecting specific gaze features (e.g., RSF, LREG, saliency degrees) to sentiment polarity rather than to general processing difficulty? Are there analyses showing that gaze features correlate with polarity even when controlling for readability and length?
2) Ablation details: Among the cognitive features, which groups (basic vs saliency-graph, regressions vs skips vs durations) contribute the most? A fine-grained ablation would clarify which signals drive the gains.
3) Reader aggregation: How many readers per snippet are necessary to achieve the reported gains? How does performance degrade if fewer readers are available, or if the gaze data is noisier (e.g., mobile eye-trackers)?
4) Generalization: Do the gains hold across domains beyond movies/tweets? How sensitive are the gaze features to domain shifts or to differences in annotator populations?
5) Inference-time assumptions: Is gaze required at training only, inference only, or both? If gaze is unavailable at inference, can a model trained with gaze features transfer improvements to text-only inference?
6) Ethics and consent: How do you envision handling privacy, consent, and data protection for large-scale eye-tracking, especially in commercial settings?

Additional comments
- The paper persuasively argues that cognitive features help on nuanced phenomena (e.g., irony). Clarifying whether the gains on straightforward polarity cases are meaningful would help position the contribution.
- A more detailed error analysis connecting specific failure modes to feature limitations (e.g., when regressions capture incongruity but not polarity direction) would strengthen the narrative.
- The feasibility section would benefit from concrete pilot studies with mobile eye-tracking (even small-scale) to support claims about real-world applicability.
