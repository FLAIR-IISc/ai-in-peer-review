Summary
The paper proposes incorporating eye-tracking–derived features as cognitive signals for sentiment analysis. The central idea—using gaze as a proxy for cognitive load to improve sentiment prediction—is interesting and timely. The paper provides a clear discussion of the features and a useful comparison of feature sets. I also appreciate that the feasibility of deploying eye-tracking in practical settings is explicitly addressed in Section 7.

Strengths
- Novel contribution: Introducing gaze-based features to sentiment analysis is a compelling direction that bridges cognitive signals with NLP tasks.
- Clarity of feature engineering: The feature definitions and comparisons are well presented, making it easier to assess what each feature family contributes.
- Feasibility discussion: Section 7 thoughtfully considers how eye-tracking could be collected or approximated in practice.

Main concerns and suggestions
- Domain conflation in datasets: Mixing movie reviews and tweets within training and evaluation likely obscures both performance and interpretation.
  - Please report in-domain results separately for each domain (e.g., train/test on movie reviews only; train/test on tweets only). This would clarify how effective the approach is per domain and may improve absolute performance.
  - In addition, report cross-domain transfer (e.g., train on movie reviews, test on tweets, and vice versa). This would illuminate the robustness and generalization of gaze features.
  - A combined setting (pooled domains) can still be reported, but the primary results should be per-domain for interpretability.

- Context for Table 2 results: The reported numbers appear low relative to state-of-the-art results on the Pang & Lee dataset but look much more competitive for Twitter data.
  - Please provide direct comparisons to strong text-only baselines under your exact splits (e.g., bag-of-ngrams with linear SVM/logistic regression, or standard neural baselines if appropriate).
  - Include ablations quantifying the incremental benefit of gaze features over text-only models. This will clarify whether gaze brings consistent gains and under what conditions.
  - Where possible, compare against established benchmarks or cite results under comparable splits to frame the magnitude of improvements.

- Train/test separation and potential overlap: In Section 3.3, please confirm that there are no overlapping snippets between training and test sets for Datasets 1 and 2, even if they originate from the same sources (e.g., Pang & Lee and Sentiment140). If there is any risk of near-duplicates or retweets, please describe the deduplication procedure.

- Statistical validity: Please report statistical significance (e.g., paired bootstrap, McNemar’s test) for the differences among feature sets and for the improvements due to gaze features, especially when margins are small.

- Feature robustness and controls:
  - Consider controlling for confounds such as sentence length and lexical difficulty, which can influence both gaze behavior and sentiment predictions.
  - If multiple readers contributed gaze data, clarify aggregation strategies (e.g., averaging across readers, normalization for individual reading speed).
  - Discuss data efficiency: how much gaze data is needed to see gains, and how performance scales with less gaze supervision?

- Feasibility and scalability: The discussion in Section 7 is valuable. It would further strengthen the paper to consider:
  - Using predicted gaze (from text-only models trained on a smaller gaze-annotated subset) to scale to larger corpora without new eye-tracking.
  - Substituting related cognitive proxies (e.g., reading times, surprisal) when eye-tracking is not available, and comparing their utility to actual gaze features.

Questions for the authors
- Can you confirm that there is no overlap between training and test snippets in Datasets 1 and 2, including near-duplicates?
- How many readers contributed eye-tracking data per instance, and how were inter-reader differences handled?
- Are the gaze features normalized for individual differences (e.g., baseline reading speed) and text length?
- Do the gains from gaze features persist across both domains when evaluated in-domain?

Minor comments
- The extra use of boldface in the text and tables is somewhat distracting; consider reserving bold for key takeaways only.
- Please ensure all tables clearly indicate domains, splits, and whether results are in-domain, cross-domain, or pooled.
- If space allows, provide a short error analysis to illustrate cases where gaze features are especially helpful (e.g., sarcasm, negation, or syntactically complex sentences).

Overall assessment
This is a well-motivated paper with a clear feature-design narrative and a promising direction at the intersection of cognitive signals and sentiment analysis. The main limitation is the current experimental setup, which conflates domains and makes it difficult to assess the true impact of gaze features relative to strong text-only baselines. With per-domain and cross-domain evaluations, stronger baselines, explicit ablations, and clearer train/test separation, the contribution would be significantly stronger and easier to interpret.
