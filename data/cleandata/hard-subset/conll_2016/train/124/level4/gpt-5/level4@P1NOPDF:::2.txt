This paper introduces eye-tracking features as cognitive signals for sentiment analysis. The idea of using eye-tracking as a proxy for cognitive load in this context is interesting and well-motivated.

The discussion of the features and the comparison across feature sets are clear and informative. I also appreciate that the authors address the feasibility of the approach in Section 7.

I wonder whether the evaluation would be stronger if the datasets did not conflate different domains (e.g., movie reviews and tweets). In particular, performance on movie reviews (respectively, tweets) might improve if the training data did not include tweets (respectively, movie reviews), and the results would be easier to interpret. As presented, the results in Table 2 look relatively low compared with state-of-the-art results on the Pang and Lee dataset, but they appear much more competitive when compared with results on Twitter data.

In Section 3.3, please confirm that there are no overlapping snippets between the training and test sets for datasets 1 and 2, even though they originate from the same sources (Pang & Lee and Sentiment140).

Minor: The extensive use of boldface is somewhat distracting.
