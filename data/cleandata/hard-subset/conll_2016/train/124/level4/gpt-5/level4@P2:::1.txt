Summary and contributions:
The paper augments standard textual features for sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection with cognitive features derived from readers’ eye-movement patterns. Using these features, the authors report F-score gains on two datasets, a feature-importance analysis, and results on a held-out subset emphasizing complex phenomena (irony/sarcasm). They also discuss feasibility and potential applications.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical study showing that gaze-derived cognitive features, when added to sentiment and sarcasm/thwarting features, yield small but statistically significant improvements on two datasets (max +0.6 and +1.5 F over Sn+Sr with SVM on D1 and D2, respectively).
2) A concrete set of gaze features (basic and graph-based “gaze-saliency” measures) integrated into a standard supervised SA pipeline.
3) Evidence that these features help more on instances with complex constructs (irony/sarcasm) than on simple instances in a held-out evaluation.

Strengths:
1) Clear presentation and organization. The paper is generally well written and easy to follow, with datasets, features, and models described in a straightforward way.
2) Interesting and potentially influential idea. Leveraging eye-tracking to capture cognitive signals could inspire related work in sentiment analysis and other NLP tasks involving pragmatics (e.g., sarcasm, irony), as well as in feature learning settings.
3) Reasonable empirical methodology. The authors evaluate across two datasets, compare multiple classifiers, and provide significance testing and feature-importance analysis, which helps substantiate their claims.

Weaknesses:
1) Motivation for sentiment polarity remains underdeveloped. While eye movements plausibly correlate with reading difficulty, ambiguity, or surprisal, the paper does not convincingly explain why these signals should predict sentiment polarity itself (as opposed to just complexity). A clearer theoretical or empirical link between specific gaze patterns and positive vs. negative polarity would strengthen the case. For example, an analysis showing which polarity categories benefit most from which gaze features, controlling for readability and length, would help.
2) Marginal incremental gains from cognitive features. The headline improvements over Sn+Sr after adding gaze features are modest (SVM: D1 72.9 to 73.5; D2 70.3 to 71.8). Although statistically significant, the practical impact is limited, especially given the added complexity and data requirements. A more granular ablation of individual gaze features, and qualitative error analyses of cases fixed only by gaze, would make the benefit more compelling.
3) Feasibility and deployment remain unconvincing. Section 7 outlines scenarios leveraging mobile eye-tracking, but the practicality is unclear. The example in 7.2 (e.g., e-commerce reviews) does not convincingly motivate why real-time eye-tracking would be available, affordable, and acceptable at scale for end users, nor how consent, privacy, and device variability would be addressed. Stronger evidence or a pilot demonstrating data collection in-the-wild would help.

Questions to authors (optional):
- Can you disentangle the contribution of gaze features from readability and length effects beyond the current controls? For instance, partial correlation analyses or regression with readability and length as covariates, followed by incremental contribution of gaze features.
- Which specific error types are corrected by gaze features that Sn or Sn+Sr cannot handle? Please include representative examples illustrating how particular gaze signals led to the correct prediction.
- How robust are the gaze features across hardware (SR Research vs. Tobii) and sampling rates? Do you observe consistent gains when training and testing across devices?
- How do the proposed features compare against stronger text-only baselines (e.g., recent neural models) or simple ensembling with them? Even a small experiment would help contextualize the usefulness of gaze features today.
- What is the anticipated cost and scale of data collection for practical deployment? Please discuss user consent, privacy safeguards, and anticipated drop-off in data quality on commodity devices.

Overall evaluation:
The paper introduces a creative idea and provides careful initial evidence that gaze features can yield statistically significant improvements, especially on complex phenomena. However, the conceptual link to sentiment polarity is not fully justified, the incremental gains over strong textual baselines are small, and the feasibility claims need firmer grounding.
