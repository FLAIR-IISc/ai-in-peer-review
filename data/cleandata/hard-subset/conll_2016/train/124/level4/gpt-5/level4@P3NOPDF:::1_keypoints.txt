- Clear, well-structured paper that is easy to follow.
- Novel idea: incorporating eye-tracking (gaze) features into sentiment and sarcasm modeling is interesting and cross-disciplinary.
- Weak motivation for sentiment: the causal/correlational link between gaze patterns and sentiment polarity is not convincingly established.
- Reported gains from adding gaze are marginal; statistical significance, consistency across datasets/classes, and practical importance are unclear.
- Practical deployment and use-case discussion are insufficient; examples do not show why gaze provides unique, necessary signal beyond text models.
- Strengthen motivation by proposing concrete hypotheses linking specific gaze phenomena to sentiment/sarcasm and citing relevant psycholinguistics.
- Deepen analysis: report significance and effect sizes, perform ablations and feature-importance analyses, and provide per-class and per-example error analyses.
- Explicitly state whether sarcasm benefits more than sentiment and show where gaze helps (e.g., negation-heavy or subtle sarcasm cases).
- Clarify cost-benefit and scalability: discuss equipment, participant variability, calibration, domain transfer, and compare to cheaper alternatives (more text data, stronger encoders).
- Revise Section 7/7.2 with quantitative estimates (collection time/cost, expected lift) and a detailed case study showing how gaze resolves specific failures of text-only models.
- Consider hybrid/proxy approaches (limited gaze for calibration, predicting gaze from text) and discuss trade-offs.
- Improve experimental clarity: define Sn, Sr, and Gz, ensure fair comparisons, and add modern strong text-only baselines.
- Address reproducibility and ethics: release code/sanitized data if possible and discuss privacy/consent when collecting cognitive signals.
- Overall: creative and promising, especially for sarcasm, but needs stronger theoretical grounding, deeper empirical evidence, and a more convincing deployment story.
