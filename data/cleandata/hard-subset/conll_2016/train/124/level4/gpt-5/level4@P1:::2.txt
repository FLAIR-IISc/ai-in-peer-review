Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes augmenting standard textual features for sentiment analysis with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking, treating gaze behavior as a proxy for cognitive processing difficulty.
- It provides a clear, systematic comparison of feature sets (sentiment features, sarcasm/thwarting features, and gaze-based features), with ablations that isolate each group’s contribution.
- It discusses the feasibility of deploying gaze-based features at scale via mobile eye-tracking, an important practical consideration often omitted in similar work.

Strengths
- Novel idea with clear motivation: Leveraging eye movements as cognitive signals for sentiment analysis is interesting and well argued, especially for handling nuanced phenomena like sarcasm and implicit sentiment.
- Transparent feature engineering and ablation: The feature definitions are clearly described, and the comparisons across Uni, Sn, Sr, and Gz (e.g., Table 2) are easy to follow and informative.
- Practicality addressed: Section 7’s feasibility discussion (availability of mobile eye-trackers, consent considerations, and application scenarios) is thoughtful and strengthens the paper’s applied relevance.

Weaknesses
- Domain conflation in training/evaluation: Mixing movie reviews and tweets complicates interpretation and may depress performance due to domain shift. It would likely improve both accuracy and clarity to avoid training a model for one domain using data from another. In particular, the Table 2 results look low relative to well-known results on Pang & Lee when considered in isolation, but are more reasonable for Twitter; separating domains would make this explicit and fairer.
- Baseline comparability: Related to the above, comparisons against state-of-the-art baselines on standard datasets (e.g., Pang & Lee) are difficult to interpret when trained/evaluated on mixed-domain data. Stronger domain-controlled baselines (or per-domain reporting) would improve the empirical case.
- Potential data leakage (unclear): In Section 3.3 you state that the statistical classifiers are trained on snippets from Pang & Lee and Sentiment140. Datasets 1 and 2 also draw from similar sources. The paper should clearly confirm that there are no overlapping snippets between training and test (Datasets 1 and 2), even if they originate from the same corpora, to rule out leakage.

Questions to authors
- Can you confirm that there is zero overlap between the training data (collected from Pang & Lee and Sentiment140) and the test items in Datasets 1 and 2? If you deduplicated, how was this ensured (exact match, normalization, or fuzzy matching)?
- Could you provide per-domain results (movie reviews vs. tweets) and/or train domain-specific models? This would clarify how much each domain benefits from gaze features and how much domain mixing affects overall performance.
- If feasible, can you report comparisons against standard baselines on Pang & Lee using only in-domain training and testing, to contextualize the absolute F-scores relative to typical results on that dataset?

Additional comments
- Minor: Some of the extra use of boldface is visually distracting. Consider reducing the emphasis to improve readability.
