This paper proposes an innovative approach to sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection by incorporating cognitive features derived from human annotators’ eye-movement patterns. The central claim is that these features, reflecting cognitive processes during reading, can enhance sentiment analysis performance, representing a novel contribution to the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The integration of eye-tracking data and cognitive features into a traditional sentiment analysis pipeline is a genuinely novel contribution. While eye-tracking has been used in psycholinguistic studies related to language processing, its application to improve sentiment classification is a fresh perspective.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate a thoughtful approach to feature engineering, combining traditional sentiment features with both sarcasm/thwarting related features *and* a detailed set of cognitive features. The exploration of gaze-saliency graphs to derive complex features is particularly commendable.
3. **Empirical Validation & Statistical Significance:** The experiments on two datasets demonstrate statistically significant performance improvements with the inclusion of cognitive features (up to 9.3% on Dataset 2). The use of a held-out dataset and statistical testing (McNemar test) strengthens the validity of these findings.
4. **Detailed Error Analysis & Feasibility Discussion:** The paper includes a discussion of potential error sources and a practical assessment of the approach’s feasibility, acknowledging challenges and proposing potential solutions (e.g., the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers).

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Justification of Cognitive Feature Relevance:** While the inclusion of cognitive features yields performance gains, the paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of *why* these specific features are indicative of sentiment or sarcasm. The connection between eye-movement patterns and underlying cognitive processes relating to sentiment interpretation requires further elaboration.
2. **Magnitude of Improvement:** Although statistically significant, the performance improvement achieved by adding cognitive features (particularly when compared to the baseline of sentiment + sarcasm features) is relatively modest. A deeper analysis of *when* the cognitive features are most effective (e.g., for specific types of sentences or sentiment expressions) would be valuable.
3. **Practical Applicability Concerns:** The discussion of feasibility, while appreciated, does not fully address the practical challenges of obtaining eye-tracking data at scale. The example application in Section 7.2 (e-commerce reviews) feels somewhat tenuous without a more concrete explanation of how this system would integrate into a real-world application and address user privacy concerns.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed theoretical justification for why the selected eye-movement features (e.g., fixation duration, regression count) are expected to correlate with sentiment or sarcasm detection?
2.  Did you investigate which specific cognitive features contributed the *most* to the observed performance improvements? A feature ablation study would be helpful.
3.  How sensitive are the results to variations in eye-tracker quality (e.g., comparing results from the high-quality Eyelink 1000 to the lower-quality Tobii T120)?
4.  Regarding the feasibility discussion, what strategies would be employed to address potential privacy concerns when collecting eye-tracking data from users in a real-world application?
