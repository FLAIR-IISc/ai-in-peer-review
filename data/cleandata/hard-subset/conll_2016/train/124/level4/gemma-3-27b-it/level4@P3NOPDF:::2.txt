This paper explores the incorporation of eye-tracking features into sentiment analysis, framing them as indicators of cognitive load. The central idea – utilizing eye-tracking data as a proxy for cognitive processes during sentiment assessment – is an interesting and potentially valuable contribution.

The discussion of the features themselves, and the comparison of different feature set configurations, is particularly clear and insightful. The authors are also to be commended for addressing the practical feasibility of their approach in Section 7, which strengthens the overall impact of the work.

However, I have some concerns regarding the evaluation methodology. The current datasets conflate distinct domains – specifically, movie reviews and tweets. This mixing may hinder performance and complicate interpretation. It’s plausible that model performance on movie reviews (and conversely, on tweets) could be improved by training and testing on data from the same domain. Separating the datasets would also allow for a more focused and interpretable analysis of the results. For instance, the reported results in Table 2 appear relatively low when benchmarked against state-of-the-art performance on the Pang and Lee dataset, but become more competitive when considered in the context of Twitter sentiment analysis. Domain separation would clarify these comparisons.

Further clarification is needed regarding data contamination between training and testing sets. In Section 3.3, the authors state there are no overlapping snippets between the training and testing data for Datasets 1 and 2. Could the authors confirm this is the case *even when* considering data originating from the same sources, such as Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140? Ensuring the absence of such overlap is crucial for robust evaluation.

Finally, a minor stylistic point: the frequent use of bold text is somewhat distracting and could be reduced for improved readability.
