This paper proposes a novel and interesting approach to sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection by incorporating cognitive features derived from human annotators’ eye-movement patterns. The authors claim this is the first work to integrate such cognitive features into the NLP pipeline.

**Contributions:**

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of the potential benefits of augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive data obtained through eye-tracking. While the idea of utilizing psycholinguistic data in NLP is not entirely new, this paper represents a valuable exploration of eye-movement patterns as a source of information for improving sentiment classification, particularly for nuanced language like sarcasm. A secondary contribution lies in the curated datasets utilized, which contain instances with complexities like implicit sentiment, sarcasm, and thwarted expectations – making them suitable for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. Finally, the detailed feature engineering, encompassing both traditional linguistic features and novel gaze-based features, provides a solid foundation for future research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea of leveraging cognitive features for sentiment analysis is genuinely novel and has the potential to inspire research beyond sentiment analysis, potentially impacting areas like text comprehension and human-computer interaction. The exploration of eye-movement data offers a new perspective on understanding how humans process sentiment-bearing text.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of existing sentiment analysis techniques by incorporating a wide range of traditional features. The development of novel gaze-based features, particularly those derived from the saliency graph, showcases a creative approach to extracting meaningful information from eye-tracking data.
3. **Rigorous Experimental Evaluation:** The paper includes a thorough experimental evaluation with two datasets and a comparison against baseline systems. The statistical significance tests and the ablation studies (demonstrated in Table 2) support the claim that the inclusion of cognitive features leads to performance improvements.
4. **Insightful Error Analysis and Feasibility Discussion:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach and discuss potential challenges, such as the practicalities of data collection and the influence of external factors. The detailed discussion on the feasibility of the approach, including potential applications with mobile eye-trackers, is well-reasoned and strengthens the paper.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Justification for Cognitive Features:** While the concept is intriguing, the paper could benefit from a more robust theoretical justification for *why* specific eye-movement patterns are expected to correlate with sentiment polarity. The current explanation relies largely on the assumption that cognitive processes are related to eye movements, but a more detailed explanation of the underlying mechanisms would strengthen the argument.
2. **Marginal Improvement in Some Cases:** The performance improvement observed when adding cognitive features is not substantial in all experiments, particularly when comparing Sn+Sr+Gz to Sn+Sr. While statistically significant, the practical impact of a small F-score increase should be discussed more critically.
3. **Limited Discussion of Individual Feature Contributions:** While the paper performs feature significance analysis (Table 3), it lacks a detailed discussion of which specific cognitive features are most impactful and why. This would provide valuable insights for future research and feature engineering efforts.
4. **Practical Application Concerns:** The feasibility discussion, while well-intentioned, does not fully address the potential challenges of collecting and processing eye-tracking data in real-world scenarios. The example provided in Section 7.2 relies on a somewhat idealized application context and could be strengthened by considering more realistic use cases.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed theoretical explanation of the cognitive processes that are hypothesized to underlie the observed correlations between eye-movement patterns and sentiment polarity?
2. Which specific cognitive features were found to be the most consistently predictive of sentiment, and what might explain their effectiveness?
3. How might the performance of the proposed approach be affected by variations in eye-tracker quality (e.g., sampling rate, accuracy) and individual differences in reading habits?
4. Beyond the application scenario discussed in Section 7.2, can you envision other realistic applications where the benefits of incorporating cognitive features would outweigh the costs and complexities of data collection?
