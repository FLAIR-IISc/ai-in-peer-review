- Paper proposes using human eye-movement (cognitive) features to improve sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection; claims first integration of such features into NLP pipeline.  
- Main contribution: shows potential benefit of augmenting traditional sentiment features with eye-tracking data.  
- Secondary contribution: curated datasets containing implicit sentiment, sarcasm, and thwarted expectations for evaluation.  
- Contribution: detailed feature engineering combining traditional linguistic and novel gaze-based (saliency graph) features.  
- Strength: novel idea with potential impact beyond sentiment analysis (text comprehension, HCI).  
- Strength: comprehensive inclusion of traditional features and creative gaze-based feature design.  
- Strength: rigorous experiments on two datasets, baseline comparisons, significance tests, and ablation studies.  
- Strength: thoughtful error analysis and feasibility discussion, including mobile eye-tracker applications.  
- Weakness: lacks stronger theoretical justification for why specific eye-movement patterns map to sentiment polarity.  
- Weakness: improvements from cognitive features are marginal in some comparisons; practical significance needs discussion.  
- Weakness: limited detailed analysis of which individual cognitive features drive performance and why.  
- Weakness: feasibility discussion does not fully address real-world data-collection and processing challenges.  
- Question: request for a detailed theoretical account of cognitive processes linking eye movements to sentiment polarity.  
- Question: which specific cognitive/gaze features were most predictive and why?  
- Question: how do eye-tracker quality and individual reading differences affect performance?  
- Question: what additional realistic applications justify the cost/complexity of collecting cognitive features?  
- Additional: add a visual example of a saliency graph to clarify Section 4.4.  
- Additional: replace unclear abbreviation "NB SVM RB" in Table 1 with full classifier names.  
- Additional: ensure consistent terminology (e.g., "gaze features" vs. "cognitive features").
