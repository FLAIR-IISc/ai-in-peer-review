- Novelty: Integrating eye-tracking-derived cognitive features into sentiment and sarcasm detection is a genuinely novel contribution.  
- Feature engineering: Thoughtful combination of traditional sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, and detailed cognitive features, including gaze-saliency graph-derived features.  
- Empirical validation: Experiments on two datasets show statistically significant improvements (up to 9.3%) with held-out testing and McNemar significance testing.  
- Error analysis & feasibility: Provides detailed error analysis and a realistic feasibility discussion, acknowledging challenges and proposing possible solutions (e.g., mobile eye-trackers).  
- Theoretical justification lacking: The paper needs a deeper explanation of why specific eye-movement features reflect sentiment or sarcasm-related cognitive processes.  
- Modest improvement size: Performance gains, while significant, are relatively modest over baseline; more analysis is needed on when cognitive features are most effective.  
- Practical applicability concerns: Feasibility discussion does not fully address large-scale data collection, real-world integration, or user privacy implications.  
- Question on theory: Request for a more detailed theoretical justification linking fixation duration, regression count, etc., to sentiment/sarcasm detection.  
- Question on feature importance: Did the authors perform a feature ablation or otherwise identify which cognitive features contributed most to gains?  
- Question on eye-tracker variability: How sensitive are results to eye-tracker quality (e.g., Eyelink 1000 vs Tobii T120)?  
- Question on privacy/feasibility: What strategies will be used to address privacy concerns and practical data-collection challenges in real-world deployments?
