This paper introduces eye-tracking features as cognitive features to enhance sentiment analysis performance. The core idea – leveraging eye-movement patterns as a proxy for cognitive load during sentiment interpretation – is interesting and potentially valuable. The paper provides a clear discussion of the features used and a comprehensive comparison of different feature set combinations, which is a significant strength. The inclusion of a feasibility discussion in Section 7 is also commendable, demonstrating consideration of practical limitations.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the demonstration that incorporating cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data can measurably improve sentiment analysis accuracy, particularly for texts with complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. While feature engineering in sentiment analysis is not novel, the specific application of eye-tracking data in this context and the associated performance gains represent a worthwhile contribution. A second, valuable contribution is the detailed feature analysis, which provides insights into which eye-movement metrics are most informative for sentiment detection. Finally, the paper’s exploration of the feasibility of this approach, acknowledging the challenges of data acquisition and user consent, is a pragmatic and important addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Improvement in Sentiment Analysis:** The paper demonstrates statistically significant improvements in F-score (up to 9.3% on Dataset 2) by augmenting traditional features with cognitive features. This improvement is particularly pronounced for complex texts, as highlighted in Section 5.1 and Table 4, indicating the method’s potential to address limitations of existing approaches. The rigorous experimental setup, including 10-fold cross-validation and a statistical significance test, strengthens this argument.
2. **Novelty of Cognitive Feature Integration:**  The incorporation of eye-tracking data as cognitive features within a sentiment analysis framework is a novel approach. This moves beyond purely linguistic features and attempts to capture the cognitive processes involved in sentiment interpretation. The detailed explanation of these features (Section 4.3 and 4.5) and their theoretical grounding in cognitive science contribute to the paper's value.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Analysis:** The feature significance analysis (Table 3) provides valuable insights into which cognitive features are most informative for sentiment detection. Identifying that gaze-based features contribute significantly to performance is a key finding, guiding future research in this area.
4. **Pragmatic Discussion of Feasibility:** Section 7 addresses the practical challenges of acquiring eye-tracking data, acknowledging the need for mobile eye-trackers and user consent. The discussion of potential application scenarios (e.g., mobile applications for e-commerce) demonstrates the authors’ awareness of real-world constraints and opportunities.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dataset Heterogeneity:** The datasets (Dataset 1 and Dataset 2) combine data from different sources (movie reviews and tweets). This heterogeneity may introduce noise and limit the generalizability of the results. It would be beneficial to see performance reported on more homogenous datasets, or to perform separate analyses for each data source. As the authors note, it impacts the interpretation of results relative to existing benchmarks (e.g., Pang & Lee).
2. **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** While the paper demonstrates improvement over a baseline system trained on standard datasets, it doesn’t thoroughly compare against more recent, state-of-the-art sentiment analysis models, particularly those employing deep learning techniques. A more comprehensive comparison would better position the contribution of this work within the broader field.
3. **Potential for Data Dependency:** The cognitive features are derived from specific annotators’ eye-movement patterns. The extent to which these patterns generalize to other populations or annotation tasks is unclear.  Further investigation into the robustness of these features across different annotator groups would strengthen the findings.
4. **Averaging of Individual Eye-Tracking Data:** The method of averaging gaze data across participants (Section 4.5) might lose valuable information about individual cognitive strategies. Exploring alternative approaches that preserve individual-level data could potentially lead to further improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide clarification on the process used to ensure no overlap between the training and testing data in Datasets 1 and 2, specifically regarding the source datasets (Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140)?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the specific parameters used for deriving the cognitive features (e.g., the choice of edge density thresholds in the saliency graph)? Were any sensitivity analyses performed?
3.  What steps were taken to control for potential biases in the eye-tracking data, such as variations in participants' reading habits or visual acuity?



**Additional Comments:**

The use of bold text throughout the paper is somewhat distracting and could be reduced for improved readability. Also, it might be useful to include a table summarizing the key statistics of the two datasets (e.g., size, sentiment distribution, average sentence length).
