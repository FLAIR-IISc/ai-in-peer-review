This paper presents an innovative approach to sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection by incorporating cognitive features derived from human annotators’ eye-movement patterns. The authors posit this as a novel contribution to the NLP community.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Feature Integration:** The primary contribution is the introduction of cognitive features, extracted from eye-tracking data, into a traditional sentiment analysis pipeline. This expands the feature space beyond typical linguistic characteristics.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The paper provides empirical evidence, demonstrating a performance improvement – up to 9.3% – on two datasets when utilizing the augmented feature set compared to systems relying solely on traditional features. This offers initial support for the hypothesis that cognitive features can enhance sentiment analysis accuracy.
3. **Exploration of Gaze Data:** The work explores several gaze-based features, including both basic statistical measures and more complex graph-based representations derived from saccade patterns, offering a detailed analysis of potentially informative cognitive signals.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Organized Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it relatively easy to understand the methodology and results. The background information on sentiment analysis and related challenges is comprehensive.
2. **Interesting and Potentially Impactful Idea:** The core concept of leveraging cognitive data for NLP tasks is genuinely intriguing and has the potential to inspire further research in areas beyond sentiment analysis, such as readability assessment or comprehension modeling.
3. **Rigorous Experimentation:** The authors employ a reasonable experimental setup, including 10-fold cross-validation and comparison against baseline systems. The inclusion of a held-out dataset specifically designed to contain complex linguistic constructs (irony, sarcasm) strengthens the validity of the findings.
4. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The paper provides a thorough description of the diverse feature set used, including both traditional sentiment features and the newly introduced cognitive features. This level of detail allows for reproducibility and facilitates future work building upon these findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Justification of Cognitive Features:** The rationale for *why* specific eye-movement patterns should correlate with sentiment detection remains somewhat superficial. While the authors suggest a link to cognitive processing, the underlying mechanisms are not deeply explored, and the connection feels largely empirical.  A clearer theoretical justification would strengthen the work.
2. **Marginal Improvement:**  The performance gains achieved by adding cognitive features (particularly on Dataset 2) are relatively small, particularly given the complexity of acquiring eye-tracking data. While statistically significant, the practical impact of a 1-2% improvement may be limited in some applications.  Further investigation into feature selection and optimization might yield more substantial gains.
3. **Feasibility Concerns & Application Scenario:** The discussion of the approach’s feasibility, particularly in Section 7, feels optimistic. The scenario presented involving mobile eye-tracking for e-commerce (Section 7.2) lacks concrete details and doesn’t adequately address the challenges of deploying eye-tracking technology in real-world, uncontrolled settings. The benefits of the approach within that scenario are not clearly demonstrated.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The error analysis section is brief and lacks specific examples of cases where the cognitive features were particularly helpful or detrimental. Providing a more nuanced understanding of the types of errors made by the system could offer valuable insights for future improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you elaborate on the cognitive theories underpinning the selection of specific eye-tracking features (e.g., fixation duration, saccade length)? What cognitive processes are these features intended to capture, and how do they relate to sentiment processing?
2.  The improvement in F-score is more pronounced on Dataset 1 than Dataset 2. Can you discuss potential reasons for this difference, and explore whether the characteristics of the datasets (e.g., annotation quality, data distribution) influence the effectiveness of the cognitive features?
3.  Regarding the feasibility discussion, can you provide a more detailed analysis of the practical challenges associated with acquiring and processing eye-tracking data from a large user base, including issues related to data privacy, hardware cost, and data noise?
