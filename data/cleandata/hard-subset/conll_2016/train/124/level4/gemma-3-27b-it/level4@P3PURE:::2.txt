This paper introduces eye-tracking features for sentiment analysis, framing them as a type of cognitive feature. The idea of using eye-tracking data as a proxy for cognitive load in sentiment analysis is interesting and potentially valuable.

The discussion of features and the comparison of different feature sets are clear and particularly helpful. I also appreciate that the feasibility of the approach is addressed in Section 7.

I wonder if the evaluation would benefit from using datasets that are not conflated across different domains, such as the movie review corpus and the tweet corpus. Separating the domains might improve the prediction accuracy for movie reviews (and tweets, respectively) by preventing domain confusion during training. Furthermore, it would facilitate a more straightforward interpretation of the results. The results in Table 2 appear relatively low when compared to state-of-the-art performance on the Pang and Lee dataset, but are more competitive when evaluated against results for Twitter data. 

In Section 3.3, could the authors confirm that there is no overlap between the training and testing data for datasets 1 and 2, even when the data originates from the same sources (e.g., Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140)?

Minor comment: the frequent use of bold text is somewhat distracting.
