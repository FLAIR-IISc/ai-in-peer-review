This paper explores the incorporation of cognitive features, derived from eye-tracking data, to enhance sentiment analysis performance. The core idea – leveraging eye-movement patterns as a proxy for cognitive processing during sentiment interpretation – is a novel and potentially valuable contribution. The authors convincingly argue for the limitations of traditional feature-based approaches in handling nuanced sentiment expression, and position their work as a step toward addressing these challenges.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the demonstration that augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data leads to measurable improvements in sentiment polarity detection, achieving up to a 9.3% F-score increase on the tested datasets. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive feature engineering process, encompassing sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting-related, cognitive, and reading difficulty features, and a thorough ablation study to assess their individual and combined impacts. Finally, the paper's discussion of the feasibility and potential applications of this approach, particularly given the increasing availability of mobile eye-tracking technology, is a valuable addition.

Strengths:
1. **Novelty of Approach:** The integration of eye-tracking data as cognitive features for sentiment analysis is a fresh perspective in the field. While eye-tracking has been used in related psycholinguistic studies, its application to directly improve sentiment classification accuracy is a significant step. The results support the hypothesis that readers’ cognitive processes, as reflected in their eye movements, provide valuable information for sentiment detection.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors have meticulously crafted a rich feature set, going beyond standard sentiment lexicon-based approaches. The inclusion of features related to sarcasm, thwarting, and reading difficulty demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the challenges inherent in sentiment analysis. The ablation studies effectively highlight the contributions of each feature category.
3. **Rigorous Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup, involving two datasets and multiple classifiers, provides a solid foundation for evaluating the proposed approach. The use of a held-out dataset, specifically designed to include complex constructs like irony and sarcasm, strengthens the validity of the findings. The statistical significance testing further supports the reported improvements.
4. **Practical Considerations:** Addressing the feasibility of the approach – particularly the increasing accessibility of mobile eye-trackers – demonstrates a pragmatic awareness of real-world implementation challenges. The discussion of potential applications in areas like e-commerce and online learning adds to the paper’s impact.

Weaknesses:
1. **Dataset Heterogeneity:** The combination of movie reviews and tweets within the datasets introduces potential domain-specific biases. While the authors acknowledge this, the impact on the reported performance gains is unclear. The different characteristics of these text types may obscure the true effectiveness of the cognitive features.
2. **Limited Generalizability:** The use of specific eye-tracking hardware (SR-Research Eyelink-1000 and Tobii T120) and the inherent variability in eye-tracking data across individuals could limit the generalizability of the findings. The performance gains observed might be specific to the characteristics of the participants and the recording environment.
3. **Feature Aggregation:** The averaging of eye-tracking parameters across participants during the graph-based feature extraction may lead to a loss of potentially valuable individual-level information. Exploring alternative aggregation methods or incorporating participant-specific features could further improve performance.
4. **Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The discussion of the baseline performance (Table 1) lacks sufficient context regarding the current state-of-the-art results on the Pang and Lee dataset. A more direct comparison to recent deep learning-based approaches would provide a clearer benchmark for assessing the contribution of the proposed method.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you elaborate on the potential impact of dataset heterogeneity (mixing movie reviews and tweets) on the observed performance gains? Did you explore training and evaluating models separately on each domain?
2. How sensitive are the results to the specific eye-tracking hardware used? Have you considered methods to mitigate the effects of individual variability in eye-tracking data?
3. Did you investigate alternative methods for aggregating eye-tracking data across participants, such as incorporating participant-specific features or using more sophisticated statistical modeling techniques?
4. How do the results compare to more recent, deep learning-based approaches on the Pang and Lee dataset, specifically in terms of F-score and other relevant metrics?
