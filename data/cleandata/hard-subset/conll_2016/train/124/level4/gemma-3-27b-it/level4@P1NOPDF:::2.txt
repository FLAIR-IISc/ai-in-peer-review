This paper introduces eye-tracking features for sentiment analysis, framing them as a type of cognitive feature. I find the idea of using eye-tracking data as a proxy for cognitive load in sentiment analysis to be an interesting and potentially valuable approach.

The discussion of features and the comparison of different feature sets are clear and particularly helpful. I also appreciate that the feasibility of the proposed approach is explicitly addressed in Section 7.

I wonder if the evaluation would be strengthened by using datasets that are less heterogeneous in domain. Specifically, separating the movie review corpus from the tweet corpus might be beneficial. Training and testing on distinct domains – for example, training on tweets and testing on movie reviews, or vice versa – could improve prediction accuracy for each domain. Furthermore, it would simplify the interpretation of the results. The reported results in Table 2 appear somewhat low when compared to state-of-the-art performance on the Pang and Lee dataset, but are more competitive when evaluated against results for Twitter data.

In Section 3.3, could the authors confirm that there is no overlap between the training and testing data for datasets 1 and 2, even though they originate from the same sources (such as Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140)? 

Minor comment: the frequent use of bold text is somewhat distracting, though this may be a matter of personal preference.
