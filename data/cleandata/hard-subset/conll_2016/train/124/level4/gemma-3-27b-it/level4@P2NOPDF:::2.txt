This paper explores the integration of eye-tracking data as cognitive features for sentiment analysis, a potentially valuable approach. The core idea – leveraging eye-tracking as a proxy for cognitive load during sentiment expression – is compelling and offers a novel perspective.

The presentation of features and the comparative analysis of different feature sets are particularly well-executed and informative. I also appreciate the inclusion of a feasibility discussion in Section 7, which demonstrates a realistic assessment of the methodology.

I suggest the authors consider the impact of dataset composition on the evaluation. The current combination of corpora, specifically movie reviews and tweets, may introduce confounding factors. Performance could potentially be improved by training and evaluating models on data from the same domain; for instance, a model trained on tweets might perform better when tested exclusively on tweets, and vice-versa. Furthermore, separating the datasets would facilitate a clearer interpretation of the results. While the accuracy reported in Table 2 appears relatively low when compared to established state-of-the-art results on the Pang and Lee dataset, it appears more competitive when assessed against results reported for Twitter sentiment analysis. Disentangling these comparisons would strengthen the paper's contribution.

I have a clarifying question regarding data contamination. In Section 3.3, can the authors confirm that there is no overlap between the training and testing sets for datasets 1 and 2, even considering that data originates from the same sources (e.g., Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140)? Explicitly addressing this point would bolster confidence in the experimental methodology.

Finally, a minor stylistic note: the frequent use of bold text occasionally feels distracting. A more judicious application of emphasis might improve readability.
