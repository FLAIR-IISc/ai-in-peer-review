This paper investigates the use of eye-tracking data, framed as cognitive features, to enhance sentiment analysis performance. The core idea – leveraging readers’ eye movements as a proxy for cognitive processing during sentiment interpretation – is a novel and potentially valuable contribution. The authors augment traditional sentiment analysis features with these cognitive features and demonstrate performance improvements on two datasets.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the exploration of eye-tracking data as a source of information for improving sentiment analysis, specifically for nuanced and complex text. While the individual components (sentiment analysis, eye-tracking) are well-established, their combination and the demonstrated performance gains represent a significant step forward. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive feature engineering and ablation study, clearly outlining the impact of different feature combinations, including the cognitive features. Finally, the inclusion of a feasibility discussion acknowledging the practical challenges and potential solutions is commendable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The integration of cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data into sentiment analysis is a novel approach. This moves beyond purely textual analysis and incorporates insights into the cognitive processes involved in sentiment understanding. The presented results support the hypothesis that readers' eye movements provide valuable information for handling linguistic nuances.
2. **Thorough Feature Engineering & Analysis:** The paper presents a detailed description of the feature set, categorizing features into sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, cognitive, and reading difficulty groups. The ablation studies (Table 2) provide a clear understanding of the contribution of each feature group, demonstrating the effectiveness of the combined approach. The chi-squared analysis (Table 3) further reinforces the importance of the gaze-based features.
3. **Practical Considerations:** The authors proactively address the feasibility concerns related to data acquisition (Section 7), discussing the increasing availability of mobile eye-trackers and potential application scenarios. This demonstrates a realistic understanding of the challenges and potential for real-world deployment.
4. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The use of two distinct datasets and a held-out test set provides a robust evaluation of the proposed method. The statistical significance testing (McNemar test) adds further confidence to the reported results. The observation of increased improvements for complex texts (Table 4) supports the claim that cognitive features are particularly helpful in handling nuanced sentiment.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dataset Heterogeneity:** The datasets (D1 & D2) combine data from different sources (movie reviews and tweets). This heterogeneity could potentially obscure the true performance gains and make it difficult to generalize the results. A more controlled evaluation using datasets from a single domain would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Baseline Comparison:** While the paper establishes a baseline using SVM, NB, and a rule-based classifier, a comparison to more recent state-of-the-art deep learning models specifically designed for sentiment analysis (e.g., transformer-based models) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the method’s effectiveness. The current comparison (Table 1) indicates relatively low F-scores even for the baseline systems, raising questions about the difficulty of the datasets or the baselines’ implementation.
3. **Data Independence:** Clarification is needed regarding the independence of the training and testing data in Section 3.3. Specifically, confirming whether there is any overlap between the snippets used for training the baseline models (Pang & Lee, Sentiment 140) and the testing data (Datasets 1 & 2) would be crucial to ensure a fair evaluation.
4. **Averaging of Gaze Data:** The method of averaging gaze data across participants to create graph-based features (Section 4.5) may result in the loss of individual reader-specific information. Exploring alternative approaches that capture inter-subject variability could potentially improve performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the criteria used to ensure the independence of the training and testing data, particularly given the use of publicly available datasets like Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140?
2. What were the primary reasons for combining movie reviews and tweets within the same datasets? How might the results differ if evaluated on domain-specific datasets?
3. Have you considered evaluating your approach against more recent state-of-the-art deep learning models for sentiment analysis? If so, what were the results? If not, why?
