- Core contribution: using readers’ eye-tracking (cognitive) features to improve sentiment analysis, especially for nuanced/complex text.  
- Secondary contributions: comprehensive feature engineering with ablation and chi-squared analysis showing each feature group’s impact.  
- Practical considerations: feasibility discussion on data acquisition and mobile eye-tracker availability for real-world use.  
- Evaluation rigor: tested on two datasets with a held-out test set and McNemar significance testing; larger gains observed for complex texts.  
- Novelty: integrates cognitive signals with text-based sentiment analysis, a novel direction beyond purely textual methods.  
- Strength in analysis: detailed categorization of features (sentiment, sarcasm/thwarting, cognitive, reading difficulty) and transparent ablation results.  
- Weakness — dataset heterogeneity: mixing movie reviews and tweets may obscure true performance gains and hinder generalization.  
- Weakness — baselines: lack of comparison to recent transformer-based or other state-of-the-art deep models limits assessment of effectiveness.  
- Weakness — data independence: needs clarification on potential overlap between training resources (e.g., Pang & Lee, Sentiment140) and test data.  
- Weakness — gaze averaging: averaging gaze data across participants may lose inter-subject variability and useful reader-specific signals.  
- Question: please clarify criteria ensuring independence of training and testing data given public datasets used.  
- Question: why combine movie reviews and tweets, and how would results differ on domain-specific datasets?  
- Question: have you compared against recent state-of-the-art deep learning sentiment models, and if so, what were the outcomes?  
- Additional comment: reduce the excessive use of bold text for improved readability.
