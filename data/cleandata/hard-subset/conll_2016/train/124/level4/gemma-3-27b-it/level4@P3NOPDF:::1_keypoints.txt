- Novel idea: uses annotator eye-tracking-derived cognitive features for sentiment and sarcasm detection, claimed as first work of its kind.  
- Clear and readable: paper is well-written and conveys methodology and results effectively.  
- High potential impact: approach is innovative and could inspire extensions to other NLP tasks involving human cognition.  
- Weak motivation: insufficient theoretical justification linking specific eye-movement patterns to sentiment polarity.  
- Marginal gains: performance improvements from cognitive features appear limited, questioning practical benefit versus added complexity.  
- Statistical concerns: need for more rigorous significance testing of observed improvements.  
- Feasibility doubts: Section 7’s example does not convincingly show real-world utility or clear advantages over traditional methods.  
- Tenuous linkage: connection between cognitive effort (eye movements) and sentiment expression is underdeveloped.  
- Overall assessment: valuable preliminary work with novelty and good presentation but limited impact due to weak theory, small gains, and unconvincing feasibility.  
- Recommendation — stronger theoretical grounding: integrate cognitive psychology literature to justify feature use.  
- Recommendation — statistical rigor: perform more thorough significance analyses.  
- Recommendation — better demonstrations: provide more persuasive, real-world examples where cognitive features clearly help.  
- Recommendation — error analysis: include detailed cases showing where cognitive features helped or failed to improve classification.
