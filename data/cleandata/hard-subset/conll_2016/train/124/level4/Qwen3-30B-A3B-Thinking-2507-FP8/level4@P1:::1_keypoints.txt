- Proposes using cognitive features from annotators' eye-movement patterns for sentiment analysis, claiming up to 3.7% and 9.3% F-score improvements and better handling of sarcasm/irony.  
- Contribution: introduces eye-tracking–derived cognitive features as a novel feature set for sentiment analysis.  
- Contribution: provides empirical evidence that cognitive features improve performance on complex sentiment constructs.  
- Contribution: offers detailed feature significance analysis showing the specific value of cognitive features.  
- Strength: genuinely novel interdisciplinary approach integrating cognitive science with NLP.  
- Strength: thorough experimental design with multiple classifiers and two datasets comparing against traditional features.  
- Strength: feature significance (Table 3) indicates cognitive features are top contributors for sarcasm and irony detection.  
- Weakness: weak motivation/theoretical justification linking eye movements specifically to sentiment rather than general text complexity.  
- Weakness: marginal gains over strong baselines (Sn+Sr: 0.6% and 1.5%) undermine practical value given the cost of collecting eye-tracking data.  
- Weakness: feasibility/application discussion (mobile eye-trackers for reviews) is unconvincing and doesn't show clear added value over text features.  
- Question: please provide more evidence for the cognitive mechanism tying eye-movement patterns to sentiment polarity vs text complexity.  
- Question: how do you justify the added complexity and data requirements of eye-tracking given minimal improvements over Sn+Sr?  
- Question: how do you explain held-out results where improvement on simple constructs (2.1%) is not far from complex constructs (6.1%), and what does that imply about the specific utility of cognitive features?
