

This paper proposes an innovative approach to sentiment analysis by incorporating eye-tracking features as proxies for cognitive load, presenting a promising avenue for integrating cognitive science insights into NLP. The core idea is well-motivated and merits attention, particularly given the growing interest in understanding user engagement through physiological signals. The discussion of feature engineering in Section 3 and the comparative analysis of feature sets in Section 4 are exceptionally clear and well-structured, providing valuable guidance for future work in this space. Additionally, the feasibility assessment in Section 7 thoughtfully addresses practical implementation challenges, strengthening the paper’s contribution.

The primary concern revolves around the evaluation methodology, specifically the conflation of distinct domains (movie reviews and tweets) in the training and test sets. This design choice obscures the model’s true performance and interpretability. For instance, the results in Table 2 appear modest when compared to state-of-the-art on the Pang & Lee movie review corpus—yet this is largely attributable to the model being trained on Twitter data (Sentiment 140) and tested on movie reviews, a cross-domain scenario where performance naturally degrades. Conversely, the results for Twitter data appear more competitive, but this comparison is misleading without domain-specific evaluation. To enhance validity, the authors should re-evaluate using strict domain separation: training and testing exclusively on movie reviews (Pang & Lee) *and* separately on tweets (Sentiment 140). This would clarify whether the approach genuinely improves sentiment analysis within a single domain or merely capitalizes on cross-domain patterns. The current setup risks overestimating the model’s utility for specific applications, as practitioners would typically deploy it within a single domain.

A related methodological point concerns data overlap in Datasets 1 and 2 (Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140). Section 3.3 states that "the training and testing data contain no overlapping snippets," but it is unclear whether this holds *across* datasets. If, for example, the Pang & Lee training set includes snippets also present in Sentiment 140 (even if from different sources), this would constitute data leakage, artificially inflating results. The authors should explicitly confirm that *all* data across datasets is disjoint, including source-level uniqueness. This clarification is critical for reproducibility and credibility.

Minor stylistic improvements would further elevate the paper. The excessive use of bold formatting in figures and tables (e.g., Table 2) is distracting and detracts from the data’s clarity; the authors should adhere to standard academic presentation norms. Additionally, the phrasing in Section 3.3 regarding dataset composition could be tightened for precision—e.g., "The training and test sets for each dataset are entirely disjoint" would eliminate ambiguity.

In summary, the paper advances sentiment analysis through a novel cognitive feature framework with strong theoretical grounding. The feature discussion and feasibility analysis are particularly compelling. However, the evaluation’s domain mixing significantly undermines the reported results’ interpretability and applicability. Addressing this—through domain-separated experiments and explicit data disjointness verification—would transform a promising idea into a robust, actionable contribution. With these revisions, the work would make a significant impact at CONLL.
