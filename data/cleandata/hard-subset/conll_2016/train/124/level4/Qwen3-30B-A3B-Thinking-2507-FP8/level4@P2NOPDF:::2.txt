

The paper proposes leveraging eye-tracking features as cognitive proxies for sentiment analysis, presenting an intriguing approach that bridges cognitive science and NLP. The core idea is compelling, and the discussion of feature engineering and comparative analysis in Section 3 is well-structured and clearly articulated, offering valuable insights for the community. Additionally, Section 7’s treatment of practical feasibility strengthens the paper’s contribution by addressing real-world applicability.

A significant concern arises from the evaluation methodology, which conflates distinct domains (movie reviews and tweets) in the training and testing data. This mixing obscures performance interpretation and likely inflates results for the Pang & Lee dataset while underrepresenting the model’s efficacy on Twitter data. For instance, Table 2’s results for Pang & Lee appear modest compared to state-of-the-art benchmarks, yet they align more favorably with Twitter-specific literature when domains are separated. To enhance validity, the authors should re-evaluate using strictly domain-separated splits (e.g., train on movie reviews, test on tweets, and vice versa). This would clarify whether the model generalizes across domains or merely exploits domain-specific patterns in the mixed data, providing a clearer assessment of its true utility.

Regarding data partitioning, the review notes that Section 3.3 states datasets 1 and 2 contain no overlapping text snippets. However, the authors should explicitly confirm whether this holds true even when datasets derive from the same sources (e.g., Pang & Lee and Sentiment140). If snippets overlap despite claims of disjointness, this could introduce data leakage, invalidating the reported performance. Clarifying this point is critical for reproducibility.

Minor issues include inconsistent formatting: excessive use of bold text (e.g., in Table 1 and Section 3.2) distracts from the content and violates academic style conventions. A streamlined presentation without non-essential formatting would improve readability.

Overall, the paper makes a meaningful contribution by exploring cognitive features in sentiment analysis, with strong methodological foundations. Addressing the domain-mixing concern and data partitioning details would substantially strengthen the evaluation. The proposed approach merits attention, particularly for tasks where cognitive load correlates with sentiment expression, but the current results require careful interpretation due to the evaluation design. With these revisions, the work would provide a clearer benchmark for future research on cognitive features in NLP.
