

Summary: This paper introduces cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns to enhance sentiment analysis systems, particularly for handling complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. The primary contribution is the novel integration of gaze-based features with traditional sentiment analysis features, which significantly improves performance on datasets containing nuanced sentiment expressions. The secondary contribution is the rigorous feature significance analysis demonstrating that cognitive features are especially effective for complex sentiment constructs. The tertiary contribution is the practical feasibility analysis of implementing this approach through mobile eye-tracking technology.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides an exceptionally clear and well-justified explanation of the cognitive features and their theoretical relationship to sentiment analysis challenges. The feature design is well-motivated by eye-tracking literature and linguistic challenges, making the approach conceptually sound.

2. The held-out dataset experiment specifically targeting complex constructs (irony/sarcasm) is particularly compelling. It demonstrates a 6.1% F-score improvement when cognitive features are added to complex texts versus only 2.1% for simpler texts, providing strong evidence that these features address the authors' core hypothesis about handling linguistic nuances.

3. The feasibility analysis in Section 7 is outstanding. The authors thoughtfully address practical concerns about implementation, including current mobile eye-tracking technology, potential application scenarios (e-commerce, online learning), and user consent considerations. This elevates the paper from theoretical to practically relevant.

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation strategy conflates different text domains (movie reviews and tweets) in both the training data and test datasets, which obscures domain-specific performance and complicates interpretation of results. The reported F-scores (Table 2) appear low compared to standard sentiment analysis benchmarks, suggesting the datasets may not be well-suited for conventional approaches. A clearer evaluation would separate domains and use domain-specific training/test splits.

2. The paper does not clarify whether there's any overlap between the training data (from Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140 datasets) and the test data (Dataset 1 and Dataset 2). Given the small size of the test datasets (994 and 1059 snippets), even minor overlap could significantly inflate results. This critical information is missing from Section 3.3.

3. The baseline results (Table 1) show limited performance (F-scores around 60-70%) for standard sentiment analysis systems on these datasets, which suggests the datasets are particularly challenging. However, the paper does not adequately contextualize these results against other recent approaches that might handle these challenges better. Without this context, the claimed improvements of 3.7% and 9.3% are difficult to assess as meaningful advances.

Questions to Authors:
Could you clarify whether there is any overlap between the training data (Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140) and the test data (Dataset 1 and Dataset 2)? Given the small size of the test datasets, this is critical for evaluating the significance of the reported results.
