- Introduces cognitive features from human eye-movement patterns as a novel input for sentiment analysis.
- Claims cognitive features improve performance on complex sentiment constructs (6.1% F-score gain on held-out data).
- Provides detailed engineering and analysis of basic and complex gaze-derived features.

- Cognitive features show substantial gains specifically for sarcasm/irony (6.1% vs 2.1% for simple constructs).
- Thorough and well-motivated feature engineering, including fixation, saccade, regression metrics and gaze-saliency graphs.
- Focuses on the hardest sentiment cases (sarcasm/irony), addressing a known weakness of existing methods.

- Weak theoretical justification tying specific eye-movement features to sentiment polarity rather than general reading difficulty.
- Marginal additional gains (0.6% and 1.5%) when cognitive features are added to strong existing feature sets, making claimed max improvements misleading.
- Feasibility argument for large-scale/mobile eye-tracking is speculative and practical data-collection challenges are unaddressed.
- Fails to clearly separate features that capture general text complexity from those that capture sentiment-specific complexity.

- How do you distinguish features reflecting reading difficulty from those specifically indicating sentiment complexity?
- What is the theoretical cognitive mechanism linking eye movements to sentiment understanding?
- Given small gains over Sn+Sr, why implement eye-tracking in practice versus using simpler, comparable features?
