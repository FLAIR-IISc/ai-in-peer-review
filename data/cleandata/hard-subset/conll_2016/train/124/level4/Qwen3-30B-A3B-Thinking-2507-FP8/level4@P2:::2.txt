

Summary: This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of human readers. The authors argue these cognitive features help sentiment analyzers better handle nuanced sentiment expressions, including sarcasm and implicit sentiment. The main contributions I identify are: (1) the novel integration of eye-tracking data as cognitive features for sentiment analysis, which represents a significant departure from conventional approaches; (2) empirical demonstration that these cognitive features improve performance on complex sentiment constructs, with up to 9.3% F-score improvement over traditional feature sets; and (3) a comprehensive feasibility analysis addressing practical implementation challenges for real-world applications.

Strengths: 
1. The paper makes a compelling case for the cognitive perspective in sentiment analysis. The authors effectively argue that eye-movement patterns correlate with cognitive processing of sentiment nuances, providing a theoretically grounded approach to address lexical, syntactic, semantic, and pragmatic challenges in sentiment analysis. The feature engineering is particularly strong, with well-motivated cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data that directly address the paper's research question.

2. The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-structured. The authors compare multiple feature combinations across two distinct datasets, with appropriate statistical validation (McNemar test) for the significance of results. The held-out dataset experiment specifically testing complex constructs (irony/sarcasm) demonstrates the cognitive features' particular value for nuanced sentiment analysis, showing 6.1% relative improvement over traditional features.

3. The feasibility analysis (Section 7) is a significant strength. The authors thoughtfully address practical implementation concerns, discussing mobile eye-tracking technology, potential application scenarios (e-commerce, online learning), and user consent issues. This elevates the work beyond theoretical contribution to a potentially actionable approach for real-world sentiment analysis systems.

Weaknesses:
1. The dataset composition creates ambiguity in results interpretation. The authors combine movie reviews and tweets in both training and testing data, which conflates domains and makes it difficult to assess whether improvements stem from the cognitive features or simply from domain adaptation. This is particularly problematic since the results (Table 2) appear relatively low compared to state-of-the-art for standard sentiment analysis datasets (e.g., Pang & Lee), but would be more impressive if evaluated specifically on Twitter data.

2. The paper lacks meaningful comparison with deep learning approaches that have achieved superior performance in sentiment analysis. The authors acknowledge these approaches (Section 2) but exclude them from their experiments, which weakens the evaluation of their feature-based approach. Given that deep learning methods can automatically learn complex features, the paper would be stronger with a comparison to these state-of-the-art techniques.

3. The paper doesn't clearly establish whether the eye-tracking data was collected specifically for this sentiment analysis task or repurposed from prior work. If the data was collected for another purpose, the authors should clarify how they validated that the eye-movement patterns are relevant to sentiment processing rather than other cognitive processes. This is particularly important given the claim that cognitive features "empower sentiment analyzers to handle complex constructs."

Questions to Authors:
1. In Section 3.3, the paper states that the training data for the baseline systems comes from Pang & Lee (2004) and Sentiment 140, while Datasets 1 and 2 are separate. Can you confirm there is no overlap between the training data and the test data (Datasets 1 and 2)? This is critical for proper evaluation.

2. The results in Table 2 show modest performance improvements (max 9.3% F-score) compared to state-of-the-art sentiment analysis systems. Given that the authors claim their approach is "the first of its kind," could they clarify why the absolute performance is relatively low compared to other sentiment analysis approaches, particularly those using deep learning?

3. The paper combines movie reviews and tweets in Datasets 1 and 2. Would the results change if the authors evaluated separately on movie reviews (Dataset 1) and tweets (Dataset 2) without mixing domains? This would help clarify whether the improvements are specific to certain text types or generalizable across domains.
