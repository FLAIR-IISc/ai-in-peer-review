- Novel interdisciplinary idea: use eye-tracking-derived cognitive features for sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection.  
- Writing is clear and the paper is accessible.  
- Conceptual novelty: systematically extract and integrate eye-movement features (fixation duration, saccade count) into models.  
- Bridges cognitive psychology and NLP, offering a fresh perspective that could inspire related work.  
- Motivation is underdeveloped; weak theoretical/empirical link between eye movements and sentiment/sarcasm.  
- Specific ambiguity: unclear why longer fixations on negative words would indicate sarcasm rather than straightforward negativity.  
- Empirical gains are marginal (typically under 1% F1), calling into question practical utility.  
- Lack of rigorous statistical validation and unclear robustness across diverse datasets.  
- Feasibility claims (real-time/social-media deployment) are unconvincing given hardware, latency, and scalability barriers.  
- High resource burden: eye-tracking requires specialized hardware, controlled settings, and extra annotation effort.  
- Recommended fixes: explicitly link cognitive features to sentiment/sarcasm mechanisms, provide statistical significance/effect-size analyses, and address scalability with concrete solutions or benchmarks.  
- Overall: intellectually stimulating and worthwhile to read, but not yet a compelling or actionable advancement for NLP.
