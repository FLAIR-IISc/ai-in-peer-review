

This paper introduces eye-tracking features as cognitive proxies for sentiment analysis, presenting an interesting approach. The authors' discussion of feature design and comparative analysis of feature sets is clear and highly valuable. Additionally, the exploration of practical feasibility in Section 7 is a strong point.

A significant methodological concern arises from the evaluation strategy: the datasets conflate distinct domains (movie reviews and tweets), which complicates interpretation and potentially inflates results. Separating these domains would substantially strengthen the evaluation. Specifically, training and testing on distinct domains (e.g., training only on movie reviews, testing only on tweets, and vice versa) would provide clearer insights into model performance per domain. This adjustment would also resolve the apparent discrepancy in Table 2: results for the Pang and Lee dataset currently appear modest compared to state-of-the-art sentiment analysis benchmarks, but become significantly more competitive when benchmarked against Twitter-specific results (as seen in the Sentiment 140 results). The current conflation obscures this important nuance.

Furthermore, it is critical to confirm whether Dataset 1 (Pang and Lee) and Dataset 2 (Sentiment 140) exhibit no overlapping text snippets between training and test splits, even though they originate from similar sources. The absence of such leakage is essential for a valid evaluation, as any overlap would constitute data contamination and artificially boost performance.

Minor stylistic note: Some instances of excessive bold text appear distracting and are unnecessary for clarity.
