

Summary: This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of readers. The authors claim that these cognitive features improve sentiment analysis performance, particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm and irony, by up to 9.3% in F-score on two datasets.

Main contributions: The primary contribution is the feature significance analysis demonstrating cognitive features are particularly effective for complex sentiment constructs (irony/sarcasm), showing a 6.1% improvement for complex texts versus 2.1% for simple texts. The secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration of cognitive features' effectiveness through comprehensive feature engineering, including both basic gaze features and complex gaze features derived from saliency graphs. The tertiary contribution is the feasibility analysis addressing practical implementation concerns of eye-tracking in real-world applications.

Strengths: The feature significance analysis for complex constructs is exceptionally well-executed and compelling, providing strong evidence that cognitive features specifically benefit sentiment analysis of nuanced texts. The thorough exploration of cognitive features from eye-tracking data, including both basic gaze features and complex gaze features derived from saliency graphs, demonstrates a comprehensive approach to feature engineering that is well-justified. The feasibility discussion in Section 7 is particularly strong, addressing practical implementation concerns through concrete examples of mobile eye-tracking applications and user consent considerations.

Weaknesses: The evaluation datasets conflate different text domains (movie reviews and tweets), making it difficult to interpret results and compare with standard sentiment analysis benchmarks. The reported performance improvements (up to 9.3%) are modest compared to state-of-the-art results for sentiment analysis, especially given the complexity of the proposed approach. The paper does not clearly address whether the training data (from Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140) overlaps with the evaluation datasets (Dataset 1 and 2), which affects the validity of the results.

Questions to Authors: Could you clarify whether there is any overlap between the training data (from Pang & Lee and Sentiment 140) and the evaluation datasets (Dataset 1 and 2)? How would the results change if the evaluation was conducted separately on movie reviews and tweets, rather than using a mixed dataset?
