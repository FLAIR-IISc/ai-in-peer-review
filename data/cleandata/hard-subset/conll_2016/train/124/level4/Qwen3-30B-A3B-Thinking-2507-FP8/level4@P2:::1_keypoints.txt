- Summary: Proposes augmenting traditional sentiment features with cognitive features from eye-tracking, claiming 3.7% and 9.3% gains and better handling of irony/sarcasm.
- Contribution 1: Introduces eye-tracking–derived cognitive features as a novel feature set for sentiment analysis.
- Contribution 2: Shows cognitive features yield a 6.1% improvement for complex constructs (irony/sarcasm).
- Contribution 3: Provides feature-significance analysis showing many top-ranked features are gaze-based.
- Strength 1: Presents a well-structured feature set, including novel gaze-saliency graph features.
- Strength 2: Robust evaluation with multiple classifiers, two datasets, and a held-out complex-sentiment set showing statistically significant gains for complex cases.
- Strength 3: Feature-rank analysis strongly supports the value of cognitive features (10/20 and 7/20 gaze-based top features).
- Weakness 1: Weak theoretical motivation linking eye-movements specifically to sentiment polarity rather than reading difficulty.
- Weakness 2: Marginal additional gains (0.6% and 1.5%) when adding cognitive features to the enhanced feature set, reducing practical impact versus deep learning.
- Weakness 3: Unconvincing feasibility discussion for large-scale eye-tracking deployment, ignoring privacy, consent, and cost issues.
- Weakness 4: Insufficient treatment of eye-tracking data limitations, device variability, and annotator pattern/bias handling.
- Question 1: Provide a clearer theoretical link between eye-movement patterns and sentiment polarity detection.
- Question 2: How does this approach compare to or complement recent deep learning methods given the marginal gains?
- Question 3: How will practical challenges of collecting eye-tracking data at scale and addressing privacy/consent be addressed?
- Overall: Novel and promising for nuanced sentiment cases but weakened by limited theoretical grounding, marginal overall improvements, and feasibility/data-quality concerns.
