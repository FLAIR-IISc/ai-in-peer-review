- Novel integration of eye-tracking–derived cognitive features into sentiment analysis to better handle nuanced expressions (e.g., sarcasm, implicit sentiment).
- Empirical demonstration that cognitive features improve performance on complex constructs, with up to 9.3% F-score gain and 6.1% on irony/sarcasm held-out tests.
- Comprehensive feasibility analysis addressing practical implementation (mobile eye-tracking, application scenarios, user consent).
- Strong theoretical grounding: eye-movement patterns correlate with cognitive processing of sentiment nuances and motivate the cognitive feature engineering.
- Thorough experimental evaluation: multiple feature combinations across two datasets with appropriate statistical validation (McNemar test).
- Held-out dataset experiment highlights cognitive features’ particular value for nuanced sentiment analysis.
- Dataset composition ambiguity: mixing movie reviews and tweets may conflate domains and obscure whether gains come from cognitive features or domain effects.
- No meaningful comparison with deep learning approaches, weakening assessment against state-of-the-art methods that learn complex features automatically.
- Unclear whether eye-tracking data were collected for this sentiment task or repurposed, and how relevance to sentiment processing was validated.
- Question: Confirm there is no overlap between Pang & Lee / Sentiment140 training data and Datasets 1 and 2 test data.
- Question: Clarify why absolute performance is relatively low compared to deep learning methods despite the novelty of the approach.
- Question: Would evaluating movie reviews and tweets separately (no domain mixing) change results and clarify generalizability?
