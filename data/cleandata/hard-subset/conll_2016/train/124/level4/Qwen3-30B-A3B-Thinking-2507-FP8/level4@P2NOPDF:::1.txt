

This paper introduces a novel approach to sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection by incorporating cognitive features derived from human annotators' eye-movement patterns. The authors position this as the first effort to integrate such features into NLP, proposing a unique methodology where eye-tracking data during annotation generates new linguistic features.  

**Strengths:**  
1. The manuscript is well-structured and clearly written, facilitating straightforward comprehension of the methodology and results.  
2. The core idea—leveraging cognitive processes through eye-tracking—presents a compelling interdisciplinary angle that could inspire innovative work in other NLP domains.  

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Insufficient Justification for Cognitive Features:** The paper fails to convincingly establish *why* eye-movement patterns correlate with sentiment polarity. While the authors suggest these features might reflect reading ease, they do not articulate how gaze patterns specifically distinguish sentiment (e.g., positive vs. negative) or sarcasm. Without a clear theoretical link between cognitive processes and the target task, the relevance of these features remains unclear.  
2. **Marginal Performance Gains:** The inclusion of cognitive features (Sn+Sr+Gz) yields minimal improvement over the baseline (Sn+Sr). The reported metrics (e.g., F1-score increases of ≤0.5%) do not justify the added complexity of eye-tracking data collection, especially given the resource-intensive nature of such annotation. This weakens the practical contribution of the proposed approach.  
3. **Unpersuasive Feasibility Argument:** Section 7’s discussion on real-world applicability is unconvincing. The example in Section 7.2 (e.g., applying the method to real-time social media analysis) fails to demonstrate tangible utility. The scenario assumes seamless integration of eye-tracking hardware and processing, neglecting critical barriers like user discomfort, scalability, and contextual noise in uncontrolled environments. The feasibility claim thus lacks empirical or practical grounding.  

**Recommendation:**  
While the concept is intriguing, the current work lacks sufficient justification for the proposed features, demonstrates negligible gains, and overstates practical viability. The authors should: (a) rigorously justify the cognitive-sentiment link through task-specific hypotheses; (b) compare against stronger baselines (e.g., including state-of-the-art linguistic features); and (c) provide concrete, realistic use cases that address scalability and user constraints. Without these revisions, the paper’s contribution remains speculative rather than substantive. The novelty is intriguing but not yet sufficiently validated for publication.
