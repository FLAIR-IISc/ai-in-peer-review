

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes augmenting traditional sentiment analysis features with cognitive features derived from eye-movement patterns of readers. The authors' primary contribution is demonstrating that gaze-based cognitive features significantly improve sentiment classification, particularly for complex constructs like sarcasm and irony. The secondary contribution is providing a systematic feature significance analysis showing cognitive features' effectiveness for nuanced sentiment. The tertiary contribution is addressing the feasibility of deploying this approach through mobile eye-tracking technology.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel Feature Set with Strong Empirical Validation**: The paper introduces a well-structured set of cognitive features derived from eye-tracking data, a first for sentiment analysis. The authors rigorously evaluate these features across multiple classifiers and datasets, showing statistically significant improvements (up to 9.3% F-score) when cognitive features are added to traditional sentiment and sarcasm features. The feature significance analysis (Table 3) is particularly compelling, revealing gaze-based features as top-ranked predictors for complex sentiment.

2. **Clear Demonstration of Domain-Specific Utility**: The paper effectively demonstrates that cognitive features provide greater benefit for complex sentiment constructs (e.g., irony/sarcasm) than for straightforward sentiment. The held-out dataset experiment (Table 4) shows a 6.1% F-score improvement for irony-containing texts versus only 2.1% for simpler texts, directly supporting their hypothesis about cognitive features' value for nuanced sentiment.

3. **Practical Feasibility Discussion**: Section 7 provides a thoughtful analysis of real-world implementation challenges and opportunities, particularly regarding mobile eye-tracking technology. The discussion of Cogisen's mobile eye-tracking patent and potential applications in e-commerce and online reviews makes the approach seem less theoretical and more practically viable than many NLP proposals.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Dataset Conflation and Evaluation Concerns**: The evaluation conflates two distinct domains (movie reviews and tweets) in both training and testing data, which undermines the reported performance gains. The authors use Pang and Lee movie reviews for training but test on mixed-domain datasets (Dataset 1: reviews + tweets; Dataset 2: movie reviews + tweets). This violates standard evaluation practices, as results would be artificially inflated due to domain overlap. The baseline results in Table 1 (F-scores around 50-70%) seem low for standard sentiment analysis tasks, suggesting the evaluation design may be problematic. The authors should re-evaluate on strictly domain-separated data.

2. **Lack of Comparison with Deep Learning Approaches**: The paper explicitly excludes deep learning approaches (e.g., CNNs, RNNs) as "beyond the scope," despite their dominance in modern sentiment analysis. This omission is problematic because the authors claim their feature-based approach outperforms "state-of-the-art" systems, yet don't compare against the most effective current methods. For instance, Maas et al. (2011) achieved over 80% accuracy on the same Pang and Lee dataset with a CNN, which would make the reported 74.3% F-score (Table 1) appear quite low.

3. **Ambiguous Feature Engineering Justification**: The paper introduces numerous gaze features (e.g., "Largest Regression Position," "Edge Density of the saliency gaze graph") without sufficient theoretical grounding or clear explanation of why these particular metrics correlate with sentiment complexity. For example, the rationale for "Largest Regression Position" (Section 4.4) is tenuous—why would regression position specifically indicate sentiment complexity rather than general reading difficulty? More detailed justification would strengthen the contribution.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How would the results change if the training and test datasets were strictly domain-separated (e.g., train on movie reviews, test on tweets only, and vice versa)? Given the domain conflation in current evaluation, the reported gains may not be as significant as claimed.

2. Why were deep learning approaches excluded from comparison, given their current prominence in sentiment analysis? Would the cognitive features be equally effective when combined with deep learning architectures, or would they only benefit traditional feature-based systems?
