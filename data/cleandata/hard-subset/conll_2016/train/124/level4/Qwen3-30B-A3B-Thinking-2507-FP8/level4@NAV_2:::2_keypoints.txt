- Summary: Proposes augmenting standard sentiment features with cognitive (eye‑movement) features, claiming up to 9.3% F‑score gains on two datasets, especially for sarcasm/irony.  
- Primary contribution: Feature significance analysis showing cognitive features yield 6.1% improvement for complex texts vs 2.1% for simple texts.  
- Secondary contribution: Empirical demonstration via comprehensive feature engineering, including basic gaze features and saliency‑graph–derived complex gaze features.  
- Tertiary contribution: Feasibility analysis addressing practical eye‑tracking deployment concerns.  
- Strength: Compelling, well‑executed analysis that cognitive features help with nuanced sentiment (sarcasm/irony).  
- Strength: Thorough exploration and justification of a wide range of cognitive features.  
- Strength: Strong practicality discussion with mobile eye‑tracking examples and consent considerations.  
- Weakness: Evaluation mixes domains (movie reviews and tweets), complicating interpretation and benchmark comparisons.  
- Weakness: Reported improvements (≤9.3%) are modest relative to state‑of‑the‑art given method complexity.  
- Weakness: Unclear whether training data overlaps with evaluation sets, which could invalidate results.  
- Question: Please clarify any overlap between Pang & Lee/Sentiment140 training data and Datasets 1 and 2.  
- Question: How would results change if evaluated separately on movie reviews and tweets instead of a mixed dataset?
