- Summary: Proposes gaze-derived cognitive features integrated with traditional features to improve sentiment analysis, especially for sarcasm/irony; includes feature-significance and mobile eye-tracking feasibility analyses.

- Strength 1: Clear, well-justified cognitive feature design grounded in eye-tracking literature and linguistic motivation.

- Strength 2: Held-out experiment on ironic/sarcastic texts shows a 6.1% F-score gain with cognitive features (vs 2.1% for simpler texts).

- Strength 3: Thorough feasibility analysis addressing mobile eye-tracking technology, application scenarios, and user consent.

- Weakness 1: Training and test data mix domains (movie reviews and tweets), obscuring domain-specific performance and complicating interpretation; reported F-scores seem low versus benchmarks.

- Weakness 2: Unclear whether training sets (Pang & Lee, Sentiment140) overlap with test sets (Dataset 1, Dataset 2); small test sizes could inflate results and Section 3.3 omits this.

- Weakness 3: Baselines show modest F-scores (~60–70%) and the paper lacks comparison to recent approaches, making the reported 3.7% and 9.3% improvements hard to contextualize.

- Question: Please clarify if there is any overlap between the training data (Pang & Lee, Sentiment140) and the test data (Dataset 1 and Dataset 2).
