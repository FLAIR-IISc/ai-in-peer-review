

This paper proposes a novel application of eye-tracking-derived cognitive features for sentiment analysis and sarcasm detection, marking a potentially significant interdisciplinary contribution. The core idea—leveraging human annotators' eye-movement patterns as features—is intriguing and represents a genuine attempt to integrate cognitive science insights into NLP. The writing is clear and accessible, and the work’s novelty could inspire similar approaches in other NLP tasks, making it a worthwhile read despite its limitations.

The primary strength lies in the paper’s clear exposition and the conceptual novelty of its feature set. The authors successfully demonstrate that eye-tracking data (e.g., fixation duration, saccade count) can be systematically extracted and integrated into sentiment models, a direction that has not been explored in NLP to date. This bridges a gap between cognitive psychology and computational linguistics, offering a fresh perspective on how human processing might inform machine learning.

However, three critical weaknesses significantly undermine the paper’s impact. First, the motivation for using cognitive features remains underdeveloped. While the authors posit that eye movements reflect "cognitive load" or "reading ease," they fail to establish a concrete theoretical or empirical link between these patterns and *sentiment polarity* or *sarcasm detection*. For instance, why would a longer fixation on a negative word (e.g., "hate") indicate sarcasm rather than straightforward negativity? Without such justification, the feature’s relevance to the task appears speculative rather than grounded.

Second, the empirical gains are modest and raise questions about practical utility. The model incorporating cognitive features (Sn + Sr + Gz) shows only marginal improvements over the baseline (Sn + Sr)—typically under 1% in F1 scores across datasets. Given the complexity of collecting and processing eye-tracking data (which requires specialized hardware, controlled environments, and additional annotation effort), such minimal gains do not convincingly justify the added resource burden. The paper does not sufficiently address whether these improvements are statistically significant or robust across diverse datasets.

Third, the feasibility argument in Section 7 is unconvincing. Section 7.2 claims the approach could be applied in real-time sentiment analysis for social media, citing a hypothetical example where eye-tracking data is streamed alongside text. However, this overlooks critical practical barriers: eye-tracking hardware is impractical for large-scale, asynchronous platforms like Twitter; the latency introduced by data collection would negate real-time benefits; and the model’s marginal gains (as noted above) do not offset these challenges. The example fails to demonstrate a viable path to deployment, leaving the claimed feasibility unsupported.

In summary, while the paper’s core idea is intellectually stimulating and well-presented, its contributions are constrained by weak theoretical grounding, negligible empirical impact, and an unsubstantiated feasibility claim. To strengthen the work, the authors should: (1) explicitly link cognitive features to sentiment/sarcasm mechanisms (e.g., via cognitive load theory or attentional cues), (2) provide rigorous statistical validation of gains (noting significance and effect size), and (3) address scalability challenges in feasibility discussions with concrete solutions or benchmarks. As it stands, the work offers an interesting starting point but does not yet deliver a compelling or actionable advancement for the NLP community.
