This paper revisits coreference resolution in the specific context of Wikipedia articles, focusing on identifying all mentions of the main concept described in an article. The authors argue that existing coreference systems, trained primarily on news data, perform poorly on Wikipedia, and propose a classifier specifically tailored for this task. The primary contribution is a feature-rich binary classifier that significantly outperforms baseline coreference systems when identifying mentions of the main concept. A secondary contribution is the creation of a dataset (WCR) for evaluating systems on this task, and demonstrating improvements when integrating the classifier into a rule-based coreference pipeline.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Task Focus:** The paper addresses a relatively underexplored problem – adapting coreference resolution to the specific characteristics of Wikipedia. While coreference in Wikipedia has been touched upon before (cited in the paper), a dedicated evaluation and approach tailored to identifying mentions of the main concept is a valuable contribution, especially given Wikipedia's prevalence as a knowledge source.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently demonstrate a significant improvement over baseline coreference systems (Dcoref and Scoref) on the WCR dataset. The reported F1 score gains (up to 15 points) are substantial and indicate a meaningful advance in performance for this specific task. The ablation study, detailing the impact of different feature families, provides a clear understanding of what drives the performance improvements.
3. **Well-Designed Feature Set:** The authors demonstrate a thoughtful approach to feature engineering, leveraging Wikipedia’s markup (links, redirects) and external knowledge bases (Freebase) effectively. The combination of features, particularly the inclusion of MC attributes and features inspired by related work, appears to be well-motivated and impactful.
4. **Practical Demonstration of Integration:**  The integration of the classifier into the Dcoref system and the resulting improvement in overall coreference performance (4% F1 gain) shows the practical applicability of the work. This suggests that the approach isn’t just effective in isolation but can enhance existing systems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dataset Size and Generalizability:** The WCR dataset, with 30 documents and ~7000 mentions, is relatively small. While the authors acknowledge this, the limited size raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.  The performance gains might be specific to the characteristics of these 30 articles and may not hold up on a much larger and more diverse dataset of Wikipedia articles.
2. **Over-reliance on Freebase:** The approach relies heavily on Freebase for extracting attributes (entity type, gender, number, profession). Given that Freebase is no longer actively maintained, this dependency is a potential long-term limitation. The paper could benefit from a discussion of alternative knowledge sources or strategies for mitigating this risk.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors provide some example errors (Figure 3), the error analysis is somewhat limited. A more detailed qualitative analysis of the types of errors made by the system would provide valuable insights for future improvements. Specifically, investigating *why* the classifier makes certain mistakes (e.g., incorrect pronoun resolution, linking mentions to the wrong entities) would be beneficial.
4. **Baselines are not State-of-the-Art in Coreference:** While the baselines outperform the existing systems on the WCR dataset, they are not the absolute most recent and advanced coreference systems overall. Including a comparison against more contemporary end-to-end neural coreference models would strengthen the evaluation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Dataset Expansion:** Do you have plans to expand the WCR dataset? If so, what strategies will you employ to ensure diversity and representativeness of Wikipedia articles?
2. **Freebase Dependency:**  What are your plans to address the dependency on Freebase, given its discontinuation? Have you considered alternative knowledge sources (e.g., Wikidata, DBpedia) or methods for inferring attributes without relying on external knowledge bases?
3. **Error Analysis Details:** Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the error types made by the classifier? Specifically, what are the most common sources of error, and what features seem to be most responsible for these errors?
4. **Neural Coreference Comparison:** Have you considered evaluating your approach against more recent end-to-end neural coreference systems (e.g., those based on transformers)? If not, why, and do you anticipate that such systems would perform similarly or differently on the WCR dataset?
