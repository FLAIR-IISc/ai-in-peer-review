## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

This paper revisits the task of identifying mentions of the main concept within a Wikipedia article, framing it as a binary classification problem. The authors leverage Wikipedia’s markup and external knowledge bases like Freebase to extract features for a classifier, significantly outperforming baseline coreference resolution systems on a constructed dataset (WCR). A key contribution is the creation and release of the WCR dataset itself, along with a dump of main concept mentions from all English Wikipedia articles, providing a valuable resource for future research. Further, demonstrating a positive impact when integrating their classifier into an existing rule-based coreference system (Dcoref) is also a notable contribution. 

The strengths of this submission are several. First, the problem addressed is relevant; adapting coreference resolution to specific genres like Wikipedia is important given performance degradation when applying models trained on news data. Second, the feature engineering is well-motivated and demonstrates a thoughtful integration of Wikipedia-specific information (markup, links) and external knowledge. The extensive feature ablation study (Table 2) provides valuable insight into feature importance. Finally, the experiments are well-conducted, with clear comparisons to baselines and state-of-the-art systems, and the reported improvements are substantial. The released dataset and mention dump are a significant benefit to the research community.

However, there are weaknesses. The dataset size (30 articles) is quite small and may limit the generalizability of the findings. While the authors acknowledge this, the impact on statistical significance isn’t thoroughly discussed. Secondly, while the classifier outperforms baselines, the core novelty of the approach—feature engineering—feels incremental. Utilizing Wikipedia markup and external knowledge for coreference isn’t entirely new (as acknowledged in Related Work), and the specific features, while well-chosen, lack a groundbreaking innovation. Finally, the presentation of results could be improved. Table 5 includes a long list of systems, making direct comparison difficult.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the small dataset size, can you provide more details on the statistical significance of the observed improvements?
2.  Could you elaborate on the process of creating the WCR dataset and the inter-annotator agreement?
3.  While you mention a dump of main concept mentions will be released, can you describe the format and scale of this dump (e.g., number of articles/mentions)?
4.  The phrase "Dcoref++" is used late in the paper without prior definition. Please define it earlier for clarity.
