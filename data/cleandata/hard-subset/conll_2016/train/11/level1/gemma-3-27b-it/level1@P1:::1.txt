This paper revisits coreference resolution in the specific context of Wikipedia articles, focusing on the task of identifying all mentions of the main concept described in an article. The authors argue that existing CR systems, largely trained on news data, perform poorly on Wikipedia due to genre differences, and propose a classifier leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase knowledge to improve performance on this task. They present a dataset (WCR) and demonstrate their approach outperforms baseline CR systems and enhances a rule-based system (Dcoref).

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Dataset (WCR):** The creation and release of a dedicated dataset (WCR) for evaluating coreference resolution focusing on main concept identification in Wikipedia articles is the most significant contribution. While relatively small (30 documents), it addresses a clear gap in available resources for this specific task and allows for focused evaluation.
2. **Feature Engineering & Classification Approach:** The careful design of features, particularly those exploiting Wikipedia's markup (links, redirects) and Freebase information, and the framing of the problem as a binary classification task represents a solid contribution. The distinction between pronominal and non-pronominal mention features is sensible and leads to improved performance.
3. **Demonstrated Improvement on a Targeted Task:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that their classifier outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines on the WCR dataset and improves the performance of a rule-based system when integrated. This empirically validates their approach and highlights the benefits of tailoring CR systems to the unique characteristics of Wikipedia.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition:** The authors clearly define the "main concept resolution" task and justify its importance, particularly in the context of information extraction from Wikipedia.
2. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, using a dedicated dataset and appropriate evaluation metrics. The comparison against established baselines and the ablation study (feature analysis) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Resourcefulness:** The authors effectively leverage readily available resources like Wikipedia markup and Freebase, avoiding the need for complex and potentially error-prone named entity linking pipelines.
4. **Practical Integration:** The integration of their classifier into an existing rule-based system (Dcoref++) and the resulting performance improvement demonstrate the practical applicability of their work.
5. **Detailed Feature Analysis:** The ablation study (Table 2 and associated discussion) provides valuable insights into the importance of different feature sets, helping to understand *why* the approach works.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** The WCR dataset is quite small (30 documents). While acknowledged, this limits the generalizability of the results and the potential for training more complex models. A larger dataset would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Reliance on Existing CR Systems for Baselines:**  The baselines are built *on top of* existing CR systems. While this is a reasonable approach, it means the gains demonstrated are relative to the performance of these systems *on this specific task*. A stronger baseline might involve a completely independently developed system tailored to Wikipedia.
3. **Overly Detailed Feature Engineering:** While the feature engineering is a strength, it is also very detailed and somewhat manually crafted. This raises questions about the scalability and maintainability of the approach. Investigating automated feature learning techniques could be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis Detail:** While the authors mention a few examples of errors, a more thorough error analysis would provide further insights into the limitations of the approach and potential areas for improvement.  Specifically, a breakdown of errors by mention type (e.g., pronominal vs. non-pronominal) would be informative.
5. **Website Link Placeholder:** The statement about sharing the dataset and results on "www.somewhere.country" needs to be replaced with a working link for reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What were the primary challenges in creating the WCR dataset? Were there any annotation disagreements, and how were they resolved?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of your classifier to the quality of the Freebase data? Were there instances where incorrect or missing Freebase information negatively impacted the results?
3.  Could you elaborate on the specific types of errors observed in the integration with Dcoref++? Were there any unexpected interactions between your classifier and the rule-based system?
4.  Have you experimented with other machine learning models besides SVMs (e.g., neural networks)? If so, how did they compare in performance?
5.  Given the relatively small size of the WCR dataset, how confident are you in the generalizability of your findings to other Wikipedia articles? Do you plan to expand the dataset in the future?
