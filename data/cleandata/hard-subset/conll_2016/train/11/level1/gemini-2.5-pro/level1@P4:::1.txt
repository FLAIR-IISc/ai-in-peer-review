### Summary of the paper

This paper addresses the problem of coreference resolution (CR) on Wikipedia articles, a domain where standard CR systems trained on newswire text are shown to underperform. The authors focus on a specific sub-task: "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main topic. They frame this as a binary classification problem and develop two SVM classifiers (one for pronominal mentions, one for non-pronominal mentions) that leverage features derived directly from Wikipedia's markup (e.g., internal links, redirects) and linked data from Freebase. This approach avoids the need for a separate, potentially error-prone entity linking step. The authors introduce a new annotated dataset, WCR, to demonstrate the domain gap and evaluate their method. Their proposed classifier significantly outperforms baselines built from state-of-the-art CR systems on the MCR task. Finally, they show that integrating their classifier as a high-precision sieve into a rule-based CR system (Dcoref) improves performance on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia text.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A highly effective, feature-engineered classifier for Main Concept Resolution in Wikipedia.** The primary contribution is a method that intelligently exploits the unique structural properties of Wikipedia and its connection to knowledge bases like Freebase. The careful feature engineering, which directly uses hyperlink targets and knowledge base attributes, proves to be a powerful strategy for this specific domain, achieving state-of-the-art results on the proposed task.
2.  **The creation and benchmarking of a new dataset (WCR) for coreference in Wikipedia.** The paper introduces a new, albeit small, annotated resource that serves two purposes. First, it provides quantitative evidence that existing state-of-the-art CR systems struggle when applied to the encyclopedic genre, motivating the need for domain-specific solutions. Second, it provides a testbed for evaluating the proposed MCR system and any future systems developed for this task.
3.  **A demonstration of the utility of solving the MCR sub-task for improving a full CR system.** The work shows that by accurately identifying the main concept's coreference chain early in a pipeline, overall CR performance can be substantially improved. Integrating the MCR classifier into the Dcoref sieve-based system yields a significant 4-point F1 gain, highlighting the practical value of this focused approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Rigorous Evaluation.** The proposed method achieves an F1 score of 88.79 on the MCR task, an absolute improvement of over 13 F1 points compared to the best-performing baseline (Table 4). This is a substantial gain. The baselines are well-conceived, representing plausible strategies for adapting existing SOTA systems to this task. The evaluation is further strengthened by a detailed ablation study (Tables 2 and 3) that clearly demonstrates the impact of each feature family, confirming that the use of Wikipedia- and Freebase-derived information is critical to the model's success.
2.  **Effective Exploitation of Domain-Specific Information.** A key strength is the system's ability to leverage the rich, structured information inherent in Wikipedia. By using internal hyperlinks as a substitute for a general-purpose entity linker and extracting attributes from Freebase, the method accesses high-quality semantic information with less noise than typical NLP pipelines. This design choice is well-suited to the target domain and is a primary driver of the system's high performance.
3.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation.** The paper is well-motivated, beginning with a clear demonstration that state-of-the-art CR systems perform poorly on Wikipedia text (Table 1). The performance drop of up to 17 CoNLL F1 points between OntoNotes and the new WCR dataset makes a compelling case for the research. The focused task of MCR is a practical and valuable sub-problem for understanding encyclopedic articles, and the experimental results validate its utility.
4.  **Demonstrated Impact on the End-to-End Task.** The authors successfully show that their work is not just an academic exercise on a novel sub-task. The Dcoref++ experiment (Table 5) integrates the MCR classifier into a full CR system and demonstrates a significant improvement across all standard metrics (MUC, B3, CEAF, and CoNLL F1). This result substantially strengthens the paper by showing the practical impact of accurately resolving the main concept chain.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scale and Generalizability of the Dataset.** The newly introduced WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, this is a very small corpus. Conclusions drawn from it, including the performance gap of SOTA systems and the effectiveness of the proposed model, may not generalize to the full diversity of Wikipedia. The stability and statistical significance of the results could be questioned given the small test set size implicit in a 10-fold cross-validation on 30 documents.
2.  **Dependence on a Deprecated Knowledge Base.** The methodology is heavily dependent on Freebase for extracting crucial entity attributes (e.g., type, gender, number, aliases). Freebase is a static, deprecated resource that has been superseded by Wikidata. This reliance raises concerns about the long-term maintainability and reproducibility of the work. The paper does not discuss the challenges or feasibility of adapting the approach to more current knowledge bases like Wikidata.
3.  **Classical Model without Comparison to Neural Alternatives.** The core of the proposed method is a linear SVM with engineered features. While effective, this represents a classical machine learning approach. The paper does not explore or compare against more modern, neural-based architectures that have become standard for many NLP tasks, including coreference resolution. It is unclear if a simpler neural model could achieve similar performance without such extensive feature engineering.
4.  **Unclear Handling of Singleton Mentions in Training.** The paper states that 13,400 automatically extracted singleton mentions were added to the 7,000 annotated mentions for the training set (Section 6.1). However, their role in training the binary MCR classifier is not explained. It is presumably the case that all singletons are treated as negative examples, but this is a critical detail that is omitted. If so, this could heavily skew the class distribution and affect the learning process. The handling of these mentions should be clarified.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given the WCR dataset's small size (30 articles), could you comment on the statistical significance of your results and the generalizability of your findings to the wider Wikipedia domain? Did you perform any analysis of performance variation across different article categories (e.g., people vs. organizations) within your dataset?
2.  Your method relies heavily on Freebase, which is no longer actively maintained. Have you considered adapting your feature extraction process to use Wikidata? How much of your system's performance do you believe is tied to the specific schema and data available in Freebase versus what is available in more modern knowledge graphs?
3.  The integration with Dcoref is implemented as a high-precision first sieve. Could you elaborate on why a rule-based, sieve architecture was chosen for this integration experiment over a learning-based system like Scoref? Does your classifier's output lend itself particularly well to this type of hard-coded, high-precision rule?
