### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the challenge of coreference resolution (CR) on Wikipedia text, a domain where standard models trained on newswire data perform poorly. The authors focus on a specific, high-impact sub-task: "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main subject. They propose a binary classification approach using Support Vector Machines, with separate models for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. The key insight is to leverage the rich, structured information inherent in Wikipedia (markup, redirects, internal links) and linked knowledge bases (Freebase) to engineer a powerful set of features. The proposed model is shown to drastically outperform several strong baselines built from state-of-the-art CR systems. Furthermore, integrating the MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into a rule-based CR system (Dcoref) is shown to improve performance on the full coreference task for Wikipedia articles.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:

1.  **A highly effective, feature-driven method for Main Concept Resolution in Wikipedia.** The core contribution is the design and successful application of a classifier that intelligently exploits domain-specific signals from Wikipedia and Freebase. The feature engineering is thoughtful and demonstrates a clear understanding of the domain's structure, leading to a substantial performance gain (+13 F1 points) over the best baseline on this task.
2.  **The creation and analysis of a new annotated resource (WCR).** By creating a 30-document corpus of Wikipedia articles annotated for coreference, the authors provide a valuable resource for the community. Their initial analysis on this corpus convincingly demonstrates the performance gap of existing state-of-the-art systems on this genre, motivating the need for domain-specific adaptation.
3.  **A practical demonstration of improving a full CR system.** The authors show that solving this specific sub-task can directly benefit a general-purpose CR system. By integrating their classifier into Dcoref, they achieve a 4-point F1 improvement on the WCR dataset, providing a clear pathway for leveraging this work in broader applications.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Thorough Evaluation:** The paper presents a very convincing empirical case. The proposed MCR classifier achieves an F1 of 88.8%, which is a massive improvement over strong baselines derived from both rule-based (Dcoref) and learning-based (Scoref) SOTA systems. The evaluation is comprehensive, including detailed ablation studies (Tables 2 & 3) that clearly illustrate the utility of each feature family. The analysis of errors (Figure 3) is also insightful.
2.  **Clever and Effective Use of Domain-Specific Knowledge:** The main methodological strength lies in how the authors bypass the need for a potentially noisy, full-fledged entity linking system. By directly using Wikipedia's human-curated hyperlinks to access Freebase entries, they can source high-quality semantic attributes (type, gender, number, aliases) for mentions. This is a very pragmatic and effective approach that avoids the cascading errors common in multi-stage NLP pipelines.
3.  **Well-Motivated and Impactful Problem Formulation:** The paper correctly identifies that standard CR systems fail to generalize and focuses on a sub-problem that is both tractable and highly useful. The main concept of an article is arguably the most important entity for downstream tasks like Information Extraction, Relation Extraction, and Question Answering. Solving MCR with high accuracy is a significant practical contribution.
4.  **Valuable Resource and Benchmark:** The introduction of the WCR dataset and the benchmarking of several SOTA systems (Table 1) is a welcome contribution. It not only provides a testbed for future work but also quantifies the domain adaptation problem for CR in a concrete way, showing a performance drop of 10-17 F1 points for SOTA systems compared to their performance on OntoNotes. The observation that the rule-based Dcoref outperforms more recent learning-based systems on this dataset is particularly interesting and warrants further investigation.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty and Reliance on Feature Engineering:** The core machine learning method is a standard SVM classifier. All of the model's power comes from extensive, hand-crafted feature engineering. While highly effective, this approach feels somewhat dated for 2016, a time when the field was rapidly moving towards end-to-end neural models that learn representations automatically. The paper would be stronger if it discussed how its insights could inform feature representations in a neural architecture, rather than relying solely on a classic ML pipeline.
2.  **Dependence on a Defunct Knowledge Base:** The system relies heavily on Freebase for extracting crucial semantic features. Freebase was officially shut down in 2016 and its data was migrated to Wikidata. This significantly impacts the long-term reproducibility and applicability of the described method. While this was a reasonable choice at the time of submission, the lack of discussion regarding potential migration to Wikidata or other knowledge bases is a notable omission that dates the work.
3.  **Small Dataset Size and Potential Generalization Issues:** The WCR dataset, with only 30 documents, is very small. While the authors state they sampled for variety, it is difficult to be confident that the results and the specific feature weights learned will generalize across the vast and diverse landscape of Wikipedia articles. A larger-scale evaluation, even on an automatically- or distantly-supervised dataset, would have strengthened the claims of the paper considerably.
4.  **Limited Integration Experiment:** The integration of the MCR classifier into Dcoref is promising but narrow in scope. It's unclear why the rule-based Dcoref was chosen for modification over the more powerful learning-based systems like Scoref. An explanation of this choice is missing. Furthermore, the integration strategy—inserting a single, powerful sieve at the start and preventing further modifications to the MC chain—is quite rigid. It would have been interesting to see an analysis of whether this hard constraint hurts recall in complex scenarios where a mention could ambiguously refer to the MC or another entity.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Your choice to integrate the MCR classifier into the rule-based Dcoref system is well-motivated by its strong baseline performance on WCR. However, could you elaborate on the feasibility of integrating your classifier's predictions as features or constraints into a learning-based system like Scoref? Would you expect similar or greater gains in such a framework?
2.  Given the deprecation of Freebase, have you considered adapting your feature extraction process to use Wikidata? Do you foresee any major challenges, and would you expect the quality of features (e.g., "notable types", aliases) from Wikidata to be comparable for this task?
3.  The feature ablation for non-pronominal mentions (Table 3) shows that the `name variants` feature family gives a large boost in recall at the cost of precision, which you attribute to noisy redirects. Could you provide examples of such noisy redirects and perhaps discuss potential strategies for filtering them to improve the precision of this feature set?
