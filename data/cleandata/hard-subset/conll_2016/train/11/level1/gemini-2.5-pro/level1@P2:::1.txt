**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution specifically for Wikipedia articles, focusing on a sub-problem: identifying all mentions of the article's "main concept" (MC). The authors argue that standard coreference systems, trained primarily on newswire, perform poorly on this genre. They propose a machine learning approach, framing the task as a binary classification problem for each mention (i.e., does it refer to the MC or not?). The core of their method lies in extensive feature engineering that leverages the unique structure of Wikipedia (markup, hyperlinks) and linked external knowledge from Freebase to create rich representations for both the mention and the MC. They evaluate their system on a newly created, annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR), demonstrating that their classifier significantly outperforms baselines built from state-of-the-art coreference systems. Finally, they show that integrating their classifier as a high-precision sieve into a rule-based system (Dcoref) improves performance on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia articles.

**Contributions**
1.  **Effective Domain-Specific Feature Engineering:** The primary contribution is the design and successful application of a feature set tailored to resolving the main concept in Wikipedia. By directly exploiting Wikipedia's internal hyperlinks and structured markup, along with linked Freebase data, the authors create a powerful classifier that circumvents the need for a potentially noisy, separate entity linking step. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the high value of these features.
2.  **Creation and Benchmarking of a New Resource (WCR):** The paper introduces the WCR dataset. While small, this resource is valuable as it provides a concrete benchmark for the understudied problem of coreference on encyclopedic text. The authors' initial experiments on WCR (Table 1) provide strong empirical evidence that domain adaptation is a significant challenge for modern coreference systems, motivating the need for work like this.
3.  **A Focused and Effective Model for Main Concept Resolution:** The authors present a simple yet very effective model that achieves a large performance gain (over 13 F1 points) on the targeted task compared to strong baselines. They also provide a proof-of-concept for how solving this sub-task can be leveraged to improve a full coreference resolution system.

**Strengths**
1.  **Clear Motivation and Strong Empirical Justification:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The results in Table 1, showing a dramatic performance drop for several state-of-the-art systems when applied to the WCR dataset, provide a compelling argument for the necessity of this work. This clear gap in performance makes the problem setting relevant and the contributions significant.
2.  **Impressive Performance on the Targeted Task:** The proposed classifier achieves an F1 score of 88.79, which is a substantial improvement over the strongest baselines derived from powerful systems like Dcoref and Scoref (B4 baselines at 75.77 and 73.69, respectively). This is not an incremental gain but a significant leap in performance, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach for the main concept resolution task. The detailed ablation studies in Tables 2 and 3 effectively dissect the contribution of each feature family.
3.  **Intelligent Use of Domain-Specific Information:** A key strength is the direct use of Wikipedia's structure. Instead of relying on a generic wikification or entity linking pipeline, which can introduce errors, the authors leverage the human-curated hyperlinks within articles to assign high-quality semantic attributes to mentions. This is a clever and pragmatic design choice that is well-suited to the specific domain of Wikipedia.
4.  **Contribution to the Community:** The authors promise to release the annotated WCR dataset and the large-scale dump of classified mentions for all of English Wikipedia. This is a valuable contribution that will enable replication and encourage further research on coreference resolution beyond the standard newswire domain.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Dataset Size and Generalizability:** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 articles. While the authors state they sampled for variety, this is a very small sample size from which to draw broad conclusions about performance on a resource as vast and diverse as Wikipedia. It raises concerns about how well the trained classifiers and the observed performance gains will generalize to articles on different topics (e.g., abstract concepts, historical events), of different lengths, or with different structural properties (e.g., lower link density).
2.  **Dependency on a Deprecated Resource:** The methodology relies heavily on Freebase for extracting crucial attributes like entity types, gender, and aliases. Freebase has been officially deprecated since 2016, and its data has been subsumed by Wikidata. This presents a significant challenge for the long-term reproducibility and applicability of the proposed method. The paper does not discuss this limitation or a potential migration path to using Wikidata, which is the modern standard for this kind of structured knowledge.
3.  **Limited Evaluation of the Full Coreference Pipeline:** The authors demonstrate the utility of their MC classifier by integrating it into a single, rule-based system (Dcoref). While the 4-point F1 improvement is notable, it is unclear if this gain would translate to more recent, learning-based, end-to-end systems like Scoref. Such systems learn complex interactions between mentions, and inserting a hard-coded, high-precision sieve might not be the optimal integration strategy or could potentially disrupt the learned model. This limits the generality of the claim that their approach improves full coreference resolution on Wikipedia.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The WCR dataset size of 30 documents is a potential concern for generalizability. Could you elaborate on the sampling strategy and comment on whether you observed significant performance variations across different article types (e.g., person, organization, event) within your cross-validation folds? This might help alleviate concerns about the model's robustness.
2.  Your feature extraction relies on Freebase, which is now a deprecated resource. Have you considered adapting your approach to use Wikidata instead? Do you foresee any major challenges in mapping the features you extract from Freebase (e.g., "notable types") to equivalents in the Wikidata ontology?
3.  You show a strong improvement when integrating your classifier as a first-pass sieve into the rule-based Dcoref system. Could you speculate on how this focused MC knowledge could be integrated into a modern neural coreference system? For instance, could the classifier's output be used as a feature, or could it inform a constrained decoding process?
