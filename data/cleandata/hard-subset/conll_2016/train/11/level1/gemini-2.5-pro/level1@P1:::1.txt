### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the task of coreference resolution on Wikipedia text, a genre on which standard systems are known to underperform. The authors focus on a specific, practical sub-problem: identifying all mentions of the article's main concept (MC). They frame this as a binary classification task and develop two SVM classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) that leverage rich, domain-specific features extracted from Wikipedia's markup (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked knowledge bases (Freebase). The authors introduce a new annotated dataset, WCR, to benchmark performance on this genre. Their proposed classifier is shown to substantially outperform baselines derived from state-of-the-art coreference systems. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of their MC classifier by integrating it into a full rule-based coreference system, achieving a significant performance boost on the full coreference task on Wikipedia data.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A new benchmark and dataset (WCR) for coreference resolution on Wikipedia.** The paper provides compelling evidence that state-of-the-art coreference systems struggle on Wikipedia text. The creation and analysis of the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution that enables and motivates future research in genre adaptation for coreference resolution.
2.  **A highly effective and well-engineered system for Main Concept Resolution.** The proposed classification method is simple but achieves a very large performance gain (+13 F1) over strong baselines. The key insight is to exploit the high-quality, human-annotated structure of Wikipedia to extract features, cleverly bypassing the need for a noisy, general-purpose entity linking pipeline.
3.  **A practical demonstration of the value of MC resolution for full coreference.** By using their classifier to create a high-precision first sieve in an existing system (Dcoref), the authors improve its performance on the full coreference task by a significant margin (~3.3 CoNLL F1). This showcases a clear path for improving general-purpose coreference systems on this important domain.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear motivation and problem definition.** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the need for domain-specific coreference resolution for Wikipedia. The empirical results in Table 1, showing a dramatic performance drop for multiple state-of-the-art systems on the WCR dataset, provide a powerful argument. Framing the problem around "Main Concept Resolution" is an intelligent simplification that is both tractable and highly useful for downstream tasks.
2.  **Strong empirical results and rigorous evaluation.** The proposed method achieves a substantial improvement over several well-conceived baselines, with an absolute F1 gain of over 13 points (Table 4). The evaluation is thorough, including an ablation study to analyze the impact of different feature families (Table 2 and text) and an extrinsic evaluation that demonstrates the system's utility in improving a full coreference pipeline (Table 5). This comprehensive analysis strongly supports the paper's claims.
3.  **Clever and effective use of domain-specific features.** A key strength of the paper is its exploitation of Wikipedia's unique structure. By using internal hyperlinks to connect mentions to Freebase entities and using page redirects as name variants, the system acquires high-quality semantic information. This approach avoids the cascading errors common in systems that rely on separate, general-purpose entity linkers, which is a significant methodological advantage.
4.  **Contribution of a valuable new resource.** The WCR dataset, while small, provides a much-needed benchmark for this task. The authors' commitment to releasing the dataset, along with a dump of all identified MC mentions for English Wikipedia, represents a major contribution to the community that will facilitate future research and applications.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The novelty is in the engineering, not the ML method.** The core of the proposed method is a standard SVM with engineered features. While the feature engineering is excellent and highly effective, the work does not introduce a novel machine learning algorithm or coreference resolution paradigm. The contribution is primarily one of effective application and careful engineering for a specific domain.
2.  **The dataset size is very small.** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 articles. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, this is a very small sample from which to draw conclusions about a resource as vast and varied as Wikipedia. Performance could vary significantly on articles with different characteristics (e.g., stubs, list articles, pages on less popular topics) that might be underrepresented in the sample. The model's reliance on Freebase could also be a weakness for concepts not well-covered there.
3.  **The baselines could be stronger.** The baselines used (B1-B4) are all heuristic post-processing rules applied to the output of off-the-shelf CR systems. A more direct and compelling baseline would involve training a standard coreference classifier on the WCR data itself, using a generic feature set (e.g., string matching, distance, syntactic features) *without* the Wikipedia-specific features. This would better isolate the performance gain that is directly attributable to the novel features proposed in the paper versus the gain from simply training on in-domain data.
4.  **Minor clarity issues in the experimental description.** Table 3, detailing the ablation study for non-pronominal mentions, is referenced but missing from the paper, making it difficult to fully assess the impact of those feature sets. Additionally, the description of how the Dcoref++ sieve is implemented is slightly ambiguous ("merges two chains whenever they both contain mentions to the MC"). Clarifying whether this merges two pre-existing chains or operates at the mention-level would improve reproducibility.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given the small size of the WCR dataset (30 documents), could you report on the variance of the results across the 10 cross-validation folds? This would help gauge the stability of the performance gains.
2.  Your method relies heavily on Wikipedia's rich markup and links to Freebase. Have you analyzed how performance correlates with article maturity or link density? How would you expect the system to perform on "stub" articles with sparse internal linking?
3.  In the Dcoref++ system, you state that you "prevent other sieves from appending new mentions to the MC coreference chain." This seems like a hard constraint that might hurt recall. Did you experiment with a softer integration, for instance, by allowing other very high-precision sieves to modify the MC chain?
