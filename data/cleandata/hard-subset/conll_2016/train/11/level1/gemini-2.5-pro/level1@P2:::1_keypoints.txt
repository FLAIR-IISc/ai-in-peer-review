- Task: binary classification to detect all mentions of a Wikipedia article's "main concept" (MC), using Wikipedia structure and Freebase features, evaluated on a 30-article WCR dataset and used as a high-precision sieve in Dcoref.

- Contribution: domain-specific feature engineering exploiting Wikipedia markup, hyperlinks, and Freebase; ablation studies show these features are highly valuable.

- Contribution: creation and benchmarking of the WCR dataset (30 articles) to highlight domain adaptation challenges for coreference on encyclopedic text.

- Contribution: a simple, effective MC classifier that yields a large gain (≈13 F1 points) over strong baselines and can improve full coreference when used as a sieve.

- Strength: clear motivation and strong empirical evidence (state-of-the-art systems drop sharply on WCR), justifying the problem and approach.

- Strength: impressive targeted performance (F1 88.79) substantially above Dcoref/Scoref baselines (~75), with detailed ablation analyses.

- Strength: pragmatic, intelligent use of human-curated Wikipedia hyperlinks instead of error-prone generic entity linking.

- Strength: commitment to release WCR and a large-scale classified-mentions dump for English Wikipedia, aiding reproducibility and follow-up work.

- Weakness: very small dataset (30 articles), raising concerns about generalizability across topics, lengths, and link densities.

- Weakness: heavy reliance on deprecated Freebase for entity attributes, with no discussed migration to Wikidata, threatening long-term reproducibility.

- Weakness: evaluation of integration limited to rule-based Dcoref; unclear whether gains hold or can be integrated into modern neural end-to-end coreference systems.

- Question: please detail the WCR sampling strategy and whether performance varied by article type (person, organization, event) across folds.

- Question: have you considered migrating feature extraction from Freebase to Wikidata, and what mapping challenges do you anticipate?

- Question: how might the MC classifier be incorporated into neural coreference systems (e.g., as features, auxiliary loss, or constrained decoding) without disrupting learned interactions?
