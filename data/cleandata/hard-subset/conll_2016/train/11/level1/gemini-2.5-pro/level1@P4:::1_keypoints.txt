- Paper tackles Main Concept Resolution (MCR) in Wikipedia, framing it as binary classification to identify mentions of an article's main topic using Wikipedia markup and Freebase features.  
- Authors build two SVMs (pronominal and non-pronominal) leveraging internal links, redirects, and Freebase attributes to avoid a separate entity-linking step.  
- Introduces a new annotated WCR dataset to demonstrate domain gap and evaluate the MCR approach.  
- MCR classifier achieves strong results (88.79 F1), substantially outperforming adapted SOTA CR baselines.  
- Careful feature engineering exploiting Wikipedia structure and Freebase yields high performance with low-noise semantic signals.  
- Clear motivation: shows large performance drop of newswire-trained CR systems on Wikipedia, justifying the focused MCR task.  
- Demonstrates practical value by integrating MCR as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref, improving full CR performance (≈4-point CoNLL F1 gain).  
- Dataset is very small (30 articles), raising concerns about generalizability, stability, and statistical significance.  
- Heavy dependence on deprecated Freebase threatens long-term reproducibility and portability to current KBs like Wikidata.  
- Uses a classical linear SVM with engineered features and lacks comparison to modern neural CR models.  
- Unclear how automatically extracted singleton mentions were used in training and whether they biased class distribution.  
- Question: How statistically significant and generalizable are results given only 30 articles, and was performance analyzed across article types (people vs organizations)?  
- Question: Can the feature extraction be adapted to Wikidata, and how tied is performance to Freebase's schema/data?  
- Question: Why integrate via a rule-based sieve (Dcoref) rather than a learning-based resolver, and does the classifier particularly suit a high-precision sieve design?
