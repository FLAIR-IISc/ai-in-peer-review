**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Main Concept Resolution (MCR) in Wikipedia, which involves identifying all mentions that refer to the subject of an article. The authors frame this as a binary classification problem and train two SVM classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using features derived from Wikipedia's internal structure (markup, links) and the Freebase knowledge graph. This approach significantly outperforms baselines built from state-of-the-art coreference systems. The classifier is then integrated as a high-precision sieve into a rule-based system, improving its performance on full coreference resolution for Wikipedia articles.

The main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:
1.  A highly effective, feature-engineered classification model for Main Concept Resolution that demonstrates the power of leveraging domain-specific information from Wikipedia’s structure and linked knowledge bases, achieving an impressive F1 score of nearly 89%.
2.  The creation and analysis of the WCR dataset. Although small, it is used effectively to demonstrate that general-domain coreference systems perform poorly on Wikipedia, clearly motivating the need for specialized approaches.
3.  A practical demonstration of the model's utility by successfully integrating it into a full coreference resolution pipeline, resulting in a notable 4-point F1 score improvement on the downstream task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The paper presents compelling results, showing a large absolute F1 improvement of over 13 points against well-conceived baselines. The ablation studies in Tables 2 and 3 are particularly convincing, clearly illustrating the impact of each feature family and reinforcing the central thesis that Wikipedia-specific features are critical for this task.
2.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The work is well-motivated. By first evaluating several state-of-the-art systems on their new WCR dataset (Table 1), the authors establish a clear performance gap and justify the need for research on coreference resolution tailored to the Wikipedia genre.
3.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The authors show that their MCR model is not just an isolated academic exercise. By integrating it as a new first sieve into the Dcoref system, they improve performance on the full coreference task. This highlights the practical value and potential impact of their method for downstream NLP applications that consume Wikipedia content.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited and Inaccessible Dataset:** The evaluation is performed on WCR, a new dataset of only 30 documents. This small size raises concerns about the generalizability of the reported results. Furthermore, the dataset is not available for review (the link is anonymized), which hinders reproducibility and prevents the community from building upon this work.
2.  **Reliance on a Deprecated Knowledge Base:** The methodology depends heavily on features extracted from Freebase, which was officially deprecated in 2016. The paper does not discuss the implications of this or propose a path forward using a modern, maintained knowledge graph like Wikidata. This dependency significantly limits the long-term viability and ease of implementation of the proposed system.
3.  **Dated Methodology:** The core of the method is a feature-engineered SVM, which, while effective here, is less common in state-of-the-art coreference resolution today. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of how these powerful, domain-specific features could be incorporated into modern end-to-end neural coreference models.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Your method relies heavily on Freebase. Have you considered adapting it to use Wikidata, and what challenges do you foresee in migrating the feature extraction process?
2.  Could you elaborate on the sampling strategy for the 30 documents in WCR to help alleviate concerns about the generalizability of the results given the small dataset size?
3.  You successfully integrated your model into the rule-based Dcoref system. Have you explored how your high-precision MCR predictions could benefit a statistical system like Scoref, for example, as hard constraints or as features for its neural scoring model?
