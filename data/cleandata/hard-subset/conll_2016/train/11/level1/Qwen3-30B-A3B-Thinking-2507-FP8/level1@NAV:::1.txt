

This paper introduces "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), a specific coreference task for Wikipedia articles that focuses on identifying all mentions of the central concept described in the article. The authors frame this as a binary classification problem rather than full coreference resolution, leveraging Wikipedia's unique markup structure and Freebase attributes to distinguish MC mentions from other references.

Primary contributions:
1) The novel task definition of MCR, distinct from general coreference resolution, which addresses a significant gap in the literature as existing CR systems trained on news data perform poorly on Wikipedia.
2) A feature-rich classifier that effectively exploits Wikipedia's internal links, redirects, and Freebase attributes, achieving 89% F1 (13-15 points higher than baselines).
3) Demonstrated practical utility by integrating the classifier into a coreference pipeline, improving overall F1 by 4 points.

Strengths:
1) The paper identifies a meaningful research gap: existing CR systems trained on news data fail on Wikipedia, and this task is directly relevant for NLP applications using Wikipedia.
2) The feature engineering is well-justified, leveraging Wikipedia's unique structural elements (links, redirects) in a way that would be impossible with generic text sources.
3) The experimental results are robust, showing significant improvements over multiple baselines, and the integration into a full coreference pipeline demonstrates practical value.

Weaknesses:
1) The dataset is extremely small (30 documents, 60k tokens, 7k mentions), limiting statistical significance and raising concerns about generalizability.
2) The baselines (B1-B4) are overly simplistic and don't compare against more sophisticated approaches, making the performance gains less impressive.
3) The paper doesn't adequately address limitations or provide thorough analysis of failure cases beyond a few examples.

Questions:
1) How do you plan to scale this approach to handle the full Wikipedia corpus (millions of articles)?
2) Have you evaluated the approach on Wikipedia articles in languages other than English?
