

# Peer Review for "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper addresses the underexplored task of identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) in Wikipedia articles, where the MC is the central entity described by the article. The authors demonstrate that standard coreference resolution (CR) systems trained on news text perform poorly on Wikipedia, with F1 scores dropping from ~60 to ~50 (Table 1). They propose a binary classification approach to determine if a mention refers to the MC, leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase features. The main contributions as I see them are:

1. A well-constructed dataset (WCR) of 30 Wikipedia articles (60k tokens) annotated for main concept resolution, which is a valuable resource for future research.
2. A novel feature engineering approach that leverages Wikipedia-specific characteristics (markup, links, Freebase) to significantly outperform baselines on the MC resolution task.
3. Demonstration that integrating their approach into a full CR pipeline (Dcoref++) improves overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia by 4 F1 points.

## Strengths

1. **Identification of a meaningful gap in coreference research**: The paper effectively demonstrates that standard CR systems trained on news text perform poorly on Wikipedia (Table 1), which is a widely used resource in NLP. This is a significant gap that needs addressing, as Wikipedia's unique characteristics (structured markup, multilingual, multi-domain) differ substantially from news corpora.

2. **Well-designed feature engineering leveraging Wikipedia's unique properties**: The authors' approach to extract features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase (e.g., using hyperlinks as a substitute for named-entity linking) is thoughtful and directly addresses the specific challenges of the Wikipedia domain. The feature families (title, inferred type, name variants, etc.) are carefully designed to capture Wikipedia's structural richness, as evidenced by the significant performance gains (Table 2, Table 4).

3. **Practical utility demonstrated through integration**: The paper doesn't just focus on the narrow main concept resolution task but shows how this approach can improve full coreference resolution when integrated into a pipeline (Dcoref++). The 4-point F1 improvement (Table 5) is substantial for a pipeline modification, demonstrating real-world applicability.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient dataset size and justification**: The WCR dataset is relatively small (30 articles, 60k tokens), which limits statistical power. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't adequately justify why this small dataset is sufficient for their claims, especially given the complexity of coreference resolution. This raises concerns about the generalizability of their results.

2. **Narrow scope of the main concept resolution task**: The paper focuses on identifying mentions of the main concept (a single entity), which is a constrained subset of the full coreference resolution problem. The paper doesn't sufficiently address how this approach would scale to identifying all coreference chains (not just those related to the MC), which is the ultimate goal of CR. The connection between this specialized task and general CR is not fully established.

3. **Lack of analysis on why standard CR systems fail on Wikipedia**: The authors observe that standard CR systems perform poorly on Wikipedia (Table 1) but don't investigate why. Is it due to text genre differences (news vs. Wikipedia), annotation differences (OntoNotes vs. WCR), or other factors? Without this analysis, it's difficult to determine whether their approach addresses the root cause or just a symptom.

4. **Limited comparison to other CR systems**: The authors only compare to two systems (Dcoref and Scoref) despite mentioning several others in Table 1. The omission of comparisons to other state-of-the-art systems (e.g., the Martschat and Strube system, which performs well on OntoNotes) weakens their claim of outperforming "state-of-the-art" approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. How did you select the 30 Wikipedia articles for the WCR dataset? Were they chosen to represent specific characteristics (e.g., different article lengths, topic diversity, internal link density), or was this a random sample? How might the results differ if the dataset were larger or more diverse?

2. The paper mentions that Wikipedia is "a large, multilingual, highly structured, multi-domain encyclopedia," but the dataset and experiments are limited to English Wikipedia. How would the approach generalize to other languages, and what additional challenges would arise?

3. The authors state that "the main concept" is defined by the article title. How would the approach handle Wikipedia articles where the title doesn't clearly represent the main concept (e.g., articles about multiple related entities or articles with ambiguous titles)?

4. In Table 5, Dcoref++ shows improved performance on WCR, but the paper doesn't report results on standard CR datasets (e.g., OntoNotes). How does the integration of the MC classifier affect performance on news text or other genres? Would it harm performance on the original training data?
