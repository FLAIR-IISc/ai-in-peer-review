

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions
This paper addresses a specific task of identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) in Wikipedia articles, which the authors call "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR). The authors argue that existing coreference resolution (CR) systems, trained primarily on news text, perform poorly on Wikipedia, and propose a binary classification approach to identify MC mentions using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. The main contributions as I see them (in order of strength) are:

1. A well-structured dataset (WCR3) for the MCR task, which fills a gap in the literature and provides a standard benchmark for future work.
2. A feature-rich classifier that leverages Wikipedia-specific markup and Freebase information to identify MC mentions, demonstrating significant performance gains over baselines.
3. A practical integration of their classifier into a state-of-the-art CR system (Dcoref), showing measurable improvements in overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a meaningful gap**: The paper effectively demonstrates that existing CR systems trained on news text perform significantly worse on Wikipedia (10-15 F1 point drop), which is a widely used resource in NLP. The authors provide strong empirical evidence through their experiments with multiple state-of-the-art systems (Table 1), showing that the performance gap is substantial and warrants attention.

2. **Well-structured dataset**: The WCR3 dataset (30 Wikipedia articles, 60k tokens) is carefully constructed following OntoNotes guidelines, with detailed annotation of mentions and their relation to the main concept. The authors provide clear justification for their dataset selection (size, topic diversity, internal link density) and make the dataset publicly available, which is a significant contribution to the community.

3. **Technical soundness of the approach**: The authors' feature engineering is comprehensive and well-justified, with clear differentiation between pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. Their incremental feature addition experiments (Table 2) provide strong evidence for the value of each feature family. The classifier's performance (88.99% F1 for pronominal mentions, 88.67% for non-pronominal) significantly outperforms all baselines (Table 4), with absolute gains of 13-15 F1 points.

4. **Practical impact**: The integration of their classifier into the Dcoref system (Dcoref++) demonstrates real-world applicability, improving the CoNLL F1 score by 4 points on the full coreference resolution task (Table 5). This shows that their approach isn't just useful for the specific MCR task but has broader implications for Wikipedia coreference resolution.

5. **Clear experimental design**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation across multiple metrics (MUC, B3, CEAF, CoNLL) and conduct ablation studies to analyze the contribution of each feature family. Their comparison to multiple baselines and state-of-the-art systems provides a comprehensive picture of their approach's strengths.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the task**: While the authors identify a gap in adapting CR to Wikipedia, they acknowledge that similar tasks were explored by Nakayama (2008) and Nguyen et al. (2007). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from these prior works in terms of technical novelty, making it difficult to assess how much this work advances the field beyond previous studies.

2. **Small dataset size**: The WCR3 dataset (30 articles, 60k tokens) is substantially smaller than standard CR datasets (OntoNotes has over 1M tokens). This limits the statistical significance of the results and raises questions about how well the findings generalize to larger Wikipedia corpora. The paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for why this small dataset is sufficient for their claims.

3. **Lack of comparison to Wikipedia-specific approaches**: The paper doesn't compare their approach to methods that directly leverage Wikipedia's internal structure for coreference resolution (e.g., using anchor text or redirects for disambiguation). This omission makes it difficult to assess whether their feature engineering is truly novel or just an adaptation of existing techniques.

4. **Insufficient analysis of the rule-based vs. statistical performance gap**: The paper observes that rule-based Dcoref outperforms statistical systems on Wikipedia (Table 1), but doesn't explore this finding in depth. This could be a significant contribution if properly analyzed, but the paper treats it as a footnote rather than a central research question.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address potential limitations of their approach, such as performance on non-English Wikipedia articles, handling of Wikipedia articles with different structures (e.g., infoboxes vs. plain text), or robustness when Freebase information is missing.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper observes that rule-based Dcoref outperforms statistical systems on Wikipedia (Table 1), but doesn't analyze why. Can the authors provide more detailed analysis of this finding and its implications for CR system design on non-news text genres?

2. The dataset size (30 articles, 60k tokens) is significantly smaller than standard CR datasets. How do the authors expect their results to generalize to larger Wikipedia corpora, and what steps would be needed to validate this?

3. The paper uses Freebase as a source of features, but doesn't address how the approach would perform without Freebase or when Freebase information is incomplete. How would the authors handle cases where Freebase lacks relevant information?

4. The paper reports significant performance gains (13-15 F1 points) on the MCR task. How do these gains compare to those achieved by other domain adaptation techniques for CR on Wikipedia, and what is the relative contribution of each feature family to these gains?

5. The paper states that the MC is the concept being described by the article. How do the authors handle cases where Wikipedia articles describe multiple related concepts (e.g., articles about organizations with multiple branches or related entities)?
