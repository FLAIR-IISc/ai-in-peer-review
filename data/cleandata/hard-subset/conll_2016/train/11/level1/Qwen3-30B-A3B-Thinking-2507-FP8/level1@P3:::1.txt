

# Peer Review: "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper addresses the underexplored task of identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) in Wikipedia articles, rather than full coreference resolution. The authors' primary contributions are: (1) creation of a new dataset (WCR) for evaluating MC resolution in Wikipedia, (2) development of a novel classifier leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase features to identify MC mentions, and (3) demonstration that this classifier outperforms baselines built on state-of-the-art CR systems and improves full CR performance when integrated into a rule-based system.

## Strengths

1. **Identification of a meaningful gap in NLP research**: The paper correctly identifies that most CR systems are trained on news data and perform poorly on Wikipedia, which is widely used in NLP applications. The authors provide compelling evidence of this performance gap (Table 1), showing significant drops in F1 scores (up to 17 points) when applying state-of-the-art CR systems to Wikipedia. This is a valuable contribution to the field.

2. **Innovative use of Wikipedia-specific features**: The paper's key strength is the clever exploitation of Wikipedia's rich markup (internal links, redirects, Freebase connections) rather than relying on standard NER or wikification pipelines. This approach avoids the error propagation common in cascaded systems (Zheng et al., 2013), as demonstrated by the authors' careful preprocessing. The feature engineering (Section 4.2) is particularly strong, with thoughtful distinctions between pronominal and non-pronominal mentions.

3. **Significant empirical results on the proposed task**: The classifier achieves 88.99% F1 on pronominal mentions and 88.67% on non-pronominal mentions (Table 4), representing an absolute 13-15 point gain over the best baseline. The integration into Dcoref (Dcoref++) achieves a 4-point absolute improvement on the standard CoNLL F1 metric (Table 5), demonstrating real practical impact for CR on Wikipedia.

## Weaknesses

1. **Extremely limited dataset size**: The WCR dataset consists of only 30 articles (60k tokens), which is substantially smaller than standard CR datasets (e.g., OntoNotes has ~500k tokens). This severely limits the statistical significance of the results. The authors acknowledge this but provide insufficient justification for why such a small dataset is representative. The claim that "we believe our results to be representative" (Section 5) is unsubstantiated without a proper analysis of dataset diversity and statistical power.

2. **Narrow task definition limits generalizability**: The task of identifying MC mentions is a highly specific subtask of coreference resolution, not a general CR solution. The authors acknowledge this but don't adequately address why this narrow task is valuable beyond the specific context of Wikipedia. The paper doesn't demonstrate how this approach would generalize to other text genres or to full coreference resolution, which is a major limitation for broader impact.

3. **Superficial comparison to baselines**: The baselines (B1-B4) are extremely simple heuristics (e.g., "pick the longest chain") that don't represent serious CR approaches. The paper doesn't compare to more sophisticated CR adaptation methods for Wikipedia (e.g., domain adaptation techniques), making the performance gains appear artificially large. The claim that "our classifier drastically outperforms fair baselines" is misleading when the baselines are so weak.

4. **Lack of error analysis beyond examples**: The paper provides a few error examples (Figure 3) but lacks systematic error analysis. For instance, it doesn't analyze whether errors are more common in certain article types (people vs. organizations), mention types (pronouns vs. noun phrases), or specific features. This limits the insights into the model's strengths and weaknesses.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would the performance of your classifier scale to a larger Wikipedia dataset (e.g., 1,000+ articles)? Would the same feature set remain effective, or would additional features be needed?

2. The paper demonstrates improvement on the MC resolution task and shows benefits for full CR when integrated into Dcoref. However, it doesn't demonstrate how this approach would benefit other Wikipedia-based NLP tasks (e.g., knowledge base population, question answering). What is the broader impact of this work beyond the specific MC resolution task?

3. The authors note that "statistical systems can be trained or adapted to the WCR dataset" but don't explore this. Given the small dataset size, would domain adaptation techniques (e.g., transfer learning) from news to Wikipedia be more effective than the proposed approach, and how would it compare?

This paper identifies a valuable niche in NLP research and provides a well-executed solution for a specific task. However, the limited dataset size and narrow task definition significantly constrain the broader impact of the work. With a larger dataset and more comprehensive comparison to relevant adaptation techniques, this could become a stronger contribution to the field. As it stands, the work is interesting but requires more substantial validation to be considered a major contribution.
