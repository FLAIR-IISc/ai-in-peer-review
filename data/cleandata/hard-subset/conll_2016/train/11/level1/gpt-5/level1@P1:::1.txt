Summary
This paper revisits “main concept” (MC) resolution in Wikipedia: given an article, identify all mentions (proper names, nominals, pronouns) that refer to the article’s main concept. The authors construct a small OntoNotes-style Wikipedia dataset (WCR), design two linear SVM classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions), and leverage Wikipedia markup and Freebase-derived attributes to create features that compare each mention to the article’s MC (title, redirects/aliases, inferred and KB types, gender/number, etc.). They show large gains over baselines derived from state-of-the-art CR systems (Dcoref, Scoref) and further demonstrate a 4-point absolute CoNLL F1 improvement when integrating their MC classifier as an early sieve in Dcoref.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A targeted, high-accuracy MC-resolution classifier for Wikipedia that exploits human-curated links and KB attributes, substantially outperforming baselines derived from generic CR systems (≈+13–15 F1 on the MC task; ≈+4 CoNLL F1 when plugged into Dcoref).
2) A small, annotated Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR) and a testbed for evaluating MC resolution, along with ablations that illuminate which Wikipedia/KB-derived features matter.
3) A simple method to inject MC decisions into a full CR pipeline (Dcoref sieve), yielding consistent gains on Wikipedia texts.

Strengths
- Clear task definition with practical impact on Wikipedia-centric NLP: MC identification is directly useful for OIE, IE, and entity-centric analytics. Wikipedia’s genre differs markedly from news; results that highlight systematic failures of news-trained CR (Table 1) and demonstrate focused, genre-aware gains are valuable.
- Effective exploitation of Wikipedia structure and KB attributes: Using internal links, redirects, aliases, and Freebase types as high-precision surrogates for named entity linking sidesteps typical linker recall/precision trade-offs and cascading errors. The ablations substantiate that title/variants/inferred types are the main drivers, with additional boosts from MC coarse attributes and positional/tag features (Table 2 discussion).
- Strong empirical improvements on both the targeted task and full CR: On the MC task, F1 ≈88.8 vs 73–76 for baselines; for pronominal mentions, F1 ≈89 vs 77–84 for baselines, indicating that the approach handles the hardest class well. When integrated into Dcoref (Dcoref++), CoNLL F1 increases from 51.77 to 55.11 on WCR, with consistent improvements across MUC/B3/CEAF.
- Sensible engineering and analysis: Separate models for pronouns vs non-pronouns; careful feature families; inclusion of normalized frequency and positional cues that are well-motivated for Wikipedia’s discourse structure; thoughtful baseline construction (B1–B4, with pronoun recall heuristic).
- Reproducibility intent: The authors indicate plans to release the WCR dataset, features, and large-scale outputs, which would catalyze further work on Wikipedia CR.

Weaknesses
- Evaluation methodology may inflate performance: The 10-fold cross-validation split is not specified as document-level. If folds mix mentions from the same article, the model can see labeled mentions from the test article during training, which is problematic because MC-specific features (title, variants, inferred type) are article-bound and could encourage overfitting to article idiosyncrasies. A leave-one-document-out or document-level CV would be more appropriate.
- Small dataset and limited diversity: WCR contains only 30 documents (~60k tokens), with 1,800 MC mentions. Claims of representativeness are not sufficiently substantiated. The full-CR gains (4 CoNLL F1) are reported only on WCR; broader validation (more articles, different topics, longer pages) is needed to support generality.
- Baselines may be too weak relative to strong “direct match” heuristics: The paper provides four baselines derived from CR outputs and simple aggregations, plus a pronoun heuristic. However, a competitive and natural baseline for this task is a purely deterministic MC resolver: aggregate all mentions matching title/redirects/aliases/first-sentence head type, plus rule-based pronominal attachment based on gender/number and local salience. As the best features are string-based and KB-driven, such a baseline could close much of the gap; it is not reported.
- Dependency on deprecated / potentially sparse KBs: Freebase is discontinued; coverage for attributes (gender, number, notable types) may be uneven. The paper does not provide coverage statistics (how often Freebase fields are present, or the proportion of mentions that can be linked via Wikipedia hyperlinks). The robustness of the approach without KB attributes (or with Wikidata as a replacement) remains unclear.
- Incomplete reporting and clarity issues: The text references a Table 3 for non-pronominal ablations but only presents Table 2 and Table 4/5 in the provided excerpt. More thorough error analysis is needed, especially on confusions like organization vs location (e.g., “Houston” city vs “Houston Texans” team), and on cases where redirects introduce noise.

Questions to Authors
- How were the CV folds constructed? Were they document-level (i.e., no article appears in both train and test folds), or were mentions randomly split across folds? Please report performance under leave-one-document-out CV.
- What is the coverage of the Wikipedia/Freebase attributes you rely on (percent of mentions with link-derived attributes; percent of MCs with Freebase gender/number/type)? How does performance degrade if KB-derived attributes are ablated, or if only Wikipedia markup (no Freebase) is used?
- Can you report results for a stronger deterministic baseline that uses: (a) exact/partial match to title, redirects, and aliases; (b) inferred MC head type from the first sentence; and (c) simple pronoun rules (gender/number compatibility and nearest compatible named entity preference)?
- How is the “inferred type” extracted from the first sentence; how accurate is it, and what is its coverage? Please provide precision/recall of inferred types versus Freebase types.
- For Dcoref++, what fraction of errors are due to preventing later sieves from adding mentions to the MC chain? Would relaxing this constraint help recall without hurting precision?

Additional comments
- Consider replacing Freebase with Wikidata and reporting coverage and performance parity.
- Please provide inter-annotator agreement for WCR and more detail on the annotation protocol (e.g., treatment of appositives, copular, attribution).
- A topic-wise breakdown (persons/orgs/locations/events) and macro-averaged scores across articles would increase confidence in robustness.
