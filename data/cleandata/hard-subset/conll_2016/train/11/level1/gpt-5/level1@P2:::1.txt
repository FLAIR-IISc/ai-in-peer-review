Summary and contributions
This paper targets “main concept resolution” (MCR) in Wikipedia: identifying all mentions in an article that refer to the article’s main concept (MC). The authors build a feature-based classifier that exploits Wikipedia markup (titles, redirects, internal links) and Freebase attributes, with separate models for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. They also introduce a small Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR) and show that integrating their MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref improves full coreference on Wikipedia.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A simple, effective MCR classifier leveraging Wikipedia/Freebase signals that substantially outperforms strong coreference-derived baselines (up to +13–15 F1 over adapted Dcoref/Scoref heuristics; ~89 F1 overall).
2) Evidence that adding an MCR sieve improves end-to-end Wikipedia coreference (Dcoref++: +4 CoNLL F1 on WCR), highlighting the utility of early, high-precision MC decisions in a pipeline.
3) A small, curated Wikipedia coreference testbed (30 documents; ~60k tokens) and analysis showing that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on OntoNotes degrade sharply on Wikipedia, underscoring domain mismatch.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains with clear diagnostics:
  The proposed classifier yields large, consistent improvements across pronouns and non-pronouns over thoughtful baselines constructed from two SOTA CR systems (rule-based Dcoref and learning-based Scoref) with several heuristics (longest-chain, title-aware aggregation, pronoun boosting). The absolute gains (≈13–15 F1) are meaningful for practical Wikipedia IE pipelines. Integration as a first-pass sieve into Dcoref yields +4 CoNLL F1, indicating pipeline-level benefits beyond the targeted subtask.

- Clever exploitation of Wikipedia markup and Freebase:
  Avoiding full NEL, the method uses human-curated internal links, redirects, and Freebase attributes to derive unambiguous cues (title, variants, entity types, coarse attributes). This is a judicious design choice that increases precision and reduces error propagation typical of NEL pipelines. The ablation studies convincingly show which families of features contribute (e.g., title and inferred type for non-pronominals; position and distance for pronouns).

- Problem relevance and clear motivation:
  The paper demonstrates that top CR systems suffer notable performance drops on Wikipedia (e.g., Scoref CoNLL F1 from 63.6 to 46.4), making a focused adaptation worthwhile. MCR is a practically useful subtask for Wikipedia-centric IE and summarization and a stepping stone to better full CR.

- Methodical evaluation and transparency of engineering:
  The use of predicted mentions (plus added singletons) mirrors realistic pipelines, and the feature definitions are described at a workable level. The choice of linear SVMs with class weighting is sensible for high-dimensional sparse features, and the 10-fold document-level evaluation (assumed) mitigates overfitting at the mention level.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and generalization:
  WCR contains only 30 documents (~7k non-singleton mentions; ~1.8k MC mentions). While the per-mention counts are reasonable, the document count is small for cross-validation and may not capture the breadth of Wikipedia styles and domains. The large reported gains could be sensitive to this sampling; no results are shown on a held-out set of different articles or larger-scale evaluation using distant supervision (e.g., against automatically constructed silver labels).

- Baselines and scope of comparisons:
  The baselines, although thoughtful, derive from CR pipelines plus simple chain-aggregation heuristics. Missing are strong string/feature-based learning baselines tailored to MCR (e.g., logistic regression using only title/variant/type overlaps) or a named-entity linking-based baseline (e.g., wikification + simple MC matching), which would better isolate the value of the proposed feature families versus off-the-shelf solutions. It’s unclear how far the gains stem from Wikipedia-specific cues (title/redirects) that a dedicated heuristic baseline could capture.

- Dependence on Freebase and link density:
  The approach uses Freebase (now deprecated) and relies on the presence and accuracy of internal links and redirects. The paper does not quantify how often mentions receive Freebase-derived attributes or how performance degrades in articles with sparse linking, long disambiguation sections, or noisy redirects. This raises concerns about robustness across Wikipedia articles and portability beyond Wikipedia or to other languages.

- Ambiguities in link-to-mention attribute assignment:
  The preprocessing assigns attributes to any mention whose surface string matches that of any internal link elsewhere in the article. This can conflate homonyms or overgeneralize attributes across contexts where the same surface form links to different pages. The paper lacks an error analysis quantifying how often such propagation is wrong and its impact on precision.

- Reproducibility and clarity gaps:
  Some implementation details are under-specified: how Wu & Palmer similarity is computed for proper names, exact definitions of the 10 string-similarity booleans, the split protocol (document-level CV?), and sensitivity to the class-weight hyperparameter. The dataset is anonymized at review time, with release “promised,” which limits immediate reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Evaluation protocol: Are the 10 folds split at the document level (i.e., no sentences from the same article appear in both train and test)? Please clarify to avoid leakage via MC metadata.
- Attribute propagation: When the same surface form anchors links to different Wikipedia pages within an article, how do you resolve conflicting attributes? Did you consider frequency- or context-based tie-breaking?
- Dependency on external knowledge: What fraction of mentions (pronominal and non-pronominal) receive Freebase-derived attributes? Please report performance with Freebase features ablated and on low-link-density articles.
- Robustness by entity type: Can you break down MCR performance by MC type (PER/ORG/LOC/other) and by article structure (e.g., biographies vs organizations)?
- Baselines: How would a learned baseline using only simple string features (title/variants/type overlaps) perform? This would help calibrate the incremental value of the richer feature families.
- Sieve interaction: In Dcoref++, you prevent later sieves from appending mentions to the MC chain. Did you observe cases where this harms recall for the MC chain or induces errors for other chains? Any trade-off analysis?

Overall assessment
A well-motivated, practically effective contribution that exploits Wikipedia-specific signals to deliver sizable gains on a focused but impactful subtask, with promising downstream effects. The main reservations concern dataset scale, robustness, and missing competitive baselines. If the dataset and code are released and the authors can clarify robustness and protocol, I lean toward acceptance.
