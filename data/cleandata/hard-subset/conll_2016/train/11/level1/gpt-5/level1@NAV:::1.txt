Summary
The paper revisits a focused subtask of coreference in Wikipedia: identifying all mentions that refer to an article’s main concept (MC). The authors construct a feature-based SVM classifier (separate models for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) leveraging Wikipedia markup (titles, redirects, internal links) and Freebase attributes, and compare against heuristics built on top of state-of-the-art CR systems. They additionally insert their MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref, showing gains on full-document CR.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A task formulation and testbed (WCR) for Main Concept Resolution in Wikipedia, with evaluation protocol and baselines.
2) A simple, high-precision MC mention classifier exploiting Wikipedia/Freebase signals that substantially outperforms adapted CR baselines on MC identification.
3) Evidence that injecting MC decisions into a coreference pipeline (Dcoref++) yields a notable CoNLL F1 improvement on Wikipedia.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus with practical utility: MC resolution is a high-impact, Wikipedia-specific capability that benefits downstream IE/OIE and entity-centric processing. The paper convincingly motivates genre mismatch and quantifies large drops of news-trained CR systems on WCR.
- Strong empirical gains on the target task: The classifier achieves ~89 F1 on MC identification, outperforming Dcoref/Scoref-derived baselines by 13–15 F1. The ablation shows well-reasoned contributions from markup-derived features (title, inferred type, variants).
- Pipeline benefit demonstrated: Integrating the classifier as a leading sieve in Dcoref improves CoNLL F1 by 4 points, indicating the MC signal is both high-precision and useful for subsequent sieves.
- Sensible exploitation of Wikipedia structure: Bypassing noisy entity linking and instead using human-curated links/redirects is a robust design choice that explains the strong precision, especially for non-pronominal mentions.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and scope: WCR has only 30 documents (60k tokens). While diverse, this limits statistical confidence and may overstate generalization across Wikipedia’s domains and link densities.
- Potential cross-fold leakage: The 10-fold CV appears to operate at the mention level; it is unclear if folds are split by document. Mixing mentions from the same article across train/test would inflate scores.
- External KB dependence and aging: Heavy reliance on Freebase (now deprecated) raises concerns about reproducibility and future applicability; coverage gaps or noisy redirects already manifest in error analysis.
- Baseline strength and contemporaneity: Baselines are heuristics atop older CR systems; no comparison to stronger modern neural CR/domain adaptation or to a supervised MC classifier without KB features is provided.

Questions to Authors
- Is cross-validation document-level (i.e., no article appears in both train and test)? If not, can you report doc-level CV results?
- How sensitive are results to removal of Freebase features? Can Wikidata substitute Freebase without loss?
- What is the failure profile by page type (biographies vs organizations vs locations) and by link density? Can you report per-category metrics?
- Can you release the WCR dataset and code at review time (anonymized) to substantiate reproducibility claims?
