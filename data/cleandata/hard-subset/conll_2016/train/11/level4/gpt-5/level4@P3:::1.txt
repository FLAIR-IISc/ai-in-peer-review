Summary
This paper revisits coreference resolution for Wikipedia by focusing on “main concept (MC) resolution”: identifying all mentions in a Wikipedia article that refer to the article’s primary entity. The authors construct a small Wikipedia coreference dataset (30 articles, ~60k tokens), show that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on newswire degrade substantially on this domain, and propose a binary classifier (separate models for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) that leverages Wikipedia markup and Freebase attributes to determine whether a mention refers to the MC. The classifier outperforms baselines derived from Dcoref and Scoref, and its integration as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref yields notable gains on end-to-end coreference in Wikipedia.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A clearly defined subtask—Main Concept Resolution—together with a testbed (WCR) and evaluation protocol demonstrating that this is a practically relevant and challenging slice of coreference for Wikipedia.
2) A simple, effective classifier for MC resolution that exploits Wikipedia links, redirects, and Freebase attributes, achieving large gains over reasonable baselines and over outputs from strong CR systems.
3) A successful integration of the MC classifier as an early sieve in a full CR pipeline (Dcoref), producing an approximately 4-point CoNLL F1 improvement on Wikipedia.

Strengths
- Strong motivation and clear task definition. Main concept resolution is well-justified for Wikipedia and for IE scenarios that center on a single entity per document (e.g., biographies, organization pages). The paper convincingly shows that general CR systems underperform on this domain (e.g., Scoref drops from 63.61 to 46.39 CoNLL F1), motivating domain-specific approaches.

- Effective use of Wikipedia structure and external knowledge. The approach avoids noisy end-to-end entity linking by leveraging human-curated Wikipedia markup (internal links, redirects) and Freebase attributes to enrich mention and MC representations. This yields robust, high-precision cues (e.g., aliases, inferred types) that are particularly suitable for Wikipedia.

- Substantial empirical improvements on the target task. The MC classifier reaches about 89 F1 (10-fold CV) and exceeds baselines derived from Dcoref/Scoref by 13–15 absolute F1 points on all mentions. The feature ablations for pronouns are informative, showing steady gains from base to +main to +position/+tag/+distance.

- Demonstrable impact on full CR. Integrating the MC classifier as a high-precision sieve improves Dcoref by ~4 CoNLL F1 points on WCR, with precision gains spilling over to later sieves. This shows practical utility beyond the standalone MC task.

- Potential to bridge CR and EL research. By focusing on MC resolution in Wikipedia and drawing on structured knowledge, the work creates a testbed that could foster cross-pollination between coreference and entity linking communities.

Weaknesses
- Small dataset and limited diversity. WCR contains only 30 documents. While the authors sampled across topics and sizes, the dataset is too small to fully characterize generalization across Wikipedia styles and entity types. Statistical conclusions, especially for feature ablations and pronominal phenomena, may be brittle.

- Baselines could be stronger. The baselines primarily re-interpret chains from existing CR systems via simple heuristics (longest chain, title match, aggregation, pronoun sweeping). Additional learning-based baselines that use the same features but different architectures, or stronger chaining heuristics with oracle title/type constraints, would better contextualize the gains.

- Clarity and framing of the model. The paper delays stating that MC resolution is framed as a binary classification problem with separate pronominal and non-pronominal SVMs; this should be explicit earlier. Some terminology is also unclear (e.g., “candidate list”), and Section 6.1’s description of mention sets appears to conflict with Section 4.1.

- Reliance on Wikipedia/Freebase specifics. The approach leans on high-quality internal links and Freebase attributes. This is a strength for Wikipedia but may limit portability to other corpora or to Wikipedia pages with sparse linking. The paper notes noisy redirects can hurt precision; more analysis of robustness to such noise would help.

- Limited analysis of negative cases and feature interactions. The error analysis is brief, and the non-pronominal ablation is summarized at a high level. Given the centrality of title, type, and alias features, more granular ablation (and learning curve analysis with respect to article link density) would strengthen claims.

Questions to Authors
- Data/mention set: In Section 6.1, you “consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by Raghunathan et al. (2010).” Section 4.1 seems to imply mention detection follows that method. Please clarify precisely how gold vs. predicted mentions are combined for training/testing, and whether evaluation is on predicted mentions only. How do you avoid leakage across folds (e.g., ensure folds are split by article)?

- Candidate list: What exactly is the “candidate list” when enriching mentions via hyperlinks? Is it the set of extracted mentions within the article that match a link’s surface form? How do you handle multiple links with the same surface, and unlinked occurrences?

- Robustness to sparse/noisy links: How does performance vary with link density? Do pages with few internal links degrade substantially? Can you quantify the impact of noisy redirects/aliases on precision?

- Feature ablations: You provide a detailed ablation for pronouns. Can you include a comparable ablation table for non-pronominal mentions to substantiate the incremental contributions of title, inferred type, name variants, entity type, and main feature families?

- Integration details: In Dcoref++, once the MC chain is formed, you prevent later sieves from appending mentions to it. Did you explore allowing later sieves to add with a high-confidence threshold? How sensitive are results to the sieve’s position?

Additional comments (minor)
- Organization: Consider introducing the dataset immediately after Related Work to ground the Baselines and Approach sections.

- Clarity: State early that MC resolution is cast as binary classification with two SVMs (pronominal/non-pronominal). Define “candidate list” precisely.

- Citations/naming: When first mentioning Dcoref and Scoref, explicitly tie them to the Stanford systems and cite the corresponding papers.

- Terminology: When describing hyperlink-based enrichment, be explicit that link targets supply gender/number/type via Freebase; clarify behavior when links are absent or ambiguous.

- Reproducibility: The commitment to release data and outputs is valuable. Please include splits and exact preprocessing versions (e.g., CoreNLP version, Wikipedia dump date, Freebase snapshot), and confirm folds are by document.
