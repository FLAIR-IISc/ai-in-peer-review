The authors present a new variant of the coreference task tailored to Wikipedia: identify the coreference chain corresponding to the article’s main entity. They annotate 30 documents with all coreference chains; roughly 25% of mentions refer to the article’s “main concept” (MC). They propose simple baselines and a basic classifier that outperforms them, and they integrate this classifier into the Stanford rule-based coreference system, yielding substantial gains over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia. I find this task compelling from an information extraction (IE) perspective. It has the potential to reinvigorate coreference research and to bridge coreference and entity linking.

I am often skeptical of papers that define a new task where standard systems perform poorly and then add a tweak so their system looks good. Here, however, the task itself is well motivated: standard systems perform poorly in a setting that actually matters.

THE TASK: Main concept resolution is an intriguing IE task. Many documents center on a particular entity (e.g., biographies, dossiers or briefings about a person or event, clinical records), and the information we care about extracting is specific to that entity. The standard coreference task includes many mentions—such as generic expressions—that are largely irrelevant to most IE scenarios; by contrast, this task focuses on mentions that do matter.

Methodologically, the notion of a “main concept” provides a discourse anchor that should help coreference, though there still appears to be significant headroom beyond the baselines, especially on non-pronominal mentions. Working directly on Wikipedia also invites richer use of background knowledge, which the authors begin to exploit. I expect this domain to serve as a useful testbed for ideas that can improve coreference overall but are hard to demonstrate robustly in the general setting, where gains can be swamped by other aspects of the problem. Unlike prior work that carves off narrow slices of coreference (e.g., Winograd schemas), this paper yields sizable improvements on standard coreference metrics in a domain—Wikipedia—of broad interest to the ACL community.

THE TECHNIQUES: The techniques are not the main contribution, though they are effective. The features are sensible; additional feature conjunctions might further help, and it is unclear whether the authors experimented with them. Please also state earlier that the primary MC-resolution module is a binary classifier; this is not made explicit soon enough, leaving the model unspecified during the featurization description.

MINOR DETAILS:
- Organization: I suggest introducing the dataset immediately after Related Work (as a new Section 3), so that concrete results can be reported in Baselines to better motivate the Approach.
- When Section 4 refers to Dcoref and Scoref, please cite the Stanford coreference papers or explicitly say you are using the Stanford coreference system; many readers will not recognize the Dcoref/Scoref names.
- Clarify “candidate list,” especially in the following passage: “We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link.” Make clear that the candidate list is the set of mentions in the article that are possible candidates for being coreferent with the MC. Most readers will infer that this module imports semantic information from Wikipedia’s link structure (e.g., if a mention is hyperlinked to an article whose Freebase entry is female, then the mention is female), so keeping the terminology precise will help.
- Section 6.1 says “we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010).” However, Section 4.1 implies these are the same. Where do additional WCR mentions come from? Please clarify.
