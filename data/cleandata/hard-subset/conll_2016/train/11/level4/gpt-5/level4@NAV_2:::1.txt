The authors introduce a variant of coreference tailored to Wikipedia: identifying the coreference chain corresponding to the entity that the article is about. They annotate 30 articles with full coreference, with roughly a quarter of mentions referring to the “main concept” (MC). They propose simple baselines and a straightforward classifier that surpasses them, and further embed this classifier into the Stanford deterministic coreference system, yielding notable gains over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia. I find the task compelling from an information extraction (IE) standpoint, with the potential to re-energize coreference research and to connect coreference methods with entity linking. I am often skeptical of “new-task-then-tweak” papers where standard systems fare poorly and a small modification wins; here, however, the task itself is well-motivated, and the paper convincingly shows that current systems underperform in a domain that actually matters.

THE TASK: Main concept resolution is a natural and useful IE problem. Many documents are centered on a single entity (e.g., biographies, briefings about a person or event, clinical narratives), and the content we want to extract concerns that entity. Traditional coreference includes many mentions that are of limited value for most IE scenarios (e.g., generic mentions), whereas this task focuses on mentions that are clearly relevant. Methodologically, the “main concept” supplies a discourse anchor that helps coreference, though there remains significant headroom over baselines, especially for non-pronominal mentions. Working directly on Wikipedia enables richer use of background knowledge, which the paper demonstrates. As a result, this domain looks like a strong testbed for ideas that could improve coreference broadly, but that are harder to validate in the general case where other challenges dominate. Unlike prior work that slices off a narrow facet of coreference (e.g., Winograd schemas), this paper improves end-to-end coreference metrics on Wikipedia, a domain of wide interest to the ACL community.

THE TECHNIQUES: The methods are not the main novelty but are effective. The feature design is sensible; exploring additional feature conjunctions could plausibly help, and it is unclear whether such ablations were attempted. It would also help to state earlier that the MC resolver is a binary classifier; this is not clearly specified up front, leaving the model underspecified during the featureization section.

MINOR DETAILS:
- Organization: Consider introducing the dataset immediately after Related Work (as a new Section 3) so that concrete statistics can be referenced in Baselines, further motivating the Approach.
- When Section 4 references Dcoref and Scoref, please cite the Stanford papers or explicitly note that these are the Stanford systems, as many readers will not recognize these nicknames.
- The term “candidate list” is ambiguous, particularly here: “We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article... we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link.” Please clarify that the “candidate list” is the set of mentions in the article that could be coreferent with the MC. Most readers will infer that this module imports semantic attributes via Wikipedia links (e.g., if a mention links to a Freebase entry marked female, the mention is female), so keeping the terminology precise would help.
- Section 6.1 states “we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010),” but Section 4.1 suggests these are the same. Where do the additional WCR mentions originate?
