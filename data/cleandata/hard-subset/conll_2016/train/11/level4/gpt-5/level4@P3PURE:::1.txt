This paper introduces a new variant of the coreference task tailored to Wikipedia: identifying the coreference chain corresponding to the entity the article is about (the “main concept,” MC). The authors annotate 30 Wikipedia articles with complete coreference chains, finding that roughly 25% of all mentions refer to the MC. They present simple baselines and a basic classifier that outperforms them, and they integrate this classifier into the Stanford rule-based coreference system, achieving substantial gains over all state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia. I find this task compelling from an information extraction (IE) perspective, with real potential to reinvigorate coreference research and to bridge coreference and entity linking in a meaningful way. While I am often skeptical of papers that define a new task where standard systems perform poorly and then introduce a tweak that helps, here the task itself is well motivated and highlights an important, realistic setting in which standard systems underperform.

THE TASK: Main concept resolution is an intriguing and practically useful formulation. Many documents focus primarily on a particular entity (e.g., biographies, dossiers, event briefings, clinical records), and the information we care about extracting is often specific to that entity. Standard coreference evaluations include many mentions (e.g., generic mentions) that are largely irrelevant for most IE applications, whereas this task targets mentions that directly matter. Methodologically, the “main concept” provides a discourse anchor that can aid coreference, though there still appears to be substantial work required to improve beyond the baselines, especially for non-pronominal mentions. Doing coreference directly on Wikipedia also enables richer use of background knowledge, which the authors illustrate. As such, this domain looks like a strong testbed for techniques that might improve coreference more broadly but are harder to make robust in the general setting and are often overshadowed by other aspects of the problem. Unlike prior efforts that carve off a narrow slice of coreference (e.g., Winograd schemas), this work shows sizable improvements on overall coreference metrics in a domain (Wikipedia) that is of significant interest to the ACL community.

THE TECHNIQUES: The methods are not the primary novelty of the paper, but they appear effective. The features are sensible; additional feature conjunctions might further help, and it is unclear whether the authors experimented along these lines. The paper should also state earlier that the primary MC-resolution model is a binary classifier. This is not made explicit early on, leaving the core model ambiguous during the feature description.

MINOR DETAILS AND CLARIFICATIONS:
- Organization: Consider introducing the dataset immediately after Related Work (as a new Section 3). Presenting concrete dataset details first would better ground the Baselines and motivate the Approach.
- Terminology and citations: When referring to Dcoref and Scoref in Section 4, please cite the Stanford coreference papers or explicitly state that these are components of the Stanford coreference system; many readers may not recognize the Dcoref/Scoref names.
- “Candidate list” clarity: The term “candidate list” is unclear in the passage: “We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link.” Please clarify that the “candidate list” is the set of mentions in the article that are considered possible candidates for being coreferent with the MC. Most readers will understand that this module imports semantic information from Wikipedia’s link structure (e.g., if a mention is hyperlinked to an article whose Freebase entry is female, then that mention is female), so keeping the terminology precise will help.
- Section cross-references: Section 6.1 says “we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010).” However, Section 4.1 seems to imply these are the same. Where do additional WCR mentions come from beyond those predicted by Raghunathan et al. (2010)? Please clarify this discrepancy.
