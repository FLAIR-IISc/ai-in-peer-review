Summary and claimed contributions
This paper defines and studies a variant of coreference tailored to Wikipedia: identifying all mentions that refer to the article’s main concept (MC). The authors annotate 30 Wikipedia documents with coreference chains (about 25% of mentions refer to the MC), propose simple baselines, and develop a binary classifier that substantially outperforms these baselines. They also integrate their classifier into the Stanford deterministic coreference system (Dcoref), yielding strong gains over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A focused task formulation and testbed for “main concept” resolution in Wikipedia, showing that off-the-shelf coreference systems degrade substantially on this domain.
2) A simple but effective binary classifier leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase features that clearly outperforms adapted baselines for MC mention identification.
3) Demonstration that incorporating MC decisions into a full coreference pipeline (Dcoref++) improves end-to-end coreference performance on Wikipedia.

Strengths
- Strong, practical problem motivation. MC resolution is a compelling IE task: many documents (e.g., biographies, dossiers, clinical records) are centered on a single entity, and MC mentions are the ones most relevant to downstream extraction. Unlike carving off a narrow phenomenon (e.g., Winograd schemas), this work improves overall coreference metrics in a domain of broad interest to ACL/NLP.

- Clear empirical impact and competitive results. The classifier yields large gains over baselines for both pronominal and non-pronominal mentions (e.g., ~89 F1 overall; +13–15 F1 over adapted Dcoref/Scoref baselines). When injected as a high-precision sieve, Dcoref++ improves CoNLL F1 by about 4 absolute points over strong systems on the WCR dataset. These are substantial, practical improvements.

- Sensible and effective use of knowledge. The approach leverages human-curated Wikipedia structure and Freebase attributes to avoid noisy entity linking, reducing ambiguity while remaining simple to implement. The feature design is intuitive and well aligned with the domain.

- Potential to bridge communities and spur progress. The task provides a discourse anchor that encourages principled use of knowledge in coreference, potentially linking coreference and entity linking literatures. The negative results for news-trained systems on Wikipedia underscore the need for domain-aware methods and make WCR a valuable testbed.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The core method is a straightforward binary classifier with largely standard features. This is not a drawback for impact, but the technical contribution is modest. The paper would benefit from more systematic exploration of feature conjunctions and interactions; it is unclear whether such experiments were attempted.

- Clarity about the model and pipeline. The paper should state early and explicitly that MC resolution is formulated as a binary classification problem; currently, the model is not clearly defined at the point where features are introduced. Likewise, when referring to Dcoref and Scoref, please cite and/or explicitly note these are Stanford coreference systems to avoid confusion.

- Dataset size and generality. WCR comprises only 30 documents. While diverse, this is small, and it limits the strength of conclusions about generalization across Wikipedia topics or beyond Wikipedia. The paper does a nice job showing degradation of news-trained systems, but further validation (or a path to scaling annotation) would strengthen the case.

- Terminology and data-processing clarity. The term “candidate list” is ambiguous in Section 4.1. It appears to be the set of extracted mentions in the article that could be coreferent with the MC, which are then enriched via hyperlink-derived attributes. This should be stated plainly. Relatedly, Section 6.1 says the system uses the union of WCR mentions and mentions predicted by Raghunathan et al. (2010), whereas Section 4.1 could be read as implying these are the same. Please clarify where additional mentions beyond WCR annotations are coming from.

Questions to authors
- Please state early (e.g., in the Introduction or Approach overview) that MC resolution is cast as a binary classification problem over detected mentions, with separate models for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions.

- Can you clarify the “candidate list” terminology? Is it simply the set of mentions detected by the Raghunathan et al. (2010) pipeline (plus WCR mentions), which you then enrich using Wikipedia links and Freebase attributes?

- In Section 6.1, how exactly does the “union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by Raghunathan et al. (2010)” differ from what is described in Section 4.1? Where do additional mentions not present in WCR come from, and how many are added per document on average?

- Did you experiment with feature conjunctions/interactions (e.g., conjunctions between MC attributes and positional or tag features)? If so, what was their effect; if not, is there a reason (e.g., overfitting, sparsity) they were avoided?

- When referring to Dcoref and Scoref, please add explicit citations and note they are Stanford systems. Also, a brief description of how the new sieve integrates into Dcoref (and prevents later sieves from modifying the MC chain) would help readers reproduce the setup.

Overall evaluation
An interesting and well-motivated task with clear empirical value and potential to catalyze progress at the intersection of coreference and entity linking. The techniques are simple but effective; with improved clarity and minor methodological exploration, this paper would make a strong contribution.
