The authors introduce a new variant of the coreference task tailored to Wikipedia: identifying the coreference chain corresponding to the entity that the article is about (the “main concept”). They annotate 30 documents with all coreference chains, finding that about 25% of mentions refer to the main concept. They present simple baselines and a basic classifier that outperforms them. They also integrate this classifier into the Stanford rule-based coreference system and report substantial gains over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia. I find the task compelling from an information extraction standpoint; it has the potential to reinvigorate coreference research and to bridge coreference and entity linking. I am often skeptical of papers that invent a new task on which standard systems underperform and then add a tweak to win on that task; here, however, the task feels genuinely important, and the poor performance of standard systems seems like a meaningful gap rather than goalpost shifting.

THE TASK: Main concept resolution is an appealing problem for IE. Many documents center on a single entity (e.g., biographies, dossiers or briefings about a person or event, clinical records), and the information we care about is specific to that entity. Traditional coreference includes many mentions (such as generic ones) that are largely irrelevant to most IE applications, whereas this task focuses on mentions that truly matter. Methodologically, the “main concept” offers a useful discourse anchor for coreference, yet there still appears to be significant work required to surpass baselines, especially for non-pronominal mentions. Operating directly on Wikipedia invites richer use of external knowledge, which the authors illustrate. This domain should serve as a productive testbed for techniques that could improve coreference more broadly—techniques that might be harder to make robust, or whose impact might be overshadowed, in the general setting. Unlike prior efforts that carve off narrow subproblems (e.g., Winograd schemas), this paper moves the needle on overall coreference metrics in a domain—Wikipedia—that many in the ACL community care about.

THE TECHNIQUES: The methods are not the paper’s strongest contribution, though they appear effective. The features are sensible; additional feature conjunctions might help, and it is unclear whether the authors experimented with them. The paper should also state earlier that the primary main concept resolution system is a binary classifier; this is not made explicit soon enough, leaving the model underspecified during the feature description.

MINOR DETAILS:
- Organization: Consider introducing the dataset immediately after Related Work (as the new Section 3), so that Baselines can reference concrete results and better motivate the Approach.
- When Section 4 refers to Dcoref and Scoref, please cite the Stanford coreference papers or state explicitly that these are the Stanford coreference systems, since many readers may not recognize the Dcoref/Scoref names.
- The term “candidate list” is unclear, especially in the passage: “We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link.” Please clarify that the “candidate list” is the set of mentions in the article that are potential candidates for being coreferent with the main concept. Most readers will understand that this module imports semantic information from Wikipedia’s link structure (e.g., if a mention links to an article labeled as female in Freebase, that mention is female), so keep the terminology precise.
- Section 6.1 states that “we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010).” However, Section 4.1 seems to imply these are the same. It is unclear where additional WCR mentions would come from; please clarify this discrepancy.
