- Paper defines "main concept" (MC) resolution for Wikipedia, annotates 30 documents, proposes baselines, builds a binary classifier that outperforms them, and integrates it into Dcoref for strong gains.

- Contribution 1: Presents a focused task and testbed showing off-the-shelf coreference systems degrade on Wikipedia.

- Contribution 2: Proposes a simple binary classifier using Wikipedia markup and Freebase features that outperforms adapted baselines for MC identification.

- Contribution 3: Demonstrates that injecting MC decisions into a coreference pipeline (Dcoref++) improves end-to-end performance on Wikipedia.

- Strength: Strong, practical motivation—MC resolution is relevant for many single-entity-centered documents and useful for downstream extraction.

- Strength: Clear empirical impact—large F1 gains (~89 F1; +13–15 over baselines) and ~4-point CoNLL F1 improvement when used as a high-precision sieve.

- Strength: Sensible, effective use of human-curated Wikipedia structure and Freebase to reduce ambiguity with intuitive feature design.

- Strength: Potential to bridge coreference and entity-linking communities and provide a valuable domain-aware testbed (WCR).

- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—the core approach is a straightforward binary classifier with mostly standard features.

- Weakness: Need for clarity about the model/pipeline—MC resolution as binary classification should be stated earlier and Dcoref/Scoref should be identified as Stanford systems.

- Weakness: Small dataset—WCR has only 30 documents, limiting claims about generalization and scaling.

- Weakness: Ambiguous terminology and data-processing (e.g., the meaning of "candidate list" and how additional mentions are sourced and combined with WCR).

- Question: State early that MC resolution is cast as binary classification over detected mentions, with separate models for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions.

- Question: Clarify whether "candidate list" equals mentions from the Raghunathan et al. (2010) pipeline plus WCR mentions, enriched with links/Freebase.

- Question: Explain precisely how the "union of WCR mentions and mentions predicted by Raghunathan et al. (2010)" differs from Section 4.1 and how many extra mentions are added per document on average.

- Question: Report whether feature conjunctions/interactions were tried, and if not, why (e.g., overfitting/sparsity).

- Question: Add explicit citations for Dcoref and Scoref, note they are Stanford systems, and describe how the new sieve is integrated and protected from later sieves.

- Overall: Well-motivated, empirically strong contribution with simple but effective techniques; paper would be stronger with improved clarity, minor methodological exploration, and discussion of dataset scaling.
