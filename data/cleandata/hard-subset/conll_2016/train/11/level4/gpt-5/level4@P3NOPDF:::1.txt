This paper introduces a focused variant of coreference resolution tailored to Wikipedia: identifying the coreference chain corresponding to the article’s main concept (MC). The authors annotate 30 Wikipedia documents with full coreference, noting that about 25% of mentions refer to the MC. They propose simple baselines and a learned classifier that outperforms them, and then integrate this classifier into Stanford’s rule-based coreference system, yielding substantial gains over prior systems on Wikipedia. The task is well-motivated, the empirical results are clear, and the work nicely connects coreference with entity linking in a way that feels both natural and useful.

Significance and motivation:
- Main concept resolution is an intriguing and practically relevant task for IE. Many real-world documents revolve around a single salient entity (e.g., encyclopedia entries, biographies, dossiers, clinical notes), and the mentions that matter most are those tied to that entity. Traditional coreference evaluation often includes many mentions (e.g., generic or incidental ones) that may be irrelevant to downstream tasks; by contrast, MC mentions are precisely those likely to matter.
- The notion of an MC offers a discourse anchor that is helpful for coreference. At the same time, the results suggest there remains meaningful headroom beyond simple heuristics, especially for non-pronominal mentions.
- Doing coreference directly on Wikipedia enables leveraging structured knowledge (e.g., hyperlink targets, Freebase attributes). The paper demonstrates how to exploit this signal, and the domain seems like a strong testbed for ideas that may be hard to validate in broader, more heterogeneous settings.
- Unlike some work that carves off narrow subsets of the problem (e.g., Winograd-style pronouns), this paper shows tangible improvements on overall coreference metrics within Wikipedia—a domain of broad interest in the ACL community.
- I am generally skeptical of papers that define a new task where standard systems do poorly and then tune for it; here, however, the task feels genuinely important. The poor performance of standard systems seems to expose a substantive gap rather than a contrived one.

Technical assessment:
- The techniques are not the primary contribution, but they are effective. The feature design is sensible, and the classifier improves over reasonable baselines. The gains when integrating the MC classifier into a rule-based coreference pipeline further support its utility.
- The paper should state early that the main MC resolution component is a binary classifier (a mention-level decision of “is MC or not”). As written, the model remains underspecified during the feature description, which hampers readability.
- It would be useful to discuss whether feature conjunctions or simple interaction features were explored; the current set is intuitive, and additional interactions might yield further improvements, particularly on non-pronominal mentions.

Clarity and organization:
- Consider moving the dataset description to directly follow Related Work. Presenting the corpus and annotation details earlier would ground the baselines and better motivate the subsequent approach.
- When referring to Dcoref and Scoref, please cite the Stanford coreference papers and explicitly state that these are the Stanford systems; not all readers will recognize these names.
- The term “candidate list” needs clearer definition, especially in the context of using hyperlinks to enrich mentions with shallow semantic attributes. As I understand it, the “candidate list” is the set of detected mentions in the article that are possible candidates for being coreferent with the MC. Making this explicit will help readers follow the hyperlink-based enrichment procedure (e.g., if a mention is hyperlinked to an entity whose Freebase entry indicates female gender, then the mention is assigned female).
- There appears to be a discrepancy between Sections 4.1 and 6.1. Section 6.1 states that “we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010).” Section 4.1, however, seems to imply these mention sets coincide. Please clarify whether additional WCR mentions are added beyond those detected by the Stanford system, and if so, how these are obtained and reconciled.

Evaluation and scope:
- The empirical results convincingly show that the basic MC classifier outperforms simple baselines, and that integrating it into a rule-based coreference pipeline yields marked gains on Wikipedia. This helps establish that MC resolution is not just a repackaged task but a useful lever for improving coreference where knowledge resources are abundant.
- The discussion of error types suggests promising room for improvement, especially on non-pronominal mentions, where lexical and knowledge-based features might play a larger role. Highlighting where the classifier helps or fails most (e.g., named mentions vs. nominals vs. pronouns) would sharpen the takeaways and guide future work.

Suggestions for strengthening the paper:
- State clearly and early that the core model is a binary classifier for MC mention detection; briefly summarize the decision function, training objective, and how predictions are used downstream.
- Where possible, include a short ablation or sensitivity analysis over the feature groups (especially hyperlink-derived features) to quantify their contribution.
- Clarify terminology around “candidate list,” “WCR mentions,” and system-specific mention sets to avoid confusion about what is extracted where and by whom.
- Provide citations and explicit references when using system nicknames (Dcoref/Scoref), and ensure the reader can connect these to the Stanford coreference work.

Overall assessment:
This is a well-motivated paper that proposes a clean, useful reframing of coreference for Wikipedia. The task aligns well with practical IE needs, and the results demonstrate that a focused, knowledge-aware approach can deliver improvements, both in isolation and when integrated into an established coreference system. While the modeling itself is straightforward, the contribution lies in defining and validating a task that bridges traditional coreference and entity linking, with clear benefits for a widely used domain. With minor clarifications to the exposition and terminology and a bit more explicitness about the classifier, this will be a strong and impactful contribution.
