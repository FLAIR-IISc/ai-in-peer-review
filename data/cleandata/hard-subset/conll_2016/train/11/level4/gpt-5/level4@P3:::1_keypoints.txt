- Paper revisits coreference on Wikipedia, defining "main concept (MC) resolution" and builds a small WCR dataset (30 articles) to evaluate it.
- Authors show state-of-the-art CR systems trained on news degrade on Wikipedia and propose separate binary classifiers for pronominal and non-pronominal MC resolution using Wikipedia markup and Freebase.
- Classifier outperforms Dcoref/Scoref-derived baselines and, when used as a high-precision sieve in Dcoref, yields ~4-point CoNLL F1 gains on Wikipedia.

Contributions:
- Defines the MC resolution subtask with a testbed (WCR) and evaluation protocol for Wikipedia-specific coreference.
- Proposes a simple, effective classifier leveraging internal links, redirects, and Freebase attributes for MC detection.
- Demonstrates successful integration of the MC classifier as an early sieve in a full CR pipeline, improving end-to-end performance.

Strengths:
- Well-motivated task and clear definition targeting single-entity documents like biographies and organization pages.
- Effective exploitation of Wikipedia structure and Freebase to obtain high-precision cues (aliases, inferred types).
- Strong empirical gains on the target task (≈89 F1, +13–15 points over baselines) with informative pronoun feature ablations.
- Demonstrable positive impact when integrated into full CR (≈4 CoNLL F1 improvement), showing practical utility.
- Creates a potential bridge between coreference and entity linking research communities.

Weaknesses:
- Very small and limited dataset (30 documents), risking brittle statistical conclusions and limited generalization.
- Baselines are relatively weak/heuristic; stronger learning-based or oracle-constrained baselines would better contextualize gains.
- Model framing is unclear early on—binary classification with two SVMs is delayed and some terminology (e.g., “candidate list”) is vague.
- Heavy reliance on Wikipedia links and Freebase may limit portability and robustness to sparse/noisy linking.
- Error analysis and non-pronominal feature ablations are limited; more granular analysis and learning curves are needed.

Questions to authors:
- Clarify how gold vs. predicted mentions are combined for training/testing, whether evaluation uses predicted mentions only, and how fold splits avoid leakage (by document?).
- Precisely define the “candidate list” used for hyperlink-based enrichment and how multiple/unaligned links are handled.
- Provide analysis of robustness to link density and quantify the impact of noisy redirects/aliases on precision.
- Include detailed ablation results for non-pronominal mentions to show incremental contributions of title, inferred type, aliases, and other features.
- In integration, did you try allowing later sieves to add to the MC chain with high-confidence thresholds, and how sensitive are results to the sieve’s position?

Minor/additional comments:
- Suggest moving dataset description earlier (after Related Work) to ground subsequent sections.
- State early that MC resolution is cast as binary classification with two SVMs and define “candidate list.”
- When introducing Dcoref/Scoref, explicitly tie to Stanford systems and cite their papers.
- Clarify that hyperlink enrichment uses link targets to supply gender/number/type via Freebase and describe fallback behavior.
- For reproducibility, release data/splits and specify preprocessing versions (CoreNLP, Wikipedia dump date, Freebase snapshot) and confirm folds are by document.
