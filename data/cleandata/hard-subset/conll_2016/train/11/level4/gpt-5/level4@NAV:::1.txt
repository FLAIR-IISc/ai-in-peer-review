Review- The paper introduces a Wikipedia-specific variant of the coreference task: identifying the single coreference chain that corresponds to the article’s subject entity. The authors annotate 30 articles with complete coreference, finding that about 25% of mentions refer to the “main concept” (MC). They present a few simple baselines and a lightweight classifier that outperforms them. They further plug this classifier into the (rule-based) Stanford coreference system and observe sizable gains over prior state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia. I find this a compelling reframing of coreference that makes strong sense for information extraction, has the potential to reinvigorate the area, and could help connect coreference and entity linking research in a meaningful way. I’m often skeptical of work that defines a new setting where standard systems fail and then adds a tweak to win on that setting; here, however, the task itself is well motivated and, rather than searching for a friendly domain, the paper highlights a genuinely important scenario where existing systems underperform.

THE TASK: From an IE standpoint, resolving the main concept is an appealing and practical objective. Many document types revolve around a primary entity (e.g., biographies, dossiers or briefings about a person or event, clinical narratives), and the information of interest often targets that central entity. Traditional coreference includes many mentions that are likely irrelevant for most IE use cases (e.g., generic mentions), whereas this task focuses on mentions that matter. Methodologically, the “main concept” acts as a helpful discourse anchor for coreference; nonetheless, there remains notable room to surpass the baselines, especially on non-pronominal mentions. Operating directly on Wikipedia also invites richer use of background knowledge, which the authors demonstrate. Consequently, this domain seems like a promising testbed for ideas that could lift coreference performance more broadly, but that are harder to realize robustly in the general setting where other challenges dominate. Moreover, unlike prior work that isolates a narrow slice of coreference (e.g., Winograd schemas), this paper substantially moves overall coreference metrics on a domain (Wikipedia) that many in the ACL community care about.

THE TECHNIQUES: The methods are not the primary novelty, but they are effective. The features are sensible; additional feature conjunctions might further help (it’s not clear whether the authors explored this). The paper should also state earlier that the primary MC-resolution approach is a binary classifier; this is not made explicit soon enough, leaving the model unspecified through the feature description.

MINOR DETAILS:
Organization: I would suggest introducing the dataset immediately after “Related Work” (as a new Section 3) so that “Baselines” can present concrete numbers earlier, thereby strengthening the motivation for “Approach.”
When Section 4 refers to Dcoref and Scoref, please cite the Stanford system papers or explicitly state that these are the Stanford coreference systems (many readers may not recognize the Dcoref/Scoref names).
The term “candidate list” is ambiguous, particularly in the passage: “We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link.” Please clarify that the “candidate list” is the set of mentions in the article that could be coreferent with the MC. Most readers will infer that this module imports semantic information from Wikipedia’s link structure (e.g., if a mention links to an article marked as female in Freebase, then that mention is female), so clarity in terminology will help.
Section 6.1 states “we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010).” However, Section 4.1 seems to imply these are identical. It’s unclear where additional WCR mentions arise.
