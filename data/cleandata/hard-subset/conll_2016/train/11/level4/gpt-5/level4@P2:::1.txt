Summary and contributions
- The paper reframes coreference for Wikipedia by focusing on “main concept resolution”: identifying all mentions in an article that refer to the entity the article is about. The authors construct a small annotated dataset (30 articles; ~7k non-singleton mentions, ~25% linked to the main concept), propose several baselines derived from existing CR systems, and train a binary classifier—separately for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions—that leverages Wikipedia markup and Freebase attributes. The classifier substantially outperforms the baselines on the main concept task and, when integrated as a high-precision sieve into Stanford’s deterministic system (Dcoref), improves end-to-end CR on Wikipedia.
- In my view, the primary contributions are:
  1) Task and testbed: formalizing main concept resolution for Wikipedia and providing a dataset and evaluation protocol.
  2) Effective use of Wikipedia/Freebase features to build a simple binary classifier that beats strong coreference-derived baselines on this task.
  3) Demonstrated downstream impact: integrating the classifier into Dcoref yields notable gains on full CR for Wikipedia articles.

Strengths
- Well-motivated task with clear IE utility. Many documents naturally center on a single entity (e.g., biographies, dossiers, event reports, clinical notes). Focusing on the article’s main entity prevents effort on generic or irrelevant mentions and aligns with practical extraction goals. This is a compelling slice of the problem that feels substantively important rather than opportunistic domain selection.
- Promising empirical gains. The classifier achieves large F1 improvements over baselines for both pronominal and non-pronominal mentions (e.g., ~13–15 absolute F1 over baselines on the main concept task), and incorporating it into Dcoref improves CoNLL F1 by ~4 points on the WCR dataset. These are sizable effects, suggesting the utility of the approach.
- Leverages Wikipedia structure instead of noisy entity linking. By exploiting human-curated links, redirects, and Freebase attributes, the method sidesteps common precision/recall trade-offs in NEL pipelines and provides reliable features (e.g., type, gender, aliases) that are directly relevant to main concept recognition.
- Useful bridge between CR and EL communities. The task encourages methods that combine discourse phenomena with knowledge signals, and the dataset may help test knowledge-aware CR ideas in a controlled, high-impact setting.
- Potential to catalyze research beyond newswire. Results highlight how state-of-the-art CR systems degrade on Wikipedia and demonstrate that targeted adaptation can recover significant performance, likely stimulating broader work on genre transfer.

Weaknesses
- Methodological novelty is limited. The approach is largely a linear SVM with feature engineering. While the features are sensible and effective, there is little model-level innovation, and the paper would benefit from deeper ablations (e.g., richer feature conjunctions, alternative classifiers, or learning paradigms).
- Small dataset and generalization concerns. The WCR corpus has only 30 articles, which raises questions about robustness and statistical reliability. It is unclear how well the models generalize across topics, languages, or to Wikipedia pages with sparse linking, and whether gains persist without Freebase coverage.
- Exposition gaps and clarity issues.
  - The paper should state early and explicitly that main concept resolution is framed as a binary classification problem, with separate models for pronouns and non-pronouns.
  - The terms Dcoref and Scoref should be clearly tied to the Stanford systems and properly cited where first mentioned.
  - The “candidate list” terminology is confusing; please define it as the set of detected mentions considered as possible main concept mentions within the article.
  - Section 6.1 says you take the union of WCR mentions and mentions predicted by Raghunathan et al. (2010), but Section 4.1 suggests you use that detector for mention extraction. Please clarify what is gold vs. predicted, where additional mentions come from, and how this affects training/testing.
- Limited analysis of error modes and feature reliability. Some errors appear driven by noisy redirects or conflation of location/organization names; more quantitative analysis of link sparsity, redirect noise, and Freebase coverage would help. It would also be useful to know how performance holds when Freebase attributes are missing or replaced (e.g., with Wikidata).
- Evaluation details could be strengthened. Given the small dataset, please ensure cross-validation is by article (no leakage of entity attributes across folds). Also, clarify whether the pronominal baselines include the Nguyen et al. heuristic uniformly across systems, and how often this heuristic helps vs. hurts precision.

Questions to authors
- Task/model clarity: Please state up front that the core task is binary classification and briefly describe the two models (pronoun vs. non-pronoun) before detailing features.
- Dataset and splits: Are cross-validation folds created at the article level? Could you report variance across folds or topics? Any plan to expand WCR or release it?
- Feature design: Did you try feature conjunctions beyond what is reported, or alternative classifiers (e.g., logistic regression, tree-based, neural)? Any systematic comparison?
- Knowledge sources: Freebase is deprecated. Can your pipeline use Wikidata equivalently? How sensitive are results to missing links or sparse markup?
- Baselines and ablations: Could you include a variant that uses only Wikipedia-derived features (no Freebase), and another using only Freebase, to quantify each source’s contribution?
- Integration into CR: Beyond Dcoref, did you attempt integrating the classifier into Scoref or other statistical systems? If not, what obstacles did you anticipate?

Additional comments
- Consider moving the dataset section immediately after Related Work to motivate baselines and approach with concrete statistics.
- Define “candidate list” clearly when first used and consider renaming it to “candidate mention set.”
- When first mentioning Dcoref/Scoref, note they are Stanford systems and add citations.
- Please reconcile the apparent inconsistency between Sections 4.1 and 6.1 regarding mention extraction and the union with gold mentions.
