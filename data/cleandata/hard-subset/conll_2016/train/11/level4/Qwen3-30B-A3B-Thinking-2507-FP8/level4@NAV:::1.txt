

The authors introduce a novel coreference task specifically designed for Wikipedia, focusing on identifying coreference chains corresponding to the article's primary entity. They annotate 30 documents with full coreference chains, revealing that approximately 25% of mentions pertain to the "main concept" (MC) of the article. Simple baselines and a basic classifier are presented, with the classifier outperforming these baselines. Integrating the classifier into the Stanford (rule-based) coreference system yields significant improvements over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia.  

This work proposes a compelling coreference refinement with strong information extraction relevance, the potential to invigorate coreference research, and a meaningful bridge between coreference and entity linking literature. Unlike typical papers that invent new tasks to showcase system improvements, this task genuinely addresses a critical setting where standard coreference systems underperform—specifically, scenarios where documents revolve around a single salient entity (e.g., biographies, clinical records). The main concept resolution task explicitly focuses on mentions relevant to IE, eliminating the noise of generic references prevalent in standard coreference. While the "main concept" provides a useful discourse anchor, substantial challenges remain in improving beyond baselines, particularly for non-pronominal mentions. Wikipedia’s structured knowledge also enables novel knowledge utilization, positioning this domain as a promising testbed for coreference advancements that might be harder to demonstrate in broader settings. Critically, this work impacts standard coreference metrics on a domain (Wikipedia) of significant interest to the ACL community, unlike prior slice-based approaches (e.g., Winograd schema).  

The methodology, however, is not the paper’s strongest aspect. The features are sensible but could benefit from additional conjunctions (which the authors do not explore). The primary MC resolution system is a binary classifier, a detail that should be explicitly stated earlier, as the model remains undefined during featurization description.
