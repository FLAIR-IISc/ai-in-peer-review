

Overall, the authors present a novel coreference task tailored to Wikipedia, defined as identifying coreference chains specifically corresponding to the entity central to the article. They annotate 30 documents with all coreference chains, finding that approximately 25% of mentions refer to the article's "main concept" (MC). The authors describe simple baselines and a basic classifier that outperforms them. Integrating this classifier into the Stanford (rule-based) coreference system yields substantial improvements over all state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia. This work proposes an interesting twist on coreference that aligns well with information extraction (IE) goals, has the potential to revitalize coreference research, and may bridge a meaningful gap between coreference and entity linking literature. While some papers propose new tasks where standard systems perform poorly and then tweak systems to succeed, this work stands out because the task itself is genuinely motivating—it addresses a setting where standard coreference systems perform poorly, and this setting is clearly important for practical IE applications, not merely a pretext for new domain testing.

**THE TASK:** Main concept resolution is a compelling task from an IE perspective. Many document types—such as biographical articles, dossiers, clinical records, or event briefings—center primarily around a specific entity, and the relevant information to extract is specific to that entity. The standard coreference task suffers from including large numbers of irrelevant mentions (e.g., generic references), whereas this task exclusively comprises mentions that matter for IE. The "main concept" provides a useful discourse anchor for coreference, though substantial overhead remains to improve beyond baselines, particularly for non-pronominal mentions. Performing coreference directly on Wikipedia also enables richer use of knowledge resources, as demonstrated. This domain is likely to serve as an excellent testbed for coreference improvements that might be difficult to demonstrate robustly in general settings due to competing challenges. Crucially, unlike prior work that isolated niche subtasks (e.g., Winograd schema), this paper achieves significant impact on overall coreference metrics within a domain (Wikipedia) of high interest to the ACL community.

**THE TECHNIQUES:** The methodology is not the paper's strongest aspect, though the techniques appear effective. The feature set is sensible, but it remains unclear whether additional feature conjunctions were explored (and the authors should report such experiments). Critically, the primary MC resolution system is a binary classifier, a detail that should be explicitly stated early in the paper, as the model remains undefined throughout the featurization description.

**MINOR DETAILS:**
*   **Organization:** Introduce the dataset immediately after "Related Works" (as a new Section 3) so concrete results can be presented in "Baselines," further motivating "Approach."
*   **Terminology:** When Section 4 references "Dcoref" and "Scoref," cite the Stanford coreference papers or explicitly state "Stanford Coreference System (Dcoref/Scoref)" to avoid confusion.
*   **"Candidate List":** Clarify that the "candidate list" refers to the set of mentions in the article that are possible coreferents of the MC. The description ("For each link... look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link") is unclear without this definition. The intended use of hyperlink structure (e.g., inferring gender from linked Freebase entities) is sound but requires precise terminology.
*   **Section 6.1 vs. 4.1:** Section 6.1 states "we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)," yet Section 4.1 implies these are identical. The source of additional WCR mentions is unclear.
