

The authors introduce a novel coreference task specifically designed for Wikipedia articles, requiring the identification of coreference chains corresponding to the article's primary entity (the "main concept"). They annotate 30 documents, finding that approximately 25% of mentions relate to this main concept. The authors present simple baselines and a basic classifier that outperforms them. Integrating this classifier into the Stanford (rule-based) coreference system yields significant improvements over all state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia data.  

This paper proposes a compelling variation of coreference that is strongly motivated by information extraction (IE) needs. It addresses a genuine limitation of standard coreference tasks—namely, the prevalence of irrelevant mentions (e.g., generic references)—by focusing exclusively on mentions pertinent to the article’s central entity. Unlike approaches that invent new tasks solely to demonstrate system superiority, this work identifies a meaningful setting (e.g., biographical documents, clinical records, or event briefings) where standard coreference systems underperform, making the task inherently valuable. The main concept resolution task has the potential to reinvigorate coreference research and bridge gaps between coreference and entity linking literature. Methodologically, the "main concept" provides a useful discourse anchor, though substantial challenges remain for non-pronominal mentions. The Wikipedia domain also enables richer integration of external knowledge, offering a promising testbed for coreference innovations that might be difficult to validate in broader settings. Crucially, this work impacts coreference metrics on a domain (Wikipedia) of significant interest to the ACL community, unlike more niche prior efforts (e.g., Winograd schema).  

The techniques, while effective, are not the paper’s strongest aspect. The feature set is sensible but lacks exploration of additional feature conjunctions (it remains unclear whether such experiments were conducted). The primary system is a binary classifier for main concept resolution, but this is not explicitly stated early enough, and the model remains undefined during featurization description.  

**Minor Details:**  
- **Organization:** Introduce the dataset immediately after "Related Works" (as Section 3) to enable concrete results in "Baselines" and better motivate "Approach."  
- **Citations:** Clarify that "Dcoref" and "Scoref" refer to the Stanford coreference system (cite relevant Stanford papers).  
- **Terminology:** Define "candidate list" explicitly as "the set of mentions in the article potentially coreferent with the main concept" (e.g., "For each hyperlink, we search the candidate list for mentions matching the link’s surface string"). The role of hyperlink-based semantic enrichment (e.g., inferring gender from linked Freebase entities) is clear, but the term "candidate list" requires this definition.  
- **Section 6.1 Consistency:** The statement "we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by Raghunathan et al. (2010)" conflicts with Section 4.1, which implies these are identical. Clarify where additional WCR mentions are sourced.
