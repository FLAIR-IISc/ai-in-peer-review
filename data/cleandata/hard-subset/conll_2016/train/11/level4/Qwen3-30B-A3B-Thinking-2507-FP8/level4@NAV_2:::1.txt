

Summary: This paper introduces a novel coreference task specifically tailored for Wikipedia articles: identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) described by the article. The authors create a new dataset (WCR3) comprising 30 Wikipedia articles annotated with main concept mentions (1800 out of 7000 non-singleton mentions). They propose a binary classifier for MC resolution that leverages Wikipedia markup and Freebase attributes, outperforming baselines built on state-of-the-art coreference systems. The primary contributions are: (1) defining and formalizing the main concept resolution task for Wikipedia; (2) creating the WCR3 dataset for evaluating this task; (3) developing a classifier that significantly outperforms baselines (13-15 F1 points) and improves full coreference resolution when integrated into Dcoref.

Strengths:  
1. The task definition is highly relevant to real-world NLP applications, particularly information extraction, where identifying mentions of the central entity (e.g., in biographies or event descriptions) is crucial. Unlike generic coreference resolution—which includes irrelevant mentions like "the foundation stone" in OIE—this task focuses exclusively on conceptually significant mentions, directly addressing a long-standing limitation in coreference literature.  
2. The proposed classifier achieves substantial performance gains (88.79% F1 on all mentions vs. 75.77% for the best baseline), demonstrating that Wikipedia-specific features (markup, Freebase attributes) significantly improve MC identification. This validates the authors' core insight that Wikipedia's structured nature enables more precise coreference resolution than generic systems.  
3. Integrating the classifier into Dcoref (yielding Dcoref++) improves the full coreference resolution F1 by 4 points on WCR3, proving practical utility beyond the narrow MC task. This bridges coreference resolution and entity linking, offering a pathway to enhance existing systems for Wikipedia and similar structured texts.

Weaknesses:  
1. The feature engineering lacks novelty; the approach primarily combines existing techniques (Wikipedia/Freebase features, part-of-speech tags) without rigorous exploration of feature interactions or higher-order combinations (e.g., feature conjunctions). The paper states features are "inspired by" prior work but does not demonstrate how their specific configuration yields the observed gains.  
2. The dataset size (30 articles, 60k tokens) is very limited compared to standard coreference benchmarks (e.g., OntoNotes has 1.5M tokens). While the authors justify diversity in article topics, the small scale raises concerns about statistical significance and generalizability to broader Wikipedia content.  
3. The classifier's binary nature is not clearly articulated early in the paper, leading to ambiguity in the methodology description. The authors refer to "the model" throughout the feature section without explicitly stating it is a binary classifier, which is critical for understanding the task framing.

Questions to Authors:  
1. How would the classifier perform on a larger, more diverse Wikipedia subset (e.g., 100+ articles) to assess robustness beyond the current small dataset?  
2. Could the authors provide a qualitative analysis of the top 5 misclassified instances for non-pronominal mentions (e.g., examples like "Houston" vs. "Houston Texans") to clarify the error patterns?
