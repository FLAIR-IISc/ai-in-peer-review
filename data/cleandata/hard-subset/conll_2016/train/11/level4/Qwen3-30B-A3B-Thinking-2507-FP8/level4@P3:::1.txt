

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper introduces "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), a novel task for Wikipedia articles that identifies all mentions of the central entity described in the article (the "main concept" or MC). The authors annotate 30 Wikipedia articles with 7,000 mentions (1,800 referring to the MC), creating a new dataset (WCR3) for evaluating MCR systems. The main contributions are:

1. A well-motivated task that addresses a real need in information extraction, where standard coreference resolution produces many irrelevant mentions for IE applications.

2. A binary classifier that identifies mentions co-referent to the MC by leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase attributes, significantly outperforming baselines built on state-of-the-art coreference systems.

3. Demonstration that integrating this classifier into a rule-based coreference system (Dcoref++) improves coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia by 4 absolute F1 points.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation for the Task**: The paper convincingly argues that standard coreference resolution produces many irrelevant mentions for information extraction applications, while the MCR task focuses on mentions that actually matter for downstream applications. The task is particularly relevant for Wikipedia, which is widely used in NLP applications but has been neglected in coreference research. Unlike many papers that simply identify a new domain where standard systems underperform, this work identifies a genuinely useful task that has practical implications for information extraction.

2. **High-Quality Dataset and Evaluation**: The WCR3 dataset is carefully constructed with attention to Wikipedia article characteristics (size, topic, link density). The dataset size (30 articles, 60k tokens) is sufficient to draw meaningful conclusions, and the paper provides a valuable resource for future research. The evaluation clearly demonstrates that state-of-the-art coreference systems trained on news data perform poorly on Wikipedia (51-56 F1 vs. 62-64 F1 on OntoNotes), confirming the need for domain adaptation.

3. **Significant Performance Gains**: The proposed classifier achieves 88.99% F1 on pronominal mentions and 88.67% F1 on non-pronominal mentions, representing an absolute 13-15 point improvement over the best baselines. The integration into Dcoref (Dcoref++) achieves a 4-point absolute improvement on the standard CoNLL F1 score for coreference resolution on Wikipedia, demonstrating the practical value of the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Clear Task Definition Early On**: The paper does not clearly distinguish the MCR task from standard coreference resolution until later sections. The binary classification nature of the main approach (classifying mentions as MC-related or not) is not explicitly stated early enough, making the paper harder to follow for readers unfamiliar with the specific task.

2. **Limited Exploration of Feature Engineering**: While the paper describes a comprehensive set of features, it does not explore feature combinations or interactions that might further improve performance. The paper mentions "we tried with less success other configurations" but doesn't provide details or rationale for these experiments, leaving readers wondering about the full potential of the feature set.

3. **Insufficient Discussion of Generalizability**: The paper focuses exclusively on Wikipedia, but doesn't sufficiently address whether the approach could generalize to other text genres or whether the Wikipedia-specific features (markup, Freebase links) would be applicable elsewhere. The paper claims this task "might bridge the gap between coreference literature and entity linking literature," but doesn't elaborate on how.

4. **Ambiguous Mention of "Candidate List"**: The paper frequently refers to a "candidate list" when describing the preprocessing step, but never clearly defines what this list is. This creates confusion about the methodology, especially when the paper states in Section 4.1 that the candidate list is the set of mentions in the article that could be coreferent with the MC.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the task is to identify in a Wikipedia article all the mentions of the main concept," but doesn't clarify whether the main concept is defined by the article title or whether it's an entity that can be inferred from the content. Could the authors clarify this distinction?

2. The paper mentions that "the main concept of a Wikipedia article is represented by its title, its inferred type (a common noun inferred from the first sentence of the article)." How is the inferred type determined, and what happens when the article title and inferred type conflict?

3. The authors note that "the ranking of the statistical systems on this dataset differs from the one obtained on the OntoNotes test set." Could the authors provide more insight into why this happens, and whether this indicates fundamental differences in the way Wikipedia texts should be processed compared to news texts?

4. The paper states that "our classifier exploits carefully designed features extracted from Wikipedia markup and characteristics, as well as from Freebase; many of which we borrowed from the related literature." Could the authors clarify which specific features were borrowed versus newly proposed, and why they chose those particular features?

This paper presents a well-motivated, valuable contribution to the coreference resolution literature with clear practical implications for information extraction. The main concept resolution task is compelling and the results are impressive. With some clarification on the methodology and task definition, this work would be even stronger. I recommend acceptance.
