

Review:  
This paper introduces a novel coreference task—main concept resolution (MCR)—tailored to Wikipedia, where the goal is identifying mentions specifically pertaining to the article’s central entity rather than all coreference chains. The authors annotate 30 Wikipedia articles, with approximately 25% of mentions corresponding to the main concept (MC). They propose a binary classifier for MCR (effectively identifying MC mentions) that outperforms simple baselines and integrates seamlessly into the Stanford CoreNLP coreference system, yielding substantial gains over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia.  

The task is compelling and well-motivated. Standard coreference evaluation suffers from irrelevant mentions (e.g., generic references), which are largely unhelpful for information extraction (IE). MCR directly addresses this by focusing *only* on mentions relevant to the article’s primary subject—critical for applications like biographical summaries, clinical records, or entity-centric IE pipelines. This shift reframes coreference as a task aligned with practical IE needs, avoiding the common pitfall of proposing artificial tasks where systems *must* be tweaked to succeed. The authors convincingly argue that Wikipedia’s structure (e.g., hyperlinks to entity-centric articles) naturally supports this task, making it both theoretically sound and empirically grounded. Crucially, the task’s impact is meaningful: it directly improves coreference metrics on a domain (Wikipedia) highly relevant to the ACL community, unlike narrower efforts (e.g., Winograd schemas) that isolate fragments of the problem.  

Methodologically, the paper’s strengths lie in task design, not novelty of techniques. The classifier uses sensible features (e.g., semantic attributes from Wikipedia hyperlinks), but the approach lacks depth. Key gaps include:  
1. **Clarity on the model**: The binary classification nature of the MC system is not explicitly stated early, leaving the model undefined during feature description.  
2. **Feature exploration**: The authors do not explore feature conjunctions (e.g., combining syntactic and semantic features), which could yield further gains.  
3. **Integration**: While the Stanford integration is effective, the paper does not fully leverage Wikipedia’s knowledge graph (e.g., Freebase attributes) beyond surface-string matching.  

The work’s true value is its *task design*, not the classifier itself. MCR provides a rigorous, domain-specific benchmark that could catalyze broader coreference research. By focusing on mentions *directly relevant* to the article’s core entity, it sidesteps the noise of general coreference and offers a clear path to evaluating systems on IE-relevant output. This bridges a gap between coreference and entity linking literature, where both fields prioritize the central entity. The task’s viability is further validated by the substantial gains over SOTA on Wikipedia—demonstrating that even modest improvements here have outsized impact due to the task’s constrained scope.  

**Minor revisions required**:  
- **Organization**: Move dataset description to Section 3 (after Related Work) to immediately ground baselines and results in concrete data.  
- **Terminology**: Clarify "candidate list" as "the set of all mentions in the document eligible for coreference with the MC" (e.g., in the hyperlink enrichment section).  
- **Citations**: When referencing Stanford’s "Dcoref" and "Scoref," cite the original CoreNLP papers or use the system’s full name.  
- **Section 6.1**: Resolve the ambiguity about WCR mentions. Section 4.1 states WCR mentions are extracted via the Raghunathan et al. (2010) method, but Section 6.1 refers to a "union" of WCR mentions and those from the proposed method. Clarify whether these are distinct sets or redundant.  

**Overall assessment**:  
This paper makes a strong case for MCR as a meaningful, practical coreference task with clear IE utility. It avoids the trap of *creating* a task to showcase a system, instead identifying a genuine gap in how coreference is applied to real-world documents. While the technical approach is modest, the task’s significance—its potential to reshape coreference evaluation and bridge IE subfields—elevates the work. The integration with Stanford CoreNLP and measurable improvements on Wikipedia provide compelling validation. The minor methodological gaps (e.g., feature exploration) are acceptable given the focus on task design, but clarity on the classifier’s binary nature and dataset structure must be addressed.  

**Recommendation**: **Accept**. This work offers a valuable, well-motivated contribution that could influence coreference research directions and provide a useful benchmark for entity-centric IE. The task’s relevance to Wikipedia—a key domain for NLP research—ensures its impact will extend beyond the paper’s immediate scope.
