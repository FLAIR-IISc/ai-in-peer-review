

The authors propose a novel coreference task tailored to Wikipedia, focusing on identifying the coreference chain corresponding to the article's main entity. They annotate 30 documents with complete coreference chains, finding that approximately 25% of mentions refer to this "main concept" (MC). Simple baselines and a basic classifier are introduced, with the classifier outperforming these baselines. Integrating this classifier into the Stanford coreference system yields significant improvements over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia.  

This work presents a compelling shift in coreference research with strong motivation from an information extraction (IE) perspective. Unlike many papers that propose new tasks solely to demonstrate superior performance on a niche domain, this task genuinely addresses a meaningful gap: standard coreference systems struggle in scenarios where documents revolve around a single salient entity (e.g., biographies, clinical records, or dossiers), yet the standard task includes numerous irrelevant mentions (e.g., generic references). The MC resolution task explicitly focuses on mentions that matter for IE, making it both practical and theoretically sound. It has the potential to revitalize coreference research and bridge the gap between coreference and entity linking literature.  

The task’s domain—Wikipedia—is particularly valuable. It provides a natural discourse anchor via hyperlinks, enabling richer knowledge integration (e.g., leveraging Freebase gender annotations from linked entities). This domain also serves as an ideal testbed for coreference improvements that might be difficult to demonstrate on general datasets due to noise from irrelevant mentions. Crucially, unlike past work that isolated narrow subtasks (e.g., Winograd schemas), this paper achieves substantial gains on the *overall* coreference metrics within a domain of broad interest to the ACL community.  

Methodologically, the techniques are functional but not the paper’s strongest aspect. The feature set is sensible, though exploring additional feature combinations (e.g., complex conjunctions) could yield further gains. The primary MC resolution system is a binary classifier, but this key detail is not clearly stated early enough; the model remains undefined during the featurization description.  

**Minor Suggestions for Clarity:**  
1.  **Organization:** Introduce the dataset immediately after "Related Works" (as Section 3). This would allow concrete results to be presented in "Baselines," strengthening motivation for the proposed approach.  
2.  **Stanford System Clarification:** When referencing "Dcoref" and "Scoref" in Section 4, cite the original Stanford papers or explicitly state "the Stanford coreference system" (many readers will be unfamiliar with the acronyms).  
3.  **"Candidate List" Definition:** The term "candidate list" is ambiguous. Define it early as "the set of mentions in the article potentially coreferent with the MC." The description of leveraging hyperlinks to enrich mentions should clarify that this module imports semantic attributes (e.g., gender from Freebase via linked entities) into the candidate list.  
4.  **WCR Mention Consistency:** Section 6.1 states that the evaluation considers "the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by Raghunathan et al. (2010)." However, Section 4.1 implies these are identical. Please clarify whether additional WCR mentions are extracted or if this is a redundancy.  

Overall, this paper’s core contribution—redefining coreference around the main entity—is well-motivated and impactful. While the techniques are straightforward, the task design is compelling, and the results on Wikipedia demonstrate clear value. The work effectively shifts focus to entity-relevant mentions, addressing a persistent weakness in standard coreference evaluation. This could spur new research directions that benefit both coreference and IE communities.
