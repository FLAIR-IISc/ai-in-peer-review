

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**  
This paper introduces "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), a novel coreference task focused on identifying all mentions of the main concept (the entity a Wikipedia article describes) rather than all coreference chains. The authors annotate 30 Wikipedia articles with 7,000 mentions (25% referring to the main concept) following OntoNotes guidelines, creating the WCR dataset. They propose a binary classifier that uses features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase to distinguish main concept mentions from others, and demonstrate that this classifier outperforms baselines built on state-of-the-art coreference systems. Their approach is integrated into the Lee et al. (2013) rule-based system (Dcoref++), yielding a 4-point CoNLL F1 improvement on Wikipedia texts.

The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:  
1. A meaningful adaptation of coreference resolution to Wikipedia, where standard systems perform poorly, with a task highly relevant for information extraction.  
2. The WCR dataset, enabling evaluation of coreference systems on Wikipedia texts.  
3. A binary classifier leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase to identify main concept mentions, outperforming baselines by up to 15 F1 points.

**Strengths**  
1. **Relevant task with clear practical motivation**: The paper identifies a significant gap in coreference research—standard systems trained on news text fail on Wikipedia, which is widely used in NLP. The MCR task directly addresses a real-world need: in many NLP applications (e.g., information extraction), only mentions of the main concept matter, while standard coreference systems generate irrelevant mentions (e.g., generic references). This is not a contrived task but a meaningful adaptation to a domain where coreference resolution is critically important.  

2. **Well-constructed dataset**: The WCR dataset (30 articles, 60k tokens, 7,000 mentions) is carefully annotated following OntoNotes guidelines, with 25% of mentions referring to the main concept. The authors justify the dataset's representativeness by sampling articles across diverse topics, sizes, and link densities. This resource enables future research on adapting coreference resolution to Wikipedia and provides a solid benchmark for evaluating domain-specific systems.  

3. **Effective integration into a coreference pipeline**: The authors demonstrate that their classifier improves performance on a full coreference resolution task (Dcoref++), not just the MCR task. The 4-point CoNLL F1 gain over Dcoref on Wikipedia texts shows tangible utility. This bridges the gap between task-specific improvements and broader coreference performance, making the contribution more impactful than typical "task-specific" papers.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Underdeveloped baselines and comparison**: The baselines (B1-B4) are overly simplistic, relying on heuristics like "longest chain" or "exact title match." These do not fairly compare to state-of-the-art coreference systems. The paper claims their classifier "drastically outperforms fair baselines," but the baselines are not representative of current coreference techniques. A more robust comparison would include direct application of systems like Scoref or Dcoref to the MCR task, rather than post-hoc heuristics.  

2. **Small dataset limits statistical significance**: With only 30 articles (60k tokens), the dataset is too small to draw strong conclusions about generalizability. While the authors acknowledge this, they do not justify why such a limited sample suffices for their claims. The paper would benefit from a larger dataset or more rigorous statistical analysis to support the reported gains.  

3. **Incomplete feature exploration**: The authors mention experimenting with "a few hundred features" but only report on a subset. The paper lacks exploration of feature combinations (e.g., interactions between title, entity type, and gender features) or more complex models (e.g., neural networks). Without this, it's unclear whether the current feature set is optimal or if further improvements are possible.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How would you compare your binary classifier approach to a full coreference resolution model trained directly on the MCR task? Would such a model outperform your post-processing approach?  
2. Given the small size of WCR (30 articles), can you provide more details on the sampling strategy to justify its representativeness across Wikipedia's diverse content?  
3. Have you considered using WCR to train a dedicated coreference system for Wikipedia, rather than just integrating your classifier into existing pipelines? If so, what challenges would arise?
