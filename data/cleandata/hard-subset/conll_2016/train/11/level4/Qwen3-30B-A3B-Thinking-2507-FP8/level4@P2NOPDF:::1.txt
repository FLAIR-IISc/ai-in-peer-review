

The authors introduce a novel coreference task focused on Wikipedia, requiring the identification of coreference chains specifically tied to the article's central entity ("main concept"). They annotate 30 Wikipedia articles, with ~25% of mentions belonging to this main concept. Simple baselines and a basic classifier are presented, with the classifier outperforming baselines and integrating successfully into Stanford's rule-based coreference system, yielding substantial gains over state-of-the-art methods on Wikipedia.  

This task represents a compelling shift in coreference research. It addresses a genuine need in information extraction: documents often revolve around a single salient entity (e.g., biographies, clinical records), where only mentions of that entity carry meaningful information. Standard coreference systems struggle with irrelevant mentions (e.g., generics), making this task uniquely relevant for IE. The "main concept" provides a clear discourse anchor, potentially enabling more robust coreference resolution. Crucially, the task isn't contrived; it genuinely highlights a meaningful limitation of existing systems in a domain (Wikipedia) with significant community interest. Unlike prior work that isolates narrow subtasks (e.g., Winograd schema), this approach impacts coreference metrics broadly within a high-impact domain, offering potential to bridge coreference and entity linking research.  

Methodologically, the paper's contributions are modest but effective. The binary classifier approach—used for main concept resolution—is sensible but lacks detailed explanation early in the paper (the model is undefined during feature description). While features are reasonable, the paper omits exploration of feature conjunctions, which could further improve results. The integration into Stanford Coref demonstrates practical utility, though the methodology itself isn't groundbreaking.  

**Minor Suggestions for Clarity and Organization:**  
1. **Structure:** Move the dataset description to Section 3 (after Related Work) to immediately ground the baselines in concrete results.  
2. **Terminology:** Explicitly define "candidate list" as the set of potential coreference candidates within the article (not the general mention set). Clarify that "shallow semantic attributes" derive from Wikipedia hyperlinks (e.g., gender inferred from linked Freebase entries).  
3. **Stanford System:** When referencing "Dcoref" and "Scoref" (Section 4), cite the Stanford coreference papers or state "Stanford Coref system" to ensure accessibility.  
4. **Inconsistency:** Section 6.1 mentions "the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by Raghunathan et al. (2010)," but Section 4.1 suggests these are equivalent. Clarify whether additional WCR mentions are extracted elsewhere.  

**Overall Assessment:**  
This paper proposes a well-motivated, practically significant task that challenges coreference research to prioritize entity-centric information extraction. The Wikipedia focus is justified, the evaluation is meaningful, and the results demonstrate tangible progress. While the core technique is straightforward, the task’s relevance and the strong empirical results make it a valuable contribution. The work has the potential to reshape coreference evaluation and spur new research directions that better align with IE needs. It avoids the common pitfall of creating artificial tasks for benchmarking, instead highlighting a genuine gap in existing systems.  

**Recommendation:** Accept. The paper makes a compelling case for a more targeted coreference task with clear practical utility. The minor organizational and definitional issues noted above are easily addressable in revision.
