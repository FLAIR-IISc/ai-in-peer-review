

Summary: This paper introduces a novel coreference resolution task for Wikipedia: identifying all mentions of the "main concept" (the entity the article describes) in a Wikipedia article. The authors create a new dataset (WCR) with 30 Wikipedia articles annotated for this task, and propose a binary classifier that significantly outperforms baselines built on state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems. They further integrate their classifier into the rule-based coreference system Dcoref, demonstrating improved performance on full coreference resolution for Wikipedia texts.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1. A well-motivated task definition and dataset (WCR) for main concept resolution in Wikipedia, addressing a significant gap in coreference research with practical implications for information extraction.
2. A simple yet effective binary classifier achieving 88.99% F1 on pronominal mentions and 88.67% F1 on non-pronominal mentions, outperforming all baselines by 13-15 F1 points.
3. Demonstration that integrating their classifier into a state-of-the-art coreference system (Dcoref++) leads to a 4-point absolute improvement in CoNLL F1 score on Wikipedia texts.

Strengths:
1. The task is exceptionally well-motivated from an information extraction perspective. Standard coreference resolution often identifies many irrelevant mentions, while this task focuses on mentions that are actually relevant to the article's main subject. The authors convincingly argue that this is a more practical approach for many NLP applications, particularly in domains like biographical articles, clinical records, and entity-focused documents.

2. The empirical evidence of the performance gap between general-purpose coreference systems and Wikipedia texts is compelling. Table 1 shows state-of-the-art systems drop dramatically in performance on the WCR dataset compared to standard benchmarks (e.g., Scoref drops from 63.61 to 46.39 F1), providing strong justification for their work.

3. The integration of their classifier into Dcoref (Dcoref++) demonstrates tangible benefits for the full coreference resolution task. As shown in Table 5, Dcoref++ achieves a 4-point absolute improvement in CoNLL F1 score over the original Dcoref, showing that their work has practical implications beyond the specific main concept resolution task.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks sufficient detail about the classifier's implementation and training. The authors mention using SVMs with a linear kernel but don't provide crucial information about feature selection, hyperparameter tuning, or how they handled class imbalance beyond the "weight of 2.0" for the negative class. This makes it difficult to assess the robustness of their approach.

2. The WCR dataset is quite small (30 articles, 60k tokens), which limits the statistical significance of their results. While the authors note they included articles of varying characteristics, the small size makes it challenging to generalize their findings to the broader Wikipedia corpus.

3. The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. For example, they note that "noisy redirects in Wikipedia" cause some errors (Table 3), but don't explore how these might be systematically reduced or whether they would persist in a larger dataset.

Questions to authors:
1. How did you determine the class weight of 2.0 for the negative class? Did you perform hyperparameter tuning to find this value, and how does it compare to other possible weights?

2. Section 6.1 states "we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)," but Section 4.1 suggests these are the same mentions. Can you clarify the difference between the WCR mentions and the Raghunathan mentions, and how you generated the latter?

3. In Table 4, your classifier achieves 88.99% F1 for pronominal mentions, but the Dcoref++ system achieves 85.46% F1. Why does the integrated system perform worse than the standalone classifier for pronominal mentions?

Overall, this paper presents a well-motivated task with practical implications for coreference resolution in Wikipedia. While the methodology is somewhat simple, it's effective and demonstrates clear improvements over baselines. The main limitations are the small dataset size and insufficient detail about the classifier implementation. The work makes a meaningful contribution to bridging coreference research with entity linking, and has potential to revitalize the field by providing a more targeted approach to coreference resolution.
