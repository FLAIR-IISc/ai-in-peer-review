The authors introduce a novel coreference task focused on Wikipedia articles, specifically identifying coreference chains for the entity the article describes – the “main concept.” They present a new annotation of 30 documents, identifying approximately 25% of mentions as relating to this main concept. They demonstrate that a basic classifier outperforms simple baselines and, crucially, substantially improves the state-of-the-art when integrated with the Stanford rule-based coreference system.

This work proposes a compelling and well-motivated adaptation of the coreference task. It addresses a real need within information extraction – accurately resolving references to the primary entity discussed in a document – and presents a scenario where existing systems demonstrably struggle. Unlike some task-creation efforts, this work doesn’t appear to be driven by a desire to find a domain where systems fail, but rather by recognizing a crucial setting where improved performance is valuable. 

THE TASK: Main concept (MC) resolution offers a valuable perspective for information extraction. Many real-world documents (biographies, reports, clinical records) center on a specific entity, and extracting information *about* that entity necessitates accurate coreference resolution focusing on mentions relevant to it. Standard coreference tasks are often diluted by irrelevant generic mentions; this task specifically addresses mentions of core importance. The identification of a “main concept” provides a useful discourse anchor for coreference, though substantial room remains for improvement, particularly for non-pronominal mentions. Applying coreference techniques directly to Wikipedia also facilitates the incorporation of knowledge-based features, as demonstrated in this work. This domain presents a promising testbed for developing coreference improvements that might be difficult to observe or overshadowed in more general settings. Furthermore, this work significantly impacts coreference evaluation metrics within a domain – Wikipedia – of considerable interest to the ACL community, unlike prior work that focused on isolated sub-problems.

THE TECHNIQUES: While the techniques presented are not the primary contribution, they are effective. The employed features are sensible, and exploring feature conjunctions could potentially yield further gains. The authors should more clearly articulate, earlier in the paper, that their primary MC resolution system is a binary classifier, as the model remains somewhat undefined during the featurization description. 

MINOR DETAILS:

Organization: Consider moving the dataset description to immediately after the “Related Works” section (i.e., as a new Section 3). This would allow for concrete results to be presented in the “Baselines” section, better motivating the “Approach.”

Clarity: When referencing Dcoref and Scoref in Section 4, either cite the relevant Stanford papers or explicitly state that these refer to the Stanford coreference system, as many readers may be unfamiliar with these names.

Terminology: The term “candidate list” is unclear. Specifically, in the sentence: “We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link,” clarify that the “candidate list” represents the set of mentions within the article potentially coreferent with the MC. While the intention – to import semantic information from Wikipedia’s link structure (e.g., gender from Freebase) – is generally clear, precise terminology is important. 

Consistency: Section 6.1 states “we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010).” However, Section 4.1 suggests these are identical. Clarify where additional WCR mentions are extracted.
