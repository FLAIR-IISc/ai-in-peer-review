- Novel task: "main concept resolution" — identify the coreference chain for the main entity in a Wikipedia article.  
- New dataset (WCR): 30 documents, ~7,000 mentions, ~25% labeled as main concept mentions.  
- Proposed classifier outperforms simple baselines and, when integrated, significantly improves Stanford Dcoref to state-of-the-art on Wikipedia.  
- Task is well-motivated for information extraction and for documents centered on a single entity.  
- Work could bridge coreference resolution and entity linking, providing a focused testbed.  
- Reported performance gains are substantial and practically meaningful for the Wikipedia domain.  
- Effectively leverages Wikipedia structure (markup, hyperlinks, Freebase) to boost performance.  
- Experiments are clear and include useful ablation studies on feature importance.  
- Authors plan to release dataset and processed mentions, facilitating further research.  
- Methods (feature engineering + SVM) are effective but not particularly novel.  
- The binary classifier is introduced too late in the text and should be presented earlier for clarity.  
- WCR’s small size (30 docs) raises concerns about generalizability and potential overfitting.  
- The term "candidate list" is ambiguous and needs explicit definition.  
- Potential inconsistency between Sections 4.1 and 6.1 regarding mention extraction requires clarification.  
- Question: Have the authors tried more advanced models (e.g., neural networks) beyond SVMs?  
- Question: What steps were taken to mitigate overfitting during feature selection and training given the small dataset?  
- Question: How is the classifier’s output concretely integrated into Dcoref’s rule-based coreference decisions?  
- Question: Can the authors provide error-case examples and analysis of failure modes?  
- Suggestion: Move the dataset description immediately after Related Work to contextualize experiments.  
- Suggestion: Explicitly cite Stanford coreference system papers when referring to Dcoref and Scoref.  
- Suggestion: Soften strong wording in the abstract (e.g., replace "drastically outperforms").
