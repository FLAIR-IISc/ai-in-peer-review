The authors present a new version of the coreference task tailored to Wikipedia, focusing on identifying coreference chains specifically corresponding to the entity that the Wikipedia article is about. They annotate 30 documents with complete coreference chains, noting that roughly 25% of the mentions refer to the “main concept” of the article. They then describe simple baselines and a basic classifier which outperforms these, and demonstrate substantial benefit by integrating their classifier into the Stanford (rule-based) coreference system, achieving state-of-the-art results on Wikipedia.

This paper proposes an interesting and motivating twist on coreference that makes good sense from an information extraction perspective. It has the potential to revitalize and reshape coreference research, and may bridge the gap between coreference and entity linking. I am often critical of papers that introduce a new task where standard systems perform poorly, then propose a limited improvement. However, in this case, the task itself is compelling, and it doesn’t feel like the authors are simply searching for a new domain, but rather highlighting a genuine shortcoming of existing systems in a setting of practical importance.

THE TASK: Main concept resolution is an intriguing task from an information extraction perspective. I can envision numerous applications where documents primarily revolve around a specific entity—biographical documents, dossiers, briefings, or clinical records—and where extracting information relevant specifically to that entity is critical. The standard coreference task often includes a large number of mentions irrelevant for most IE problems (e.g., generic mentions); this task, by contrast, focuses on mentions that demonstrably matter. 

From a methodological standpoint, the notion of a “main concept” provides a useful discourse anchor for coreference. However, substantial overhead remains to improve performance beyond the baselines, particularly on non-pronominal mentions. Performing coreference directly on Wikipedia also opens the door to more effective knowledge integration, as the authors illustrate. This domain is likely to serve as an interesting testbed for ideas that improve coreference overall, but which may be difficult to demonstrate in a general setting, and whose improvements would be obscured by other aspects of the problem.

Moreover, unlike prior work that has focused on specific subproblems of coreference (e.g., the Winograd schema work), this paper has a significant impact on the metrics of the overall coreference problem on Wikipedia—a domain of broad interest within the ACL community.

THE TECHNIQUES: The techniques presented are not the strongest aspect of this paper, though they do appear effective. The features are sensible, and experimentation with conjunctions of these features may prove beneficial. The authors should also explicitly state earlier in the work that their primary main concept (MC) resolution system is a binary classifier; this is not stated clearly enough, and the model remains undefined during the description of feature engineering.

MINOR DETAILS:

Organization: I suggest introducing the dataset immediately after the “Related Works” section (i.e., as the new Section 3) to enable concrete results to be presented in “Baselines,” thereby further motivating the “Approach” section.

When Section 4 refers to Dcoref and Scoref, the authors should cite the relevant Stanford papers or clarify that these refer to the Stanford coreference system, as many readers may be unfamiliar with these names.

The term “candidate list” is unclear, particularly in the following passage: “We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link.” Please clarify that the “candidate list” is the set of mentions within the article potentially coreferent with the main concept. I believe most readers will understand that this module aims to incorporate semantic information from Wikipedia’s link structure (e.g., identifying a mention as female if hyperlinked to a female entity in Freebase); clear terminology will facilitate this understanding.

Section 6.1 states, “we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010).” However, Section 4.1 implies that these are identical. It is unclear where additional WCR mentions are extracted.
