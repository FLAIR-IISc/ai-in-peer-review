The authors present a novel coreference task focused on identifying the coreference chain corresponding to the main entity described in a Wikipedia article. They introduce a new annotated dataset of 30 documents, where approximately 25% of mentions refer to the "main concept." They demonstrate a classifier that outperforms simple baselines on this task and significantly improves performance when integrated into the Stanford rule-based coreference system, achieving state-of-the-art results on Wikipedia data.

This paper proposes a compelling and relevant extension to the standard coreference task. The focus on identifying mentions of the main concept is well-motivated from an information extraction perspective, addressing a practical need for extracting focused information from documents centered around a specific entity. Unlike some work introducing new tasks solely to demonstrate system shortcomings, this task feels grounded in a real-world problem. This work also has the potential to revitalize coreference research by providing a focused testbed and potentially bridging the gap between coreference and entity linking. Importantly, the improvements achieved are substantial and demonstrate a clear benefit in a domain (Wikipedia) of significant interest to the ACL community.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Task Definition:** The primary contribution is the formulation of "main concept resolution" – a focused coreference task with clear relevance to information extraction, particularly in document types centered on a single entity. This framing shifts the focus from resolving *all* coreference to efficiently identifying mentions of the central topic.
2. **New Dataset:** The creation of the WCR dataset (30 documents, approximately 7000 mentions) provides a valuable resource for evaluating coreference systems specifically on this task. The annotation scheme, ensuring 25% of mentions relate to the main concept, encourages focused development and evaluation.
3. **Effective System and Integration:** The authors demonstrate a classifier that outperforms baselines and, critically, improves the performance of a state-of-the-art rule-based coreference system (Stanford’s Dcoref) when integrated. This practical demonstration highlights the utility of their approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Task Motivation & Relevance:** The "main concept resolution" task is intuitively valuable. The authors effectively articulate its utility for information extraction scenarios where identifying all mentions of the core entity is paramount. This practical grounding sets this work apart.
2. **Significant Performance Gains:** The reported improvements over baselines, and the substantial boost to Dcoref’s performance, are significant. These results suggest the approach is not merely a minor tweak but a meaningful advancement. The provided results in Table 5 convincingly show the benefit.
3. **Bridging Coreference and Knowledge Resources:** The work effectively leverages the rich structure of Wikipedia (markup, hyperlinks, Freebase links) to enhance coreference resolution, demonstrating a beneficial interplay between coreference and knowledge-based approaches.
4. **Clear Presentation of Results:** The experiments are well-structured, and the results are presented clearly and concisely, with appropriate comparisons to existing state-of-the-art systems. The ablation studies regarding feature importance are helpful for understanding the model's behavior.
5. **Availability of Resources:** The authors' commitment to sharing the dataset and processed data (mentions and associated information) is commendable and will facilitate further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Technique Novelty:** While effective, the techniques employed (feature engineering with an SVM classifier) are not particularly novel in themselves. The strength lies more in the application of these techniques to a focused task and the intelligent use of Wikipedia’s structure.
2. **Classifier Definition:** The introduction of the binary classifier for main concept resolution is somewhat delayed in the text. Explicitly stating it earlier, perhaps immediately after outlining the task, would improve clarity.
3. **Limited Dataset Size:** While valuable, the WCR dataset is relatively small (30 documents). This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results and the potential for overfitting.
4. **Ambiguous Terminology:** The use of "candidate list" in Section 4.1 is unclear. Clarifying this term as the set of mentions within the article potentially coreferent with the main concept is crucial for reader understanding.
5. **Discrepancy in Mention Extraction:** There's a potential inconsistency between Sections 4.1 and 6.1 regarding mention extraction. Section 4.1 suggests using a specific mention detection algorithm, while Section 6.1 mentions adding mentions predicted by a different method. Clarifying this process is important.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you experimented with more sophisticated machine learning models beyond SVMs (e.g., neural network architectures) for the main concept resolution task? If not, why?
2.  Given the limited size of the WCR dataset, what steps were taken to mitigate potential overfitting, particularly during feature selection and model training?
3.  Can you elaborate on the process of combining the output of your classifier with the Dcoref system? Specifically, how is the classifier's output used to guide the rule-based system's coreference decisions?
4.  Could you provide examples of error cases where the system fails to correctly identify mentions of the main concept, and discuss potential reasons for these failures?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider moving the dataset description to immediately after the "Related Works" section to provide context for the subsequent experiments.
*   Explicitly cite the Stanford coreference system papers when referring to Dcoref and Scoref.
* The phrase "drastically outperforms" in the abstract is a bit strong.
