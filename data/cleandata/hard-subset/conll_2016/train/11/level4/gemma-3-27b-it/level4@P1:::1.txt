The authors present a new version of the coreference task tailored to Wikipedia. The task is to identify the coreference chain specifically corresponding to the entity that the Wikipedia article is about. The authors annotate 30 documents with all coreference chains, of which roughly 25% of the mentions refer to the “main concept” of the article. They then describe some simple baselines and a binary classifier which outperforms these. Moreover, they integrate their classifier into the Stanford (rule-based) coreference system and see substantial benefit over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia.

I think this paper proposes an interesting and worthwhile twist on coreference that makes good sense from an information extraction perspective, has the potential to revitalize and stimulate new research in coreference, and might bridge the gap in an interesting way between coreference and entity linking literature. I am often unimpressed by papers that introduce a new task where standard systems perform poorly, and then propose a tweak that improves their system’s performance. However, in this case, the task itself is highly motivating, and it doesn’t feel like the authors are simply searching for a new domain to apply existing techniques. Instead, it genuinely highlights a situation where “standard systems perform poorly in a setting that’s actually important.”

THE TASK: Main concept resolution is an intriguing task from an information extraction perspective. I can imagine many applications where documents revolve primarily around a particular entity (biographical documents, dossiers or briefings about a person or event, clinical records, etc.) and where the information we care about extracting is specific to that entity. The standard coreference task often includes a large number of mentions that are irrelevant for most information extraction problems (like generic mentions); this task unequivocally focuses on mentions that *do* matter. 

From a methodological standpoint, the notion of a “main concept” provides a useful discourse anchor for coreference, but there appears to be substantial room for improvement beyond the baselines, particularly for non-pronominal mentions. Performing coreference directly on Wikipedia also opens the door for more interesting use of knowledge, which the authors illustrate effectively. Thus, I believe this domain is likely to be a valuable testbed for ideas that could improve coreference overall, but which would be harder to robustly evaluate and might be overshadowed by other issues in more general settings. 

Moreover, unlike past work that has focused on narrow slices of coreference (e.g., the Winograd Schema work), this paper has a significant impact on the metrics of the overall coreference problem within a domain (Wikipedia) that many in the ACL community find interesting.

THE TECHNIQUES: The techniques themselves are not the strongest aspect of this paper, though they are effective. The features seem sensible, but experimenting with additional conjunctions of these features might be beneficial. The authors should also state earlier in the paper that their primary main concept resolution system is a binary classifier; this is not explicitly stated early enough, and the model remains undefined throughout the description of featurization.

MINOR DETAILS:

Organization: I suggest introducing the dataset immediately after “Related Works” (i.e., make it the new Section 3) so that concrete results can be presented in “Baselines,” further motivating the “Approach” section.

When Section 4 refers to Dcoref and Scoref, please cite the original Stanford papers, or clarify that these refer to the Stanford coreference system (many readers will be unfamiliar with the names Dcoref/Scoref).

The use of the term “candidate list” was unclear, particularly in the following sentence: “We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link.” Please clarify that the “candidate list” is the set of mentions within the article that are potential candidates for being coreferent with the main concept. I believe most readers will understand that this module is intended to import semantic information from Wikipedia’s link structure (e.g., if a mention is hyperlinked to an article that is identified as female in Freebase, the mention is likely female). Therefore, maintain clarity in the terminology.

Section 6.1 states, “we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010).” However, Section 4.1 suggests these are the same? I am unclear about how additional WCR mentions would be extracted.
