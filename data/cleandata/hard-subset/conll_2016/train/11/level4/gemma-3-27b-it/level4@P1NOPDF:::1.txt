The authors present a new coreference task tailored to Wikipedia, focusing on identifying coreference chains specifically corresponding to the entity that the Wikipedia article is about. They annotate 30 documents with all coreference chains, finding that roughly 25% of the mentions refer to the article’s “main concept.” They then describe simple baselines and a basic classifier that outperforms them. Importantly, integrating their classifier into the Stanford (rule-based) coreference system yields substantial improvements over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia.

I believe this paper proposes an interesting and valuable twist on coreference. It makes good sense from an information extraction perspective, has the potential to reinvigorate coreference research, and interestingly bridges the gap between coreference and entity linking. I am often skeptical of papers that introduce a new task where standard systems perform poorly, then simply demonstrate improved performance with a minor tweak. However, in this case, the task itself is highly motivating. It doesn’t feel like the authors are simply seeking a new domain; rather, it genuinely highlights a situation where standard systems struggle in a setting that is actually quite important.

**THE TASK:** Main concept resolution is an intriguing task from an information extraction perspective. I can envision numerous scenarios where documents primarily revolve around a specific entity – biographical documents, dossiers or briefings about a person or event, clinical records, and so on – where the information we need to extract is specifically related to that entity. The standard coreference task often includes a large number of mentions that are irrelevant for most IE problems (such as generic mentions). This task, in contrast, focuses on mentions that demonstrably *do* matter.

From a methodological standpoint, the notion of a “main concept” provides a useful discourse anchor for coreference. There still appears to be significant room for improvement beyond the baselines, particularly concerning non-pronominal mentions. Performing coreference directly on Wikipedia also opens opportunities for leveraging knowledge, which the authors demonstrate in this work. I anticipate this domain will be an interesting testbed for ideas that improve coreference generally, but where robust improvements might be more difficult to achieve and obscured by other challenges in a more general setting. 

Furthermore, unlike previous work that has isolated a specific aspect of coreference (e.g., the Winograd Schema work), this paper significantly impacts the metrics of the overall coreference problem within a domain (Wikipedia) that is of considerable interest to the ACL community.

**THE TECHNIQUES:** The techniques themselves aren’t the strongest aspect of this paper, though they appear effective. The features used seem sensible, and exploring conjunctions of these features might prove beneficial. It's unclear whether the authors experimented with this. They should also explicitly state earlier in the paper that their primary main concept resolution system is a binary classifier; this isn't clearly stated initially, and the model remains undefined throughout the featurization description.

**MINOR DETAILS:**

Organization: I suggest introducing the dataset immediately after the “Related Works” section (making it the new Section 3), allowing for concrete results to be presented in “Baselines” and further motivating the “Approach” section.

When Section 4 refers to Dcoref and Scoref, please cite the relevant Stanford papers or clarify that these refer to the Stanford coreference system. Many readers will be unfamiliar with these specific names.

The term “candidate list” is unclear, particularly in the following passage: “We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link.” 

Please clarify that the “candidate list” refers to the set of mentions within the article that are potential coreference candidates for the main concept. I believe most readers will understand that this module aims to import semantic information from Wikipedia’s link structure (e.g., if a mention is hyperlinked to a Freebase article identified as female, that mention is also classified as female). Therefore, maintaining clear terminology is crucial.

Section 6.1 states, “we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010).” However, Section 4.1 implies these are identical. I’m unclear on where additional WCR mentions would be extracted.
