The authors present a novel coreference task focused on Wikipedia articles: identifying the coreference chain corresponding to the article’s main concept. They introduce a newly annotated dataset of 30 documents, where approximately 25% of mentions pertain to this “main concept.” They then demonstrate that a classifier, designed specifically for this task, outperforms established baselines and significantly improves performance when integrated with the Stanford rule-based coreference system, achieving state-of-the-art results on Wikipedia text.

This paper proposes a compelling adaptation of coreference resolution with clear relevance for information extraction. Unlike many papers that introduce new tasks solely to showcase improvements over standard systems, this work addresses a genuinely important problem. The task of main concept resolution is highly motivated—many real-world information extraction applications focus on extracting information specifically *about* a central entity, rendering irrelevant the numerous generic coreferences typically found in standard coreference datasets. This work feels less like domain-seeking and more like identifying a crucial subproblem within coreference.

**Contributions:**

1.  **A Novel and Motivated Coreference Task:** The authors define and investigate the task of main concept resolution in Wikipedia, which is inherently useful for information extraction and distinguishes itself from existing coreference benchmarks. This task provides a focused setting for evaluating coreference systems in a practical context.
2.  **A New Dataset:** The authors provide a valuable resource – a manually annotated dataset (WCR) specifically designed for evaluating main concept resolution. This dataset enables targeted evaluation and comparison of coreference systems.
3.  **Improved Performance on a Challenging Domain:** The authors demonstrate significant improvements in coreference performance on Wikipedia text, a notoriously difficult domain, by tailoring a classifier to the main concept resolution task and integrating it with a strong rule-based system.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Task Motivation:** The task is well-justified and relevant to many information extraction applications. The authors clearly articulate why identifying mentions of the main concept is a valuable goal, particularly in scenarios dealing with biographical articles, reports, and clinical records.
2.  **Dataset Quality:** The creation of a dedicated dataset allows for rigorous evaluation of systems designed for this specific task. The size of the dataset, while modest, appears sufficient to demonstrate meaningful improvements.
3.  **Impactful Results:** The performance gains achieved by integrating the authors’ classifier with Dcoref are substantial, indicating a genuine contribution to the field. Surpassing state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach.
4.  **Bridging the Gap:** This work connects coreference resolution with entity linking, potentially stimulating cross-pollination of ideas and techniques between these two research areas.
5.  **Practical Applicability:** The authors’ approach focuses on a scenario where coreference resolution is often a bottleneck in information extraction pipelines, making the work immediately applicable to real-world problems.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Feature Engineering:** While the features used are sensible, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of their relative importance or exploration of more complex feature combinations (e.g., conjunctions). A more thorough feature ablation study would strengthen the paper.
2.  **Classifier Description:** The paper doesn't clearly state upfront that the core of the main concept resolution system is a binary classifier. This information is revealed later, which can lead to confusion. The model should be explicitly described early in the work.
3.  **Ambiguous Terminology:** The term "candidate list" is used unclearly, especially when describing the hyperlink structure enrichment process. Clarifying its meaning would improve readability.
4.  **Potential Data Discrepancy:** There’s a possible inconsistency between Sections 4.1 and 6.1 regarding the inclusion of additional mentions extracted using the Raghunathan et al. (2010) method. The authors should clarify whether additional mentions were added to the WCR dataset and how.
5.  **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors mention some errors, a more in-depth error analysis would provide valuable insights into the limitations of their approach and potential areas for future improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Did you experiment with conjunctions of features, and if so, what were the results?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the "candidate list" mentioned in Section 4.1?
3.  Could you clarify if additional mentions were added to the WCR dataset using the Raghunathan et al. (2010) method, and if so, how?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, including common types of errors and potential reasons for those errors?
