This paper introduces a novel coreference resolution task focused on identifying mentions of the main concept within a Wikipedia article. The authors annotate 30 documents with full coreference chains, highlighting those mentions (approximately 25%) that refer to the article’s central entity. They present baseline performance and a binary classification approach that surpasses these baselines, and importantly, demonstrate substantial improvements when integrating this classifier into the Stanford coreference system, achieving state-of-the-art results on Wikipedia coreference. 

The task itself is a compelling contribution. While I am often critical of work that defines new tasks simply to demonstrate shortcomings of existing systems, this work presents a genuinely motivating problem. Identifying coreference specifically related to the main subject of a document – a scenario common in information extraction tasks involving biographical texts, reports, or clinical records – is a valuable capability. Standard coreference resolution often includes many irrelevant mentions, while this task focuses on extracting information directly pertaining to the core entity.

From a methodological perspective, framing the task around a “main concept” provides a useful discourse anchor for coreference, though significant improvements beyond the initial baselines remain possible, especially for non-pronominal mentions.  The application of coreference resolution directly to Wikipedia data also facilitates the incorporation of external knowledge resources, a point well-illustrated by the authors. This domain offers a promising testbed for coreference techniques that might yield more robust improvements in this focused setting than would be achievable in general coreference, where gains can be obscured by the complexity of broader linguistic phenomena. Furthermore, unlike prior work that isolates specific aspects of coreference (such as Winograd schemas), this research demonstrably impacts overall coreference metrics within a widely studied domain – Wikipedia – relevant to a broad segment of the ACL community.

The technical contributions, while effective, are not the paper’s strongest element. The features employed are sensible, and exploring combinations of these features could potentially yield further improvements. Crucially, the paper would benefit from an earlier and more explicit statement that the primary main concept (MC) resolution system is a binary classifier. Currently, the model remains implicitly defined throughout the description of feature engineering.

Several organizational and clarity improvements would strengthen the paper. I recommend moving the dataset description immediately after the Related Works section (creating a new Section 3) to better motivate the subsequent sections on baselines and the proposed approach with concrete results. In Section 4, when referencing Dcoref and Scoref, either cite the original Stanford coreference system papers or clearly identify them as components of the Stanford system, as these names may be unfamiliar to many readers. 

The term “candidate list” requires clarification. Specifically, the explanation following "We leverage the hyperlink structure..." is unclear.  The paper should explicitly state that the “candidate list” refers to the set of mentions within the article considered as potential coreferents of the main concept. While readers will likely infer the intended function of incorporating semantic information from Wikipedia’s link structure (e.g., gender information from Freebase), maintaining clear terminology is vital.

Finally, there is an apparent inconsistency between Section 6.1 and Section 4.1. Section 6.1 states that the system considers the union of WCR mentions and mentions predicted by Raghunathan et al. (2010), while Section 4.1 suggests these are the same set of mentions. The source of any additional WCR mentions needs clarification.
