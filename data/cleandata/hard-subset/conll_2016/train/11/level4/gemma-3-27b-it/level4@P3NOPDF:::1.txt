This paper presents a novel and valuable task within coreference resolution: identifying coreference chains specifically related to the main concept of a Wikipedia article. The authors annotate 30 documents, identifying approximately 25% of mentions as referring to the article’s central entity, and demonstrate that a simple classifier outperforms baseline methods. Crucially, integrating this classifier into the Stanford coreference system yields substantial improvements, establishing new state-of-the-art performance on Wikipedia coreference.

I find this work particularly compelling. While I am often skeptical of papers introducing new tasks simply to demonstrate weaknesses in existing systems, this task is inherently motivating from an information extraction perspective. It addresses a practical and important scenario – documents centered around a specific entity, such as biographies or clinical records – where focusing coreference on the primary subject matter is critical. The authors don’t appear to be seeking a domain simply to achieve performance gains; instead, they highlight a genuine gap where standard coreference systems perform poorly in a particularly relevant setting. 

The task of main concept resolution is insightful from an IE standpoint. Standard coreference resolution often generates numerous mentions that are largely irrelevant for many downstream IE applications (e.g., generic references). This new task focuses explicitly on mentions that *do* matter, providing a discourse anchor beneficial for coreference and facilitating the incorporation of external knowledge readily available in Wikipedia. The authors rightly suggest that this domain provides a valuable testbed for coreference improvements, potentially revealing advancements that would be difficult to isolate and measure in a more general setting. Furthermore, this work stands out from other focused coreference efforts (e.g., Winograd schema) by delivering a significant impact on coreference metrics within a widely studied domain – Wikipedia – attracting considerable interest from the ACL community.

The technical contributions, while not the primary strength of the paper, are nonetheless effective. The features employed are sensible and well-motivated. Exploring combinations of these features, however, could potentially yield further gains, and the extent of this exploration should be clarified. A key improvement would be to explicitly state early in the paper that the primary MC resolution system is a binary classifier. Currently, the model remains undefined during the featurization description, hindering comprehension.

Several minor organizational and clarity issues could be addressed. I suggest moving the dataset description immediately after the “Related Works” section (becoming the new Section 3). This would allow for the presentation of concrete results in “Baselines,” further emphasizing the motivation for the “Approach” section. 

In Section 4, when referencing Dcoref and Scoref, please either cite the relevant Stanford papers or explicitly state that these refer to the Stanford coreference system. Many readers will be unfamiliar with these abbreviations. 

The term “candidate list” requires clarification, particularly in the following sentence: “We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link.” Please clearly define the “candidate list” as the set of mentions within the article that are potential coreferents of the main concept. While the intent – importing semantic information from Wikipedia’s link structure (e.g., gender information from Freebase) – is generally understandable, precise terminology will enhance clarity.

Finally, Section 6.1 states, “we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010).” However, Section 4.1 implies that these are identical. Clarify where additional WCR mentions are extracted to resolve this apparent discrepancy.
