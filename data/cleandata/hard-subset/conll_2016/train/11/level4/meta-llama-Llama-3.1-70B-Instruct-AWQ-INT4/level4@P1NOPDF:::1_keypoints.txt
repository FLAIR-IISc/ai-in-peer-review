- Presents a novel "main concept" coreference task for Wikipedia, with 30 fully annotated articles, simple baselines, a classifier that beats them, and integration into Stanford coref yielding strong improvements.  
- Task is well-motivated and could bridge coreference and entity linking, with potential to refocus coreference research toward information extraction use-cases.  
- Reviewer is generally positive and finds the task meaningful rather than a contrived domain tweak.  
- Main concept resolution targets mentions that matter for IE (avoiding generic/irrelevant mentions) and is useful for documents centered on a single entity.  
- The "main concept" acts as a useful discourse anchor, but substantial work remains to improve beyond baselines, especially on non-pronominal mentions.  
- Using Wikipedia enables incorporation of external knowledge and makes a promising testbed for methods that might be hard to validate in the general setting.  
- Unlike niche splits (e.g., Winograd), this task has measurable impact on overall coreference metrics in a community-relevant domain.  
- Techniques are effective but not novel; feature design is sensible but may benefit from feature conjunctions and more experimentation.  
- The primary MC resolution system is a binary classifier, but this is not stated early enough and the model is insufficiently defined during feature discussion.  
- Recommend moving the dataset description earlier (after Related Work) so baselines/results can better motivate the approach.  
- Ask to cite/clarify Dcoref and Scoref as the Stanford coreference system (cite relevant Stanford papers).  
- The term "candidate list" is unclear—clarify it means the set of mentions in the article considered as MC candidates when describing hyperlink-based semantic enrichment.  
- Point out an apparent inconsistency: Section 6.1's union of WCR mentions and mentions from Raghunathan et al. (2010) conflicts with Section 4.1—clarify where additional WCR mentions are coming from.
