The authors present a novel adaptation of the coreference task, specifically designed for Wikipedia. The task involves identifying the coreference chain that corresponds to the entity that the Wikipedia article is about. The authors annotate 30 documents with all coreference chains, of which approximately 25% of the mentions refer to the "main concept" of the article. They then describe simple baselines and a basic classifier that outperforms these. Furthermore, they integrate their classifier into the Stanford (rule-based) coreference system and achieve substantial improvements over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia.

I believe this paper proposes an interesting twist on coreference that makes sense from an information extraction perspective, has the potential to revitalize and shake up coreference research, and might bridge the gap between coreference literature and entity linking literature in an interesting way. I am sometimes unimpressed by papers that introduce a new task that standard systems perform poorly on and then propose a tweak to improve their system's performance. However, in this case, the actual task itself is quite motivating, and rather than the authors seeking a new domain to apply their system, it genuinely feels like they are addressing a significant limitation of standard systems in a setting that is actually pretty important.

The task of main concept resolution is intriguing from an information extraction perspective. I can imagine numerous scenarios where documents primarily revolve around a particular entity (biographical documents, dossiers or briefings about a person or event, clinical records, etc.) and where the information we care about extracting is specific to that entity. The standard coreference task has always had the issue of large numbers of mentions that would seemingly be irrelevant for most information extraction problems (like generic mentions), and this task is undoubtedly composed of mentions that actually do matter.

From a methodology standpoint, the notion of a "main concept" provides a useful discourse anchor for coreference, but there appears to still be substantial overhead to improve beyond the baselines, particularly on non-pronominal mentions. Doing coreference directly on Wikipedia also opens the doors for more interesting use of knowledge, which the authors illustrate here. I believe this domain is likely to be an interesting testbed for ideas that would improve coreference overall, but which in the general setting would be more difficult to achieve robust improvements with and would be overshadowed by the amount of work dealing with other aspects of the problem.

Moreover, unlike past work that has carved off a slice of coreference (e.g., the Winograd schema work), this paper makes a significant impact on the metrics of the overall coreference problem on a domain (Wikipedia) that many in the ACL community are interested in.

The techniques presented in the paper are not its strong point, although they do seem to be effective. The features appear sensible, but it seems like additional conjunctions of these may help (and it's unclear whether the authors experimented with this). The authors should also explicitly state earlier in the work that their primary main concept (MC) resolution system is a binary classifier; this is not clearly stated early enough, and the model is left undefined throughout the description of featurization.

In terms of minor details, I suggest introducing the dataset immediately after "Related Works" (i.e., having it be the new Section 3) so that concrete results can be presented in "Baselines," further motivating "Approach." When Section 4 refers to Dcoref and Scoref, it would be helpful to cite the Stanford papers or make it clear that it's the Stanford coreference system (many readers may be unfamiliar with the Dcoref/Scoref names).

The use of the term "candidate list" was unclear, especially in the following sentence: "We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link." It would be helpful to clarify that the "candidate list" refers to the set of mentions in the article that are possible candidates for being coreferent with the MC. I believe most readers will understand that this module is intended to import semantic information from the link structure of Wikipedia (e.g., if a mention is hyperlinked to an article that is female in Freebase, that mention is female), so it's essential to keep the terminology clear.

Section 6.1 states, "we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)." However, Section 4.1 implies that these are the same? I'm missing where additional WCR mentions would be extracted.
