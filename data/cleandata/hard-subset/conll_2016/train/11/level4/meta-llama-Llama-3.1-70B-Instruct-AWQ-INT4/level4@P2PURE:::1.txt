The authors propose a novel version of the coreference task, tailored to the Wikipedia domain, where the objective is to identify the coreference chain corresponding to the entity that the Wikipedia article is about. They annotate 30 documents with all coreference chains, of which approximately 25% of the mentions refer to the "main concept" of the article. The authors then describe simple baselines and a basic classifier that outperforms these. Furthermore, they integrate their classifier into the Stanford (rule-based) coreference system, achieving substantial benefits over all state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia.

This paper presents an interesting twist on coreference that makes sense from an information extraction perspective, with the potential to revitalize and shake up coreference research. It may also bridge the gap between coreference literature and entity linking literature in an interesting way. Unlike papers that introduce a new task that standard systems perform poorly on and then propose a tweak to improve performance, this paper's task is well-motivated, and it genuinely addresses a significant shortcoming of standard systems in an important setting.

The main concept resolution task is intriguing from an information extraction perspective, as it focuses on extracting information specific to the primary entity in a document. This task is composed of relevant mentions, avoiding the issue of generic mentions that are often irrelevant in standard coreference tasks. The notion of a "main concept" provides a useful discourse anchor for coreference, although there is still room for improvement beyond the baselines, particularly for non-pronominal mentions. Performing coreference directly on Wikipedia also opens up opportunities for more interesting uses of knowledge, as illustrated by the authors.

This domain is likely to be an interesting testbed for ideas that could improve coreference overall, but which might be difficult to evaluate in a general setting. Unlike past work that has focused on a specific aspect of coreference, this paper has a significant impact on the overall coreference problem in a domain (Wikipedia) that is of great interest to the ACL community.

While the techniques presented in the paper are effective, they are not its strongest aspect. The features used seem sensible, but additional conjunctions of these features may help improve performance. It is unclear whether the authors experimented with this approach. The authors should also explicitly state earlier in the paper that their primary main concept (MC) resolution system is a binary classifier, as this is not clearly stated until later in the paper.

Some minor suggestions for improvement include reorganizing the paper to introduce the dataset immediately after the "Related Works" section, allowing for more concrete results to be presented in the "Baselines" section. When referencing Dcoref and Scoref in Section 4, the authors should cite the Stanford papers or clarify that it refers to the Stanford coreference system, as many readers may be unfamiliar with these terms.

The term "candidate list" is unclear, particularly in the sentence: "We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link." It would be helpful to clarify that the "candidate list" refers to the set of mentions in the article that are possible candidates for being coreferent with the main concept.

Finally, there appears to be a discrepancy between Section 6.1, which mentions considering the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010), and Section 4.1, which implies that these are the same. It is unclear where additional WCR mentions would be extracted.
