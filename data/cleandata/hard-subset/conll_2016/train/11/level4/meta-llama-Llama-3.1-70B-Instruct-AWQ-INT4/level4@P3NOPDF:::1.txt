The authors present a new version of the coreference task tailored to Wikipedia, focusing on identifying the coreference chain specifically corresponding to the entity that the Wikipedia article is about. This task, referred to as main concept resolution, is an intriguing twist on traditional coreference that makes good sense from an information extraction perspective. By annotating 30 documents with all coreference chains, the authors demonstrate that roughly 25% of the mentions refer to the "main concept" of the article, highlighting the potential of this task to revitalize and shake up coreference research.

The authors propose simple baselines and a basic classifier that outperforms these baselines. Furthermore, they integrate their classifier into the Stanford (rule-based) coreference system, resulting in substantial benefits over all state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia. This integration demonstrates the potential of main concept resolution to bridge the gap between coreference literature and entity linking literature.

The task itself is well-motivated and addresses a significant limitation of standard coreference systems. Unlike papers that introduce new tasks solely to demonstrate the shortcomings of existing systems, this paper presents a task that is genuinely important and relevant to information extraction. The authors' approach feels more like a genuine attempt to address a real-world problem rather than simply trying to create a new domain to showcase their system.

From a methodology standpoint, the notion of a "main concept" provides a useful discourse anchor for coreference. However, there appears to be substantial overhead to improve beyond the baselines, particularly on non-pronominal mentions. The use of Wikipedia as a domain for coreference opens up interesting possibilities for incorporating knowledge, as illustrated by the authors.

The techniques presented in the paper are effective but not particularly novel. The features used seem sensible, but it is unclear whether the authors experimented with additional conjunctions of these features. The authors should explicitly state earlier in the paper that their primary main concept resolution system is a binary classifier, as this is not clearly defined until later in the description of featurization.

In terms of minor details, the organization of the paper could be improved by introducing the dataset immediately after the "Related Works" section. This would allow for concrete results to be presented in the "Baselines" section, further motivating the "Approach" section. Additionally, the authors should clarify the use of the term "candidate list" and provide a clear definition of this term. In Section 6.1, the authors should clarify the relationship between WCR mentions and the mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010).

Overall, this paper presents an interesting and well-motivated task that has the potential to revitalize coreference research. While the techniques presented are not particularly novel, the authors' approach to main concept resolution is effective and demonstrates the potential of this task to address real-world problems in information extraction.
