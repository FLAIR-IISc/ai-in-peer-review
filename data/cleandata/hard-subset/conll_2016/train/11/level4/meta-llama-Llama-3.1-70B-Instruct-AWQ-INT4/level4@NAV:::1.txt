The authors introduce a novel adaptation of the coreference task, specifically designed for Wikipedia articles. This task involves identifying the coreference chain that corresponds to the entity central to the article. To achieve this, the authors annotated 30 documents, encompassing all coreference chains, and found that approximately 25% of the mentions pertain to the article's primary concept. They then present simple baselines and a basic classifier that outperforms these, ultimately integrating their classifier into the Stanford coreference system and demonstrating significant improvements over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia.

I find this paper's proposal to be an intriguing variation on coreference, offering a compelling perspective from an information extraction standpoint. It has the potential to revitalize coreference research and bridge the gap between coreference and entity linking literature in a meaningful way.

The task of main concept resolution is particularly captivating from an information extraction perspective, as it can be applied to various document types, such as biographical documents, clinical records, and event briefings, where the primary entity is the focal point. This task effectively filters out irrelevant mentions, addressing a long-standing issue in standard coreference tasks.

From a methodological standpoint, the concept of a "main concept" provides a useful discourse anchor for coreference. However, there is still room for improvement beyond the baselines, particularly for non-pronominal mentions. Conducting coreference directly on Wikipedia also presents opportunities for incorporating knowledge, as demonstrated by the authors.

The techniques presented in this paper, while effective, are not its strongest aspect. The features appear sensible, but additional experimentation with conjunctions of these features may be beneficial. The authors should explicitly state that their primary main concept resolution system is a binary classifier earlier in the paper.

Minor suggestions for improvement include reorganizing the paper to introduce the dataset immediately after the "Related Works" section, providing clearer citations for the Stanford coreference system, and clarifying the term "candidate list" to refer to the set of mentions in the article that are potential candidates for being coreferent with the main concept. Additionally, the authors should revisit the description of their method in Section 6.1 to ensure consistency with the implications in Section 4.1.
