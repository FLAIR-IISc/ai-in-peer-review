**Summary of the Paper**

The authors present a new version of the coreference task tailored to Wikipedia. The task is to identify the coreference chain specifically corresponding to the entity that the Wikipedia article is about. The authors annotate 30 documents with all coreference chains, of which roughly 25% of the mentions refer to the "main concept" of the article. They then describe some simple baselines and a basic classifier which outperforms these. Moreover, they integrate their classifier into the Stanford (rule-based) coreference system and see substantial benefit over all state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Introduction of the Main Concept Resolution Task**: The authors propose a new task that is an interesting twist on coreference, making good sense from an information extraction perspective.
2. **Development of a Classifier for Main Concept Resolution**: The authors develop a basic classifier that outperforms simple baselines and integrates well with the Stanford coreference system.
3. **Improvement of Coreference Resolution on Wikipedia**: The authors show that their approach improves coreference resolution on Wikipedia, a domain of interest to the ACL community.

**Strengths**

1. **Interesting and Motivating Task**: The main concept resolution task is intriguing from an information extraction perspective and has the potential to revitalize and shake up coreference research.
2. **Effective Techniques**: The authors' techniques, although not the strong point of the paper, seem to be effective in improving coreference resolution on Wikipedia.
3. **Impact on the ACL Community**: The paper makes a big impact on the metrics of the overall coreference problem on a domain (Wikipedia) that many in the ACL community are interested in.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Techniques Not the Strong Point**: The techniques presented in the paper are not particularly innovative or strong.
2. **Lack of Experimentation**: The authors do not provide enough experimentation with additional conjunctions of features or other techniques that could improve the results.
3. **Unclear Terminology**: Some terminology, such as "candidate list," is unclear and should be better explained.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more experimentation with additional conjunctions of features or other techniques that could improve the results?
2. Can you clarify the terminology used in the paper, such as "candidate list"?
3. Can you provide more details on how the WCR mentions and predicted mentions are extracted and used in the approach?
