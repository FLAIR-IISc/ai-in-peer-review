The authors present a novel version of the coreference task, tailored to the Wikipedia domain, which focuses on identifying the coreference chain corresponding to the entity that the article is about. They annotate 30 documents with all coreference chains, where approximately 25% of the mentions refer to the "main concept" of the article. The authors then describe simple baselines and a basic classifier that outperforms these. Furthermore, they integrate their classifier into the Stanford rule-based coreference system, achieving substantial benefits over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia.

This paper proposes an interesting twist on coreference, making good sense from an information extraction perspective. It has the potential to revitalize and shake up coreference research, bridging the gap between coreference literature and entity linking literature in an intriguing way. Unlike papers that introduce a new task where standard systems perform poorly and then propose a tweak to improve performance, this task is motivating and addresses a significant issue. Standard systems perform poorly in this setting, which is actually quite important.

The task of main concept resolution is intriguing from an information extraction perspective. Documents often revolve around a particular entity, such as biographical documents, dossiers, or clinical records, where the information to be extracted is specific to that entity. The standard coreference task has the issue of large numbers of irrelevant mentions, whereas this task focuses on relevant mentions. The notion of a "main concept" provides a useful discourse anchor for coreference, but there is still substantial overhead to improve beyond the baselines, particularly on non-pronominal mentions. Doing coreference directly on Wikipedia opens the doors for more interesting use of knowledge, as illustrated by the authors.

This domain is likely to be an interesting testbed for ideas that improve coreference overall, but which in the general setting would be more difficult to achieve robust improvements with. Unlike past work that has carved off a slice of coreference, this paper has a significant impact on the metrics of the overall coreference problem on a domain that many in the ACL community are interested in.

The techniques presented in the paper are not its strong point, although they seem to be effective. The features appear sensible, but additional conjunctions of these may help. It is unclear whether the authors experimented with this approach. The authors should state earlier in the work that their primary MC resolution system is a binary classifier, which is not explicitly stated early enough, and the model is left undefined throughout the description of featurization.

To improve the paper, consider the following minor details. The organization could be improved by introducing the dataset immediately after "Related Works" so that concrete results can be given in "Baselines", further motivating "Approach". When referring to Dcoref and Scoref in Section 4, cite the Stanford papers or make it clear that it's the Stanford coreference system. The use of the term "candidate list" was unclear, especially in the context of leveraging the hyperlink structure of the article. Please clarify that the "candidate list" refers to the set of mentions in the article that are possible candidates for being coreferent with the MC.

In Section 6.1, it is stated that the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010) is considered. However, Section 4.1 implies that these are the same. It is unclear where additional WCR mentions would be extracted.
