This paper introduces Main Concept Resolution, a novel variant of the coreference resolution task tailored to Wikipedia. The goal is to identify the coreference chain corresponding to the primary entity of a given article. To support this task, the authors have created a new dataset by annotating 30 Wikipedia articles with full coreference chains, finding that approximately 25% of all mentions refer to the article's "main concept." They present several baselines and a classifier-based approach that outperforms them. Furthermore, integrating their classifier into the Stanford rule-based coreference system yields substantial improvements over state-of-the-art systems on this Wikipedia dataset.

This paper proposes an interesting direction for coreference resolution that is well-motivated from an information extraction (IE) perspective. The task has the potential to stimulate new research and provides a valuable bridge between the coreference and entity linking literature. While new tasks are sometimes proposed simply to highlight the deficiencies of existing systems, the task presented here is compelling in its own right. Rather than being an artificially constructed problem, it highlights a genuine and important setting where standard systems underperform, thereby motivating a valuable line of research.

**The Task**

Main Concept Resolution is an intriguing task from an IE perspective. In many applications, documents revolve around a central entity (e.g., biographies, dossiers, clinical records), and the primary goal is to extract information specifically about that entity. A common challenge in standard coreference resolution is the prevalence of mentions, such as generics, that are often irrelevant to downstream IE tasks. In contrast, this task focuses exclusively on the mentions that are most critical for understanding the document's main subject.

Methodologically, the "main concept" provides a useful discourse anchor for coreference. However, the results show there is still substantial room for improvement over the baselines, particularly for non-pronominal mentions. Furthermore, applying coreference resolution to Wikipedia enables the integration of external knowledge, as the authors demonstrate. This focused domain is therefore a promising testbed for developing new ideas that could benefit coreference resolution more broadly. It allows for experimenting with techniques whose effects might be harder to isolate or measure in a general setting, where they could be overshadowed by other challenges of the full coreference task.

Moreover, unlike some prior work that has focused on narrow subsets of the coreference problem (e.g., the Winograd Schema Challenge), this work has a significant impact on overall coreference performance on Wikipedia, a domain of considerable interest to the research community.

**The Techniques**

The technical contribution of this paper is not its primary strength, although the proposed methods are effective. The features used are sensible, but it is possible that additional feature conjunctions could provide further gains; it is unclear if the authors explored this. A more significant point of clarification is needed regarding the model itself: the authors should state explicitly and much earlier that their primary system is a binary classifier. The model architecture is not defined until late in the paper, leaving the reader unclear during the feature description section.

**Minor Details**

*   **Organization**: I suggest introducing the dataset immediately after the "Related Work" section. This would allow the "Baselines" section to present concrete results on the dataset, which would in turn better motivate the proposed "Approach."

*   In Section 4, when referring to Dcoref and Scoref, the authors should cite the relevant papers or explicitly state that these are components of the Stanford coreference system, as these names may be unfamiliar to some readers.

*   The term "candidate list" is unclear, particularly in the sentence: "We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link." The authors should explicitly define the "candidate list" as the set of all mentions in the article being considered as potentially coreferent with the main concept. While the overall goal of using Wikipedia's link structure to import semantic information is understandable, clear terminology is essential.

*   There appears to be a contradiction or lack of clarity between Section 4.1 and 6.1. Section 6.1 states that the system considers "the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)," but Section 4.1 seems to imply that these two sets of mentions are identical. It is unclear where any additional WCR mentions would originate.
