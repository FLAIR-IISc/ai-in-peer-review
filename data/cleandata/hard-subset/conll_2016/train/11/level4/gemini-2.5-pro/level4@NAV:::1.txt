This paper introduces a novel adaptation of the coreference task, specifically designed for the Wikipedia domain. The objective is to resolve the coreference chain for the primary entity that serves as the subject of a given Wikipedia article. To this end, the authors have created a new dataset by annotating 30 documents with full coreference chains, finding that approximately 25% of mentions refer to the article's "main concept." The paper presents several simple baselines, which are subsequently outperformed by a basic classifier developed by the authors. Furthermore, by integrating their classifier into the Stanford rule-based coreference system, they demonstrate significant performance gains over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia data.

In my view, this paper offers a compelling new direction for coreference research. It is well-grounded in the needs of information extraction, has the potential to reinvigorate the field, and could effectively bridge the gap between the coreference and entity linking communities. While many papers that define a new task merely to highlight the shortcomings of existing systems can be unconvincing, this work is a notable exception. The task itself is highly motivated, and the authors effectively demonstrate that standard systems fail in a genuinely important and practical setting, rather than simply identifying a niche domain for their method.

**THE TASK:** From an information extraction standpoint, the proposed task of main concept resolution is particularly compelling. There are numerous applications where documents are centered on a specific entity—such as biographies, intelligence briefings, or clinical records—and the primary goal is to extract information pertinent to that entity. A known limitation of the standard coreference task is its inclusion of numerous mentions (e.g., generic mentions) that are often irrelevant for downstream IE applications. This new task, by contrast, focuses exclusively on mentions of high importance.

Methodologically, defining a "main concept" introduces a valuable discourse anchor for the coreference process. Nevertheless, the results indicate that significant challenges remain in moving beyond the baseline performance, especially for non-pronominal mentions. Performing coreference on Wikipedia also creates opportunities for the novel integration of external knowledge, a potential which the authors begin to explore. Consequently, this domain appears to be a promising testbed for developing ideas that could advance coreference research more broadly. Such ideas might be difficult to evaluate or show robust improvements for in a general setting, where their impact could be overshadowed by other aspects of the problem.

Moreover, in contrast to previous work that has focused on narrow subsets of the coreference problem (such as the Winograd schema), this paper's contribution has a substantial impact on overall coreference metrics within a domain of wide interest to the ACL community.

**THE TECHNIQUES:** The primary strength of this paper lies more in the task formulation than in the technical approach, although the proposed methods are demonstrated to be effective. The feature set is logical, though it is unclear if the authors experimented with feature conjunctions, which could potentially yield further improvements. For clarity, the authors should specify earlier in the manuscript that their main concept resolution model is a binary classifier. This detail is currently absent from the feature description section, leaving the model undefined for too long.

**MINOR DETAILS:**
*   **Organization:** It is suggested to restructure the paper by introducing the dataset immediately following the "Related Works" section. This would allow the "Baselines" section to present concrete results, which would better motivate the "Approach" section that follows.
*   **Citations:** In Section 4, the references to "Dcoref" and "Scoref" should be accompanied by citations to the relevant Stanford papers or it should be explicitly stated that these are components of the Stanford coreference system, as these names may not be familiar to all readers.
*   **Terminology:** The term "candidate list" is used ambiguously, for instance, in the sentence, "For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link." It should be explicitly defined that the "candidate list" refers to the set of all mentions within the article that are candidates for being coreferent with the main concept. The intention of this module—to import semantic information from Wikipedia's link structure—is understandable, but precise terminology is crucial for clarity.
*   **Inconsistency:** There appears to be an inconsistency between Section 6.1 and Section 4.1. Section 6.1 states that the evaluation considers "the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)," whereas Section 4.1 seems to suggest these two sets of mentions are identical. Clarification is needed on where any additional WCR mentions originate.
