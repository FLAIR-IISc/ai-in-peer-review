### Summary of the paper

This paper introduces and formalizes the task of Main Concept Resolution (MCR) for Wikipedia, which involves identifying all mentions within an article that refer to the article's main topic. The authors create a new dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles annotated with full coreference chains to serve as a testbed. They demonstrate that state-of-the-art coreference systems perform poorly on this domain. To address the MCR task, they develop a binary SVM classifier that leverages features derived from Wikipedia's structure (e.g., hyperlinks) and external knowledge from Freebase. Their approach significantly outperforms several strong baselines built from state-of-the-art systems. Furthermore, they show that integrating their MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into the Stanford rule-based coreference system (Dcoref) improves its performance on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia articles.

### Contributions

In my view, the main contributions of this work, in order of importance, are:

1.  **The proposal and formalization of the "Main Concept Resolution" task.** This is an interesting and practical specialization of the general coreference problem. It is highly relevant for information extraction tasks where the primary goal is to gather facts about a specific, central entity in a document. The creation and release of the WCR dataset provides a valuable resource for future research on this task and on coreference in the Wikipedia domain.

2.  **A clear empirical demonstration that state-of-the-art coreference systems struggle on Wikipedia text.** By evaluating five recent systems on their new dataset, the authors provide compelling evidence for the domain-specificity of coreference models and motivate the need for new, domain-adapted approaches. This finding highlights a significant gap in current coreference resolution technology.

3.  **An effective, knowledge-rich classifier for the MCR task.** While the machine learning techniques are standard, the authors design an effective feature set that successfully exploits the unique structure of Wikipedia and linked knowledge bases. The integration of this classifier into a full system (Dcoref++) demonstrates a practical pathway for improving general-purpose coreference resolvers in this domain.

### Strengths

1.  **The proposed task is well-motivated and potentially impactful.** Many real-world applications, particularly in Information Extraction, are concerned with a document's primary subject (e.g., a person in a biography, a company in a report). By focusing on the main concept's coreference chain, this work sidesteps many irrelevant mentions (e.g., generics) that complicate the standard coreference task, while focusing on mentions that are highly valuable. This framing provides a compelling bridge between the coreference resolution and entity linking communities and has the potential to stimulate new research directions.

2.  **The paper provides a strong justification for the work through a thorough analysis of existing systems.** The authors do not simply claim that a new task is necessary; they provide clear evidence (Table 1) that five different state-of-the-art systems, trained on newswire, experience a significant performance drop on Wikipedia data. This rigorous analysis establishes the importance of the problem and makes a convincing case that Wikipedia is a domain worthy of dedicated study.

3.  **The empirical results are strong and well-supported.** The proposed MCR classifier achieves an F1 score of 88.79, an absolute improvement of over 13 points compared to the best-performing baseline. The ablation study (Tables 2 and 3) clearly demonstrates the contribution of each feature family. Furthermore, the improvement shown by Dcoref++ on the full coreference task (a 3.34 point F1 gain in Table 5) validates the utility of the MCR component in a larger pipeline.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The technical novelty of the proposed method is limited.** The core of the approach is a binary SVM classifier with hand-engineered features. While the features are well-chosen and effective for this task, the underlying methodology (using string matching, POS tags, and knowledge base attributes as features) is well-established in the coreference literature. The primary contribution lies in the application and task formulation rather than in a new machine learning technique.

2.  **The model description and experimental setup lack some clarity.** The paper does not explicitly state that the core MCR model is a binary classifier until Section 6.2, leaving the reader to infer the model from the description of the features in Section 4. Additionally, some terminology is ambiguous. For instance, the term "candidate list" in Section 4.1 is not clearly defined. A clearer explanation of how mentions are identified and processed would improve the paper's replicability.

3.  **The paper's organization could be improved.** The dataset (WCR) is introduced in Section 5, after the baselines and the proposed approach have already been described. Presenting the dataset and the initial evaluation of state-of-the-art systems (Table 1) earlier in the paper (e.g., after Related Work) would more effectively motivate the need for the new baselines and the proposed approach described in Sections 3 and 4.

### Questions to Authors

1.  In Section 6.1, you state that your final mention set is "the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)." However, Section 4.1 states that mentions are extracted using the algorithm from that same paper. Could you clarify the distinction between these two sets? Does the WCR annotation contain mentions that the Raghunathan et al. (2010) method does not extract?

2.  The features for your classifier appear to be used independently within the linear SVM. Did you experiment with feature conjunctions, especially for the non-pronominal model? It seems plausible that combinations of features (e.g., a partial string match *and* a matching entity type) could provide a stronger signal.
