- Introduces and formalizes "Main Concept Resolution": finding the complete coreference chain for the primary entity in a Wikipedia article.  
- Provides a new annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia documents for this task and shows SOTA coreference systems perform poorly on it.  
- Proposes a feature-based binary classifier that outperforms baselines built on existing coreference systems.  
- Integrates the classifier into Stanford Dcoref and achieves substantial improvements on full coreference for Wikipedia.  
- Contribution: formalizes a practically important subproblem of coreference focused on extracting mentions of the most salient entity.  
- Contribution: releases a Wikipedia coreference dataset highlighting domain adaptation issues from newswire to encyclopedic text.  
- Strength: task is well-motivated and valuable for downstream IE in entity-centric documents (e.g., biographies, org profiles, clinical records).  
- Strength: convincingly demonstrates a performance gap of newswire-trained SOTA systems on Wikipedia, motivating domain-specific work.  
- Strength: provides a useful constrained testbed (main concept) for future coreference and entity-linking research.  
- Weakness: limited technical novelty—method is a standard binary classifier with intuitive features rather than new ML techniques.  
- Weakness: model description is poorly organized; the classifier type (SVM) is introduced late and should appear earlier.  
- Weakness: unclear extent of feature engineering and whether feature interactions or conjunctions were explored given a linear kernel.  
- Question: consider reorganizing to introduce the dataset earlier (after Related Work) for clearer motivation with baseline numbers.  
- Question: please add explicit citations for Dcoref and Scoref when first mentioned to aid non-specialist readers.  
- Question: clarify "candidate list" in Section 4.1—is it the full set of extracted mentions?  
- Question: clarify relationship between manually annotated WCR mentions and mentions extracted via Raghunathan et al. (2010)—are they disjoint, overlapping, or does automated extraction miss gold mentions?
