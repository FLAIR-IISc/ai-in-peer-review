### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces and formalizes the task of "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR) for Wikipedia articles. The goal of this task is to identify all mentions within an article that refer to the article's main subject, or "main concept" (MC). The authors create and annotate a new 30-document dataset (WCR) for this purpose. They demonstrate that state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems, which are typically trained on newswire text, perform poorly on this dataset. To address this, they propose a feature-based binary classifier that leverages Wikipedia's unique structure (markup, hyperlinks) and external knowledge from Freebase to determine if a mention refers to the MC. This classifier significantly outperforms baselines derived from existing systems. Finally, the authors show that integrating their specialized classifier into a general rule-based coreference system (as a high-precision sieve) substantially improves its overall performance on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia text.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **The proposal and formalization of Main Concept Resolution as a new, well-motivated task.** This work carves out a practically significant sub-problem of coreference resolution. Identifying the central entity chain is highly relevant for information extraction, and the focus on Wikipedia provides a rich testbed for knowledge-intensive coreference methods. This contribution has the potential to connect the coreference and entity linking communities and spur new research directions.
2.  **The creation of a new dataset (WCR) and an empirical demonstration of the domain-gap problem.** The paper provides a valuable resource for the community and clearly shows that leading coreference systems struggle when applied to the important domain of Wikipedia. This finding highlights the need for domain adaptation and genre-specific modeling in coreference resolution.
3.  **A simple yet effective method that successfully improves a full coreference system.** The authors' classifier serves as a strong baseline for the MCR task. More importantly, its successful integration into the Stanford coreference system demonstrates that solving this sub-task can yield significant benefits for the general coreference problem in this domain.

### Strengths

1.  **High-impact and well-motivated task definition.** The paper successfully argues for the importance of Main Concept Resolution. In many real-world scenarios (e.g., biographical articles, organizational briefings, clinical records), documents are centered on a primary entity, and extracting information related to that entity is the main goal. Standard coreference resolution often produces many chains for entities of secondary importance. By focusing on the "main concept," this task isolates the most critical mentions for downstream IE applications and provides a clear, anchored structure for discourse analysis.
2.  **Creation of a valuable new testbed and strong benchmarking.** The paper does not merely identify a problem; it provides the resources to solve it. The WCR dataset, though small, is a welcome addition for studying coreference outside of the newswire domain. The authors' rigorous evaluation, showing a significant performance drop for multiple state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia text, is a key finding that underscores the importance of this work and motivates future research.
3.  **Strong empirical results demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach.** The proposed classifier achieves a substantial F1 improvement of over 13 points compared to the strongest baseline on the MCR task. Furthermore, the integration experiment (Dcoref++) is very compelling; improving a state-of-the-art system's CoNLL F1 score by over 3 absolute points on the full coreference task is a significant achievement and demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited methodological novelty.** The primary strength of this paper is its problem formulation and evaluation, not the technical sophistication of the proposed model. The core of the approach is a standard SVM classifier with well-chosen, but largely straightforward, features derived from Wikipedia and Freebase. While effective, the machine learning methodology itself is not novel. The work would be stronger if it explored more advanced modeling techniques or provided deeper insights into feature interactions.
2.  **Clarity of the model and feature engineering could be improved.** The paper does not explicitly state that the core model is a binary classifier until late in the description (Section 6.2). This should be clarified earlier, in Section 4, when the approach is first introduced. Additionally, while the feature families are sensible, the description is somewhat high-level. It is unclear, for example, whether feature conjunctions were explored, which is a common technique for linear classifiers and could have a significant impact on performance.
3.  **The baselines, while reasonable, may not be the strongest possible heuristics.** The baselines (B1-B4) are clever heuristics for extracting the MC chain from a full coreference system's output. However, they rely on post-processing. A more direct comparison might involve training a state-of-the-art statistical system (like Scoref) on the WCR data using only the features available to that system. While this would require re-training, it would provide a clearer measure of how much of the performance gain comes from the Wikipedia-specific features versus the task formulation itself. This is not a major flaw but a point for consideration.

### Questions to Authors

1.  There appears to be a slight contradiction or point of confusion between Sections 4.1 and 6.1 regarding the set of mentions used. Section 4.1 states, "We extract mentions using the same mention detection algorithm embedded in Dcoref and Scoref," which is the method from Raghunathan et al. (2010). Section 6.1 then says, "we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)." This phrasing implies the WCR-annotated mentions and the automatically predicted mentions are two distinct sets. Could you clarify if the WCR annotation was performed on a pre-existing set of automatically detected mentions, or if WCR contains gold mentions that are not always found by the automatic method?

### Additional Comments

-   **Organization:** The paper's narrative could be strengthened by introducing the WCR dataset (Section 5) immediately after "Related Works." This would allow the "Baselines" section to present concrete performance numbers on the new dataset, which would better motivate the need for the new approach described in the subsequent section.
-   **Clarity of Terminology:**
    -   In Section 4.1, the term "candidate list" is used without a clear definition. I inferred it means the set of all mentions detected in the article. Please clarify this for the reader. A clearer phrasing might be: "...we search through the set of all detected mentions in the article for any that match the surface string of the link."
    -   When introducing "Dcoref" and "Scoref," it would be helpful to explicitly state that these are names for the Stanford and Berkeley coreference systems, respectively, and add the appropriate citations in their first mention, as some readers may not be familiar with these specific project names.
