### Summary

This paper introduces and formalizes a new variant of the coreference resolution task, termed "Main Concept Resolution," which is specifically tailored to Wikipedia articles. The task is to identify the complete coreference chain corresponding to the primary entity described in a given article. The authors have created a new dataset of 30 annotated documents for this purpose, demonstrating that state-of-the-art coreference systems perform poorly in this setting. They propose a feature-based binary classifier that significantly outperforms baselines built on these systems. Furthermore, they show that integrating their specialized classifier into a state-of-the-art rule-based system (Stanford's Dcoref) yields substantial improvements on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia articles.

### Contributions

1.  **Introduction and formalization of the "Main Concept Resolution" task.** This is a practically significant sub-problem of coreference resolution, especially for information extraction, as it focuses on identifying all mentions of the most salient entity in a document.
2.  **Creation of a new annotated dataset for coreference in Wikipedia.** This resource not only provides a benchmark for the new task but also serves as valuable evidence that state-of-the-art coreference systems struggle to generalize from newswire to the encyclopedic genre, highlighting a critical domain adaptation challenge.

### Strengths

1.  **The proposed task is well-motivated and highly relevant.** While some papers introduce new tasks of limited scope, this work successfully frames Main Concept Resolution as an important problem for downstream applications. In many real-world scenarios (e.g., biographical articles, organizational briefings, clinical records), documents are centered around a single entity, and extracting information specific to that entity is the primary goal. By focusing on the main concept, the task isolates a set of high-value mentions that are crucial for information extraction, while filtering out less relevant ones.
2.  **The paper highlights a significant performance gap in state-of-the-art systems.** The authors convincingly demonstrate that top-performing coreference systems, largely trained on newswire, are not robust when applied to Wikipedia, an important and widely used resource in the NLP community. This finding helps revitalize a crucial line of inquiry into the domain-generality of coreference models and underscores the need for domain-specific adaptation.
3.  **The work provides an excellent testbed for future research.** The concept of a "main concept" offers a strong discourse anchor that simplifies certain aspects of coreference, allowing researchers to focus on other challenging problems, such as knowledge integration. The authors effectively illustrate how Wikipedia's structure can be leveraged for this purpose. This constrained setting could prove to be a valuable environment for developing and evaluating new ideas that might be difficult to test in a general coreference setting, potentially bridging research in coreference resolution and entity linking.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The technical novelty of the proposed method is limited.** The main strength of the paper lies in its formulation of the task and its empirical findings, rather than in its methodology. The proposed system is a standard binary classifier using a set of intuitive and sensible features. While effective, the approach itself does not introduce novel machine learning techniques or feature representations.
2.  **The model description could be clearer and introduced earlier.** The paper describes the feature extraction process in detail in Section 4.2 before explicitly stating that the model is a binary SVM classifier (which is only mentioned in Section 6.2). Stating the model type upfront in Section 4 would improve the clarity and structure of the presentation, allowing the reader to better contextualize the purpose of the features as they are being described.
3.  **The extent of feature engineering is unclear.** The features presented are effective, but they appear to be used in a relatively straightforward manner. It is not clear whether more complex feature interactions, such as conjunctions, were explored. Given the use of a linear kernel, such feature engineering could potentially yield further improvements.

### Questions for Authors

1.  **Organization:** A minor suggestion regarding the paper's structure: would it be clearer to introduce the dataset (Section 5) immediately after Related Works? This would allow concrete performance numbers to be presented alongside the baseline descriptions in the subsequent section, which could further motivate the need for the authors' proposed approach.
2.  **Citations:** When Dcoref and Scoref are first mentioned in Section 4, please consider citing the original Stanford papers. While these system names are familiar to specialists, providing explicit citations would improve clarity for a broader audience.
3.  **Clarification of "candidate list":** The term "candidate list" in Section 4.1 is slightly ambiguous. Could you clarify its precise meaning? Is it the full set of mentions extracted from the article? A clearer definition would help the reader better understand the feature extraction process.
4.  **Mention Sets:** There appears to be a potential contradiction between Section 4.1, which states that mentions are extracted using the algorithm from Raghunathan et al. (2010), and Section 6.1, which describes the final training set as "the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)." Could you clarify how the manually annotated WCR mentions differ from or augment the set of automatically extracted mentions? Are they two disjoint sets, or does the automated method fail to recover some of the gold mentions?
