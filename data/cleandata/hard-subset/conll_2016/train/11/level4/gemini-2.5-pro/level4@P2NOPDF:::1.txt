This paper introduces Main Concept Resolution (MCR), a novel coreference task focused on identifying all mentions of the central entity in a Wikipedia article. To support this task, the authors have created a new dataset by annotating 30 Wikipedia articles with full coreference chains, finding that approximately 25% of all mentions refer to the article's main concept. They propose straightforward baselines and a classifier-based approach that outperforms them. Notably, integrating their classifier into the Stanford coreference system yields significant improvements over state-of-the-art systems on this Wikipedia dataset.

This paper presents a compelling and well-motivated contribution to coreference research. The proposed task is highly relevant from an information extraction perspective, has the potential to revitalize the field, and provides an interesting bridge between the coreference and entity linking literature. While some papers introduce new tasks simply to highlight the shortcomings of existing systems, this work is different. The MCR task feels genuinely important and effectively demonstrates that standard coreference systems underperform in a significant, real-world setting.

### Strengths and Contributions (The Task)

The MCR task is particularly compelling from an Information Extraction (IE) perspective. Many real-world documents, such as biographies, clinical records, or intelligence briefings, are centered on a primary entity. In such cases, the most valuable information to extract is directly related to that entity. Standard coreference resolution often produces chains for mentions that are of little relevance to downstream IE tasks (e.g., generic mentions). In contrast, the main concept chain is, by definition, composed of high-value mentions.

Methodologically, the main concept serves as a useful discourse anchor. The paper shows that there is still significant room for improvement beyond the baselines, especially for non-pronominal mentions. Furthermore, focusing on Wikipedia allows for novel ways to incorporate external knowledge, as the authors demonstrate. This suggests that the MCR task could serve as an excellent testbed for new coreference ideas that might be difficult to evaluate or show marginal gains in more general settings.

Unlike some prior work that has focused on narrow subsets of coreference (e.g., the Winograd Schema Challenge), this paper addresses a task that significantly impacts overall coreference performance on a domain of broad interest to the community.

### Weaknesses (The Techniques)

The primary strength of this paper lies in the task formulation rather than the technical approach. The proposed classifier uses a set of sensible, standard features. However, the authors could explore whether feature conjunctions provide additional performance gains, as this was not discussed. For clarity, the authors should also explicitly state earlier in the paper that their main concept resolution system is a binary classifier. The model remains somewhat undefined during the feature description section, which could confuse the reader.

### Minor Details and Suggestions for Improvement

*   **Organization:** The paper's organization could be improved by introducing the dataset immediately after the "Related Work" section. This would allow the "Baselines" section to present concrete results, which would in turn better motivate the proposed "Approach."

*   **Citations:** When Section 4 refers to Dcoref and Scoref, the paper should cite the relevant Stanford publications or otherwise clarify that these are components of the Stanford coreference system, as many readers may be unfamiliar with these specific names.

*   **Clarity on "Candidate List":** The term "candidate list" is unclear, particularly in the sentence: "For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link." The authors should clarify that the "candidate list" is the set of all mentions in the article that are potential candidates for being part of the main concept's coreference chain. The goal of this module—importing semantic information from Wikipedia's link structure—is clear, but the terminology should be more precise.

*   **Mention Set Definition:** A point of confusion arises in Section 6.1, which states, "we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)." However, Section 4.1 seems to imply that these two sets are the same. Please clarify where the additional WCR mentions are coming from.
