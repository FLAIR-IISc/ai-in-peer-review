The authors introduce a new coreference resolution task, Main Concept Resolution (MCR), which is tailored to Wikipedia articles. The goal is to identify the specific coreference chain corresponding to the main entity of a given article. To support this task, the authors have annotated 30 documents with full coreference chains, finding that approximately 25% of all mentions refer to the article's "main concept." They present several simple baselines and a classifier that outperforms them. Furthermore, by integrating their classifier into the Stanford rule-based coreference system, they demonstrate substantial performance gains over state-of-the-art systems on the Wikipedia domain.

This paper proposes an interesting and well-motivated variation of the coreference task. From an information extraction perspective, the task is highly practical and has the potential to revitalize coreference research and bridge the gap with the entity linking literature. While many papers introduce new tasks simply to highlight the poor performance of standard systems before presenting a tailored solution, this work feels different. The task of main concept resolution is compelling in its own right, and the authors effectively demonstrate that standard systems perform poorly on a task of genuine practical importance.

**THE TASK**
Main concept resolution is an intriguing task, particularly from an IE perspective. In many real-world scenarios—such as biographical articles, intelligence briefings, or clinical records—documents center on a single primary entity, and the most salient information is that which pertains directly to it. This new task addresses a long-standing issue in standard coreference, where systems must process a large number of mentions (e.g., generics) that are often irrelevant to downstream IE applications. In contrast, this task focuses exclusively on mentions that are, by definition, highly salient.

Methodologically, the "main concept" serves as a useful discourse anchor for the coreference task. The results show, however, that significant challenges remain in moving beyond the baselines, especially for non-pronominal mentions. Furthermore, grounding the task in Wikipedia opens promising avenues for incorporating external knowledge, as the authors demonstrate. This makes the domain an excellent testbed for novel coreference ideas that might be difficult to evaluate or show robust improvements for in a more general setting, where their effects could be overshadowed by other confounding factors.

Finally, unlike previous work that has focused on narrow subsets of the coreference problem (e.g., the Winograd Schema Challenge), this paper's contributions have a significant impact on overall coreference metrics within a domain of broad interest to the community.

**THE TECHNIQUES**
The technical contributions are not the primary strength of this paper, although the proposed methods are effective. The feature set is sensible, but it is unclear if the authors experimented with feature conjunctions, which could potentially offer improvements. A key point of clarification is needed earlier in the paper: the model for main concept resolution should be explicitly introduced as a binary classifier before the feature descriptions, as its identity is left ambiguous for too long.

**MINOR DETAILS**
*   **Organization:** I suggest moving the dataset description to appear immediately after the "Related Work" section (i.e., making it the new Section 3). This would allow the "Baselines" section to present concrete results on the data, which would in turn better motivate the proposed "Approach."

*   **Citations:** The first mention of Dcoref and Scoref (Section 4) should include citations to the relevant papers and clarify that these are components of the Stanford Coreference system, as these specific names may not be widely known.

*   **Terminology:** The term "candidate list" is used unclearly. For instance, in the sentence "we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link," it is not immediately obvious what this list contains. Please explicitly define the "candidate list" as the set of all mentions in the article being considered for coreference with the main concept. The goal of this module—to import semantic information from Wikipedia's link structure—is intuitive, but clearer terminology would strengthen the explanation.

*   **Clarity on Mention Sets:** There appears to be a contradiction between Section 4.1 and Section 6.1 regarding the set of mentions used. Section 6.1 states that the system uses "the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)," but Section 4.1 seems to imply these two sets are identical. Please clarify if the WCR annotations include mentions beyond those extracted by the Raghunathan et al. method.
