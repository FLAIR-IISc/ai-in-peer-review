This paper introduces a novel and well-motivated version of the coreference resolution task, which it terms Main Concept Resolution (MCR), tailored specifically to Wikipedia articles. The goal is to identify the coreference chain corresponding to the primary entity that a given article is about. To support this task, the authors have created a new dataset by annotating 30 documents with full coreference chains, finding that approximately 25% of all mentions refer to the article's main concept. The paper presents several simple baselines and a classifier-based approach that outperforms them. Crucially, when this classifier is integrated into the Stanford rule-based coreference system, it yields substantial improvements over state-of-the-art systems on the Wikipedia domain.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its compelling framing of a problem that is highly relevant from an information extraction (IE) perspective. The proposed task has the potential to stimulate new research directions in coreference and build a valuable bridge to the entity linking literature. While some papers introduce new tasks primarily to highlight the weaknesses of existing systems, this work feels different. The authors have identified a genuinely important setting—understanding the central entity of a document—where standard coreference systems underperform, thereby motivating a meaningful and practical research problem.

**Strengths and Contributions:**

1.  **Task Motivation:** Main Concept Resolution is an intriguing task. In many real-world scenarios, such as processing biographical documents, dossiers, or clinical records, documents revolve around a central entity. The most valuable information to extract is often directly related to that entity. Standard coreference resolution can be noisy for these IE applications, as it expends effort on resolving large numbers of generic or peripheral mentions. By focusing exclusively on the mentions that "matter," this task aligns coreference resolution more closely with downstream applications.

2.  **A New Research Direction:** From a methodological standpoint, defining a "main concept" provides a strong discourse anchor that can simplify and guide the resolution process. The experiments show that there is still significant work needed to improve beyond simple baselines, particularly for non-pronominal mentions, leaving ample room for future research. Furthermore, using Wikipedia as a domain opens the door to innovative uses of structured knowledge (e.g., hyperlinks, infoboxes), as demonstrated by the authors. This domain could serve as an excellent testbed for ideas that might improve coreference resolution more broadly but are difficult to validate in general settings, where their effects might be overshadowed by other complexities.

3.  **Impact on the Field:** Unlike previous work that has focused on narrow slices of the coreference problem (e.g., the Winograd Schema Challenge), this paper addresses a task that has a significant impact on overall coreference metrics within a domain of widespread interest to the NLP community.

**Areas for Improvement:**

While the proposed task is highly compelling, the technical contribution is more incremental. The features used in the classifier are sensible and effective but largely standard. The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough exploration of the feature space; for instance, it is unclear if the authors experimented with feature conjunctions, which could potentially improve performance.

A more significant point of confusion is the delayed introduction of the model itself. The paper describes the featurization in detail before explicitly stating, much later, that the core MCR system is a binary classifier. This model should be defined clearly and early in the "Approach" section to provide context for the feature descriptions that follow.

**Minor Details and Suggestions:**

*   **Organization:** The paper's organization could be improved by introducing the dataset immediately after the "Related Work" section. This would allow the "Baselines" section to present concrete results on this dataset, which would in turn better motivate the proposed approach.

*   **Clarity on Prior Systems:** In Section 4, the systems "Dcoref" and "Scoref" should be clearly identified as components of the Stanford Coreference system. The relevant papers (e.g., Lee et al., 2013; Raghunathan et al., 2010) should be cited for readers who may be unfamiliar with these internal names.

*   **Ambiguous Terminology:** The term "candidate list" is used ambiguously. For example, the sentence "we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link" is unclear. The authors should explicitly define this term, presumably as the set of all mentions within the document that are candidates for being part of the main concept's coreference chain. The description of how hyperlink information is used could also be stated more directly—for instance, by explaining that the goal is to import semantic attributes (e.g., gender from a linked entity's Freebase entry) to the candidate mention.

*   **Potential Inconsistency:** There appears to be a potential inconsistency between Section 4.1 and Section 6.1 regarding mention extraction. Section 4.1 implies that mentions are generated by the method of Raghunathan et al. (2010), while Section 6.1 describes the evaluation set as the "union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted" by that method. Please clarify if these sets are different and, if so, where the additional WCR mentions originate.

Overall, this is a strong and interesting paper that introduces a valuable task and provides a solid foundation for future work. The critiques are minor and can be addressed in a final version.
