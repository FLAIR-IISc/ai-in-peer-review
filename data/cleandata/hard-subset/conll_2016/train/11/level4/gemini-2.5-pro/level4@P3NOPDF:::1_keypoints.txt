- Introduces Main Concept Resolution (MCR), a novel, well-motivated coreference task focused on identifying the primary entity in Wikipedia articles.  
- Authors created a new dataset of 30 fully-annotated documents and found ~25% of mentions refer to the article's main concept.  
- Presents simple baselines and a classifier that outperforms them and, when integrated into Stanford’s rule-based system, yields substantial improvements on the Wikipedia domain.  
- Task is highly relevant to information extraction and bridges coreference resolution with entity linking and downstream IE applications.  
- Defining a “main concept” provides a useful discourse anchor that can simplify resolution and spur new research directions using Wikipedia’s structured knowledge.  
- The task has broader impact compared to prior narrow coreference challenges, affecting coreference metrics in a widely interesting domain.  
- Technical contribution is incremental: classifier features are sensible but largely standard.  
- Paper should explore feature space more thoroughly (e.g., feature conjunctions) to potentially improve performance, especially on non-pronominal mentions.  
- The model (binary classifier) is introduced too late; it should be defined early in the Approach section to contextualize feature descriptions.  
- Organization could be improved by presenting the dataset right after Related Work so baselines can be evaluated concretely on it.  
- Clarify that “Dcoref” and “Scoref” are components of the Stanford Coreference system and cite the relevant papers (e.g., Lee et al., 2013; Raghunathan et al., 2010).  
- The term “candidate list” is ambiguous and should be explicitly defined; hyperlink usage (importing semantic attributes) should be described more directly.  
- Potential inconsistency between Sections 4.1 and 6.1 about mention extraction needs clarification regarding the source and union of mentions.
