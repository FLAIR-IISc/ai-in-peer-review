This paper introduces a novel coreference resolution task tailored to Wikipedia: identifying the coreference chain for the main subject entity of an article. The authors have created a new dataset by annotating 30 Wikipedia articles with full coreference chains, finding that mentions of the "main concept" (MC) constitute roughly 25% of all mentions. They present several simple baselines and a classifier-based approach that outperforms them. Furthermore, by integrating their MC resolution classifier into the Stanford rule-based coreference system, they report substantial improvements over state-of-the-art systems on this Wikipedia dataset.

This is an interesting and well-motivated twist on the standard coreference task. From an information extraction perspective, it makes a great deal of sense. The work has the potential to revitalize coreference research and build a valuable bridge between the coreference and entity linking communities.

While papers that define a new task simply to show their own system's superiority can be unconvincing, this is not the case here. Rather than simply seeking out a new domain, the authors convincingly argue that standard systems perform poorly in a setting that is both common and practically important.

**The Proposed Task**

The task of "main concept resolution" is highly compelling from an Information Extraction (IE) perspective. One can imagine many scenarios where documents revolve around a primary entity (e.g., biographies, dossiers, clinical records) and the key information to be extracted is specific to that entity. The standard coreference task often includes many mentions (e.g., generics) that are irrelevant for most IE applications. In contrast, this task focuses exclusively on mentions that are, by definition, highly relevant.

From a methodological standpoint, the "main concept" provides a natural discourse anchor that is useful for resolution. At the same time, the results show that there is still substantial overhead required to improve beyond the baselines, particularly for non-pronominal mentions. Performing coreference on Wikipedia also enables more sophisticated use of external knowledge, as the authors demonstrate. Consequently, this domain is likely to be an interesting testbed for new ideas that could improve coreference resolution overall. This focused setting may be an ideal environment for testing novel techniques that, in a general coreference setting, might yield only marginal gains or be overshadowed by other aspects of the problem.

Moreover, unlike past work that has focused on a narrow subproblem of coreference (e.g., the Winograd schema challenge), this paper demonstrates a significant impact on overall coreference metrics within a domain of significant interest to the ACL community.

**Technical Approach**

The technical methods presented are effective, though they are not the paper's primary contribution. The features used in the classifier are sensible. However, it seems likely that additional feature conjunctions could provide further improvements, and it is unclear if this was explored.

A point of clarification is needed regarding the model itself. The authors should state explicitly early in the paper that their primary MC resolution system is a binary classifier. This is not mentioned until later, leaving the model undefined throughout the description of the feature set.

**Minor Points and Suggestions for Clarification**

*   **Organization:** The paper's organization could be improved by introducing the dataset immediately after the "Related Work" section (as a new Section 3). This would allow the "Baselines" section to present concrete results on the dataset, which would better motivate the authors' proposed "Approach".

*   **Citations:** When referring to Dcoref and Scoref in Section 4, please cite the relevant papers for the Stanford coreference system, as many readers may be unfamiliar with these specific internal names.

*   **Clarity on "Candidate List":** The use of the term "candidate list" was unclear, particularly in this passage: "We leverage the hyperlink structure of the article in order to enrich the list of mentions with shallow semantic attributes. For each link found within the article under consideration, we look through the candidate list for all mentions that match the surface string of the link." Please clarify that the "candidate list" refers to the set of all mentions extracted from the article that are candidates for being coreferent with the main concept. The goal of this module appears to be importing semantic information from Wikipedia's link structure (e.g., if a mention is hyperlinked to an article about a person identified as female in Freebase, that mention is likely female). Using clearer terminology would help readers understand this process.

*   **Mention Extraction:** There appears to be a potential contradiction regarding mention extraction. Section 6.1 states, "we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)." However, Section 4.1 seems to imply that the mention detection method *is* the one from Raghunathan et al. (2010). Please clarify where the additional WCR mentions come from.
