## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

This paper introduces a focused coreference resolution task – identifying mentions of the main concept within a Wikipedia article – and demonstrates its practical utility. The authors address a gap in coreference research by revisiting Wikipedia as a domain, providing a new annotated dataset (WCR) of 30 articles, and showing significant gains by integrating a binary classifier into the Stanford Dcoref system. The core contribution lies in framing this “main concept” resolution as a valuable task, particularly for information extraction applications where focusing on relevant mentions is crucial. The dataset itself is a significant contribution, offering a resource for future research in this area. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that even a relatively simple classifier, leveraging Wikipedia’s markup and external knowledge, can substantially improve coreference performance on this specific task.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novel and Motivated Task:** The paper tackles a genuinely important problem – filtering coreference to focus on the central entity of a document. This is particularly relevant for applications like biography creation or dossier building.
*   **Valuable Dataset:** The WCR dataset is a strong asset. The annotation of full coreference chains, with a clear focus on the main concept, provides a solid foundation for evaluating and developing coreference systems tailored to Wikipedia. The 25% prevalence of main concept mentions is a useful characteristic.
*   **Significant Performance Gains:** The integration of the classifier into Dcoref yields substantial improvements over the state-of-the-art, demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach. The reported gains are not merely incremental.
*   **Clear Presentation of Experiments:** The experimental setup is well-defined, and the ablation studies (Table 2) provide insight into the contribution of different feature sets.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty in Method:** While effective, the core classification technique itself isn’t groundbreaking. The features used are sensible but largely standard in the field. The paper would benefit from exploring more sophisticated feature engineering or model architectures.
*   **Delayed Model Definition:** The paper initially presents the work as a general approach to coreference, but it’s not until Section 4 that it’s revealed the primary system is a binary classifier. This should be stated upfront for clarity.
*   **Dataset Description Placement:** The description of the WCR dataset is buried in Section 5. Moving this section earlier would improve the paper’s flow and allow readers to better understand the evaluation context.
*   **Ambiguity in Data Combination:** Section 6.1’s explanation of combining WCR mentions with those from Raghunathan et al. is unclear. It's not immediately obvious where the additional WCR mentions originate.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential for extending this work to other domains beyond Wikipedia? Are there specific characteristics of Wikipedia that make this task particularly well-suited to this domain?
2.  The paper mentions noisy redirects in Wikipedia impacting performance. Have you considered techniques to mitigate the effects of these redirects, such as filtering or weighting them differently?
3.  What is the computational cost of incorporating the Wikipedia markup and Freebase information? Is this a significant bottleneck for scaling the approach to larger datasets?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Explicitly cite Stanford CoreNLP, Dcoref, and Scoref in the introduction or related work section.
*   Clarify the meaning of "candidate list" in Section 4.1. How are hyperlinks used to add semantic attributes – is this a direct lookup, or is there a more complex process involved?
