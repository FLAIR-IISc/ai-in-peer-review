

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary
The paper introduces a new coreference resolution task specifically for Wikipedia: identifying all mentions of the main concept (the entity the article is about) rather than full coreference chains. The authors create a new dataset (WCR) of 30 Wikipedia articles annotated with coreference chains, where approximately 25% of mentions refer to the main concept. They present a binary classifier that identifies whether a mention refers to the main concept, using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. The classifier outperforms baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference systems, and when integrated into the rule-based Dcoref system, it improves coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel coreference resolution task for Wikipedia: identifying mentions of the main concept (the entity the article describes), which has significant practical applications in information extraction (e.g., focusing on relevant mentions rather than all coreferent mentions).
2. A new dataset (WCR) of 30 Wikipedia articles with coreference annotations, providing a dedicated resource for evaluating systems on this task.
3. A simple yet effective binary classifier that significantly outperforms baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference systems, with the added benefit of being integrable into full coreference pipelines.

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies a genuinely important and underexplored task for Wikipedia coreference resolution with clear practical applications in information extraction. The focus on the main concept (as opposed to full coreference resolution) provides a meaningful discourse anchor that filters out generic mentions, which is particularly valuable for applications like biographies, dossiers, and clinical records.

2. The authors provide a new dataset (WCR) that fills a significant gap in coreference resources. While Wikipedia is widely used in NLP, there has been little work adapting coreference resolution to this text genre. The WCR dataset, with its 30 articles (~60k tokens) annotated with coreference chains, provides a much-needed testbed for evaluating systems on Wikipedia.

3. The approach demonstrates substantial empirical gains (13-15 F1 points over baselines) and shows practical utility by integrating the classifier into the Dcoref system, improving coreference resolution performance by 4 F1 points on the Wikipedia domain. This demonstrates that the task is not merely academic but has tangible benefits for real-world applications.

## Weaknesses
1. The novelty of the approach is limited. The core idea of using Wikipedia markup and Freebase features for coreference resolution is not new (cited in Section 2), and the binary classifier is relatively simple. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of how this work advances beyond previous studies that used similar approaches.

2. The paper does not clearly distinguish between the main concept resolution task and full coreference resolution, which could lead to confusion about the relationship between the two tasks. The paper states the main concept resolution task is "revisiting" a task from prior work, but doesn't sufficiently explain how it differs from standard coreference resolution.

3. The dataset is quite small (30 articles, ~60k tokens), which limits the statistical significance of the results and the generalizability of the findings. While the authors note they sampled articles of various characteristics, the small size makes it difficult to draw strong conclusions about performance across the diverse Wikipedia domain.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do the authors justify that the main concept resolution task is fundamentally different from standard coreference resolution? Is it simply a subset of the full coreference problem, or does it require different modeling approaches?

2. The paper mentions "the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)" in Section 6.1. Could the authors clarify exactly how the Raghunathan mentions were generated and why they were added to the WCR dataset?

3. The paper states that "the main concept" is defined as the entity the article is about, but the annotation guidelines aren't clearly explained. How did the authors determine what constitutes the main concept for each article, and how was this determined consistently across the 30 articles?

4. The paper notes that the rule-based system (Dcoref) outperforms statistical systems on the WCR dataset. Given this finding, how does the authors' classifier interact with the rule-based system versus statistical systems? Would the gains be similar for statistical systems?
