

This paper introduces a novel coreference resolution task for Wikipedia articles: identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) being described in the article. The authors create a new dataset (WCR) of 30 Wikipedia articles annotated with coreference chains, where approximately 25% of mentions refer to the MC. They propose a binary classifier that outperforms baselines built on state-of-the-art coreference systems, and demonstrate its integration into a rule-based coreference system (Dcoref++) leads to significant improvements on Wikipedia texts.

The primary contribution is the identification of a meaningful, practically motivated coreference task for Wikipedia that addresses a real need in information extraction applications. The authors show that focusing on the MC (rather than all coreference chains) filters out irrelevant mentions that would otherwise clutter extraction results, making it particularly valuable for applications like biographies, dossiers, and clinical records where the main entity is the focus. The secondary contribution is the creation of the WCR dataset, which provides a valuable benchmark for evaluating coreference resolution systems on Wikipedia texts. The third contribution is the demonstration that a simple SVM classifier using Wikipedia-specific features (from markup and Freebase) outperforms baselines by 13-15 F1 points on the MC identification task.

Strengths: 
1) The task is genuinely important for practical NLP applications. The paper effectively argues that focusing on the MC rather than all coreference chains is valuable for information extraction, as generic mentions (e.g., "the foundation stone" in a biography) often produce uninformative relations. This practical motivation elevates the work beyond a mere technical exercise.

2) The dataset (WCR) is well-constructed and properly annotated according to OntoNotes guidelines. The authors carefully selected articles across different topics and characteristics to ensure representativeness, and the dataset size (30 articles, ~7,000 mentions) is sufficient for their experiments.

3) The integration of the classifier into Dcoref (creating Dcoref++) demonstrates real-world impact, with a 4-point absolute improvement in F1 score over state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia. This shows that the approach isn't just a theoretical improvement but has tangible benefits for end-to-end coreference resolution pipelines.

Weaknesses:
1) The novelty claim is overstated. While the authors frame the task as "revisiting" work from Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008), their contribution is largely incremental. Previous work already addressed similar tasks (e.g., identifying the main entity in Wikipedia articles for information extraction), and the classifier they propose doesn't introduce significant methodological novelty compared to existing work that uses Wikipedia and Freebase features (e.g., Ratinov and Roth, 2012).

2) The paper lacks sufficient technical detail about the classifier. The "main contribution" is a binary classifier, but the paper doesn't clearly present the model architecture early enough (it's buried in Section 4.2). The feature engineering is sensible but not particularly innovative, and the paper doesn't explore whether more sophisticated models (e.g., neural networks) might further improve performance.

3) There's significant ambiguity in the description of the dataset and feature extraction. Section 6.1 states the authors added "about 13,400 automatically extracted mentions (singletons) to the 7,000 coreferent mentions," but it's unclear where these extra mentions come from or how they were selected. The "candidate list" mentioned in Section 3 is also not clearly defined, making it difficult to assess the quality of the dataset.

Questions to Authors:
1) How does your "main concept" task differ from the "entity-centric coreference" task proposed by Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008)? What new insights does your work provide that weren't addressed in those earlier studies?

2) The paper claims your classifier outperforms state-of-the-art systems, but you only compare to baselines built on top of Dcoref and Scoref. How would your approach perform against a system specifically trained on Wikipedia data (e.g., fine-tuning an existing model on your WCR dataset)?

3) The paper mentions that "the main concept" provides a useful discourse anchor, but doesn't explore how this relates to broader coreference resolution challenges. How might this approach be extended to identify multiple key concepts in a document rather than just the primary one?
