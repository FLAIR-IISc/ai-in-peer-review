

This paper introduces a novel coreference task on Wikipedia: identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) described in an article, rather than resolving all coreference chains. The authors annotate 30 Wikipedia articles with full coreference chains, finding that approximately 25% of mentions refer to the MC. They propose a binary classifier that outperforms baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference systems (Dcoref and Scoref) by 13-15 F1 points, and demonstrate that integrating this classifier into Dcoref significantly improves coreference resolution on Wikipedia (4 F1 point gain).

The primary contribution is the introduction of a meaningful, practically motivated coreference task that addresses a genuine gap in NLP applications (e.g., biographies, clinical records), where identifying MC mentions is more valuable than resolving all coreference chains. The secondary contribution is the WCR dataset, which provides a valuable testbed for evaluating systems on Wikipedia text. The tertiary contribution is the effective integration of the classifier into a full coreference pipeline, showing tangible improvements.

Strengths include: (1) The task addresses a real-world need by filtering out generic mentions and focusing on what matters for information extraction, which is particularly relevant for Wikipedia's structure. (2) The dataset is well-constructed and carefully annotated, providing a solid foundation for future research. (3) The approach demonstrates significant gains over baselines, with clear evidence of practical utility when integrated into existing systems.

Weaknesses include: (1) The techniques themselves are not novel, with features being sensible but not innovative—this is a strong application of existing methods rather than a methodological advancement. (2) The binary classifier approach isn't clearly introduced early enough, making it difficult to grasp the core methodology initially. (3) Several organizational issues hinder readability: the dataset description should precede the baselines, key systems (Dcoref/Scoref) need explicit citations, and ambiguous terms like "candidate list" require clarification.

The work successfully establishes a promising new direction for coreference research on meaningful domains, though the novelty is more in the task definition than the technical approach. The authors should clarify the classifier's nature early and address the organizational issues before publication.
