

# Review: Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution

## Summary
This paper introduces "main concept resolution" - identifying all mentions of the primary entity being described in a Wikipedia article. The authors annotate 30 Wikipedia articles with full coreference chains, finding that approximately 25% of mentions refer to the main concept. They present a simple binary classifier that outperforms baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems. The classifier integrates Wikipedia markup and Freebase information to distinguish main concept mentions from other mentions, and when incorporated into Stanford's rule-based coreference system, achieves substantial improvements on Wikipedia data.

## Main Contributions
1. A practically motivated coreference task (main concept resolution) with clear applications in information extraction (biographies, clinical records), focusing on mentions that matter rather than all mentions.
2. A well-constructed dataset (WCR) of 30 Wikipedia articles with full coreference chains annotated for main concept mentions, demonstrating the gap between standard coreference systems and Wikipedia texts.
3. A simple yet effective classifier that outperforms baselines and significantly improves state-of-the-art coreference resolution when integrated into a full pipeline.

## Strengths
1. **Genuinely important task with practical motivation**: The paper correctly identifies that standard coreference resolution is inadequate for Wikipedia because it treats all mentions equally, while in practice, information extraction systems need to focus on the main entity being described. The task is well-motivated by real-world applications (biographies, dossiers, clinical records), making it a meaningful contribution rather than a purely academic benchmark.

2. **Clear demonstration of the Wikipedia coreference gap**: The authors provide strong evidence that standard coreference systems perform poorly on Wikipedia (Table 1), with performance dropping dramatically from OntoNotes to WCR. This is a valuable contribution that highlights a real problem in the field, showing that the standard "news-focused" coreference systems don't generalize to this important text genre.

3. **Substantial practical improvement**: The integration of their classifier into Stanford's coreference system (Dcoref++) achieves a 4-point absolute gain in CoNLL F1 score (Table 5), demonstrating real-world impact. This isn't just a new task, but a solution that improves performance on a major NLP resource, making it valuable for practitioners.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty in techniques**: While the task is important, the classifier itself is not novel - it's a simple binary SVM with features borrowed from prior work. The paper would have been stronger with a more thorough exploration of feature combinations, which the authors acknowledge could be improved ("richer feature conjunctions might help").

2. **Poor organization and clarity**: The paper's structure is confusing. The binary classifier model definition should appear much earlier (in Section 4), not buried in the middle of the paper. The dataset description (Section 5) should be moved earlier to provide context for the task. The ambiguity between Sections 6.1 and 4.1 regarding the union of WCR mentions and mentions from Raghunathan et al. needs clarification.

3. **Insufficient citations**: The paper fails to explicitly cite Stanford's Dcoref system or Scoref in the introduction, which weakens the context for their work. The paper should also clarify how their "candidate list" works and how hyperlinks add semantic attributes to mentions.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims the main concept task is "genuinely important" but doesn't sufficiently explain why standard coreference resolution is inadequate for this specific use case. Could you clarify the specific failures of standard coreference systems that make this task necessary?

2. The ambiguity between Sections 4.1 and 6.1 regarding the union of WCR mentions and mentions from Raghunathan et al. needs clarification. Where do the "extra WCR mentions" come from in Section 6.1?

3. The paper mentions that "the primary system is a binary classifier but this is not stated early enough." Could you revise the paper to state the binary classification approach earlier (e.g., in the introduction) to improve clarity?

4. The paper states "techniques are effective but not novel." Would you consider exploring more sophisticated feature combinations (e.g., richer feature conjunctions) to further improve performance?
