Summary
This paper defines and studies the “main concept” (MC) coreference task on Wikipedia: given an article, identify the coreference chain for the entity the article is about. The authors annotate 30 Wikipedia pages (WCR) with full coreference chains and show that ~25% of mentions refer to the MC. They formulate MC detection as binary classification over detected mentions (is this mention coreferent with the MC?), engineer features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, and train separate linear SVMs for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. Their classifier substantially outperforms several baselines derived from state-of-the-art CR systems (Dcoref, Scoref) and, when inserted as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref, yields strong gains on Wikipedia articles.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) Task definition and evaluation resource: Reinstates and formalizes “main concept resolution” for Wikipedia, and provides a small but carefully annotated dataset (30 docs, 60k tokens, ~7k non-singleton mentions; ~25% MC mentions) with a principled evaluation protocol. This is the most significant contribution.
2) Effective knowledge-informed baseline for MC resolution: A simple, well-motivated binary classifier leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase attributes that clearly beats strong, adapted baselines (absolute F1 gains of 13–15 points for MC detection).
3) Demonstrated utility in full CR: Integrating MC classification into the Stanford rule-based system improves end-to-end CR on Wikipedia (≈+4 CoNLL F1), showing the MC as a useful discourse anchor and a practical way to improve CR in this genre.

Strengths
- Clear, practically important task with broad IE relevance: Focusing on the entity that matters (biographies, dossiers, clinical narratives) filters generic mentions and aligns with real IE needs. This is a meaningful reframing rather than an artificially hard setting.
- Strong empirical gains and careful baselines: The authors build fair, informative baselines from Dcoref/Scoref (including chain aggregation and pronoun heuristics), and show consistent improvements on pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. The ablations are illuminating (e.g., title, inferred type, and name-variant features drive large gains).
- Sensible exploitation of Wikipedia structure and Freebase: Using human-created internal links, redirects, and notable types to assign semantic attributes avoids brittle, low-recall entity linking pipelines and is a practical way to inject knowledge.
- Evidence of broader CR impact: Integrating the MC classifier as an early sieve improves overall CR on Wikipedia, suggesting the MC serves as a robust discourse anchor and that knowledge-augmented sieves can guide downstream clustering.
- Potential to bridge CR and entity linking: The work naturally connects mention classification with KB-derived attributes, offering a testbed to study hybrid CR/EL approaches where the MC is anchored to a KB entity.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of methods: The core technique is a binary classifier with straightforward features; while effective, it is not methodologically novel. Richer feature conjunctions or structured models might further improve performance; the paper could better acknowledge this tradeoff earlier.
- Small dataset and generalization concerns: WCR includes only 30 articles; despite diversity claims, robustness across topics, languages, or denser/hyperlink-sparse pages is unclear. Results may be sensitive to Wikipedia-specific markup quality and Freebase coverage.
- Clarity/organization issues: The paper delays stating that the primary model is a binary classifier; the model definition should appear in the introduction or early in Section 3/4. The dataset description could move earlier to ground the task and baselines. The terms “candidate list” and the mechanism by which hyperlinks propagate semantic attributes to mentions need clearer definitions and examples.
- Ambiguity about mention sets (Section 6.1 vs 4.1): It is unclear how the “union of WCR mentions and mentions from Raghunathan et al.” is constructed and where the “extra WCR mentions” originate. This affects training distributions and label balance, and thus evaluation comparability.
- Novelty pattern caveat: The paper shows standard CR systems perform poorly on WCR, then introduces a tailored task/system that excels. The authors rightly argue the task is genuinely important, but the framing should emphasize transfer and broader impact beyond this niche, or include cross-genre control experiments.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Mention inventory: Please clarify the creation of the final mention set. In Section 6.1 you “consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by Raghunathan et al. (2010).” Are these predicted mentions all singletons? How are labels assigned to predicted mentions not present in WCR (especially negatives vs MC positives)? Do you allow overlap/duplicates, and how are conflicts resolved?
- “Candidate list” definition: What exactly constitutes the candidate list of mentions matched to hyperlinks? Do you match by exact span, head, or any substring? How do you handle multiple surface strings mapping to the same link target, nested mentions, and plural/possessive inflections?
- Hyperlink-to-attribute propagation: When propagating Freebase attributes from a link to mentions with the same surface string elsewhere, do you disambiguate homonyms or restrict to same section/sentence? How do you prevent noise from redirects (you note Johnston and Sand Islands) and pages with ambiguous titles?
- Baseline details: For the pronoun “add-all” heuristic, is it applied identically across Dcoref and Scoref baselines? Are thresholds or filters (e.g., sentence/section scopes) used? Please report its isolated effect on precision/recall for pronouns.
- Leakage control: In 10-fold CV, are knowledge-derived features (e.g., MC name variants from redirects) inherently global? If so, is there any chance of fold leakage (e.g., feature engineering tuned on all data)? Please detail the CV protocol to ensure strict separation.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Move the dataset description to earlier in the paper and cite Stanford/Dcoref/Scoref explicitly and consistently when first introduced.
- State upfront that the core model is a binary mention-level classifier (with separate pronominal/non-pronominal models), and briefly list the feature families in the intro.
- Clarify how hyperlinks “add semantic attributes” with a running example, and define the “candidate list” precisely.
- Consider adding simple feature conjunctions and local context features for pronouns (e.g., appositions, governing verbs) to address remaining pronoun errors.
- Report results by page type (PER/ORG/LOC) and hyperlink density to assess generalization; discuss replacing Freebase with a maintained KB.
