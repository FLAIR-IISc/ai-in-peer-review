Summary
The paper revisits “main concept” (MC) resolution in Wikipedia: given an article, identify the coreference chain for the entity the article is about. The authors annotate 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR) with full coreference chains (~25% of mentions refer to the MC), train a binary classifier to decide whether a mention refers to the MC, propose fair baselines built atop Dcoref/Scoref, and show that (i) their classifier substantially outperforms baselines on MC detection and (ii) integrating it as a sieve in Stanford’s rule-based system yields notable gains on Wikipedia coreference.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Problem and dataset: Clear operationalization of MC resolution in Wikipedia with a publicly released testbed and protocol.
2) Empirical finding: A simple binary classifier leveraging Wikipedia markup/Freebase decisively improves MC detection and, when integrated into Dcoref, raises end-to-end CR performance on Wikipedia.
3) Practical framing: Articulation of MC as a useful discourse anchor for IE, with potential to bridge coreference and entity linking.

Strengths
- Importance and focus: The task targets the mentions that matter for IE (biographies, dossiers, clinical records) and offers a principled discourse anchor. This is a meaningful domain where generic CR struggles.
- Strong empirical impact: The classifier achieves ~89 F1 on MC detection, outperforming baselines by 13–15 F1 points; Dcoref++ improves CoNLL F1 by ~4 points on WCR, indicating downstream utility.
- Sensible, high-quality signals: Clever use of human-curated links, redirects, and Freebase attributes avoids noisy EL pipelines and enables effective features, with informative ablations (e.g., title, inferred type, variants).
- Resource contribution: The WCR annotations (30 articles, full chains) provide a needed evaluation bed for Wikipedia CR and may catalyze research bridging CR and EL.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core model is a binary classifier with largely standard features; novelty rests more in task/dataset and integration than in technique. The fact that the primary system is a binary classifier is not stated early enough; define the model earlier.
- Dataset scale and generalization: Only 30 documents; 10-fold CV on a small set risks overfitting and topic leakage. No cross-genre or larger-scale validation.
- Baselines/comparability: Only two CR systems are deeply analyzed; clarify tuning and whether pronoun expansion heuristics create asymmetric advantages. Consider stronger or more recent systems if feasible.
- Clarity/organization issues: Move dataset description earlier; explicitly cite Stanford CoreNLP/Dcoref/Scoref; define “candidate list”; detail how hyperlinks propagate semantic attributes; clarify Section 6.1 vs 4.1 about the union of WCR mentions with Raghunathan et al.’s predictions (where do extra WCR mentions come from?).

Questions to Authors
- Precisely define the “candidate list” and the rule by which hyperlink targets assign attributes to unmatched surface mentions. Any filtering to prevent noisy redirects?
- In Section 6.1, what is the exact composition of the final mention set? How many come from WCR vs Raghunathan et al.’s detector per fold?
- Do MC title/aliases used as features risk trivial matches or leakage? How do you prevent train/test contamination across CV folds when building MC variants from markup?
