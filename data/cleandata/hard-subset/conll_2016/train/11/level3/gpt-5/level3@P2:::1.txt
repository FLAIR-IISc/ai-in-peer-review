Summary
This paper defines and studies “Main Concept Resolution” (MCR) for Wikipedia articles: identifying all mentions that refer to the article’s primary entity. The authors build baselines by extracting the main-concept chain from outputs of state-of-the-art coreference systems (Dcoref, Scoref) with several aggregation heuristics, and propose a binary classifier (separate models for pronominal vs. non-pronominal mentions) that leverages Wikipedia markup and Freebase-derived attributes. On a new annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR), the classifier substantially outperforms the baselines in identifying main-concept mentions. When added as a high-precision sieve to Dcoref, it improves end-to-end coreference on WCR by about 4 CoNLL F1 points. The paper argues that MCR is practically useful (e.g., for IE) and that Wikipedia is a challenging domain for standard systems trained on news-like data.

Contributions (as I see them; ordered by significance)
1) A focused task and testbed for Wikipedia coreference: the MCR formulation, a new WCR dataset (30 docs, 60k tokens), and an empirical demonstration that SOTA newswire-trained CR systems degrade markedly on Wikipedia.
2) An effective, knowledge-informed binary classifier for MCR using features from Wikipedia links/redirects and Freebase attributes, outperforming strong baselines by 13–15 F1 points on 10-fold CV.
3) Integration of the classifier as a sieve in Dcoref, yielding a substantial (+4 CoNLL F1) end-to-end improvement on Wikipedia text.

Strengths
- Clear practical motivation and impact. MCR focuses on the entity that “matters” in a Wikipedia article; this is immediately useful for IE pipelines where filtering generic or irrelevant mentions is valuable. The task also offers a clean discourse anchor for modeling.
- Strong empirical gains with simple techniques. The classifier attains ~89 F1 on MCR, an absolute gain of 13 points over the best Dcoref-based baseline and 15 over Scoref-based baselines. As a sieve in Dcoref, it adds ~4 CoNLL F1 on WCR—substantial given the maturity of baselines.
- Sensible exploitation of Wikipedia markup. Using human-curated internal links, redirects, and Freebase attributes avoids the brittle entity-linking step and yields high-quality features (title, inferred type, aliases, coarse attributes). This is a pragmatic and robust design choice for Wikipedia.
- Careful baseline construction and analysis. The authors adapt multiple heuristics (longest chain, title-matching chain, aggregation by title/substring, pronoun expansion) on two distinct CR systems and show where these fail, especially on pronouns.
- Useful domain-shift evidence. The comparison of several SOTA CR systems on WCR vs. OntoNotes quantifies the genre mismatch and motivates specialized approaches for Wikipedia.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. The approach is a straightforward binary classifier with mostly standard, surface/knowledge features. The main technical ideas (feature families, SVM) are not novel; the contribution is primarily problem definition and empirical validation.
- Small dataset and unclear accessibility. WCR comprises only 30 documents; cross-validation therefore splits over ~3 docs per fold, raising variance/overfitting concerns. The paper references a “nonanonymized version” for details and promises data release at a placeholder URL; reproducibility hinges on actual release. Please clarify availability and provide variance or per-fold statistics.
- Clarity and organization issues. The fact that the core model is a binary classifier emerges late; readers would benefit from an early, crisp problem and model definition. The dataset description could be moved earlier to ground the task. Some terms and processes need clarification (e.g., “candidate list” of mentions and how hyperlink-derived attributes are attached; details of “inferred type” extraction).
- Ambiguity around mention sets (Section 6.1 vs. 4.1). It is unclear how the “union of WCR mentions and Raghunathan et al. mentions” is used at train/test time, and whether any extra “WCR mentions” are added beyond the original annotations. Please clarify the exact mention detection protocol and whether gold spans are used anywhere.
- Potential leakage and weaker alternatives. Assigning attributes to any mention matching the surface form of a link could bias the classifier toward the main concept title/aliases and inflate performance, especially for non-pronominals. Stronger supervised baselines (e.g., a purely lexical classifier without Freebase/Wikipedia attributes; or a variant that masks title/alias features) would help isolate the contribution of knowledge features and assess robustness.

Questions to Authors
- Data and splits: Are the 10-fold CV splits document-level (no cross-document leakage)? Please report standard deviations across folds. Will you release WCR with train/dev/test splits?
- Mention detection: At train and test time, what proportions of mentions are gold vs. automatically detected? Are singletons only from the automatic detector? How are errors in mention boundaries handled in evaluation?
- Hyperlink-based attributes: How exactly are attributes propagated from hyperlinks to mentions? Do you disambiguate when the same surface form links to multiple pages in the article? How do you avoid attaching MC attributes to non-MC mentions that share substrings?
- Inferred type extraction: How is the common noun “type” derived from the first sentence (rules/examples)? How often does it disagree with Freebase types, and which source is trusted on conflict?
- Pronominal distance features: How is “compatibility” determined using Freebase within a sentence, and what is the coverage when Freebase attributes are missing?

Additional Comments
- Move the task and model definition earlier; state explicitly that you train two binary SVMs (pronominal vs. non-pronominal).
- Cite the specific versions of Dcoref/Scoref early; specify SVM C, class weights, and feature normalization; indicate whether features are standardized.
- Consider reporting ablations for non-pronominal features in a table (analogous to Table 2 for pronouns).
- Provide a few more failure cases, especially where redirects/aliases cause errors or where place names collide with organizations (e.g., “Houston”).
