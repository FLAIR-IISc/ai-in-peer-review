### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces the task of "Main Concept Resolution" for Wikipedia articles, which involves identifying all mentions that refer to the central entity of the article. The authors frame this as a binary classification problem: for each mention in an article, decide if it refers to the main concept (MC) or not. To support this work, they have created a new dataset (WCR) of 30 Wikipedia articles with full coreference annotations. The authors demonstrate that state-of-the-art coreference systems perform poorly on this dataset and task. They then propose a feature-based SVM classifier that leverages Wikipedia-specific markup and linked knowledge from Freebase to significantly outperform several strong baselines. Finally, they show that integrating their high-precision MC classifier as a preliminary sieve into a rule-based coreference system (Stanford's Dcoref) improves its overall performance on full coreference resolution for Wikipedia articles.

The main contributions of this work, in order of significance, are:

1.  **The formulation and motivation of the Main Concept Resolution task and the creation of an accompanying annotated dataset (WCR).** This is the paper's strongest contribution. It identifies a practically important sub-problem of coreference resolution, especially for downstream tasks like Information Extraction from encyclopedic texts. The dataset, though small, provides a valuable resource and testbed for studying domain adaptation and knowledge-rich coreference resolution.

2.  **A strong empirical analysis showing the performance gap of state-of-the-art coreference systems on Wikipedia text.** The paper clearly demonstrates that systems trained on newswire data struggle in this new domain, reinforcing the need for genre-specific adaptation.

3.  **An effective, domain-specific classifier for the MC resolution task.** While the methods are not novel, the authors show that a straightforward classifier with carefully engineered features drawn from Wikipedia's structure and external knowledge bases can be highly effective, substantially outperforming generic systems on this focused task.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and practically relevant task.** The paper convincingly argues for the importance of Main Concept Resolution. In many applications (e.g., building dossiers, biographical information extraction), identifying the coreference chain of the primary entity is more critical than resolving every single mention in the document. By focusing on the MC, the task provides a useful discourse anchor and filters out less relevant mentions, which is a significant practical advantage. This work has the potential to bridge research in coreference resolution and entity linking.

2.  **Valuable new dataset and benchmarking.** The creation and release of the WCR dataset is a significant contribution to the community. By annotating full coreference, the authors enable not only the evaluation of the MC task but also broader coreference research on Wikipedia text. The benchmarking of several recent state-of-the-art systems (Table 1) provides a clear and useful baseline, highlighting the challenges of this domain.

3.  **Strong and convincing experimental results.** The proposed classifier dramatically outperforms a series of well-designed baselines built on top of state-of-the-art systems (Table 4), with an F1 score of nearly 89%, an absolute gain of over 13 points. The ablation study (Tables 2 and 3) effectively demonstrates the contribution of each feature family. Furthermore, the integration into a full coreference system (Dcoref++) shows a tangible improvement of 4 CoNLL F1 points on the full coreference task (Table 5), proving the practical utility of the proposed method beyond the isolated MC task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited methodological novelty.** The core of the proposed system is a standard SVM classifier using a set of hand-engineered features. While the feature engineering is sensible and effective for this specific domain, the techniques themselves (string matching, feature extraction from knowledge bases) are well-established. The paper's contribution lies in the application and task definition rather than in advancing machine learning methodology for NLP.

2.  **Clarity and organization could be improved.** The paper's structure is somewhat unconventional and hinders readability.
    *   The model is not clearly defined early on. The introduction frames the task, but the fact that the core approach is a binary classifier for mentions is not made explicit until later sections. This should be stated upfront in the introduction to better frame the work.
    *   The dataset description (Section 5) appears very late in the paper, after the description of the baselines (Section 3) and the authors' approach (Section 4). The reader evaluates the proposed methods without knowing the nature of the data they were designed for, which is confusing. This section should be moved up, likely before the baselines.
    *   Some terms are used without clear definition. For instance, in Section 4.1, the paper mentions a "candidate list" for looking up mentions that match a hyperlink's surface string, but it's not specified what this list contains (e.g., all NPs, all detected mentions from the Raghunathan et al. method?).

3.  **Ambiguity in data preparation for the classifier.** Section 6.1 describes the training data as "the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)." This is confusing. WCR contains ~7,000 gold-annotated, non-singleton mentions. The system then adds ~13,400 automatically extracted singleton mentions. It is unclear how these sets are combined for training. Are the 7,000 WCR mentions used to provide positive/negative labels for the MC task, while the singletons are only treated as negative instances? A clearer description of the instance creation process for the SVM is needed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the data preparation process in Section 6.1? Specifically, how is the set of 20,362 mentions constructed from the 7,000 gold WCR mentions and the 13,400 predicted mentions? How are the labels (MC or not-MC) assigned to mentions in this final training set?

2.  The feature set is effective but largely based on direct comparisons and coarse attributes. Did you experiment with richer feature conjunctions or interaction terms? Given the performance gains from each feature family, it seems plausible that combinations (e.g., a mention being of a certain `inferred type` AND appearing in a specific `position`) could further improve the model.

3.  In Section 4.1, you describe enriching mentions by matching them to hyperlinks. Could you elaborate on the matching process? For example, given the mention "the performer" and a link `[[Chilly Gonzales|the Canadian performer]]`, how is the match determined? Is it based on headword overlap, string inclusion, or another heuristic?
