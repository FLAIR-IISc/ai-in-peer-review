- Introduces "main concept resolution" for Wikipedia: identify mentions referring to an article's main topic and motivate its practical importance for IE tasks.  
- Creates the WCR dataset (30 Wikipedia articles) and shows state-of-the-art coreference systems perform poorly on this domain.  
- Proposes a binary classifier using Wikipedia markup, links, and Freebase features, achieving ~89% F1 and ~13-point absolute improvement over the best baseline.  
- Integrates the classifier as a high-precision sieve into Stanford Dcoref, improving full coreference performance by ~4 CoNLL F1 on WCR.  
- Main contribution is the task formulation and dataset, which shift focus to an entity-centric, high-value subproblem of coreference.  
- Demonstrates a simple, knowledge-rich approach that is effective and practically useful despite its simplicity.  
- Task is well-motivated and useful for applications (e.g., dossiers, biographical summaries); ~25% of mentions in WCR refer to the main concept.  
- WCR is a valuable resource and exposes a large performance gap relative to OntoNotes, motivating domain-specific work.  
- Strong empirical evidence: competitive baselines, clear ablation studies, and measurable gains when integrated into full coreference systems.  
- Work enables closer integration of coreference and entity linking, opening avenues for leveraging structured knowledge.  
- Methodological novelty is limited: standard SVM classifier and feature engineering adapted from prior work.  
- Paper clarity and organization need improvement: model definition appears late and dataset description is placed after methodology.  
- Experimental ambiguities: unclear training-data composition (relation between ~13,400 auto mentions and ~7,000 annotated mentions) and imprecise description of "candidate list" and hyperlink-based attribute enrichment.  
- Question: clarify the composition of the training data—are the ~13,400 automatically extracted mentions predicted singletons from the same 30 WCR articles?  
- Question: define "candidate list" precisely and explain the mechanism for assigning attributes from hyperlinks (e.g., is exact anchor-text matching required?).  
- Question: did you try richer feature conjunctions or alternative models (e.g., neural networks) to capture more complex interactions between mentions and the main concept?
