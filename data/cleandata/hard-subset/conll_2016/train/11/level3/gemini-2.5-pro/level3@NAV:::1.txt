**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces and formalizes the task of "main concept" coreference resolution on Wikipedia, which involves identifying the complete coreference chain for the entity an article is about. The authors contribute a new dataset of 30 annotated Wikipedia articles, finding that roughly 25% of mentions refer to the main concept. They present a binary classifier that uses features derived from Wikipedia's structure and linked knowledge bases to significantly outperform simple baselines built from state-of-the-art systems. Furthermore, they demonstrate that integrating this specialized classifier into Stanford's Dcoref system yields substantial improvements on the full coreference task for this domain.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **The formulation of a novel, practically motivated task and an accompanying dataset.** The "main concept" resolution task is highly relevant for information extraction applications, and the WCR dataset provides a valuable resource for future research in this area.
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of the value of domain-specific knowledge.** The paper shows that a relatively simple classifier leveraging domain-specific features (from Wikipedia markup and Freebase) can drastically outperform complex, general-purpose coreference systems, highlighting the limitations of current SOTA models on new genres.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Practical Motivation:** The paper successfully avoids the common pitfall of defining a new task simply to show that existing systems perform poorly. The "main concept" task is genuinely important for downstream applications like biography generation and knowledge base population, as it focuses on resolving the most salient entity and filtering noise.
2.  **Valuable New Resource:** The annotated WCR dataset, though modest in size, is a significant contribution. It provides a much-needed testbed for coreference on encyclopedic text and establishes a benchmark for this new task, which could help revitalize coreference research on domains beyond newswire.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Setup and Strong Results:** The authors build sensible baselines and show that their focused classifier achieves a large performance gain (over 13 F1 points). Crucially, they close the loop by integrating their component into a full coreference system and demonstrating a significant end-to-end improvement, which validates the utility of their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** While effective, the techniques employed are not novel. The system is a standard binary SVM classifier with well-established feature types (string matching, entity types, etc.). The innovation lies in the problem definition and feature engineering for the specific domain, not in the core methodology.
2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper's structure could be improved. The central model—a binary classifier—is not clearly defined until late in the paper; this crucial architectural detail should appear much earlier. Similarly, the dataset description in Section 5 should logically precede the approach in Section 4, as the approach is evaluated on that dataset.
3.  **Ambiguity in Data Preparation:** Section 6.1 is confusing regarding the composition of the training data. It states the data is a union of annotated WCR mentions and mentions from the Raghunathan et al. method, but it is unclear how the final set of ~20,000 mentions is formed from the 7,000 annotated non-singleton mentions. This needs explicit clarification.
