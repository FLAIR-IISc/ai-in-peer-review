### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces the task of "Main Concept Resolution," a sub-problem of coreference resolution focused on identifying all mentions of the primary entity described in a Wikipedia article. The authors frame this as a binary classification problem (mention refers to the Main Concept or not) and develop a system using features derived from Wikipedia's internal markup and the Freebase knowledge base. To evaluate their approach, they created a new dataset of 30 annotated Wikipedia articles (WCR), on which they show that state-of-the-art coreference systems perform poorly. Their proposed classifier significantly outperforms strong baselines built from these systems. Furthermore, they demonstrate that integrating their classifier as a high-precision sieve into a rule-based coreference system (Dcoref) improves its performance on the full coreference resolution task on their new dataset.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:

1.  **The introduction, motivation, and evaluation of the "Main Concept Resolution" task, including the creation of the new WCR dataset.** The paper makes a compelling case for this focused task as a pragmatically useful problem for applications like Information Extraction. By creating and releasing a new annotated resource, they provide a valuable testbed and convincingly demonstrate a domain-shift problem for existing coreference systems.
2.  **An effective, knowledge-rich classifier that serves as a strong baseline for the new task.** While the techniques are not novel, the system successfully demonstrates that leveraging Wikipedia's unique structural properties and linked external knowledge can lead to high performance on this task, achieving an F1 score of nearly 89%.
3.  **A demonstration that solving this targeted sub-task can improve a general-purpose coreference system.** By integrating their classifier into the Dcoref system, the authors show a substantial 4-point absolute improvement in CoNLL F1 score, suggesting that focusing on the main concept can provide a powerful anchor for resolving other entities in a document.

### Strengths

1.  **A well-motivated and practically useful problem formulation.** Focusing on the main concept of a document is highly relevant for many downstream NLP tasks, such as building dossiers from biographies or summarizing key information about an entity. This framing provides a strong discourse anchor that simplifies the coreference problem in a meaningful way. It represents a promising direction that could bridge entity linking and coreference research while revitalizing work on new, impactful domains beyond newswire.
2.  **Strong and thorough empirical results.** The paper presents a convincing case for its approach through extensive experiments. The proposed classifier dramatically outperforms several well-designed baselines derived from state-of-the-art systems (e.g., +13 F1 over the best Dcoref baseline). The ablation study clearly shows the contribution of each feature family. Crucially, the authors go beyond the bespoke task and show the practical utility of their model by integrating it into a full coreference pipeline (Dcoref++), where it outperforms all baseline systems on the WCR dataset.
3.  **The creation of a new, useful resource for the community.** The WCR dataset, though modest in size (30 articles), is a valuable contribution. The authors use it effectively to highlight the performance drop of SOTA systems when moving from newswire to encyclopedic text, justifying the need for domain-specific adaptation. The commitment to release the dataset and system outputs will enable reproducibility and encourage future work on this important domain.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited technical novelty of the proposed model.** The primary contribution of the paper is the problem formulation and application, not a novel machine learning method. The core of the proposed system is a standard SVM with carefully engineered features. While the features are sensible and demonstrably effective, they are largely inspired by prior work that incorporates knowledge from resources like Wikipedia and Freebase into coreference resolution.
2.  **The paper's organization and clarity could be improved.** The core of the authors' method is a binary classifier, but this is not made explicit until Section 4. Stating this framing more clearly in the introduction would help orient the reader. Similarly, the dataset description in Section 5 is crucial context for all the experiments, yet it appears after the baselines and the model have been fully described. Moving the dataset description earlier would improve the paper's flow.
3.  **Ambiguity in the data preparation for the classifier.** Section 6.1 states that the training set consists of "the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)." This is confusing. WCR contains gold-annotated coreferent mentions. The mention detector finds candidate mentions, including singletons. It is unclear if the final set is composed of (a) gold mentions plus automatically detected singletons, or (b) all automatically detected mentions, which are then labeled as positive/negative using the WCR annotations. This is a critical detail for understanding the experimental setup and reproducibility.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact procedure for constructing the set of mentions used to train and test your classifier, as described in Section 6.1? Specifically, how are the gold mentions from WCR combined with the automatically detected mentions? Are gold mention boundaries used during training/testing, or are only the labels used to supervise the classifier on automatically detected mention candidates?
2.  The Dcoref++ system prevents other sieves from adding new mentions to the MC coreference chain after your new sieve has run. Have you analyzed the impact of this hard constraint? It seems plausible that this could hurt recall if a later, lower-precision sieve was able to correctly identify a particularly difficult or unusual mention of the main concept that your classifier missed.
