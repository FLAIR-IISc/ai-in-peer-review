### **Summary**

This paper introduces and tackles the task of "main concept resolution" in Wikipedia, which involves identifying all mentions within an article that refer to the article's main topic. The authors motivate this as a practically important sub-problem of coreference resolution, particularly for downstream information extraction tasks. To support this research, they have created and annotated a new dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR), on which they show that state-of-the-art coreference systems perform poorly. They propose a binary classification approach that leverages features from Wikipedia's structure (markup, links) and external knowledge from Freebase. Their classifier substantially outperforms several strong baselines built on top of existing coreference systems. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of their approach by integrating it as a high-precision sieve into Stanford's Dcoref system, achieving a significant improvement in performance on the full coreference resolution task for their Wikipedia dataset.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **Introduction and Motivation of a New, Practical Task and Dataset:** The paper's primary contribution is the formalization of the "main concept resolution" task. The authors make a compelling case for its practical importance, shifting focus from generic coreference to a high-value, entity-centric sub-problem. The accompanying WCR dataset, though small, is a valuable contribution that provides a new benchmark for coreference in the encyclopedic domain and clearly demonstrates the performance gap of existing systems.
2.  **Demonstration of a Simple but Highly Effective Knowledge-Rich Approach:** The paper demonstrates that a straightforward classification model using well-engineered features from Wikipedia and Freebase can be highly effective for this task. The model achieves an F1 score of nearly 89%, a 13-point absolute improvement over the best baseline. The successful integration of this model into a full SOTA coreference system (Dcoref) further validates the approach and shows its practical benefit for the broader coreference problem on this domain.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated and Important Task:** The paper successfully reframes a part of the coreference problem in a way that is immediately recognizable as useful for applications like building dossiers or summarizing biographical information. By focusing on the central entity of a document, the task filters out less relevant mentions and provides a strong discourse anchor. The finding that ~25% of mentions in the annotated corpus refer to the main concept underscores the task's significance. This is a good example of creating a new task that is genuinely important, rather than just one where existing systems fail.
2.  **Valuable New Resource and Analysis:** The creation of the WCR dataset is a significant strength. The analysis presented in Table 1, which shows a dramatic performance drop for several state-of-the-art coreference systems on WCR compared to OntoNotes, provides strong motivation for domain-specific coreference research and establishes WCR as a useful and challenging testbed.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed classifier achieves impressive results, convincingly outperforming a series of well-designed and competitive baselines. The feature ablation studies (Tables 2 and 3) are clear and effectively illustrate the impact of different knowledge sources. Furthermore, the final experiment showing that integrating the classifier into Dcoref improves full coreference resolution by 4 CoNLL F1 points is a strong piece of evidence for the approach's utility.
4.  **Potential for Broader Impact:** This work sits at a productive intersection of coreference resolution and entity linking. By anchoring coreference to a known entity, it opens promising avenues for leveraging structured knowledge more deeply and could help revitalize coreference research on meaningful, knowledge-rich domains beyond newswire.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Novelty:** While effective, the techniques employed are not novel. The system is a standard SVM classifier with feature engineering. The features themselves, while thoughtfully selected for this task, are largely adaptations of ideas from prior work on using Wikipedia and other knowledge bases for NLP. The paper's strength lies in the problem formulation and application, not in advancing machine learning methodology for coreference.
2.  **Clarity and Organization:** The paper's structure could be improved. The core model is a binary classifier, but this is not stated clearly until Section 6.2. A more explicit definition of the model should appear much earlier, ideally in Section 4. Additionally, the dataset description (Section 5) comes after the methodology (Section 4), which is unconventional. Presenting the dataset and the baseline results on it first would provide stronger context and motivation for the proposed method.
3.  **Ambiguity in Experimental Setup:** Several key details of the data preparation and feature extraction are unclear.
    *   The description of the training data in Section 6.1 is confusing. It states the dataset is the "union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)." This makes it difficult to understand the origin of the ~13,400 automatically extracted singleton mentions relative to the 7,000 annotated coreferent mentions.
    *   In Section 4.1, the process of enriching mentions is not fully specified. The term "candidate list" is not defined, and the mechanism for using hyperlinks to add semantic attributes is vague. A more precise explanation of how a mention is matched to a hyperlink and how attributes are transferred would strengthen the paper.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the composition of the training data described in Section 6.1? Specifically, are the ~13,400 automatically extracted mentions the predicted singletons from the same 30 WCR articles, which were excluded from the original annotation effort?
2.  Regarding the preprocessing in Section 4.1: what is the precise definition of the "candidate list"? And could you elaborate on the process for assigning attributes from hyperlinks? For example, is an exact string match between the mention text and the hyperlink anchor text required?
3.  The feature set is clearly effective. However, given that the features are largely atomic, did you consider exploring more complex feature conjunctions or alternative models (e.g., neural networks) that might capture more complex interactions between a mention and the main concept's properties?
