## Review of “Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution”

This paper introduces a focused coreference resolution task – identifying mentions of the main concept within a Wikipedia article – and demonstrates significant improvements on this task by integrating a binary classifier with a rule-based coreference system. The work is well-motivated, leveraging the unique structure of Wikipedia and addressing a practical need in information extraction. While the technical contributions are incremental, the dataset and resulting performance gains are valuable, and the work highlights a promising direction for coreference research.

**Summary:**

The authors tackle the problem of identifying all mentions referring to the central topic of a Wikipedia article. They argue that existing coreference systems, trained largely on newswire data, perform poorly on Wikipedia text. They create a dataset of 30 annotated Wikipedia articles (WCR), demonstrate a simple binary classifier trained on Wikipedia-specific features outperforms baseline approaches, and show substantial improvements when this classifier is integrated into Stanford’s Dcoref system.  The core idea is to focus coreference resolution efforts on mentions relevant to the article’s main topic, improving efficiency and accuracy for downstream IE tasks.

**Main Contributions (in order of strength):**

1. **The WCR Dataset:** The creation and release of a dedicated, annotated dataset for “main concept” coreference resolution in Wikipedia is the most significant contribution. This provides a valuable resource for evaluating and comparing coreference systems tailored to this specific, practically relevant task. The annotation guidelines and dataset characteristics (25% of mentions refer to the main concept) are clearly defined.
2. **Demonstration of Task Importance and Performance Gains:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that identifying coreference chains for the main concept is a challenging problem for existing systems and that a targeted approach, leveraging Wikipedia's structure and external knowledge, significantly improves performance. The 4% F1 gain on full coreference resolution within Dcoref is substantial.
3. **Focused Coreference as a Useful Task:** The authors articulate a compelling argument for focusing coreference resolution on the main concept of a document.  This is particularly useful for applications like biography extraction and knowledge base construction, where identifying references to the central entity is paramount.


**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing coreference systems when applied to Wikipedia and establishes a strong motivation for the proposed task. The connection to practical IE applications is well-explained.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results demonstrate a consistent and significant performance improvement of the proposed approach over baselines and state-of-the-art systems on the WCR dataset. The analysis of feature importance provides valuable insights.
3. **Effective Leverage of Wikipedia Structure:** The authors effectively utilize the hyperlink structure of Wikipedia, enriching mention representations with semantic attributes from Freebase. This demonstrates a smart approach to exploiting the unique characteristics of the resource.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, including a 10-fold cross-validation procedure and comparison to several established coreference systems. Inclusion of multiple evaluation metrics (MUC, B3, CEAF, CoNLL) provides a comprehensive assessment.
5. **Integration into Existing System:** Successfully integrating the classifier into a complex, rule-based system like Dcoref and achieving performance gains highlights the practical utility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Method:** While the experimental results are strong, the core techniques (SVM classifier, feature engineering) are not particularly novel. The features themselves, though sensible, are largely inspired by existing work.  The primary novelty lies in the application of these techniques to the specific “main concept” resolution task.
2. **Delayed Model Definition:** The paper initially frames the work around a general approach without clearly stating that the core component is a *binary* classifier until Section 4. Presenting this fundamental aspect of the method earlier would improve clarity.
3. **Organizational Issues and Clarity:** Several organizational and clarity issues detract from the presentation:
    * The dataset description (Section 5) should be moved earlier, ideally after the introduction. This would provide essential context for understanding the experimental setup.
    * The paper should explicitly cite relevant systems like Stanford CoreNLP, Dcoref, and Scoref upon first mention.
    * The term “candidate list” (Section 4.1) is not well-defined. It needs a clearer explanation of its composition and how it relates to mention detection.
    * The explanation of how hyperlinks add semantic attributes could be more detailed.
4. **Ambiguity Regarding WCR Mentions:** Section 6.1’s discussion of the union of WCR mentions and mentions from Raghunathan et al. is confusing. The origin of the “extra WCR mentions” needs to be clarified.  Why were additional mentions added and how does this impact the evaluation?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the annotation process for the WCR dataset, including inter-annotator agreement scores?
2.  In Section 6.1, you mention adding mentions from Raghunathan et al. to the WCR dataset. What criteria were used to include these mentions, and how did this affect the dataset’s characteristics?
3.  You state that you shared the dataset and dumps of identified mentions "at www.somewhere.country."  Please provide a concrete, working URL for this resource.
4. Were there any error analysis conducted in identifying the types of errors?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the approach, such as its reliance on accurate mention detection and the potential for errors in Freebase data.
*   Consider discussing how the approach might be extended to handle more complex document structures or multiple main concepts.
