This paper revisits the task of identifying mentions of the main concept (MC) within a Wikipedia article, framing it as a coreference resolution problem specifically adapted to this unique text genre. The authors argue that existing coreference systems, largely trained on news data, perform poorly on Wikipedia and present a new annotated dataset (WCR) of 30 articles with full coreference annotations, highlighting that approximately 25% of mentions relate to the article's MC. They propose a binary classifier leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase knowledge to identify MC mentions, demonstrating significant performance gains over strong baselines built on state-of-the-art coreference systems. Furthermore, integrating this classifier into the Stanford Dcoref system yields substantial improvements in overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia text.

The primary contribution of this work is the introduction of a focused coreference task—main concept resolution in Wikipedia—along with a corresponding annotated dataset. While coreference resolution on Wikipedia has been previously explored, this work provides a dedicated resource and evaluation framework, recognizing the distinct characteristics of this domain. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the demonstration that incorporating readily available information from Wikipedia’s structure and linked knowledge bases can significantly improve performance on this task, outperforming general-purpose coreference systems. Finally, the successful integration of their classifier into an existing rule-based system suggests a practical path toward enhancing coreference resolution in Wikipedia and related applications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel and Relevant Task:** The paper identifies a practically motivated and previously under-addressed problem – identifying mentions of the central entity in a Wikipedia article. This is a key strength. Many coreference tasks focus on general entity resolution; this work zeroes in on a crucial element for downstream tasks like information extraction and knowledge base construction. The justification for the task’s importance, especially for filtering irrelevant mentions in IE applications, is well articulated.

2. **High-Quality Dataset:** The creation of the WCR dataset is a significant contribution. The authors provide details on the annotation process and dataset characteristics (size, annotation guidelines consistency with OntoNotes). The 25% prevalence of MC mentions suggests the task isn’t trivially solvable and offers ample opportunity for improvement.  The dataset is crucial for enabling future research in this area.

3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors convincingly demonstrate that their approach outperforms strong baselines, including state-of-the-art coreference systems (Dcoref and Scoref). The reported F1 gains are substantial and highlight the effectiveness of the proposed features and classifier. Integrating the classifier into Dcoref further reinforces these findings and showcases practical applicability.

4. **Effective Feature Engineering:**  The features used in the classifier are sensible and leverage Wikipedia’s unique properties (markup, hyperlinks, Freebase links) effectively. The careful consideration of different feature families and their incremental impact on performance provides valuable insight into the factors driving success.  

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methodology:** While the task and dataset are novel, the underlying machine learning approach – a binary SVM classifier with manually engineered features – is relatively standard. The authors acknowledge this, but the novelty lies more in the *application* of these techniques to a specific and important problem. Exploring more sophisticated models (e.g., neural networks, contextual embeddings) could be a direction for future work.

2. **Delayed Model Definition:** The paper introduces the task early but doesn’t clearly state that the proposed approach is a binary classifier until later sections. A clearer upfront framing of the problem as a binary classification task would improve readability and comprehension.

3. **Dataset Description Placement:** The detailed description of the WCR dataset appears in Section 5, after the baselines and approach are introduced. Moving this section earlier in the paper would provide important context for understanding the experimental setup and evaluation results.

4. **Clarification Needed Regarding Mention Sources:** Section 6.1 mentions a union of WCR mentions and those from Raghunathan et al. (2010). The origin and nature of the extra mentions extracted from Raghunathan et al. are unclear. Is this an extension of WCR with automatically generated mentions? A more detailed explanation is needed to understand how the training data was constructed.  The phrase “candidate list” also needs definition – does it refer to all mentions or some subset?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Have you investigated the potential of more sophisticated machine learning models (e.g., neural networks) for this task, and if so, what were the results?

2. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the types of errors made by the classifier, particularly for cases where it incorrectly identifies mentions as referring to the main concept?

3.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing a weight of 2.0 for penalizing the negative class during SVM training? What impact did this have on performance?

4. What are the primary challenges in scaling up the WCR dataset to a larger size? Are there automated or semi-automated annotation techniques that could be explored?
