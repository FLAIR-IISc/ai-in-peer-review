- Frames identifying main-concept mentions in Wikipedia as a domain-specific coreference problem.  
- Presents WCR, a new annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles with full coreference annotations (~25% of mentions refer to the main concept).  
- Proposes a binary classifier using Wikipedia markup and Freebase links to detect main-concept mentions.  
- Classifier significantly outperforms strong baselines (Dcoref, Scoref) on Wikipedia text.  
- Integrating the classifier into Stanford Dcoref yields substantial improvements in overall coreference resolution on Wikipedia.  
- Main contribution: introduces focused task (main concept resolution) and provides a dedicated dataset/evaluation framework.  
- Secondary contribution: shows that Wikipedia structure and linked KB features materially boost performance over general-purpose coreference systems.  
- Practical contribution: successful integration into an existing rule-based system suggests a path to improve real-world Wikipedia coreference.  
- Novel and relevant task with clear motivation and downstream utility (IE, KB construction).  
- High-quality dataset with annotation process details and non-trivial prevalence of MC mentions.  
- Strong empirical results with substantial F1 gains validating the approach.  
- Effective, sensible feature engineering leveraging Wikipedia-specific signals.  
- Methodological novelty is limited—uses a binary SVM with manual features rather than modern neural approaches.  
- Paper delays clear statement of the model; the binary-classifier framing appears late.  
- WCR dataset description is placed late in the paper (Section 5), reducing needed context for experiments.  
- Ambiguity about mention sources: unclear how WCR mentions and Raghunathan et al. (2010) mentions are combined or defined; “candidate list” is undefined.  
- Question: Did the authors try more sophisticated models (e.g., neural/contextual embeddings), and what were the results?  
- Question: Request for detailed error analysis, especially false positives for main-concept classification.  
- Question: Why was a weight of 2.0 chosen for penalizing the negative class in SVM training, and what was its impact?  
- Question: What are the challenges and possible automated/semi-automated approaches for scaling up WCR?  
- Additional: Explicitly cite Stanford CoreNLP, Dcoref, and Scoref in intro/related work.  
- Additional: Add a small end-to-end example (mention detection → classification) to clarify the pipeline.
