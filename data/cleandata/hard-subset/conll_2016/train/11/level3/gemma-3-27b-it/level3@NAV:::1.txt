## Review of “Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution”

This paper introduces a focused coreference resolution task – identifying mentions of a Wikipedia article’s main concept – and demonstrates its practical utility, particularly for information extraction. The authors address a gap in coreference research by adapting techniques to the specific characteristics of Wikipedia text, where existing systems struggle. The creation of a new, annotated dataset (WCR) with 30 Wikipedia articles is a significant contribution, as is the empirical demonstration that a simple classifier can substantially improve coreference performance when integrated into a rule-based system (Stanford’s Dcoref). The paper convincingly argues that focusing coreference resolution on mentions relevant to the ‘main concept’ is a valuable direction, offering a discourse anchor and providing a testbed for knowledge-enhanced coreference methods.

The primary strength of this work lies in the novel task definition and the resulting dataset. While coreference resolution is a well-studied area, targeting the main concept introduces a meaningful constraint and real-world relevance (e.g., biography summarization). The authors clearly demonstrate that standard systems perform poorly on this task, highlighting the need for specialized approaches. The subsequent gains achieved by integrating their classifier into Dcoref are compelling and validate the approach. A second, related strength is the practical application of Wikipedia’s inherent structure (hyperlinks, redirects) to enrich mention representations, circumventing the challenges of named entity linking.

However, the technical contribution of the classifier itself is relatively incremental. The features used are sensible and well-motivated, but lack significant novelty.  The paper would benefit from exploring more complex feature interactions or incorporating more sophisticated machine learning techniques. A key weakness is the late introduction of the system’s core functionality – that it’s a binary classifier – which disrupts the flow of the paper.  The model description should be presented much earlier. Additionally, clarity could be improved by explicitly citing Stanford/Dcoref/Scoref and by providing a clearer explanation of the "candidate list" and how hyperlinks contribute semantic attributes. The connection between the union of WCR mentions and those from Raghunathan et al. in Section 6.1 is also unclear and requires clarification. 

**Questions for Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the limitations of the dataset (WCR)? What are the potential biases introduced by the selection of 30 articles, and how might those biases impact the generalizability of the results?
*   The paper demonstrates improvements within the Dcoref framework. How might this approach be adapted or integrated with other coreference systems, particularly those based on neural network architectures?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the classifier to the quality of the information extracted from Wikipedia and Freebase?
