

# Review for CONLL

## Summary of the Paper
The paper addresses coreference resolution (CR) in Wikipedia articles, a text genre where existing CR systems trained on news data perform poorly. The authors define a specific task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) in a Wikipedia article. They propose a binary classification approach using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. The authors evaluate their method against baselines based on state-of-the-art CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref) and demonstrate that their approach outperforms these baselines. They further show that integrating their classifier into Dcoref improves overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia articles.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Task Definition and Dataset:** The authors define a novel task (Main Concept Resolution) for Wikipedia articles and create a new dataset (WCR3) of 30 Wikipedia articles annotated according to OntoNotes guidelines.
2. **Feature Engineering for Wikipedia:** They develop a set of features specifically designed for Wikipedia's unique structure (markup, redirects, internal links) and Freebase information to distinguish mentions of the main concept.
3. **System Integration:** They demonstrate that their MCR system can be integrated into an existing CR pipeline (Dcoref) to improve overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia articles.

## Strengths
1. **Strong Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper provides a compelling rationale for focusing on Wikipedia as a text genre for coreference resolution. The authors clearly articulate why existing CR systems fail on Wikipedia and why this gap matters for NLP applications. They effectively cite relevant literature to support their claims about the limitations of current CR systems on non-news text.

2. **Novel Feature Set:** The feature engineering is thoughtful and leverages Wikipedia's unique structure in ways that generic CR systems don't. For example, using redirects, internal links, and Freebase attributes to enrich mentions is a clever approach that should be valuable for Wikipedia-specific CR. The paper demonstrates that these features significantly improve performance (e.g., adding the "title" feature family increases F1 by 7 points).

3. **Significant Performance Gains:** The results show substantial improvements over baselines (13-15 F1 points for the MCR task, 4 F1 points for full CR when integrated into Dcoref). These gains are meaningful in the context of CR research and demonstrate the practical utility of their approach.

4. **Reproducibility:** The authors plan to share their dataset and results, which is a significant contribution to the community and will facilitate future work. This is particularly valuable given the limited existing resources for Wikipedia-specific CR.

5. **Practical Integration:** The paper demonstrates how their approach can be integrated into an existing CR pipeline (Dcoref) to improve performance, showing practical utility beyond just the MCR task. The Dcoref++ system shows a clear improvement in standard CR metrics.

## Weaknesses
1. **Extremely Limited Dataset Size:** The WCR3 dataset is very small (30 articles, ~60k tokens, 1800 MC mentions). This is orders of magnitude smaller than standard CR datasets (e.g., OntoNotes has thousands of documents). This limitation severely impacts the statistical significance of the results and raises serious questions about whether the findings generalize to the broader Wikipedia corpus. The authors acknowledge this size limitation but don't sufficiently address how the results might scale to larger datasets.

2. **Lack of Comparison with Prior Work on Wikipedia CR:** The authors mention two prior studies (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) that worked on Wikipedia-related CR but don't compare their approach to these works. It's unclear how their method differs from or improves upon these prior approaches. This is a significant gap in the related work and evaluation sections.

3. **Unclear Relationship to General Coreference Resolution:** The paper focuses on a specific task (MCR) rather than general coreference resolution. The authors don't sufficiently explain how MCR relates to or could be extended to the full CR problem. The paper would benefit from more discussion on the broader implications for CR research and how their approach might be adapted for full CR.

4. **Insufficient Methodological Details:** The paper lacks sufficient detail about how the classifier was trained and validated. For example, it mentions a "greedy fashion" for adding feature families but doesn't explain how the order was determined. The paper also doesn't provide enough detail about the training process to ensure reproducibility, such as hyperparameter selection.

5. **Evaluation Metrics for MCR Task:** The paper doesn't clearly explain how the MCR task relates to standard CR evaluation metrics. It's unclear whether the MCR task is a necessary or sufficient condition for good CR performance, which limits the interpretation of the results. The authors should clarify how MCR fits into the broader CR landscape.

## Questions to Authors
1. How did you select the 30 articles for your WCR3 dataset? Was the selection process random, or were specific characteristics (e.g., article length, topic, link density) considered to ensure diversity? How representative do you believe this small sample is of the broader Wikipedia corpus?

2. Why didn't you compare your approach to the two prior works on Wikipedia CR (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008)? How does your method differ from or improve upon these approaches?

3. The paper focuses on a specific task (MCR) rather than general coreference resolution. Can you elaborate on how your approach could be extended to handle full coreference resolution in Wikipedia articles? What additional challenges would need to be addressed?

4. The dataset size is very small (30 articles). How do you address concerns about the statistical significance of your results given this limited sample size? Would you recommend using this dataset for training models, or is it primarily for evaluation?

5. The paper mentions that the WCR dataset is "far smaller than the OntoNotes one" but still claims it's "representative." Can you provide more justification for this claim, especially given the significant size difference?

## Recommendation
While the paper identifies an important gap in coreference resolution research and presents a novel approach for Wikipedia articles, the extremely limited dataset size and lack of comparison with prior work on Wikipedia CR are significant weaknesses that prevent the paper from meeting the high standards expected at CONLL. The methodological details are also insufficient for full reproducibility.

I recommend **Rejection** with the following suggestions for improvement:
1. Expand the dataset to include at least 100-200 articles to ensure statistical significance and better represent the diversity of Wikipedia content.
2. Conduct a thorough comparison with prior work on Wikipedia-specific CR (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) to demonstrate the novelty and improvement of the proposed approach.
3. Provide more detailed methodological descriptions, including hyperparameter selection, feature selection process, and validation procedures.
4. Clarify the relationship between the MCR task and general coreference resolution, and discuss how the approach could be extended to full CR.
5. Provide stronger justification for why the small dataset is representative of Wikipedia content.

With these improvements, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the field. However, as it stands, the limitations are too significant to warrant acceptance at CONLL.
