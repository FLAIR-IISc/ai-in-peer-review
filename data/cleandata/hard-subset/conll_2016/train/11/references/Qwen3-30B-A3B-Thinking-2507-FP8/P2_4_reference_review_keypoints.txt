- Contribution: Introduces WCR, a 30-article (60k token) dataset for evaluating main-concept (MC) identification in Wikipedia.
- Contribution: Proposes a binary classifier using Wikipedia markup and Freebase-derived features to detect MC mentions.
- Contribution: Reports strong results—~89% F1—outperforming baselines by 13–15 points and improving a rule-based CR system (Dcoref++) by 4 CoNLL F1 points.
- Strength: Tackles a genuine problem—state-of-the-art CR systems trained on news perform poorly on Wikipedia—and motivates the need for Wikipedia-specific methods.
- Strength: Strong empirical evidence with high F1 scores for pronominal and non-pronominal MC mentions and practical improvement when integrated into Dcoref.
- Strength: Careful, insightful feature engineering leveraging Wikipedia-specific signals and Freebase to avoid NER/linking errors; ablation analysis shows feature contributions.
- Weakness: Narrow focus on identifying only the main concept rather than full coreference chains, with unclear generalization to full CR tasks.
- Weakness: Very small dataset (30 articles); 10-fold cross-validation may not ensure generalization to larger Wikipedia corpora.
- Weakness: Lacks direct comparison to prior Wikipedia-specific work (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008), making novelty/improvement unclear.
- Weakness: Limited discussion of limitations and edge cases (e.g., articles where the title isn't the MC, event/concept pages, non-English Wikipedia).
- Question: How does this work differ from and improve upon Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008)?
- Question: How confident are the results given the small dataset, and have the authors considered transfer learning or augmentation?
- Question: How can the MC-focused approach be extended to full coreference resolution in Wikipedia articles?
- Question: How robust is the title-as-main-concept assumption for articles where the title poorly represents the MC (e.g., event names vs. entity names)?
- Overall assessment: Valuable, well-executed study with strong empirical results but limited scope, small dataset, and missing comparisons—suitable as a workshop paper but not yet a major conference contribution.
