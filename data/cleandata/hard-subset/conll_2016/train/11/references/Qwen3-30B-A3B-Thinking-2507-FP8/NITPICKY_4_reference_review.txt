

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach to identifying mentions of the main concept (MC) in Wikipedia articles, framing it as a binary classification problem. The authors introduce a classifier that leverages Wikipedia-specific features and Freebase attributes to distinguish MC mentions from other mentions. The main contributions claimed are:
1. A dedicated testbed for evaluating coreference resolution systems on Wikipedia (WCR dataset)
2. A novel binary classifier for identifying MC mentions with high precision/recall
3. Integration of the classifier into an existing coreference system (Dcoref) to improve overall performance

## Strengths

1. **Novel Application Domain**: The paper correctly identifies a gap in coreference resolution research for Wikipedia texts, which is a significant resource in NLP. The authors provide a clear motivation for why existing CR systems perform poorly on Wikipedia.

2. **Well-Defined Task**: The "main concept resolution" task is well-defined and has practical applications in information extraction from Wikipedia, as evidenced by the cited prior work (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008).

3. **Strong Baselines**: The four baseline approaches (B1-B4) are well-motivated and systematically designed to test different heuristics for identifying MC mentions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Significant Dataset Description Inconsistencies**: The paper claims the dataset "consists of 30 documents, comprising 60k tokens," but then states "the resource contains roughly 7000 non singleton mentions, among which 1800 refer to the main concept, which is to say that 30 chains out of 1469 make up for 25% of the mentions annotated." This is logically inconsistent - if there are 30 chains, they should be 30 out of 1469 *chains*, not mentions. The phrasing "30 chains out of 1469 make up for 25% of the mentions" is mathematically incorrect (30/1469 ≈ 2%, not 25%). This inconsistency undermines the credibility of the dataset description.

2. **Critical Citation and Terminology Errors**: 
   - The paper cites (Ratinov and Roth, 2012) as using GLOW for wikification, but GLOW was introduced in Ratinov et al. (2011), not Ratinov and Roth (2012). This is a significant error in the related work section.
   - The paper claims OntoNotes is the reference for CoNLL 2012 test data (Table 1), but OntoNotes and CoNLL 2012 are distinct resources. The CoNLL 2012 test set was derived from OntoNotes but is not the same as OntoNotes itself.
   - The paper states "Jena2" for the Freebase query tool, but the correct reference is "Apache Jena" (not Jena2).

3. **Reproducibility Issues**: 
   - The paper states "We share all the datasets we used in this study" but provides a placeholder URL "www.somewhere.country" instead of a real URL. This renders the claim of reproducibility meaningless.
   - Figure 2 (referenced in Section 4.2.1) is not included in the paper, making it impossible to verify the "first four fields of a MC representation" that the authors claim to use for feature extraction.

4. **Methodological Inconsistencies in Results**:
   - Table 4 reports "P R F1" values for "All" mentions that don't match the simple average of the pronominal and non-pronominal values. For example, Dcoref B1 shows pronominal: 64.51/76.55/70.02, non-pronominal: 70.33/63.09/66.51, but "All" shows 67.92/67.77/67.85. The values don't align with a simple average, suggesting a calculation error.
   - In Table 2, the "always positive" baseline shows recall of 100% but precision of 46.70%, which is impossible (if all mentions are positive, recall would be 100% only if all actual MC mentions are identified, but precision would be 100% if all predicted MC mentions are correct).

5. **Technical Implementation Errors**:
   - In Section 6.2, the paper states "we selected the Csupport vector classification" which is a clear typo that should be "C-Support vector classification."
   - The paper claims "We further prevent other sieves from appending new mentions to the MC coreference chain" but doesn't explain how this is implemented in the Dcoref++ system, making the method opaque.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify the relationship between the 30 documents (60k tokens), the 1469 chains, and the 1800 mentions that refer to the main concept? Specifically, how do the 30 chains relate to the 1469 total chains, and how does 30 chains represent 25% of the mentions?

2. The paper states "We use predicted results from 10-fold cross-validation" for Dcoref++ evaluation in Table 5, but the evaluation should be done on gold mentions to be comparable with other systems. Could you explain why predicted mentions were used for this evaluation?

3. Figure 2 (referenced in Section 4.2.1) is not included in the paper. Could you provide the missing figure or describe the four fields of the MC representation in detail?

4. The paper claims "the resulting classifier can link the mention the team to the MC Houston Texans (see example (d)) because it correctly identifies the term team as a type." Could you provide the exact feature values that led to this decision, to verify the claim?

## Overall Recommendation

**Reject**

This paper presents a well-motivated task with potential applications, but the numerous minor methodological inconsistencies and technical errors significantly undermine the credibility of the work. The dataset description contains mathematical inconsistencies, the related work contains critical citation errors, the paper contains multiple typos and technical inaccuracies, and key figures are missing. While the core idea has merit, the execution is flawed in ways that would make reproduction of the results impossible and the results questionable.

The paper's claims of "89% F1 score" and "absolute gain of 13 F1 points over the best baseline" are undermined by the inconsistencies in the reported results (Table 4, Table 5). The lack of a real dataset URL and missing figure 2 make the work non-reproducible, which is a critical failure for a computational NLP paper.

These are not minor issues that could be fixed in a camera-ready version - they are fundamental flaws that impact the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of a paper with these issues, even if the core idea is promising.
