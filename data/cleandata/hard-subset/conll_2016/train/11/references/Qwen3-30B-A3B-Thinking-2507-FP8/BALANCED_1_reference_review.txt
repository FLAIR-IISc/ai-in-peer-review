

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper investigates coreference resolution (CR) specifically for Wikipedia articles, noting that existing CR systems trained on news text perform poorly on Wikipedia. The authors focus on a specific task: identifying all mentions of the "main concept" (MC) in a Wikipedia article (e.g., all mentions of "Chilly Gonzales" in an article about Chilly Gonzales). They frame this as a binary classification problem (does this mention refer to the MC?) and propose a classifier using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **The WCR dataset**: A dedicated dataset for main concept resolution in Wikipedia (30 articles, 60k tokens, 7000 non-singleton mentions annotated with OntoNotes guidelines). This is a significant contribution as it fills a gap in the literature, as Wikipedia has been widely used in NLP but coreference resolution for it has been underexplored.

2. **The binary classification approach for main concept resolution**: A novel framing of the problem (as binary classification rather than full coreference resolution) that leverages Wikipedia-specific features (markup, redirects, Freebase). The approach is simple but effective, outperforming baselines by 13-15 F1 points.

3. **Demonstrating the effectiveness of Wikipedia-specific features**: The paper shows that features derived from Wikipedia's structure (title, inferred type, name variants, etc.) are highly effective for identifying main concept mentions, providing valuable insights for adapting NLP systems to different text genres.

## Strengths

1. **The WCR dataset is a significant contribution**: This is a well-constructed dataset that will be valuable for future research on Wikipedia-specific NLP tasks. The authors have taken care to sample articles with varying characteristics (size, topic, internal link density), making the dataset representative. The dataset is annotated following OntoNotes guidelines, ensuring compatibility with standard evaluation frameworks. This dataset fills an important gap in the literature, as the paper rightly points out that Wikipedia has been widely used in NLP but coreference resolution for it has not been well-studied.

2. **The binary classification approach is well-motivated and effective**: Instead of tackling the full coreference resolution problem (which is complex and has been extensively studied), the authors focus on the specific, well-defined task of identifying mentions of the main concept. This is a practical and useful task for Wikipedia-based NLP applications (e.g., information extraction, knowledge base population). The paper demonstrates that this approach is highly effective (88.99% F1 for pronominal mentions and 88.67% for non-pronominal mentions), outperforming baselines by 13-15 F1 points. The simplicity of the approach makes it both elegant and practical.

3. **The feature engineering leverages Wikipedia-specific knowledge**: The authors make excellent use of Wikipedia's unique structure (hyperlinks, redirects, categories) and Freebase information to create features highly relevant to the task. The paper's detailed analysis of feature contributions (Table 2) shows how each feature family improves performance, providing valuable insights into what makes Wikipedia text special for coreference resolution. This is a key contribution that could inform future work on adapting NLP systems to different text genres.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of the main concept resolution task**: The paper focuses on identifying mentions of the main concept (the entity being described by the article), but this is not the standard coreference resolution task. Coreference resolution aims to group all mentions of all entities, not just the main concept. While the authors acknowledge this, it's important to clarify that their work does not address the full coreference resolution problem, which is the standard task in the field. This limits the applicability of their approach to other coreference-related tasks, and the paper should more clearly distinguish between the main concept resolution task and standard CR.

2. **Small dataset size**: The WCR dataset consists of only 30 Wikipedia articles (60k tokens), which is quite limited for training a classifier, especially given the complexity of the task. The paper reports using 10-fold cross-validation, but with only 30 samples, this might not be sufficient to obtain reliable performance estimates. The authors acknowledge that the dataset is smaller than OntoNotes, but they don't adequately address how this limitation affects the generalizability of their results. A larger dataset would strengthen the paper's claims.

3. **Lack of comparison to Wikipedia-specific CR systems**: The authors compare their approach to generic CR systems (Dcoref, Scoref) and baselines, but they don't compare to systems that have been specifically designed for Wikipedia (like the ones mentioned in the related work section: Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008). It would be valuable to see how their approach compares to these dedicated Wikipedia CR systems, which might have been designed with similar features. This comparison would better demonstrate the novelty and value of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the main concept is "the entity being described by the article." However, Wikipedia articles often describe multiple entities (e.g., an article about a person might also describe their family, organizations they're part of, etc.). How did the authors handle cases where the main concept isn't clearly defined or where multiple entities are equally important? Did they have to manually resolve these ambiguities during annotation?

2. The authors claim their approach outperforms generic CR systems by 13-15 F1 points on the main concept resolution task. However, the main concept resolution task is different from the standard coreference resolution task. How would you expect these results to translate to the standard coreference resolution task, where the goal is to identify all mentions of all entities (not just the main concept)?

3. The paper mentions "a dump of all the mentions in English Wikipedia our classifier identified as referring to the main concept." How did they apply the classifier to the entire Wikipedia corpus? Did they use the same training data for this, or did they use a different training procedure? How many Wikipedia articles were processed in this dump?

## Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The WCR dataset is a valuable contribution to the field, and the binary classification approach for main concept resolution is well-motivated and effective. The paper provides useful insights about leveraging Wikipedia-specific features for coreference-related tasks. However, the limited scope of the main concept resolution task (vs. full coreference resolution) and the small dataset size are significant limitations that need to be addressed.

**Suggested Improvements:**

1. More clearly distinguish the main concept resolution task from standard coreference resolution throughout the paper, and discuss how the results might translate to the full CR task.

2. Expand the dataset size to provide more robust evaluation results. If this isn't possible, acknowledge the limitation more thoroughly and discuss how the results might generalize to larger datasets.

3. Add a comparison to Wikipedia-specific CR systems (like Nguyen et al., 2007 and Nakayama, 2008) to better demonstrate the novelty and value of their approach.

4. In the final version, provide a concrete URL for the WCR dataset and the classifier results dump to improve reproducibility.

This paper makes a useful contribution to understanding how to adapt coreference resolution to Wikipedia, and with the suggested improvements, it could make a strong contribution to the field.
