

# Review for CONLL: Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a task of identifying all mentions of the "main concept" (MC) in a Wikipedia article, where the MC is the concept being described by the article. The authors present a binary classification approach to determine if a mention refers to the MC, using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. They claim their approach outperforms state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems on this specific task by 13-15 F1 points, and they demonstrate that integrating their classifier into a rule-based coreference system improves overall coreference resolution performance.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1. A new task definition (main concept resolution in Wikipedia) and a dedicated dataset (WCR) for evaluating systems on this task
2. A simple yet effective binary classification approach for identifying MC mentions, leveraging Wikipedia's rich structure
3. A demonstration that their approach can improve existing coreference resolution systems when integrated

## Strengths

1. **Novel task definition**: The paper identifies a gap in the literature for coreference resolution specifically designed for Wikipedia. The main concept resolution task is well-motivated and represents a useful subtask for Wikipedia-based NLP applications.

2. **Careful feature engineering**: The authors demonstrate thoughtful feature selection that leverages Wikipedia's unique structure (markup, redirects, Freebase attributes) rather than relying solely on generic coreference features. The distinction between features for pronouns and non-pronominals is well-justified.

3. **Clear evaluation framework**: The paper provides a well-defined dataset (WCR) with annotations following OntoNotes guidelines, and compares their approach against multiple baselines built on top of state-of-the-art systems. The evaluation metrics are standard and appropriate.

## Weaknesses

1. **Severe methodological weakness with dataset size**: The paper uses a dataset of only 30 documents (60k tokens) for evaluation, which is extremely small for coreference resolution research. The WCR dataset contains only 1800 mentions referring to the main concept (out of 7000 non-singleton mentions). This is insufficient to draw meaningful conclusions about performance differences between systems, especially when claiming absolute F1 improvements of 13-15 points. The authors acknowledge the small size but don't adequately address how this limitation affects the validity of their results.

2. **Numerous formatting and grammatical errors**: The paper contains numerous minor but pervasive errors that undermine professionalism and readability:
   - Inconsistent citation formatting (e.g., "(e.g., (Hirshman and Chinchor, 1998))" with extra parenthesis)
   - Subject-verb agreement errors ("systems... performs")
   - Redundant phrases ("increasingly large wealth of knowledge")
   - Inconsistent hyphenation ("state-of-the-art" vs. "state of the art")
   - Formatting errors in references (e.g., "4http://conll.github.io/" with misplaced reference number)
   - Inconsistent capitalization ("Freebase" vs. "freebase")
   - Typos ("coreferent" instead of "coreferential" in some places)
   - Inconsistent number formatting ("60k" vs. "7000")

3. **Insufficient justification for the main concept resolution task**: The paper doesn't adequately explain why identifying MC mentions is more valuable than full coreference resolution for Wikipedia. The connection to practical NLP applications is weak, and the authors don't provide convincing evidence that this task is more useful than standard coreference resolution for the intended applications.

4. **Lack of ablation study for the MC representation**: The paper claims their representation of the main concept (title, inferred type, name variants, etc.) is important, but doesn't provide a proper ablation study to show how much each component contributes. The feature analysis in Table 2 only covers the pronominal classifier, not the full system.

5. **Questionable generalizability**: The paper claims their approach "outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines on this task," but the task is highly specialized to Wikipedia and main concept resolution. The paper doesn't provide evidence that this approach would generalize to standard coreference resolution tasks, which is the more common objective in the field.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states the WCR dataset contains "30 documents, comprising 60k tokens" and "7000 non singleton mentions." Could you provide more details about the document characteristics (e.g., average length, topic diversity, internal link density) to better assess whether the dataset is representative of Wikipedia as a whole?

2. The paper claims "an absolute gain of 13 F1 points over the best baseline" but doesn't provide a standard error or confidence interval for this improvement. Given the small dataset size, how confident can you be that this difference is statistically significant?

3. In Table 5, the paper reports "Dcoref++" with a 4-point improvement over Dcoref. However, Dcoref++ is not described in detail - could you clarify exactly how the classifier is integrated into Dcoref (e.g., which sieve is modified, how the classifier output is used to filter mentions)?

## Final Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the paper identifies an interesting niche task and presents a reasonable approach, the methodological weakness of using an extremely small dataset (30 documents, 60k tokens) for evaluation is too severe to overlook. The numerous formatting and grammatical errors further undermine the paper's professionalism and attention to detail. The claimed performance gains (13-15 F1 points) are likely inflated due to the small dataset size, and the paper fails to adequately address the limitations of their evaluation framework.

The paper's contribution is valuable for a specific niche task, but the methodological shortcomings and presentation issues make it unsuitable for publication at CONLL. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these concerns, including collecting a much larger dataset for evaluation, improving the writing quality, and providing a more rigorous analysis of their results.

**Overall Recommendation: Reject**
