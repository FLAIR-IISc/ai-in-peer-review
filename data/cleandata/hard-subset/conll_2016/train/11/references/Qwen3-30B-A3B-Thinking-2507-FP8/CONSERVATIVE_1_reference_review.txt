

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary
This paper proposes a new task called "main concept resolution" for Wikipedia articles, where the goal is to identify all mentions of the main concept (the entity being described in the article). The authors introduce a dataset (WCR) with 30 Wikipedia articles annotated for coreference, and propose a classifier that uses Wikipedia-specific features to identify mentions of the main concept. They claim their approach outperforms baselines by 13-15 F1 points on this specific binary classification task, and integrating it into Dcoref improves coreference resolution by 4 F1 points.

## Main Contributions
1. A new dataset (WCR) for coreference resolution on Wikipedia articles (a significant contribution)
2. A clear demonstration that standard CR systems perform poorly on Wikipedia (a useful contribution)
3. A binary classification approach for identifying mentions of the main concept in Wikipedia articles (a minor contribution)

## Strengths

1. **Identification of a genuine problem**: The paper correctly identifies that standard CR systems trained on news data perform poorly on Wikipedia, providing strong evidence through their experiments showing a significant drop in performance (e.g., Scoref's F1 drops from 63.61 to 46.39 on WCR). This is a valuable contribution to the community, as it highlights an important gap in current NLP resources.

2. **WCR dataset creation**: The authors have created a new dataset specifically for Wikipedia coreference, which fills a notable gap in the field. The dataset is annotated following OntoNotes guidelines, making it comparable to standard CR resources. This dataset is likely to be valuable for future research on adapting CR to Wikipedia and other non-news genres.

3. **Clear comparison of existing systems**: The paper provides a thorough comparison of multiple state-of-the-art CR systems on the WCR dataset, demonstrating that performance drops dramatically across the board. This provides valuable insights for the community about the limitations of current CR systems on Wikipedia text.

## Weaknesses

1. **Narrow task definition**: The paper's main contribution is a binary classification task (is this mention the main concept?), not a full coreference resolution system. This is a very specific subtask that doesn't generalize to the broader coreference resolution problem. The authors don't convincingly argue why this narrow task is more important or meaningful than full coreference resolution, which is the standard CR task. The paper's title and claims suggest they're addressing coreference resolution broadly, but their actual contribution is much narrower.

2. **Insufficient dataset size**: The WCR dataset is extremely small (30 documents, 60k tokens) compared to standard CR datasets like OntoNotes (which has over 1 million tokens). This makes it difficult to draw statistically significant conclusions about the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors acknowledge this but don't provide justification for why such a small dataset is sufficient for their claims.

3. **Inadequate baseline comparison**: The paper compares to their own baselines (B1-B4) and two CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref), but fails to compare to relevant methods specifically designed for Wikipedia. They cite Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008) as previous work, but don't compare to their approaches. This is a major omission, as these papers were specifically designed for Wikipedia coreference.

4. **Misleading performance claims**: The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines" by 13-15 F1 points, but this comparison is made on a binary classification task (main concept resolution), not on the full coreference resolution task. The actual CR performance improvement (4 F1 points) is modest and not convincingly linked to the binary classification task's performance.

5. **Lack of generalizability analysis**: The authors provide no analysis of how their approach performs across different types of Wikipedia articles (e.g., articles about people vs. places vs. organizations). With only 30 articles in the dataset, it's impossible to determine if their method works equally well across different Wikipedia article types.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to "outperform state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines," but your evaluation is on a binary classification task (main concept resolution), not full coreference resolution. How do you justify this claim, and what is the relationship between your binary classification task and the full coreference resolution task?

2. The WCR dataset is extremely small (30 articles). How did you ensure that this small dataset is representative of Wikipedia's diversity? What criteria did you use to select the 30 articles?

3. You cite Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008) as previous work on Wikipedia coreference, but don't compare to their methods. Why not? How do your results compare to these earlier approaches designed specifically for Wikipedia?

4. The paper mentions that "the integration of our classifier into the state-of-the-art rule-based coreference system of Lee et al. (2013) improves the detection of coreference chains in Wikipedia articles." How much of this improvement is due to the classifier itself versus the fact that you're using the same mention detection pipeline as Lee et al.?

5. The paper states that "the main concept" is "the concept being described by this article." How do you define and identify the main concept for articles where multiple concepts are equally prominent (e.g., articles about organizations with multiple important members)?

## Overall Assessment

This paper identifies a genuine problem (CR systems trained on news data perform poorly on Wikipedia) and creates a useful dataset to study it. However, the contribution is limited to a narrow binary classification task that doesn't advance the broader coreference resolution field. The small dataset size, inadequate baseline comparison, and misleading performance claims significantly undermine the paper's contribution. While the WCR dataset is valuable, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support its main claims about the effectiveness of their approach.

I recommend rejection as the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support its claims about outperforming state-of-the-art CR systems, and the task it addresses is too narrow to be considered a meaningful contribution to the coreference resolution field. The authors would need to substantially expand their evaluation to a full coreference resolution task, provide a much larger dataset, and compare to relevant baselines before this work could be considered for publication.
