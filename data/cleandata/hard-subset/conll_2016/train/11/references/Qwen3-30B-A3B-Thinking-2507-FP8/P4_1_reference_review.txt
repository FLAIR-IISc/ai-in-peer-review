

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper identifies a significant gap in coreference resolution (CR) research: current systems are primarily trained on news-like texts (e.g., ACE, OntoNotes) and perform poorly on Wikipedia articles. The authors propose a new task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), defined as identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) in a Wikipedia article (e.g., all mentions of "Chilly Gonzales" in the corresponding article). The authors create a new dataset (WCR3) with 30 Wikipedia articles annotated according to OntoNotes guidelines, containing 7,000 non-singleton mentions, 1,800 of which refer to the main concept. They propose a binary classification approach for MCR that leverages Wikipedia-specific features (markup, Freebase attributes) and outperforms baselines built on state-of-the-art CR systems.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. Identification of a specific problem in coreference resolution (systems trained on news texts underperform on Wikipedia) and creation of a new task (Main Concept Resolution) with a well-defined evaluation framework.
2. Creation of a new dataset (WCR3) specifically for evaluating systems on Wikipedia texts, which is a valuable resource for future research.
3. A simple yet effective binary classification approach for MCR that achieves 89.29% F1, representing an absolute gain of 13-15 F1 points over the best baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant research gap**: The paper effectively demonstrates that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on news data perform dramatically worse on Wikipedia texts (e.g., Scoref's F1 drops from 63.61 on OntoNotes to 46.39 on WCR). The authors provide strong empirical evidence for this gap, which is a well-motivated and important problem for the NLP community, given Wikipedia's widespread use in NLP applications.

2. **Creation of a dedicated evaluation dataset**: The WCR3 dataset is a significant contribution. It is annotated according to OntoNotes guidelines, contains 30 diverse Wikipedia articles (covering various topics, sizes, and link densities), and provides a standardized benchmark for evaluating systems on Wikipedia texts. This dataset fills a clear need in the field and enables future research on adapting CR to Wikipedia.

3. **Strong empirical results with clear ablation studies**: The authors demonstrate a substantial improvement over baselines (13-15 absolute F1 points), with detailed ablation studies showing how each feature family contributes to performance. The results are consistent across precision, recall, and F1 metrics, and the authors provide concrete examples of successful and failed cases to illustrate the model's behavior. The integration of their classifier into Dcoref (creating Dcoref++) also shows a 4-point improvement in CoNLL F1 on the full coreference resolution task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the approach**: While the MCR task is novel, the classification approach itself is relatively simple and builds directly on existing feature engineering techniques for CR (e.g., using Wikipedia and Freebase attributes). The paper does not clearly distinguish their approach from previous work that has used similar external knowledge sources (e.g., Ratinov and Roth 2012, Hajishirzi et al. 2013). The core technical contribution is modest, as it's essentially applying standard feature engineering to a new task rather than proposing a novel algorithmic approach.

2. **Small dataset size for machine learning**: The WCR3 dataset is quite small (30 articles, 60k tokens), which limits the robustness of the machine learning approach. The authors acknowledge this but don't adequately address how this small size might affect the generalizability of their results or the reliability of their model. The dataset is significantly smaller than standard CR datasets (e.g., OntoNotes has approximately 1.5M tokens), making it difficult to draw strong conclusions about the approach's effectiveness.

3. **Lack of comparison to task-specific approaches**: The paper compares their approach to baselines built on general CR systems, but doesn't compare to other methods specifically designed for the MCR task (e.g., Nguyen et al. 2007 and Nakayama 2008, which are cited as related work but not directly compared). This weakens the novelty claim, as it's unclear whether their approach is genuinely better than existing task-specific solutions.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors note that Wikipedia articles have a highly structured format with internal links and redirects. How would their approach scale to handle the full Wikipedia corpus (with millions of articles), and what challenges might arise with this scale?

2. The paper demonstrates that their MCR approach outperforms general CR systems on Wikipedia, but the CR systems are not fine-tuned on Wikipedia data. How would the performance of their approach compare if the CR systems were first fine-tuned on Wikipedia data?

3. The authors mention that the main concept is often referred to by its type (e.g., "the team" for "Houston Texans"). How would their approach handle cases where the main concept has multiple types (e.g., a person who is both an actor and a director), and how would this affect the performance?
