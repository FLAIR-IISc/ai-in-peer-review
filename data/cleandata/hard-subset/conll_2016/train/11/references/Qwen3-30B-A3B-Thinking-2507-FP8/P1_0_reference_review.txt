

# Peer Review: Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper addresses coreference resolution (CR) specifically for Wikipedia articles, a text genre that existing CR systems perform poorly on. The authors propose a new task called "main concept resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions in a Wikipedia article that refer to the main concept (MC) being described by the article. The authors frame MCR as a binary classification problem (does a mention refer to the MC?) and develop a classifier that leverages features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1. A dedicated dataset (WCR) for Wikipedia coreference resolution, which is valuable for future research in this specific domain.
2. A novel approach to main concept resolution that outperforms baselines built on top of state-of-the-art CR systems by 13-15 F1 points.
3. Demonstration that integrating their classifier into a rule-based CR system (Lee et al., 2013) improves coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia by 4 F1 points.

Note: I don't consider the "revisiting" of the task as a primary contribution since the authors acknowledge previous work on this specific task (Nakayama, 2008), and their main contribution is the testbed, classifier, and evaluation.

## Strengths

1. **Identification of a significant gap in the literature**: The paper clearly establishes that existing CR systems trained on news text perform poorly on Wikipedia, which is a widely used resource in NLP. The authors provide compelling evidence through experiments showing that state-of-the-art systems (Dcoref, Scoref, etc.) see dramatic performance drops (15-17 F1 points) on their WCR dataset compared to OntoNotes. This is a substantial contribution to the field as it highlights a real-world limitation of current CR systems.

2. **Well-motivated use of Wikipedia's unique structure**: The authors cleverly leverage Wikipedia's inherent structure (links, redirects, Freebase connections) to extract features without relying on error-prone named-entity linking pipelines. This is a significant strength as it directly addresses the problem of "cascading errors" mentioned in the related work. By using human-made links as a substitute for NER linking, they achieve higher quality features, which is a smart adaptation to the specific text genre.

3. **Clear evaluation framework**: The paper provides a well-defined task (main concept resolution) with a dedicated dataset (WCR) and a clear evaluation metric (F1 score on identifying MC mentions). The comparison against multiple baselines (B1-B4) built on top of state-of-the-art systems is thorough, and the feature ablation study (Table 2) provides valuable insights into what contributes most to the model's performance. The demonstration of improved coreference resolution when integrating their classifier into Dcoref (Table 5) is a strong practical contribution.

4. **Practical utility and reproducibility**: The authors plan to share their dataset and classifier results, which is highly valuable for the community. They've also made a clear contribution to the field by providing a resource that can be used for future research on Wikipedia-specific CR, which is currently underexplored.

## Weaknesses

1. **Small and potentially unrepresentative dataset**: The WCR dataset consists of only 30 articles (60k tokens, 7000 non-singleton mentions), which is significantly smaller than standard CR datasets like OntoNotes (over 1 million tokens). While the authors claim they carefully selected articles of various characteristics, the small size limits the statistical significance of their results and makes it difficult to generalize their findings. The paper doesn't provide a thorough analysis of the dataset's representativeness across Wikipedia's diverse topics and structures.

2. **Narrow task definition without clear justification**: The paper focuses on "main concept resolution" (identifying mentions of the MC) rather than full coreference resolution. While the authors show that their approach improves full CR when integrated into Dcoref, they don't clearly establish why MCR is the right way to approach Wikipedia CR. Why not directly adapt existing CR systems to Wikipedia? The paper doesn't provide a convincing argument for why MCR is superior to other approaches to Wikipedia CR, which weakens the broader impact of their contribution.

3. **Lack of comparison with Wikipedia-specific CR systems**: The paper compares against baselines built on generic CR systems, but doesn't compare against any existing Wikipedia-specific CR approaches. For example, Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008) developed rule-based systems specifically for Wikipedia. The paper claims these didn't develop a "fully-fledged CR designed for Wikipedia," but doesn't show how their approach compares to these prior efforts. This omission makes it difficult to assess the true novelty of their contribution.

4. **Limited analysis of error patterns**: The paper identifies some error examples (Figure 3) but doesn't provide a comprehensive analysis of the types of errors made by their system or baselines. Understanding the specific failure modes (e.g., when the classifier confuses different entities with similar names) would be valuable for future work and would strengthen the evaluation.

5. **Scalability concerns**: The paper doesn't address how their approach would scale to the entire Wikipedia corpus (over 6 million articles in English). Their method relies on Freebase and Wikipedia's markup structure, but Freebase has been discontinued and Wikipedia's structure may change over time. The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of their approach or how it would work on the full Wikipedia dataset.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the WCR dataset is "far smaller than the OntoNotes one," but doesn't provide justification for why such a small dataset is sufficient for their claims. Could you elaborate on how you determined the dataset size was adequate for evaluating your approach?

2. The paper claims that "the integration of our classifier into the state-of-the-art rule-based coreference system of Lee et al. (2013) improves the detection of coreference chains in Wikipedia articles." However, your evaluation (Table 5) shows that Dcoref++ only improves the CoNLL F1 score by 4 points (from 51.77 to 55.11). Given that the baseline Dcoref was already performing better than statistical systems on Wikipedia, could you explain why this improvement is significant for the broader field?

3. The paper states that "our approach outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines on this task." However, your task (main concept resolution) is not the same as standard coreference resolution. How do you reconcile the fact that your system was evaluated on a different task than standard CR, yet you claim to outperform CR systems?

4. The paper mentions that "we share all the datasets we used in this study" but doesn't provide a clear link or description of where these will be available. Could you clarify the availability of the dataset and classifier results for reproducibility purposes?
