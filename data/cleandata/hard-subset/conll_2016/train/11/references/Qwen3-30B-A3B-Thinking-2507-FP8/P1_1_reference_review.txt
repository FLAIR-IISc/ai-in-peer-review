

# Peer Review: Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution

## Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the problem of coreference resolution (CR) in Wikipedia articles, focusing specifically on identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) described in a Wikipedia article. The authors note that most existing CR systems are trained on news-like texts (from datasets like ACE or OntoNotes) and perform poorly on Wikipedia. They propose a binary classification task to determine whether a mention refers to the main concept, leveraging Wikipedia's unique markup structure and Freebase information. The authors create a new dataset called WCR (30 Wikipedia articles, 60k tokens) annotated with coreference information. They develop a two-class classifier (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using features extracted from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. Their approach outperforms state-of-the-art CR systems by 13-15 F1 points on the main concept resolution task, and integrating their classifier into the rule-based CR system Dcoref improves full coreference resolution performance by 4 F1 points.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **WCR dataset**: A new, carefully curated dataset specifically for evaluating coreference resolution in Wikipedia articles, which fills a critical gap in the literature.
2. **Main concept resolution task**: A well-defined, practically useful task that focuses on identifying mentions of the main concept in Wikipedia articles, distinct from full coreference resolution.
3. **Feature engineering approach**: A novel set of features leveraging Wikipedia's unique structure (links, redirects, Freebase) to create a highly effective classifier for this specific task.

## Strengths

1. **Significant dataset contribution**: The creation of WCR is a major contribution, as it's the first dedicated dataset for evaluating coreference resolution on Wikipedia. The authors demonstrate that standard CR systems perform poorly on Wikipedia (with F1 scores dropping from ~63 on OntoNotes to ~50 on WCR), highlighting a genuine need for this resource. The dataset is carefully constructed with articles of varying characteristics (size, topic, internal link density), and the authors provide detailed information about their annotation process.

2. **Well-motivated task definition**: The main concept resolution task is clearly justified and practically relevant for many NLP applications (e.g., information extraction from Wikipedia). The paper convincingly argues that identifying MC mentions is a prerequisite for many NLP tasks, and the authors provide strong evidence that standard CR systems fail at this specific task.

3. **Effective feature engineering**: The authors demonstrate excellent feature engineering by leveraging Wikipedia's unique structure. They use Wikipedia links and Freebase to extract attributes without relying on named-entity linking, which is a clever solution to the ambiguity problem in other approaches. The ablation studies (Table 2) show that each feature family contributes meaningfully to performance, with the final model achieving 89% F1 on the main concept resolution task.

4. **Strong empirical results**: The results are impressive and well-documented. The authors show a 13-15 F1 point absolute gain over the best baseline on the main concept resolution task, and a 4-point improvement in CoNLL F1 when their classifier is integrated into Dcoref. These results are statistically significant and well-supported by the data.

5. **Practical utility**: The paper demonstrates clear utility for the approach. The authors show how their classifier can improve existing CR systems, which is valuable for practitioners. They also provide all datasets and code for reproducibility, which is a significant contribution to the community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Extremely small dataset size**: The WCR dataset is only 30 articles (60k tokens), which is too small to draw robust conclusions about performance across Wikipedia. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address how they've mitigated it. For comparison, the OntoNotes dataset used for training and testing in the paper has thousands of articles. The small size makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the results and raises questions about the statistical significance of the reported performance gains.

2. **Lack of comparison to previous Wikipedia-specific work**: The authors mention two previous works on Wikipedia coreference (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008), but they don't compare their approach to these systems. Instead, they build baselines on top of generic CR systems. This is a significant weakness because it makes it difficult to assess how much improvement their approach represents compared to previous Wikipedia-specific work. The paper states that these previous works "did not spend much efforts in designing a fully-fledged CR designed for Wikipedia," but it's unclear whether their approach would outperform these existing Wikipedia-specific systems.

3. **Questionable evaluation metric**: The primary evaluation metric is F1 for identifying mentions of the main concept, which is a binary classification task. While this is a useful task, it's not the standard coreference resolution metric. The authors do show that their approach improves full coreference resolution metrics in Table 5, but the primary contribution is framed as a binary classification task, which might mislead readers about the scope of the contribution.

4. **Limited analysis of why Wikipedia is different**: The paper states that CR systems trained on news perform poorly on Wikipedia, but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why this is the case. A deeper analysis of the linguistic and structural differences between news text and Wikipedia text would strengthen the motivation for their work. The paper mentions Wikipedia's "highly structured, multi-domain" nature but doesn't elaborate on how this specifically affects coreference.

5. **Insufficient discussion of failure cases**: The paper mentions some failure cases (e.g., "the classifier associates the mention it with the MC instead of the Johnston Atoll 'Safeguard C' mission"), but doesn't provide a comprehensive analysis of the types of errors made by their system. Understanding the limitations of the approach would be valuable for future work.

## Questions to Authors

1. The WCR dataset is very small (30 articles). Have you considered creating a larger dataset, perhaps by automatically annotating more Wikipedia articles using the same guidelines? How would you address the scalability of the annotation process, given that manual annotation is expensive?

2. The authors mention two previous works on Wikipedia coreference (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008), but don't compare their approach to these systems. Could you explain why you didn't include these as baselines, and how your approach differs from theirs in terms of methodology and results?

3. The paper focuses on identifying mentions of the main concept, but the ultimate goal for many NLP tasks is full coreference resolution. How do you see your approach fitting into a complete coreference resolution pipeline, beyond just identifying the main concept mentions?

4. The authors state that "the rule-based system of (Lee et al., 2013) works better than the machine-learning based systems on the WCR dataset." This is interesting but not fully explained. Could you provide more analysis on why rule-based systems might perform better on Wikipedia, and whether this suggests a different approach to CR for Wikipedia?

5. The paper mentions that "the integration of our classifier into the state-of-the-art rule-based coreference system of Lee et al. (2013) improves the detection of coreference chains." How does this improvement compare to other approaches for adapting CR systems to Wikipedia?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a valuable contribution to the field. The authors have addressed an important gap in coreference resolution research by focusing on Wikipedia, a widely used resource that has been largely overlooked in CR research. The dataset creation and feature engineering are particularly strong aspects of the work. However, the small dataset size and lack of comparison to previous Wikipedia-specific work are significant limitations that need to be addressed.

I recommend this paper for acceptance with minor revisions, primarily to address the dataset size limitation and to better contextualize the work within previous Wikipedia-specific approaches. The authors should also clarify the relationship between their main concept resolution task and full coreference resolution, as this is a key point of confusion in the paper.
