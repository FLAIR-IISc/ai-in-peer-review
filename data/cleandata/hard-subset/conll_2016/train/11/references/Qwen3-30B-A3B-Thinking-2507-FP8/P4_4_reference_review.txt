

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) in a Wikipedia article, which refers to the central entity being described by the article. The authors argue that existing coreference resolution (CR) systems, trained primarily on newswire data, perform poorly on Wikipedia text. They propose a binary classification approach to determine whether a mention refers to the main concept, leveraging Wikipedia's unique markup structure (hyperlinks, redirects) and Freebase attributes to extract features. The authors create a dedicated dataset (WCR) for this task and evaluate their approach against several baselines built on top of state-of-the-art CR systems. They demonstrate that their method outperforms the baselines by significant margins (13-15 absolute F1 points) and show that integrating their classifier into the Dcoref system improves overall coreference resolution performance by 4 F1 points.

## Main Contributions (as assessed by the reviewer)
1. A novel binary classification approach for identifying mentions of the main concept in Wikipedia articles, which outperforms existing baselines by significant margins (13-15 absolute F1 points).
2. A dedicated dataset (WCR) for evaluating main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles, which fills a gap in the literature for this specific text genre.
3. A practical integration of their classifier into an existing coreference resolution system (Dcoref) that improves overall coreference resolution performance.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant research gap**: The paper effectively demonstrates that current CR systems trained on newswire data perform poorly on Wikipedia text, citing relevant literature (Hendrickx and Hoste, 2009; Schäfer et al., 2012) to establish the problem's importance. This is a strong motivation for the work, as Wikipedia is a widely used resource in NLP that has been largely overlooked for CR adaptation.

2. **Well-constructed dataset (WCR)**: The creation of a dedicated dataset for the main concept resolution task is a substantial contribution. The dataset contains 30 Wikipedia articles annotated with OntoNotes guidelines, with 7000 non-singleton mentions (1800 referring to the main concept). The authors provide sufficient detail about the annotation process and dataset characteristics, making it a valuable resource for future work.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates clear improvements over baselines (13-15 absolute F1 point gains) with a well-designed experimental setup. The ablation studies (Tables 2 and 3) provide valuable insights into the contribution of each feature family, showing how the authors incrementally improved performance through feature engineering.

4. **Practical application**: The integration of the classifier into the Dcoref system (Dcoref++) to improve overall coreference resolution performance by 4 F1 points demonstrates real-world utility of the approach. This shows that the method isn't just useful for the specific main concept resolution task but can enhance broader CR systems.

5. **Clear technical presentation**: The methodology is well-described with sufficient detail to be reproducible. The distinction between pronominal and non-pronominal mention features, the feature families, and the classifier design are all clearly explained, with appropriate references to related work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Small dataset size**: The dataset consists of only 30 Wikipedia articles (60k tokens), which is quite limited for a study claiming to address a significant NLP task. While the authors acknowledge this and state they sampled articles with diverse characteristics, a dataset this small raises concerns about the generalizability of the results and the robustness of the reported performance gains.

2. **Insufficient analysis of why rule-based systems outperform statistical systems**: The authors note that the rule-based Dcoref system outperforms statistical systems (Durrett and Klein, Martschat and Strube, Clark and Manning) on the WCR dataset, which they describe as "surprising." However, they provide no detailed analysis of this phenomenon, which would be valuable for understanding the limitations of current CR systems on Wikipedia text.

3. **Limited novelty of the core approach**: While the application to Wikipedia is novel, the classification approach itself (using SVM with features derived from Wikipedia structure) is not particularly innovative in the broader NLP literature. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from similar work that has been done in other domains, such as using external knowledge resources for coreference resolution.

4. **Insufficient detail on dataset construction**: The paper lacks details about the article selection criteria, annotation verification process, and how the 30 articles were chosen to represent Wikipedia's diversity. Without this information, it's difficult to assess the quality and representativeness of the dataset.

5. **Lack of discussion on task limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the main concept resolution task itself. For example, how generalizable is this task to Wikipedia articles that describe multiple related concepts or where the main concept is ambiguous? The authors don't discuss potential challenges in applying this approach to articles where the main concept isn't clearly defined.

## Questions to Authors

1. The dataset size (30 articles) is quite small for the claims made about the performance gains (13-15 F1 points). Could you provide more details about the data selection process, including how the articles were chosen to ensure diversity in topic, size, and internal link density, and how you assessed the generalizability of your results given this limited dataset?

2. You note that the rule-based Dcoref system outperforms statistical CR systems on the WCR dataset, which you describe as "surprising." Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, and how this finding relates to the broader challenge of adapting CR systems to different text genres?

3. The paper mentions that "no system readily available for our task" existed. However, the authors cite Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008), which both address similar tasks. Could you clarify how your work differs from these earlier studies, particularly in terms of methodology and evaluation?

4. The paper states that the classifier sometimes makes errors due to "noisy redirects in Wikipedia" (e.g., Johnston and Sand Islands being a redirect of Johnston Atoll). How did you handle such cases during dataset annotation, and what impact did these errors have on the overall performance metrics?

5. The paper claims "our approach outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines on this task" with "13 points over Dcoref, and 15 points over Scoref." However, the baselines in Table 4 show that B4 achieves 84.17% F1 for pronominal mentions, while your approach achieves 88.99% F1. Could you clarify the exact comparison being made (e.g., is the 13-point gain relative to the best baseline or the second-best baseline)?

## Overall Assessment
The paper identifies a meaningful research gap and provides a well-structured solution with clear empirical results. The creation of the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution to the community, and the practical integration of the classifier into Dcoref demonstrates real utility. However, the small dataset size and limited analysis of the surprising performance of rule-based systems on Wikipedia text are significant concerns. The novelty of the core approach is somewhat limited, as the classification method itself isn't particularly innovative, though its application to Wikipedia is novel.

The paper makes a solid contribution to the adaptation of coreference resolution to Wikipedia, but the limited dataset size and lack of deeper analysis of the rule-based vs. statistical performance discrepancy prevent it from being a highly impactful contribution. With additional data and more thorough analysis of the surprising results, this work could be significantly strengthened.
