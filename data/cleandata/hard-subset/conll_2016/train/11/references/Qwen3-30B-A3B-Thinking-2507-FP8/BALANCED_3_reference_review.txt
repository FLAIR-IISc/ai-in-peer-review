

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of identifying all mentions of the "main concept" (MC) in Wikipedia articles - that is, all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the concept being described by the article. The authors argue that existing coreference resolution (CR) systems, trained primarily on news-like texts, perform poorly on Wikipedia. They propose a binary classification approach to identify mentions that refer to the MC, using features extracted from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. They evaluate their approach against several baselines built on top of state-of-the-art CR systems, and demonstrate that their approach outperforms these baselines. They also integrate their classifier into a rule-based CR system (Dcoref) to improve overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia.

## Main Contributions
1. **A new dataset (WCR3) for main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles:** The authors created a dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles annotated with main concept mentions following OntoNotes guidelines, specifically designed for this task.
2. **A binary classification approach for main concept resolution:** The authors propose a method that frames the main concept resolution task as a binary classification problem, using carefully designed features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, which outperforms baselines by 13-15 F1 points.
3. **Integration of main concept resolution into coreference resolution:** The authors show that incorporating their classifier into a state-of-the-art CR system (Dcoref) improves overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia, increasing the CoNLL F1 score by 4 points.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a meaningful gap in the literature:** The paper effectively identifies a significant limitation of current coreference resolution systems - their poor performance on Wikipedia, which is a widely used resource in NLP. The authors provide empirical evidence through experiments showing that state-of-the-art CR systems have dramatically lower performance on their WCR dataset compared to standard benchmarks like OntoNotes. This is an important contribution as it highlights a real-world limitation of current CR systems that has not been adequately addressed.

2. **Well-designed dataset (WCR3) and careful evaluation:** The authors created a new dataset specifically for main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles, annotated following OntoNotes guidelines. They carefully sampled articles from different domains, sizes, and with varying link densities to ensure representativeness. The evaluation is thorough, comparing their approach against multiple baselines and demonstrating the practical utility of their method by integrating it into a state-of-the-art CR system.

3. **Clear and compelling results:** The paper demonstrates a substantial performance improvement (13-15 F1 points) over the best baselines. The results are clearly presented and supported by detailed analysis of feature importance. The integration of their classifier into Dcoref (creating Dcoref++) shows a meaningful improvement (4 F1 points) on a full coreference resolution task, demonstrating practical utility beyond the main concept resolution task.

4. **Reproducibility and practical contribution:** The authors make their dataset, code, and classifier results publicly available, which is a significant contribution to the research community. This will enable other researchers to build upon their work, replicate their results, and develop new approaches for Wikipedia coreference resolution.

5. **Insightful feature analysis:** The paper provides a detailed analysis of which features contribute most to the performance of their classifier, particularly for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. This analysis helps explain why their approach works well and provides valuable insights for future work on Wikipedia-specific coreference resolution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of the main concept resolution task:** The paper focuses specifically on identifying mentions of the main concept (the concept being described by the article), rather than full coreference resolution. This is a narrower task than standard coreference resolution, which aims to group all mentions of all entities. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how this task relates to the broader coreference resolution problem or how it could be integrated into a full CR system beyond the simple integration they demonstrated with Dcoref.

2. **Lack of comparison to specialized Wikipedia coreference systems:** The authors claim that "we are not aware of much work conducted to adapt CR to this text genre" and cite two previous works (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008), but these papers focus on information extraction, not coreference resolution. They don't compare their approach to more recent specialized Wikipedia CR systems, which is a significant omission. For example, the work by Medelyan et al. (2009) on Wikipedia-based entity linking or Ferragina and Scaiella (2010) on Wikipedia-based NER might be relevant.

3. **Small dataset size:** The WCR3 dataset consists of only 30 articles (60k tokens), which is quite small compared to standard coreference datasets like OntoNotes (1.3M tokens) or ACE (1.1M tokens). This limits the statistical significance of their results and makes it difficult to draw broad conclusions about the generalizability of their approach. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide sufficient justification for the small dataset size.

4. **Limited analysis of why Wikipedia coreference is harder:** While the paper shows that CR systems perform worse on Wikipedia, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why this is the case. Understanding the specific challenges of Wikipedia coreference (e.g., different writing style, structure, or entity representation) would strengthen the paper and provide more concrete guidance for future work.

5. **Unclear relationship to standard coreference resolution:** The paper doesn't clearly explain how the main concept resolution task relates to standard coreference resolution. In standard CR, the goal is to group all mentions of all entities, not just the main concept. The paper doesn't discuss how their approach could be extended to full CR or how it would interact with other entities in the document.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the main concept resolution task relate to the standard coreference resolution task? Specifically, how could your approach be extended to identify mentions of all entities in a Wikipedia article, not just the main concept?

2. You claim there's "not much work conducted to adapt CR to this text genre" and cite Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008). However, those papers focus on information extraction, not coreference resolution. Have you considered comparing your approach to more recent specialized Wikipedia CR systems, such as those described in Medelyan et al. (2009) or Ferragina and Scaiella (2010)?

3. The WCR3 dataset is quite small (30 articles, 60k tokens). How do you address concerns about the statistical significance of your results given this limited dataset size? Could you provide more details about the diversity of the 30 articles you sampled?

4. You mention that "the integration of our classifier into the state-of-the-art rule-based coreference system of Lee et al. (2013) improves the detection of coreference chains in Wikipedia articles." Can you elaborate on how this integration works beyond the simple sieve approach described in Section 6.4? How would this approach scale to other CR systems?

5. The paper focuses on Wikipedia, but how would you adapt your approach for other text genres, such as news articles or social media posts? What would be the main challenges in doing so?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection of this paper. While the paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature and presents a well-designed approach for main concept resolution in Wikipedia, the limitations in scope, lack of comparison to relevant prior work, and small dataset size significantly weaken the contribution. The main concept resolution task is too narrow to be considered a significant contribution to the coreference resolution field, and the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how this task relates to or advances the broader coreference resolution problem.

However, the authors have identified a genuine problem (poor performance of standard CR systems on Wikipedia) and made a valuable dataset available. With a more comprehensive comparison to relevant prior work, a larger dataset, and a clearer explanation of how their approach relates to standard coreference resolution, this work could be significantly strengthened. I encourage the authors to address these issues in a future submission.
