

# Peer Review: "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant gap in coreference resolution (CR) research: the poor performance of existing CR systems on Wikipedia articles, which are widely used in NLP but not well-suited to systems trained primarily on news data. The authors focus on a specific subtask—identifying all mentions of the "main concept" (MC) described in a Wikipedia article (e.g., all mentions of "Chilly Gonzales" in an article about him). They propose a binary classification approach to determine if a mention refers to the MC, leveraging features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. The authors create a new dataset (WCR) with 30 Wikipedia articles annotated for coreference and demonstrate that their approach outperforms baselines built on top of state-of-the-art CR systems by 13-15 F1 points. They further integrate their classifier into the Dcoref system (creating Dcoref++), improving overall coreference resolution performance by 4 F1 points.

## Main Contributions
1. Creation of WCR, the first dataset specifically designed for evaluating coreference resolution on Wikipedia articles.
2. Demonstration that existing CR systems trained on news data perform poorly on Wikipedia (with F1 drops of 10-15 points).
3. Development of a simple yet effective binary classification approach for identifying main concept mentions in Wikipedia, outperforming baselines by a significant margin.

## Strengths
1. **Identification of a meaningful gap**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that current CR systems perform poorly on Wikipedia (Table 1), which is a critical resource for many NLP applications. This is a significant contribution as it highlights a practical limitation of existing technology that has been overlooked in the literature.

2. **Valuable dataset creation**: WCR fills a critical resource gap in the field. While small (30 articles), it's the first dataset specifically designed for Wikipedia coreference resolution, with careful sampling across article sizes, topics, and link densities. This resource will be valuable for future research in the area.

3. **Simple yet effective approach**: The authors successfully leverage Wikipedia's unique structure (markup, links, Freebase) without needing complex feature engineering. Their binary classification approach is elegant in its simplicity while achieving significant improvements (13-15 F1 points) over baselines. The integration into Dcoref (Dcoref++) demonstrates practical utility, with a 4-point F1 improvement on the CoNLL metric.

4. **Clear practical application**: The paper demonstrates how their approach can improve existing NLP pipelines that rely on Wikipedia, showing the real-world value of their work. This is particularly relevant for information extraction and knowledge base construction tasks where Wikipedia is commonly used.

## Weaknesses
1. **Insufficient dataset size**: The WCR dataset (30 articles, ~60k tokens) is too small to draw strong generalizable conclusions. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the small size makes it difficult to assess whether their results would hold across the entire Wikipedia corpus. The paper states they sampled for diversity, but with only 30 articles, this is questionable.

2. **Limited baseline comparison**: The paper focuses on only two baseline systems (Dcoref and Scoref), despite mentioning several other state-of-the-art systems. The authors justify this by claiming these two use the same pipeline but different techniques, but this doesn't fully address the concern that their results might be biased toward these particular systems.

3. **Lack of feature engineering details**: The paper describes the features but doesn't explain how they were selected or why other potential features were discarded. This makes it difficult to assess whether their feature set is optimal or if they might have missed better features. The "greedy" feature addition process (Table 2) is not sufficiently explained.

4. **Misleading claims about outperforming state-of-the-art**: The paper claims their approach "outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines," but the comparison is made to baselines built on top of these systems, not to the systems themselves. This is misleading as the full CR systems would likely perform differently.

5. **No discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't address key limitations, such as how the approach would handle Wikipedia articles with few links or redirects, or how it would perform on non-English Wikipedia. The mention of "a natural extension" to identify all coreference relations lacks concrete details.

## Questions to Authors
1. Given the small size of WCR (30 articles), how confident are you that your results generalize to all Wikipedia articles? Have you considered expanding the dataset to strengthen your claims?

2. Why did you choose to focus on only two baseline systems (Dcoref and Scoref) when several other state-of-the-art systems are mentioned in the paper? Would you expect similar results with other systems?

3. In Table 5, Dcoref++ shows a 3.34-point improvement in CoNLL F1 (55.11 - 51.77 = 3.34), but the paper states "4 absolute points." Can you clarify this discrepancy?

4. Could you elaborate on your plans for extending this work to identify all coreference relations in Wikipedia articles? How would this differ from your current approach?

## Recommendation
The paper makes a valuable contribution by highlighting a significant gap in coreference resolution research and providing a first step toward addressing it with a new dataset and simple, effective approach. While the small dataset size and limited baseline comparison are notable weaknesses, the practical significance of the work and the quality of the WCR dataset make it a strong candidate for publication with minor revisions. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above.
