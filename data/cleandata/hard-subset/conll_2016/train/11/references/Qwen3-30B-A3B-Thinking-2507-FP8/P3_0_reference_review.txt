

# Peer Review: "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a significant gap in coreference resolution (CR) research: the poor performance of existing CR systems on Wikipedia text. The authors define a new task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), where the goal is to identify all mentions of the main concept (MC) described in a Wikipedia article (e.g., all mentions of "Chilly Gonzales" in the article about Chilly Gonzales). They frame MCR as a binary classification problem and propose a classifier that leverages Wikipedia's rich markup (links, redirects, categories) and Freebase information to distinguish MC mentions from other mentions. The authors create a new dataset called WCR (30 English Wikipedia articles, ~60k tokens, annotated with OntoNotes guidelines) and demonstrate that their approach outperforms baselines by 13-15 F1 points on the MCR task. They further integrate their classifier into the rule-based CR system Dcoref, creating Dcoref++, which improves overall CR performance on Wikipedia by 4 F1 points.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **The Main Concept Resolution task and dataset**: The authors clearly define a new task that addresses a specific need in Wikipedia processing, and create a new evaluation dataset (WCR) specifically for this task. This is significant because it provides a well-defined framework for evaluating CR systems on Wikipedia text.

2. **Wikipedia-specific feature engineering**: The authors' approach of leveraging Wikipedia's rich markup (links, redirects, categories) to build features for MC identification is well-motivated and effective, avoiding the cascading errors common in NEL-based approaches.

3. **Demonstrated improvement of CR systems for Wikipedia**: The authors show that their MCR system can be integrated into a CR pipeline (Dcoref++) to improve overall CR performance on Wikipedia, demonstrating practical utility beyond just the MCR task.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant research gap**: The authors compellingly demonstrate that existing CR systems perform poorly on Wikipedia text (Table 1 shows 10-15 F1 point drops compared to OntoNotes). This gap has been noted in prior work (Hendrickx and Hoste, 2009; Schäfer et al., 2012), but this paper provides concrete evidence and a new evaluation framework specifically for Wikipedia.

2. **Effective use of Wikipedia's unique structure**: The authors' approach of directly leveraging Wikipedia's markup (instead of relying on NEL pipelines) is particularly strong. As they note, this avoids the cascading errors common in other approaches (Zheng et al., 2013). Their feature engineering (Tables 2 and 3) clearly demonstrates the value of Wikipedia-specific features over generic CR features.

3. **Practical integration into CR pipeline**: The authors don't just propose a standalone MCR system but show how it can be integrated into a CR system (Dcoref++) to improve overall performance. Table 5 shows consistent improvements across all metrics (MUC, B3, CEAF, CoNLL), demonstrating the real-world utility of their work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to prior Wikipedia-specific CR approaches**: The authors mention two prior works (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) but don't compare their approach to these directly. Since these were rule-based CR systems specifically designed for Wikipedia, it's surprising they don't include them as baselines. The paper claims to "revisit" the task from Nakayama (2008), but without direct comparison, it's hard to assess how much improvement they've actually achieved.

2. **Small and potentially unrepresentative dataset**: The WCR dataset is quite small (only 30 articles, ~60k tokens). While the authors claim it's representative, the dataset size is insufficient for robust statistical evaluation. More critically, the dataset is limited to English Wikipedia, making it unclear how well the approach would generalize to other languages or to Wikipedia content in different domains (e.g., technical articles vs. biographies).

3. **Limited theoretical contribution**: The paper presents an effective engineering solution but makes no significant theoretical contribution to coreference resolution. The approach is largely based on leveraging existing resources (Wikipedia markup, Freebase) rather than proposing new insights about coreference patterns in Wikipedia text. This limits the broader impact of the work.

4. **Insufficient error analysis**: The paper provides some examples of errors (Figure 3), but lacks a thorough error analysis. For instance, what percentage of errors are due to poor feature engineering vs. inherent ambiguities in Wikipedia text? How do errors differ between pronouns and non-pronominal mentions? This is particularly important given the high precision/accuracy claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors mention that they "created a new dataset called WCR" but state that "details will be described in the non-anonymized version." Could you provide more details about the article selection process? Specifically, how many articles were initially considered, and what criteria were used to select the final 30 articles to ensure diversity in size, topic, and link density?

2. The paper states that "the WCR dataset contains roughly 7000 non singleton mentions, among which 1800 refer to the main concept." How did you determine which mentions refer to the main concept? The authors mention using "the title, its inferred type (a common noun inferred from the first sentence of the article)," but this seems somewhat subjective. Could you clarify the annotation guidelines for identifying MC mentions and how you ensured inter-annotator agreement?

3. In Table 5, the authors report that Dcoref++ improves the CoNLL F1 score by 4 absolute points over Dcoref (55.11 vs. 51.77). However, the numbers in the table show a 3.34-point improvement (55.11 - 51.77 = 3.34), not 4 points. Could you clarify this discrepancy?

4. The authors claim their approach is "simple yet powerful," but don't clearly explain why it works better than the baselines. What is the key insight that makes their feature set effective for MCR? Specifically, which feature family (title, inferred type, name variants, entity type, main) contributes most significantly to the performance gains?

5. The paper mentions that "we share all the datasets we used in this study" and "provide a dump of all the mentions in English Wikipedia our classifier identified as referring to the main concept." However, the URL provided ("www.somewhere.country") is a placeholder. Could you confirm the actual URL where these resources will be available?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the adaptation of coreference resolution systems to Wikipedia text, with clear evidence of the performance gap between existing systems and Wikipedia text. The proposed MCR task and dataset provide a useful new benchmark for the community. However, the small dataset size, lack of comparison to prior Wikipedia-specific CR approaches, and limited theoretical contribution prevent this from being a major contribution to the field. With more thorough evaluation and comparison to prior work, this could be a strong paper. As it stands, I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses listed above, particularly the comparison to prior work and the dataset size limitations.
