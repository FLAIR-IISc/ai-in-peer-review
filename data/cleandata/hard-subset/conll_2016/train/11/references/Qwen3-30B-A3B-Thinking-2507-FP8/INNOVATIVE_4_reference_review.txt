

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary
This paper introduces a new task called "Main Concept Resolution" for Wikipedia articles, which involves identifying all mentions of the main concept (the concept being described by the article) rather than performing full coreference resolution. The authors propose a binary classification approach that determines whether a mention refers to the main concept, using features extracted from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. They demonstrate that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems on this specific task, and that integrating their classifier into an existing coreference system (Dcoref) improves overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia articles.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **Novel Task Definition**: The paper identifies and formalizes "Main Concept Resolution" as a distinct task from standard coreference resolution, specifically designed for Wikipedia articles. This reframes the problem in a way that better suits Wikipedia's unique characteristics.
2. **Demonstrated Effectiveness**: The authors show a significant performance improvement (13-15 F1 points) over state-of-the-art coreference systems on their specific task, demonstrating the value of their novel approach.
3. **Practical Integration**: The paper demonstrates that their classifier can be integrated into existing coreference resolution systems to improve performance on Wikipedia articles, showing practical utility beyond the specific task.

## Strengths
1. **Novel Task with Strong Motivation**: The paper identifies a fundamental limitation in current coreference resolution systems (poor performance on Wikipedia) and proposes a novel task definition that directly addresses this limitation. The motivation is compelling: Wikipedia is a major NLP resource that has been neglected in coreference resolution research, and standard CR systems perform poorly on it. This is a bold conceptual shift that opens up new research directions.

2. **Significant Empirical Results**: The results are impressive - their approach achieves 89% F1 on the main concept resolution task, which is a 13-15 point absolute improvement over the best baselines. This is not just incremental improvement; it's a substantial gain that demonstrates the value of their novel task definition.

3. **Practical Utility**: The paper doesn't just propose a new task - it shows how this task can be practically integrated into existing coreference resolution pipelines (as Dcoref++), improving performance on Wikipedia articles. This demonstrates clear real-world applicability and shows how their approach can benefit existing NLP systems.

4. **Dataset Creation**: The authors created and shared a new dataset (WCR3) specifically for this task, which will be valuable for future research in this area. This is a significant contribution that enables reproducibility and further research.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scope and Generalization**: The paper is exclusively focused on Wikipedia, which is a specific domain. While this is valid for their task, they don't sufficiently address how this task would generalize to other domains or why this specific task is more important than standard coreference resolution. The paper lacks discussion of how this approach might apply to other text genres beyond Wikipedia.

2. **Small Dataset Size**: The WCR3 dataset is relatively small (30 documents, 60k tokens), which limits the statistical power of their results. While they acknowledge this, they don't sufficiently address how this impacts the generalizability of their findings or how their approach would scale to larger Wikipedia datasets.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Foundation**: The approach is empirical and effective, but the paper lacks a deeper theoretical justification for why this specific task is better suited for Wikipedia than standard coreference resolution. They don't explore the fundamental differences between the two tasks or provide a formal analysis of why their approach works better.

4. **Limited Comparison to Dedicated Wikipedia Systems**: The paper compares to general coreference resolution systems, but doesn't compare to systems that have been specifically designed for Wikipedia (like the ones mentioned in the related work section by Nguyen et al. and Nakayama). This makes it hard to assess whether their approach represents a genuine advance over existing Wikipedia-specific approaches.

5. **Questionable Task Significance**: While the task is novel, the paper doesn't convincingly argue why identifying mentions of the main concept is more important than standard coreference resolution for the broader NLP community. The connection to practical applications is somewhat tenuous.

## Questions to Authors
1. How would you expect your approach to generalize to other text genres beyond Wikipedia (e.g., news, blogs, social media)? What specific adaptations would be needed?

2. The paper mentions that Wikipedia is a "large, multilingual, highly structured, multi-domain encyclopedia," but your dataset is limited to English. How would you address the multilingual aspect of Wikipedia in future work?

3. You show that your classifier can be integrated into Dcoref to improve overall coreference resolution. Could you elaborate on how this integration works in practice, and what specific improvements were observed in the full coreference resolution pipeline?

## Overall Recommendation
**Accept**

This paper introduces a bold and novel concept - Main Concept Resolution - which represents a significant shift in how we approach coreference resolution for Wikipedia. While the methodology is relatively simple, the novelty of the task definition and the strong empirical results (13-15 point F1 improvement) make this a significant contribution. The paper is well-motivated, demonstrates clear practical utility, and creates a valuable new dataset for future research.

I recognize that the scope is limited to Wikipedia, and the dataset is relatively small, but as a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. The authors have identified an important gap in current coreference resolution research and proposed a novel way to address it that shows substantial empirical gains. This is the kind of paper that could inspire new research directions, which is exactly what I'm looking for in a CoNLL submission.
