

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary
This paper proposes a task for identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) in Wikipedia articles, framing it as a binary classification problem. The authors develop a classifier using features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, and demonstrate that their approach outperforms baselines built on state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems. The paper introduces a dataset (WCR3) and shows that their classifier can be integrated into existing CR systems to improve performance.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **The WCR3 dataset**: A Wikipedia-specific coreference dataset with 30 documents annotated with MC mentions.
2. **A novel binary classification approach** for identifying main concept mentions using Wikipedia-specific features.
3. **Demonstration of integration** into existing CR systems (Dcoref) to improve overall coreference resolution performance.

## Strengths

### 1. Novel Dataset for Wikipedia Coreference
The WCR3 dataset is a significant contribution as it fills a gap in coreference resources. The paper correctly identifies that Wikipedia texts present unique challenges for existing CR systems, and creating a dataset specifically for this genre is valuable. The dataset contains 30 articles with 7,000 non-singleton mentions (1,800 referring to the MC), which is a reasonable starting point for this niche domain. The careful attention to article diversity (size, topic, internal link density) is commendable.

### 2. Well-Designed Feature Set
The authors' feature engineering is thoughtful and leverages Wikipedia-specific markup and Freebase information effectively. The distinction between features for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions is appropriate. The incremental feature addition (Table 2) demonstrates a methodical approach to feature selection, showing how each feature family contributes to performance.

### 3. Practical Integration into Existing Systems
The paper successfully demonstrates that their classifier can be integrated into a rule-based CR system (Dcoref) to improve overall coreference resolution performance (Table 5). This is a strong practical contribution, showing that their work has real application value beyond just the specific MC identification task.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Methodological Inconsistencies in Dataset Description
The dataset description contains several critical inconsistencies that undermine the paper's credibility:
- The paper states "The resource contains roughly 7000 non singleton mentions, among which 1800 refer to the main concept, which is to say that 30 chains out of 1469 make up for 25% of the mentions annotated." This is mathematically impossible. If 1800 mentions refer to the MC out of 7000 total non-singleton mentions, that's 25.7%, but 30 chains out of 1469 chains would be 2.04%, not 25%. The paper confuses mentions with chains, which is a fundamental error in describing the dataset.

- The paper claims "Since WCR does not contain singleton mentions (in conformance to the OntoNotes guidelines), we consider the union of WCR mentions and all mentions predicted by the method described in (Raghunathan et al., 2010)." This is confusing and methodologically questionable. If the dataset was annotated according to OntoNotes guidelines (which explicitly exclude singletons from coreference chains), then adding singleton mentions via an external system creates an inconsistent evaluation framework.

- The paper states "We added about 13 400 automatically extracted mentions (singletons) to the 7 000 coreferent mentions annotated in WCR." But if WCR already contains only non-singleton mentions (by OntoNotes guidelines), then adding singletons creates a mixed dataset that doesn't conform to standard coreference evaluation practices.

### 2. Critical Numerical Inconsistency
The paper claims "our best classifier yields an absolute F1 increase of 13 points over Dcoref, and 15 points over Scoref." However, Table 4 shows:
- Dcoref B4: 75.77 F1
- This work: 88.79 F1
- Difference: 13.02 points (not 13 points, but the paper is correct here)

But then it states "15 points over Scoref." Table 4 shows Scoref B4: 73.69 F1, so 88.79 - 73.69 = 15.1 points. This is consistent with the paper's claim, but the paper's phrasing is ambiguous. The paper should explicitly state "13 points over Dcoref and 15 points over Scoref" rather than "13 points over Dcoref, and 15 points over Scoref" which could be misinterpreted as 13 points over Dcoref and 15 points over Scoref (which is correct) but the phrasing is slightly off.

### 3. Poorly Executed Baselines and Terminology
- The baselines (B1-B4) are described as "aggregating chains," but the paper doesn't clarify how this aggregation is performed or how it differs from standard coreference resolution. This makes the baselines difficult to understand and compare to the authors' approach.

- The paper uses inconsistent terminology. For example, it refers to "coreference type" (identity, attributive, copular) but this typically refers to relations between mentions, not the type of mention itself. The paper should clarify whether "coreference type" refers to the relation between mentions or the mention type (named entity, noun phrase, pronominal).

- In Table 4, the authors use "this work" as a system name, which is not standard academic practice. They should use "Our Approach" or "Proposed Classifier."

### 4. Missing Reference and Incomplete Methodology
- The paper cites "(Raghunathan et al., 2010)" as the source for the mention detection algorithm used in Dcoref and Scoref, but this reference does not appear in the provided reference list. This is a critical omission that undermines the paper's credibility.

- The paper states "We represent a mention by three strings (actual mention span, head word, and span up to the head noun), as well as its coarse attributes (entity type, gender and number)." However, it doesn't specify how the "head word" is determined or how "span up to the head noun" is computed, making it difficult to reproduce their approach.

### 5. Formatting and Grammatical Errors
The paper contains numerous minor formatting and grammatical errors that suggest a lack of careful proofreading:
- "increasingly large wealth of knowledge" (redundant phrasing)
- "We use the WikipediaMiner (Milne and Witten, 2008) API for easily accessing any piece of structure (clean text, labels, internal links, redirects, etc) in Wikipedia, and Jena2 to index and query Freebase." (Should be "Jena 2" or "Jena2" consistently)
- "For instance, it will aggregate the coreference chain referring to University of Sydney concept with a chain containing the mention Sydney." (Missing "the" before University of Sydney)
- "We selected5 the Csupport vector classification" (typo: "5" and "Csupport" should be "the C-support vector classification")

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states "30 chains out of 1469 make up for 25% of the mentions annotated." This is mathematically incorrect. Please clarify whether 30 chains or 1800 mentions represent the 25% figure, and provide the correct calculation.

2. The paper claims the WCR3 dataset was annotated with OntoNotes guidelines, but then states that they added singleton mentions to the dataset. OntoNotes guidelines explicitly exclude singletons from coreference annotation. Please clarify how the dataset was constructed to be consistent with OntoNotes guidelines.

3. The reference to Raghunathan et al. (2010) is cited in the text but not in the provided reference list. Please provide the complete reference or remove the citation.

4. The paper describes integrating the classifier into Dcoref as a "sieve" but doesn't explain why the sieve is ranked first. The paper states "Sieves in Dcoref are ranked in decreasing order of precision," but doesn't provide evidence that their classifier has higher precision than other sieves. Please clarify the rationale for ranking this sieve first.

## Overall Assessment

This paper has a clear motivation and addresses a valid gap in coreference resolution research for Wikipedia texts. The proposed approach shows promising results, and the dataset is a valuable contribution. However, the paper contains numerous methodological inconsistencies, numerical ambiguities, and grammatical errors that significantly undermine its credibility.

The critical issue is the dataset description inconsistency (30 chains vs. 1800 mentions for 25% of mentions), which suggests the authors may not have fully understood their own dataset. This, combined with the missing reference to Raghunathan et al. (2010), indicates a lack of careful attention to detail that is expected in a CONLL paper.

While the core idea is promising, the methodological flaws and inconsistencies are substantial enough to warrant rejection. The authors should thoroughly revise the paper to address these issues before resubmission.

**Recommendation: Reject**
