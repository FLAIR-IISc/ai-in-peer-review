

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a new task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR) for Wikipedia articles, defined as identifying all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) of the concept being described by the article (the "main concept" or MC). The authors frame this as a binary classification problem (does this mention refer to the MC?) and propose an SVM-based approach using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. They evaluate their approach against several baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems (Dcoref, Scoref) on their WCR dataset (30 Wikipedia articles, 60k tokens).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A new task definition and evaluation framework for identifying mentions of the main concept in Wikipedia articles (not standard coreference resolution)
2. A feature set specifically designed for Wikipedia text using markup and Freebase attributes
3. A demonstration that their approach outperforms baselines on their specific task

## Strengths

1. **Identification of a genuine gap in the literature**: The paper correctly identifies that coreference resolution systems trained on news data perform poorly on Wikipedia, which is a widely used resource in NLP. The authors cite appropriate prior work (Hendrickx and Hoste, 2009; Schäfer et al., 2012) to support this claim.

2. **Valuable dataset contribution**: The WCR dataset (30 Wikipedia articles, 60k tokens) is a useful resource for researchers studying coreference in Wikipedia, especially given the lack of existing datasets for this specific task. The dataset is annotated following OntoNotes guidelines, which is a strong point.

3. **Practical integration into existing systems**: The demonstration that their classifier can be integrated into Dcoref to improve coreference resolution performance (Dcoref++ gains 4 F1 points on WCR) shows tangible utility for the broader coreference community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Novelty of the task is questionable**: The paper claims to revisit a task initially discussed in Nakayama (2008), but the task they define (identifying mentions of the main concept) is significantly narrower than standard coreference resolution. The authors don't convincingly argue why this specific subtask is more important than the broader coreference resolution problem. Standard coreference resolution systems aim to group *all* mentions of *all* entities in a document, not just the main concept. The paper fails to demonstrate how this task relates to or improves the broader coreference resolution problem that the field has been working on for decades.

2. **Extremely limited evaluation**: The dataset is minuscule (30 documents, 60k tokens) compared to standard coreference datasets like OntoNotes (which has ~1.5M tokens) or CoNLL 2012 (which has ~200k tokens). With only 30 documents, the results are highly susceptible to dataset bias and lack statistical significance. The paper doesn't provide any analysis of why their dataset is representative of Wikipedia articles, despite acknowledging the small size.

3. **Insufficient comparison to standard coreference metrics and baselines**: The paper claims their approach "outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines," but this is misleading. They're not comparing against standard coreference resolution performance on the standard task; they're comparing against baselines built *on top* of coreference resolution systems for a specific subtask. The paper doesn't provide any results on standard coreference resolution metrics (MUC, B3, CEAF, CoNLL F1) for the full coreference task, only for their specific main concept resolution subtask.

4. **Lack of generalization evidence**: The paper demonstrates success on Wikipedia, but provides no evidence that their approach would generalize to other text genres. The authors acknowledge that "statistical systems can be trained or adapted to the WCR dataset" but don't provide any evidence that their feature set would work outside Wikipedia. For a paper claiming to solve a problem with coreference resolution systems, this is a significant limitation.

5. **Methodological concerns with the baselines**: The baselines (B1-B4) are ad-hoc heuristics that don't represent reasonable approaches to the main concept resolution problem. For example, B4 "aggregates all chains that contain at least one mention whose span is a substring of the title," which is clearly a weak baseline that would introduce many false positives (as the authors themselves acknowledge). The paper doesn't provide strong justification for why these baselines are meaningful comparisons.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your "main concept resolution" task relate to the standard coreference resolution task? What practical applications would benefit specifically from identifying only the main concept mentions rather than all coreference chains?

2. Given the extremely small dataset size (30 documents), how do you address concerns about statistical significance and representativeness of your results? What evidence do you have that your dataset is representative of Wikipedia articles across different topics and structures?

3. Why did you choose to frame this as a binary classification problem rather than as a standard coreference resolution task? What evidence do you have that this approach would generalize to other text genres beyond Wikipedia?

4. Can you provide more details about the limitations of your WCR dataset? How was it constructed, and what steps were taken to ensure it's representative of Wikipedia articles?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper identifies a genuine problem (coreference resolution systems perform poorly on Wikipedia), the proposed solution is a narrow subtask that doesn't advance the broader coreference resolution field. The evaluation is severely limited by the small dataset size, and the paper fails to demonstrate how their approach would generalize to the standard coreference resolution task. The methodological approach is not sufficiently rigorous, and the paper doesn't provide convincing evidence that their features or approach would be useful beyond Wikipedia.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
1. Framing the work within the standard coreference resolution task
2. Using a much larger dataset for evaluation
3. Providing comparisons to standard coreference resolution metrics and baselines
4. Demonstrating generalization to other text genres

As it stands, the paper presents a solution to a narrowly defined problem without sufficient evidence that it advances the broader field of coreference resolution.
