

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces a specific subtask of coreference resolution called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions of the main concept (the entity being described) in a Wikipedia article. The authors propose a binary classification approach to determine whether a mention refers to the main concept, using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. They claim their method outperforms state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems on this specific task and demonstrate that integrating their classifier into Dcoref improves coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia articles.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Creation of a dedicated dataset (WCR) specifically for evaluating Main Concept Resolution on Wikipedia, which fills a gap in the literature.
2. Clear articulation of the MCR task and its practical utility for information extraction from Wikipedia.
3. Demonstration that a straightforward binary classification approach can effectively identify main concept mentions in Wikipedia articles.

## Strengths

1. **Valuable Dataset Creation**: The authors have created a dedicated dataset (WCR) for the MCR task, which is a significant contribution. The paper acknowledges that most CR systems are trained on news data and perform poorly on Wikipedia, so having a dataset specifically for this purpose is genuinely useful for future research in this area.

2. **Clear Task Definition**: The paper clearly defines the MCR task and demonstrates its relevance for information extraction applications. The examples provided (e.g., identifying all mentions of "Chilly Gonzales" in the article about Chilly Gonzales) are well-chosen and illustrate the practical utility of the task.

3. **Thorough Baseline Comparison**: The authors provide a well-justified set of baselines (B1-B4) derived from state-of-the-art CR systems, which makes the evaluation more credible. The ablation study (Table 2) provides useful insights into feature importance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Methodological Novelty**: The core approach is not novel - it's a straightforward application of existing techniques (binary classification with features from Wikipedia markup) to a specific subtask of coreference resolution. The features used (title, entity type, etc.) are well-established in the literature (as cited in Section 2), and the paper doesn't present a fundamentally new algorithmic approach. This is particularly problematic given the paper's claim that it "revisits" a task previously discussed by Nakayama (2008).

2. **Insufficient Dataset Size**: The dataset is extremely small (30 documents, 60k tokens, 7000 mentions), which makes the evaluation results questionable. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide sufficient evidence that the results would generalize to larger datasets. For comparison, OntoNotes has over 1 million mentions across hundreds of documents. The small dataset size severely limits the statistical significance of the results.

3. **Failure to Compare to Relevant Prior Work**: The authors cite two previous works on Wikipedia CR (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) but fail to compare their approach to these systems. This is a major omission, as these papers were specifically designed for Wikipedia and directly relevant to the problem. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine whether the authors' approach actually represents progress over prior work.

4. **Overstated Significance of Results**: The paper claims their approach "outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines on this task," but the task they're evaluating (MCR) is not the standard coreference resolution task. The improvement on MCR doesn't necessarily translate to improved coreference resolution performance on standard benchmarks, which is the main goal of coreference resolution research. The authors don't convincingly argue why this specialized task is more important than standard coreference resolution.

5. **Potential Data Leakage**: The authors use Wikipedia markup and Freebase information to enrich mentions. Since they're using this information to train a classifier that's supposed to work on Wikipedia articles, there's a risk of data leakage. For example, if the classifier is trained on Wikipedia articles that use specific markup patterns, it might not generalize well to other Wikipedia articles. The paper doesn't adequately address this concern.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims this is a "revisit" of the task discussed in Nakayama (2008), but doesn't compare to that work. Why didn't you include Nakayama's system as a baseline, given that it was specifically designed for Wikipedia coreference resolution?

2. The dataset is very small (30 documents). How confident are you that the results would generalize to larger Wikipedia articles or to other text genres? Have you tested your approach on a larger sample of Wikipedia articles?

3. The authors state "it is now widely accepted that coreference resolution systems trained on newswire data performs poorly when tested on other text genres (Hendrickx and Hoste, 2009; Schäfer et al., 2012), including Wikipedia texts." However, your evaluation shows that Dcoref (a news-trained system) still outperforms statistical systems on Wikipedia. How do you reconcile this with the cited literature?

4. You claim your approach "outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines on this task." But the task you're evaluating (MCR) is not the standard coreference resolution task. How does this improvement on MCR translate to improved coreference resolution performance on standard benchmarks?

5. The paper uses Wikipedia markup to enrich mentions. Since the classifier is trained on Wikipedia articles, isn't there a risk of data leakage (i.e., the classifier is simply learning to recognize the specific markup patterns used in the training data)? How did you ensure this isn't the case?

## Overall Recommendation

I am not recommending acceptance of this paper. While the dataset creation is a valuable contribution, the paper fails to demonstrate sufficient methodological novelty or rigorous validation to justify its claims. The lack of comparison to relevant prior work on Wikipedia CR, the extremely small dataset size, and the overstatement of the significance of the results make it difficult to justify as a meaningful contribution to the coreference resolution research community. The approach is a straightforward application of existing techniques to a specialized subtask, not a fundamentally new method for coreference resolution.

The paper would benefit from a much larger evaluation on more diverse Wikipedia articles, comparison to the relevant prior work on Wikipedia CR, and a clearer justification for why this specialized task is more important than standard coreference resolution. Without these improvements, the paper does not meet the high standards for publication at CoNLL.
