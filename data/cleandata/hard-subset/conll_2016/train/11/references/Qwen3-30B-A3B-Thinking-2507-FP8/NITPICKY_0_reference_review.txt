

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary
The paper proposes a binary classification approach for identifying mentions of the "main concept" (MC) in Wikipedia articles. The authors frame the task as identifying all mentions that refer to the MC (e.g., all mentions of "Chilly Gonzales" in the article about him). They introduce a dataset called WCR3 (30 documents, 60k tokens) and compare their approach against baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems. The authors claim their method outperforms baselines by 13-15 F1 points and show that integrating their classifier into Dcoref improves coreference resolution performance by 4 points.

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)
1. **WCR3 dataset**: A small annotated dataset for main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles.
2. **Binary classification approach**: A simple but effective method for identifying MC mentions using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase.
3. **Integration into Dcoref**: Demonstrating that their classifier can improve a rule-based coreference resolution system.

## Strengths

1. **Novel task identification**: The paper correctly identifies a gap in the literature regarding coreference resolution specifically for Wikipedia, which has different characteristics than news articles. The task of identifying mentions of the main concept in Wikipedia articles is a well-defined niche that hasn't been thoroughly studied.

2. **Dataset contribution**: The WCR3 dataset is valuable for future research on Wikipedia-specific coreference resolution, as it provides a standardized evaluation benchmark for this specific task. The authors' willingness to share the dataset is commendable.

3. **Practical demonstration**: The paper shows a clear practical application of their approach by integrating it into an existing coreference resolution system (Dcoref) and demonstrating measurable improvements in coreference resolution performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical error in dataset description**: The paper states "1800 refer to the main concept, which is to say that 30 chains out of 1469 make up for 25% of the mentions annotated." This is mathematically incorrect. 30 chains out of 1469 is approximately 2.04%, not 25%. This is a significant error that undermines the credibility of the dataset description and suggests a lack of careful proofreading.

2. **Insufficient dataset size**: The WCR3 dataset consists of only 30 documents (60k tokens), which is extremely small for coreference resolution research. Standard datasets like OntoNotes contain thousands of documents. The paper does not adequately justify why such a small dataset is sufficient for their claims, especially since they compare against systems trained on much larger datasets. The claim that "we believe our results to be representative" is unsupported by the data.

3. **Lack of statistical significance**: The paper claims substantial improvements (13-15 F1 points) but provides no statistical significance tests for these improvements. Without this, it's impossible to determine if the improvements are meaningful or could have occurred by chance.

4. **Inconsistent and poor formatting**: The paper contains multiple formatting inconsistencies that suggest a lack of attention to detail:
   - Citations like "(Hendrickx and Hoste, 2009; Schäfer et al., 2012)" consistently lack a space after the semicolon (should be "2009; Schäfer" → "2009; Schäfer").
   - The URL "http://jena.apache.org" is not properly formatted as a hyperlink or reference.
   - The paper states "30 documents, comprising 60k tokens" - "k" should be "K" or written out as "60,000" for clarity.
   - "Csupport vector classification" should be "C-support vector classification" (with a hyphen).

5. **Unclear task justification**: The paper does not sufficiently explain why the main concept resolution task is important beyond the examples given. It's unclear how this task relates to broader coreference resolution or what practical applications it enables. The paper cites Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008) as prior work, but doesn't clearly explain how their approach differs from or improves upon those studies.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify the mathematical error in the dataset description? Specifically, how does "30 chains out of 1469" equate to "25% of the mentions annotated" when 30/1469 ≈ 2.04%?

2. Given the extremely small size of the WCR3 dataset (30 documents), how do you justify that your results are representative of Wikipedia coreference resolution performance? What evidence do you have that the dataset is sufficiently diverse?

3. The paper claims "our best classifier yields an absolute F1 increase of 13 points over Dcoref" - but Dcoref is one of the baselines, not a state-of-the-art system. How do your results compare to the state-of-the-art systems (e.g., Scoref) that the paper mentions as having better performance on OntoNotes?

## Overall Recommendation

**Reject**

While the paper identifies a potentially interesting niche (main concept resolution in Wikipedia) and provides a dataset, the numerous minor flaws (particularly the critical mathematical error in the dataset description) and significant methodological limitations (extremely small dataset size, lack of statistical significance) are substantial enough to warrant rejection. The paper's claims are undermined by these issues, and the minor formatting inconsistencies further indicate a lack of attention to detail that would be expected in a high-quality conference paper. The work would benefit from a much larger dataset, statistical significance testing, and careful proofreading before it could be considered for acceptance.

The authors' approach is interesting, but the paper as presented fails to meet the standards expected for a CoNLL publication due to the substantial number of minor flaws that collectively undermine the credibility of the work.
