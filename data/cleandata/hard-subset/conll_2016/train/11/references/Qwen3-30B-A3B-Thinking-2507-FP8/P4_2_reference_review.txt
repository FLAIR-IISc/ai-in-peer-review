

# Review: Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution

## Summary of the Paper
The paper addresses the challenge of adapting coreference resolution (CR) systems to Wikipedia articles, which are known to perform poorly with existing CR systems trained on news-like texts. The authors propose a new task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which focuses on identifying all mentions of the main concept (the concept described by the Wikipedia article) within the article. They create a new dataset (WCR3) with 30 Wikipedia articles annotated using OntoNotes guidelines, and develop a binary classification approach to identify mentions of the main concept using features extracted from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. Their approach outperforms state-of-the-art CR systems on this specific task, and when integrated into a rule-based CR system (Dcoref), it improves overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia articles.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. The authors define a novel task (Main Concept Resolution) for Wikipedia articles and create a new dataset (WCR3) specifically for this task, providing a standardized benchmark for future research on CR for Wikipedia.
2. They propose a simple yet effective binary classification approach that uses carefully designed features extracted from Wikipedia markup and Freebase to identify mentions of the main concept, achieving 89% F1 score on the MCR task.
3. They demonstrate that integrating their MCR classifier into an existing CR system (Dcoref) improves coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia articles by 4 F1 points, showing practical utility of their approach.

## Strengths

1. **Novel task definition and dataset:** The paper identifies a significant gap in the literature (lack of CR systems specifically designed for Wikipedia) and provides strong empirical evidence that existing systems perform poorly on Wikipedia (Table 1). The creation of WCR3, a new dataset with 30 Wikipedia articles annotated using OntoNotes guidelines, is a substantial contribution that will enable standardized evaluation of CR systems on Wikipedia text. The dataset covers various topics, sizes, and link densities, making it representative of Wikipedia's diversity.

2. **Clear demonstration of the problem and solution:** The paper effectively establishes the need for Wikipedia-specific CR systems through empirical evidence (Table 1 shows dramatic performance drops for all systems on WCR compared to OntoNotes). The authors' approach to MCR is well-motivated, leveraging Wikipedia's unique characteristics (hyperlinks, redirects, Freebase attributes) to design features that outperform general CR systems on this specific task.

3. **Practical integration and performance improvement:** The paper demonstrates that the MCR classifier can be integrated into an existing CR system (Dcoref) to improve overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia articles (Table 5 shows a 4-point absolute improvement in CoNLL F1 score). This shows the practical value of the work beyond just the MCR task, which is a strong contribution to the field.

4. **Reproducibility:** The authors commit to sharing their dataset and classifier results, which will facilitate reproducibility and future research. This is a significant contribution to the community, as it provides a standardized benchmark for evaluating CR systems on Wikipedia.

5. **Effective feature engineering:** The feature set is well-designed and leverages Wikipedia-specific characteristics (title, name variants, inferred types, etc.) rather than generic features. The incremental feature analysis (Table 2) demonstrates how each feature family contributes to performance, providing insight into what aspects of Wikipedia text are most relevant for MCR.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of the MCR task:** The paper focuses exclusively on identifying mentions of the main concept, which is a specific subtask rather than a complete solution for coreference resolution on Wikipedia. The authors do not discuss how their approach could be extended to identify all coreference chains (not just those related to the main concept), which would be a more comprehensive solution for Wikipedia CR.

2. **Small dataset size:** The WCR3 dataset is relatively small (30 articles, 60k tokens), which limits the statistical power of the evaluation. While the authors justify this by noting the difficulty of creating a large-scale Wikipedia coreference dataset, the small size makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of their results across the vast diversity of Wikipedia articles.

3. **Lack of comparison to related Wikipedia-specific work:** The paper mentions Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008) as related work but doesn't compare their approach to these Wikipedia-specific studies. The authors state these studies were "dedicated to extract tuples from Wikipedia articles" and didn't focus on coreference resolution, but a comparison would strengthen the claim of novelty and effectiveness of their approach.

4. **Limited failure analysis:** The paper provides some examples of errors (e.g., Figure 3, example f) but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of failure cases. A more detailed analysis of why the classifier makes certain errors (e.g., why "it" is incorrectly associated with the MC in example f) would help understand the limitations of the approach and guide future improvements.

5. **Incomplete feature ablation study:** While the paper provides some feature analysis (Table 2), it lacks a comprehensive ablation study that would help understand the relative importance of each feature family. This limits the understanding of what aspects of their approach are most critical for performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper focuses exclusively on identifying mentions of the main concept, but how would you extend your approach to identify all coreference chains in Wikipedia articles (not just those related to the main concept)?

2. Given the relatively small size of your dataset (30 articles), what steps would you take to validate that your results generalize to larger Wikipedia corpora, and how would you address potential biases in your dataset?

3. Why didn't you compare your approach to the Wikipedia-specific approaches by Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008), which were mentioned as related work but not directly compared to your method?

4. Could you provide more detailed analysis of the failure cases (e.g., the error in Figure 3, example f), including why the classifier made that mistake and how it might be addressed in future work?

5. Could you provide a more comprehensive ablation study of all feature families to better understand the relative contribution of each feature type to the final performance?
