- Proposes a new task, Main Concept Resolution (MCR), to identify all mentions of the entity an article describes in Wikipedia.

- Argues standard CR systems trained on news perform poorly on Wikipedia due to genre differences, supported by empirical evidence.

- Introduces WCR3, a new dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (60k tokens) annotated for MCR following OntoNotes guidelines and released for community use.

- Presents a binary classification approach using Wikipedia markup and Freebase-derived features to detect main-concept mentions.

- Method outperforms baselines by 13–15 F1 on MCR and improves overall CR when integrated into Dcoref (4-point F1 gain).

- Strength: Clear, well-motivated novel task addressing a real gap for Wikipedia-specific NLP applications.

- Strength: Carefully constructed, diverse dataset and reproducible evaluation protocol.

- Strength: Strong experimental results with an ablation study showing the contribution of feature families.

- Strength: Effective use of Wikipedia-specific signals (internal links, markup, Freebase) rather than standard NER pipelines.

- Weakness: Extremely small dataset (30 docs) limits statistical significance, generalizability, and raises overfitting concerns.

- Weakness: No comparison to prior Wikipedia-specific CR work (Nguyen et al. 2007; Nakayama 2008) despite citing them.

- Weakness: Limited and informal analysis of failure cases; lacks systematic error breakdown.

- Weakness: Insufficient justification for baseline selection and for not adapting prior Wikipedia systems as baselines.

- Question: Why were Nguyen (2007) and Nakayama (2008) not included as baselines, and how would the authors expect to compare?

- Question: How are overfitting concerns addressed given the small dataset; were additional-data experiments run?

- Question: How would the approach generalize to other text genres (news, blogs, scientific) and what modifications would be needed?

- Overall recommendation: Weak accept — valuable, well-motivated contribution with practical gains but limited by dataset size and missing comparisons; needs more data and broader evaluation.
