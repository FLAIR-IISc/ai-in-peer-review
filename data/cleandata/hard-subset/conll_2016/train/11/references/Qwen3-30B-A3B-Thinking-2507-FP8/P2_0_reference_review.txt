

# Review: "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper addresses a gap in coreference resolution (CR) research by focusing on Wikipedia articles, which have been largely overlooked in favor of newswire data. The authors define a specific task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) in a Wikipedia article (e.g., all mentions of "Chilly Gonzales" in the article about Chilly Gonzales). The authors propose a binary classification approach to determine whether a mention refers to the MC, leveraging features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. **Creation of a new dataset (WCR)** specifically for evaluating Main Concept Resolution on Wikipedia articles, annotated with OntoNotes guidelines.
2. **Development of a novel binary classification approach** for identifying MC mentions that outperforms state-of-the-art CR systems on Wikipedia by a significant margin (13-15 F1 points).
3. **Demonstration of practical integration** of their approach into an existing CR system (Dcoref), improving overall coreference resolution performance by 4 F1 points on Wikipedia articles.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant research gap**: The paper effectively demonstrates that standard CR systems trained on news data perform poorly on Wikipedia (Table 1 shows a dramatic drop in F1 scores), which is a well-supported claim based on prior work (Hendrickx and Hoste, 2009; Schäfer et al., 2012). This gap is important because Wikipedia is a widely used resource in NLP, yet it's been largely neglected in CR research.

2. **Strong empirical results with clear methodology**: The authors provide thorough experiments comparing their approach to multiple state-of-the-art systems (Dcoref, Scoref, etc.) across multiple metrics. Their feature analysis (Table 2) demonstrates the incremental value of different feature families, which is a strong contribution to understanding what makes their approach effective. The 89% F1 score on MC mentions is a significant improvement over the best baseline (76% F1).

3. **Leveraging Wikipedia's unique structure**: The authors' approach to using Wikipedia's markup (redirects, internal links) as a substitute for named-entity linking is a clever solution to a common problem in CR. This eliminates the need for an error-prone NEL pipeline, as they correctly note in Section 4.1: "We expect it to be of high quality because it exploits human-made links." This is a practical and well-motivated contribution.

4. **Demonstrated utility for real-world applications**: The paper shows that their approach can be integrated into existing CR systems (Dcoref++) to improve overall coreference resolution performance (Table 5). This shows that their work isn't just a theoretical exercise but has practical value for improving NLP systems that process Wikipedia content.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient dataset size and potential bias**: The WCR dataset is quite small (30 documents, 60k tokens), which limits the statistical significance of the results. The authors acknowledge this ("The WCR dataset is far smaller than the OntoNotes one"), but fail to provide a strong justification for why such a small dataset is sufficient for their claims. Additionally, the dataset consists of only 30 Wikipedia articles, which may not adequately represent the diversity of Wikipedia content (e.g., articles on different topics, writing styles, and lengths).

2. **Narrow scope of the task**: The main concept resolution task is significantly narrower than standard coreference resolution. While the authors acknowledge this (Section 1), they don't sufficiently address why this specific task is valuable beyond information extraction. The paper focuses on identifying mentions of the main concept, but doesn't clearly connect this to broader coreference resolution challenges or show how this approach could be extended to full coreference resolution.

3. **Lack of comparison to prior work on Wikipedia-specific CR**: The authors mention Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008) as related work, but they don't compare their approach directly to these methods. Given that these works also focused on Wikipedia, it's surprising that the authors didn't at least attempt to replicate or compare with these approaches, which would have strengthened their claim that their method is superior for Wikipedia.

4. **Unclear generalizability**: The paper doesn't adequately address how their approach would generalize to other text genres beyond Wikipedia. While they show good results on Wikipedia, it's unclear whether their features would work well on other structured text or if they're specifically tailored to Wikipedia's unique markup. This limits the broader impact of their work.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors state that their dataset contains "roughly 7000 non singleton mentions, among which 1800 refer to the main concept." Could you provide more detail about the distribution of these mentions across the 30 documents? Specifically, how many mentions per document were annotated as referring to the main concept, and how does this distribution vary across different types of Wikipedia articles (e.g., people vs. organizations)?

2. The paper mentions that "the integration of our classifier into the state-of-the-art rule-based coreference system of Lee et al. (2013) improves the detection of coreference chains in Wikipedia articles." Could you provide more specific examples of how the classifier improves the detection of coreference chains, particularly in cases where the classifier's decisions differ from the baseline?

3. The authors note that "the rule-based system of (Lee et al., 2013) works better than the machine-learning based systems on the WCR dataset." This is an interesting observation that contradicts the general trend in CR research. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, and whether this suggests that Wikipedia requires a different approach to CR than newswire data?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that addresses a meaningful gap in coreference resolution research. The authors clearly demonstrate that standard CR systems perform poorly on Wikipedia and propose a novel approach that significantly outperforms existing methods on this specific task. The creation of the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution to the field.

However, the small dataset size and narrow scope of the task are significant limitations that prevent this work from having broader impact on coreference resolution as a whole. The paper would be stronger if the authors had addressed the generalizability of their approach and provided more comprehensive comparisons to prior work on Wikipedia-specific CR.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily to address the dataset size concerns and clarify the broader implications of the work. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of coreference resolution in Wikipedia, and the proposed approach is well-motivated and effective for the specific task it addresses.
