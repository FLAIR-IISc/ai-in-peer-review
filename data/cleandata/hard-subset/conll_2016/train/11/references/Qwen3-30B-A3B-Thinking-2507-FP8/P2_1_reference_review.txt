

# Review for "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper addresses a significant gap in coreference resolution (CR) research: the poor performance of state-of-the-art CR systems on Wikipedia articles, which are widely used in NLP but not well-suited to existing CR approaches trained primarily on news-like texts. The authors focus on a specific task: identifying all mentions of the "main concept" (MC) in a Wikipedia article (e.g., all mentions of "Chilly Gonzales" in the article about Chilly Gonzales).

As I see it, the main contributions of this work are:

1. Creation of a dedicated dataset (WCR) for main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles, containing 30 articles annotated with main concept mentions.
2. A binary classification approach that identifies mentions of the main concept using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, which outperforms state-of-the-art CR systems on this specific task.
3. Demonstration that integrating their classifier into an existing rule-based CR system (Lee et al., 2013) improves overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia articles.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a meaningful research gap**: The paper effectively demonstrates that CR systems trained on news-like texts perform poorly on Wikipedia articles, as shown by their experiments with state-of-the-art systems on the WCR dataset. This is a well-motivated problem since Wikipedia is a widely used resource in NLP but has not received sufficient attention for CR adaptation. The paper provides concrete evidence (Table 1) showing that all CR systems tested have dramatically lower performance on WCR compared to OntoNotes, which is a compelling justification for their work.

2. **Leveraging Wikipedia's unique structure**: The authors make clever use of Wikipedia's inherent structure (hyperlinks, redirects, Freebase integration) to create features that are likely to be more accurate for this specific domain than general-purpose features. For instance, their preprocessing module uses Wikipedia links to extract semantic attributes without relying on named-entity linking pipelines, which often introduce cascading errors. This domain-aware approach is both novel and practical, and it explains why their system outperforms general-purpose CR systems.

3. **Compelling results with practical implications**: The paper demonstrates that their approach achieves 88.99% F1 on pronominal mentions and 88.67% F1 on non-pronominal mentions, which is a significant improvement (13-15 F1 points) over the best baseline. More importantly, they show that integrating their classifier into Dcoref (a state-of-the-art CR system) improves the CoNLL F1 score by 4 points on Wikipedia articles. This practical demonstration of utility makes the work valuable for NLP practitioners working with Wikipedia data.

4. **Valuable resource creation**: The authors provide a new dataset (WCR) and a classifier for identifying main concept mentions in Wikipedia articles, which will be useful for future research. They also plan to share their dataset and classifier, which is a significant contribution to the community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient dataset size and lack of detail**: The WCR dataset is quite small (30 articles, 60k tokens, 7000 non-singleton mentions), which is a significant limitation for statistical learning approaches. The authors acknowledge this (stating "the WCR dataset is far smaller than the OntoNotes one"), but don't provide sufficient justification for why this small dataset is sufficient for their claims. More importantly, they don't provide details about the dataset creation process (e.g., how the 30 articles were selected, how annotations were done, inter-annotator agreement), making it difficult to assess the quality of the dataset.

2. **Lack of comparison to relevant prior work**: The paper mentions Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008) as prior work on Wikipedia coreference, but doesn't directly compare their approach to these. Instead, they focus on comparing to general-purpose CR systems. This misses an opportunity to demonstrate how their approach improves upon prior work specifically designed for Wikipedia. The paper claims to "revisit" the task initially discussed in Nakayama (2008), but doesn't clearly articulate the differences or improvements.

3. **Limited evaluation of the main concept resolution task**: The evaluation is focused on the binary classification task (identifying mentions of the main concept), but doesn't fully demonstrate the utility of this task for downstream NLP applications. The paper mentions that this is useful for "a number of NLP tasks" but doesn't provide concrete examples or evaluations showing how this would improve those tasks.

4. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, they don't address how their method would perform on Wikipedia articles with very few internal links or redirects, or on articles in languages other than English. They also don't discuss how their approach would handle cases where the main concept is not explicitly mentioned in the article (e.g., when the article is about a concept that's only referred to implicitly).

5. **Lack of ablation study for feature families**: While they do provide some ablation results (Table 2), they don't systematically evaluate the contribution of each feature family. The paper states they "tried with less success other configurations on a heldout dataset" but doesn't share the details of these failed configurations, which would be valuable for understanding the robustness of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. How did you select the 30 articles for the WCR dataset? Were they chosen to represent a wide variety of Wikipedia articles (in terms of topic, size, internal link density, etc.), and if so, can you provide details on this selection process?

2. Can you elaborate on the annotation process for the WCR dataset? How many annotators were involved, and what was the inter-annotator agreement? What was the criteria for determining whether a mention referred to the main concept?

3. Why did you choose to focus on the main concept resolution task rather than full coreference resolution? How does your approach handle cases where there are multiple important concepts in a Wikipedia article (e.g., articles about organizations with multiple key figures)?

4. How would your approach perform on Wikipedia articles in languages other than English? What would need to be changed to make it work for other languages?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing a real gap in coreference resolution research for Wikipedia articles. The authors identify a clear problem, propose a well-motivated solution that leverages Wikipedia's unique structure, and demonstrate compelling results. The creation of the WCR dataset is particularly valuable for future research in this area.

However, the small dataset size and lack of detail about the dataset creation process are significant weaknesses that limit the confidence in the reported results. The paper also misses an opportunity to directly compare with prior work specifically designed for Wikipedia.

Given the paper's clear identification of a meaningful problem, the practical utility of the approach, and the creation of a valuable dataset, I would recommend acceptance, but with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses in their revision. The paper has the potential to significantly influence future work on coreference resolution for Wikipedia and similar text genres.
