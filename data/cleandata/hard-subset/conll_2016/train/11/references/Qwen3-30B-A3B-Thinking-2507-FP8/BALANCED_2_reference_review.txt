

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a specific coreference resolution task for Wikipedia articles: identifying all mentions of the "main concept" (MC) - the entity the article is primarily about. The authors argue that existing coreference resolution systems, trained on news data, perform poorly on Wikipedia text. They propose a binary classification approach to determine if a mention refers to the main concept, leveraging features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. They create a new dataset (WCR3) for this task and demonstrate that their approach outperforms baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference systems. 

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. **Creation of a new dataset (WCR3) specifically for the task of identifying mentions of the main concept in Wikipedia articles**, which enables future research on this specific problem.
2. **A binary classification approach that accurately identifies mentions of the main concept in Wikipedia articles**, leveraging Wikipedia's rich structure and Freebase information.
3. **Demonstration that integrating the classifier into an existing coreference system (Dcoref) improves overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia articles**.

## Strengths

1. **Identification of a meaningful gap in coreference resolution research**: The paper correctly identifies that existing coreference systems are trained on news data and perform poorly on Wikipedia, a widely used resource in NLP. The authors provide solid evidence through experiments showing dramatic performance drops (10-15 F1 points) when applying state-of-the-art systems to Wikipedia data. This gap is well-motivated and represents a valid research direction that has been underexplored.

2. **Novel use of Wikipedia's rich structure for feature extraction**: The paper's approach to leverage Wikipedia's markup (hyperlinks, redirects) and Freebase information to extract features is clever and avoids the pitfalls of named-entity linking pipelines used in other works. The features they design (e.g., comparing mention spans to MC title, name variants, inferred types) are directly relevant to the Wikipedia domain and exploit the unique characteristics of Wikipedia articles.

3. **Demonstrated practical utility**: The integration of their classifier into Dcoref (creating Dcoref++) shows a clear improvement (4 F1 points absolute gain) on the full coreference resolution task. This demonstrates that their approach isn't just useful for the specific task of identifying main concept mentions, but actually improves the performance of a full coreference resolution system. This is a strong practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Extremely limited dataset size**: The WCR3 dataset consists of only 30 Wikipedia articles (60k tokens, 7000 mentions). This is significantly smaller than standard coreference datasets (e.g., OntoNotes has 1.5M+ tokens). With such a small dataset, the risk of overfitting is high, and the reported results (89% F1) may not generalize well. The paper doesn't provide sufficient justification for the small size or evidence that it's representative of Wikipedia's diversity.

2. **Lack of rigorous comparison with domain-adapted systems**: The paper compares only to baselines built on top of generic coreference systems, but doesn't compare to systems that have been specifically adapted to Wikipedia. For example, they don't compare to the work of Nguyen et al. (2007) or Nakayama (2008), which were mentioned as related work. This weakens the claim that their approach is superior to existing Wikipedia-specific approaches.

3. **Unclear definition of the "main concept"**: The paper assumes the main concept is the title of the Wikipedia article, but this is not always accurate. Wikipedia articles often have redirects, multiple topics, or focus on a concept that isn't explicitly the title (e.g., "Chilly Gonzales" article might be about his music career rather than him personally). The paper doesn't adequately address how the main concept is defined or validated across the dataset.

4. **Insufficient detail on dataset creation and validation**: The paper lacks critical information about how the WCR3 dataset was created. How were the 30 articles selected? How many annotators were used? What was the inter-annotator agreement? Without this information, it's difficult to assess the quality of the dataset or reproduce the results.

5. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The evaluation is restricted to only two state-of-the-art systems (Dcoref and Scoref), and the comparison is on a very specific task (identifying main concept mentions), not on the full coreference resolution task. The paper claims their approach outperforms "state-of-the-art" systems, but the comparison is limited and doesn't address whether their approach could be used to improve other coreference systems.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that WCR3 contains "roughly 7000 non singleton mentions, among which 1800 refer to the main concept." How were these 1800 mentions of the main concept identified? Was this done by human annotators or automatically? If automatically, how was the automatic method validated?

2. The paper claims that "the rule-based system of (Lee et al., 2013) works better than the machine-learning based systems on the WCR dataset." This is surprising given that statistical systems usually outperform rule-based ones. Could you provide more details on why this might be the case? Is it because of the small dataset size, or are there specific characteristics of Wikipedia that make rule-based approaches more effective?

3. The authors mention that "the integration of our classifier into the state-of-the-art rule-based coreference system of Lee et al. (2013) improves the detection of coreference chains in Wikipedia articles." Could you provide more specific examples of how the classifier improves the chain detection? For instance, are there particular types of mentions or articles where the improvement is most significant?

4. The paper states that "the MC name variants extracted from the markup of Wikipedia (redirects, text anchored in links) as well as aliases from Freebase" are used. Given the potential for noisy redirects in Wikipedia (as mentioned in the paper), how did you handle cases where redirects might lead to incorrect name variants? For example, the paper mentions "Johnston and Sand Islands is a redirect of the Johnston Atoll article" causing an error - how did you mitigate such issues?

5. The dataset size (30 articles) is quite small for a machine learning approach. How did you address the risk of overfitting? Did you use techniques like cross-validation, regularization, or did you use a different evaluation strategy to account for the limited data?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **rejection** of this paper. While the paper identifies a meaningful gap in coreference resolution research and presents a clever approach to leverage Wikipedia's unique structure, the limitations are too severe to accept for a top-tier conference like CONLL.

The most critical issue is the extremely small dataset size (30 articles), which undermines the validity of the results. A dataset of this size is insufficient to support the claims of a "state-of-the-art" approach. The paper would need to significantly expand the dataset and provide more robust validation to be considered for publication.

Additionally, the lack of comparison to existing Wikipedia-specific approaches (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) and the unclear definition of the main concept further weaken the contribution.

However, with the following improvements, this paper could be significantly strengthened:

1. Expand the dataset to at least 100 articles (ideally more) to ensure sufficient data for training and evaluation.
2. Provide detailed information about the dataset creation process, including selection criteria, annotator guidelines, and inter-annotator agreement.
3. Compare against existing Wikipedia-specific coreference approaches (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) rather than just baselines built on generic systems.
4. Clarify the definition of the main concept and how it was validated across the dataset.
5. Provide more detailed analysis of the classifier's errors and how they might be addressed.

The core idea is promising, but the current work lacks the necessary scale and rigor to support the claims made in the paper. With the above improvements, this could be a valuable contribution to the field.
