

# Peer Review: "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the underexplored problem of adapting coreference resolution (CR) to Wikipedia articles. The authors define a specific subtask—"main concept resolution" (MCR)—where the goal is to identify all mentions of the central concept described in a Wikipedia article (e.g., "Chilly Gonzales" in the article about Chilly Gonzales). They propose a binary classifier to determine whether a mention refers to the main concept, leveraging Wikipedia's rich markup and Freebase information. The authors claim their approach outperforms state-of-the-art CR systems on this specific task and demonstrates improved performance when integrated into a rule-based CR system.

The primary contributions I identify are:
1. A well-defined task (MCR) that addresses a genuine gap in the literature for adapting CR to Wikipedia
2. A dataset (WCR3) of 30 Wikipedia articles annotated for main concept mentions
3. A binary classification approach using carefully engineered features from Wikipedia structure and Freebase

## Strengths

1. **Compelling Problem Statement**: The paper effectively establishes the need for domain adaptation in coreference resolution. The authors correctly note that standard CR systems trained on news data (ACE, OntoNotes) perform poorly on Wikipedia, citing relevant prior work (Hendrickx and Hoste, 2009; Schäfer et al., 2012). This is a significant gap in the literature that deserves attention.

2. **Effective Use of Wikipedia's Structure**: The authors' approach to leveraging Wikipedia's internal links, redirects, and Freebase mappings is particularly strong. By using the article's markup rather than relying on named-entity linking pipelines (as in Ratinov and Roth, 2012), they avoid cascading errors and benefit from Wikipedia's human-curated structure. The preprocessing module described in Section 4.1 is well-motivated and potentially reusable.

3. **Clear Task Definition**: The paper successfully reframes the problem as a binary classification task (mention refers to MC vs. not), which is well-suited for the domain and avoids the complexity of full coreference resolution. The evaluation metrics (precision, recall, F1) are appropriate for this task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Dataset Size and Representativeness**: The WCR dataset consists of only 30 articles (60k tokens, ~7k mentions), which is extremely small for a coreference resolution study. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't provide sufficient justification for why such a small dataset is adequate for their claims. The OntoNotes dataset used for training CR systems contains hundreds of documents. This small size makes it difficult to draw strong conclusions about generalizability and likely contributes to the modest improvements reported (13-15 F1 points over baselines).

2. **Task Scope and Practical Impact**: The main concept resolution task is a highly constrained version of coreference resolution. While the paper shows that their classifier improves performance when integrated into Dcoref (by 4 F1 points), this modest improvement on the full CR task doesn't fully justify the claimed significance. The authors don't convincingly demonstrate that solving MCR is a meaningful step toward better full CR on Wikipedia, or how this would benefit actual NLP applications beyond the narrow task they define.

3. **Limited Baseline Comparison**: The baselines (B1-B4) are relatively simple heuristics that don't compare against more sophisticated approaches to main concept resolution. The comparison is limited to two CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref), but doesn't include recent domain-adapted CR approaches or methods specifically designed for Wikipedia. The comparison against "always positive" (63.7% F1) is somewhat misleading as a baseline.

4. **Lack of Error Analysis**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough error analysis to understand *why* their approach works better than baselines. For example, they mention that most errors on pronominal mentions are due to lack of information about noun phrases surrounding pronouns, but don't provide examples or analyze the specific cases where their method fails. This limits the insights the community can gain from their work.

5. **Limited Novelty**: While the specific application to Wikipedia is valuable, the core approach (using external knowledge sources like Wikipedia and Freebase for coreference resolution) builds directly on Ratinov and Roth (2012) and Hajishirzi et al. (2013). The paper doesn't sufficiently articulate how their approach differs from or improves upon these previous works, beyond applying them to a new domain.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you justify the small dataset size (30 articles)? Could you provide evidence that this size is sufficient for drawing meaningful conclusions about the task, or how you would scale this to larger datasets?

2. The paper claims your approach outperforms state-of-the-art CR systems on the MCR task, but the main practical contribution is the integration into Dcoref to improve full CR performance by 4 F1 points. Could you elaborate on how this modest improvement compares to other domain adaptation approaches for Wikipedia CR?

3. The authors mention that "the integration of our classifier into the state-of-the-art rule-based coreference system of Lee et al. (2013) improves the detection of coreference chains in Wikipedia articles." How does this compare to simply retraining a standard CR system on Wikipedia data, which would be a more direct approach to domain adaptation?

## Overall Assessment

This paper identifies a genuine gap in the literature and makes a reasonable attempt to address it through a well-defined subtask. The use of Wikipedia's structure for feature extraction is particularly strong. However, the extremely small dataset size and the limited practical impact of the main concept resolution task (compared to full coreference resolution) significantly limit the paper's contribution. While the work is valuable as a first step in adapting CR to Wikipedia, it doesn't provide sufficient evidence to claim significant advances over prior work. The paper would benefit from a larger dataset, a more thorough comparison with domain adaptation approaches, and clearer articulation of how this work advances the broader field of coreference resolution.
