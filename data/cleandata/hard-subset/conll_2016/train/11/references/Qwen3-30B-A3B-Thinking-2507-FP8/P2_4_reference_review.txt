

# Review: Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses a significant gap in coreference resolution (CR) research: the poor performance of state-of-the-art CR systems on Wikipedia texts, which are widely used in NLP applications. The authors propose a specialized approach for identifying mentions of the "main concept" (MC) in Wikipedia articles - the entity being described by the article. Their main contributions are:

1. A novel testbed (WCR dataset) for evaluating CR systems on Wikipedia articles, consisting of 30 annotated articles (60k tokens)
2. A binary classification approach that identifies MC mentions using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase
3. Demonstration that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art CR systems on this task, with 89% F1 score (13-15 points higher than baselines)
4. Integration of their classifier into a rule-based CR system (Dcoref++) that improves overall coreference resolution performance by 4 points in CoNLL F1

## Strengths

**1. Addresses a genuine gap in NLP research:** The paper correctly identifies that CR systems trained on news data perform poorly on Wikipedia (Table 1 shows dramatic performance drops), and that this is a meaningful problem given Wikipedia's widespread use in NLP. This is a well-motivated research direction that addresses a real need in the community.

**2. Strong empirical results:** The authors provide clear evidence of their approach's superiority over baselines (Table 4). Their classifier achieves 88.99% F1 for pronominal mentions and 88.67% for non-pronominal mentions - substantial improvements over the best baselines. The integration into Dcoref (Dcoref++) also shows a significant 4-point improvement in CoNLL F1, demonstrating practical utility.

**3. Careful feature engineering:** The authors demonstrate thoughtful feature design, drawing from both coreference literature and Wikipedia-specific characteristics. Their analysis of feature contributions (Table 2) shows how each feature family improves performance, providing valuable insights for future work. The use of Wikipedia markup and Freebase to avoid the errors common in named-entity linking pipelines (Section 4.1) is particularly well-justified.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited scope of the problem:** The paper focuses on identifying mentions of the main concept, but coreference resolution typically involves identifying all coreference chains in a document. While the authors argue this is useful for information extraction, they don't clearly articulate how this specialized approach would generalize to the full coreference resolution task. The paper doesn't address how their method would handle cases where the main concept isn't the most frequently mentioned entity.

**2. Small dataset size:** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 Wikipedia articles (60k tokens). While the authors claim it's representative, this is an extremely small dataset for training and evaluating a machine learning model. Their 10-fold cross-validation is questionable given the small dataset size, and the results may not generalize well to larger Wikipedia corpora. For context, the OntoNotes dataset used for training most CR systems contains over 1 million tokens.

**3. Lack of comparison with prior Wikipedia-specific approaches:** The paper mentions two prior works (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) that focused on Wikipedia, but doesn't compare their approach to these. It's unclear how their method differs from or improves upon these prior approaches, which is a significant oversight. The authors claim they're "revisiting" the task, but don't show how their approach advances beyond the previous work.

**4. Limited discussion of limitations:** The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, it doesn't address how their method would handle Wikipedia articles where the main concept isn't explicitly stated in the title (e.g., articles about events or concepts), or how it would perform on non-English Wikipedia articles. The assumption that the title represents the main concept is a strong one that needs justification.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach differ from the prior work by Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008), and how does it improve upon them? The paper mentions these works but doesn't provide a clear comparison.

2. Given the small size of your dataset (30 articles), how confident are you that your results would generalize to a larger Wikipedia corpus? Have you considered using transfer learning or data augmentation to address the limited data size?

3. The paper focuses on identifying mentions of the main concept, but how would you extend this approach to full coreference resolution (identifying all coreference chains) in Wikipedia articles?

4. The authors use the Wikipedia article title as the main concept. How would your approach handle articles where the title doesn't perfectly represent the main concept (e.g., "The Battle of Waterloo" vs. "Waterloo")?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by highlighting the performance gap of existing CR systems on Wikipedia and proposing a specialized approach for identifying main concept mentions. The empirical results are strong, and the feature engineering is thoughtful. However, the limited scope of the problem (focusing only on the main concept rather than full coreference resolution), small dataset size, and lack of comparison with prior Wikipedia-specific approaches are significant weaknesses.

The paper would be substantially strengthened by expanding the scope to address full coreference resolution, providing a larger dataset, and comparing with prior Wikipedia-focused approaches. With these improvements, the work would have a much stronger impact on the field.

The paper addresses an important problem with meaningful empirical results, but its scope and dataset limitations prevent it from being a major contribution to coreference resolution research. It's a solid, well-executed study that would be suitable for publication as a workshop paper but might not be strong enough for a main conference like CoNLL.
