

# Review for "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary
This paper addresses a specific challenge in coreference resolution (CR) for Wikipedia articles. The authors propose a new task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which focuses on identifying all mentions of the main concept (the entity the article is describing) in a Wikipedia article. They argue that standard CR systems, trained on news text (ACE, OntoNotes), perform poorly on Wikipedia due to differences in text genre. The authors introduce a new dataset (WCR3) with 30 Wikipedia articles (60k tokens) annotated for the MCR task and propose a binary classification approach that uses features extracted from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. Their method outperforms baselines built on top of state-of-the-art CR systems by 13-15 F1 points and improves the overall coreference resolution performance when integrated into Dcoref.

## Main Contributions
1. Identification of a novel task (Main Concept Resolution) specifically for Wikipedia articles, distinct from standard coreference resolution.
2. Creation of a new evaluation dataset (WCR3) for this task, addressing the lack of resources for Wikipedia-specific coreference evaluation.
3. A binary classification approach that significantly outperforms baselines on the MCR task and improves overall coreference resolution when integrated into existing systems.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Task Definition with Clear Motivation**: The authors provide a strong motivation for why standard CR systems fail on Wikipedia (Section 1), citing empirical evidence that shows significant performance drops on Wikipedia (Table 1). Their identification of the "Main Concept Resolution" task is well-justified and addresses a real gap in the literature. Unlike standard CR, which aims to identify all coreference chains, MCR focuses specifically on the entity being described in the article, which is highly relevant for Wikipedia-based NLP applications like information extraction.

2. **Well-Defined Dataset and Evaluation**: The WCR3 dataset is carefully constructed with 30 Wikipedia articles spanning various topics and characteristics (size, topic, link density). The annotations follow OntoNotes guidelines, ensuring consistency with established CR evaluation standards. The dataset is made available to the community, which is a significant contribution for reproducibility and future work. The authors' careful description of the dataset (Section 5) and their analysis of system performance on it (Table 1) provide strong empirical evidence for their claims.

3. **Strong Experimental Results with Ablation Study**: The paper presents compelling results showing their approach outperforms baselines by 13-15 F1 points (Table 4). The ablation study (Table 2) demonstrates that each feature family contributes meaningfully to performance, providing valuable insight into what makes their approach effective. The integration of their classifier into Dcoref (Dcoref++) leads to a 4-point F1 improvement (Table 5), demonstrating practical utility beyond the specific MCR task.

4. **Leveraging Wikipedia-Specific Features**: The authors make intelligent use of Wikipedia's unique structure (markup, internal links, Freebase integration) rather than relying on standard NER pipelines. Their preprocessing (Section 4.1) that uses Wikipedia links to enrich mention attributes is particularly clever and addresses a key limitation in prior work (Section 2).

## Weaknesses

1. **Extremely Small Dataset Size**: The WCR3 dataset is quite small (only 30 documents, 60k tokens), which is significantly smaller than standard CR datasets like OntoNotes (thousands of documents). While the authors mention they sampled articles of various characteristics, this small size limits the statistical significance of their results and makes it difficult to assess whether the performance gains are generalizable. The paper doesn't address the risk of overfitting to this small dataset.

2. **Lack of Comparison to Prior Wikipedia-Specific Work**: The authors mention two prior studies (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) that worked on Wikipedia coreference but don't compare their approach to these. The paper states "we are not aware of much work conducted to adapt CR to this text genre," yet they don't evaluate against the two cited exceptions. It's unclear how their approach would compare to these specifically designed Wikipedia CR systems.

3. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases**: The paper provides some examples of classification errors (Figure 3) but lacks a systematic analysis of where the system fails. The authors don't discuss the most challenging cases for their classifier or provide a thorough breakdown of error types. This limits the understanding of the approach's limitations and potential for improvement.

4. **Insufficient Justification for Baseline Selection**: The authors chose to compare to Dcoref and Scoref because they "use the same pipeline (parser, mention detection, etc), while applying very different techniques (rules versus machine learning)." However, they don't justify why they didn't compare to other systems or why they didn't attempt to adapt the Wikipedia-specific systems (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) to their task.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions two prior studies (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) that worked on Wikipedia coreference, but doesn't compare to them. Could you explain why you didn't include these as baselines, and how you would expect your approach to compare to these specifically designed Wikipedia CR systems?

2. Given the small size of your dataset (30 documents), how do you address concerns about overfitting? Have you performed any experiments with additional data to verify that the performance gains are not specific to your small dataset?

3. Your approach is designed specifically for Wikipedia. How would you expect this approach to perform on other text genres (e.g., news, blogs, scientific articles), and what modifications would be necessary?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution by identifying a specific, well-motivated task for Wikipedia coreference resolution and providing a new dataset and baseline approach for it. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges in adapting CR to Wikipedia and provide a thoughtful solution that leverages Wikipedia's unique structure. The results are compelling for the specific task they define, and the integration into a standard CR system shows practical utility.

However, the extremely small dataset size and lack of comparison to prior Wikipedia-specific work are significant limitations that prevent the paper from making a stronger contribution to the field. These weaknesses reduce the generalizability of the results and make it difficult to fully assess the novelty and significance of the proposed approach.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept**

This paper has strong potential and makes a valuable contribution to a niche but important problem. With additional work to address the dataset size limitation (e.g., by collecting more Wikipedia articles for evaluation) and a more comprehensive comparison to prior Wikipedia-specific work, this could be a much stronger paper. As it stands, the contribution is meaningful but limited by the scope of their evaluation.
