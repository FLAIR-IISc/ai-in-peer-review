

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a method for identifying mentions of the "main concept" (MC) in Wikipedia articles, where the main concept is the entity being described by the article. The authors frame this as a binary classification task and propose a feature-based approach that leverages Wikipedia markup and Freebase information. They evaluate their approach against several baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems (Dcoref, Scoref) and demonstrate improvements in identifying MC mentions. They further integrate their classifier into Dcoref to improve full coreference resolution.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A dataset (WCR) for main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles
2. A feature-based approach for identifying MC mentions using Wikipedia markup and Freebase
3. An improved coreference resolution system (Dcoref++) by integrating their classifier

## Strengths
1. **Novel Task Definition**: The paper identifies an important gap in coreference resolution for Wikipedia texts, which is often overlooked in the literature. The focus on identifying mentions of the main concept (rather than full coreference chains) is a useful simplification for specific NLP applications like information extraction.

2. **Practical Evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach against multiple baselines and demonstrate concrete improvements (13-15 F1 points) over the best baselines. The integration of their classifier into Dcoref (Dcoref++) shows practical utility.

3. **Attention to Wikipedia-Specific Features**: The paper effectively leverages Wikipedia's unique structural features (redirects, internal links) rather than relying on standard named-entity linking pipelines, which is a significant methodological insight.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Inconsistency in Dataset Description**: The paper claims to use "WCR3" in Section 5 but consistently refers to it as "WCR" throughout the rest of the paper (Section 5, 6, Table 1, etc.). This creates confusion about the dataset name and makes it impossible to properly understand the evaluation setup. More critically, the paper states "30 documents" but later claims "30 chains out of 1469" make up 25% of mentions, which is logically inconsistent (30 chains cannot be derived from 30 documents without further explanation).

2. **Missing Figures and Tables**: The paper references "Figure 1," "Figure 2," "Figure 3," "Table 2," and "Table 3" but none are provided in the submission. Without these visual aids, it is impossible to properly evaluate the examples, feature analysis, and detailed results. This is a major methodological flaw in a conference paper.

3. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper claims significant improvements ("absolute F1 increase of 13 points") but does not provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to confirm these improvements are not due to random chance. This is a fundamental requirement in NLP research that the authors have neglected.

4. **Reproducibility Issues**: The paper states "This will be available at www.somewhere.country" for sharing datasets. This is clearly a placeholder URL, not a valid website. Without a real, accessible URL, the claim of sharing datasets is meaningless, and the work cannot be reproduced.

5. **Inconsistent Terminology and Abbreviations**: The paper uses "MC" as an abbreviation for "main concept" without defining it at first use in the abstract. It also inconsistently uses "WCR3" and "WCR" for the dataset name, creating confusion for readers.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you clarify the relationship between the 30 documents mentioned in Section 5 and the "30 chains" mentioned later in the same section? The statement "30 chains out of 1469 make up for 25% of the mentions annotated" is confusing given the context.

2. Why was the "WCR3" dataset named with a "3" when the paper consistently refers to it as "WCR" in all other sections? Please clarify the naming convention.

3. The paper claims "We share all the datasets we used in this study" but provides a placeholder URL "www.somewhere.country." What is the actual URL where these datasets will be made available?

4. The paper states "We trained two Support Vector Machine classifiers" but doesn't specify the hyperparameters used (C values, kernel parameters, etc.). Please provide these details for reproducibility.

5. How was the 10-fold cross-validation implemented? Was the data stratified by article type or topic? This is important for evaluating the generalization of the model.

## Overall Recommendation
**Reject**

While the paper addresses an interesting problem with a potentially useful application, the fundamental flaws in the paper's presentation and methodology are too severe to warrant acceptance. The inconsistent dataset description, missing figures and tables, lack of statistical significance testing, and unverifiable reproducibility claims undermine the credibility of the work. As a perfectionist reviewer, I must reject this paper because these issues are not minor—they are critical to the paper's validity and reproducibility. The authors should address these issues before considering a conference submission.
