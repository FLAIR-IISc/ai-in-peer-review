

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary

This paper introduces a task called "Main Concept Resolution" for Wikipedia articles, which involves identifying all mentions that refer to the main concept (the entity being described in the article). The authors frame this as a binary classification problem and propose an SVM-based classifier that uses features derived from Wikipedia markup (internal links, redirects) and Freebase (entity attributes like gender, nationality, etc.). They evaluate their approach on a small dataset they created (WCR, 30 articles, 60k tokens) and claim significant improvements over baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems.

## Main Contributions

1. **WCR dataset creation**: The authors created and annotated a small dataset (30 Wikipedia articles) specifically for the main concept resolution task, which fills an important gap in the literature.

2. **Task definition**: The paper clearly defines the main concept resolution task as a specific subtask of coreference resolution that has not been well-studied before.

3. **Feature engineering**: The paper presents a set of features that work well for the main concept resolution task, though the features themselves are largely borrowed from existing work.

## Strengths

1. **The WCR dataset is a genuine contribution**: Creating a dataset specifically for Wikipedia coreference resolution is valuable, as the authors correctly note that most coreference resolution systems perform poorly on Wikipedia. This dataset will be useful for future research in this area.

2. **The paper correctly identifies a gap in the literature**: The authors properly note that most coreference resolution systems are trained on news-like text and perform poorly on Wikipedia, and that there's little work specifically adapting coreference resolution to Wikipedia.

3. **The results are compelling for the specific task**: Their approach does outperform the baselines they propose (13-15 F1 points improvement), which is a positive result for the main concept resolution task they defined.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of methodological novelty**: The approach is essentially a feature engineering exercise using standard techniques (SVM, feature engineering) rather than introducing a new methodological contribution. The features used (title matches, entity types, etc.) are largely borrowed from existing work (Ratinov and Roth, 2012; Hajishirzi et al., 2013), with no significant innovation.

2. **Inadequate evaluation**: The WCR dataset is extremely small (30 articles, 60k tokens), which is orders of magnitude smaller than standard coreference evaluation datasets (e.g., CoNLL 2012 has ~500k tokens). This makes it difficult to draw strong conclusions about the generalizability of the approach. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't adequately address how the small dataset affects the reliability of their results.

3. **Misleading claims about "outperforming state-of-the-art systems"**: The paper claims that "our approach outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines on this task." However, they're not comparing to the engines themselves on the standard coreference resolution task; they're comparing to baselines built on top of those systems for a specific subtask. This is misleading and overstates the contribution.

4. **The task lacks broader relevance**: The main concept resolution task is extremely narrow and doesn't directly address the broader coreference resolution problem. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this specific task is important beyond information extraction, and doesn't show how this task leads to better overall coreference resolution.

5. **Lack of proper baseline comparison**: The paper compares to their own baselines (B1-B4), but doesn't directly compare to how standard coreference resolution systems would perform on the main concept resolution task without their classifier. This makes it difficult to determine if the improvement is due to their classifier or just a different approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "our approach outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines on this task." However, you're not comparing to the engines themselves on the main concept resolution task. Can you clarify this comparison and provide direct results of standard coreference resolution systems on your main concept resolution task?

2. Given the small size of your dataset (30 articles), how confident are you that your results generalize to the broader Wikipedia corpus? Have you considered running experiments on a larger dataset?

3. The paper states that "the integration of our classifier into the state-of-the-art rule-based coreference system of Lee et al. (2013) improves the detection of coreference chains in Wikipedia articles." How much of this improvement is due to the classifier, and how much is due to the specific architecture of Dcoref?

4. Why did you frame this as a binary classification problem rather than a coreference resolution task? How would a standard coreference resolution system perform on this task without your classifier?

5. The paper mentions that "the rule-based system of (Lee et al., 2013) works better than the machine-learning based systems on the WCR dataset." This is surprising given that most current research favors machine learning approaches for coreference resolution. Can you provide more detailed analysis of why this might be the case?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a useful dataset for a specific subtask of coreference resolution, but the methodological contribution is minimal, and the evaluation is insufficient due to the small dataset size. The claims about outperforming state-of-the-art systems are misleading. While the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate the broader significance of the main concept resolution task or provide a rigorous evaluation of the proposed approach.

I would recommend rejection due to the lack of methodological novelty and the insufficient evaluation. The dataset creation is valuable, but the paper does not present enough novel methodological contribution to warrant publication at CoNLL, especially given the small scale of the evaluation.
