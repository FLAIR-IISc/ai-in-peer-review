

# Review: "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of coreference resolution (CR) for Wikipedia articles, which differs significantly from traditional news-based CR systems. The authors focus on a specific subtask: identifying all mentions of the "main concept" (MC) in a Wikipedia article (e.g., finding all mentions of "Chilly Gonzales" in the article about him). They propose a binary classification approach that determines whether a mention refers to the MC, using features extracted from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. The authors evaluate their approach against several baselines built on top of state-of-the-art CR systems, and demonstrate that their method outperforms these baselines on the specific task of main concept resolution. They further integrate their classifier into the rule-based system Dcoref, improving overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia articles.

## Main Contributions
1. **Novel testbed for Wikipedia-specific coreference resolution**: The authors introduce WCR3, a dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles annotated with main concept mentions according to OntoNotes guidelines, which is valuable for future research in this niche.
2. **Binary classification approach for main concept mentions**: A feature-rich classifier that outperforms state-of-the-art generic CR systems on the specific task of identifying main concept mentions (by 13-15 F1 points absolute).
3. **Integration into existing CR systems**: Demonstrating that incorporating their classifier into Dcoref improves overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia articles by 4 F1 points.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and clear problem definition**: The paper effectively establishes why Wikipedia requires specialized coreference resolution, citing relevant literature (Hendrickx and Hoste, 2009; Schäfer et al., 2012) showing that news-trained CR systems perform poorly on Wikipedia. The authors clearly articulate the gap in the literature and justify their focus on Wikipedia.

2. **Novel dataset with careful curation**: The WCR3 dataset is a significant contribution, as it's one of the few resources specifically for Wikipedia coreference. The authors provide details about how they curated the dataset with articles of various characteristics (size, topic, internal link density), making it more representative than a random sample. The dataset is annotated according to OntoNotes guidelines, enabling direct comparison with other CR resources.

3. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The authors provide comprehensive evaluation with ablation studies showing the contribution of different feature sets, comparison with multiple baselines, and integration into an existing CR system. The results are convincing, with the authors showing significant improvements over baselines (e.g., 13-15 F1 points absolute gain) and demonstrating that their approach can improve full coreference resolution performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited dataset size**: The WCR3 dataset is quite small (30 articles, ~60k tokens, 7000 mentions), which is a significant limitation for a machine learning approach. The small size makes it difficult to draw strong conclusions about generalizability, and the 10-fold cross-validation might not adequately capture the variability in Wikipedia articles. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address how it affects the reliability of their results.

2. **Lack of comparison with relevant prior work**: The authors don't compare their approach with the two earlier rule-based systems specifically designed for Wikipedia (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008), which the paper itself cites as "two notable exceptions." Given that these systems were designed for the same task, the authors should either compare with them or explain why their approach is superior to these earlier efforts.

3. **Questionable scope of contribution**: The paper focuses on a specific subtask (identifying main concept mentions) rather than full coreference resolution. While the authors argue this is useful for NLP tasks, it's not clear how much this contributes to the broader field of coreference resolution. The main contribution seems to be a specialized system for a narrow task rather than a general CR advance, which limits the paper's significance for the coreference research community.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you compare your approach to the rule-based systems proposed by Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008)? Did you attempt to replicate or extend their work, and if not, why? Given that these systems were designed for the same task, it would be valuable to understand how your approach differs and whether it represents a significant improvement.

2. Given the small size of the WCR3 dataset (30 articles), how confident are you that your results generalize to larger Wikipedia articles? Could you provide more details on the diversity of the 30 articles (e.g., how many people, organizations, locations, events, etc.) to better understand the representativeness of your dataset?

3. The paper demonstrates that your classifier outperforms state-of-the-art CR systems on the WCR dataset, but doesn't show how well it performs on standard CR datasets (e.g., OntoNotes). Is your approach specifically tailored for Wikipedia, or would it generalize to other text genres? If it's Wikipedia-specific, how does that limit its broader applicability to coreference resolution research?

## Additional Comments
The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the main concept resolution task as a proxy for full coreference resolution. The authors claim that identifying main concept mentions is "useful in many NLP tasks," but it's not clear how much this specific subtask contributes to the broader goal of coreference resolution. The paper would be stronger if it positioned this work more clearly within the larger field of coreference resolution rather than presenting it as a standalone contribution.

The paper also doesn't provide sufficient details about the computational complexity of their approach or how it would scale to larger Wikipedia articles. Given that Wikipedia has millions of articles, this is an important consideration for practical applications.

## Overall Recommendation
I recommend **weak reject** for this paper. While the paper has several strengths, including a well-motivated problem and a novel dataset, the weaknesses (particularly the small dataset size and lack of comparison with relevant prior work) significantly limit the paper's contribution to the field. The authors have made a useful contribution to a niche area, but the scope of the contribution is too narrow to be considered a significant advance in coreference resolution research.

**Recommendation for Improvement**: To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Expand the dataset to include more Wikipedia articles (ideally at least 100 articles) to better validate their approach.
2. Compare their approach directly with the two Wikipedia-specific rule-based systems cited in the related work.
3. Provide more detailed discussion about how their main concept resolution approach relates to full coreference resolution and whether it can be extended to that broader task.
4. Address the limitations of their approach in more depth, particularly regarding the small dataset size and potential lack of generalizability.
