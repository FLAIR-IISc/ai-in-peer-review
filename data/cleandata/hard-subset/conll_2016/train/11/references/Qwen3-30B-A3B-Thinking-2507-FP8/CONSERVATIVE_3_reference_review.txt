

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a method for identifying mentions of the "main concept" (MC) in Wikipedia articles - i.e., all mentions that refer to the concept being described by the article. The authors frame this as a binary classification problem (whether a mention refers to the MC) and propose a classifier using features extracted from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. They evaluate their approach against baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems (Dcoref and Scoref) on a small Wikipedia-specific dataset they created called WCR (30 articles, ~60k tokens).

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)
1. Creation of a small-scale dataset (WCR) specifically for identifying main concept mentions in Wikipedia articles.
2. A specialized classifier for the specific task of identifying main concept mentions, which outperforms baselines on this narrow task.
3. Demonstration of practical integration of this classifier into a state-of-the-art coreference resolution system (Dcoref) to improve overall performance.

## Strengths

1. **Well-identified problem**: The paper correctly identifies a real issue in coreference resolution - that systems trained on news perform poorly on Wikipedia. This is a valid observation that should be addressed in the field, and the authors provide empirical evidence through their experiments showing significant performance degradation (15-17 F1 points) when applying standard CR systems to Wikipedia.

2. **Useful dataset**: The creation of WCR, even though small, provides a resource for future work on coreference resolution in Wikipedia. This is a concrete contribution that can benefit the community, especially given the lack of dedicated resources for this domain.

3. **Practical integration**: The integration of their classifier into Dcoref to improve coreference resolution performance is a tangible result that shows practical utility. The paper demonstrates that their specialized approach can be leveraged within an existing pipeline to improve overall performance (4 F1 points on CoNLL metric).

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty**: The proposed method is not novel - it uses well-established features (title, entity types, etc.) and a standard SVM classifier. The paper doesn't present a fundamentally new method, just a specialized application of existing techniques to a specific task. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find this to be a significant weakness. The features they use are explicitly cited as being "borrowed from the related literature" (Section 4.2), and the approach is essentially a rule-based extension of previous work rather than a novel contribution.

2. **Insufficient dataset size**: The WCR dataset is very small (30 articles, ~60k tokens), which makes the evaluation statistically weak. Standard coreference datasets like OntoNotes contain hundreds of articles. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't adequately address how the small size limits the generalizability of their results. With only 30 samples, 10-fold cross-validation (used for their classifier) involves training on only 3 articles per fold, which is insufficient for reliable machine learning evaluation. This makes the reported 13-15 point F1 improvements questionable in terms of statistical significance.

3. **Overstated claims**: The paper claims their approach "outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines on this task" but the "task" is highly specific (identifying mentions of the main concept) rather than full coreference resolution. The improvements they report (13-15 F1 points) are for a very narrow subtask, not for the full coreference resolution task. This misrepresents the significance of their contribution, as the main concept resolution task is not the standard coreference resolution problem.

4. **Lack of comparison to relevant domain adaptation methods**: The paper doesn't compare their approach to other domain adaptation methods for coreference resolution, which is a standard way to handle the issue they identify (systems trained on news perform poorly on Wikipedia). They only compare to baselines built on top of existing CR systems, not to methods specifically designed for adapting to Wikipedia or other text genres.

5. **Questionable evaluation methodology**: The paper uses a 10-fold cross-validation on a dataset of only 30 articles (3 articles per fold), which is statistically unsound for a machine learning classifier with many features. The paper also doesn't provide confidence intervals or statistical significance testing for their results, which is expected for a rigorous evaluation.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you justify the claim that your approach outperforms "state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines" when your evaluation is specifically for a narrow subtask (main concept mentions) rather than full coreference resolution? 

2. Given the extremely small size of your dataset (30 articles), what evidence do you have that your results generalize to the full Wikipedia corpus? Would you expect similar performance improvements on a larger, more diverse Wikipedia dataset?

3. Why didn't you compare your approach to other domain adaptation techniques for coreference resolution (e.g., transfer learning, multi-task learning, or fine-tuning on Wikipedia data) rather than just baselines built on top of existing CR systems?

## Overall Recommendation

I cannot recommend acceptance of this paper. While the problem they identify is valid and their dataset is a useful resource, the contribution is not significant enough for a top-tier conference like CONLL. The approach lacks novelty (it's simply applying well-established features and methods to a specific task), the evaluation is statistically weak due to the small dataset size, and the claims are overstated. The paper does not present a new method or significant insight that would advance the field of coreference resolution. The results are interesting for a very specific niche but do not constitute a substantial contribution to the field.

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find that this paper fails to meet the high standards of rigor and novelty expected for CONLL. The authors have not provided sufficient evidence to support their claims, and the contribution is too incremental to warrant publication at this venue.
