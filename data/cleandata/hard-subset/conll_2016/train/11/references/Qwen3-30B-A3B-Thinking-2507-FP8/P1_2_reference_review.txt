

# Peer Review: "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of coreference resolution (CR) in Wikipedia articles, specifically focusing on identifying all mentions of the "main concept" (MC) - the concept being described by the article. The authors argue that state-of-the-art CR systems, trained primarily on news text, perform poorly on Wikipedia. They propose a binary classification approach that determines whether a mention refers to the MC, using features extracted from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. The authors evaluate their approach against several baselines built on top of state-of-the-art CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref), and demonstrate that their method outperforms these baselines. They further show that integrating their classifier into Dcoref improves coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia articles.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A well-defined testbed and evaluation framework for main concept resolution in Wikipedia, along with a new dataset (WCR) specifically for this task.
2. A simple yet effective binary classification approach for identifying main concept mentions, leveraging Wikipedia's unique structural features (hyperlinks, redirects, Freebase attributes) rather than relying on standard coreference resolution pipelines.
3. A practical demonstration of how a domain-specific classifier can be integrated into an existing coreference resolution system to improve performance on Wikipedia text.

## Strengths

1. **Novel evaluation task with clear motivation**: The paper identifies a meaningful gap in current coreference resolution research - the poor performance of news-trained systems on Wikipedia. The authors provide a thorough empirical analysis showing that state-of-the-art CR systems perform significantly worse (4-17 F1 points lower) on Wikipedia than on standard news datasets. This is a strong contribution that highlights a real-world limitation in current NLP technology.

2. **Well-constructed dataset and evaluation**: The authors provide a new dataset (WCR) with 30 Wikipedia articles annotated according to OntoNotes guidelines. This dataset is valuable for future research, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of multiple state-of-the-art systems on this dataset. The comparison between performance on WCR versus OntoNotes is particularly insightful and demonstrates the domain shift problem convincingly.

3. **Practical integration and improvement**: The authors demonstrate that their classifier can be meaningfully integrated into an existing rule-based coreference system (Dcoref), improving the overall coreference resolution performance by 4 absolute F1 points. This shows that their approach has practical utility beyond just the specific main concept identification task, which is a strong contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of the problem**: The paper focuses specifically on identifying mentions of the main concept, which is a narrower task than full coreference resolution. While the authors claim this is a prerequisite for many NLP tasks, they don't sufficiently justify why this specific subtask is more important than solving the full coreference resolution problem for Wikipedia. The connection between main concept resolution and general coreference resolution is weak, with the paper only showing a small improvement (4 points) when integrated into an existing system.

2. **Lack of comparison to domain-specific approaches**: The authors mention two previous studies (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) that were dedicated to Wikipedia coreference, but they don't compare their approach to these specifically. Instead, they compare to general CR systems. This makes it difficult to assess whether their approach is truly novel or just an extension of previous Wikipedia-specific work. The authors should have compared against these domain-specific approaches to establish their novelty.

3. **Small dataset size and potential overfitting concerns**: The WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents (~60k tokens), which is significantly smaller than standard coreference datasets like OntoNotes. This small size limits the statistical power of their evaluation and raises concerns about overfitting to this limited dataset. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address how it affects the generalizability of their results or the reliability of their performance claims.

4. **Unclear contribution to coreference resolution literature**: The paper's title and focus suggest it's about coreference resolution, but the actual contribution is a binary classification task for identifying main concept mentions. The authors don't convincingly demonstrate how their work advances the broader coreference resolution literature. The paper would be stronger if they framed their contribution as a component for improving coreference resolution in Wikipedia rather than as a standalone task.

5. **Lack of thorough feature analysis**: While the paper describes a rich set of features, they don't provide a comprehensive ablation study showing the contribution of each feature family. The analysis in Table 2 is limited to pronominal mentions, and there's no similar analysis for non-pronominal mentions. A more thorough analysis would provide better insights into what makes their approach work and how features contribute to the final performance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the rule-based system of (Lee et al., 2013) works better than the machine-learning based systems on the WCR dataset." Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, and whether this observation suggests a fundamental limitation of statistical approaches for Wikipedia coreference, or whether it's simply due to the small size of the WCR dataset?

2. The authors claim their approach "outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines on this task." However, the task they're evaluating is specifically "main concept resolution," not full coreference resolution. Could you clarify how your results for main concept resolution translate to improvements in the broader coreference resolution task? The paper currently shows a 4-point improvement in CoNLL F1 when integrating your classifier into Dcoref, but this is still a modest improvement.

3. The WCR dataset is small (30 documents). How did you ensure that this dataset is representative of Wikipedia articles across different topics, structures, and sizes? What steps did you take to avoid selection bias in your dataset construction?

4. The authors mention that "the integration of our classifier into the state-of-the-art rule-based coreference system of Lee et al. (2013) improves the detection of coreference chains in Wikipedia articles." Could you provide more details about how this integration works, and whether this approach could be generalized to other coreference resolution systems beyond Dcoref?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper presents a well-motivated study of a specific challenge in coreference resolution for Wikipedia. The authors provide a valuable new dataset and demonstrate that state-of-the-art systems perform significantly worse on Wikipedia than on news text. Their binary classification approach for identifying main concept mentions is simple yet effective, and they demonstrate practical utility by integrating it into an existing coreference system.

However, the paper's contribution is limited to a specific subtask rather than advancing the broader coreference resolution problem. The small dataset size and lack of comparison to previous Wikipedia-specific approaches are significant limitations. The connection to full coreference resolution is weak, and the paper would benefit from a stronger justification of why this specific subtask is more important than solving the full coreference resolution problem for Wikipedia.

I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly regarding the scope of the contribution, comparison to domain-specific approaches, and dataset limitations. The paper has strong potential but needs to better position its contribution within the broader coreference resolution literature.
