

# Peer Review: "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary of the Paper and Primary Contributions

This paper identifies a significant gap in the coreference resolution (CR) literature: existing CR systems, trained primarily on newswire data, perform poorly on Wikipedia articles. The authors propose a specialized task they call "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions of the central entity (the "main concept" or MC) described in a Wikipedia article. Their approach frames MCR as a binary classification task (does a mention refer to the MC?) using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, rather than attempting full coreference resolution.

**Primary contributions as I see them:**

1. **A well-defined task for Wikipedia-specific coreference resolution:** The authors correctly identify that existing CR systems are ill-suited for Wikipedia and propose a more focused task (MCR) that aligns with common Wikipedia-based NLP applications.

2. **A high-quality dataset (WCR):** They created a new dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (60k tokens) annotated according to OntoNotes guidelines, specifically for evaluating MCR. This dataset is well-constructed and makes a valuable contribution to the field.

3. **A practical integration method:** The authors demonstrate how their MCR classifier can be integrated into existing CR systems (specifically Dcoref) to improve Wikipedia-specific coreference performance by 4 points in F1.

## Strengths

1. **Strong problem identification:** The authors correctly identify a significant problem in the field: CR systems trained on newswire data perform poorly on Wikipedia. Their empirical evidence (Table 1 showing dramatic performance drops on WCR) is compelling and well-documented. This is an important gap that needs addressing for real-world NLP applications that leverage Wikipedia.

2. **High-quality dataset creation:** The WCR dataset is carefully constructed with attention to diversity in article size, topic, and internal link density. The annotation follows OntoNotes guidelines, and the dataset is made publicly available. This is a substantial contribution to the field that will facilitate future research.

3. **Practical impact demonstration:** The authors show that their approach isn't just theoretically sound but has real practical value. The Dcoref++ extension improves coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia by 4 F1 points, which is a meaningful improvement in a practical setting.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misalignment between problem statement and claimed contributions:** The paper frames its contribution as a CR approach, but the actual task is much narrower: identifying mentions of a *single* entity (the main concept) rather than resolving all coreference relations. The authors repeatedly refer to their task as "coreference resolution" when it's actually a binary classification task for a specific entity. This misalignment is problematic because:
   - The paper claims their approach "outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines," but these engines aren't designed for this specific task (they're designed for full coreference resolution)
   - The evaluation metrics (precision, recall, F1 for MC mentions) don't reflect full coreference resolution performance
   - This misalignment makes it difficult to assess the true contribution to the coreference resolution field

2. **Inadequate comparison to existing Wikipedia-specific approaches:** The authors claim "we are not aware of much work conducted to adapt CR to this text genre" and cite only two studies (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008). However, they don't compare their method to these or other Wikipedia-specific CR approaches. The paper by Nguyen et al. (2007) specifically addresses "extracting tuples from Wikipedia articles" using a rule-based CR system, which is closer to the authors' approach than they acknowledge. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine if their approach is truly novel or just a minor variation of existing work.

3. **Limited dataset size and statistical significance:** The WCR dataset is very small (30 articles, 60k tokens), which limits the statistical significance of the results. The authors claim the dataset is representative due to its diversity, but with only 30 articles, it's difficult to draw strong conclusions about generalizability. The 10-fold cross-validation they use with such a small dataset is questionable and may overestimate performance.

4. **Lack of deep analysis of Wikipedia-specific features:** The paper claims that "incorporating external knowledge into a CR system is beneficial" and that Wikipedia markup provides "less ambiguous information," but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of *why* these features work. The feature ablation studies (Tables 2 and 3) are limited to a few feature families without explaining how these features exploit Wikipedia's unique characteristics. For example, they don't analyze how the use of redirects or internal links specifically helps their model.

5. **Overstated implications:** The paper claims their approach "outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines on this task," but the task isn't standard coreference resolution. They also claim their method "revisits" the task from Nakayama (2008), but their binary classification approach is fundamentally different from the rule-based CR system described in that work. These claims overstate the novelty and significance of their contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why did you choose to frame the problem as a binary classification task (is this mention the MC?) rather than a full coreference resolution task? How would your approach change if the goal was to identify *all* coreference relations in a Wikipedia article, not just those referring to the main concept?

2. The paper mentions that "mentions of the MC often are spread over several coreference chains," which is why they developed baselines B3 and B4 to aggregate chains. How would your approach handle Wikipedia articles with multiple important entities (e.g., articles about organizations with multiple key people) where the main concept isn't clearly defined?

3. The authors of Nguyen et al. (2007) developed a rule-based CR system specifically for Wikipedia. Why didn't you compare your approach directly to this system, rather than just citing it? How would your method differ in performance and implementation?

4. The paper states that "the rule-based system of (Lee et al., 2013) works better than the machine-learning based systems on the WCR dataset." This is a surprising finding that merits deeper investigation. Can you provide more insight into why rule-based approaches outperform statistical ones on Wikipedia, and whether this suggests a fundamental difference in the nature of coreference in Wikipedia versus news?

5. In Figure 3(f), the classifier associates "it" with the MC instead of "Johnston Atoll Safeguard C mission." What specific features would need to be modified or added to correctly resolve this pronoun? How would these changes affect the classifier's performance on other examples?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by identifying a significant gap in CR for Wikipedia and providing a well-constructed dataset for this specific task. The approach to MCR is practical and shows meaningful improvements over baselines. However, the misalignment between the problem statement and the claimed contributions (framing a binary classification task as a CR approach) is a serious weakness that undermines the paper's significance.

I recommend *weak acceptance* with major revisions. The authors should:
1. Clearly distinguish between their MCR task and full coreference resolution
2. Compare their approach directly to existing Wikipedia-specific CR work (particularly Nguyen et al., 2007)
3. Expand the dataset size or provide stronger justification for the small size
4. Provide deeper analysis of why Wikipedia-specific features work well for this task

With these revisions, the paper would make a meaningful contribution to the field. As it stands, the misalignment of the problem statement and the claimed contributions significantly weakens the paper's impact.
