

# Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper introduces a novel task called "Main Concept Resolution" for Wikipedia articles, where the goal is to identify all mentions (proper names, nominal phrases, pronouns) that refer to the main concept (the entity the article is primarily about). The authors create a new dataset (WCR) for this task, develop a classifier using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, and demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms existing coreference resolution systems on this specific task. They also show that integrating their classifier into an existing rule-based coreference system improves overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Novel Task Definition**: Introducing "Main Concept Resolution" as a specialized coreference task for Wikipedia, distinct from traditional coreference resolution. This is a bold conceptual shift that addresses a real gap in the field.

2. **Task-Specific Dataset**: Creating the WCR dataset (30 Wikipedia articles, 60k tokens, ~7000 mentions) specifically for evaluating systems on this new task, providing a valuable resource for future research.

3. **Leveraging Wikipedia's Structure**: Developing a novel approach that uses Wikipedia's unique structural features (hyperlinks, redirects, Freebase attributes) to create features that are unavailable in other text genres.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Task Concept**: This is the strongest contribution. The paper identifies a clear gap in the field (CR systems trained on newswire data perform poorly on Wikipedia) and proposes a meaningful new task that directly addresses this gap. The "main concept" focus is a bold, novel perspective that shifts the research focus from identifying all coreferences to identifying the most relevant ones for Wikipedia-based applications.

2. **Strong Motivation and Practical Value**: The paper convincingly argues why this task is valuable for Wikipedia-based NLP applications, particularly for information extraction. The authors demonstrate that identifying main concept mentions is directly useful for tasks like Open Information Extraction, and they show that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art systems by significant margins (13-15 F1 points).

3. **Concrete Resource Provision**: The authors provide a new dataset (WCR) and make their results publicly available, which is a significant practical contribution that enables future research and reproducibility.

4. **Clear Demonstration of Value**: The paper shows that their classifier not only solves the main concept resolution task but also improves full coreference resolution when integrated into an existing system (Dcoref++ improves CoNLL F1 by 4 points).

5. **Leveraging Wikipedia's Unique Structure**: The paper makes clever use of Wikipedia's inherent features (hyperlinks, redirects, Freebase attributes) to extract features that aren't available in other text genres. This is a novel way to adapt CR to a specific text genre and demonstrates deep understanding of Wikipedia's structure.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Task**: While the main concept resolution task is novel, it's significantly narrower than traditional coreference resolution. The paper doesn't convincingly argue why this specific task is more important or generalizable than the broader coreference resolution problem, which might limit the broader impact of the work.

2. **Small Dataset Size**: The WCR dataset is quite small (30 documents, 60k tokens) compared to standard coreference datasets like OntoNotes or ACE. This makes it questionable whether the results are robust or generalizable, and limits the significance of the empirical contribution.

3. **Methodological Simplicity**: The actual approach (SVM classifiers with hand-crafted features) is relatively simple, which might not be sufficiently innovative for a top-tier conference like CONLL, despite the novelty of the task.

4. **Lack of Clear Generalization Path**: The paper doesn't clearly articulate how this work could lead to broader improvements in coreference resolution beyond Wikipedia. If the main contribution is just a specialized solution for Wikipedia, it might not be sufficiently novel for CONLL.

5. **Potential Overfitting to Wikipedia's Structure**: The approach relies heavily on Wikipedia's unique structural features (hyperlinks, redirects), which might not generalize to other text genres. The paper doesn't provide evidence or discussion about how this approach might be adapted to other text types.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you plan to extend this work to address the full coreference resolution problem in Wikipedia (not just the main concept)? What challenges would this entail?

2. The paper claims that traditional CR systems perform poorly on Wikipedia, but the experiments only show performance on the WCR dataset. How do you know that WCR is representative of the broader Wikipedia text genre? Could you provide more details about the diversity of the 30 Wikipedia articles used?

3. The paper mentions that "the integration of our classifier into the state-of-the-art rule-based coreference system of Lee et al. (2013) improves the detection of coreference chains in Wikipedia articles." How much of this improvement is specifically due to the main concept resolution task, and how much would be achieved by simply using a better mention detector?

4. How would your approach scale to the full Wikipedia corpus (over 5 million articles)? What would be the computational cost of processing all Wikipedia articles with your classifier?

## Overall Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper introduces a bold, novel concept that addresses a real gap in the coreference resolution literature. While the methodology is relatively simple, the novelty of the task definition and the clear demonstration of its value for a widely used resource (Wikipedia) outweigh the methodological limitations. The paper provides a valuable new dataset and demonstrates significant improvements over existing approaches for this specific task. As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold approaches over methodological perfection, I find this contribution to be sufficiently significant for acceptance. The paper clearly identifies a new research direction that could lead to meaningful improvements in Wikipedia-based NLP applications, and the authors provide a solid foundation for future work in this area.
