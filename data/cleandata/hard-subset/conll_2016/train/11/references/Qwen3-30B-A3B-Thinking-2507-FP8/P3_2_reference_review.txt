

# Peer Review: Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses a significant gap in coreference resolution (CR) research: the poor performance of existing CR systems on Wikipedia articles, which are structurally and linguistically distinct from news-like texts that dominate current training data (ACE, OntoNotes). The authors propose a new task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions of the main concept (the concept being described in a Wikipedia article) within the article. They develop a binary classification approach that leverages Wikipedia-specific features (markup, redirects, Freebase attributes) to distinguish mentions of the main concept from other mentions. The authors evaluate their approach on a new dataset (WCR) consisting of 30 Wikipedia articles annotated with main concept mentions, and demonstrate that their classifier outperforms baselines built on state-of-the-art CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref) by 13-15 F1 points. They further integrate their classifier into Dcoref to improve overall coreference resolution performance on Wikipedia by 4 F1 points.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Introduction of the Main Concept Resolution (MCR) task** - Defining a specific, practical coreference resolution problem for Wikipedia that addresses a clear gap in the literature.
2. **Creation of the WCR dataset** - A carefully curated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles annotated with main concept mentions following OntoNotes guidelines.
3. **Binary classification approach for MCR** - A feature-rich, Wikipedia-specific classifier that leverages structural information and external knowledge sources (Freebase) to outperform generic CR systems on this specific task.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Well-Motivated Task Definition**: The paper correctly identifies a significant gap in coreference resolution research - the poor performance of existing systems on Wikipedia. The MCR task is well-motivated by practical NLP applications (e.g., information extraction from Wikipedia) and addresses a concrete problem that has been overlooked in the literature. The authors provide strong justification for why generic CR systems fail on Wikipedia and why a dedicated approach is needed.

2. **Careful Dataset Construction**: The WCR dataset is well-constructed, following OntoNotes guidelines with 30 articles covering diverse topics and characteristics. The authors demonstrate the dataset's utility by showing that state-of-the-art CR systems perform dramatically worse on WCR than on OntoNotes (Table 1). The public release of the dataset is a valuable contribution to the field.

3. **Practical Integration with Existing Systems**: The paper doesn't just propose a new task and classifier but demonstrates how their approach can be practically integrated into a state-of-the-art CR system (Dcoref) to improve overall performance by 4 F1 points. This shows the real-world utility of their work and goes beyond just creating a new evaluation benchmark.

4. **Effective Feature Engineering**: The authors demonstrate a deep understanding of Wikipedia's structure and how to leverage it for coreference resolution. The feature set is well-designed, incorporating both traditional coreference features (e.g., attributes, position) and Wikipedia-specific features (e.g., redirects, links, Freebase attributes). The incremental feature analysis (Table 2) shows careful consideration of feature importance.

5. **Strong Performance Results**: The authors' classifier achieves 88.99% F1 on pronominal mentions and 88.67% on non-pronominal mentions, representing a 13-15 point absolute improvement over the best baselines. This is a substantial improvement that clearly demonstrates the value of their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Extremely Small Dataset Size**: The WCR dataset consists of only 30 articles (60k tokens, 1,800 main concept mentions), which is severely underpowered for training and evaluating machine learning models. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't adequately address how the results might generalize to a larger set of Wikipedia articles. A dataset of this size makes it difficult to draw strong statistical conclusions about the generalizability of the approach.

2. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The paper claims to outperform "state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines," but fails to compare against the two prior works specifically designed for Wikipedia (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008). The authors dismiss these as "not fully-fledged" but don't provide a clear comparison showing how their approach is superior to these specifically designed Wikipedia CR systems. This is a significant omission.

3. **Lack of Error Analysis**: While the paper shows some examples of errors (Figure 3), it lacks a systematic analysis of error types. It would be valuable to understand what kinds of mentions are most challenging for the classifier (e.g., pronouns in complex sentences, mentions that are also common in other contexts) and why. This would help identify the limitations of the approach.

4. **Questionable Generalizability**: The approach relies heavily on Wikipedia's unique structural features (hyperlinks, redirects, Freebase integration) and is not designed to work on other text genres. The paper doesn't discuss how the approach would need to be adapted for other domains or how it would scale to other languages. This limits the broader applicability of the work.

5. **Lack of Comparison to Other Wikipedia-Specific Approaches**: The paper mentions "two notable exceptions" (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) but doesn't compare against them directly. Given that these are specifically designed for Wikipedia, their performance should be a key baseline. The paper also doesn't compare against other rule-based or knowledge-based approaches that could be adapted for Wikipedia.

## Questions to Authors

1. The WCR dataset is quite small (30 articles). How did you select these articles, and what evidence do you have that they represent a diverse enough sample of Wikipedia articles to support your conclusions?

2. You claim your approach outperforms "state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines," but don't compare to the two Wikipedia-specific works (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008). How does your approach compare to these specifically designed Wikipedia CR systems, and why are they not considered a more appropriate baseline?

3. The paper states that "the rule-based system of (Lee et al., 2013) works better than the machine-learning based systems on the WCR dataset." What do you think causes this performance difference, and is this a general pattern for Wikipedia text?

4. The integration of your classifier into Dcoref (Dcoref++) improves CoNLL F1 by 4 points. How much of this improvement is specifically attributable to your MCR component versus other factors (e.g., the overall pipeline)?

5. The paper uses Freebase as a knowledge source, but Freebase has been discontinued and replaced by Google Knowledge Graph. How would you adapt your approach to use a more current knowledge base?

## Additional Comments

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the MCR task relates to standard coreference resolution. Specifically, how does identifying main concept mentions help in the broader coreference resolution task?

2. The authors mention they'll share the dataset at "www.somewhere.country" - please provide the actual URL in the final version.

3. The paper mentions that "the rule-based system of (Lee et al., 2013) works better than the machine-learning based systems on the WCR dataset." It would be valuable to understand why this is the case, as it might reveal important characteristics of Wikipedia text that could inform future CR system design.

4. The paper doesn't address how their approach would handle Wikipedia articles with multiple main concepts or articles that are about multiple related concepts (e.g., articles about "The Beatles" that discuss individual band members). This is a common Wikipedia structure that would test the limits of the MCR task.

5. The paper's claim that their approach is "simple yet powerful" could be strengthened by providing a clearer explanation of what makes it simple (e.g., fewer parameters, less training data needed) compared to other approaches.

## Overall Recommendation

The paper addresses a significant gap in coreference resolution research and proposes a well-motivated, practical task (MCR) with a clear evaluation framework. The authors demonstrate that their approach significantly outperforms generic CR systems on Wikipedia for this specific task, and they show how it can be integrated into existing CR pipelines to improve overall performance. However, the extremely small dataset size and lack of comparison to prior Wikipedia-specific approaches are significant weaknesses that limit the paper's contribution. 

The paper would be significantly strengthened by expanding the dataset, providing a direct comparison to the two prior Wikipedia-specific CR systems, and including a more thorough error analysis. While the results are promising, the current evidence base is too limited to confidently claim a major contribution to the field.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** - The paper has a strong idea with practical value, but the methodological limitations (small dataset, inadequate baselines) prevent it from being a strong contribution. The authors should address these limitations in revision before the paper can be considered for acceptance at CONLL.
