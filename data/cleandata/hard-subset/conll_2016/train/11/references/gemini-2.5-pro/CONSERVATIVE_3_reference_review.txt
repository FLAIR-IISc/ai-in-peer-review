**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution (CR) within Wikipedia articles, arguing that existing systems trained on newswire data perform poorly on this genre. The authors define a new, specialized sub-task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions that refer to the main subject of a given Wikipedia article. To this end, they create a new, small annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR) and demonstrate the performance drop of several state-of-the-art CR systems on this data. Their proposed approach frames MCR as a binary classification problem, using two SVM classifiers (one for pronominal and one for non-pronominal mentions) with a large set of hand-crafted features derived from Wikipedia's structure and the Freebase knowledge graph. The authors show that their system significantly outperforms several baselines built on top of existing CR systems for the MCR task. Finally, they demonstrate that integrating their classifier as a high-precision sieve into the older, rule-based Dcoref system improves its overall coreference resolution performance on their WCR dataset.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the creation and annotation of the WCR dataset, a small corpus for coreference in Wikipedia. The accompanying analysis, which confirms that state-of-the-art systems struggle on this genre, serves as good motivation.
2.  A secondary contribution is a feature-engineered classification system for the specific task of identifying the main concept chain in a Wikipedia article. The system effectively leverages the unique structural and semantic information available in Wikipedia and Freebase.
3.  A tertiary contribution is an experimental result showing that this specialized classifier can be integrated into an existing rule-based CR system to improve its performance on the new dataset.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper effectively highlights a well-known but important problem: the domain-dependency of coreference resolution systems. The results in Table 1, showing a significant performance drop for multiple state-of-the-art systems when moving from OntoNotes to Wikipedia, provides a strong and clear motivation for the work.
2.  The methodology for feature engineering is sound. Leveraging the explicit, human-curated structure of Wikipedia (e.g., hyperlinks pointing to known entities) instead of relying on a potentially noisy upstream wikification or entity linking tool is a sensible and robust design choice. This allows the system to tap into high-quality semantic information from Freebase with high precision.
3.  The authors propose a set of strong baselines (B1-B4) that go beyond simple heuristics and attempt to intelligently extract the main concept chain from the output of full CR systems. This provides a solid point of comparison and makes the reported improvements of their own system more convincing within the context of their defined task. The public release of the dataset and system outputs is a commendable step towards reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Framing a sub-problem as a new task.** The central premise of a new task called "Main Concept Resolution" is questionable. This task is essentially the identification of a single, highly prominent coreference chain. This is a sub-problem of full coreference resolution, not a fundamentally new challenge. The problem could have been framed more conventionally as an improvement to full CR systems through better feature design or a specialized post-processing module. The authors' baselines are, in fact, post-processing heuristics. Their proposed method is a more sophisticated classifier performing the same function. This reframing seems to inflate the novelty of the work without fundamentally changing the underlying problem.
2.  **The dataset is critically small.** The entire evaluation rests on a newly created dataset of only 30 documents. While annotation is expensive, drawing strong, generalizable conclusions from such a small sample size is untenable. A 10-fold cross-validation on 30 documents means testing on 3 documents at a time. The reported performance gains, while large, could easily be artifacts of the specific topics or writing styles present in this small collection. The claim that the results are "representative" is not sufficiently supported by a sample of this size. This is a significant limitation that undermines the confidence in the paper's conclusions.
3.  **The proposed method lacks methodological novelty.** The core of the proposed system is a pair of SVMs with heavily engineered features. While the feature engineering is specific to Wikipedia, the overall approach is quite standard and has been common practice in NLP for over a decade. The contribution lies almost entirely in the specific features chosen, which limits the method's applicability to the Wikipedia domain and offers little in terms of generalizable techniques for coreference resolution.
4.  **The integration experiment is limited and unconvincing.** The authors show improvement by integrating their classifier into Dcoref, a deterministic, rule-based system from 2013 that is no longer state-of-the-art. It is unclear why this specific, older system was chosen over more modern, higher-performing statistical systems like Scoref. It is plausible that the rigid, high-precision nature of the new sieve simply patches a known weakness of Dcoref and that a more flexible statistical model would not benefit as much, or would require a more sophisticated integration. The experiment would be far more compelling if it demonstrated an improvement to a top-tier system.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to define "Main Concept Resolution" as a standalone task rather than addressing it as a component of a full coreference system? For instance, could the features you developed be used to improve mention-ranking or entity-centric models directly?
2.  The WCR dataset is very small. This raises concerns about the generalizability of your findings. How can we be sure that the impressive ~13 F1 point gain is not an artifact of the specific 30 articles chosen? Did you perform any analysis on held-out documents from a different topic distribution to test for robustness?
3.  Your integration experiment targets Dcoref, a system that is significantly outperformed by more recent statistical models on standard benchmarks (and, interestingly, not on your dataset). Why did you not attempt to integrate your classifier into a stronger, more recent system like Scoref? Does your approach offer a pathway to improving modern neural or statistical CR systems, or is its benefit largely confined to older, rule-based architectures?
