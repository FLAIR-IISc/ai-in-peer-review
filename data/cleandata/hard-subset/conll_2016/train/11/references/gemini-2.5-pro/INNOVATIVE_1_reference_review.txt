### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces and tackles a novel, well-defined sub-problem of coreference resolution: "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR) in Wikipedia. The task consists of identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main subject. The authors first demonstrate that state-of-the-art coreference systems, largely trained on newswire, perform poorly on this genre. They then frame MCR as a binary classification task and propose a feature-based SVM classifier that leverages the unique structural properties of Wikipedia and linked knowledge from Freebase. Finally, they show their method significantly outperforms baselines derived from SOTA systems on the MCR task and that integrating their classifier into a rule-based system improves performance on the full coreference task.

From my perspective, the primary contributions of this work are:
1.  **The novel formulation of the MCR task.** This is a bold and pragmatic simplification of full coreference resolution, isolating a high-impact sub-problem that is particularly relevant for information extraction from encyclopedic texts.
2.  **The creation and evaluation of a new dataset, WCR.** This resource effectively highlights the domain gap for existing coreference systems and provides a benchmark for future work on this genre.
3.  **A simple yet powerful approach that smartly exploits domain-specific information.** The key insight is to use Wikipedia's explicit structure (hyperlinks, redirects) as a high-quality, direct source of semantic information, thereby sidestepping the need for a separate, often error-prone entity linking pipeline.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Pragmatic Problem Formulation:** The paper's greatest strength is its decision to define and solve MCR. Instead of another incremental improvement on the general, and notoriously difficult, coreference task, the authors have carved out a new problem that is both more tractable and arguably more useful for many downstream applications that consume Wikipedia. This kind of creative reframing is exactly what the field needs. It moves beyond simply adapting existing methods to a new domain and instead asks what the most important version of the problem is *for this domain*.

2.  **Strong Empirical Justification:** The paper provides a clear and compelling motivation for its work. The results in Table 1, showing a dramatic performance drop for several SOTA coreference systems when applied to Wikipedia, are stark. This immediately establishes the relevance of the problem and justifies the need for a specialized approach. The paper doesn't just claim there is a problem; it proves it.

3.  **Elegant and Effective Use of Domain-Specific Knowledge:** The technical approach, while based on a standard classifier, is powered by very clever feature engineering. The decision to use the internal hyperlink structure of Wikipedia as a substitute for a general-purpose wikification system is particularly insightful. This leverages human-curated links as a high-precision source of entity information, avoiding the cascading errors that plague many NLP pipelines. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the data source and leads to a robust and effective solution.

4.  **A Promising Direction for Domain Adaptation:** This work serves as an excellent case study in domain adaptation. It shows that for a highly structured domain like Wikipedia, a simple model with rich, domain-specific features can outperform complex, general-purpose models. This is an important lesson and points toward a promising direction for future research: rather than building ever-larger monolithic models, we can achieve significant gains by building smaller, specialized systems that deeply exploit the unique characteristics of their target domain.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper's direction, there are some limitations that should be acknowledged. I view these as acceptable trade-offs for the novelty presented, rather than fatal flaws.

1.  **Limited Scale of Evaluation:** The primary weakness is the small size of the WCR dataset, consisting of only 30 documents. While the annotation appears to be of high quality, conclusions drawn from such a small sample may not be fully generalizable. I am willing to overlook this for a first-of-its-kind study, but a larger-scale evaluation will be necessary to solidify these findings.

2.  **Methodological Simplicity:** The use of an SVM classifier, while effective, feels somewhat dated. The work would be situated more firmly in the current NLP landscape if it included a comparison against a modern neural baseline (e.g., a fine-tuned BERT-based classifier) for the MCR task. While the feature-based approach is highly interpretable and works well, it's unclear if the handcrafted features would still be superior to learned representations. However, the paper's focus is on the novel task and data, making the choice of a classic, well-understood model acceptable.

3.  **Superficial System Integration:** The Dcoref++ experiment successfully demonstrates the utility of the MCR classifier. However, the integration itself—adding a single, high-priority sieve—is not deeply principled. It's a "bolt-on" solution that proves the concept but leaves open the question of how to best integrate such specialized knowledge into a general coreference system. This is a clear avenue for future work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The feature engineering is central to your model's success. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of feature importance? Specifically, how much of the performance comes from Wikipedia's internal structure (titles, redirects, links) versus the external knowledge from Freebase? This would be valuable for understanding the applicability of your approach to domains or languages where a resource like Freebase is less comprehensive.

2.  Given the small dataset, have you analyzed the stability of the results across the 10 cross-validation folds? Reporting the variance or standard deviation would provide a better sense of the confidence in the reported improvements.
