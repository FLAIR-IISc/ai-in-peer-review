### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the performance gap of state-of-the-art coreference resolution (CR) systems when applied to Wikipedia articles, a domain where they typically underperform compared to newswire text. The authors define a new, focused task called Main Concept Resolution (MCR): identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main concept (MC). To tackle this, they propose a supervised approach using two SVM classifiers (one for pronominal and one for non-pronominal mentions) trained on a rich set of features derived from Wikipedia's structure (links, redirects) and external knowledge from Freebase.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  **The introduction and evaluation of the Main Concept Resolution (MCR) task.** This is a practical and valuable sub-problem of full coreference resolution, and the paper provides a strong motivation and a clear problem definition.
2.  **The creation and analysis of a new annotated dataset, WCR.** The authors use this 30-document corpus not only to train and test their system but also to empirically demonstrate the performance degradation of several recent SOTA coreference systems on Wikipedia text, justifying the need for domain-specific approaches.
3.  **A strong empirical result for the MCR task.** The proposed feature-based classifier significantly outperforms several well-designed baselines built on top of SOTA systems (Dcoref, Scoref), achieving an F1 score of nearly 89%. The paper also demonstrates the utility of their MCR classifier by integrating it into a full CR system (Dcoref), leading to a notable improvement on the general CR task for this dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and clearly defined problem:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work. The experiment reported in Table 1, showing that multiple SOTA systems perform significantly worse on Wikipedia text than on OntoNotes, is a compelling piece of evidence. Framing the problem as MCR is a clever simplification that is both useful in its own right (e.g., for information extraction) and serves as a stepping stone towards full CR in this domain.

2.  **Thorough and convincing experimental setup:** The evaluation is robust. The authors don't just compare against a simple baseline; they devise four distinct and reasonable baselines based on the output of two very different SOTA systems (rule-based Dcoref and neural Scoref). The ablation study (Tables 2 and 3) provides clear insight into the contribution of each feature family, demonstrating that features derived from Wikipedia's title, structure, and type information are crucial for performance. The final experiment integrating the MCR classifier into Dcoref successfully closes the loop, showing that solving this sub-task can concretely benefit the broader CR task.

3.  **Valuable resource contribution:** The creation of the WCR dataset is a key strength. While small, annotated datasets for new domains are essential for moving the field forward. The authors' promise to release the dataset, along with a large-scale dump of automatically resolved MC mentions for all of English Wikipedia, represents a significant contribution to the community that will enable future research and replication.

4.  **Clarity and presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The approach, features, baselines, and experiments are described logically and in sufficient detail for the reader to understand the work. The examples in Figure 3 are effective at illustrating both successes and failures of the system.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited dataset size:** The primary weakness is the size of the WCR dataset, which consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, this is still a very small sample size. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results and the statistical significance of the reported improvements. The performance of the SVM classifiers, particularly with a large number of binary features (e.g., 2380 for pronoun POS bigrams), might be sensitive to the specific articles in this small dataset. The 10-fold cross-validation helps mitigate this, but the variance across folds is not reported.

2.  **Modest methodological novelty:** The core approach—an SVM classifier with engineered features—is standard. Many of the feature concepts, such as leveraging Wikipedia/Freebase attributes (gender, NER type) and string matching against titles/aliases, have been explored in previous CR research. The primary novelty lies in the direct and clever application of these ideas to the specific MCR task by exploiting Wikipedia's internal markup, thus avoiding a potentially noisy external wikification step. While the engineering is excellent and the results are strong, the work is more of a strong application paper than one introducing a new core ML technique.

3.  **Ambiguity in baseline pronoun handling:** The heuristic used to improve pronoun recall for the baselines is somewhat underspecified and potentially problematic. The paper states: "if the pronoun he is found in the chain identified by the baseline, all pronouns he in the article are considered to be mentions of the MC." This is a very aggressive heuristic. While it likely boosts recall as intended, it could also dramatically harm precision, and it's unclear how this affects the overall F1 score comparison. A more detailed analysis of the baseline performance with and without this heuristic would have made the comparison to the proposed learning-based model more direct.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the small dataset size (30 articles): Could you comment on the variance of performance you observed across the 10 cross-validation folds? Does the small dataset size lead to high instability in the results? This would help in assessing the robustness of your claims.

2.  The Dcoref++ integration is very interesting. You state that you "prevent other sieves from appending new mentions to the MC coreference chain." This seems like a hard constraint that could harm recall if your classifier makes an error. Did you experiment with a softer integration, perhaps by allowing high-precision sieves (e.g., exact string match) to add to the MC chain, or by simply using the MC chain to resolve pronoun antecedents without locking it?

3.  In your error analysis (e.g., Figure 3g), you note that the classifier confuses the location "Houston" with the team "Houston Texans." This seems like a classic metonymy or entity-type confusion problem. Do your current features have any way to distinguish between a location and an organization that contains the location's name, especially when Freebase might list both as related entities?
