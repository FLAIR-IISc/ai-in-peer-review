### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the performance gap of state-of-the-art coreference resolution (CR) systems, typically trained on newswire text, when applied to Wikipedia articles. The authors define a focused sub-task called Main Concept Resolution (MCR): identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main subject. They propose a feature-rich binary SVM classifier that leverages Wikipedia's internal structure (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked knowledge from Freebase to decide if a given mention refers to the main concept (MC). The authors introduce a new annotated dataset (WCR) of 30 Wikipedia articles to benchmark existing CR systems and evaluate their own approach. Their method significantly outperforms baselines derived from SOTA CR systems on the MCR task. Furthermore, they demonstrate that integrating their MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into a rule-based CR system (Dcoref) improves its performance on the full coreference resolution task on their dataset.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A new dataset (WCR) and benchmark for coreference resolution on Wikipedia.** The authors create and annotate a new resource, which they use to empirically demonstrate the significant performance degradation of several state-of-the-art CR systems on this genre. This is a valuable contribution that highlights the need for domain adaptation in CR.
2.  **A strong, domain-adapted model for the focused task of Main Concept Resolution.** The proposed classifier is a well-engineered solution that cleverly exploits domain-specific signals from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, avoiding the potential pitfalls of a full entity-linking pipeline. The model's high performance validates the task formulation and the feature design.
3.  **A practical demonstration of the utility of MCR for full coreference resolution.** By integrating their MCR classifier into an existing system (Dcoref++) and showing a notable improvement in CoNLL F1 score, the authors prove that solving this sub-task is not just an academic exercise but can provide tangible benefits for the broader goal of full CR in this domain.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and clearly defined problem.** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work by identifying a clear gap: the poor performance of standard CR systems on the important and ubiquitous domain of Wikipedia. The decision to narrow the scope to the MCR task is pragmatic and effective, resulting in a focused and solvable problem with clear utility for downstream tasks.
2.  **Thorough and convincing empirical evaluation.** The experimental setup is a major strength. The authors (i) create a new dataset, (ii) benchmark multiple SOTA systems to establish the problem, (iii) design several non-trivial baselines for their specific task, (iv) perform a detailed feature ablation study (Tables 2 & 3) to understand the contribution of different information sources, and (v) evaluate the downstream impact of their model on a full CR system. This comprehensive approach provides strong support for their claims.
3.  **Clever and effective use of domain-specific features.** A key insight of the paper is to directly leverage the rich, human-curated structure of Wikipedia (hyperlinks, redirects) and linked data from Freebase. This approach serves as a high-precision substitute for a separate, and often error-prone, entity linking system. The feature engineering is thoughtful, capturing lexical, semantic, and structural properties that are highly relevant to the task, as demonstrated by the ablation study.
4.  **Clarity and potential for reproducibility.** The paper is very well-written, easy to follow, and logically structured. The authors clearly describe their method, baselines, and experiments. Their commitment to releasing the WCR dataset, the extracted mentions, and associated information is commendable and will be a valuable asset to the community, enabling direct comparison and extension of this work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited scope of the main task relative to the title.** The title, "Coreference in Wikipedia," suggests a comprehensive study of the full coreference phenomenon in this domain. However, the paper's primary focus is on the MCR task, which resolves only one (albeit the most prominent) coreference chain per article. While the authors do connect this back to full CR in Section 6.4, the paper could benefit from a more explicit discussion of the limitations of this focus. For example, the paper notes that the MC chain accounts for 25% of mentions, which means the main task ignores the resolution of the other 75% of coreferring mentions.
2.  **Generalizability concerns due to dataset size and evaluation setup.** The WCR dataset, consisting of 30 articles, is relatively small. While the authors state it was sampled for diversity, this size may limit the generalizability of the findings regarding both the classifier's performance and the reported drop in SOTA system performance. More importantly, the evaluation of the Dcoref++ system in Section 6.4 uses predictions from a classifier trained via 10-fold cross-validation on the same dataset. This is a non-standard evaluation protocol that risks overestimating performance, as the model used for each fold has effectively been trained on other parts of the test set. A cleaner experiment would involve a strict train/test split of the WCR corpus to evaluate the final Dcoref++ system.
3.  **The novelty is in engineering and application, not core methodology.** The proposed method is a feature-engineered SVM, which is a standard machine learning approach. The novelty lies not in the algorithm but in the careful problem formulation, feature design, and application to the Wikipedia domain. This is not a major flaw, as the empirical contributions are significant, but it is worth noting that the paper does not introduce a new learning paradigm or model architecture for coreference resolution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the Dcoref++ experiment (Section 6.4): Could you clarify the evaluation protocol? The footnote states you use "predicted results from 10-fold cross-validation." Does this mean that for testing on each fold, the sieve's classifier was the one trained on the other 9 folds? This setup can be seen as using a different model for different parts of the test set. Would it be possible to report results using a more standard setup, for instance, by holding out a few documents as a final test set and training a single MCR classifier on the rest?
2.  The features rely heavily on Freebase, which has been deprecated and superseded by Wikidata. Could you comment on the feasibility of adapting your approach to use Wikidata? This seems crucial for the long-term viability and reproducibility of the method.
3.  In the feature description for pronominal mentions (Section 4.2.2), the `distance` feature family searches for a "compatible" entity. Could you provide a more precise definition of what constitutes an entity in this context and how "compatibility" is determined? Is it based on attribute matching (e.g., gender, number) from Freebase?
