### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution (CR) specifically for Wikipedia articles, arguing that state-of-the-art systems trained on newswire data perform poorly on this genre. The authors define a new, focused sub-task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR): identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main topic (the Main Concept, or MC). They frame this as a binary classification problem and develop a system based on two Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifiers (one for pronominal and one for non-pronominal mentions). The key novelty lies in the feature engineering, which heavily leverages the unique structural properties of Wikipedia (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked data from Freebase to gather rich semantic information about mentions without relying on a separate, potentially noisy, entity linking pipeline.

To evaluate their approach, the authors created a new, manually annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR). They first demonstrate that existing CR systems indeed suffer a significant performance drop on WCR. They then show that their specialized MCR classifier dramatically outperforms several strong baselines constructed from state-of-the-art CR systems. Finally, they integrate their classifier as a high-precision sieve into the rule-based Stanford Dcoref system, showing a significant improvement in performance on the full coreference resolution task on their dataset.

### Main Contributions
1.  **Problem Formulation and a High-Performing System for Main Concept Resolution:** The primary contribution is the clear definition and framing of the MCR task, a practical and important sub-problem for document understanding in encyclopedic contexts. The proposed SVM-based system, with its clever use of Wikipedia and Freebase-derived features, is shown to be highly effective, achieving an F1 score of nearly 89%, a substantial improvement over strong baselines.

2.  **A New Annotated Dataset and Genre-Specific Analysis:** The creation of the WCR dataset, while small, is a valuable contribution. It provides a benchmark for a new domain and, more importantly, is used to empirically validate the paper's core premise: that CR systems exhibit significant domain sensitivity. The analysis in Table 1 is a key result that motivates the entire work.

3.  **Demonstration of Broader Utility for Full Coreference Resolution:** The paper successfully demonstrates that solving the specialized MCR task is not an end in itself. By integrating their classifier into an existing CR system (Dcoref) and showing a 4-point F1 improvement on the full CR task, the authors provide compelling evidence that MCR can serve as a powerful building block for improving general-purpose systems in this domain.

### Strengths
1.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Clear Hypothesis:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It starts with the widely-accepted but often under-studied problem of domain adaptation for CR and provides immediate, concrete evidence (Table 1) of this problem on Wikipedia text, making a strong case for the subsequent research.

2.  **Intelligent and Effective Use of Domain-Specific Information:** The core technical strength is the feature engineering. The authors astutely recognize that for Wikipedia articles, the entity linking problem is often already solved by human editors via hyperlinks. By directly exploiting this structure, along with redirects and Freebase attributes, they sidestep the challenges of a pipelined approach and access high-precision semantic information. This is a very elegant solution tailored perfectly to the domain.

3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is excellent. The baselines are not strawmen; they are thoughtfully constructed from state-of-the-art systems to perform the specific MCR task. The ablation studies (Tables 2 and 3) are crucial for understanding the contribution of different feature families. Finally, evaluating the system both intrinsically on the MCR task and extrinsically on the full CR task provides a complete picture of its value.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The authors promise to release their newly created dataset and system outputs, which is a commendable practice that will benefit the community and facilitate future work in this area.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Scale and Generalizability of the Dataset:** The WCR dataset, with only 30 articles, is very small. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, this size raises concerns about the statistical significance and generalizability of the results. Wikipedia contains millions of articles of vastly different quality, length, and topic (e.g., articles on historical events vs. scientific concepts vs. biographies). It is unclear if a model trained on 30 articles would be robust across this spectrum, particularly on shorter "stub" articles that lack rich internal linking.

2.  **Dependence on a Mature Knowledge Ecosystem:** The approach's success is deeply intertwined with the rich markup of Wikipedia and the existence of a corresponding, well-populated Freebase entry for the MC. The performance on articles about less notable entities, or on topics not well-covered in Freebase, is not explored. Since Freebase is now defunct (succeeded by Wikidata), this reliance also makes the work slightly dated and raises questions about its direct applicability today without adaptation.

3.  **Limited Scope of the Integration Experiment:** The authors demonstrate the utility of their MCR classifier by integrating it into Dcoref, a rule-based sieve system from 2013. While this is a valid and interesting experiment, it would be significantly more impactful to show how this component could benefit a modern, end-to-end neural CR system (like Scoref, which they also benchmark). The sieve architecture is a natural fit, but it is not obvious how this high-precision signal would be best incorporated into a neural model (e.g., as a feature, a hard constraint, or during post-processing). This leaves the question of its relevance to the current state-of-the-art partially unanswered.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you provide more detail on the sampling strategy for the 30 documents in WCR? Specifically, what was the distribution of article topics (e.g., person, organization, location, event, abstract concept) and lengths? Given the small dataset size, this information is crucial for assessing how representative your findings are.

2.  The integration with the Dcoref sieve architecture is very natural. Do you have any insights or preliminary thoughts on how your MCR classifier could be used to improve a modern neural coreference system? For example, could the classifier's predictions be used to generate high-confidence antecedent candidates or to apply a loss penalty during training for incorrect MC classifications?

3.  Your method relies heavily on attributes from Freebase. Have you considered the implications of Freebase being discontinued? How readily could your feature extraction process be adapted to use a more current knowledge base like Wikidata, and do you foresee any challenges, for instance, with differences in the ontologies (e.g., mapping Freebase "notable types" to Wikidata properties)?
