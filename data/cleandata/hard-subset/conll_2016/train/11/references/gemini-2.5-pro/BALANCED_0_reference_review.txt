### **Summary of the paper and contributions**

This paper introduces the task of Main Concept Resolution (MCR) in Wikipedia, which consists of identifying all mentions in an article that refer to the article's main topic. The authors frame this as a binary classification task and propose a feature-rich Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifier that leverages the unique structure of Wikipedia (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked knowledge from Freebase. To facilitate this research, they create and evaluate on a new, manually annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR). The proposed method significantly outperforms several baselines derived from state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems. Finally, the authors demonstrate the utility of their MCR classifier by integrating it as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref rule-based system, showing a notable improvement on the full coreference resolution task on their WCR dataset.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A new dataset and benchmark for coreference resolution in the Wikipedia domain.** The paper introduces the WCR dataset and, importantly, benchmarks several state-of-the-art coreference systems on it. This analysis empirically confirms a significant performance drop when moving from newswire to encyclopedic text, highlighting a clear need for domain-specific research and providing a valuable resource for it.
2.  **An effective, feature-driven method for the specific sub-task of Main Concept Resolution.** The proposed classifier achieves high performance by cleverly exploiting Wikipedia-specific markup and structured knowledge from Freebase, without resorting to potentially noisy general-purpose entity linkers. The thorough ablation study provides clear insights into which information sources are most valuable for this task.
3.  **A proof of concept that solving MCR can benefit full coreference resolution.** By integrating their specialized classifier into a general-purpose system (Dcoref), the authors show a concrete pathway for improving coreference resolution in this domain, resulting in a 4-point F1 improvement on their dataset.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Strong Motivation:** The paper effectively defines and motivates the MCR task. The initial experiment (Table 1), showing that state-of-the-art coreference systems struggle on Wikipedia text, provides a compelling justification for the work. This domain-shift problem is well-known but under-resourced, and this paper makes a direct and welcome contribution to addressing it.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is a key strength. The authors compare their method not only against a zero-baseline but also against four non-trivial baselines derived from two different SOTA systems. The detailed ablation studies (Tables 2 and 3) are particularly strong, as they systematically dissect the contribution of each feature family, providing a clear picture of what makes the model work (e.g., the high impact of the `title` and `inferred type` features).
3.  **Valuable Resource Creation and Release:** The creation of the WCR dataset, while modest in size, addresses a clear gap in the community. The authors' commitment to releasing the annotated dataset, along with the large-scale dump of predicted MC mentions for all of English Wikipedia, represents a significant contribution that will enable future research in this area.
4.  **Intelligent Use of Domain-Specific Knowledge:** The feature engineering demonstrates a deep understanding of the data source. Instead of using a generic pipeline, the method directly leverages the reliable, human-curated structure of Wikipedia and its links to Freebase. This approach of tailoring the method to the domain's specific properties is a good example of effective NLP engineering.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scale and Generalizability of the Dataset:** The primary weakness is the small size of the new WCR dataset (30 documents). While the authors state they sampled for diversity, this small scale raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to the vast and heterogeneous content of Wikipedia. Performance metrics derived from a 10-fold cross-validation on such a small dataset can have high variance. This also limits the potential for exploring modern, data-intensive neural approaches, which now represent the state of the art in the field.
2.  **Dated Technical Approach:** The core of the proposed method is a feature-engineered SVM. While demonstrably effective here, this approach is technically not novel and stands in contrast to the end-to-end neural models that have dominated coreference resolution research for several years. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of how MCR could be modeled within a modern neural framework and a comparison against a simple neural baseline, even if trained on the small WCR dataset.
3.  **Reliance on a Deprecated Knowledge Base:** The system's features are heavily dependent on Freebase, which was officially shut down in 2016 and is no longer updated. This poses a significant risk to the long-term reproducibility and extensibility of the work. A more robust and forward-looking approach would have been to use Wikidata, the actively maintained successor to Freebase.
4.  **Narrow Scope of the Integration Experiment:** The MCR classifier is integrated only into the Dcoref rule-based system. While results are positive, it is unclear if this gain is specific to the sieve-based architecture or if the MCR information is more generally useful. A more compelling case could be made by showing how this information could also benefit a state-of-the-art statistical system like Scoref, for instance, by incorporating the MCR classifier's output as a feature in the mention-ranking model.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide more details on the sampling strategy for the 30 documents in WCR? Given the small size, have you analyzed the variance of your 10-fold cross-validation results to assess how stable the reported performance gains are?
2.  Your method relies heavily on Freebase, which is a deprecated resource. Have you considered using Wikidata as a more modern and actively maintained alternative? What challenges, if any, do you foresee in migrating your feature extraction pipeline to Wikidata?
3.  Your integration of the MCR classifier into Dcoref as a hard-coded first sieve is effective. Did you experiment with integrating this signal into a statistical system like Scoref, for example by using your classifier's prediction or confidence score as an additional feature for scoring candidate antecedents? This could help demonstrate the general applicability of your MCR module.
