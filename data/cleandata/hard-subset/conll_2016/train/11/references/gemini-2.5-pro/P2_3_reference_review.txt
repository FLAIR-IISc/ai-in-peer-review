### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution (CR) specifically for Wikipedia articles, a domain where standard news-trained systems are known to underperform. The authors define a focused sub-task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main subject (the "main concept" or MC).

The work frames MCR as a binary classification problem and develops two SVM classifiers (one for pronominal and one for non-pronominal mentions) using a rich set of features derived from Wikipedia's internal structure (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked knowledge bases like Freebase. The authors create and evaluate on a new, manually annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR), first demonstrating the performance drop of several state-of-the-art CR systems on this genre. Their proposed MCR system significantly outperforms strong baselines adapted from these SOTA systems. Finally, they show the utility of their MCR classifier by integrating it as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref system, improving its overall CR performance on the WCR dataset.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **The formulation and evaluation of the Main Concept Resolution (MCR) task.** This is a practical and useful sub-problem of coreference, and the paper provides a strong justification for its importance.
2.  **The creation and release of the WCR dataset.** A new, genre-specific annotated resource is a valuable contribution to the community, and the authors' benchmarking of existing systems on it clearly highlights the need for domain adaptation.
3.  **A highly effective, feature-engineered system for the MCR task.** While the underlying method (SVM) is standard, the clever exploitation of Wikipedia and Freebase structure to create high-quality features demonstrates a strong understanding of the domain and leads to impressive empirical gains.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Defined and Motivated Problem:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work. It clearly demonstrates that SOTA CR systems struggle with Wikipedia text (Table 1) and intelligently narrows the problem to the well-defined and impactful task of resolving the main concept, which constitutes a large percentage (25%) of mentions in their dataset. This focused approach is a significant strength.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental design is very strong.
    *   The creation of a new dataset (WCR) is commendable.
    *   The authors don't just compare against strawman baselines; they create four non-trivial baselines by adapting two different SOTA systems (rule-based Dcoref and neural Scoref) to the MCR task.
    *   The results show a very large and significant improvement (an absolute F1 increase of 13-15 points over the best baselines), leaving little doubt about the effectiveness of their approach.
    *   The ablation studies (Tables 2 and 3) clearly demonstrate the contribution of each feature family, providing valuable insights into what makes their system work.
    *   The inclusion of an extrinsic evaluation (Section 6.4), which shows that solving the MCR sub-task helps a full CR system, powerfully validates the utility of their work.

3.  **Clever Use of Domain-Specific Knowledge:** A key strength is the direct use of Wikipedia's markup and links to Freebase. This allows the system to obtain high-quality semantic information (entity types, gender, aliases) without relying on a separate, potentially error-prone entity linking pipeline. This is a smart and pragmatic design choice that is well-suited to the specific domain of Wikipedia.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The methodology, feature sets, and experiments are described in sufficient detail. The authors' commitment to releasing their annotated dataset and system outputs is a major plus for reproducibility and future research in this area.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core approach is feature engineering for a standard SVM classifier. While the features themselves are well-designed and effective for this specific task, the paper does not introduce a novel machine learning technique or architecture. The contribution is primarily empirical and resource-based rather than methodological.

2.  **Small Dataset Size:** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state they sampled for variety, this is still a very small number on which to base strong conclusions. The 10-fold cross-validation results could be susceptible to high variance, and it raises questions about how well the system and its learned feature weights would generalize to the broader diversity of articles on Wikipedia.

3.  **Limited Applicability Outside of Wikipedia:** The proposed method is highly tailored to the specific structure of Wikipedia and its integration with Freebase. This is a strength for the task at hand but also a significant weakness in terms of generalizability. The approach, as presented, would not be directly applicable to other text genres or even other encyclopedic websites that lack this rich, interconnected markup. The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of this limitation.

4.  **Extrinsic Evaluation is Limited to an Older System:** The authors demonstrate the utility of their classifier by integrating it into Dcoref, a rule-based system from 2013. While this shows a positive result, it would be more compelling to see if it could also benefit a modern, neural-based system like Scoref. Integrating a high-confidence classifier's output as a feature or a hard constraint into an end-to-end neural model is a more current and potentially more impactful research direction. The choice of only Dcoref feels somewhat limiting.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given that the WCR dataset contains only 30 documents, have you performed any statistical significance testing on your main results in Table 4? How confident are you that the large F1-score improvements are not an artifact of the specific 30 documents chosen?

2.  Your extrinsic evaluation in Section 6.4 focuses on integrating the MCR classifier into the rule-based Dcoref system. Could you elaborate on why Dcoref was chosen over a more recent learning-based system like Scoref? What challenges do you foresee in incorporating your classifier's output into a neural CR model, and do you believe it would yield similar or greater gains?

3.  In the ablation study for non-pronominal mentions (Table 3), you highlight the impact of the `title`, `inferred type`, `name variants`, `entity type`, and `main` feature families. Could you clarify if the `main` family here (encoding coarse attribute matches) is the same as the one used for pronominal mentions, and confirm that it was also the last feature added in your greedy selection process, leading to the final reported score?
