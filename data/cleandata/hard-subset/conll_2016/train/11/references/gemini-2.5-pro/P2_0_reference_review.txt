### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces and addresses the task of "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR) in Wikipedia, which consists of identifying all mentions in an article that refer to the article's main topic (e.g., finding all mentions of "Barack Obama" in his Wikipedia page). The authors argue that this is a valuable sub-problem of coreference resolution (CR) and that existing state-of-the-art CR systems, trained primarily on newswire, perform poorly on Wikipedia text.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **Problem Formulation and a New Dataset (WCR):** The paper clearly defines the MCR task and provides a new, annotated 30-document dataset (WCR) for Wikipedia coreference. The authors use this dataset to compellingly demonstrate a significant performance gap when applying existing SOTA CR systems to this domain, thus strongly motivating the need for their work.
2.  **A High-Performing MCR Classifier:** The authors develop a simple but highly effective SVM-based classifier for the MCR task. Its strength lies in carefully engineered features that directly leverage Wikipedia's internal structure (hyperlinks, redirects) and linked knowledge from Freebase, cleverly bypassing the need for a potentially noisy named-entity linking step. The system achieves a very large performance gain over strong baselines.
3.  **Extrinsic Evaluation Showing Broader Utility:** The authors demonstrate that solving the MCR sub-task is not just an end in itself. By integrating their classifier as a high-precision first-pass "sieve" into a well-known rule-based CR system (Dcoref), they achieve a significant improvement of over 3 absolute points in the CoNLL F1 score for the full coreference resolution task on their WCR dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Clearly Articulated Problem:** The paper does an excellent job of justifying its existence. The experiment reported in Table 1, showing that multiple SOTA coreference systems suffer a performance drop of 10-17 F1 points when moving from OntoNotes to the WCR dataset, is a powerful and convincing piece of evidence. This immediately establishes the relevance and challenge of coreference in Wikipedia.

2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is a major strength. The authors don't just compare against a weak baseline. They create four strong, well-reasoned baselines by adapting two different SOTA CR systems (rule-based Dcoref and neural Scoref) to the MCR task. The ablation studies (Tables 2 and 3) clearly demonstrate the impact of different feature families, providing valuable insights into what makes their model work. Finally, the extrinsic evaluation in Section 6.4, which shows a concrete improvement on the full CR task, elevates the paper from a niche task to one with broader implications.

3.  **Impressive Results:** The proposed system achieves an F1 score of 88.79 on the MCR task, which is an absolute improvement of more than 13 F1 points over the best-performing baseline (Dcoref B4 at 75.77). This is a very large and significant gain. The improvement shown in the full CR task (Dcoref++) is also substantial for this field.

4.  **Effective Feature Engineering:** While the machine learning model itself (SVM) is standard, the contribution lies in the intelligent feature design. The use of Wikipedia's internal hyperlinks as a high-quality substitute for named-entity linking is a smart choice that avoids cascading errors from a separate tool. The features derived from the MC's title, inferred type, redirects, and Freebase types are clearly effective and well-suited to the encyclopedic nature of the text.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Dataset Size:** The primary weakness is the small size of the new WCR dataset, which consists of only 30 articles. While the authors state they sampled for diversity in topic and size, 30 documents is a very small sample to confidently claim representativeness of all of Wikipedia. It raises concerns about whether the high performance and the specific feature importances observed will generalize across the vast range of topics and writing styles present in the encyclopedia.

2.  **Limited Novelty of the Machine Learning Method:** The core of the proposed approach is a feature-based SVM classifier. This is a very standard technique. The novelty lies entirely in the problem definition, the features, and the application, not in the machine learning methodology itself. This is a minor weakness, as the results are strong, but it's worth noting that the work does not advance the state-of-the-art in machine learning for NLP.

3.  **Comparison Is Against Unadapted Systems:** In Section 6.4, the authors improve Dcoref and compare this new system (Dcoref++) to the *original, unadapted* performance of other SOTA systems from Table 1. A more direct comparison would involve attempting to adapt another system (e.g., by fine-tuning Scoref on the WCR data) and comparing Dcoref++ to that. The current comparison is between a system specifically tuned for this data/task and systems that are used completely off-the-shelf, which slightly overstates the comparative advantage.

4.  **Integration is Specific to Sieve-Based Architecture:** The method for improving full CR is by adding a new sieve to the Dcoref system. This is a natural and effective integration for a rule-based, sieve-style architecture. However, it's not immediately clear how these MCR predictions could be integrated into modern, end-to-end neural coreference models (like Clark and Manning, 2015, or more recent ones), which do not follow a sieve structure. This limits the demonstrated generalizability of using MCR to improve full CR.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more detail on the sampling process for the 30 documents in WCR? For example, were the topics chosen from a pre-defined set of categories? Given the dataset's small size, this information would help assess how well the results might generalize.

2.  The "distance" feature for pronominal mentions seems crucial for the final performance boost. Could you elaborate on how "compatibility" between a pronoun and a candidate entity is determined using Freebase? For instance, does it check for gender/number agreement, or something more sophisticated like type compatibility (e.g., "it" cannot refer to a PER-type entity)?

3.  In your error analysis, you note that the classifier fails on difficult cases like "the expansion team" referring to the "Houston Texans". Do your features capture any lexical semantic information (e.g., from word embeddings) that might help bridge this gap, or are they purely based on surface forms, Wikipedia structure, and Freebase types? Would incorporating such information be a promising direction for future work?
