### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces and formalizes a new task called Main Concept Resolution (MCR): identifying all mentions of the main topic within a Wikipedia article. The authors argue that general-purpose coreference resolution (CR) systems, trained on newswire, perform poorly on Wikipedia. They propose a simple but powerful classification-based approach for MCR that leverages features derived from Wikipedia's rich structure and Freebase, avoiding the need for a separate entity linking pipeline. The authors create a new, annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR) to benchmark performance on this genre, showing that existing systems indeed struggle. Their proposed system dramatically outperforms baselines built from state-of-the-art CR systems on the MCR task. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of their MCR model by integrating it as a high-precision sieve into a full CR system, leading to significant gains on the general coreference task for Wikipedia articles.

From my perspective, the primary contributions are, in order of importance:

1.  **The conceptualization of Main Concept Resolution (MCR) as a distinct task.** This is a bold and insightful simplification of the full coreference problem. By isolating the most prominent and arguably most important coreference chain in encyclopedic articles, the authors define a problem that is both more tractable and highly valuable for downstream tasks. This is a novel direction for domain-specific NLP.
2.  **The creation and analysis of the WCR dataset.** Although small, this dataset is a valuable contribution. It provides a concrete benchmark for the new MCR task and, more importantly, empirically validates the long-suspected domain adaptation problem for SOTA coreference systems, highlighting the need for genre-specific research.
3.  **A highly effective, feature-driven model for MCR.** The proposed SVM classifier, while methodologically simple, serves as a powerful proof of concept. The authors show that by cleverly exploiting domain-specific signals (like hyperlinks and redirects) instead of a generic NLP pipeline, one can achieve very high performance on the task.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and High-Impact Task Formulation:** The paper's greatest strength is its bold reframing of the problem. Instead of another incremental improvement on a general benchmark, the authors carve out a new, well-defined, and practical sub-problem. MCR is an elegant idea. In a document type like a Wikipedia article, which is by definition focused on a single entity, resolving the main concept's mentions is the most critical part of coreference. This fresh perspective is exactly the kind of work the field needs.
2.  **Clever and Efficient Use of Domain Knowledge:** The approach smartly sidesteps the complexity and cascading errors of general-purpose entity linking by using Wikipedia's internal hyperlinks as a source of high-precision, human-annotated supervision. This is an excellent example of exploiting the "solved" parts of a problem (human-created links) to bootstrap a solution for a harder one. The feature engineering is thoughtful and directly tied to the unique characteristics of the domain.
3.  **Convincing Empirical Demonstration:** The results are striking. An F1 score of nearly 89% on the MCR task, representing a +13-15 point absolute improvement over strong baselines, is a resounding success. This isn't a minor bump; it's a demonstration that a specialized approach is vastly superior. The ablation study clearly attributes this success to the Wikipedia- and Freebase-derived features, reinforcing the paper's core thesis.
4.  **Demonstrated Utility for the General Task:** The authors close the loop by showing their MCR system isn't just a one-trick pony. Integrating it into the Dcoref system as a high-precision sieve yields a 4-point F1 improvement on the full coreference task on WCR. This is a significant gain and powerfully illustrates how solving a core sub-problem can benefit the entire system.

### Weaknesses

While I am enthusiastic about this paper, there are areas that could be strengthened. I view these as minor in the face of the conceptual novelty, but they are worth noting.

1.  **Extremely Small Dataset:** The WCR dataset, with only 30 documents, is the most significant limitation. While the authors state care was taken in sampling, it is difficult to be certain that the results will generalize across the vast diversity of Wikipedia. The stability of the 10-fold cross-validation on such a small sample is a concern. While this doesn't invalidate the core idea, a larger-scale validation would have made the empirical claims unassailable.
2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The choice of a linear SVM with engineered features, while effective, is methodologically conservative. The paper does not engage with modern neural architectures that have become standard for coreference resolution and related tasks. While I value the new task definition far more than the use of a fashionable model, the lack of comparison to a neural baseline leaves open the question of whether an end-to-end model could learn the relevant features automatically.
3.  **Superficial Integration into the Full CR System:** The integration of the MCR classifier into Dcoref is promising but feels somewhat preliminary. The decision to make the MC chain immutable after its creation by the new sieve is a hard constraint that isn't deeply explored or justified. It's possible that a "softer" integration, where the classifier's predictions provide strong features or priors for the general system rather than an irrevocable decision, might yield even better results.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The small dataset size is my main concern. Can you comment on the variance of your results across the 10 folds of cross-validation? This might give a better sense of the stability of the reported performance metrics.
2.  In the Dcoref++ system, you prevent other sieves from adding mentions to the MC chain. What was the reasoning behind this design choice? Did you experiment with a version where subsequent sieves (e.g., for pronoun resolution) could still modify the MC chain, and if so, how did it affect precision and recall?
3.  Your feature extraction relies heavily on explicit hyperlinks within Wikipedia and corresponding Freebase entries. How do you expect your system to perform on mentions of the MC that are not hyperlinked (e.g., in less link-dense sections of an article) or on articles for more obscure "long-tail" entities that may lack rich Freebase entries or numerous redirects?
