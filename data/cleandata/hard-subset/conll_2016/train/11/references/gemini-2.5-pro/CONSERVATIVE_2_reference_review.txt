**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the performance gap of state-of-the-art (SOTA) coreference resolution (CR) systems when applied to Wikipedia articles, a domain where they are known to underperform compared to their native newswire text. The authors propose a simplified, focused sub-task called Main Concept Resolution (MCR): identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main subject. They frame this as a binary classification problem and develop a Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifier that uses a rich set of hand-crafted features derived from Wikipedia's markup and the Freebase knowledge base. The authors create a new annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR) to evaluate their approach. Their classifier is shown to significantly outperform several heuristic-based baselines built on top of SOTA CR systems (Dcoref, Scoref) for the MCR task. Finally, they demonstrate that integrating their classifier as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref system yields a modest improvement on the full coreference resolution task for their dataset.

**Contributions of this work**
1.  **Dataset and Empirical Analysis:** The primary contribution is the creation of a new annotated dataset for coreference on Wikipedia (WCR) and a thorough empirical analysis demonstrating the significant performance degradation of multiple SOTA CR systems on this genre. This provides concrete evidence and a benchmark for a known problem.
2.  **A Strong Baseline for Main Concept Resolution:** The authors formulate the MCR sub-task and develop a strong, feature-engineered classifier for it. While the method itself (SVM) is standard, the careful feature engineering results in a high-performing system for this specific task, establishing a solid baseline for future work.
3.  **Integration into a Full Coreference System:** The paper demonstrates a practical application of the MCR classifier by integrating it into an established rule-based CR system (Lee et al., 2013), showing a tangible, albeit moderate, improvement in overall CR performance on their dataset.

**Strengths**
1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper is well-motivated by the documented performance drop of CR systems on non-newswire text. The analysis in Table 1 provides clear, quantitative evidence of this problem on the Wikipedia domain, justifying the need for domain-specific adaptation.
2.  **Methodologically Sound Experiments:** The authors design their experiments carefully. The baselines (B1-B4) are non-trivial and represent sensible heuristics for adapting existing CR systems to the MCR task, making the performance gains of the proposed classifier more meaningful. The feature ablation studies in Tables 2 and 3 provide useful insights into the relative importance of the different feature families.
3.  **Creation of a New Resource:** The WCR dataset, though small, is a valuable contribution to the community. Such resources are essential for encouraging research into genre adaptation for core NLP tasks. By promising to release the dataset and annotations, the authors enable reproducibility and further research.
4.  **Strong Performance on the Defined Task:** The proposed classifier achieves very high performance (88.79 F1) on the MCR task, demonstrating the effectiveness of leveraging domain-specific structural information (Wikipedia markup) and external knowledge (Freebase). This is a convincing result for the narrowly defined problem.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core of the proposed method is a linear SVM with heavily hand-crafted features. This is a very established machine learning technique. While the feature engineering is clever and effective for this specific task, the approach lacks the methodological innovation expected of a top-tier publication. It is more an application of existing methods to a new domain rather than a fundamental advance in coreference resolution.
2.  **Scope of the Task:** The paper's main focus is on the MCR task, which is a significant simplification of the full coreference resolution problem. An article contains many coreference chains, but this work focuses exclusively on identifying the single chain corresponding to the main concept. The claim that this improves full CR is supported by only one experiment integrating the classifier into a relatively dated, rule-based system (Dcoref, 2013). It is unclear if a similar benefit would be observed in a modern, end-to-end neural system. This simplification risks overstating the broader impact on the general CR problem.
3.  **Very Small and Potentially Fragile Evaluation:** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, conclusions drawn from such a small dataset, especially regarding the 4-point F1 improvement on the full CR task, are preliminary at best. The performance gains could be an artifact of the specific 30 articles chosen and may not generalize to Wikipedia at large. A more extensive evaluation would be needed to make a strong claim about improving full coreference resolution on Wikipedia.
4.  **Reliance on a Deprecated Knowledge Source:** The system's features rely heavily on Freebase, a knowledge graph that is no longer actively maintained. While a data dump exists, this reliance dates the work and raises questions about the long-term viability and reproducibility of the approach. Modern knowledge-intensive systems are more commonly built upon actively maintained resources like Wikidata.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The MCR task simplifies the coreference problem to a single entity per document. Could you elaborate on why this is a sufficient contribution on its own, rather than a preliminary step towards a more comprehensive, knowledge-aware coreference resolver for Wikipedia that handles all entities?
2.  The main experiment on the full CR task shows a 4-point F1 gain for Dcoref++. Given that the evaluation is on a small set of 30 documents, what gives you confidence that this improvement is statistically significant and not an artifact of the specific test set? Have you performed any significance testing?
3.  Your method is tightly coupled to the specific structure of Wikipedia and the (now static) Freebase knowledge base. How do you see this approach generalizing beyond Wikipedia to other domains that might have a "main concept," such as research papers or legal documents, but lack the same rich, structured markup?
