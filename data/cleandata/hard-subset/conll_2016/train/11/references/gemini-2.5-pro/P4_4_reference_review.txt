### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution (CR) specifically within Wikipedia articles, arguing that systems trained on newswire data perform poorly on this genre. The authors define and focus on a sub-problem they term "Main Concept Resolution": identifying all mentions in a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main subject (the "main concept" or MC). To facilitate this research, they introduce a new annotated dataset, WCR, consisting of 30 Wikipedia articles. They propose a feature-based Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifier that leverages the unique structural and semantic information available in Wikipedia markup and the Freebase knowledge base to decide if a given mention refers to the MC. The paper benchmarks several state-of-the-art CR systems on WCR, demonstrating a significant performance drop compared to standard datasets. Their proposed classifier is shown to substantially outperform baselines derived from these SOTA systems on the Main Concept Resolution task. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of their approach by integrating it as a high-precision sieve into a rule-based CR system (Dcoref), leading to improved performance on the full coreference task on their dataset.

### Contributions

1.  **A new dataset (WCR) and benchmark for coreference resolution on Wikipedia.** The paper introduces a novel, annotated dataset that highlights the genre-shift problem for CR systems. By evaluating several state-of-the-art systems on WCR, it provides a clear benchmark and empirical evidence that existing models struggle with Wikipedia text, justifying the need for domain-specific adaptation.
2.  **An effective, feature-engineered model for Main Concept Resolution.** The authors present a strong supervised baseline for the defined sub-task. The model's strength lies in its careful feature engineering, which directly exploits Wikipedia's hyperlink structure and Freebase attributes, bypassing the need for a potentially noisy named-entity linking pipeline. The reported performance of nearly 89% F1-score establishes a high standard for this task.
3.  **Demonstration of improving a general CR system by solving a targeted sub-task.** The paper shows that a specialized classifier for the main concept can be successfully integrated into a broader, rule-based CR system (Dcoref). The resulting system, Dcoref++, shows a notable improvement in overall CoNLL F1 score, validating the practical utility of their focused approach for the general CR task in this domain.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper provides a compelling rationale for its work, grounded in the widely-recognized problem of domain adaptation for NLP models. The performance degradation of SOTA systems on the new WCR dataset (Table 1) provides strong empirical justification. The formulation of the "Main Concept Resolution" task is a logical and practical simplification of the full CR problem, which is well-suited for a targeted investigation.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The authors first establish the problem by benchmarking multiple recent CR systems on their new dataset. For their own task, they create four non-trivial baselines based on two different SOTA systems. The evaluation of their own model includes a detailed greedy feature analysis (Tables 2 and 3), which provides clear insight into the contribution of different information sources. The experiments are well-documented and the results strongly support the claims.
3.  **Technically Sound and Effective Approach:** The proposed method is well-designed for the specific characteristics of the data. By leveraging Wikipedia's internal hyperlinks and structured data from Freebase, the model uses high-quality, human-curated information, which is a key reason for its strong performance. The decision to separate pronominal and non-pronominal mentions into two classifiers with distinct feature sets is well-justified and likely contributes to the model's accuracy.
4.  **Demonstrated Practical Impact:** The final experiment integrating the MC classifier into Dcoref (Section 6.4) elevates the paper beyond a study of an isolated sub-task. By showing a 3.3 point absolute improvement in the CoNLL F1 score on the full CR task, the authors demonstrate that their specialized model can serve as a valuable component in a larger NLP pipeline, making the work more impactful.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core of the proposed method is a feature-based SVM, a standard machine learning technique. While the feature engineering is skillful, the underlying methodology lacks novelty compared to the learning-based, neural approaches that were becoming state-of-the-art at the time of the cited systems (e.g., Clark and Manning, 2015). The paper does not explore or compare against a more modern neural baseline for the MC resolution task itself.
2.  **Small Dataset Size:** The WCR dataset, with only 30 documents, is very small. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, this size raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings. The performance of their own classifier could be overestimated due to the potential for overfitting on the specific characteristics of these 30 articles. Similarly, the conclusions about the poor performance of SOTA systems might not hold across a larger, more representative sample of Wikipedia.
3.  **Narrow Scope of CR System Integration:** The integration experiment was performed only with Dcoref, a deterministic, sieve-based system. It is not clear how the proposed MC classifier could be incorporated into an end-to-end learning-based system like Scoref, or whether it would yield similar improvements. This limits the generality of the claim that the approach can improve full CR systems, as the proposed integration method is architecture-specific.
4.  **Heavy Reliance on Now-Defunct Resources:** The approach is heavily dependent on the specific structure of Wikipedia and, critically, on Freebase. Freebase was shut down in 2016, which poses a significant challenge for reproducibility and future work building on this method. While the authors promise to release a data dump, this makes the approach less sustainable and portable compared to methods that rely on more readily available resources or learn representations from text alone.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given the small size of the WCR dataset (30 articles), can you provide more detail on the sampling methodology used to select the articles? What steps were taken to ensure diversity in topic, length, and structure, and how confident are you that the reported performance metrics (for both the SOTA systems and your own) would generalize to Wikipedia at large?
2.  The integration of your MC classifier into the sieve-based Dcoref system is straightforward. Could you elaborate on how you envision this classifier being integrated into a modern, end-to-end neural coreference system? Would it be used to generate features for mention-pair scoring, as a post-processing step to merge clusters, or in some other capacity?
3.  The choice of a feature-based SVM is a departure from the trend towards neural models in NLP. Was a neural architecture considered for the MC resolution task? If so, what were the anticipated challenges (e.g., data scarcity, feature integration) that led to the selection of the SVM?
