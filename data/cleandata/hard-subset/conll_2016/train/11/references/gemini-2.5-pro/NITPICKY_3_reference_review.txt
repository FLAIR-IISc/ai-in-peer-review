**Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR) in Wikipedia, defined as identifying all mentions within an article that refer to the article's main topic. The authors first demonstrate that state-of-the-art coreference resolution (CR) systems, largely trained on newswire, perform poorly on Wikipedia text. They introduce a new, albeit small, annotated dataset called WCR for this purpose. The primary contribution is a binary classification system that leverages features derived from Wikipedia's markup and Freebase to decide if a given mention refers to the main concept. This system is shown to significantly outperform several strong baselines built from existing CR systems. As a secondary contribution, the authors integrate their MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref rule-based system, demonstrating a notable improvement on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia articles.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1.  The creation and analysis of a new annotated dataset, WCR, which effectively highlights the domain adaptation problem for coreference resolution systems in the Wikipedia genre.
2.  An effective, feature-engineered classification model for the sub-task of Main Concept Resolution that intelligently exploits the inherent structure of Wikipedia to bypass the need for a separate entity linking pipeline.
3.  A demonstration that a specialized component for the main concept can be successfully integrated to improve a general-purpose coreference system.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Motivation and Analysis:** The paper is well-motivated. The initial experiment presented in Table 1 clearly and effectively demonstrates that modern, high-performing coreference systems suffer a significant performance degradation when applied to Wikipedia text. This provides a strong justification for developing genre-specific approaches. The observation that a rule-based system (Dcoref) outperforms more recent learning-based systems on this dataset is itself an interesting finding.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed MCR classifier achieves an F1 score of 88.79, which is a substantial absolute improvement of over 13 points compared to the best-performing baseline on non-pronominal mentions. The ablation studies in Table 2 provide a clear picture of the utility of different feature families. Furthermore, the 4-point absolute F1 gain in the full coreference task (Dcoref++) is a significant result and validates the approach of using the MCR component to guide a larger system.

3.  **Methodological Soundness of the Approach:** The core idea of leveraging Wikipedia's internal hyperlink structure and Freebase data directly, rather than relying on a potentially noisy upstream wikification tool, is sound and well-suited to the specific context of the task. The design of the features, drawing from string similarity, document structure, and external knowledge, is comprehensive.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Detail:** The paper suffers from a persistent lack of detail that hinders reproducibility and thorough evaluation.
    *   **Feature Engineering:** The description of features is often vague. For non-pronominal mentions (Section 4.2.1), the "10 boolean features" for string similarity are simply listed as "(exact match, partial match... etc.)". This is insufficient. For pronominal mentions, the `distance` feature searches for a "compatible" entity, but the definition of compatibility is not provided. The POS features for pronouns result in "2380 binary features", a highly specific number with no explanation of how it was derived.
    *   **Data Processing:** The "inferred type" from the first sentence (Section 4.1) is a key feature, yet the heuristic for its extraction is not described. Baseline B4 (Section 3) excludes "grammatical words", but no list or method for identifying these is given.

2.  **Dataset Concerns:** The new WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the creation of any new resource is commendable, this is an exceptionally small scale on which to draw broad conclusions. The authors state they "paid attention to sample Wikipedia articles of various characteristics," but without a detailed description of the sampling strategy, the claim that the results are "representative" is unsubstantiated. The small size also raises concerns about the generalizability of the trained classifiers and the feature engineering, which may be over-tuned to this specific set of documents.

3.  **Experimental Rigor and Presentation:**
    *   A critical piece of evidence, **Table 3**, is referenced in the text but is missing from the paper. This is a major oversight that leaves the non-pronominal feature ablation study completely undocumented.
    *   The justification for only showing baselines from Dcoref and Scoref is that "they use the same pipeline" (Section 5). This is factually incorrect; the Stanford CoreNLP pipeline used by Dcoref (2013) differs from the one used by Clark and Manning (2015).
    *   Footnote 6, describing the Dcoref++ experiment, is alarmingly ambiguous: "We use predicted results from 10-fold cross-validation." This could imply that for a given test fold, the classifier's predictions were generated by a model that was trained on data from that fold at some point. This must be clarified to ensure there was no information leakage.
    *   The paper has numerous minor but distracting errors: "systems ... performs poorly" (Section 1), "Queen s daughter" (missing apostrophe, Section 1), inconsistent formatting of "Schäfer" (Section 1), and a malformed URL for the CoNLL scorer (`http://conll.github.io/` is not the correct link, Section 5). An accumulation of such minor flaws detracts from the overall quality and suggests a lack of careful preparation.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the missing Table 3, which details the ablation study for the non-pronominal mention classifier?
2.  Please clarify the experimental procedure for the Dcoref++ system (Section 6.4). Specifically, for each of the 10 folds, was the MCR classifier used as a sieve trained *exclusively* on the other nine folds, with no information whatsoever from the fold currently being used for testing?
3.  Can you provide more explicit details on your feature extraction? For instance, what are the 10 boolean string comparison features, and what is the rule-based heuristic used to extract the "inferred type" of the main concept from the article's first sentence?
