### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the performance gap of standard coreference resolution (CR) systems when applied to Wikipedia articles, a genre different from the newswire text they are typically trained on. The authors define and tackle a sub-task called Main Concept Resolution (MCR): identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main topic. They formulate this as a binary classification problem and propose a system based on two Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifiers (one for pronominal mentions, one for non-pronominal) that use a rich set of features derived from Wikipedia's internal markup and the Freebase knowledge graph. To evaluate their approach, the authors create a new, manually annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR). Experiments show that their system significantly outperforms baselines constructed from state-of-the-art CR systems on the MCR task. Furthermore, they demonstrate that integrating their MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into a rule-based CR system improves its performance on the full coreference resolution task on their Wikipedia dataset.

In my assessment, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **The introduction and annotation of the WCR dataset.** This new resource for coreference resolution in Wikipedia is a valuable contribution. The authors use it effectively to quantify the domain adaptation problem for several state-of-the-art systems, providing a strong motivation for their work and a benchmark for future research.
2.  **An effective, feature-driven method for Main Concept Resolution.** The proposed classification system is shown to be highly accurate for its specific task, achieving an F1 score of nearly 89%. The strength of the method lies in its well-designed features that directly leverage the rich, structured information within Wikipedia and Freebase, avoiding the cascading errors common in systems that rely on separate, upstream entity linking components.
3.  **A demonstration of the utility of MCR for improving full coreference resolution.** By integrating the specialized MCR classifier into the Dcoref system and showing a significant 4-point absolute improvement in CoNLL F1 score, the paper validates that solving this sub-problem is beneficial for the broader goal of full CR in the Wikipedia domain.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong motivation and problem formulation.** The paper begins by clearly establishing the central problem: state-of-the-art CR systems underperform on Wikipedia text. The results in Table 1 provide compelling quantitative evidence for this claim. The decision to isolate and solve the MCR sub-task is a well-reasoned approach to tackling a key aspect of coreference in this encyclopedic genre.
2.  **Technically sound and effective methodology.** The proposed solution, while based on a standard SVM classifier, is powerful due to its clever feature engineering. The features are well-described, intuitive, and make excellent use of the reliable structural information present in Wikipedia (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked data from Freebase. The results in Table 4, showing a 13-15 point F1 improvement over strong baselines, are a clear testament to the method's effectiveness.
3.  **Thorough experimental evaluation and analysis.** The authors are diligent in their evaluation. They devise several non-trivial baselines from two different SOTA systems (Dcoref and Scoref). The greedy feature analysis presented in Tables 2 and 3 provides valuable insight into the relative importance of different information sources. The inclusion of concrete examples of correct and incorrect classifications in Figure 3 further enhances the reader's understanding.
4.  **High potential for impact and reproducibility.** The work is presented with clarity, and the methodology appears straightforward to reimplement. The authors' stated intention to release the WCR dataset and the extracted MCR data for all of English Wikipedia is a major strength, as it will enable direct replication and foster follow-up research in this area.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited novelty of the machine learning method.** The core technical approach is a feature-engineered SVM, which is a well-established technique. The novelty lies primarily in the task definition and the specific application of features to the Wikipedia domain, rather than in a new learning algorithm or model architecture. While effective, the work does not advance the state-of-the-art in CR methodology itself.
2.  **Small scale of the evaluation dataset.** The newly created WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state that care was taken to sample diverse articles, this small size raises concerns about the generalizability of the reported results. Performance metrics derived from a 30-document corpus, even with cross-validation, may not be fully representative of performance on Wikipedia at large. A larger-scale evaluation would substantially strengthen the paper's conclusions.
3.  **Dependency on a deprecated knowledge base.** The system relies on Freebase for extracting crucial semantic attributes (e.g., entity types, gender). As Freebase is no longer maintained and has been effectively superseded by Wikidata, this dependency poses a risk to the long-term viability and reproducibility of the work. The paper would be stronger if it addressed this issue, perhaps by discussing the feasibility of migrating to Wikidata.
4.  **Narrow scope of the integration experiment.** The authors demonstrate the utility of their MCR classifier by integrating it into a single, rule-based CR system (Dcoref). It remains unclear how this approach would benefit more recent, learning-based systems (like Scoref) whose architectures are not based on a sieve pipeline. A successful integration into a statistical or neural system would provide more compelling evidence for the general applicability of the MCR module.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the WCR dataset, could you provide more details on the sampling strategy used to select the 30 articles? Specifically, what is the distribution of article topics (e.g., persons, organizations, locations, events) and lengths? This information would help address concerns about the dataset's diversity and representativeness.
2.  Your feature extraction relies on Freebase. Have you considered using Wikidata as an alternative knowledge source? Do you foresee any significant challenges or advantages in adapting your feature set to a different knowledge base?
3.  The integration of your MCR classifier into Dcoref is framed as adding a high-precision sieve. Could you elaborate on the potential challenges of integrating this module into an end-to-end statistical or neural CR system, such as Scoref, which does not follow a sieve-based architecture?
