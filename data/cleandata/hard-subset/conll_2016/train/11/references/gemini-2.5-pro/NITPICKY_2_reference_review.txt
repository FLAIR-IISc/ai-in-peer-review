### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Coreference Resolution (CR) within Wikipedia articles, focusing on a specific sub-problem the authors term "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR): identifying all mentions that refer to the article's main topic. The authors' primary contributions are:

1.  **A Supervised Approach for MCR:** The core contribution is a binary classification system that determines whether a given mention refers to the main concept. The system uses two SVM models (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) trained on features derived from Wikipedia's internal structure (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and Freebase, thereby avoiding a separate, potentially noisy, entity linking step.
2.  **A New Wikipedia-Specific Dataset and Benchmark:** The paper introduces a new, albeit small, annotated dataset called WCR. It uses this dataset to demonstrate that several state-of-the-art coreference systems, primarily trained on newswire, suffer a significant performance drop on Wikipedia text, motivating the need for domain-specific solutions.
3.  **Integration into a Full Coreference System:** The authors show that their specialized MCR classifier can be integrated as a high-precision sieve into an existing rule-based system (Lee et al., 2013), leading to improved performance on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia articles.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper compellingly argues for the need for genre-adapted coreference resolution. The results in Table 1, showing a dramatic performance decrease for state-of-the-art systems on the WCR dataset, provide strong evidence for this claim. The decision to focus on the well-defined and practically useful sub-task of Main Concept Resolution is sensible.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed approach significantly outperforms a series of thoughtfully designed baselines built upon two state-of-the-art systems (Table 4). An absolute F1-score improvement of over 13 points is substantial. Furthermore, the successful integration into the Dcoref system (Dcoref++ in Table 5) demonstrates the utility of the MCR component for the general CR task, achieving a 4-point F1 gain over the original system.

3.  **Effective Use of Domain-Specific Information:** A key strength of the methodology is its direct exploitation of Wikipedia's and Freebase's rich, structured information. By leveraging human-created hyperlinks to assign attributes to mentions, the system sidesteps the need for a separate wikification tool, which can introduce cascading errors. This is an elegant and effective design choice for this particular domain.

### **Weaknesses**

While the paper presents a promising direction, it is marred by a significant number of methodological ambiguities, missing details, and presentation flaws that undermine its scientific rigor and reproducibility.

1.  **Crucial Lack of Methodological Detail:** Several key components of the feature extraction process are described too vaguely to be replicated.
    *   **String Similarity Features:** Section 4.2.1 states that a comparison between a main concept field and a mention span yields "10 boolean features" encoding similarities like "exact match, partial match... etc." The "etc." is unacceptable; these 10 features must be explicitly defined.
    *   **Inferred Type Extraction:** The main concept is represented in part by an "inferred type" which is a "common noun inferred from the first sentence of the article." The heuristic for this inference is completely missing. How is the correct common noun selected if multiple exist?
    *   **Pronoun Compatibility:** The `distance` feature for pronouns relies on finding a "compatible" entity according to Freebase information. The definition of "compatible" is not provided. Does this mean matching gender, number, NER type, or some other combination of attributes?
    *   **Baseline Substring Matching:** Footnote 1 for baseline B4 states "Grammatical words are not considered for matching." The list of these "grammatical words" (i.e., the stoplist) is not provided.

2.  **Dataset and Evaluation Concerns:**
    *   The WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the authors claim care was taken to sample diverse articles, this is an exceptionally small sample size from which to draw general conclusions about performance on Wikipedia as a whole.
    *   The evaluation of Dcoref++ (Section 6.4) is potentially flawed. Footnote 6 states, "We use predicted results from 10-fold cross-validation." This is ambiguous. If the classifier used in the sieve for a given fold was trained on the entire dataset (including that fold's test set), this constitutes data leakage and would invalidate the results. The methodology must guarantee that for each fold, the classifier is trained only on the other nine.
    *   In Section 6.2, the authors mention tuning the SVM configuration on a "heldout dataset." This seems to contradict the 10-fold cross-validation setup described for the main results. This heldout set is not described and its relationship to the 30-document WCR corpus is unclear.

3.  **Numerous Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish that is unbecoming of a top-tier conference submission.
    *   **Missing Table:** The text in Section 6.3 explicitly discusses the results for the non-pronominal mentions ablation study with reference to "Table 3," but this table is entirely absent from the paper.
    *   **Improper Citations:** Several citations are provided as bare URLs in footnotes (e.g., Jena2, CoNLL scorer) rather than being properly formatted entries in the references section.
    *   **Anonymization Hindering Review:** While necessary, the anonymization of the WCR dataset makes it impossible to assess a key contribution: the dataset itself. Without details on the annotation guidelines, process, and inter-annotator agreement, the quality of this resource cannot be verified.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the Dcoref++ experiment in Section 6.4: For the evaluation of fold `i`, was the MCR classifier used within the new sieve trained exclusively on the other nine folds? Or was a single classifier trained on all 10 folds used for all tests?
2.  Please explicitly list the 10 boolean string-matching features used in the `title`, `inferred type`, etc. feature families.
3.  Could you please detail the heuristic used to extract the "inferred type" from the first sentence of an article? What happens in cases of ambiguity?
4.  How is "compatibility" defined for the pronoun `distance` feature in Section 4.2.2?
5.  Table 3, detailing the ablation study for non-pronominal mentions, is referenced but missing. Could you please provide it?
