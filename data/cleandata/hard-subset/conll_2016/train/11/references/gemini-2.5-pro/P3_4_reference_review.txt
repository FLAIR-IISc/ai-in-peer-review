### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the performance gap of standard coreference resolution (CR) systems when applied to Wikipedia text. The authors define a specific, tractable sub-task: Main Concept Resolution (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions in a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main topic (the "Main Concept"). They frame this as a binary classification task and train two SVM classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using carefully engineered features derived from Wikipedia's internal structure (hyperlinks, redirects) and the Freebase knowledge graph. The authors introduce a new annotated dataset, WCR, to evaluate this task. Their proposed classifier significantly outperforms several strong baselines built upon state-of-the-art CR systems. Finally, they demonstrate that integrating their MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref system improves its overall coreference performance on the WCR dataset.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A new dataset (WCR) and a well-defined task (MCR)** that highlight the genre-specific challenges of coreference resolution in Wikipedia. This provides a valuable benchmark for future work in this area.
2.  **An effective and pragmatic method** for MCR that smartly leverages the unique, explicit structure of Wikipedia and linked knowledge bases to circumvent the need for a noisy, general-purpose entity linking pipeline.
3.  **A thorough empirical analysis** that not only demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach on the MCR task but also shows its practical utility by improving a full, state-of-the-art CR system.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and impactful problem.** The paper provides clear evidence (Table 1) that state-of-the-art CR systems, largely trained on newswire, struggle with Wikipedia text. Focusing on the Main Concept is a practical simplification that is highly relevant for downstream tasks like information extraction and knowledge base population from Wikipedia, where identifying the primary subject is crucial.
2.  **Clever use of domain-specific information.** The core strength of the approach is its direct exploitation of Wikipedia's structure. By using hyperlinks to map mentions to Freebase entities and leveraging redirects and article titles as strong signals, the authors create a high-precision alternative to general-purpose entity linking. This is a well-justified design choice that avoids the cascading errors common in pipelined systems. The feature engineering is detailed and appears very effective, as shown by the ablation studies (Tables 2 & 3).
3.  **Comprehensive and rigorous evaluation.** The experimental setup is strong. The authors first establish the problem by benchmarking multiple SOTA systems on their new WCR dataset. They then devise four non-trivial baselines for the MCR task, providing a solid point of comparison for their own model. The final experiment, which integrates the MCR classifier back into a full CR system (Dcoref++), successfully closes the loop and demonstrates the broader utility of their work beyond the isolated MCR task.
4.  **Clarity and potential for reproducibility.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology, features, and experiments are described in sufficient detail. The authors' commitment to releasing the annotated dataset and other resources is a major plus and will be a valuable contribution to the community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited dataset size.** The primary weakness is the small scale of the WCR dataset, which consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, results from a 10-fold cross-validation on such a small corpus may not be fully stable or representative of the vast heterogeneity of Wikipedia. This raises concerns about the generalizability of both the performance drop of SOTA systems and the high performance of the proposed classifier.
2.  **Reliance on a defunct knowledge base.** The system's features are heavily dependent on Freebase, which is no longer actively maintained. While a data dump is available, this reliance dates the work and limits its future applicability and extensibility. A discussion of the challenges or feasibility of migrating the feature extraction to a modern, actively maintained knowledge graph like Wikidata would have significantly strengthened the paper's long-term relevance.
3.  **Limited integration experiment.** The authors show that their MCR classifier can be integrated into the rule-based, sieve architecture of Dcoref. This is a strong result. However, it is unclear how this method could benefit more modern, end-to-end neural CR systems (like Scoref), which lack a similar modular architecture. As the field moves towards these models, demonstrating a viable integration strategy (e.g., using MCR predictions as features or constraints) would broaden the impact of this work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the sampling methodology used to select the 30 articles for the WCR dataset? Given the small size, what steps were taken to ensure topical and structural diversity, and how confident are you that the reported performance metrics will generalize to a much larger, random sample of Wikipedia articles?
2.  The integration into Dcoref via a new top-level sieve is very effective. Do you have any thoughts on how your MCR classifier could be integrated into an end-to-end neural CR model like Scoref? Would it be possible to use its predictions as, for example, a feature for mention-pair scoring or as a hard constraint during beam search decoding?
3.  Given that Freebase is now deprecated, have you considered migrating your feature extraction process to Wikidata? Do you foresee any major challenges, such as differences in the type system or API, that would complicate this transition?
