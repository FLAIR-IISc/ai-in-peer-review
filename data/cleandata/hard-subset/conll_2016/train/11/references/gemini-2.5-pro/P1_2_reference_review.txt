### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR) in Wikipedia articles: identifying all mentions of the concept that the article is about. The authors frame this as a binary classification task and develop a feature-rich SVM classifier that leverages Wikipedia's internal structure and linked data from Freebase. They create a new annotated dataset, WCR, to evaluate their approach and demonstrate that existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) coreference systems, trained on newswire, perform poorly on this domain. The paper shows that their specialized classifier significantly outperforms baselines built from these SOTA systems. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of their classifier by integrating it into the Dcoref system, resulting in improved performance on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia articles.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:

1.  **The creation and analysis of the WCR dataset.** The paper provides compelling evidence (Table 1) that SOTA coreference systems suffer a significant performance drop when moving from newswire (OntoNotes) to encyclopedia text (Wikipedia). This highlights an important domain adaptation problem and motivates the need for specialized approaches. The dataset itself, though small, is a valuable resource for future work.
2.  **An effective, specialized classifier for Main Concept Resolution.** The proposed system achieves a very large performance improvement (+13 F1 points) over the best-performing baseline on the MCR task. The feature engineering is thoughtful, making clever use of Wikipedia's unique structure (redirects, internal links) to avoid the need for a separate, potentially noisy wikification pipeline.
3.  **A successful demonstration of integrating a specialized component into a general system.** By incorporating their MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref system, the authors show a tangible improvement (+3.3 CoNLL F1) on the general coreference task for Wikipedia text. This validates the utility of solving the MCR sub-problem first.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated problem and strong results.** The paper clearly articulates a practical and important sub-problem within coreference resolution for a specific, high-value domain (Wikipedia). The central hypothesis—that a specialized system leveraging domain-specific features can outperform generic SOTA systems—is convincingly proven. The performance gains shown in Table 4 are substantial and leave little doubt about the effectiveness of the proposed approach for the MCR task.
2.  **Thorough analysis and ablation studies.** The authors do an excellent job of dissecting their system's performance. The feature ablation studies (Tables 2 and 3) provide clear insight into the contribution of each feature family. This analysis demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem and shows, for example, the critical importance of matching against the article `title` and the utility of the `inferred type` from the article's first sentence.
3.  **Thoughtful baseline construction.** Rather than simply comparing against generic coreference system outputs, the authors devise four non-trivial baselines (B1-B4) that attempt to adapt the output of SOTA systems to the MCR task. These baselines are sensible and serve to make the comparison with the proposed approach much stronger and more meaningful. The inclusion of both a rule-based (Dcoref) and a statistical (Scoref) system for baselines is also a good choice.
4.  **Contribution of a new resource.** The WCR dataset and the authors' promise to release it, along with the system's predictions over all of English Wikipedia, represent a significant contribution to the community. The analysis in Table 1, showing the domain gap, is an important finding in its own right and provides a benchmark for future work on coreference in Wikipedia.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited dataset size and generalizability concerns.** The primary weakness is that the entire evaluation is conducted on a small dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles. While the authors state that they sampled for diversity, this is a very small number from which to draw broad conclusions. The stability of the 10-fold cross-validation results could be questioned, and it's unclear how well the findings would generalize to the full diversity of articles on Wikipedia. The impressive performance gains might be less pronounced on a larger, more varied test set.
2.  **Methodological novelty is limited.** The core of the approach is a well-established technique: an SVM with carefully engineered features. While the feature engineering is clever and domain-specific, the machine learning methodology itself is not novel. The paper's contribution lies more in the problem definition, application, and engineering rather than in advancing the fundamental techniques of coreference resolution.
3.  **Integration experiment could be broader.** The authors show a solid improvement by integrating their classifier into Dcoref. However, Dcoref is a deterministic, rule-based system from 2013. It would have been more compelling to see if similar gains could be achieved by integrating it into a more recent and powerful statistical system like Scoref, which is also used for baselines. It is possible that the gains are specific to the sieve-based architecture of Dcoref and would be harder to realize in a different paradigm. This choice slightly limits the conclusion about the general utility of the MCR module.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the WCR dataset: Given the small size of 30 documents, have you performed any statistical significance tests on your main results in Tables 4 and 5 to confirm that the observed improvements are significant? Additionally, could you report the inter-annotator agreement for the WCR annotations to provide a measure of the dataset's quality and the task's difficulty?
2.  Regarding the Dcoref++ experiment: Your classifier provides a probability or confidence score for a mention referring to the MC. You use this in a hard-coded first sieve. Have you considered using these scores as features in a statistical coreference model (like Scoref or the Berkeley systems) instead of integrating it as a deterministic rule? This might be a more natural way to incorporate the information into a non-sieve-based system and might yield different results.
3.  The `distance` feature for pronominal mentions sounds interesting but is described quite briefly. It searches for a "compatible" entity. Could you please clarify the criteria for compatibility? For example, does it simply involve checking for agreement on attributes like gender and number extracted from Freebase?
