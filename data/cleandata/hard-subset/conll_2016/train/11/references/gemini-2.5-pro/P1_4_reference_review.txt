### Summary

This paper addresses the poor performance of standard coreference resolution (CR) systems on Wikipedia text. Instead of tackling full CR, the authors define a simpler, practical sub-task: Main Concept (MC) Resolution, which is to identify all mentions in a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's subject. They frame this as a binary classification problem and develop a feature-based SVM classifier that leverages Wikipedia's internal structure (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked data from Freebase. The authors create a new annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR) to evaluate their approach. Their proposed system significantly outperforms several strong baselines derived from state-of-the-art CR systems on the MC resolution task. Furthermore, they demonstrate that integrating their classifier as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref rule-based system improves its performance on the full CR task on their dataset.

### Contributions

1.  **Task Formulation and Benchmark Creation:** The primary contribution is the formalization of the "Main Concept Resolution" task. This is a well-motivated and practical simplification of full coreference resolution for the Wikipedia domain. To support this, the authors have created a new annotated dataset (WCR) and developed a set of strong, non-trivial baselines by adapting full CR systems to this specific task, providing a solid foundation for future work.
2.  **An Effective, Domain-Specific Classifier:** The paper presents a simple yet highly effective classifier for MC resolution. The novelty lies not in the algorithm (SVM) but in the carefully engineered features that exploit the unique structure of Wikipedia and its connection to knowledge bases like Freebase. By using internal hyperlinks as a substitute for a separate, potentially noisy named-entity linking step, the approach achieves high performance with a robust methodology.
3.  **Demonstrated Utility for Full Coreference Resolution:** The authors show that solving this sub-task can directly benefit the more general problem of coreference resolution. By integrating their MC classifier into the Dcoref system, they achieve a significant 4-point F1 improvement on their WCR dataset, demonstrating the practical value of their focused approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Task:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the need for domain-specific coreference resolution, citing the performance drop of SOTA systems on Wikipedia text (Table 1). The defined MC resolution task is practical and has clear applications for downstream tasks like Information Extraction, where resolving the main subject of an article is often the most critical step.
2.  **Strong and Creative Baselines:** The authors did not resort to simple strawman baselines. The B1-B4 baselines, derived from both a rule-based (Dcoref) and a neural (Scoref) system, are thoughtful attempts to adapt general-purpose systems to the specific task. This makes the comparison in Table 4 much more compelling and highlights the strength of their specialized approach.
3.  **Intelligent Use of Domain-Specific Information:** A key strength of the proposed method is its circumvention of a full-blown named-entity linking (NEL) pipeline. By leveraging the human-curated hyperlinks within Wikipedia to connect mentions to Freebase entities, the system benefits from high-precision semantic information while avoiding the cascading errors common in traditional NEL pipelines. This is a clever and effective design choice.
4.  **Thorough Experimental Analysis:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The ablation study (Tables 2 and 3) provides clear insight into the contribution of each feature family. The error analysis (Figure 3) is illustrative and helps contextualize the system's performance. Finally, testing the classifier's utility within a full CR system (Table 5) closes the loop and demonstrates broader impact.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Very Small and Potentially Unrepresentative Dataset:** The most significant weakness is the size of the WCR dataset, consisting of only 30 documents. While the authors state they sampled for variety, 30 articles is insufficient to make strong claims about performance across the entirety of Wikipedia. It is difficult to assess whether the reported results, based on 10-fold cross-validation on this small set, will generalize. The high performance of the rule-based Dcoref system compared to more recent neural models might also be an artifact of this small, specific dataset.
2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty and Comparison:** The core method is a feature-engineered SVM, which is a well-established technique. While the features are novel for this specific task, the paper lacks a comparison to more modern, neural approaches for the MC resolution task itself. For instance, a simple BiLSTM classifier using word embeddings alongside their proposed features could have served as a powerful contemporary baseline. The SOTA systems are only compared via the constructed baselines or on the full CR task, not trained directly for the MC resolution task.
3.  **Integration Is Limited to an Older, Rule-Based System:** The authors demonstrate the utility of their classifier by integrating it into Dcoref (Lee et al., 2013). While Dcoref performs best on their WCR dataset, it is a deterministic, rule-based system that is no longer state-of-the-art on standard benchmarks. It would be more impactful to show that this approach can also improve a modern neural coreference system like that of Clark and Manning (2015) or more recent ones. The sieve-based architecture of Dcoref may be uniquely suited for this kind of integration, a point that is not discussed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the WCR dataset: Could you provide more statistics to substantiate the claim that the 30 documents are representative? For example, what is the distribution of article topics (person, location, organization, event, etc.), lengths, or link densities compared to a random sample from Wikipedia? Given the small size, have you considered how sensitive your results are to the specific 30 documents chosen?
2.  The experimental setup for the Dcoref++ system in Section 6.4 needs clarification. The footnote says, "We use predicted results from 10-fold cross-validation." To avoid data leakage, does this mean that for each of the 10 test folds, you used a classifier trained only on the corresponding 9 training folds? Please confirm this procedure.
3.  Why was a feature-based SVM chosen over a simple neural baseline for the classification task? Given the availability of powerful pretrained embeddings (e.g., BERT), a neural model could potentially capture nuances that are missed by the handcrafted features and would provide a more modern point of comparison. Did you experiment with any such models?
