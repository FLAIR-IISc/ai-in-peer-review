### Review

**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the task of "Main Concept Resolution" in Wikipedia articles, which consists of identifying all mentions that refer to the article's main topic. The authors frame this as a binary classification problem and propose a system based on two Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using a rich set of features derived from Wikipedia's structure, Freebase, and linguistic analysis. The paper introduces a new annotated dataset, WCR, on which they first demonstrate that state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems underperform compared to their performance on newswire text. They then show that their proposed classifier significantly outperforms several baselines built upon these state-of-the-art systems. Finally, they integrate their classifier as a high-precision sieve into the Stanford Dcoref system, showing an improvement in performance on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia articles.

**Contributions**

1.  **Dataset and Analysis:** The primary contribution is the creation of the WCR dataset and the accompanying analysis showing a significant performance degradation of established coreference resolution systems when applied to the Wikipedia genre. This highlights a clear need for domain adaptation.
2.  **Specialized Classifier:** The paper presents a methodologically sound, feature-engineered SVM-based system for the specific task of identifying the main concept's coreference chain. The system achieves strong empirical results on this task, demonstrating the value of leveraging Wikipedia-specific and knowledge base features directly.
3.  **System Integration:** The authors provide a proof-of-concept demonstrating that their specialized classifier can be integrated into a general-purpose, rule-based coreference system to improve its performance on Wikipedia text.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Problem Motivation and Analysis:** The paper effectively establishes that state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems, predominantly trained on newswire, are not well-suited for the Wikipedia genre. The results in Table 1 provide compelling evidence for this domain gap and motivate the need for specialized approaches. This analysis is a valuable contribution in itself.
2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The proposed approach is grounded in established machine learning techniques (SVMs) and extensive feature engineering. The feature sets are well-motivated, drawing sensibly from the unique structural properties of Wikipedia (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and external knowledge bases (Freebase). The ablation studies (Tables 2 and 3) are thorough and provide clear insight into the contribution of each feature family. This is a solid, rigorous piece of engineering.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results on the Defined Task:** The proposed classifier achieves a very high F1 score of 88.79 on the main concept resolution task, representing a substantial improvement of over 13 F1 points compared to the strongest baseline. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed features for this specific problem.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty and Framing of the Task:** The core task, "Main Concept Resolution," is essentially a simplified sub-problem of full coreference resolution: identifying the most prominent coreference chain. While useful, framing this as a new, standalone task seems slightly overstated. Its primary value appears to be as an intermediate step for improving a full CR system, rather than a fundamental task in its own right. The paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this more directly and focused on the contribution as a component for domain adaptation.
2.  **Very Small Scale of Evaluation:** The WCR dataset, consisting of only 30 documents, is extremely small. While the authors claim to have sampled for diversity, this size raises significant concerns about the generalizability of the findings. Performance on such a small test set may not be representative of the entirety of Wikipedia, and the reported improvements could be sensitive to the specific articles chosen. A more convincing evaluation would require a substantially larger dataset, even if it had to be created through semi-supervised or distant-supervision methods.
3.  **Limited Integration and Comparison:** The integration experiment, while showing positive results, is limited to a single, dated, rule-based system (Lee et al., 2013). It is not clear whether similar gains would be achieved if the classifier were integrated into a modern, statistical system like Scoref (Clark and Manning, 2015). Statistical models may already implicitly learn some of the signals captured by the authors' feature set, potentially leading to smaller gains. By only modifying Dcoref, the claim of improving state-of-the-art coreference resolution is not fully substantiated, as Dcoref is outperformed by several other systems on standard benchmarks.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the framing of the task: Could you elaborate on the rationale for treating "Main Concept Resolution" as a distinct task, rather than a component focused on improving the identification of the primary entity cluster within a standard coreference resolution framework?
2.  The WCR dataset is quite small. How confident are you that the strong performance of your classifier and the observed performance gap of SOTA systems would hold on a much larger and more diverse sample of Wikipedia articles? Have you considered methods to create a larger, silver-standard corpus to test the robustness of your approach?
3.  The integration experiment was performed only on the rule-based Dcoref system. Why was a more modern, statistical system like Scoref not chosen for this experiment? What challenges do you foresee in integrating your classifier's output (either as hard constraints or as features) into such a system, and would you expect a similar magnitude of improvement?
