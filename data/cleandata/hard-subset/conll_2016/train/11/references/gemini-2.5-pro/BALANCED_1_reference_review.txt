**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the challenge of coreference resolution (CR) on Wikipedia text, a domain where standard newswire-trained systems perform poorly. Instead of tackling the full CR task directly, the authors define and focus on a sub-problem: "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which is the task of identifying all mentions in a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main concept (MC). The authors frame this as a binary classification problem, training separate SVM classifiers for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. The key to their approach is a rich set of features engineered to exploit the unique structure of Wikipedia (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked knowledge from Freebase, thereby avoiding a noisy entity-linking preprocessing step. The authors introduce a new annotated dataset, WCR, to evaluate their approach. They show that their method significantly outperforms several strong baselines derived from state-of-the-art CR systems. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of their MCR classifier by integrating it as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref system, leading to improved performance on the full CR task on their dataset.

**Main Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is a well-engineered and highly effective classification-based method for Main Concept Resolution in Wikipedia. The system achieves a high F1 score of nearly 89% by leveraging domain-specific features from Wikipedia's structure and Freebase, demonstrating a successful strategy for adapting CR to this specific domain and task.
2.  A valuable secondary contribution is the creation of the WCR dataset. The paper's analysis on this dataset compellingly demonstrates the performance gap of existing state-of-the-art CR systems when applied to Wikipedia, motivating the need for domain-specific approaches and providing a new benchmark for the community.
3.  The paper successfully demonstrates that solving the MCR sub-task can be beneficial for the general CR task on Wikipedia. By integrating their MCR classifier into an existing rule-based system (Dcoref), they achieve a notable improvement of 4 absolute F1 points on the full CR task, highlighting the practical utility of their focused approach.

**Strengths**

1.  **Clear Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work. It clearly shows the performance drop of SOTA CR systems on Wikipedia (Table 1) and intelligently scopes the problem down to the tractable and useful task of Main Concept Resolution. This focus allows for the development of a specialized, high-performance solution that has clear applications in downstream tasks like information extraction from Wikipedia.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Thorough Evaluation:** The proposed method substantially outperforms all baselines, achieving an absolute F1 improvement of over 13 points compared to the best-performing baseline. The evaluation is rigorous, using 10-fold cross-validation and comparing against four sensible, non-trivial baselines built upon two different SOTA systems (Dcoref and Scoref). The ablation study (Tables 2 & 3) provides clear insight into the contribution of each feature family.
3.  **Effective Use of Domain-Specific Knowledge:** A key strength is the clever feature engineering that directly leverages the rich structural information within Wikipedia and linked data from Freebase. By using internal hyperlinks as a substitute for a separate entity linking step, the system avoids a common source of pipeline errors. The features, such as those derived from the MC's title, redirects, and Freebase types, are well-conceived and proven effective by the ablation analysis.
4.  **Valuable Resource Contribution:** The introduction of the WCR dataset is a significant contribution. Furthermore, the authors' commitment to releasing the dataset, their code, and the system's output on all of English Wikipedia will be a great asset to the community, enabling reproducibility and fostering further research in this area.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Methodology:** While the feature engineering is excellent, the core technical approach relies on a standard SVM classifier. The novelty is concentrated in the task definition and feature set rather than the machine learning method itself. Many of the feature concepts (e.g., using Wikipedia/Freebase attributes for entity properties) are inspired by prior work, as the authors acknowledge.
2.  **Small and Potentially Unrepresentative Dataset:** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state they sampled for variety, this is a very small number for drawing broad conclusions about performance on a resource as vast and diverse as Wikipedia. This raises concerns about how well the results and the utility of certain features would generalize to articles with different topics, structures, or lengths. The high performance might be partially attributable to the specific characteristics of this small test set.
3.  **Baselines Are Indirect Comparisons:** The baselines are heuristics applied post-hoc to the output of full CR systems. This is a reasonable starting point, but a more direct and arguably stronger baseline would be to train a classifier on the same task using only generic CR features (e.g., string match, distance, grammatical roles) without the Wikipedia/Freebase-specific knowledge. This would more precisely isolate and quantify the benefit of the authors' key contribution: the domain-specific features.
4.  **Simplistic Integration for Full CR Task:** The integration into Dcoref as a first, hard-coded sieve is a good proof-of-concept, but it feels somewhat preliminary. The decision to "prevent other sieves from appending new mentions to the MC coreference chain" is a very strong constraint that could potentially harm recall by disallowing valid merges later in the pipeline. It would strengthen the paper to discuss the rationale for this design choice and whether alternative, softer integration strategies were considered.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the WCR dataset size: Could you provide more details on the sampling strategy used to select the 30 articles? Given the small size, have you analyzed the variance of your 10-fold cross-validation results to assess the stability of the reported improvements?
2.  Regarding the integration with Dcoref: You impose a hard constraint preventing subsequent sieves from adding mentions to the MC chain. What was the motivation for this? Did you experiment with a more flexible integration, for example, by allowing certain high-precision sieves (like Exact String Match) to modify the MC chain, or by using the classifier's confidence score to inform merge decisions?
3.  The `base` feature family for non-pronominal mentions includes mention frequency. In Table 3, this simple feature set is outperformed by several baselines. However, it seems like a simple heuristic (e.g., "any mention that exactly matches the article title or a known redirect is part of the MC") could be a very strong baseline on its own. How does such a simple rule-based approach compare to your `+base` and `+title` models?
