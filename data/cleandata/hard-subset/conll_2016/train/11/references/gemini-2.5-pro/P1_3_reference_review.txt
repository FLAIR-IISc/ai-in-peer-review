### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of coreference resolution on Wikipedia text, a domain where systems trained on newswire data perform poorly. Instead of tackling full coreference, the authors define and focus on a sub-task they term "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR): identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main concept (MC). The proposed approach frames this as a binary classification problem, training two separate SVM classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using a rich set of features derived from Wikipedia's structure (markup, links, redirects) and linked knowledge bases like Freebase. The authors introduce a new annotated dataset, WCR, to evaluate this task. They demonstrate that their specialized classifier significantly outperforms baselines built from state-of-the-art coreference systems. Finally, they show that integrating their MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref system improves its performance on the full coreference resolution task on their WCR dataset.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Introduction and Formalization of the Main Concept Resolution Task:** The paper clearly defines a practical and useful sub-problem of coreference resolution specific to encyclopedic, topic-focused documents like Wikipedia articles. This is accompanied by the creation of a new annotated dataset (WCR), which, despite its small size, effectively demonstrates the domain gap for existing coreference systems.
2.  **A Highly Effective, Feature-Engineered Classifier for the Task:** The authors present a simple but powerful classification model that intelligently leverages the unique structural and semantic information available in Wikipedia and Freebase. The method's strength lies in directly using this rich, human-curated information, bypassing the need for a potentially noisy upstream entity linking step. The empirical results, showing a >13 F1 point improvement over the best baseline, are very strong.
3.  **Demonstration of Broader Utility:** The authors successfully show that solving this specific sub-task can benefit the general coreference resolution problem. By integrating their classifier into the Dcoref system, they achieve a notable 3.4-point F1 improvement on the full task, providing a compelling argument for the utility of their approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and Clearly Defined Problem:** The paper starts with a strong motivation by empirically showing (Table 1) that several state-of-the-art coreference systems suffer a significant performance drop when applied to Wikipedia text. The decision to narrow the focus to the Main Concept is a pragmatic and impactful choice, as this is arguably the most important entity chain in a Wikipedia article for downstream tasks like information extraction.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Thorough Evaluation:** The proposed method substantially outperforms all four baselines derived from two strong coreference systems (Dcoref and Scoref), achieving an F1 score of 88.8% on the MCR task (Table 4). The authors provide a detailed ablation study (Tables 2 and 3) that clearly demonstrates the contribution of each feature family, adding credibility to their feature engineering efforts. The inclusion of multiple standard coreference metrics (MUC, B3, CEAF) in the final experiment adds to the evaluation's thoroughness.
3.  **Intelligent Use of Domain-Specific Knowledge:** A key strength of the approach is its direct exploitation of Wikipedia's internal structure and linked data from Freebase. By using hyperlinks to identify entities and their properties, the system accesses high-quality semantic information without the cascading errors typical of separate NER and entity linking pipelines. This is a simple but elegant design choice that is perfectly suited to the specific domain.
4.  **Analysis and Release of a New Resource:** The creation and analysis of the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution in itself. It provides a new, albeit small, benchmark for a non-newswire domain. The authors' promise to release the dataset, annotations, and extracted data will be beneficial for future research in domain adaptation for coreference resolution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Size of the Evaluation Dataset:** The primary weakness of this study is the small size of the WCR dataset, which consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state that they sampled for diversity, conclusions drawn from such a small dataset may not be robust or generalize to the vast variety of articles on Wikipedia. The 10-fold cross-validation mitigates this to some extent, but a larger-scale evaluation would be necessary to truly validate the findings.
2.  **Limited Integration into a Modern Coreference System:** The authors demonstrate the utility of their MCR classifier by integrating it into Dcoref, a deterministic, rule-based system from 2013. While this shows a positive result, it is not representative of the current state of the art, which is dominated by end-to-end neural models (like the co-author's own Scoref system). It is unclear how this high-precision sieve would interact with a statistical, learning-based system. Demonstrating a similar improvement on a more modern architecture would have made the contribution significantly stronger.
3.  **Novelty is in Engineering, not Method:** The core methodology—an SVM with carefully engineered features—is not technically novel. The contribution lies in the specific problem formulation and the clever feature engineering for the Wikipedia domain. While this is a valid and valuable contribution, the paper could be strengthened by discussing why this classical approach was chosen over a more modern neural approach and what challenges the latter might face.
4.  **Restrictive Sieve Integration:** In the Dcoref++ experiment, the new MC sieve not only merges chains but also "prevents other sieves from appending new mentions to the MC coreference chain." This seems overly restrictive and could potentially harm recall by blocking a subsequent, lower-precision sieve from correctly identifying a difficult MC mention that the SVM classifier missed. An analysis of the trade-offs of this decision would be beneficial.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you comment on the robustness of your results given the small size of the WCR dataset (30 documents)? Do you have any analysis (e.g., performance variance across folds or topics) that could speak to the generalizability of your findings?
2.  The integration with the rule-based Dcoref system showed impressive gains. Could you elaborate on the potential challenges or strategies for integrating your MC classifier into a modern end-to-end neural coreference system? Would you expect a similar boost in performance?
3.  Regarding the Dcoref++ experiment, could you justify the decision to completely lock the MC cluster after your sieve runs? Have you experimented with a less restrictive approach that would allow later sieves to add mentions to the MC chain, and if so, how did it perform?
