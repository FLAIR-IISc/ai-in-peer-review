### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of "Main Concept Resolution," a specialized sub-problem of coreference resolution (CR) focused on identifying all mentions of the main topic within a Wikipedia article. The authors argue that standard CR systems, trained on newswire, perform poorly on Wikipedia. They propose a supervised binary classification approach that leverages the unique structural and semantic information available in Wikipedia and its connection to knowledge bases like Freebase. The classifier is trained to determine if a given mention refers to the article's Main Concept (MC). The paper evaluates this approach on a new, small, annotated corpus of Wikipedia articles (WCR), showing that it significantly outperforms baselines derived from state-of-the-art general-purpose CR systems. Furthermore, they demonstrate the utility of their classifier by integrating it as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref system, achieving a new state-of-the-art on their dataset for the full CR task.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  A high-performing, feature-rich supervised model for the specific and practical task of Main Concept Resolution in Wikipedia. The primary novelty lies in the effective feature engineering that directly exploits Wikipedia markup and linked data (Freebase) to bypass the need for a potentially noisy entity linking step.
2.  The creation of a new annotated dataset, WCR, which, despite its small size, provides compelling evidence for the domain adaptation problem in coreference resolution and serves as a valuable benchmark for this new task.
3.  A demonstration of the downstream utility of the specialized MC resolver by successfully integrating it into a general CR system (Dcoref), significantly improving its performance on Wikipedia text and outperforming other state-of-the-art systems on the WCR dataset.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated Problem and Strong Empirical Results:** The paper clearly defines a practical and useful sub-problem of CR. The motivation—that general CR systems fail on specific genres like Wikipedia—is well-established and convincingly demonstrated in Table 1. The results are very strong: the proposed model achieves an F1 of 88.8%, an absolute improvement of over 13 points compared to the best-performing baseline on the MC resolution task. The subsequent 4-point F1 improvement on the full CR task after integration into Dcoref is also significant and demonstrates the practical value of the work.

2.  **Elegant and Effective Use of Domain-Specific Knowledge:** The core strength of the methodology is its direct and intelligent use of the rich structure of Wikipedia. By using internal hyperlinks to link mentions to Freebase entities, the authors create a high-precision substitute for a full wikification pipeline, avoiding cascading errors. The feature set is comprehensive and well-designed, capturing lexical, semantic, and structural properties of both the mentions and the main concept. The ablation study in Tables 2 and 3 effectively demonstrates the contribution of each feature family.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Evaluation:** The authors have conducted a rigorous evaluation. They created four sensible baselines that adapt two different state-of-the-art CR systems (the rule-based Dcoref and the neural Scoref) to the MC resolution task. The analysis is detailed, including separate evaluations for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions, and provides qualitative examples of successes and failures (Figure 3), which adds depth to the quantitative results. The comparison of various SOTA systems on the new WCR dataset (Table 1) is itself an interesting finding, especially the observation that the older, rule-based Dcoref outperforms newer neural models on this domain.

4.  **Potential for Broader Impact:** The promise to release the WCR dataset, the trained models, and a large-scale dump of resolved MC mentions for the entirety of English Wikipedia is a major contribution. This resource could be immensely valuable for downstream tasks like relation extraction, knowledge base population, and document summarization, which often center around the main entity of a document.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Very Small Dataset Size:** The primary weakness of this study is the size of the WCR dataset, which consists of only 30 Wikipedia articles. While the authors state that they sampled for variety, it is difficult to be confident that results on such a small dataset will generalize to the vast and diverse content of Wikipedia. Performance metrics, especially from a 10-fold cross-validation, can have high variance on a dataset of this size. This limitation tempers the claims of achieving state-of-the-art performance for coreference on Wikipedia in general.

2.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The approach is a straightforward application of a standard classifier (SVM) with carefully engineered features. While the feature engineering is excellent, the work does not propose a new learning architecture or fundamental CR methodology. It frames the problem as an independent binary classification for each mention, which ignores the rich, global, cluster-level information that modern CR systems exploit.

3.  **Simple Integration Strategy with Limited Generality:** The integration into Dcoref is done by adding a new "first sieve." This is a natural fit for Dcoref's architecture but it is not obvious how this method would be applied to modern, end-to-end neural CR systems (like Scoref) that do not use a sieve pipeline. The paper shows that Dcoref++ outperforms Scoref, but this may be because the proposed method is particularly well-suited to the Dcoref architecture. A more general approach would be to incorporate the classifier's output as a feature or a soft constraint within any CR model.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the dataset size: Could you comment on the stability of the 10-fold cross-validation results? For instance, what was the standard deviation of the F1 scores across the folds? This would help gauge the robustness of the results given the small dataset.

2.  The integration with Dcoref is clever and effective. Have you considered how the output of your MC classifier could be integrated into a neural, end-to-end system like Scoref? For example, could the classifier’s probability score for a mention being an MC be used as an additional feature for mention pair scoring in such a model?

3.  In the Dcoref++ system, you state you "prevent other sieves from appending new mentions to the MC coreference chain." Does this also prevent other sieves from *merging* the MC chain with another, distinct chain? This seems like a very strong constraint that could potentially harm recall if, for example, a high-precision anaphora resolution sieve later finds an unambiguous link between a pronoun and a non-MC mention that is already part of the MC cluster. Could you clarify the exact mechanism?
