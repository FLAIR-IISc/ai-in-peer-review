### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of Main Concept Resolution (MCR), which it defines as identifying all mentions in a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main topic (the "main concept"). The authors argue that this is a valuable sub-problem of coreference resolution and that general-purpose coreference systems, typically trained on newswire, perform poorly on Wikipedia text.

The core of their proposed method is a pair of SVM classifiers (one for pronominal mentions, one for non-pronominal) that use a rich set of features derived from Wikipedia's structure (hyperlinks, redirects, title) and the Freebase knowledge graph. They demonstrate that their purpose-built classifier significantly outperforms several strong baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference systems (Stanford's Dcoref and Scoref). Finally, they show that integrating their classifier as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref system improves its performance on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia articles.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A strong, feature-engineered model for the specific and well-motivated task of Main Concept Resolution.** The paper provides a simple yet highly effective solution that leverages domain-specific information, achieving a substantial performance gain (over 13 F1 points) over well-designed baselines.
2.  **The creation and analysis of a new annotated dataset for coreference on Wikipedia (WCR).** Although small, the dataset is a valuable contribution, and the authors' analysis effectively demonstrates the performance gap of existing SOTA systems when moving from newswire to encyclopedic text, justifying the need for this work.
3.  **A demonstration that solving the MCR sub-task can directly improve a full, general-purpose coreference system.** The Dcoref++ experiment provides a compelling use case, showing that the proposed MCR classifier is not just an isolated tool but can be integrated to yield significant gains (4 points in CoNLL F1) on the standard coreference task in this domain.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the problem. It clearly establishes that coreference systems suffer a domain-shift problem when applied to Wikipedia (Table 1) and defines a practical and useful sub-problem (MCR). This is a well-scoped and well-argued piece of research.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The proposed approach dramatically outperforms all baselines on the MCR task (Table 4), with an F1 score of 88.79 compared to the best baseline's 75.77. This large margin of victory strongly supports the authors' claims about the effectiveness of their domain-specific features. The improvement shown in the full coreference task (Table 5) is also significant and demonstrates the practical utility of the work.
3.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is strong. The authors don't just compare against a strawman; they devise four sensible and increasingly complex baselines built upon two different state-of-the-art systems (one rule-based, one statistical). The inclusion of a detailed feature ablation study (Tables 2 and 3) provides valuable insight into which aspects of their model contribute most to its success.
4.  **Clever Use of Domain-Specific Knowledge:** A key strength of the paper is its intelligent feature engineering. Instead of relying on a potentially noisy entity linking pipeline, the authors directly exploit the human-curated hyperlink structure of Wikipedia to gather high-quality semantic attributes. The features derived from the article title, redirects, and Freebase types are well-conceived and proven effective by the ablation study.
5.  **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The authors state their intention to release the WCR dataset, the experimental code, and a large-scale dump of resolved main concept mentions for all of English Wikipedia. This is a commendable practice that would significantly increase the impact of the work and benefit the research community.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** The core method is a linear SVM with carefully engineered features. While the feature engineering is excellent, the machine learning technique itself is standard. The paper does not propose a new model architecture or learning algorithm. This is not a fatal flaw, as the strong results and novel application are significant, but it's worth noting that the primary contribution is in engineering rather than fundamental ML innovation.
2.  **Small Dataset Size:** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state they were sampled for diversity, this is a very small number for a coreference task. It raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings and whether the model might be overtuned to the specific characteristics of this small set of articles. A larger dataset would make the conclusions much more robust.
3.  **Dependency on a Deprecated Resource (Freebase):** The system relies heavily on Freebase for extracting entity types, gender, number, and other attributes. Freebase was shut down in 2016 and its data was migrated to Wikidata. This reliance on a defunct service is a significant weakness for the long-term viability and reproducibility of the proposed method. The paper would be stronger if it had used a currently maintained resource like Wikidata or at least discussed how the approach could be adapted.
4.  **Integration Experiment is Limited to an Older System:** The authors demonstrate the utility of their classifier by integrating it into Dcoref (Lee et al., 2013), a rule-based system. While this is a good proof of concept, Dcoref is no longer state-of-the-art. It would have been far more compelling to show that the MCR classifier could also improve a modern, neural coreference system like Scoref or other contemporary models. It is unclear if this integration was not attempted due to technical difficulty or other reasons.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the small dataset size: Could you comment on the statistical significance of your improvements, particularly for the full coreference task in Table 5? Given only 30 documents, how confident are you that the 4-point F1 gain for Dcoref++ is robust and not an artifact of the specific test set?
2.  Regarding the dependency on Freebase: Since Freebase is deprecated, have you considered adapting your approach to use Wikidata? Do you foresee any challenges in migrating your feature extraction process, and would you expect a similar level of performance?
3.  Regarding the Dcoref++ experiment: Why was Dcoref chosen for the integration experiment over the more recent and higher-performing (on OntoNotes) Scoref system? Is there a technical barrier to incorporating a high-precision classifier as a sieve or constraint in a modern end-to-end neural coreference system?
