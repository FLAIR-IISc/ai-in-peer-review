### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of coreference resolution (CR) on Wikipedia text, a domain where standard newswire-trained systems perform poorly. Instead of tackling the full CR task, the authors define a more focused sub-problem: **Main Concept Resolution (MCR)**, which is the task of identifying all mentions in a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main subject (the "main concept").

The authors' primary contributions, as I see them, are:

1.  **Creation of a New Annotated Dataset (WCR):** The paper introduces a new dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles annotated for coreference. The authors use this dataset to provide a valuable benchmark, demonstrating a significant performance drop for several state-of-the-art CR systems when moving from newswire to Wikipedia, thus clearly motivating the need for domain-specific solutions.
2.  **A Strong, Feature-Rich Baseline for Main Concept Resolution:** The authors frame MCR as a binary classification task and build a strong SVM-based system. The system's main novelty lies in its clever feature engineering, which directly leverages Wikipedia's structure (hyperlinks, redirects) and linked knowledge from Freebase to derive high-quality semantic features, cleverly bypassing the need for a separate, potentially noisy, entity linking step.
3.  **Demonstration of Utility:** The authors show that their focused MCR system not only dramatically outperforms baselines on its specific task but can also be integrated as a high-precision sieve into a full, rule-based CR system (Lee et al., 2013's Dcoref), leading to a significant improvement in overall coreference performance on their Wikipedia dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and Clearly Defined Problem:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work. The initial experiment reported in Table 1, showing that top-tier CR systems see a 10-17 point F1 drop on Wikipedia text, provides a compelling justification for this research. The formulation of the MCR sub-task is a sensible and practical simplification that is useful in its own right for information extraction and document understanding.

2.  **Valuable New Resource and Analysis:** The introduction of the WCR dataset is a significant contribution to the community. Such resources are crucial for driving research in domain adaptation for coreference. The authors' analysis of existing systems on this data is insightful and establishes a clear performance gap that needs to be addressed.

3.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The experimental setup is robust. The authors compare their system against four well-reasoned baselines derived from two different SOTA systems. The performance gains are substantial (an absolute F1 increase of ~13-15 points on the MCR task), leaving little doubt about the effectiveness of their approach. The feature ablation study (Tables 2 and 3) clearly demonstrates the contribution of each feature family, and the integration experiment (Table 5) successfully shows the practical utility of their work for improving a full CR system.

4.  **Effective and Pragmatic Feature Engineering:** The method for enriching mentions with attributes from Freebase by leveraging Wikipedia's internal hyperlinks is a key strength. This approach avoids the cascading errors common in pipelines that rely on separate wikification or entity linking tools. The features themselves are well-designed, combining surface-level cues with structural information from Wikipedia and semantic knowledge from Freebase.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Classifier:** The core of the method is a standard SVM classifier. While the feature engineering is excellent, the machine learning approach itself is not novel. The contribution lies more in the problem formulation, dataset creation, and application of existing techniques to a new domain, rather than in advancing the fundamental machine learning methodology for coreference.

2.  **Small and Potentially Unrepresentative Dataset:** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, this is a very small number from which to draw broad conclusions about performance on Wikipedia as a whole. This raises concerns about the statistical significance of the results and whether the models might be over-tuned to the specific characteristics of this small sample.

3.  **Limited Integration Experiment:** The authors demonstrate the utility of their MCR classifier by integrating it into Dcoref, a rule-based system from 2013. Their own results in Table 1 show that Dcoref is not the state-of-the-art, even on their WCR dataset (though it performs better than more recent systems). It would have been more compelling to show that their MCR module could also improve a more modern, learning-based system like Scoref. The current experiment leaves open the question of whether the gains are specific to the architecture of the older, deterministic sieve system.

4.  **Unclear Handling of Singleton Mentions:** Section 6.1 states that the WCR dataset does not contain singletons, but then ~13,400 singleton mentions are added automatically. These singletons presumably form a large part of the negative class for the MCR classifier, but their origin and properties are not fully detailed. It's unclear how potential errors in this automatic mention detection step might affect the training and evaluation of the classifier.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the dataset size: Could you provide more details on the sampling strategy for the 30 articles in WCR to strengthen the claim of representativeness? Was any inter-annotator agreement study conducted during its creation?
2.  Regarding the integration experiment: What was the reasoning for choosing Dcoref for the integration instead of a more recent system like Scoref? Do you hypothesize that a high-precision MC sieve would provide similar benefits to a modern neural CR system, or are the gains tied to the specific sieve-based architecture?
3.  Could you please clarify the role of the 13,400 automatically detected singleton mentions? Are they all considered negative examples for the MCR task? How do you ensure that noise from the mention detection algorithm does not negatively impact the training of your otherwise high-precision, knowledge-rich classifier?
