**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution (CR) specifically within Wikipedia articles. The authors argue that standard CR systems, trained primarily on newswire text, exhibit a significant performance degradation on this domain. To address this, they define a sub-task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions that refer to the main topic of a given Wikipedia article. They formulate MCR as a binary classification task and develop a feature-based SVM classifier that leverages Wikipedia's internal structure (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and external knowledge from Freebase. The authors create a new, small dataset of 30 annotated Wikipedia articles (WCR) to evaluate their approach. Their proposed classifier is shown to substantially outperform baselines derived from state-of-the-art CR systems. Finally, they demonstrate a modest improvement on the full coreference resolution task by integrating their MCR classifier as a high-precision initial step into the rule-based Stanford Dcoref system.

**Contributions**
1.  **Dataset and Analysis:** The primary contribution is the creation of the WCR dataset, a new, albeit small, annotated resource for coreference resolution on Wikipedia. The accompanying analysis, which empirically demonstrates the performance gap of established CR systems on this new domain, is a valuable finding that reinforces the need for domain adaptation.
2.  **Specialized MCR System:** The paper presents a carefully engineered, feature-based system for the specific sub-task of Main Concept Resolution. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the utility of leveraging Wikipedia-specific and Freebase features for this task.

**Strengths**
1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper is well-motivated by the known domain-sensitivity of NLP models. The performance drop of state-of-the-art systems on the WCR dataset (Table 1) provides a strong justification for the work.
2.  **Solid Baselines:** The authors have constructed thoughtful baselines by adapting full CR systems to the MCR task using several heuristics. This provides a more rigorous comparison than a simple, naive baseline and clearly shows the value of their specialized approach.
3.  **Effective Feature Engineering:** The features used by the SVM classifier are well-chosen and intuitive for the task. The ablation studies in Tables 2 and 3 provide a clear picture of the contribution of each feature family, which is good practice and enhances the reader's understanding.
4.  **Reproducibility:** The authors' commitment to releasing their dataset and other resources is commendable and will benefit the community.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited Novelty of the Proposed Method:** The core technical contribution is a feature-based SVM classifier. This is a well-established, standard machine learning technique. While the features are tailored to the domain, they are largely extensions of prior work that incorporates external knowledge bases like Wikipedia and Freebase into CR systems. The approach lacks significant methodological innovation, especially in the current landscape of end-to-end neural models.
2.  **Scope and Significance of the Task:** The paper focuses on "Main Concept Resolution," a task that appears significantly simpler than full coreference resolution. An article is, by definition, centered on its main concept, so one would expect features related to the title, frequency, and explicit links to be highly predictive. The high performance achieved (88.79 F1) suggests the task may not be particularly challenging. It is unclear if this sub-task is of significant independent interest or merely a stepping stone. The paper's argument that it is a prerequisite for other NLP tasks is made but not strongly substantiated beyond prior work.
3.  **Evaluation on a Very Small Dataset:** The entire evaluation is conducted on a new dataset of only 30 articles. While the annotation effort is appreciated, drawing strong conclusions from such a small sample is problematic. It is difficult to ascertain whether the high performance and observed trends will generalize to the vast and diverse content of Wikipedia. The 10-fold cross-validation is appropriate but cannot fully mitigate the risks associated with a small, potentially unrepresentative dataset.
4.  **Unconvincing Integration for Full Coreference Resolution:** The Dcoref++ experiment feels preliminary. The improvement is demonstrated by modifying a single, now somewhat dated, rule-based system (Lee et al., 2013). It is not clear why a more modern statistical system, like Scoref, was not chosen for this integration experiment. The reported 4-point F1 gain is encouraging but needs to be validated on a larger test set to be considered conclusive. Furthermore, the hard-coded logic of creating the MC chain first and then preventing further modification is a strong constraint that may not be optimal and lacks general applicability to other CR architectures.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The choice of an SVM classifier seems conservative. Could you elaborate on the decision not to pursue a neural approach, for instance, by fine-tuning a pre-trained model like Scoref or a BERT-based CR model on your WCR data for the full CR task? Given the small size of the training data, were you concerned about overfitting with more complex models?
2.  The Dcoref++ integration relies on adding a new, high-precision sieve and preventing subsequent sieves from altering the main concept chain. This seems tailored to a sieve-based architecture. How would you envision integrating your MCR predictions into a mention-ranking or clustering-based model like Scoref, where such hard constraints are less natural to implement?
3.  Given that the evaluation is limited to 30 documents, how confident are you that the feature weights learned by your SVM are robust? For instance, could the model be overfitting to the specific topics (people, organizations, etc.) present in your small sample, leading to an overestimation of performance?
