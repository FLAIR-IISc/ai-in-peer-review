### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper addresses the poor performance of standard coreference resolution systems on Wikipedia text. Instead of attempting to adapt a full coreference system, the authors propose a new, more constrained task: Main Concept Resolution (MCR). The goal of MCR is to identify all mentions in a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main concept (i.e., its title). The authors frame this as a binary classification problem and develop a feature-rich SVM model that leverages the unique structural properties of Wikipedia and linked data from Freebase. They demonstrate that their specialized model significantly outperforms strong baselines derived from state-of-the-art coreference systems on this task. Finally, they show that their MCR classifier can be integrated as a high-precision component into a general-purpose coreference system to improve its overall performance on Wikipedia articles.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **The conceptualization and formalization of the Main Concept Resolution task.** This is the paper's most significant contribution. It reframes a part of the coreference problem into a tractable, domain-specific, and highly useful sub-task. This provides a novel direction for research that moves beyond chasing incremental gains on standard newswire benchmarks.
2.  **A highly effective, domain-aware method for MCR.** The authors' approach of directly using Wikipedia's hyperlink structure and Freebase connections to source high-quality semantic features is a clever way to bypass the traditional, often error-prone, entity linking pipeline. This is a strong example of tailoring a solution to the specific affordances of a data source.
3.  **The creation of a new annotated resource for coreference in Wikipedia (WCR).** While small, this dataset provides a much-needed testbed for this genre and highlights the domain-gap problem with existing systems. The authors also demonstrate the utility of their work by using it to improve a full coreference system.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Refreshing Problem Formulation:** This paper is a standout because it doesn't just present another complex neural model for a saturated task. The decision to define and tackle the MCR sub-problem is a bold and insightful one. It acknowledges that for many downstream applications, resolving the main entity is the most critical step. This pragmatic simplification of the problem is a valuable conceptual contribution and opens up a new avenue for research.
2.  **Intelligent Use of Domain-Specific Knowledge:** The method's key strength lies in its circumvention of a standard NLP pipeline. Instead of relying on a generic wikification tool (and inheriting its errors), the model directly exploits the ground-truth links curated by Wikipedia editors. Using article structure, redirects, and Freebase attributes as features is a perfect example of how domain adaptation should be done—thoughtfully and by leveraging the unique properties of the domain itself. This is far more interesting than simply fine-tuning a large pre-trained model.
3.  **Clear Demonstration of Practical Utility:** The authors close the loop effectively by showing that solving their proposed sub-task is not just an academic exercise. The Dcoref++ experiment, where the MCR classifier is used as a high-precision sieve, provides compelling evidence that this focused approach can bootstrap and improve a full, general-purpose system. The 4-point absolute F1 gain on the CoNLL score is significant and validates the usefulness of MCR as a foundational step for full coreference in this domain.
4.  **Thorough Analysis and Strong Baselines:** The authors didn't compare their approach to trivial baselines. They engineered several plausible heuristics on top of two different state-of-the-art systems (one rule-based, one neural). The fact that their focused classifier soundly beats all of them (by over 13 F1 points) strongly supports their claim that a specialized approach is necessary. The feature ablation study also clearly illustrates the contribution of each component of their model.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Very Small Scale of the Evaluation Dataset:** The most significant weakness is the reliance on the WCR dataset, which contains only 30 articles. While the creation of a new dataset is commendable, the small size limits the confidence in the generalizability of the results. It is difficult to know if the impressive performance gains would hold across the vast diversity of topics and writing styles present in Wikipedia. While I find the core ideas compelling enough to overlook this, the authors should be very clear about this limitation.
2.  **Comparison Against Off-the-Shelf Systems:** The paper shows that generic coreference systems perform poorly on Wikipedia, which motivates the new task. However, when comparing MCR performance, the baselines are heuristics applied to the *output* of these generic systems. A more direct and arguably stronger comparison would involve fine-tuning a statistical system like Scoref on the WCR data for the MCR binary classification task. This would help disentangle the benefits of the authors' feature engineering from the simple advantage of using in-domain training data.
3.  **Relatively Simple ML Methodology:** The use of an SVM with carefully engineered features feels somewhat dated in the current deep learning era. While the results are undeniably strong and the feature engineering is clever, the paper doesn't explore whether a modern neural architecture could learn these relationships automatically. However, given my preference for novel ideas over methodological complexity, I see this as a very minor point; the paper's strength is in its formulation and insights, not its choice of classifier.
