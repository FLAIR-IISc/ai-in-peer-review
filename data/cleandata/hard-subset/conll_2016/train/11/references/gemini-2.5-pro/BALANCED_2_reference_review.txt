### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution (CR) within the specific domain of Wikipedia articles. The authors argue that standard CR systems, trained predominantly on newswire text, perform poorly on this genre. To address this, they define a sub-task called Main Concept Resolution (MCR): identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main topic or "main concept" (MC). The authors frame this as a binary classification task and develop a feature-rich SVM classifier that leverages the unique structure of Wikipedia (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked knowledge from Freebase. Their method is evaluated on a newly created dataset of 30 annotated Wikipedia articles (WCR), where it is shown to significantly outperform several strong baselines derived from state-of-the-art CR systems. Finally, they demonstrate that integrating their MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into a rule-based CR system improves performance on the full coreference task.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:

1.  **The formulation and thorough benchmarking of the Main Concept Resolution (MCR) task.** The paper successfully motivates this new task as an important and challenging sub-problem of coreference. The contribution is strengthened by the creation of a new annotated dataset (WCR), a clear demonstration of the genre gap for existing SOTA systems, and the development of several competitive baselines.
2.  **An effective, domain-adapted model for MCR.** The proposed SVM-based classifier, while methodologically conventional, is highly effective due to its clever and extensive feature engineering. It demonstrates the power of leveraging domain-specific structural information (from Wikipedia and Freebase) to achieve a substantial performance gain (+13-15 F1 points) over strong baselines.
3.  **A proof-of-concept for improving full coreference resolution.** By integrating the MCR model into the Dcoref system and showing a 4-point F1 improvement on the WCR dataset, the authors demonstrate the practical utility of their focused approach for the broader CR task.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the need for domain-specific coreference resolution for Wikipedia. The performance drop of SOTA systems on the WCR dataset (Table 1) provides compelling evidence for this "genre gap". The proposed MCR task is well-defined, intuitive, and clearly useful for downstream applications like information extraction and knowledge base population from Wikipedia.
2.  **Rigorous and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental setup is a major strength. The authors not only compare their system to SOTA baselines on their proposed task but also first evaluate those SOTA systems on the full CR task to establish a performance benchmark on the new WCR dataset. The design of four distinct baselines (B1-B4) for the MCR task is thoughtful and provides a robust point of comparison. The detailed ablation study (Tables 2 & 3) clearly illustrates the impact of different feature families, providing valuable insights into what makes their model successful.
3.  **Creation of a New Community Resource:** The introduction of the WCR dataset, while modest in size, is a valuable contribution. Annotating coreference is a laborious task, and new resources that enable the study of CR in different domains are highly welcome. The authors’ commitment to release the dataset, code, and pre-computed MCR outputs for all of English Wikipedia will significantly benefit the community.
4.  **Effective Use of Domain-Specific Knowledge:** A key strength of the proposed method is its circumvention of a potentially noisy, general-purpose entity linking pipeline. By directly exploiting the human-curated hyperlink structure of Wikipedia and linked data from Freebase, the authors extract high-quality semantic attributes for mentions. This is a smart and practical engineering choice that is central to the model's high performance.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core of the proposed approach is a feature-engineered SVM, a standard and well-established machine learning technique. While the features themselves are well-designed, the overall method does not introduce a novel algorithm or architecture. In an era dominated by neural and end-to-end models for NLP tasks, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of why this "classic" approach was chosen and how it might compare to a modern neural baseline (e.g., one using contextual embeddings).
2.  **Small Dataset Size and Generalizability Concerns:** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 Wikipedia articles. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, this is a very small sample size from which to draw broad conclusions about performance on Wikipedia as a whole. It raises questions about how well the model's performance (and the reported performance gap over baselines) would generalize to a much larger and more diverse set of articles. Acknowledging this limitation and providing more detail on the sampling strategy would be beneficial.
3.  **Reliance on a Deprecated Resource (Freebase):** The system relies heavily on Freebase for extracting semantic attributes like entity type, gender, and number. As Freebase was officially shut down in 2016 and its data migrated to Wikidata, this reliance could pose challenges for the long-term reproducibility and extensibility of this work. The paper would be more forward-looking if it addressed this issue, for instance by discussing the feasibility of adapting the system to use Wikidata.
4.  **Limited Exploration of System Integration:** The integration of the MCR classifier into Dcoref is a promising result. However, its impact is only demonstrated on a single, rule-based system. It is unclear if this high-precision sieve approach would yield similar benefits for a state-of-the-art statistical or neural coreference system (e.g., Scoref), which might be less amenable to such modular additions. A more in-depth analysis of the interactions between the new MCR sieve and the existing sieves in Dcoref would also add value.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The WCR dataset of 30 documents is a valuable but small resource. Could you elaborate on the sampling methodology used to select these articles to help allay concerns about their representativeness? Have you performed any error analysis that might suggest which types of articles (e.g., by topic, length, or link density) are more challenging for your system?
2.  Your feature-engineered SVM approach is highly effective. Did you experiment with or consider any deep learning models for the MCR classification task, for instance, using pre-trained embeddings like BERT to represent the mentions and their context? A brief discussion on the potential trade-offs would be insightful.
3.  Given that Freebase is a deprecated resource, could you comment on the effort required to adapt your system to use a more current knowledge graph like Wikidata? Do you anticipate any major challenges in mapping the features you extract from Freebase to equivalents in Wikidata?
