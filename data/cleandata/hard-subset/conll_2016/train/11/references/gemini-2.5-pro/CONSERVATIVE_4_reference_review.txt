### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution (CR) within Wikipedia articles, focusing on a specific sub-problem: identifying all mentions of the article's "main concept" (MC). The authors frame this as a binary classification task and develop a feature-rich Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifier that leverages Wikipedia's specific structure (hyperlinks, redirects) and external knowledge from Freebase. They introduce a new, small annotated dataset (WCR) of 30 Wikipedia articles. Their main claims are that (1) existing state-of-the-art CR systems perform poorly on Wikipedia text, (2) their specialized classifier significantly outperforms baselines derived from these systems on the MC resolution task, and (3) integrating their classifier into an established rule-based system (Lee et al., 2013) improves its performance on the full CR task for Wikipedia articles.

From my perspective, the contributions of this work are, in order of significance:

1.  **Empirical Analysis of Domain Shift:** The paper provides clear evidence (Table 1) that state-of-the-art coreference systems, trained primarily on newswire, suffer a substantial performance degradation on Wikipedia text. This finding, while not entirely surprising, is well-demonstrated and reinforces the need for domain adaptation in CR.
2.  **A Curated Dataset for Wikipedia Coreference:** The creation and release of the WCR dataset, though small, provides a valuable resource for evaluating CR systems on this important and widely used text genre.
3.  **A Specialized Component for Main Concept Resolution:** The authors present a well-engineered, feature-based system for the specific task of identifying mentions of the main concept. While the methodology is conventional, its application to this problem and the resulting performance gains are notable within the defined task.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper is well-motivated by the observed performance gap of existing CR systems on Wikipedia. The focused task of "Main Concept Resolution" is a practical and useful sub-problem, particularly for downstream tasks like knowledge base population and information extraction from Wikipedia, as noted by the authors.
2.  **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors are diligent in their evaluation. They compare their approach against several well-conceived baselines built on top of both rule-based (Dcoref) and learning-based (Scoref) SOTA systems. The feature ablation study (Tables 2 and 3) provides useful insights into the relative importance of different information sources.
3.  **Demonstrated Utility in a Full CR System:** The most compelling result is the integration of the proposed MC classifier into the Dcoref system (Dcoref++). Showing a 4-point absolute improvement in CoNLL F1 score on the full CR task (Table 5) demonstrates that this specialized component is not just an isolated success but can provide tangible benefits to an established, general-purpose system. This strengthens the overall contribution significantly.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Extremely Small Dataset:** The primary and most significant weakness of this study is the size of the WCR dataset. All conclusions are drawn from only 30 Wikipedia articles. This raises serious concerns about the generalizability of the findings. The performance gains, both for the standalone classifier and the integrated Dcoref++ system, may not hold on a larger, more diverse set of articles. The reported performance numbers could be subject to high variance, and it is difficult to ascertain their statistical significance.
2.  **Fairness of Baseline Comparisons:** The main evaluation (Table 4) compares a classifier specifically trained for the binary MC-resolution task against post-hoc heuristics applied to the output of general-purpose CR systems. This comparison is inherently favorable to the proposed approach. A stronger and more direct baseline would involve fine-tuning a learning-based system like Scoref on the WCR data for the full CR task, and then applying the same B1-B4 heuristics. As it stands, the reported 13-15 point F1 improvement is impressive but potentially inflated due to the weakness of the baselines relative to the specialized model.
3.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The approach relies on a standard SVM classifier with extensive, manually engineered features. While the feature engineering is sound and effective, the methodology itself is conventional and does not advance the state-of-the-art in machine learning for NLP. The contribution lies in the application and engineering, not in novel techniques.
4.  **High Dependency on Wikipedia/Freebase Structure:** The proposed method is fundamentally tied to the rich markup of Wikipedia and the structured knowledge in Freebase. This makes the approach highly domain-specific and not applicable to general-domain text, which lacks this explicit structure. Furthermore, the reliance on Freebase is a practical limitation, as Freebase has been defunct for several years, which impacts the long-term viability and reproducibility of the work without substitution (e.g., with Wikidata).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given the small size of the WCR dataset (30 documents), have you performed any statistical significance testing on your main results (e.g., in Table 4 and Table 5)? It would be important to know if the observed improvements over the baselines and the original Dcoref system are statistically significant.
2.  The baselines for the MC resolution task are heuristics applied to the output of systems not trained on WCR. Could you comment on why you did not attempt to fine-tune a system like Scoref on WCR for the full coreference task as a stronger baseline? This would seem to provide a more direct comparison of the utility of your specialized features versus a more general end-to-end learning approach.
3.  Your system relies heavily on Freebase, which is no longer maintained. Could you discuss the feasibility of adapting your feature extraction pipeline to use a currently maintained knowledge base like Wikidata? Did you consider this during your work, and do you foresee any major challenges?
