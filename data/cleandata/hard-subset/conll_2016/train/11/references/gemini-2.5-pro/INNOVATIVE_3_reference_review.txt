### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the poor performance of standard coreference resolution (CR) systems on Wikipedia texts. Instead of attempting to adapt a full CR system, the authors propose a novel and pragmatic sub-task: Main Concept Resolution (MCR). MCR aims to identify all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main subject or "main concept" (MC). The authors frame this as a binary classification task and develop a feature-rich SVM classifier that leverages the unique structural properties of Wikipedia markup and linked data from Freebase. To facilitate this research, they introduce a new annotated dataset, WCR, and show that state-of-the-art CR systems perform poorly on it. Their proposed MCR classifier significantly outperforms strong baselines adapted from SOTA systems. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of their approach by integrating it as a high-precision first-pass "sieve" into a rule-based CR system, leading to substantial improvements on the full coreference task for Wikipedia articles.

### Main Contributions

1.  **The formalization and benchmarking of the "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR) task.** While the general idea has been touched upon in prior work, this paper is the first to rigorously define it as a standalone machine learning task, create a dedicated annotated dataset (WCR) for it, and systematically evaluate modern coreference systems against it. This contribution opens a new, well-motivated, and practical avenue for research in domain-specific coreference.
2.  **A simple yet highly effective classifier for MCR.** The paper presents a system that achieves impressive performance by forgoing complex, end-to-end architectures in favor of intelligent, domain-specific feature engineering. The decision to leverage Wikipedia's hyperlink structure and Freebase data directly, rather than relying on a potentially noisy entity-linking pipeline, is a key strength of the proposed method.
3.  **A compelling demonstration of how solving a specialized sub-task can improve a general system.** The "Dcoref++" experiment, where the MCR classifier is integrated into a full CR system, shows a significant boost in performance (a 4-point F1 gain). This is a powerful argument for the utility of their focused approach, suggesting that solving the most prominent coreference chain with high precision can provide a strong foundation for resolving the rest.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Insightful Problem Formulation.** The paper's primary strength is its bold reframing of the problem. Instead of another incremental improvement on a general CR model, it carves out a high-impact, tractable sub-problem. This is precisely the kind of work that can push the field in new directions. The analysis in Table 1, showing the dramatic performance drop of SOTA systems on Wikipedia text, provides a strong motivation for this new perspective and the need for the WCR dataset.
2.  **Clever, Resource-Aware Methodology.** The authors make excellent use of the specific domain. The features derived from Wikipedia markup (redirects, links) and Freebase are well-motivated and, as the ablation studies show, highly effective. This approach is a refreshing alternative to simply throwing larger neural models at a problem; it shows the continued value of careful analysis and domain adaptation. Bypassing an explicit Wikification step is a particularly smart design choice that avoids a major source of cascading errors.
3.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evaluation.** The paper's claims are well-supported by a thorough evaluation. The baselines (B1-B4) are not strawmen; they are thoughtfully designed heuristics applied to SOTA systems, making the proposed model's large performance gains (e.g., +13 F1 over the best baseline) highly significant. The feature ablation studies provide clear insight into what makes the model work, and the final Dcoref++ experiment convincingly closes the loop by showing the practical benefit for the full CR task.
4.  **Clarity and Potential for Impact.** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The task is intuitive, the method is straightforward, and the results are clear. By promising to release the dataset and the extracted mention data, the authors are laying the groundwork for future research in this area, which has a high potential for impact given the ubiquity of Wikipedia in NLP.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Dataset Size.** The primary weakness is the small scale of the newly created WCR dataset, which consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, this is a very small sample from which to draw general conclusions about performance on a resource as vast and varied as Wikipedia. The robustness of the models and the significance of the results would be much stronger if validated on a larger corpus. However, given the novelty of the task and the significant cost of annotation, this is an understandable and forgivable limitation for a foundational paper in a new area.
2.  **"Classical" Machine Learning Approach.** The choice of a feature-engineered SVM, while effective, feels somewhat dated. The paper does not explore or compare against more modern end-to-end neural approaches (e.g., using BERT) that could potentially learn relevant features automatically. While the current approach works very well, a discussion of why this method was chosen over neural alternatives, or an experiment with a simple neural baseline, would have strengthened the paper's methodological grounding.
3.  **Narrow Scope of Integration Experiment.** The utility of the MCR component is demonstrated by integrating it into a single, rule-based system (Dcoref). It is not clear whether this "high-precision sieve" strategy would yield similar benefits for a statistical, end-to-end system like Scoref, which does not follow a sieve architecture. Demonstrating this integration in different CR paradigms would make the claim of general utility more robust.

### Recommendation: Accept

This paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and practical contribution. It carves out a new research direction by formalizing the task of Main Concept Resolution in Wikipedia. While the methodology is not state-of-the-art in terms of complex architectures, its strength lies in its intelligent, domain-specific design, which leads to impressive results. The weaknesses, primarily the small dataset, are understandable for a paper introducing a new task and do not detract from the significance of the core idea. This work prioritizes a bold new concept over methodological perfection, and its potential to influence future work on domain-specific NLP is high. I strongly recommend acceptance.
