### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR) in Wikipedia, defined as identifying all mentions within an article that refer to the article's main subject. The authors argue that general-purpose coreference resolution (CR) systems, trained primarily on newswire, perform poorly on Wikipedia text, necessitating a specialized approach. Their main contributions are:

1.  **A high-performing supervised classifier for MCR in Wikipedia.** The primary contribution is a feature-rich SVM-based system that significantly outperforms several strong baselines derived from state-of-the-art CR systems. The method cleverly exploits Wikipedia's internal structure (hyperlinks, redirects) and linked knowledge bases (Freebase) to engineer effective features without relying on a separate, potentially noisy, wikification pipeline.

2.  **A new annotated dataset (WCR) and benchmark for coreference on Wikipedia.** The authors introduce a new 30-document dataset annotated for coreference. Their evaluation on this dataset provides compelling evidence for the domain adaptation problem in CR, showing that recent state-of-the-art systems underperform significantly compared to their results on OntoNotes.

3.  **Demonstration of utility for general-purpose CR.** The paper shows that their specialized MCR model can be integrated as a high-precision sieve into an existing CR system (Lee et al., 2013's Dcoref), resulting in a notable 4-point F1 improvement on the full coreference task on their WCR dataset.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated problem with strong empirical justification.** The paper opens with a clear and convincing argument for why coreference on Wikipedia is a distinct and important challenge. The results in Table 1, showing a dramatic performance drop for several state-of-the-art systems on the WCR dataset, provide a powerful justification for the work and immediately establish its relevance. The observation that the older, rule-based Dcoref outperforms more recent neural models on this new domain is itself an interesting finding for the community.

2.  **Impressive performance on the proposed task.** The proposed MCR classifier achieves an F1 score of nearly 89%, an absolute improvement of over 13 F1 points compared to the strongest baseline. This is a very strong result that demonstrates the effectiveness of their approach. The thorough baselines, which intelligently adapt full CR systems to the MCR task, make the comparison fair and the improvements meaningful.

3.  **Clever and effective feature engineering.** A key strength of the methodology is its direct use of Wikipedia's hyperlink structure to obtain semantic attributes from Freebase, bypassing the need for a separate entity linking step. This is a sound design choice that likely avoids the cascading errors common in multi-stage NLP pipelines. The features themselves are well-designed and tailored to the unique properties of an encyclopedic article, such as using redirects for name variants and the first sentence to infer the MC type. The ablation studies (Tables 2 and 3) effectively demonstrate the contribution of each feature family.

4.  **The new WCR dataset is a valuable resource.** Although small, the creation of a new, genre-specific annotated dataset is a welcome contribution. It serves as a testbed for the claims made in this paper and, if released as promised, will enable future research on domain adaptation for coreference resolution.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited scale of the evaluation dataset.** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 articles. While the authors state that they sampled for diversity, this is a very small number on which to base strong claims of generalizability across the millions of articles in Wikipedia. The performance of a feature-based system can be sensitive to the specific topics, styles, and structures present in the training data. For example, articles about historical events might have a different mention distribution than articles about people or organizations. The cross-validation results are solid for this dataset, but the dataset's small size remains a significant limitation.

2.  **The novelty of the learning method is limited.** The core of the proposed approach is a feature-based SVM, which is a very well-established technique. While the feature engineering is novel and effective, the paper does not explore more modern learning architectures. Given that the SOTA systems it compares against (e.g., Scoref, Durrett and Klein) have moved towards neural, end-to-end models, the choice of an SVM feels somewhat dated. It leaves open the question of whether a simple neural model using embeddings could capture these feature interactions more effectively or with less manual engineering.

3.  **The integration experiment is confined to an older, rule-based system.** The authors demonstrate the utility of their MCR classifier by integrating it as a sieve into Dcoref (Lee et al., 2013). This is a logical choice given Dcoref's sieve architecture and its strong performance on WCR. However, it is not clear how this component could be integrated into modern, end-to-end neural CR systems that do not follow a sieve structure. This limits the generality of the claim that MCR can easily benefit full CR systems, as the field is largely moving away from the architecture used in the integration experiment.

4.  **The pronoun resolution heuristic for the baselines may be a weak strawman.** The heuristic described at the end of Section 3, which adds *all* instances of a pronoun to the MC chain if it appears there even once, is an aggressive, low-precision strategy. While it may boost recall, it will almost certainly harm precision and overall F1. This could potentially overstate the performance gap between the baselines and the authors' more sophisticated pronominal classifier.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the sampling strategy for the 30 articles in the WCR dataset? Given the dataset's small size, it would be helpful to understand the distribution of article types (e.g., PER, ORG, LOC, EVENT) to better assess the potential generalizability of your findings.

2.  You chose Dcoref for the integration experiment in Section 6.4. Could you comment on the feasibility of integrating your MCR classifier's predictions into a modern end-to-end neural system like Scoref? For instance, could the classifier's output be used as a feature or a soft constraint during clustering?

3.  While the feature-based SVM performs exceptionally well, did you experiment with any neural architectures? It would be interesting to see if a model using pre-trained embeddings (e.g., BERT) could achieve comparable results with less hand-crafted feature engineering, especially given the rich textual context available in Wikipedia.
