### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the problem of coreference resolution (CR) on Wikipedia articles, a domain where standard systems trained on newswire data perform poorly. The authors define and focus on a specific sub-task: Main Concept Resolution (MCR), which is the identification of all mentions referring to the main topic of a given Wikipedia article. To support this research, the paper introduces a new, manually annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR). The authors demonstrate that state-of-the-art CR systems exhibit a significant performance drop on this dataset. They then propose a binary classification approach for MCR using two Support Vector Machine (SVM) models (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) trained on a rich set of features derived from Wikipedia's internal structure (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and the Freebase knowledge graph. This approach substantially outperforms several strong baselines built from existing CR systems. Finally, the authors show that integrating their MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into the rule-based Dcoref system improves its performance on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia articles.

In my assessment, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A new dataset and benchmark analysis for coreference in Wikipedia:** The paper introduces WCR, a new annotated corpus for a non-newswire domain. The accompanying benchmark analysis of several state-of-the-art systems provides clear, quantitative evidence of the domain adaptation problem for coreference resolution and motivates the need for domain-specific approaches.
2.  **An effective, feature-engineered model for Main Concept Resolution:** The proposed SVM-based classifier is technically sound and demonstrates high performance on the MCR task. Its main strength lies in the careful engineering of features that directly leverage the unique structural properties of Wikipedia and linked data from Freebase, bypassing the need for a potentially noisy, intermediate entity-linking step.
3.  **A practical demonstration of improving a full coreference system:** The paper shows that solving the MCR sub-task is not merely an isolated result but can be practically applied to enhance a general-purpose CR system. Integrating the MCR classifier into Dcoref results in a significant performance gain on the full coreference task, highlighting the utility of the proposed method.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and clearly defined problem:** The paper establishes a strong motivation by demonstrating the performance degradation of existing CR systems on Wikipedia text (Table 1). The formulation of the Main Concept Resolution task is clear, intuitive, and represents a logical and valuable step towards solving the broader challenge of coreference in this domain.
2.  **Valuable new resource and insightful analysis:** The creation of the WCR dataset, while modest in size, is a significant contribution to the community, which lacks annotated coreference resources outside of standard domains like newswire. The analysis on this dataset is insightful, revealing not only the domain gap but also the surprising result that an older rule-based system (Dcoref) outperforms more recent learning-based systems, a finding of interest for future research in domain adaptation.
3.  **Technically sound and highly effective method for MCR:** The proposed classification approach is well-executed. The feature design is thorough and tailored specifically to the data source, exploiting Wikipedia's rich markup and structured information from Freebase. The empirical results are convincing, showing a large absolute F1-score improvement of over 13 points compared to the best-performing baseline (Table 4). The feature analysis in Tables 2 and 3 provides good transparency into the model's sources of performance.
4.  **Demonstrated utility for the full coreference task:** The integration of the MCR model into Dcoref (the Dcoref++ system) provides strong evidence for the practical value of the work. By using the MCR classifier as an initial, high-precision sieve, the authors achieve a notable 3.3 absolute point increase in the CoNLL F1 score over the original system. This successfully links the focused sub-task back to the general problem of coreference resolution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited methodological novelty:** The core method is a feature-engineered SVM, a well-established technique. The novelty of the work lies primarily in the task definition, the dataset, and the domain-specific feature engineering, rather than in a new learning algorithm or model architecture. While effective, the approach does not advance the state-of-the-art from a machine learning methodology perspective.
2.  **Small dataset size and potential generalizability issues:** The WCR dataset, comprising 30 documents, is relatively small. While the authors state that the articles were sampled for variety, the small size raises questions about how representative the dataset is of the vast and diverse content of Wikipedia. Consequently, the performance gains reported, though significant on this dataset, may not generalize to the full spectrum of Wikipedia articles. The conclusions would be more robust if validated on a larger test set.
3.  **Dependency on a static external resource (Freebase):** The system's features rely heavily on Freebase, which is no longer actively maintained. This poses a risk to the long-term reproducibility and applicability of the method. Although the authors plan to release a data dump, future work aiming to build upon this approach would need to adapt it to a current knowledge graph like Wikidata. The paper lacks a discussion of this limitation or potential migration paths.
4.  **Limited scope of the integration experiment:** The authors demonstrate the utility of their MCR classifier by integrating it only into the rule-based Dcoref system. It is unclear why a learning-based system like Scoref was not also chosen for a similar experiment. This omission makes it difficult to assess whether the proposed MCR component is broadly applicable for improving modern statistical coreference systems or if its benefits are specific to the architecture of a deterministic, sieve-based system.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your method relies significantly on Freebase. Given that Freebase is a static resource, have you considered the feasibility of adapting your feature set to use a more current knowledge graph such as Wikidata? What would be the primary challenges in such a migration?
2.  The experiment integrating your MCR classifier into a full CR system was conducted only with the rule-based Dcoref. Could you elaborate on the rationale for this choice over a statistical system like Scoref? Do you foresee any specific challenges in integrating your classifier as a feature or a component in a modern neural coreference resolver?
3.  Regarding the WCR dataset, could you provide more details about the sampling methodology? Specifically, what was the distribution of article topics (e.g., persons, organizations, locations, events) and other characteristics (e.g., length, link density) in the 30-document sample, and how does this reflect the overall distribution in Wikipedia?
