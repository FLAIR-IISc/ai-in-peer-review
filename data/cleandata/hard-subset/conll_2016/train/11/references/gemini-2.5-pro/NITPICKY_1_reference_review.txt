### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the task of "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR) in Wikipedia, which consists of identifying all mentions in an article that refer to the article's main topic. The authors frame this as a binary classification problem. They introduce a new, small annotated dataset called WCR to demonstrate that state-of-the-art coreference systems trained on newswire perform poorly on Wikipedia text. They propose two SVM classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using a rich set of features derived from Wikipedia's structure and Freebase. Their approach is shown to substantially outperform several heuristic baselines built upon existing coreference systems. Finally, they integrate their MCR classifier into the rule-based Dcoref system, demonstrating a notable improvement in general coreference resolution performance on their WCR dataset.

### Contributions of this Work

1.  **Dataset and Benchmark:** The primary contribution is the creation of the WCR dataset and its use to benchmark existing coreference resolution systems, quantitatively demonstrating the performance degradation of SOTA systems when applied to the Wikipedia genre.
2.  **Task-Specific Classifier:** The paper presents a simple but effective, feature-engineered SVM classifier for the focused task of Main Concept Resolution, which could be a useful component for downstream information extraction tasks centered on Wikipedia articles.
3.  **System Integration:** The authors provide a proof-of-concept showing that a high-precision classifier for this sub-task can be integrated as a sieve into a general-purpose system to improve overall coreference resolution performance in this specific domain.

### Strengths

1.  **Problem Motivation:** The paper is well-motivated. The degradation of coreference systems across domains is a known issue, and the experiments presented in Table 1 provide clear, quantitative evidence of this problem for the important domain of Wikipedia. This successfully establishes the need for genre-specific adaptation.
2.  **Focused Task Definition:** The task of Main Concept Resolution is well-defined and practically relevant. For many applications using Wikipedia (e.g., knowledge base population), isolating the primary entity's mentions is a critical and high-value sub-problem.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results on MCR:** The proposed classifier achieves very high performance on the MCR task, showing an absolute F1 improvement of over 13 points compared to the strongest baseline (Table 4). The ablation studies, while presented incompletely, suggest that the engineered features are effective.

### Weaknesses

This paper, while presenting a promising direction, is undermined by significant methodological opacities, presentation flaws, and an accumulation of minor errors that collectively cast doubt on the reliability and reproducibility of the results.

1.  **Incomplete and Unprofessional Presentation:** The submission is critically incomplete. It repeatedly refers to Figures 1, 2, 3, and Table 3, none of which are included in the manuscript. Without these, it is impossible to understand the core representation of mentions and main concepts (Figs 1 & 2), follow the error analysis (Fig 3), or see the full ablation results (Table 3). This oversight is severe enough to warrant rejection on its own.
2.  **Insufficient Methodological Detail:** The methodology lacks the precision expected in a scientific publication.
    *   **Feature Engineering:** The feature selection process is described as ad-hoc ("focusing on the most promising ones that we found simple enough to compute"). Key feature definitions are vague, using terms like "etc." to describe string similarity measures, which is unacceptable. The origins of large, specific feature counts (e.g., "2380 binary features" for POS bigrams) are completely unexplained.
    *   **Classifier Training:** Hyperparameter choices for the SVM are not justified. The authors state a penalty weight of 2.0 was chosen due to class imbalance but do not describe any tuning process. Footnote 5 dismissively states that "other configurations" were tried "with less success on a held-out dataset," providing no actionable information.
3.  **Questionable Experimental Design:** The evaluation of the integrated Dcoref++ system appears to be flawed. The authors state they "use predicted results from 10-fold cross-validation" (Footnote 6) to build the MC chain in the first sieve. This implies that for a given test fold, the classifier's predictions are derived from a model that was trained on the other nine folds, which include the test data in their own CV splits. This constitutes a form of data leakage. A rigorous evaluation would require a strictly separate test set or a properly nested cross-validation procedure. Furthermore, no statistical significance tests are performed to validate the claimed improvements, which is a notable omission given the small size of the test set.
4.  **Dataset Limitations:** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the authors assert it is "representative," this claim is unsubstantiated by any description of the sampling strategy. Conclusions drawn from such a small dataset may not generalize. Additionally, crucial details about the dataset's construction and annotation guidelines are deferred to a "non-anonymized version," hindering a proper assessment during peer review.
5.  **Numerous Minor Flaws:** The paper is littered with small inconsistencies and formatting errors.
    *   URLs are placed in footnotes (e.g., for Jena, CoNLL scorer) instead of being properly formatted in the references section.
    *   Terminology is imprecise (e.g., "grammatical words" instead of "stop words"; "spurious mentions" without definition).
    *   The prose contains clichés ("simple yet powerful") and awkward phrasing ("basically imputable to").
    *   The reported improvement for Dcoref++ is rounded up from 3.34 to "4 absolute points".

While the core idea is sound, the execution and presentation fall far short of the standards for a top-tier conference. The combination of missing figures, methodological ambiguity, and potential experimental flaws makes it impossible to confidently accept the paper's conclusions.
