### Summary of the paper and main contributions

This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution on Wikipedia text, a domain where standard systems trained on newswire data are known to underperform. The authors define a specific sub-task called Main Concept Resolution (MCR): identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main subject. They frame this as a binary classification problem and propose a feature-rich SVM-based model that leverages the unique structural properties of Wikipedia (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked knowledge from Freebase. The authors introduce a new, manually annotated dataset (WCR) of 30 Wikipedia articles to evaluate their approach. They show that their method significantly outperforms several strong baselines derived from state-of-the-art coreference systems. Finally, they demonstrate the practical utility of their MCR classifier by integrating it as a high-precision sieve into a rule-based coreference system, achieving a substantial improvement on the full coreference resolution task for their dataset.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong, domain-adapted method for Main Concept Resolution in Wikipedia.** The primary contribution is the development and thorough evaluation of a classification model that effectively uses features derived from Wikipedia's structure and Freebase. The feature engineering is thoughtful, and the ablation study clearly demonstrates its effectiveness, achieving a high F1 score of nearly 89%.
2.  **The creation and analysis of a new dataset (WCR) for coreference in Wikipedia.** The paper introduces a new resource and uses it to provide a valuable empirical analysis showing that state-of-the-art coreference systems struggle on this genre. This result clearly motivates the need for genre-specific adaptation and grounds the paper's contributions.
3.  **Demonstration of downstream utility for full coreference resolution.** The authors successfully integrate their specialized MCR model into a general coreference system (Dcoref) and show significant gains on the full coreference task. This elevates the work from solving a niche task to providing a tangible improvement for a core NLP problem in a specific domain.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated problem and strong empirical grounding.** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the research. The initial experiment presented in Table 1, which shows a dramatic performance drop for several state-of-the-art coreference systems when applied to the Wikipedia domain, provides a compelling argument for the necessity of this work. It clearly establishes a research gap that the paper then proceeds to address.
2.  **Impressive performance and thorough analysis.** The proposed method achieves an F1 score of 88.8% on the MCR task, an absolute improvement of over 13 points compared to the best-performing baseline. This is a very strong result. The accompanying ablation studies (Tables 2 and the analysis for Table 3) are insightful, clearly demonstrating the impact of each feature family and confirming that the domain-specific features derived from Wikipedia and Freebase are crucial for success.
3.  **Clever use of domain-specific information.** A key strength is the model's feature engineering, which directly exploits Wikipedia's markup and its connection to Freebase. By using internal hyperlinks as a high-precision substitute for a general-purpose entity linker, the authors avoid a common source of pipeline errors. This is a well-justified and effective design choice that is well-suited to the target domain.
4.  **Demonstrated impact on the general coreference task.** The Dcoref++ experiment is a significant strength. By showing that their MCR classifier can improve a full coreference system's CoNLL F1 score by 3.3 absolute points, the authors prove that their work has practical value beyond the specific sub-task they defined. This makes the contribution more impactful for the coreference community.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited dataset size and concerns about generalizability.** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, this is a very small number for drawing general conclusions about performance on a resource as vast and varied as Wikipedia. A 10-fold cross-validation mitigates this to some extent, but the risk of overfitting to the specific style or topics of these 30 articles remains. The claims would be much stronger if evaluated on a larger, held-out test set.
2.  **Methodological novelty is limited.** The core approach is a standard SVM classifier with carefully engineered features. While the application and the features are novel and effective for the domain, the underlying machine learning technique is not new. The main contribution lies in the problem formulation and domain adaptation rather than a novel CR algorithm. This is acceptable, but it does limit the paper's broader methodological impact.
3.  **Baselines could be more direct.** The baselines are heuristics applied post-hoc to the outputs of full CR systems. While reasonable, they do not perfectly isolate the contribution of the Wikipedia-specific features. A more direct baseline would be to train the same SVM classifier using only generic features (e.g., from the `base` and `tag` feature sets) without the Wikipedia/Freebase knowledge. This would provide a clearer measure of the performance gain attributable to the novel, domain-specific features.
4.  **Lack of clarity regarding singleton mentions in data preparation.** Section 6.1 states that the training data was augmented with ~13,400 automatically extracted "singleton" mentions. This is potentially confusing. Are these mentions all treated as negative examples for the MCR task? If so, this dramatically alters the class balance and could make the classification task easier than it would be in a more natural setting. Clarifying the role of these mentions is crucial for interpreting the results.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more detail on the sampling strategy for the 30 documents in WCR? Given the small size, what gives you confidence that the dataset is representative of Wikipedia as a whole and that the results will generalize?
2.  Please clarify the role of the 13,400 automatically extracted mentions added to the training set in Section 6.1. Are they all treated as negative instances (i.e., not referring to the MC)? How does this curated training set composition compare to the distribution of mentions one would encounter when applying the system to a new, unseen Wikipedia article?
3.  The Dcoref++ experiment integrates your classifier as a sieve using predictions from a 10-fold cross-validation setup. Are the final scores reported in Table 5 an average over the 10 folds? Could you comment on the variance of performance across these folds, as high variance might suggest the gains are not stable?
