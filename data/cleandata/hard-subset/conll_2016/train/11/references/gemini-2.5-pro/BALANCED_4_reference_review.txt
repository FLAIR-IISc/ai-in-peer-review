### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of coreference resolution (CR) in Wikipedia, demonstrating that state-of-the-art systems trained on newswire data perform poorly on this genre. The authors define a specific and practical sub-task: Main Concept Resolution (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main topic (the "main concept"). They frame MCR as a binary classification task and develop a feature-rich SVM classifier that leverages Wikipedia's internal structure (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked data from Freebase to make its predictions. The paper's main contributions, as I see them, are:

1.  **A new dataset (WCR) and a compelling benchmark for coreference in Wikipedia.** The paper introduces a 30-document corpus annotated for coreference and uses it to show a significant performance drop for several state-of-the-art CR systems, empirically motivating the need for domain-specific solutions.
2.  **An effective, feature-engineered system for Main Concept Resolution.** The proposed SVM-based classifier substantially outperforms strong baselines built from SOTA CR systems, demonstrating the effectiveness of using domain-specific signals from Wikipedia and Freebase for this focused task.
3.  **A proof of concept for improving general CR via MCR.** The authors show that integrating their MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into the rule-based Dcoref system leads to a notable improvement in overall coreference resolution performance on their dataset.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Formulation.** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work. The benchmark results in Table 1 provide clear, quantitative evidence that genre adaptation is a real problem for CR systems when applied to Wikipedia. The decision to focus on the MCR sub-task is well-justified and practical, as resolving the main entity is a critical first step for many downstream applications like information extraction and knowledge base population.
2.  **Valuable New Resource and Empirical Analysis.** The introduction of the WCR dataset is a significant contribution. Although small, it provides a much-needed testbed for this domain. The thorough benchmarking of multiple modern CR systems is a service to the community, establishing a clear baseline and highlighting the performance gap. The detailed error analysis and examples in Figure 3 provide valuable qualitative insights.
3.  **Impressive Empirical Results.** The proposed classifier achieves an F1 score of nearly 89% on the MCR task, an absolute improvement of over 13 points compared to the best-performing baseline (Table 4). This is a substantial gain. The ablation studies (Tables 2 and 3) are thorough and clearly demonstrate the contribution of each feature family, confirming that the carefully engineered features from Wikipedia and Freebase are key to the system's success.
4.  **Intelligent Use of Domain-Specific Information.** A key strength of the approach is its direct use of Wikipedia's rich structure (hyperlinks, redirects) and Freebase data. This allows the system to source high-quality semantic information without relying on a separate, and potentially error-prone, general-purpose entity linking pipeline. This is a smart design choice that effectively tailors the solution to the specific characteristics of the domain.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Scale of the WCR Dataset.** The primary weakness is that the new WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, this is a very small sample size from which to draw general conclusions about a resource as vast and varied as Wikipedia. Performance metrics derived from 10-fold cross-validation on such a small dataset may not be stable or generalize well. This limitation tempers the significance of the reported performance gains and the benchmark results.
2.  **Incremental Methodological Novelty.** The core method—an SVM with engineered features—is standard. While the feature engineering is well-executed and effective for this specific task, the approach itself does not introduce a novel machine learning technique. The primary novelty lies in the task definition and the direct sourcing of features from Wikipedia's structure, rather than in the underlying classification algorithm.
3.  **Limited Scope of the Integration Experiment.** The authors demonstrate the utility of their MCR system by integrating it into the Stanford Dcoref system. However, Dcoref is an older, rule-based system. It would be far more compelling to show that this approach can also benefit a modern, top-performing statistical system like Scoref. The choice to only modify Dcoref feels somewhat limited and may be due to the convenience of its sieve architecture rather than its relevance as a SOTA system. This makes it harder to assess the full potential impact of the MCR module.
4.  **Reliance on a Defunct Knowledge Base.** The system relies heavily on Freebase, which has been deprecated for several years. This poses a significant challenge for reproducibility and future work. A modern system should be built using a sustained resource like Wikidata. While the migration might be straightforward, the paper does not acknowledge this limitation or discuss a path forward, which slightly dates the work.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you provide more detail on the sampling strategy used to select the 30 articles for the WCR dataset? Given the dataset's small size, how can we be confident that the results are representative of Wikipedia as a whole and not biased by the specific articles chosen? For example, what is the distribution of topics (people, organizations, events, etc.)?
2.  Your integration experiment was limited to the rule-based Dcoref system. Have you considered how your MCR classifier could be integrated into a modern statistical CR system like Scoref? What do you see as the primary challenges and potential benefits of such an integration?
3.  Your system's features are heavily dependent on Freebase, which is no longer maintained. Have you investigated migrating your feature extraction process to use Wikidata instead? Do you foresee any challenges, and would you expect a similar level of performance?
