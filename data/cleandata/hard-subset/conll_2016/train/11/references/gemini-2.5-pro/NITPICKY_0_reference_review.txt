### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of poor performance of standard coreference resolution (CR) systems on Wikipedia text. The authors define a sub-task, "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) of a given Wikipedia article. They propose a feature-based SVM classifier that leverages Wikipedia-specific markup and linked data from Freebase to perform this task. The authors' main contributions, as I see them, are:

1.  **A new annotated dataset (WCR) for coreference on Wikipedia.** The analysis accompanying this dataset provides strong evidence for the domain adaptation problem in CR, showing that several state-of-the-art systems perform significantly worse on Wikipedia text than on newswire.
2.  **A specialized classifier for the MCR task.** The system demonstrates strong performance on this sub-task, and the ablation study provides useful insights into the relative importance of different feature families derived from Wikipedia's structure.
3.  **A demonstration of the utility of MCR for full coreference resolution.** By integrating their classifier as a high-precision sieve into an existing rule-based system, the authors show a notable improvement in overall CR performance on their dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper clearly articulates a known but under-explored problem: the genre-sensitivity of CR systems. Focusing on Wikipedia is highly relevant given its ubiquity as a knowledge source in NLP. The sub-task of MCR is practical and useful for downstream applications like Information Extraction.
2.  **Valuable Resource and Analysis:** The introduction of the WCR dataset is a welcome contribution. Although small, the analysis presented in Table 1 is compelling, effectively quantifying the performance degradation of state-of-the-art systems and justifying the need for domain-specific approaches.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results on the Defined Task:** The proposed classifier achieves a substantial improvement of over 13 F1 points compared to the best-performing baseline on the MCR task. The feature ablation studies in Tables 2 and (the referenced but missing) 3 are thorough and provide a clear picture of what information sources are most beneficial.
4.  **Demonstrated Downstream Utility:** The authors go beyond solving the isolated MCR task and show its value for the broader CR problem. The Dcoref++ experiment, which improves a full CR system, is a strong argument for the practical relevance of this work.

### Weaknesses

Despite its strengths, the paper suffers from a significant number of methodological ambiguities, missing information, and formatting issues that severely undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Crucial Missing Information:** The paper is incomplete. **Table 3, Figure 1, and Figure 2 are all referenced but are not present in the document.** Table 3 is essential for understanding the non-pronominal classifier's ablation results. Figures 1 and 2 are critical for understanding the core data representation of mentions and main concepts, which is the foundation of the entire feature engineering process. This is a major oversight that makes a full evaluation of the work impossible.
2.  **Methodological Opacity:** The description of the methodology lacks the precision required for scientific replication.
    *   **Feature Engineering:** The feature descriptions are vague. For example, the "title" family of features includes "string similarities (exact match, partial match, one being the substring of another, sharing of a number of words, etc.)." What are the precise definitions of these similarities? The origins of the exact number of binary `tag` features ("34x4" and "2380") are left unexplained. The authors state they "experimented with a few hundred features," but the selection process for the "most promising ones" is not described.
    *   **Classifier Training:** Details on hyperparameter tuning are insufficient. The choice of a linear kernel and a penalty weight of 2.0 is justified only by a footnote stating "We tried with less success other configurations on a heldout dataset." The lack of detail on this process is a weakness.
3.  **Potentially Flawed Evaluation:**
    *   **Baseline Pronoun Heuristic:** The heuristic applied to all baselines in Section 3, which adds *all* occurrences of a pronoun if it appears once in a baseline chain, seems overly aggressive. This likely inflates baseline recall significantly. It is unclear if this same post-processing step was applied to the authors' own system for the comparison in Table 4. If not, the comparison is unfair.
    *   **Use of Cross-Validation Data:** Footnote 6 states that the Dcoref++ experiment uses "predicted results from 10-fold cross-validation." This is alarmingly ambiguous. If the classifier's predictions for the full dataset are used to build the sieve, this means that for each fold, the sieve is making decisions using a classifier that was trained on that fold's data. This constitutes a leak from the training set into the evaluation and would invalidate the results in Table 5. A correct protocol would involve retraining the classifier on the 9 training folds for each of the 10 splits of the Dcoref++ evaluation.
4.  **Numerous Formatting and Technical Errors:** The paper is riddled with minor but distracting errors that suggest a lack of careful preparation.
    *   **Anonymization/Placeholders:** The conclusion contains the placeholder "This will be available at www.somewhere.country," which is unprofessional.
    *   **Citations and Footnotes:** URLs are used directly in footnotes (2, 4) instead of being properly cited. Footnote 1 ("Grammatical words are not considered") is vague.
    *   **Typos and Inconsistencies:** There are several grammatical errors (e.g., "data performs poorly," "in a slight expense of precision"). The system names "Dcoref" and "Scoref" are capitalized inconsistently.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Please provide the missing Table 3 and Figures 1 and 2. Their absence makes it impossible to fully assess your methodology and results.
2.  Can you please clarify the experimental setup for the Dcoref++ system described in Section 6.4 and Footnote 6? Specifically, did you ensure that for each fold of the evaluation, the classifier used for the sieve was trained only on the other nine folds to prevent data leakage?
3.  Was the aggressive pronoun-expansion heuristic (Section 3) applied to your own system's output for the comparison in Table 4? If not, could you justify why this comparison to the modified baselines is fair?
4.  Could you provide more precise definitions for your features? For example, what constitutes a "partial match," and how were the precise numbers of binary POS tag features (e.g., 2380) derived?
