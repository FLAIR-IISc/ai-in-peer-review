### Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses the performance degradation of state-of-the-art coreference resolution (CR) systems when applied to Wikipedia articles, a domain distinct from the newswire text they are typically trained on. The authors introduce a specific sub-task, "Main Concept Resolution," which involves identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main topic (the "main concept" or MC).

To facilitate this research, the authors have created a new annotated corpus, WCR, consisting of 30 Wikipedia articles. They use this corpus to demonstrate that existing CR systems indeed perform poorly on this genre. Their proposed solution frames Main Concept Resolution as a binary classification problem. They train two Support Vector Machine (SVM) classifiers (one for pronominal and one for non-pronominal mentions) using a rich set of features derived from Wikipedia's internal structure (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and the Freebase knowledge base, avoiding the need for a separate, potentially noisy, entity linking step.

The proposed classifier is shown to significantly outperform several baselines derived from state-of-the-art CR systems. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate the practical utility of their approach by integrating the classifier as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref rule-based system, which results in improved performance on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia articles.

### Main Contributions

1.  **Creation and benchmarking of a new dataset for coreference in Wikipedia.** The paper introduces the WCR dataset, a new resource for a domain where standard CR systems are known to underperform. The authors provide a valuable service by benchmarking several strong, recent CR systems on this data, quantitatively establishing the domain adaptation problem and providing a solid baseline for future research.
2.  **An effective, feature-driven method for the Main Concept Resolution task.** The paper presents a simple but highly effective classification-based approach to identify all mentions of an article's main topic. The primary contribution here is the careful engineering and analysis of features derived from Wikipedia's unique structural properties and external knowledge bases, which leads to substantial performance gains over strong baselines.
3.  **A demonstration of how solving a sub-task can improve full coreference resolution.** By integrating their specialized classifier into an existing CR system (Dcoref), the authors show that accurately resolving the main concept chain can serve as a powerful anchor that improves performance on the general CR task within this domain.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Formulation.** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It starts from the widely acknowledged issue of domain shift for CR systems and provides concrete evidence of this performance drop on Wikipedia text using their new WCR dataset (Table 1). The formulation of the "Main Concept Resolution" task is a logical and practical simplification of the full problem, with clear applications for downstream tasks like information extraction.
2.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation.** The experimental design is sound and comprehensive. The authors first establish the shortcomings of existing systems, then evaluate their proposed method against a set of well-conceived baselines for the specific sub-task, and finally demonstrate the utility of their method in the context of the full CR task. The reported performance gains are substantial and statistically significant (e.g., an absolute F1 increase of over 13 points on the MC resolution task, Table 4).
3.  **Detailed Feature Analysis.** The paper provides a clear ablation study (Tables 2 and 3) that methodically demonstrates the contribution of each feature family. This analysis provides valuable insights into what sources of information (e.g., MC title, inferred type from the article's first sentence) are most crucial for this task, which is useful for future work. The feature engineering is well-justified and leverages the specific structure of Wikipedia effectively.
4.  **Resource Contribution.** The creation and promised release of the WCR dataset, along with the system's predictions across all of English Wikipedia, represent a significant and valuable contribution to the NLP community. This will enable replication and encourage further research into coreference resolution beyond newswire.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Core Method.** The proposed solution is a feature-based SVM, a standard classification technique. While the feature engineering is strong, the underlying machine learning methodology lacks novelty compared to the end-to-end neural architectures that currently dominate coreference resolution research. The paper does not explore how these more modern architectures could be adapted to the Main Concept Resolution task, which would have made for a more forward-looking contribution. The baselines are also post-hoc heuristics on CR system outputs, rather than systems trained for the binary task directly.
2.  **Small Dataset Size.** The WCR corpus consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state that they sampled for diversity, this is a very small number on which to base general conclusions about performance on a resource as vast and varied as Wikipedia. A larger-scale annotation effort would be needed to build confidence that the models and findings will generalize across the full spectrum of Wikipedia articles.
3.  **Dependence on Specific Resources.** The approach relies heavily on the rich markup of Wikipedia and the structured data in Freebase. This makes the method highly effective for its target domain but limits its transferability to other genres that lack such curated metadata. Furthermore, the reliance on Freebase, a deprecated knowledge base, is a concern for the long-term reproducibility and maintenance of the system.
4.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison in the Full CR Experiment.** The Dcoref++ system incorporates a classifier trained (via cross-validation) on the WCR test domain. In contrast, the competing SOTA systems are used off-the-shelf without any domain adaptation or fine-tuning on WCR. This setup demonstrates the benefit of in-domain training but does not conclusively prove that the proposed sieve-based integration is superior to other adaptation methods, such as simply fine-tuning a statistical model like Scoref on the WCR data.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The choice of a feature-based SVM is a departure from current trends favoring end-to-end neural models. Could you elaborate on the reasoning for this choice? Did you consider adapting a model like Scoref for this binary task, for instance, by treating the main concept as a special antecedent and training the model's mention-ranking component accordingly?
2.  The Dcoref++ experiment shows a significant improvement. To better contextualize this gain, have you considered a comparative experiment where a strong statistical system like Scoref is fine-tuned on the WCR training folds? This would help disentangle the benefits of your specific approach from the general benefits of in-domain training.
3.  Given that Freebase is no longer actively maintained, have you considered the feasibility of migrating your feature extraction process to a more current knowledge graph like Wikidata? This would seem crucial for the long-term usability and reproducibility of your work.
