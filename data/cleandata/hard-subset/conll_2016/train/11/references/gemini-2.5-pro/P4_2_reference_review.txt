### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of coreference resolution (CR) in Wikipedia articles, observing that state-of-the-art systems trained on newswire data perform poorly on this genre. The authors define a specific sub-task called "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR), which involves identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main concept (MC). To facilitate this research, they introduce a new annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles, WCR. Their proposed approach frames MCR as a binary classification task, training two separate SVM classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using a rich set of features derived from Wikipedia's markup, its hyperlink structure, and Freebase. The authors create several baselines by adapting existing CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref) to the MCR task. Their proposed classifier significantly outperforms these baselines. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of their MCR classifier by integrating it as a high-precision sieve into the rule-based Dcoref system, showing a notable improvement on the general CR task for the WCR dataset.

### Contributions

1.  **Formulation and Benchmarking of the Main Concept Resolution Task on Wikipedia:** The primary contribution is the formalization of the MCR task for Wikipedia text. This includes the creation and proposed release of the WCR dataset, a new annotated resource. The authors use this dataset to provide a compelling benchmark that demonstrates a significant performance gap when applying state-of-the-art coreference systems to the Wikipedia genre, thus motivating the need for domain-specific solutions.
2.  **A High-Performing Feature-Based System for MCR:** The paper presents a strong baseline system for the MCR task. While the core machine learning method (SVM) is standard, the system's effectiveness stems from well-designed, domain-specific features that directly leverage Wikipedia's structure (e.g., links, redirects) and linked knowledge bases (Freebase). The system achieves high performance (88.79 F1), establishing a robust benchmark for future work on this task.
3.  **Demonstration of Utility for General-Purpose Coreference Resolution:** The authors show that solving the MCR sub-task can benefit the broader CR task. By integrating their MCR classifier into the Dcoref system as a new top-level sieve, they achieve a 4-point absolute improvement in CoNLL F1 score, validating the practical applicability of their focused approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem and Clear Task Definition:** The paper presents a clear and convincing argument for its necessity. The initial experiment reported in Table 1, which shows a dramatic performance drop (10-17 F1 points) for multiple state-of-the-art CR systems when moving from OntoNotes to the WCR dataset, provides strong evidence that coreference in Wikipedia is an open and challenging problem. The proposed MCR task is a well-scoped, intuitive, and useful simplification of the full problem.
2.  **Thorough and Sound Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive. The authors compare their method against four distinct, reasonably designed baselines derived from two different SOTA systems. The use of 10-fold cross-validation, standard metrics, and a detailed ablation study (Tables 2 and 3) provides a clear understanding of the contribution of each feature family. The extrinsic evaluation via the Dcoref++ system (Section 6.4) further strengthens the paper's claims by demonstrating practical utility.
3.  **Creation of a New Public Resource:** The introduction and promised release of the WCR dataset, along with the extracted mentions and features, is a valuable contribution. It enables reproducibility and provides a foundation for the community to build upon, encouraging further research into coreference on encyclopedic texts.
4.  **Effective Use of Domain-Specific Information:** A key technical strength is the direct exploitation of Wikipedia's rich structure and its links to Freebase. By using human-curated hyperlinks to infer entity attributes, the approach avoids the cascading errors common in pipelines that rely on separate, potentially noisy named-entity linking or wikification systems. This design choice is well-justified and contributes significantly to the system's high performance.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Dataset Size and Generalizability:** The primary weakness is the small size of the WCR dataset, which consists of only 30 documents. While the authors state that the articles were sampled for diversity, this is a very small sample from which to draw broad conclusions about performance on Wikipedia. The results, especially from a 10-fold cross-validation, may have high variance and might not generalize robustly to the full diversity of articles on Wikipedia.
2.  **Lack of Methodological Novelty:** The core technical approach is a feature-engineered SVM, a standard classification technique that has been superseded in many NLP tasks by end-to-end neural methods. While effective, the work does not introduce new machine learning techniques. Its contribution lies more in the task definition, feature engineering, and resource creation rather than in advancing core CR methodology.
3.  **Limited Exploration of Integration with Modern Architectures:** The integration experiment is confined to Dcoref, a rule-based sieve architecture. It is unclear how the proposed MCR classifier could be effectively integrated into modern, end-to-end neural CR systems like Scoref, which have a different architectural paradigm (e.g., mention-pair scoring). A discussion of potential integration strategies for such models would have made the contribution feel more current and broadly applicable.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given the WCR dataset contains only 30 documents, could you comment on the stability of the 10-fold cross-validation results? Specifically, what was the standard deviation of the F1 scores across the folds? This would help in assessing the robustness of the reported performance gains.
2.  The Dcoref++ experiment successfully demonstrates the utility of your classifier in a rule-based system. What are your thoughts on integrating the MCR model's predictions into an end-to-end neural system like Scoref? For instance, could the binary prediction (or the classifier's confidence score) be used as an additional feature for the mention-ranking component in such models?
3.  The heuristic applied to the baselines to improve pronoun recall (adding all instances of a pronoun if one instance is linked to the MC) appears quite powerful. Could you provide the performance of the baselines (specifically B1-B4 for Dcoref and Scoref) without this heuristic? This would help to more clearly delineate the performance of the underlying CR systems on the task versus the effect of this specific post-processing step.
