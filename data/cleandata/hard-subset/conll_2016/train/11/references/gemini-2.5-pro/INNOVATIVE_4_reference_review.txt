### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces and formalizes the task of "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR) in Wikipedia, which consists of identifying all mentions within a Wikipedia article that refer to the article's main topic. The authors argue that standard coreference resolution (CR) systems, trained predominantly on newswire text, perform poorly on this genre. They create a new annotated dataset, WCR, to demonstrate this performance gap. They then propose a binary classification approach using two SVMs (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) with a rich set of features derived directly from Wikipedia's structure and linked Freebase data. Their approach substantially outperforms baselines built from state-of-the-art CR systems on the MCR task. Finally, they show that integrating their MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into a rule-based CR system (Dcoref) improves its performance on the full coreference task for Wikipedia articles.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel and Practical Problem Formulation:** The paper's most significant contribution is the formalization of Main Concept Resolution as a distinct and valuable NLP task. By isolating the most frequent and arguably most important coreference chain in an encyclopedic article, the authors define a tractable sub-problem with immediate applications for information extraction and document understanding. This is a bold conceptual shift from trying to solve the entirety of coreference in a new domain.
2.  **A New Dataset and Benchmark for a Neglected Domain:** The authors create and analyze the WCR dataset, providing a crucial resource for studying coreference outside of newswire. Their benchmarking of state-of-the-art systems on WCR provides compelling evidence of a domain adaptation problem and strongly motivates their work and future research in this area.
3.  **An Effective, Domain-Specific Model:** The paper presents a simple yet powerful classification model that significantly outperforms complex, general-purpose coreference systems on the MCR task. The key insight is to bypass noisy, general pipelines like entity linking by directly exploiting the rich, semi-structured information inherent to Wikipedia (e.g., titles, redirects, hyperlinks), which proves to be a highly effective strategy.

### Strengths
1.  **Clear Conceptual Novelty:** I find the central idea of this paper—reframing the problem to focus on the main concept—to be highly innovative and refreshing. Instead of another incremental improvement on a standard benchmark, this work carves out a new, well-motivated problem. The insight that an article about a concept *is itself* a rich source of structured knowledge for resolving references *to that concept* is elegant and powerful. This is exactly the kind of creative, problem-driven research that moves the field forward.
2.  **Compelling Motivation and Results:** The paper does an excellent job of motivating the work. The initial experiment showing the dramatic performance drop of top-tier CR systems on Wikipedia text (Table 1) is stark and convincing. The subsequent results, where the proposed model achieves an absolute F1 gain of over 13 points against the best baseline (Table 4), provide unequivocal evidence for the value of their specialized approach. This large performance delta underscores that the contribution is not minor but a significant leap forward for this specific task.
3.  **Intelligent Use of Domain-Specific Knowledge:** I am particularly impressed by the feature engineering, which cleverly leverages the unique properties of the Wikipedia domain. By using internal links as a substitute for a full entity linking system, and mining redirects and article structure for name variants and entity types, the authors build a robust system from high-quality, human-curated signals. This is a much bolder and more interesting approach than simply fine-tuning a large pre-trained model, as it relies on genuine insight into the data's structure.
4.  **Demonstrated Utility for the General Task:** The final experiment integrating the MCR classifier into Dcoref is a crucial addition. It shows that solving this specialized sub-task is not a dead end but can directly benefit the broader, more complex task of full coreference resolution. The 3.3 point F1 improvement is a substantial gain in this space and validates MCR as a useful building block.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Scale of the WCR Dataset:** The primary limitation is the small size of the WCR dataset, with only 30 annotated articles. While the authors note that they sampled for diversity, this small scale raises natural questions about how well the findings and the trained model will generalize to the vast and heterogeneous content of Wikipedia. However, given the novelty of the task and the prohibitive cost of annotation, I view this as an acceptable starting point rather than a fatal flaw. The strength of the core idea outweighs the limited initial validation.
2.  **"Classical" Methodology:** The use of SVMs and extensive feature engineering could be viewed as dated in the current deep learning era. The work does not compare against a neural baseline trained specifically for the MCR task. While a neural approach might yield further gains, the current method serves as a strong and interpretable proof-of-concept for the task itself. The paper's contribution lies in the problem definition and feature-source insights, not in pioneering a new learning algorithm, and for that, the chosen method is perfectly adequate.
3.  **Ad-hoc System Integration:** The integration of the MCR classifier into Dcoref as a first, immutable sieve is a straightforward but somewhat brittle mechanism. A more deeply integrated approach might yield better synergies. Nonetheless, this experiment successfully demonstrates the potential of the MCR component, and perfecting the integration can be left to future work. It is sufficient to prove the principle.

### Overall Recommendation: Accept
This paper presents a novel, well-motivated, and interesting contribution to the field of coreference resolution. Its main strength lies in its bold reconceptualization of the problem for the important domain of Wikipedia. The proposed "Main Concept Resolution" task is a clever simplification that proves highly effective. The authors support their claims with strong empirical results on a new, valuable dataset, and demonstrate that their specialized tool can also improve a general-purpose CR system. While the evaluation scale is limited and the methodology is classical, these are minor points when weighed against the novelty and potential impact of the core idea. This work opens up a promising new direction for domain-specific coreference resolution, and I recommend its acceptance without hesitation.
