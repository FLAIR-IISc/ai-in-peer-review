**Summary of the Paper**
This paper addresses the task of "Main Concept Resolution" (MCR) in Wikipedia, which consists of identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) of an article. The authors argue that standard coreference resolution (CR) systems, trained on newswire, perform poorly on Wikipedia text. They introduce a new, small dataset called WCR (30 articles) to demonstrate this performance gap. Their proposed approach frames MCR as a binary classification task, training two SVM classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using features derived from Wikipedia's structure and Freebase. The authors show that their classifier outperforms several baselines constructed from state-of-the-art CR systems. Finally, they integrate their classifier into the Dcoref rule-based system as a high-precision sieve, demonstrating an improvement in overall coreference performance on their WCR dataset.

**Contributions**
1.  The introduction of WCR, a new annotated dataset for coreference resolution on Wikipedia articles. The accompanying analysis of several state-of-the-art systems on this dataset provides clear evidence of the domain adaptation problem for CR.
2.  A focused system for the sub-task of Main Concept Resolution that effectively leverages Wikipedia-specific structural information and linked data from Freebase, showing strong performance on this specific task.

**Strengths**
1.  **Problem Formulation:** The paper identifies and addresses a practical and important problem. Wikipedia is a ubiquitous resource, yet coreference resolution for this genre is under-explored. The focused task of Main Concept Resolution is a sensible and useful simplification of the full CR problem.
2.  **Dataset and Analysis:** The creation of the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution. The empirical results in Table 1, showing a significant performance drop for multiple state-of-the-art systems when moving from OntoNotes to WCR, strongly motivate the need for genre-specific CR models and provide a useful benchmark for future work.
3.  **Approach and Integration:** The approach of using a dedicated classifier for the MC is intuitive. The features, drawing on Wikipedia markup and Freebase, are well-motivated. The experiment in Section 6.4, which integrates the MCR classifier into a full CR system (Dcoref++) and shows improved performance, is a compelling demonstration of the utility of their approach beyond the isolated MCR task.

**Weaknesses**
Despite its merits, the paper suffers from a significant number of methodological omissions, a lack of experimental rigor, and numerous minor formatting and clarity issues. These flaws collectively undermine the confidence in the results and the paper's contribution.

1.  **Critical Methodological Opacity:** The paper omits crucial details about the features and methodology, making the work impossible to reproduce.
    *   **Feature Definitions:** The definitions for several feature families are unacceptably vague. The "10 boolean features" for string similarity (Section 4.2.1) are not enumerated. The `distance` feature for pronouns relies on identifying a "compatible" entity, but the criteria for compatibility are never defined.
    *   **Unspecified Resources:** The paper mentions using the Wu and Palmer (1994) similarity score, but fails to state the lexical resource (presumably WordNet) used to compute it. Similarly, the POS tagset used (which determines the `tag` features) is not specified.
    *   **Feature Engineering:** The derivation of the feature vector sizes is opaque. The paper states the `tag` features for non-pronominal mentions become "34×4 binary features" and for pronominal mentions become "2380 binary features" without any explanation of how these numbers are derived.

2.  **Lack of Experimental Rigor and Missing Results:** The experimental evaluation is not sufficiently robust, and a key piece of evidence is missing entirely.
    *   **Missing Table:** The paper explicitly references "Table 3" in Section 6.3 to report results for the non-pronominal mentions classifier. This table is not present in the paper, creating a major gap in the results. This is a critical oversight.
    *   **Small Dataset and No Significance Testing:** The WCR dataset contains only 30 documents. While valuable, this is a very small sample size. Given this, it is imperative to report statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons. The paper makes numerous claims of improvement (e.g., Dcoref++ over Dcoref, various feature ablations) without any statistical validation, making it difficult to ascertain if these gains are meaningful or simply due to chance on a small test set.
    *   **Questionable Dcoref++ Evaluation:** The methodology for evaluating Dcoref++ is concerning. The authors state they "use predicted results from 10-fold cross-validation" to build the new sieve. This needs much clearer explanation. To avoid any potential data leakage, the classifier integrated into Dcoref++ should be trained strictly on training folds and evaluated on a held-out test fold in a cross-validation loop. The current description is ambiguous and raises concerns about proper data hygiene.

3.  **Numerous Minor but Distracting Flaws:** The manuscript is unpolished and contains many small errors that, in aggregate, detract from its professional quality.
    *   **Citation and Formatting Issues:** Citation format is inconsistent, with double parentheses used in the introduction (e.g., `(e.g., (Hirshman and Chinchor, 1998))`). Footnotes are used to provide raw URLs for Jena and the CoNLL scorer, which is improper formatting for an academic paper.
    *   **Vague Language:** The paper relies on vague descriptions. For example, Footnote 1 states "Grammatical words are not considered for matching" without defining what constitutes a "grammatical word." The feature selection process is hand-wavingly described as "focusing on the most promising ones that we found simple enough to compute."
    *   **Inconsistencies:** There are minor textual inconsistencies, such as capitalizing "Section" in some places but not others ("section 6"). The caption for Table 4 is inaccurate as it includes "this work", which is not a baseline.

While the paper presents a promising direction of research and a useful dataset, its execution is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from critical methodological omissions (missing table, undefined features) to a lack of experimental rigor (no significance tests). The sheer volume of these issues prevents a thorough and confident assessment of the work's validity and reproducibility. Therefore, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current state.
