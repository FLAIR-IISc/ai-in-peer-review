## Peer Review: Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the task of identifying mentions of a Wikipedia article's main concept (MC) – a task the authors frame as binary classification. Recognizing the poor performance of generic coreference resolution (CR) systems on Wikipedia text, they propose a system leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase knowledge, combined with carefully engineered features. They evaluate their approach against established CR systems and demonstrate improvement, further showing integration into a rule-based system boosts overall CR performance. The authors also release a dataset (WCR) for this task.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A Dedicated Dataset (WCR):** The creation and release of a small but focused dataset specifically for evaluating main concept resolution in Wikipedia is the most significant contribution. This provides a much-needed resource for research in this area.
2. **Empirical Demonstration of Wikipedia-Specific CR Challenges:** The paper clearly illustrates the performance gap between general CR systems and the specific challenges posed by Wikipedia text, reinforcing the need for specialized approaches.
3. **A Competitive Baseline & Feature Engineering:** The proposed approach, while not groundbreaking in terms of methodology, offers a competitive baseline with thoughtfully chosen features that leverage the unique characteristics of Wikipedia.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the "main concept resolution" task and convincingly argues why existing CR systems struggle with Wikipedia. The motivation for focusing on this task, given Wikipedia’s importance as a knowledge source, is well-articulated.
2. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:**  The experiments are well-designed and demonstrate a clear improvement over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art CR systems.  The inclusion of results on the CoNLL dataset provides a useful benchmark. The ablation study of features (Table 2) provides valuable insight into feature importance.
3. **Resource Contribution:** The release of the WCR dataset and the mention database is a significant contribution to the community. This enables reproducibility and further research in this area.
4. **Practical Approach:**  The exploitation of readily available information from Wikipedia markup and Freebase makes the proposed approach practical and avoids the complexities (and errors) of full-fledged named entity linking.
5. **Improvement to Existing System:** Showing a performance gain by integrating the classifier into an existing rule-based system demonstrates the practical utility of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** The WCR dataset, while valuable, is relatively small (30 documents). This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results and the statistical significance of the improvements observed. Results may be highly sensitive to the specific articles selected.
2. **Methodological Novelty:** The core methodology (binary classification with engineered features) is not novel.  The authors primarily *apply* existing techniques to a specific task, rather than presenting a fundamentally new approach. The improvement comes from the careful feature engineering and adaptation to the Wikipedia domain, rather than a novel algorithm.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis Depth:** While the paper mentions a brief error analysis (Figure 3), a more thorough analysis of the types of errors the system makes would be beneficial.  Understanding *why* the system fails in certain cases would provide valuable insight for future improvements.  For instance, a detailed breakdown of false positives and false negatives would be helpful.
4. **Limited Baselines:** The comparison is mainly against a few CR systems.  Exploring more diverse baselines, particularly those specifically designed for Wikipedia or knowledge graph completion, might provide a more comprehensive evaluation.
5. **Presentation could be streamlined:** The writing style is somewhat verbose and repetitive. The paper could benefit from more concise explanations and a clearer focus on the key contributions.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Expansion:** What are your plans for expanding the WCR dataset?  Would you consider a crowdsourcing effort to increase its size and diversity?
2. **Generalizability:** Given the small size of WCR, how confident are you that the reported performance gains will generalize to larger and more diverse sets of Wikipedia articles? Have you performed any preliminary experiments on a larger, unseen dataset?
3. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the types of errors your system makes, including examples and potential causes? Specifically, what types of mentions are most frequently misclassified (e.g., complex noun phrases, ambiguous pronouns)?
4. **Feature Ablation Explanation:**  The feature ablation study (Table 2) shows significant impact of features. Could you elaborate on *why* each added feature family is so effective?  What linguistic or domain-specific knowledge explains these gains?
5. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of your approach compared to the baselines? Are the feature extraction steps particularly expensive?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper refers to a website for data release but does not provide a specific URL. Please include a link.
*   Consider discussing the limitations of Freebase as a knowledge source, particularly given its deprecation and the rise of alternatives like Wikidata.
*   The phrase "Dcoref++" feels a bit awkward. Consider a more descriptive name.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution in the form of a new dataset and demonstrates the challenges of adapting CR systems to the Wikipedia domain. While the methodological novelty is limited, the empirical results are solid and the resource contribution is significant. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly by providing a more detailed error analysis and clarifying the generalizability of the results, would strengthen the paper considerably.
