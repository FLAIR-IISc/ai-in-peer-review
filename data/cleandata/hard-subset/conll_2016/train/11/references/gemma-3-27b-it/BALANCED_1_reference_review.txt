This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution specifically tailored to Wikipedia articles, focusing on identifying mentions of the “main concept” (MC) of an article. The authors revisit this task, providing a testbed (WCR dataset) and comparing state-of-the-art systems alongside a novel approach leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase information.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **The WCR Dataset:** The creation and release of the WCR dataset is the most significant contribution. This dataset is specifically designed for evaluating coreference resolution systems on Wikipedia text, a domain known to be challenging for systems trained on newswire data. The annotation guidelines (following OntoNotes) and the size of the dataset (30 articles, 60k tokens) make it a valuable resource for the community.
2. **A Feature-Rich Classifier for MC Coreference:** The authors propose a binary classification approach for identifying MC mentions, employing a comprehensive set of features derived from Wikipedia’s structure (hyperlinks, redirects) and Freebase. This is a solid engineering effort and demonstrates the potential of leveraging domain-specific knowledge.
3. **Demonstration of Domain Adaptation Benefit:** The authors convincingly demonstrate that adapting coreference resolution techniques to the Wikipedia domain yields performance improvements. The experimental results show their approach outperforms generic coreference engines, and integrating it into a rule-based system (Dcoref) further enhances coreference chain detection.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of applying coreference resolution to Wikipedia and motivates the need for domain-specific approaches. The limitations of existing systems trained on newswire data are well-established.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing their approach to several state-of-the-art baselines.  The use of a 10-fold cross-validation procedure adds to the robustness of the results.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The extensive feature engineering, combining information from Wikipedia markup, Freebase, and linguistic features, is a major strength. The breakdown of feature impact (Table 2) provides valuable insights.
4. **Practical Integration and Improvement:** The integration of the proposed classifier into an existing rule-based system (Dcoref++) and the resulting performance gain demonstrates the practical applicability of the research.
5. **Resource Contribution:** The authors’ commitment to releasing the WCR dataset and a dump of MC mentions is commendable and will greatly benefit the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dataset Size:** While valuable, the WCR dataset is relatively small (30 articles). This limits the statistical power of the experiments and the generalizability of the findings.  Results on a larger dataset would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Limited Baseline Diversity:** The selection of baselines, while including prominent systems, could be more diverse. Exploring other coreference resolution approaches, particularly those specifically designed for knowledge graphs, might provide a more comprehensive comparison.
3. **Feature Selection Justification:** While the feature ablation study (Table 2) is helpful, the initial selection of “a few hundred features” feels somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic feature selection method or justification for the chosen features could strengthen the work.
4. **Error Analysis Depth:** The error analysis, while mentioning some examples in Figure 3, could be more in-depth. A more systematic analysis of common error types would provide valuable insights for future research.
5. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The paper reports performance improvements but doesn't consistently include statistical significance tests to demonstrate whether the observed differences are truly meaningful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What were the specific criteria used to select the 30 articles for the WCR dataset? Were efforts made to ensure diversity in topic, length, and structure?
2.  Could you elaborate on the challenges encountered during the annotation process for the WCR dataset? Were there any disagreements among annotators, and if so, how were they resolved?
3.  Given the limited size of the WCR dataset, have you considered techniques like data augmentation or transfer learning to improve the generalization performance of your classifier?
4.  The paper mentions noisy redirects in Wikipedia impacting performance. How could these be filtered or accounted for to improve the accuracy of the system?
5.  What is the computational cost of adding the new sieve to Dcoref++? Does it significantly increase processing time?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise.
*   The related work section is adequate but could be expanded to include more recent advances in knowledge-aware coreference resolution.
*   The link to the dataset is currently "www.somewhere.country." Please provide a valid URL in the final version of the paper.

**Overall:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of coreference resolution. The WCR dataset is a significant resource, and the proposed approach demonstrates the benefits of adapting coreference techniques to the specific characteristics of Wikipedia text. While the small dataset size and limited baseline diversity are weaknesses, the overall quality of the work and the potential impact of the findings justify acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
