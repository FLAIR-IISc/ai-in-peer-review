## Peer Review: Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of the "main concept" (MC) within Wikipedia articles. Recognizing the limitations of coreference resolution (CR) systems trained on newswire data when applied to Wikipedia, the authors propose a binary classification approach to detect MC mentions. They leverage features extracted from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, evaluating their approach against state-of-the-art CR engines and demonstrating improvements, both as a standalone system and integrated into a rule-based CR system. The authors also provide a dataset (WCR) for this specific task.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Focused Task Definition & Dataset:** The paper clearly defines a specific and useful coreference subtask – identifying mentions of the main concept within Wikipedia articles – and importantly, provides a dedicated dataset (WCR) for evaluating systems on this task. This is arguably the most significant contribution, filling a gap in resources for Wikipedia-specific CR research.
2. **Demonstrated Performance Improvement:** The authors show their binary classification approach outperforms existing generic CR systems on the WCR dataset, indicating the value of tailoring methods to the unique characteristics of Wikipedia text.
3. **Feature Engineering for Wikipedia:** The paper provides a thoughtful selection of features specifically designed to exploit the structural information available in Wikipedia (markup, links, Freebase integration), offering insights into effective feature engineering for this domain.



**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem: existing CR systems struggle with Wikipedia due to domain mismatch. This is a well-established issue and makes the work highly relevant to the NLP community.
2. **Sound Experimental Setup:** The authors perform a thorough evaluation, comparing against multiple strong baselines (existing CR systems) and utilizing a cross-validation scheme. The inclusion of both standalone performance and integration into an existing rule-based system adds further robustness.
3. **Detailed Feature Description:** The detailed description of the features is a significant strength. It provides valuable insight into what aspects of the text and its associated knowledge graph are useful for MC identification. The ablation study (Table 2) is particularly helpful in understanding feature importance.
4. **Publicly Available Resources:** The commitment to releasing the WCR dataset and a dump of identified MC mentions is commendable and will greatly benefit future research in this area.
5. **Clear Writing & Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it easy to understand the proposed approach and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** The WCR dataset, consisting of only 30 documents, is quite small. While the authors acknowledge this, it limits the generalizability of the results and raises concerns about the statistical significance of the performance improvements.  A larger and more diverse dataset would substantially strengthen the claims.
2. **Baseline Reliance on Existing Systems:** While comparing against existing CR systems is good, the baselines are somewhat limited by the performance of those systems *on this task*. Their relatively poor performance on WCR might inflate the perceived gains from the proposed approach. A more robust baseline could involve adapting existing systems specifically for the MC identification task.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. While Figure 3 shows a few examples, a systematic analysis of the types of errors made by the system would provide valuable insights into areas for improvement.  For example, are errors more common with ambiguous concepts, or in specific sections of articles?
4. **Simplistic Classification Framing:** Framing the task as a binary classification problem feels somewhat limiting.  Coreference is inherently a relational task, and a more sophisticated approach that directly models the relationships between mentions might yield better results.  This is not a fatal flaw, but a potential avenue for future work.
5. **Limited Discussion of Scalability:** While the approach seems promising, the paper doesn't address the scalability of the feature extraction process to the entire Wikipedia corpus. Some features (e.g., querying Freebase for every mention) could become computationally expensive.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Dataset Expansion:** What are your plans for expanding the WCR dataset?  Will you be releasing more data in the future, and if so, what is the expected size and diversity?
2.  **Baseline Improvement:** Have you considered any strategies to specifically adapt the baseline CR systems to the MC identification task, beyond simply running them and using their output?  For example, could you retrain them on a larger corpus of Wikipedia articles?
3.  **Error Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by your system?  What are the most common sources of errors?
4.  **Computational Cost:** Can you discuss the computational cost of feature extraction, particularly for features that rely on external resources like Freebase?  How would this scale to the entire Wikipedia corpus?
5. **Alternative Modeling:** Have you considered exploring alternative modeling approaches beyond binary classification, such as directly modeling coreference relationships between mentions?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "www.somewhere.country" is a placeholder and needs to be replaced with the actual URL.
*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work on knowledge-aware coreference resolution.
*   Consider including a table summarizing the key characteristics of the WCR dataset (e.g., average article length, number of mentions per article, proportion of pronominal mentions).




**Overall Recommendation:**

Accept with Minor Revisions.

Despite the limitations related to dataset size and error analysis, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of coreference resolution. The clear task definition, the publicly available dataset, and the demonstrated performance improvements make this work worthy of acceptance. Addressing the questions raised above and expanding the error analysis would further strengthen the paper.
