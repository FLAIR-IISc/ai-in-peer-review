This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of the main concept (MC) within Wikipedia articles, a problem distinct from general coreference resolution. The authors frame this as a binary classification problem and compare their approach against existing CR systems and several baselines. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the creation and evaluation of a dedicated dataset (WCR) for this specific task, coupled with a feature-rich binary classifier tailored to the characteristics of Wikipedia text and external knowledge sources like Freebase. A secondary, albeit less substantial, contribution is demonstrating that adapting a state-of-the-art rule-based system (Lee et al., 2013) with their classifier yields modest improvements in coreference chain detection.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Task & Dataset:** The focus on coreference *to the main concept* within Wikipedia is a legitimate and valuable distinction from standard coreference. The WCR dataset is a significant asset. The authors correctly identify the limitations of applying general CR systems to this specific domain and provide a resource for future research. The dataset size, while relatively small, appears thoughtfully constructed with attention to article characteristics.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate a thorough understanding of relevant features. They’ve judiciously combined features derived from Wikipedia markup, Freebase, and established coreference literature. The separation of features for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions is a sensible approach, indicating a nuanced understanding of the challenges. The feature ablation study (Table 2) provides valuable insight into feature importance.
3. **Clear Experimental Setup & Baselines:** The experimental setup is well-defined, with clear explanations of the baselines used. Comparing against both rule-based and statistical state-of-the-art systems is commendable. The inclusion of results on the CoNLL 2012 dataset provides a useful point of reference.
4. **Performance Improvement:** The reported performance improvements over the baselines, while not dramatic, are statistically significant and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The integration of the classifier into Dcoref and the resulting improvement (Table 5) further support the value of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size & Generalizability:** The WCR dataset, comprising only 30 documents, is severely limited in size. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results and the robustness of the models. While the authors acknowledge this, it remains a critical flaw. The results might be heavily influenced by the specific characteristics of these 30 articles.
2. **Overly Complex Feature Set & Lack of Justification:** While the comprehensive feature set is a strength in terms of exploration, many features appear redundant or lack clear justification. The extensive list of features (seven families with numerous sub-features) increases model complexity and the risk of overfitting, especially given the small dataset. A more rigorous feature selection process with clear rationale for each feature would have been beneficial. The justification for some features (e.g., specific POS tag combinations) feels weak.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The authors mention error analysis (e.g., Figure 3), but it is superficial. A more in-depth error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by the system (e.g., ambiguity, lexical variation, long-distance dependencies), would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and guide future improvements. The example errors presented are anecdotal and insufficient to draw broader conclusions.
4. **Incremental Improvement Rather Than Breakthrough:** While the results are positive, the improvement over strong baselines isn't substantial enough to warrant strong acceptance. The work largely refines existing techniques for a specific task rather than presenting a truly novel paradigm. The claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines" feels overstated given the highly specialized nature of the task and the limited dataset.
5. **Formatting Inconsistencies & Typos:** Throughout the paper, there are numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, spacing issues) and typos. While not critical, these detract from the professionalism of the presentation. For example, in Section 6.1, "do not use gold mention attributes" is awkwardly phrased.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the process for expanding the WCR dataset? What are the plans to address the issue of limited data size and ensure generalizability of the results?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the inclusion of each feature family?  Specifically, which features within each family contribute the most to performance gains, and why?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by the system and providing specific examples of each error type?
4.   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., C in the SVM classifier, penalty weight for the negative class)? Was a formal hyperparameter optimization procedure employed?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The URL for the dataset release ("www.somewhere.country") is a placeholder and needs to be updated.
*   Consider adding a discussion of limitations in the Conclusion section.
*   Review the manuscript carefully for grammatical errors and formatting inconsistencies before final submission.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the creation of a valuable dataset and a thoughtful approach to the problem, the limited dataset size, overly complex feature set, and incremental nature of the improvements raise significant concerns. While the paper has merit, I lean towards **rejection** due to the aforementioned weaknesses. Addressing the dataset size and feature complexity issues would significantly improve the quality of the work and increase its potential impact.
