This paper addresses the task of main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles, framing it as a binary classification problem to identify mentions referring to the article’s central topic. The authors present a dataset (WCR) and evaluate several baselines built upon existing coreference resolution systems, alongside their own feature-based classifier. The primary contribution appears to be the creation of the WCR dataset and the demonstration of the limitations of general coreference systems when applied to Wikipedia text, rather than a fundamentally novel coreference method. A secondary contribution is the observed performance gain when integrating their classifier into a rule-based system (Dcoref).

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Creation:** The WCR dataset is a valuable resource. While the size is modest (30 documents), the annotation scheme focusing on main concept coreference is a necessary step for research in this specific area. The authors acknowledge the dataset’s limitations in size but argue for its representativeness, which is reasonable.
2. **Empirical Validation & Baseline Comparison:**  The authors rigorously compare their approach against several established coreference systems (Dcoref, Durrett & Klein, Martschat & Strube, Clark & Manning). The consistent outperformance of their method, even with relatively simple features, provides some evidence of its effectiveness, and importantly, highlights the challenges of applying general CR systems to Wikipedia.
3. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the task of main concept resolution and justifies its relevance within the context of information extraction from Wikipedia.  The motivation for focusing on the main concept is understandable.
4. **Systematic Feature Engineering:** The feature set, while not groundbreaking, is thoughtfully designed, leveraging both Wikipedia-specific markup and external knowledge from Freebase. The ablation study (Table 2) provides insights into the importance of different feature groups, enhancing the credibility of their results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core methodology – a feature-based binary classifier – is not novel. The work primarily adapts existing techniques to a specific task and dataset. The authors do not present a new coreference *algorithm* but rather a task-specific adaptation and evaluation. The claim of "revisiting" the task feels overstated.
2. **Modest Performance Gains:** While the performance improvements over baselines are statistically significant, the absolute F1 scores remain relatively low (around 89% for their best model). This suggests that the task itself is challenging and that further improvements are needed, but also raises questions about the overall impact of the approach. The relatively small dataset size might contribute to inflated performance estimates.
3. **Dependence on Existing Systems:** The baselines rely heavily on the performance of existing coreference systems, which themselves have limitations. The authors acknowledge this, but the results are inherently tied to the quality of these base systems. The benefit of their classifier appears to be *correcting* errors in these existing systems, rather than offering a fundamentally superior coreference solution.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. While Figure 3 provides a few examples, a more systematic analysis of the types of errors made by the system would be valuable for understanding its limitations and guiding future research.  The authors briefly mention needing more information on surrounding noun phrases for pronoun resolution, but this needs further elaboration.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the relatively low F1 scores, what is the primary source of errors in your system? Could you provide a more in-depth error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made (e.g., spurious coreference, missed coreference, errors due to ambiguity)?
2.  The WCR dataset is small. Have you considered releasing a larger version of the dataset, or perhaps a method for automatically generating similar datasets from Wikipedia?
3.  The paper mentions that Wikipedia markup provides "less ambiguous information on mentions" than named entity linking. Could you elaborate on this claim with specific examples? What are the potential pitfalls of relying solely on Wikipedia markup?
4.  The integration with Dcoref improved performance. Have you explored integrating your classifier with other coreference systems besides Dcoref?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "www.somewhere.country" should be replaced with an actual URL for dataset access.
*   Consider adding a discussion section to contextualize the findings within the broader field of coreference resolution and information extraction.
*   The writing quality is generally good, but some sentences are overly verbose and could be simplified.
