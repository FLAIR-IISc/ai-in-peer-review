## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of the main concept (MC) within Wikipedia articles. Recognizing the limitations of existing coreference resolution (CR) systems when applied to Wikipedia text, the authors propose a binary classification approach to determine whether a given mention refers to the article’s MC. They leverage features derived from Wikipedia markup, Freebase, and existing CR literature. They present a testbed (WCR) and demonstrate their approach outperforms state-of-the-art generic CR engines and improves a rule-based system (Dcoref).

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A focused dataset (WCR) for Main Concept Resolution:** This is arguably the most significant contribution.  Existing CR datasets are largely news-focused, and a dedicated resource for evaluating MC resolution in Wikipedia is valuable to the community.
2. **A feature-rich binary classification approach:** The authors' engineered features, combining Wikipedia-specific information (markup, links) with external knowledge (Freebase), show promising results.
3. **Demonstration of improvement to a rule-based system:** Integrating the classifier into Dcoref provides practical evidence of the approach's utility within an existing CR pipeline.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of adapting CR to the unique characteristics of Wikipedia and justifies its importance.  The observation that systems trained on news data perform poorly on Wikipedia is well-established, and this work tackles that gap.
2. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, comparing against several strong baselines and a state-of-the-art system. The use of 10-fold cross-validation provides confidence in the results.  The demonstration of improvements to Dcoref is particularly compelling.
3. **Resource Contribution:** The creation and potential release of the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution to the research community.  The authors’ willingness to share data and processed results is commendable.
4. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate a thoughtful approach to feature engineering, drawing inspiration from existing CR work while incorporating Wikipedia-specific attributes.  The ablation study (Table 2) effectively highlights the impact of different feature families.
5. **Well-Written and Structured:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized.  The explanations are clear, and the motivation for design choices is generally provided.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** While WCR is a valuable resource, its size (30 documents) is relatively small. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results and the statistical power of the experiments. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it remains a significant concern.  Further evaluation on a larger dataset is crucial.
2. **Baseline Limitations:**  The baselines, while reasonable, are somewhat simplistic. The aggregation strategies (B3 and B4) seem particularly prone to introducing noise. A more sophisticated baseline leveraging a broader range of CR techniques could provide a more challenging comparison.
3. **Classifier Complexity & Explainability:** While the feature engineering is comprehensive, the model itself (SVM with linear kernel) is relatively basic. Exploring more advanced classifiers (e.g., neural networks) might yield further improvements.  More importantly, the paper lacks detailed analysis of *why* certain features are effective and where the model fails. Understanding the model's behavior is crucial for future improvements.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors provide a few examples of errors (Figure 3), a more thorough error analysis would be beneficial. Categorizing the types of errors the model makes (e.g., ambiguity, coreference across sentences, domain-specific knowledge) would provide valuable insights.
5. **Dependence on External Resources:** The reliance on Freebase introduces a potential point of failure and limits the portability of the system.  Exploring alternative knowledge sources or techniques for handling missing or incorrect Freebase data would be beneficial.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Expansion:** What are your plans for expanding the WCR dataset? Will you consider incorporating automatic annotation methods to increase the scale of the resource?
2. **Error Analysis Details:** Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by the system and discussing potential mitigation strategies?
3. **Feature Importance:**  Can you provide any insights into the relative importance of different feature families?  Which features are most predictive of correct MC resolution?
4. **Freebase Dependency:** How robust is your system to errors or missing information in Freebase? Have you explored alternative knowledge resources?
5. **Generalizability:** Given the limited size of the WCR dataset, how confident are you in the generalizability of your results to other Wikipedia articles and domains?



**Additional Comments:**

* The reference to “somewhere.country” for data availability needs to be replaced with a concrete URL.
* The claim of “significant improvement” in the conclusion should be qualified, acknowledging the limitations of the dataset size.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid contribution with the potential to advance research in coreference resolution for Wikipedia. The creation of the WCR dataset is particularly valuable. While the small dataset size and relatively simple classifier are weaknesses, the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the problem and provide a strong empirical evaluation. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, contingent on a commitment to expanding the WCR dataset and providing more in-depth error analysis.
