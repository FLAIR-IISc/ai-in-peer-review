This paper addresses the task of main concept resolution (MCR) in Wikipedia articles, framing it as a binary classification problem to identify mentions referring to the article's main concept. The authors revisit a previously explored task, providing a testbed (WCR dataset) and demonstrating that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines. They further show improved performance when integrating their classifier into a rule-based coreference system.

**Contributions:**

1. **WCR Dataset:** The creation and release of the WCR dataset is the most significant contribution. A dedicated dataset for MCR in Wikipedia is a valuable resource for the community, addressing a clear gap in available data for this specific task. The annotation guidelines aligning with OntoNotes are also a positive aspect.
2. **Binary Classification Approach for MCR:** Framing MCR as a binary classification problem, coupled with carefully engineered features leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase, is a solid approach.  This provides a focused and effective method for identifying mentions of the main concept.
3. **Demonstrated Improvement via Integration:**  Successfully integrating the classifier into an existing rule-based system (Lee et al., 2013) and demonstrating improved coreference resolution performance is a practical demonstration of the approach’s utility.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the MCR task and motivates its importance, particularly in the context of Wikipedia as a rich knowledge source. The contrast with generic CR systems and their limitations on Wikipedia text is well-articulated.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The inclusion of multiple baselines, including strong contemporary CR systems, provides a robust comparative analysis. The use of a 10-fold cross-validation procedure is appropriate.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate a thoughtful approach to feature engineering, drawing from existing CR literature and adapting it to the specifics of Wikipedia. The distinction between features for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions is a good design choice. The analysis of feature importance (Table 2) is insightful.
4. **Practical Integration & Results:** Showing a performance boost in a full coreference resolution pipeline (Dcoref++) strengthens the practical relevance of the work. The results in Table 5 are encouraging.
5. **Dataset Availability:** The authors’ commitment to releasing the dataset and extracted information is highly commendable and will foster further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dataset Size & Generalizability:** While the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution, its relatively small size (30 documents) raises concerns about generalizability. The results might not be representative of the broader range of Wikipedia articles.  A larger, more diverse dataset would significantly strengthen the findings.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper briefly touches upon error analysis (Figure 3), but a more thorough and systematic analysis of error types would be beneficial. Understanding *why* the classifier fails in specific cases could reveal areas for improvement in feature engineering or model design. Specifically, exploring the errors related to ambiguous mentions like “Houston” would be useful.
3. **Baseline Limitations:** The baselines, while well-chosen, are somewhat limited in their adaptation to the MCR task.  A more sophisticated baseline specifically designed for MCR (perhaps leveraging Wikipedia’s internal link structure more extensively) might provide a stronger benchmark.  The baseline descriptions in Section 3 are a bit sparse – more detail would be helpful.
4. **Feature Engineering Reliance:** The success of the approach heavily relies on the manually engineered features.  While effective, this limits the potential for scalability and adaptability to other domains. Exploring automated feature learning techniques (e.g., neural networks with attention mechanisms) could be a valuable direction for future work.
5. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The results presented lack statistical significance testing. While the improvements observed appear substantial, it’s important to demonstrate that they are statistically significant and not due to chance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What steps were taken to ensure the inter-annotator agreement on the WCR dataset? Reporting the agreement scores would be helpful.
2. Could you provide more details on the selection criteria for the 30 Wikipedia articles used in the WCR dataset? What was the range of article lengths and topics covered?
3. The paper mentions noisy redirects in Wikipedia causing errors.  How did you attempt to mitigate the impact of these noisy redirects during feature extraction?
4.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the Dcoref rule-based system that make it susceptible to improvement with the addition of the classifier? What specific rules were most often overridden or modified by the classifier’s predictions?
5. What is the plan for maintaining and potentially expanding the WCR dataset? Will there be opportunities for other researchers to contribute to its growth?

**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and concise.
* The related work section could be slightly expanded to include more recent advances in coreference resolution and knowledge-aware NLP.
*  The link to the dataset (www.somewhere.country) needs to be replaced with a valid URL during the final submission.



Overall, this is a solid and valuable contribution. The WCR dataset is a significant asset to the research community, and the proposed approach for MCR demonstrates promising results. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly related to dataset size, error analysis, and statistical significance, would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
