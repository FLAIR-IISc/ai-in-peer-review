## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the task of identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) within a Wikipedia article. Recognizing the poor performance of general coreference resolution (CR) systems on Wikipedia text, the authors propose a binary classification approach to identify MC mentions. They leverage features extracted from Wikipedia markup (links, redirects) and Freebase, and demonstrate improved performance compared to existing CR engines and a modified rule-based system. A key contribution is the creation and use of a dedicated dataset (WCR) for evaluating this specific task.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Dedicated Dataset (WCR):** The creation of the WCR dataset is the most significant contribution. It provides a valuable resource for evaluating systems specifically designed for main concept coreference in Wikipedia, something previously lacking.
2. **Demonstration of Wikipedia-Specific Adaptation:** The paper convincingly shows that adapting CR techniques to the specific characteristics of Wikipedia text yields performance improvements over general-purpose systems.
3. **Effective Feature Engineering:** The feature set, leveraging Wikipedia’s structure and Freebase, proves to be effective for the task, indicating valuable insights into what information is crucial for MC identification.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The authors clearly articulate the problem and explain why existing CR systems are inadequate for Wikipedia data. The motivation for focusing on the main concept is well-justified within the context of NLP tasks like information extraction.
2. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing their approach against several state-of-the-art CR systems and well-defined baselines. The use of a 10-fold cross-validation approach strengthens the reliability of the results. The inclusion of performance metrics on the CoNLL 2012 dataset allows for a comparison to more standard CR benchmarks, highlighting the difficulty of the WCR task.
3. **Effective Feature Set:** The features designed by the authors demonstrate a good understanding of the information available within Wikipedia.  The combination of markup features, Freebase information, and grammatical characteristics appears to be a particularly strong combination. The ablation study (Table 2) clearly shows the contribution of each feature family.
4. **Positive Impact on a Rule-Based System:** Integrating the classifier into the Dcoref system and demonstrating an improvement in overall coreference performance is a positive result, suggesting practical applicability of the proposed approach.
5. **Resource Sharing:** The authors' commitment to sharing the dataset and annotated mentions is commendable and will likely facilitate further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** While the WCR dataset is a significant contribution, its relatively small size (30 documents) raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.  Performance gains on such a small dataset should be interpreted with caution. It’s not clear how well the approach will scale to larger, more diverse sets of Wikipedia articles.
2. **Classifier Complexity & Limited Analysis:** The description of the classifier itself is somewhat brief. While the feature list is detailed, the choice of a linear SVM and the hyperparameter tuning process could benefit from more explanation. A deeper analysis of the types of errors made by the classifier would be beneficial.  The examples in Figure 3 are helpful, but more systematic error analysis would be valuable.
3. **Baseline Limitations:**  The baselines, while reasonable, are relatively simple.  More sophisticated baselines, perhaps incorporating some of the insights from the feature engineering process, could provide a more challenging benchmark.
4. **Lack of Discussion of Error Sources:** The paper acknowledges some error cases, but a more in-depth discussion of the systematic sources of error would be valuable. For instance, how do ambiguous mentions or complex sentence structures impact performance?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Expansion:**  Do you have plans to expand the WCR dataset? If so, what are the strategies for ensuring diversity and representativeness of the added articles?
2. **Classifier Justification:** Why was a linear SVM chosen over other potentially more powerful classification algorithms?  Did you experiment with other models, and if so, what were the results?
3. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the classifier makes (e.g., false positives due to ambiguous mentions, false negatives due to coreference chains spanning multiple sentences)?
4. **Feature Importance:** Beyond the ablation study, can you provide information on the relative importance of different features within the classifier? (e.g., feature weights from the SVM).



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   The references are comprehensive and relevant.
*   The link to the data sharing website is currently a placeholder – ensure this is updated upon submission.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a solid contribution to the field of coreference resolution, particularly within the context of Wikipedia data. The creation of the WCR dataset is a significant step forward, and the demonstrated improvements in performance justify acceptance.  However, the limited dataset size and the lack of in-depth error analysis are weaknesses that should be addressed in a revision. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and questions outlined above.
