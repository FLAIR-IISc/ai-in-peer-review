This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of the main concept (MC) within Wikipedia articles, a task largely unexplored despite the prevalence of Wikipedia in NLP. The authors revisit this problem, framing it as a binary classification task and evaluating state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems on a dedicated testbed. They also demonstrate improvement by integrating their classifier into a rule-based coreference system.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Dedicated Dataset and Evaluation:** The creation of the WCR dataset, specifically designed for evaluating systems on this task, is the most significant contribution. The dataset, despite its modest size, provides a valuable resource for researchers interested in adapting coreference resolution to Wikipedia text. The accompanying evaluation of existing systems highlights the challenges and performance gaps in this domain.
2. **Effective Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate that carefully designed features, leveraging Wikipedia markup and external knowledge (Freebase), can significantly improve performance on identifying MC mentions. The systematic ablation study (Table 2) clearly shows the impact of each feature family, providing valuable insights into the characteristics of MC mentions in Wikipedia.
3. **Practical Improvement to Coreference:** The successful integration of the classifier into the Dcoref system and the resulting performance gain on a full coreference task (Table 5) demonstrate the practical utility of the approach beyond just MC identification.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the task and convincingly motivates its importance, highlighting the limitations of existing coreference systems when applied to Wikipedia.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Methodology:** The use of a 10-fold cross-validation procedure, comparison against multiple baselines, and a feature ablation study demonstrate a thorough and systematic approach to evaluation.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The analysis of the experimental results provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the different features and systems, leading to a better understanding of the task.
4. **Publicly Available Resources:** The authors’ commitment to releasing the dataset and a dump of MC mentions is commendable and will facilitate further research in this area.
5. **Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-organized and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the methods and results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** While the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution, its relatively small size (30 documents, ~7000 mentions) limits the generalizability of the results. The conclusions drawn from this dataset should be interpreted with caution.  A larger, more diverse dataset would be needed to fully validate the approach.
2. **Dependence on External Resources:** The reliance on Freebase introduces a potential point of failure and may limit the applicability of the approach to languages or domains where Freebase is not readily available. A discussion of potential alternatives or strategies for handling missing Freebase data would be beneficial.
3. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** The feature set, while effective, is relatively complex and requires significant engineering effort. Exploring more automated feature learning techniques could potentially simplify the approach and improve its scalability.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors mention some errors, a more detailed error analysis would be helpful in identifying areas for further improvement. For example, categorizing the types of errors (e.g., ambiguity, coreference resolution errors, feature limitations) would provide valuable guidance for future work.
5. **Baseline Selection:** The choice of Dcoref and Scoref as baselines is somewhat limited. While these are state-of-the-art systems, exploring a wider range of approaches, including more recent neural models, would provide a more comprehensive comparison.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What measures were taken to ensure the quality and consistency of the annotations in the WCR dataset?  Specifically, what inter-annotator agreement scores were achieved?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the penalty weight for the negative class in the SVM classifier? Did you explore other regularization parameters or optimization techniques?
3.  Could you provide more detail on the specific challenges encountered during the integration of the classifier into the Dcoref system? Were there any unexpected interactions or difficulties?
4.  Given the limitations of Freebase, have you considered exploring alternative knowledge sources or methods for handling missing data?
5.  What are the computational costs associated with feature extraction and classification, and how do they scale with the size of the input article?
