## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

This paper tackles a focused, and surprisingly under-explored, coreference problem: identifying mentions of a Wikipedia article’s main concept. While the overall framing feels somewhat incremental – applying existing CR techniques to a specific task – the authors identify a genuine gap in the literature and provide a valuable resource (the WCR dataset) and a surprisingly effective, feature-rich binary classification approach. The novelty lies less in the core method and more in the *application* to this specific task and the demonstrated performance gain, particularly considering the challenges of Wikipedia text.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Introduction of the WCR Dataset:** This is arguably the strongest contribution. A dedicated dataset for main concept resolution in Wikipedia is a significant boon to the research community.
2. **Demonstration of Feature Engineering Effectiveness:** The authors successfully leverage Wikipedia-specific features (markup, Freebase links) within a binary classification framework to outperform general-purpose CR systems on this task.
3. **Integration into a Rule-Based System:** Showing improvement when integrating the classifier into Dcoref provides a pragmatic demonstration of the approach’s utility.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper succinctly defines the "main concept resolution" task and clearly motivates its importance, particularly in the context of information extraction from Wikipedia.
2. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The thorough comparison against several state-of-the-art CR systems (both statistical and rule-based) provides a convincing benchmark for the proposed approach.  The fact that a rule-based system initially outperforms statistical ones on this task is a noteworthy observation, highlighting the specificity of Wikipedia text.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors present a comprehensive set of features, demonstrating a thoughtful understanding of the task and the available data. The ablation study in Section 6.3 convincingly shows the contribution of each feature family.
4. **Practical Impact Demonstrated:** The improvement to Dcoref illustrates the potential for practical application of the approach and provides a path for future work.
5. **Resource Availability:** The authors explicitly state their intent to release the dataset and processed data, which significantly enhances the value of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** The WCR dataset, while valuable, is relatively small (30 articles). This limits the generalizability of the results and raises concerns about potential overfitting, especially given the large number of features used. While the authors acknowledge this, it remains a significant limitation.
2. **Incremental Nature of the Approach:** The underlying classification technique (SVM with engineered features) is not particularly novel. The strength of the paper resides in the adaptation to the specific task and data source, rather than a breakthrough in core methodology.
3. **Dependence on External Resources:** The reliance on Freebase introduces a potential point of failure and limits applicability to scenarios where Freebase is unavailable or incomplete. The authors acknowledge this to some degree, but a discussion of alternatives or robustness to missing data would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors mention examples of errors (Figure 3), a more systematic error analysis would provide deeper insights into the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for improvement.  Specifically, understanding *why* the classifier fails in certain cases could guide feature engineering or model selection.
5. **Presentation of Results:** Table 5 presents results with many metrics. While comprehensive, it's a bit overwhelming and obscures the main takeaway: the improvement over Dcoref. Focusing on the most relevant metric (likely F1) would make the results more digestible.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Expansion:** Do you have plans to expand the WCR dataset? If so, what strategies will you employ to ensure representativeness and avoid bias?
2. **Freebase Dependence:** Have you explored alternative knowledge bases or strategies for mitigating the reliance on Freebase? What is the impact on performance when Freebase information is unavailable?
3. **Error Analysis Details:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors the classifier makes? Are there specific categories of mentions (e.g., ambiguous pronouns, complex noun phrases) that consistently cause problems?
4. **Scalability:** How computationally expensive is the feature extraction process? Is the approach scalable to the entire English Wikipedia?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 3 is helpful, but could be improved by highlighting the specific features that led to the correct or incorrect classifications.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise, but some sections (e.g., Section 4.2.1) are quite dense and could benefit from further streamlining.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the weaknesses, I lean towards **acceptance**. The introduction of the WCR dataset and the demonstrated performance gains on a challenging task outweigh the incremental nature of the methodology. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of coreference resolution and provides a solid foundation for future research on adapting CR techniques to specialized text genres. The authors clearly identify a gap in the literature and offer a practical solution with tangible benefits.
