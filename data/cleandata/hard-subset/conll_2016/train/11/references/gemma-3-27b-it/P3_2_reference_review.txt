## Review: Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the task of identifying mentions of a Wikipedia article’s main concept (MC) – essentially, coreference resolution focused on a single, central entity within a document. Recognizing the poor performance of general CR systems on Wikipedia text, the authors propose a binary classification approach utilizing features extracted from Wikipedia markup (links, redirects) and Freebase, combined with existing coreference systems. They introduce a new dataset (WCR) for evaluating this task and demonstrate that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art CR engines and improves a rule-based system (Dcoref) when integrated.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A focused dataset (WCR) and evaluation setup for Main Concept Resolution (MCR) in Wikipedia.** This is the strongest contribution.  The lack of dedicated resources for this specific task hindered progress, and the creation of WCR provides a valuable benchmark.
2. **Demonstration of the inadequacy of general-purpose CR systems on Wikipedia.** While not entirely novel (prior work acknowledges this), the paper provides concrete evidence through experiments on the WCR dataset, quantifying the performance gap.
3. **A feature-rich classifier for MCR leveraging Wikipedia-specific information.** The authors successfully integrate Wikipedia’s structural properties and external knowledge from Freebase into a binary classification framework, achieving strong results.



**Strengths:**

1. **Well-defined and relevant problem:** Focusing on the main concept of a Wikipedia article is a practical and useful task with clear applications in information extraction and knowledge graph construction.  The problem is well-motivated and clearly articulated.
2. **Strong experimental evaluation:** The authors perform a thorough comparison against several strong baselines, including established CR systems.  The use of 10-fold cross-validation provides confidence in the results.  The inclusion of performance on the broader CoNLL dataset contextualizes the WCR results.
3. **Effective feature engineering:**  The features employed, drawing upon Wikipedia markup, Freebase, and existing CR features, demonstrate a good understanding of the domain and the factors that contribute to successful MCR. The ablation study (Table 2) effectively highlights the contribution of each feature family.
4. **Practical improvement to an existing system:** Successfully integrating the classifier into Dcoref and showing a performance boost demonstrates the practical applicability of the approach.  This isn’t just a standalone classifier; it’s a potential enhancement to existing infrastructure.
5. **Resource contribution:** The authors’ commitment to releasing the dataset and extracted information is commendable and will be beneficial to the research community.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:**  The WCR dataset, while valuable, is relatively small (30 documents, ~60k tokens).  This limits the generalizability of the findings and raises concerns about potential overfitting, especially given the large number of features.  While the authors acknowledge this, its impact needs stronger discussion.
2. **Classifier Complexity & Feature Engineering:** While feature engineering is a strength, the sheer number of features (hundreds) could benefit from dimensionality reduction or feature selection techniques. The description in section 4.2 is quite dense and could be better structured for clarity. It’s difficult to grasp the overall design philosophy without wading through a long list.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  The paper presents quantitative results, but lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis.  Understanding *why* the system fails on certain examples would provide valuable insights for future improvement.  The brief examples in Figure 3 are a good start, but more systematic analysis is needed. The discussion of the errors in Figure 3 feel superficial.
4. **Baseline Limitations:** While the baselines are reasonable, their reliance on existing CR systems means their performance is inherently limited by those systems’ weaknesses on Wikipedia data. Exploring more tailored baselines specifically designed for MCR, even simple rule-based approaches, could provide a more informative point of comparison.
5. **Overstated Claims:** The phrase "outperforms state-of-the-art generic coreference resolution engines" feels somewhat strong. While the system outperforms those engines *on this specific task*, it doesn’t necessarily imply superiority in general coreference resolution.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Expansion:** What plans do you have for expanding the WCR dataset?  What challenges do you foresee in scaling the annotation effort?
2. **Feature Selection:**  Have you experimented with feature selection or dimensionality reduction techniques to simplify the classifier and potentially improve generalization? If so, what were the results?
3. **Error Analysis Details:** Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the system makes (e.g., misidentification of entity types, incorrect pronoun resolution, confusion with related entities)?  Can you link these error types to specific features?
4. **Generalizability:** How well do you believe this approach would generalize to other knowledge bases or encyclopedic texts beyond Wikipedia? What modifications would be necessary?
5. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of training and applying this system, particularly given the large number of features?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and organized, but could benefit from more visual aids (e.g., diagrams illustrating the feature extraction process).
*   The referencing is good, but could be more comprehensive, especially when discussing related work on Wikipedia-specific NLP.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential ethical considerations, such as bias in the Freebase data used for feature extraction.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that addresses a relevant and challenging problem. The creation of the WCR dataset is a significant contribution, and the proposed approach demonstrates promising results. While the dataset size and classifier complexity are concerns, the strengths of the paper outweigh its weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses and questions.
