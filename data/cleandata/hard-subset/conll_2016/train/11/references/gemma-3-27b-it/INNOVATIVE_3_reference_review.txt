## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

This paper tackles a surprisingly neglected, yet important, problem: coreference resolution specifically tailored for Wikipedia articles, focusing on identifying mentions of the “main concept” (MC). While coreference resolution is well-studied, its application to the unique characteristics of Wikipedia – its structure, interlinking, and encyclopedic nature – appears largely unexplored. The authors rightly point out the performance drop when generic CR systems are applied to Wikipedia. The primary contribution of this work is framing this specific task – MC resolution – as a binary classification problem and demonstrating that a carefully designed feature set, leveraging Wikipedia markup and external knowledge, significantly outperforms existing generic CR engines on this task. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the creation and release of a dedicated dataset (WCR) for evaluating MC resolution systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Problem:** The focus on coreference *specifically* for the main concept of a Wikipedia article is genuinely novel. Most CR work targets general coreference, and adapting it to the nuances of Wikipedia, particularly this focused task, hasn't received sufficient attention. This is a focused, well-defined problem with clear practical applications (e.g., improving information extraction from Wikipedia).
2. **Smart Feature Engineering:** The authors intelligently leverage Wikipedia-specific features – hyperlinks, redirects, Freebase links – rather than relying solely on traditional CR features. This demonstrates a good understanding of the data and a creative approach to problem-solving. The feature extraction process, although described at a high level, appears well-considered.
3. **Demonstrated Performance Gains:** The empirical results clearly show that the proposed approach outperforms several strong baseline CR systems on the WCR dataset. The magnitude of improvement (up to 15 F1 points) is substantial and suggests the effectiveness of the proposed features and classification framework. The addition to Dcoref showing improvements is also positive.
4. **Dataset Release:** The creation and planned release of the WCR dataset is a significant contribution to the community. A dedicated, annotated dataset is crucial for fostering further research in this area. The inclusion of annotations aligning with established guidelines (OntoNotes) is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** While the creation of WCR is appreciated, its size (30 documents) is extremely small. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results. A larger, more representative dataset is needed to confidently assess the robustness and scalability of the proposed approach. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it remains a significant concern.
2. **Evaluation Primarily Relative to Baselines:** The paper's strength heavily relies on comparison to existing *generic* CR systems. While outperforming these systems is valuable, the absolute performance metrics are not stellar. It's unclear how well this approach would perform against a CR system specifically *designed* and optimized for Wikipedia. The comparisons feel somewhat like a 'low bar' has been cleared.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis.  Understanding *why* the system fails in certain cases (beyond the brief mention of challenging cases in section 6.3) would provide valuable insights for future improvements. A more granular analysis of error types would strengthen the paper. The one example given (Johnston Atoll) is helpful, but insufficient.
4. **Methodological Detail:** While the feature set is described, the specific implementation details of the pre-processing and linking steps are somewhat vague. More detailed explanation of how WikipediaMiner and Jena are used would be beneficial for reproducibility.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What are your plans for scaling the WCR dataset? Do you anticipate releasing a larger version in the future?
2. Have you considered or experimented with pre-training language models (e.g., BERT) on Wikipedia text to improve the performance of the classifier? If not, why?
3. Can you elaborate on the specific types of errors the system makes most frequently? What categories of mentions or contexts pose the biggest challenges?
4. Can you provide more detail on the implementation of the hyperlink-based attribute extraction? How are ambiguous links (links pointing to multiple entities) resolved?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is strong, and the proposed approach is conceptually sound. Despite the methodological concerns regarding dataset size and error analysis, the novelty of the task and the demonstrated performance gains are compelling. I lean towards acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the dataset and providing more detailed error analysis. This feels like a promising direction, and a larger dataset could cement it as a significant contribution.
