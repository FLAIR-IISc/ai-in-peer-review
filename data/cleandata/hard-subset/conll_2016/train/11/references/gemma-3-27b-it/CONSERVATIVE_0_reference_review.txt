## Review of “Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution”

This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of a Wikipedia article’s main concept, framing it as a binary classification problem. The authors compare several established coreference resolution systems, augmented with features derived from Wikipedia and Freebase, against their own classifier. They demonstrate improvements over baselines and show that integrating their classifier into an existing rule-based system yields further gains.

The primary contribution of this work is the creation and evaluation of a dedicated dataset (WCR) for assessing main concept resolution in Wikipedia. While the dataset is relatively small (30 documents), its focused nature is valuable. A secondary contribution is a reasonably thorough comparison of existing coreference systems on this task, revealing their limitations when applied to Wikipedia-specific challenges. Finally, the proposed feature set, while largely leveraging existing approaches, demonstrates the benefit of incorporating Wikipedia-specific markup and external knowledge for this specific task.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Creation:** The WCR dataset is a welcome addition to the resources available for evaluating coreference resolution, particularly focusing on the distinct characteristics of Wikipedia text. The annotation scheme aligns with established guidelines (OntoNotes), facilitating comparability with other datasets. The authors acknowledge the size limitation but argue for its representativeness, which is plausible given the sampling strategy described.
2. **Comprehensive Baseline Comparison:**  The systematic evaluation of several state-of-the-art coreference systems provides a valuable benchmark. Demonstrating that existing systems struggle with this task highlights the need for tailored approaches. The selection of Dcoref and Scoref, representing rule-based and machine learning approaches respectively, is sensible.
3. **Methodological Rigor:** The experimental setup, including 10-fold cross-validation and use of standard evaluation metrics (MUC, B3, CEAF, CoNLL F1), is sound. The ablation study exploring the impact of different feature families is well-executed and provides insights into feature importance.
4. **Practical Improvement via Integration:** Showing that incorporating the classifier into Dcoref improves overall coreference resolution performance demonstrates the practical utility of the approach, beyond merely identifying main concept mentions. The reported performance gain is moderate but noteworthy.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core technical approach – a binary classifier leveraging features from existing systems – lacks substantial novelty. The features themselves are largely borrowed from related work, and the classifier used (SVM) is a standard choice. While the *combination* of these elements is sensible, the paper doesn’t present a breakthrough in coreference methodology.
2. **Small Dataset Size:** While the WCR dataset is valuable, its small size (30 documents) limits the generalizability of the findings. Results could be heavily influenced by the specific articles included and may not hold across the broader spectrum of Wikipedia content. The statistical significance of the reported improvements warrants further scrutiny given the dataset size.
3. **Insufficient Error Analysis:**  The paper mentions errors (e.g., example (f) in Figure 3) but lacks a thorough error analysis. A deeper dive into *why* the system fails in specific cases would be crucial for identifying areas for improvement and guiding future research. What types of mentions are consistently misclassified? Are there systematic biases in the feature set?
4. **Over-Reliance on External Resources:** The approach heavily relies on Freebase and Wikipedia markup. While exploiting these resources is reasonable for Wikipedia text, it raises questions about the portability of the method to other domains where such resources are not readily available or as reliably maintained. The potential for inaccuracies in Freebase (mentioned in Section 2) is acknowledged but not fully addressed in terms of impact on performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the dataset size, can you provide more details about the criteria used to select the 30 articles for WCR?  What steps were taken to ensure diversity in terms of topic, length, and complexity?
2.  The paper mentions that errors often occur with complex coreference cases. Could you provide more concrete examples of these cases and discuss potential solutions to address them (e.g., more sophisticated features, different machine learning models)?
3.  Given the reliance on Freebase and Wikipedia markup, how robust is the system to changes in these resources (e.g., links breaking, aliases being updated)? Have you conducted any experiments to assess this robustness?
4. Can you provide results from statistical significance tests (e.g. paired t-tests) to demonstrate the improvements are not simply due to chance, given the dataset size?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The reference to "www.somewhere.country" needs to be replaced with a valid URL for the dataset and code release.
*   Consider expanding the related work section to include more recent advancements in coreference resolution, particularly those incorporating knowledge graphs or contextual embeddings.
