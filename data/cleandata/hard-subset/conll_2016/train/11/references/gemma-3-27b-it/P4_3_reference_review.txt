This paper addresses the task of main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles, framing it as a binary classification problem to identify mentions referring to the article’s main concept. The authors revisit previous work on this task, providing a testbed (WCR dataset) and comparing several state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems and their own approach, which leverages features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. The primary contribution is the creation and evaluation of the WCR dataset and the demonstration that their approach outperforms existing systems on this task, as well as improving a rule-based system when integrated. A secondary contribution is a detailed feature engineering process tailored to the characteristics of Wikipedia text and the specific task of main concept resolution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Dataset (WCR):** The creation of the WCR dataset is the strongest aspect of this work. Wikipedia presents unique challenges for coreference resolution due to its structure and style, and a dedicated dataset is crucial for evaluating systems on this domain. The dataset description, while brief in the submitted version, suggests a careful consideration of article characteristics. The availability of this dataset will benefit future research.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over baseline systems on the WCR dataset. The performance gains, reported using standard CoNLL metrics, are substantial (e.g., a 13-point F1 increase over Dcoref). The detailed ablation studies on feature sets further strengthen these results, providing insights into feature importance.
3. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The paper presents a thorough exploration of features tailored to Wikipedia text and the main concept resolution task. The combination of features from Wikipedia markup, Freebase, and traditional coreference features is well-justified and contributes significantly to the performance gains. The categorization of features (base, title, tag, main, etc.) makes the analysis clear.
4. **Integration with Existing System:** Successfully integrating the proposed classifier into an existing rule-based system (Dcoref) and showing further improvements demonstrates the practical applicability of the research. This provides evidence that the approach isn't just a standalone improvement but can enhance existing coreference resolution pipelines.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** While the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution, its size (30 documents) is relatively small. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results and the statistical significance of the observed improvements. While the authors acknowledge this, it remains a significant limitation.
2. **Dataset Creation Details:** The paper lacks sufficient detail about the dataset creation process. Specifically, the annotation guidelines and inter-annotator agreement are not mentioned. Without this information, it's difficult to assess the quality and reliability of the annotations. The description of the dataset is insufficient to ascertain how representative it is of the broader range of Wikipedia articles.
3. **Baseline System Selection:** The choice of baselines is somewhat limited. While Dcoref and Scoref are state-of-the-art systems, exploring a wider range of approaches (e.g., more recent neural coreference models) could provide a more comprehensive comparison.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** Although the authors provide some examples of correct and incorrect classifications, a more systematic error analysis is missing. Understanding the types of errors made by the system could lead to further improvements and insights into the challenges of the task. Specifically, the authors mention noisy redirects in Wikipedia, which can affect the performance, but they don't fully investigate the impact of such noise.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide more details about the dataset creation process, including the annotation guidelines used and the inter-annotator agreement achieved.
2.  What is the rationale for choosing only 30 documents for the WCR dataset? Were any criteria used to select these documents (e.g., topic diversity, length)?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, including a breakdown of the types of errors made by the system (e.g., errors related to pronominal resolution, errors due to ambiguity, errors due to noisy redirects)?
4.  How robust is the performance of your approach to variations in Wikipedia article structure (e.g., articles with fewer hyperlinks, articles with more complex sentence structures)?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly recent advances in neural coreference resolution.
* The link to the dataset (www.somewhere.country) is currently non-functional.
* The notation “Dcoref++” feels somewhat informal. A more descriptive name might be preferable.
