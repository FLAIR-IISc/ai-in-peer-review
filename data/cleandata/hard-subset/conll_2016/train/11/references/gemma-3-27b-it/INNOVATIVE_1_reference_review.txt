This paper tackles the problem of coreference resolution specifically within the context of Wikipedia articles, focusing on identifying mentions of the “main concept” (MC) of an article. While the overall framing isn’t groundbreaking – identifying the central topic’s mentions is a natural task – the paper’s primary contribution lies in *defining and providing a testbed (WCR dataset) for this specific task*. The secondary contribution is demonstrating that adapting existing coreference systems with features tailored to Wikipedia’s structure, and particularly leveraging its markup and Freebase links, yields significant improvements. A third, smaller contribution, is showing the benefit of incorporating the developed classifier into a rule-based coreference system.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Defined Task & Dataset:** The explicit focus on resolving coreference *to the main concept* of a Wikipedia article, and the creation of the WCR dataset, are genuinely valuable. Existing CR datasets are overwhelmingly news-focused, and Wikipedia presents unique characteristics (internal links, structured markup, direct Freebase connections) that necessitate a dedicated evaluation resource. This dataset fills a significant gap.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results convincingly demonstrate that the proposed approach outperforms existing state-of-the-art coreference systems on the WCR dataset. The gains are substantial (up to 13 F1 points), indicating the effectiveness of the Wikipedia-specific features and the tailored classification approach.
3. **Practical Integration & Improvement:** The demonstrated improvement in the Dcoref system through integration of the classifier is a positive sign. It suggests a practical path for enhancing existing coreference tools to better handle Wikipedia data, and the impact on overall coreference performance is noteworthy.
4. **Feature Engineering is Thoughtful:** The authors clearly articulate and experiment with a range of features, effectively combining information from Wikipedia markup, Freebase, and standard NLP features. This shows a good understanding of the domain and relevant resources.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability & Scope:** The entire evaluation is confined to the WCR dataset, which, despite being a valuable contribution, is relatively small (30 documents). The results' generalizability to other Wikipedia datasets, or even slightly different article structures, is unknown and needs further investigation. The authors acknowledge this, but it’s a crucial limitation.
2. **Classifier Complexity & Explainability:** While the feature engineering is thoughtful, the resulting classifier's inner workings aren't easily interpretable. It's a black box, making it difficult to understand precisely *why* certain mentions are correctly or incorrectly linked to the MC. More analysis of feature importance or error cases would be beneficial.
3. **Baselines Could Be Stronger:** While baselines are included, they feel somewhat simplistic. Exploring more sophisticated baselines – perhaps leveraging Wikipedia’s link structure directly for identifying MC mentions without full CR – could provide a more robust comparison.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The error analysis presented (Figure 3) is limited. While illustrative, a more systematic error analysis – categorizing error types and identifying patterns – would provide deeper insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What are the authors’ plans for expanding the WCR dataset? Are there plans to release larger versions or variations of the dataset to facilitate broader research?
2.  Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of feature importance? Which features contribute most significantly to the model's performance, and are there any surprising or counterintuitive findings?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of the classifier to the quality of the Freebase data? Are there strategies for mitigating the impact of inaccuracies or missing information in Freebase?
4.  The paper mentions inaccuracies in Wikipedia markup can lead to errors. Could the authors elaborate on the types of inaccuracies encountered and how they might be addressed?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly structured.
*   The related work section is reasonably comprehensive.
*   Providing code and the dataset will be crucial for reproducibility and further research.



**Overall:**

Despite some limitations, I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The novelty of the defined task, the creation of a dedicated dataset, and the strong empirical results outweigh the weaknesses. The paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of coreference resolution, particularly for the increasingly important domain of Wikipedia knowledge extraction. The approach is promising, and the authors have laid a solid foundation for future research in this area. I am prioritizing novelty and a well-defined problem space over methodological perfection in this evaluation.
