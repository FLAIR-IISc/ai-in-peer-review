## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of a Wikipedia article's main concept, framing it as a binary classification problem and evaluating several existing coreference resolution systems alongside a novel classifier. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the creation and evaluation of a dedicated dataset (WCR) for this specific task, coupled with a comparative analysis of existing systems' performance on it. While the proposed approach shows improvements, the advancements feel incremental rather than groundbreaking. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that adapting existing rule-based systems with a targeted classifier can yield performance gains.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Creation & Availability:** The WCR dataset is a significant asset. Existing coreference resources are heavily biased towards newswire, and a dedicated dataset tailored to the unique characteristics of Wikipedia is valuable for the community. The authors’ stated intention to release the dataset and mention annotations is commendable.
2. **Comprehensive Baseline Comparison:** The authors rightly point out the performance drop of generic CR systems on Wikipedia data.  The thorough comparison against established systems like Dcoref, Durrett & Klein, Martschat & Strube, and Clark & Manning provides a necessary benchmark and establishes the difficulty of the task. The inclusion of multiple baselines built *on top of* these systems is a strong point.
3. **Systematic Feature Engineering:** The feature set is well-motivated and draws upon established techniques from coreference resolution and knowledge base integration. The categorization of features and the ablation study (Table 2) provide a good understanding of which features contribute most to performance.
4. **Practical Improvement to Existing System:** Demonstrating a performance gain when integrating the classifier into Dcoref (Dcoref++) provides a concrete, albeit modest, improvement to a readily available system. This indicates a practical applicability of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Approach:** The core approach – a binary classifier leveraging features – is not particularly novel. The novelty lies primarily in the application to this specific task and the adaptation of existing features, rather than a fundamentally new methodological contribution. The classification framework feels standard.
2. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While examples are provided in Figure 3, the error analysis is superficial. A more in-depth analysis of *why* the system fails in specific cases would be greatly beneficial. What types of mentions are most frequently misclassified? Are there systematic biases in the feature set? Understanding these limitations is crucial for future improvements.
3. **Dataset Size Concerns:**  The WCR dataset, while valuable, is relatively small (30 documents, ~60k tokens).  While the authors acknowledge this, the limited size raises concerns about the generalizability of the results.  Performance improvements on a small dataset may not translate to larger, more diverse corpora. A larger dataset is needed to establish more statistically significant conclusions.
4. **Over-Reliance on Freebase:** The strong dependence on Freebase for attributes introduces a potential point of failure. Freebase is not a perfect knowledge base, and inaccuracies can propagate through the system. The paper briefly acknowledges this in related work, but the extent to which Freebase errors impact performance isn't quantified.
5. **Limited Scope of Coreference Resolution:** The paper focuses solely on identifying mentions of the main concept. This is a useful task, but it's a restricted view of coreference resolution.  A full coreference system aims to resolve all mentions within a document, not just those related to the main entity.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more detail on the process used to create the WCR dataset? What guidelines were followed during annotation, and what inter-annotator agreement was achieved?
2.  What is the impact of errors or missing information in Freebase on the performance of your system? Have you conducted any experiments to assess the robustness of your system to Freebase inaccuracies?
3.  How do the performance improvements generalize to larger datasets? Do you have preliminary results on a larger, held-out test set?
4.  The Dcoref++ results (Table 5) show improvements, but the gains are modest. Are there other ways to integrate your classifier into Dcoref that might yield larger performance gains?
5. How does the approach handle articles that have multiple, equally valid main concepts?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent.
*   The link to the dataset in the conclusion is placeholder text ("www.somewhere.country").
*   The use of "Dcoref++" is slightly awkward terminology. A more descriptive name might be preferable.
