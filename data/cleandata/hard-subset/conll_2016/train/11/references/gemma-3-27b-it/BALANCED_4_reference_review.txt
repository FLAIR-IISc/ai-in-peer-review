This paper addresses the task of main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles, focusing on identifying all mentions referring to the central topic of an article. The authors revisit this task, providing a testbed (WCR dataset) and comparing state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems, enhanced with features specifically tailored for Wikipedia’s structure and external knowledge from Freebase.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **The WCR Dataset:** The creation and release of the WCR dataset is the most significant contribution. A dedicated dataset for main concept resolution in Wikipedia is a valuable resource, particularly given the demonstrated poor performance of generic CR systems on this text genre. The annotation guidelines aligned with OntoNotes add to its usability.
2. **Feature Engineering for Wikipedia CR:** The authors demonstrate that incorporating features derived from Wikipedia’s markup (redirects, internal links) and Freebase significantly improves performance compared to generic CR systems. This highlights the importance of adapting techniques to the specific characteristics of the text.
3. **Improved Coreference via MC Resolution:** Successfully integrating the developed classifier into an existing rule-based CR system (Dcoref) showcases the practical application and benefits of the proposed approach, leading to measurable improvements in overall coreference resolution performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of adapting coreference resolution to the Wikipedia domain and provides a compelling motivation based on the limitations of existing systems trained on news-like text.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing their approach to several state-of-the-art baselines (Dcoref, Scoref, Durrett & Klein systems) and performing ablation studies to analyze the contribution of different feature sets. The 10-fold cross-validation strengthens the reliability of the results.
3. **Strong Baseline Selection:** Choosing both a rule-based (Dcoref) and machine learning-based (Scoref) system as baselines provides a fair comparison and reveals the strengths of the proposed approach in different paradigms.
4. **Practical Integration:** The integration of the classifier into Dcoref demonstrates the practical utility of the research and shows how it can improve an existing, established system. The reported performance gains (4% in CoNLL F1 score) are noteworthy.
5. **Resource Contribution:** The commitment to releasing the dataset and mention data to the community is a significant positive aspect, fostering further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** While the creation of the WCR dataset is a major strength, its relatively small size (30 documents) limits the statistical significance of the results and the generalizability of the findings. The authors acknowledge this but it remains a concern.
2. **Feature Engineering Complexity:** The feature set, while effective, is quite extensive and requires significant manual effort to design and implement. A discussion of feature selection methods or an attempt to automate feature engineering would have been beneficial.
3. **Shallow Error Analysis:** The error analysis presented (Figure 3) is limited and focuses on a small number of examples. A more systematic error analysis, categorized by error type (e.g., misidentified entity type, pronoun resolution errors), would provide deeper insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the system.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Wikification Approaches:** The paper positions its approach as a substitute for named entity linking pipelines. However, a direct comparison with state-of-the-art wikification systems (e.g., those utilizing deep learning) would help clarify the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.
5. **Over-reliance on Freebase:** The approach heavily relies on Freebase for attribute information. The authors briefly mention potential issues with inaccuracies but do not explore alternative knowledge sources or techniques for handling noisy data. With Freebase being sunsetted, this reliance is increasingly problematic.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Expansion:** Do you have plans to expand the WCR dataset? If so, what are your strategies for ensuring diversity and representativeness?
2. **Feature Selection:** Did you experiment with automated feature selection methods? If so, what were the results?
3. **Freebase Alternatives:** Have you considered using alternative knowledge bases (e.g., Wikidata) to mitigate the reliance on Freebase?
4. **Error Analysis Details:** Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the error analysis, categorizing errors by type and frequency?
5. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of the feature extraction process, particularly considering the reliance on external knowledge sources like Freebase?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly structured.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of relevant research.
*   The acronyms are consistently defined.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the study in the conclusion.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of coreference resolution by addressing the specific challenges of applying CR to Wikipedia articles. The creation of the WCR dataset is a significant achievement, and the proposed approach demonstrates promising results. While the small dataset size and reliance on Freebase are limitations, the strengths of the paper outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions raised above. The potential impact of the WCR dataset on future research in this area is considerable.
