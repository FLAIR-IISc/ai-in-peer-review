## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of a Wikipedia article’s main concept. The authors frame this as a binary classification problem and propose a feature-rich classifier, demonstrating improvements over existing coreference resolution systems when applied to this specific task. They also show a marginal improvement to a rule-based system by integrating their classifier.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A focused dataset and evaluation:** The creation of the WCR dataset, even at a relatively small size, provides a valuable resource for evaluating coreference systems specifically on the challenging domain of Wikipedia articles and the unique task of main concept resolution.
2. **Demonstration of the difficulty of adapting general CR systems:** The paper clearly highlights the performance gap between general-purpose coreference systems and this specialized task, reinforcing the need for domain-specific adaptation.
3. **Comprehensive feature engineering:** The authors explore a wide range of features, leveraging Wikipedia’s structure and external knowledge sources like Freebase, offering a detailed exploration of potentially relevant signals.

**Strengths:**

1. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors perform a reasonable comparative evaluation against several state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems, including both rule-based and machine learning approaches. The inclusion of baselines constructed *on top* of these systems is particularly insightful.
2. **Detailed Feature Analysis:** The ablation study (Table 2) provides a granular understanding of the contribution of each feature family to the performance of the classifier, which is valuable for future research.
3. **Leveraging Wikipedia Structure:** The intelligent use of Wikipedia’s markup (redirects, internal links) as a source of information is a strong point.  Avoiding the error-prone pipeline of named entity linking, as the authors note, is a sensible approach.
4. **Clear Problem Definition:**  The task of identifying mentions of the main concept is well-defined and relevant to numerous downstream NLP applications that utilize Wikipedia as a knowledge source.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** The WCR dataset, with only 30 documents and ~7000 mentions, is *extremely* small. While the authors acknowledge this, its limited size significantly restricts the generalizability of the findings and the statistical power of the experiments.  Claims of “outperforming state-of-the-art” should be tempered given this limitation. A more substantial dataset is critical to validate the approach.
2. **Overly Optimistic Reporting:** The performance gains, while statistically significant according to the authors’ cross-validation, are presented without sufficient context. The absolute F1 scores, particularly on the full dataset, remain relatively low. Improvements of 13-15 points are reported but start from a relatively low base, and the impact on real-world applications isn’t sufficiently demonstrated.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis Detail:**  While Figure 3 provides a few example errors, a more systematic error analysis is needed. Understanding the *types* of errors made by the system (e.g., spurious mentions, incorrect coreference resolution, failure to identify pronouns) would provide valuable insights for future improvements. The discussion of error cases feels superficial.
4. **Classifier Complexity & Redundancy:** The feature set, while comprehensive, appears somewhat redundant. Many features seem highly correlated, and the benefit of adding *all* of them is questionable. A feature selection process or dimensionality reduction technique could potentially simplify the model and improve its efficiency without sacrificing performance.
5. **Presentation of Results:** Table 4 is difficult to read, due to its size. Results should be presented more concisely, focusing on the key comparisons and highlighting the significance of the improvements.


**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Expansion:** What are your plans for expanding the WCR dataset? How will you ensure its quality and representativeness?  Specifically, what annotation guidelines are being followed beyond those of OntoNotes?
2. **Feature Importance:** Can you provide a more detailed analysis of feature importance, perhaps using feature weights from the SVM or a feature selection algorithm? Which features are *most* crucial for achieving good performance?
3. **Error Analysis:** Provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the system makes and identifying the underlying causes.
4. **Generalizability:**  Given the small dataset size, how confident are you that the results will generalize to other Wikipedia articles and domains?  Have you considered testing the system on a held-out set of articles that were not used during training or development?
5. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of your approach, particularly the feature extraction process? How does this compare to the cost of the baseline systems?



**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear, but some sentences are overly verbose.
*  Minor formatting inconsistencies exist (e.g., inconsistent use of italics).
* The reference to “somewhere.country” for data availability is not useful. Provide a specific URL or a clear commitment to sharing the data.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a worthwhile contribution in terms of a focused dataset and a detailed exploration of feature engineering, the small dataset size and overly optimistic reporting raise significant concerns. I recommend **rejection** at this time. The authors should prioritize expanding the dataset and conducting a more thorough error analysis before resubmitting. While the work is promising, it's not yet robust enough to warrant acceptance to a competitive conference like CoNLL.
