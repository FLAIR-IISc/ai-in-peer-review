This paper addresses the task of main concept resolution (MCR) in Wikipedia articles, framing it as a binary classification problem. The authors revisit a previously explored task, providing a testbed (WCR dataset) and comparing state-of-the-art systems. The primary contribution lies in the creation and release of the WCR dataset, along with the demonstration of improved performance on this specific task compared to generic coreference resolution engines. A secondary contribution is the integration of their classifier into a rule-based system, showing improved coreference chain detection. A tertiary contribution is the detailed feature engineering and ablation study demonstrating the importance of various features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Dataset:** The creation of the WCR dataset is a significant contribution. Existing coreference datasets primarily focus on news-like text. Wikipedia’s unique structure and writing style necessitate a dedicated resource for evaluating coreference systems in this domain. The dataset size (60k tokens, ~7k mentions, ~1.8k MC mentions) is reasonable for initial exploration and benchmarking. The authors' attention to sampling diverse articles is also a strength.
2. **Empirical Evaluation and Baselines:** The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation. The comparison against several strong baselines (both generic CR systems and heuristics tailored to the task) establishes a clear advantage for the proposed approach. The systematic exploration of baseline variations (B1-B4) demonstrates a good understanding of the challenges involved.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The authors thoroughly describe the features used, inspired by prior work but adapted for the specific characteristics of Wikipedia. The distinction between features for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions is well-justified. The ablation study (Table 2) provides valuable insights into the relative importance of different feature families.
4. **Positive System Integration:**  Demonstrating an improvement in coreference resolution performance when integrating the classifier into Dcoref (Table 5) validates the utility of the MCR approach as a component of a larger system. This strengthens the practical relevance of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size & Annotation Details:** While the creation of the WCR dataset is valuable, its size is relatively small. The limited scope raises concerns about the generalizability of the results. The description of the annotation process is brief. More details on inter-annotator agreement and annotation guidelines would strengthen confidence in the dataset's quality.
2. **Classifier Complexity & Explainability:** The reliance on SVMs with hundreds of features, while effective, limits the interpretability of the model. It would be beneficial to understand *which* features are most predictive for specific error cases. Exploring simpler, more interpretable models might offer insights for further improvement.
3. **Dependence on External Resources:** The approach heavily relies on Freebase and Wikipedia markup. While leveraging this information is sensible for Wikipedia text, the performance is directly tied to the quality and completeness of these external resources. The robustness of the system to errors or missing information in these sources is not thoroughly investigated.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper mentions some error examples (Figure 3), but a more systematic error analysis would be valuable. Categorizing the types of errors made by the system (e.g., ambiguity, lack of context, feature limitations) could guide future research directions.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What was the inter-annotator agreement on the WCR dataset? Please provide detailed annotation guidelines.
2. Could you elaborate on the types of errors made by the classifier, perhaps with a more quantitative breakdown?
3. How sensitive is the performance of your system to errors or missing information in Freebase? Have you explored techniques for handling such cases?
4. Given the dataset size, have you considered using pre-trained language models to potentially improve performance and generalization?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The URL for the dataset release ("www.somewhere.country") needs to be replaced with an actual link.
*   The formatting of some tables (e.g., Table 1) could be improved for readability.
*   The introduction could benefit from a more concise summary of related work and a clearer statement of the research gap the paper addresses.
