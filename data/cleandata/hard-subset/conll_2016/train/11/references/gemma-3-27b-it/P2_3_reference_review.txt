## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

**Summary & Contributions:**

This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of a Wikipedia article’s main concept (MC). The authors recognize that coreference resolution (CR) systems trained on news data perform poorly on Wikipedia, and aim to provide a testbed and evaluation of existing and a novel approach to this specific task. The primary contribution is a binary classification approach, leveraging features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, to identify MC mentions. Secondary contributions include a dataset (WCR) and an improvement to a rule-based CR system (Dcoref) through integration of the classifier.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses an important gap:** The paper rightly points out the lack of dedicated CR resources for Wikipedia, despite its widespread use in NLP. This focus on a specific genre is valuable, as it acknowledges the domain adaptation problem in CR.
2. **Well-motivated approach:** Framing the task as binary classification of MC mentions is a sensible simplification. The features used, combining Wikipedia structure (links, redirects) and knowledge base information (Freebase), are intuitively relevant and likely to be informative for this task.
3. **Empirical validation and comparison:** The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation, comparing their approach against several state-of-the-art CR systems and well-defined baselines.  The results demonstrate a clear improvement over existing methods on this specific task.
4. **Practical Improvement to Existing System:** Demonstrating an improvement to an existing rule-based system (Dcoref) shows practical applicability of the work.  Integrating the classifier as a sieve is a clever way to leverage the strengths of both approaches.
5. **Resource Contribution (WCR):** While the paper only briefly mentions the dataset, the creation of WCR is a positive contribution to the community, providing a resource for future research on Wikipedia coreference.  (Further details on size & annotation quality would be beneficial – see questions).



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Method:** While the feature engineering is relevant, the core method – a binary classifier using SVM – isn't inherently novel. The key contribution lies in *applying* this well-established method to a specific problem with carefully chosen features. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the novelty compared to existing feature-based CR approaches.
2. **Dataset Size & Representativeness:** The WCR dataset, with 30 documents, is quite small. While the authors attempt to address representativeness, a larger and more diverse dataset would significantly strengthen the conclusions.  The paper lacks a detailed description of the selection criteria for these 30 articles. Are they representative of the broader range of Wikipedia articles?
3. **Over-reliance on Freebase:** The approach heavily depends on Freebase, which is no longer actively maintained. This raises concerns about the long-term viability of the method. The authors should acknowledge this limitation and discuss potential alternatives for acquiring knowledge base information.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:**  While the paper mentions some error cases (e.g., in Figure 3), a more comprehensive error analysis would be valuable. Understanding *why* the classifier makes mistakes could inform future improvements and provide insights into the challenges of MC resolution.  What types of errors are most frequent?  Are they related to specific features or article types?
5. **Lack of Ablation Study Details:** The discussion of feature ablation (Table 2) is a bit brief.  While it shows the impact of each feature family, it doesn't provide detailed results for individual features *within* each family.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Details:**  Please provide a more detailed description of the WCR dataset, including: (a) The criteria used for selecting the 30 Wikipedia articles. (b) The average length of the articles in terms of tokens. (c) A more detailed breakdown of the annotation statistics, including inter-annotator agreement. (d) The availability of the dataset (will it be publicly released?).
2. **Freebase Dependency:**  Recognizing the limitations of Freebase, what alternative knowledge bases have you considered, and how feasible would it be to adapt your approach to use them?
3. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide a more comprehensive error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the classifier makes and identifying the most common sources of misclassification?
4. **Feature Importance:** Beyond the family-level ablation study, can you provide insights into the most important individual features contributing to the classifier's performance? Which features have the strongest positive or negative impact?
5. **Scalability:** How well do you anticipate this approach would scale to larger Wikipedia articles or to processing an entire corpus of Wikipedia content?  Are there computational bottlenecks that would need to be addressed?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   The references are relevant and up-to-date.
*   The paper could be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of related work in the field of knowledge-based coreference resolution and domain adaptation.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that addresses an important problem and provides a valuable contribution to the field of coreference resolution. While the core method isn't groundbreaking, the focus on Wikipedia, the careful feature engineering, and the empirical evaluation are all strengths. The small dataset size and reliance on Freebase are weaknesses, but the authors can address these concerns in their response.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and weaknesses outlined above.
