This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of a Wikipedia article's main concept (MC), framing it as a binary classification problem. The primary contribution, as I see it, is the creation and release of a dataset (WCR) specifically tailored for this task, annotated with OntoNotes guidelines and enriched with Freebase information. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that a classifier leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase data can outperform generic coreference resolution systems on this specific task, and further improve a rule-based system (Dcoref). A third, more minor contribution, is a series of ablation studies exploring the impact of different feature sets on classifier performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Valuable Dataset:** The creation of the WCR dataset is a significant strength. Existing coreference datasets are heavily biased towards news-like text. A dedicated resource for Wikipedia text, annotated to a high standard, is a crucial step towards developing systems that generalize beyond this bias. The dataset size, while not enormous, appears reasonable for initial experimentation and serves as a strong foundation for future work.
2. **Sound Experimental Setup:** The paper employs a relatively thorough experimental setup, comparing the proposed approach against several state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems and providing ablation studies. The use of a 10-fold cross-validation procedure and established evaluation metrics (MUC, B3, CEAF, CoNLL F1) strengthens the validity of the results. Reporting performance on both WCR and OntoNotes allows for a direct comparison of generalization capability.
3. **Clear and Logical Approach:** The proposed approach is logically sound. Utilizing Wikipedia-specific features (markup, links) and integrating external knowledge (Freebase) is a sensible strategy for adapting coreference resolution to this unique text genre. The separation of classifiers for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions is justified and potentially beneficial.
4. **Demonstrated Improvement:** The results demonstrate a clear improvement over baseline systems, both in terms of identifying MC mentions and enhancing the performance of the Dcoref system. The gains are statistically significant and suggest the proposed features are indeed effective.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** The core approach – training a classifier with features derived from existing resources – isn't particularly novel. Feature engineering, while well-executed, feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The method relies heavily on the quality of existing resources (Wikipedia and Freebase) and doesn’t present a truly innovative coreference *resolution* technique. It’s a clever application of existing tools to a specific problem, but doesn’t advance the underlying field of coreference resolution in a fundamental way.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** While some error analysis is provided (examples in Figure 3), it's superficial. A more in-depth analysis of *why* the system fails in certain cases would be highly valuable. What types of mentions are consistently misclassified? What are the limitations of the features used? Deeper insights into error patterns would inform future research.
3. **Dependence on Pre-existing Systems:** The baselines are built *on top* of existing coreference resolution systems. The performance of the proposed approach is thus inherently tied to the quality of these underlying systems. The paper doesn’t adequately address the potential for biases introduced by this dependency, or explore the possibility of a truly independent implementation.
4. **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost or scalability of the approach. Extracting features from Wikipedia and Freebase can be resource-intensive. A discussion of the practical limitations and potential optimizations would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper states the WCR dataset contains roughly 7000 non-singleton mentions. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the dataset size: number of articles, average number of mentions per article, distribution of mention types (named entities, noun phrases, pronouns)?
2. What is the computational cost of feature extraction and classification for a single Wikipedia article?  How does this scale with article length?
3. The error analysis (Figure 3) is limited. Could you provide a more comprehensive analysis of the most common error types, and discuss potential strategies for mitigating them?
4. You mention inaccuracies in Freebase and Wikipedia can cause cascading errors. How were these inaccuracies addressed during data preparation and feature engineering? Did you implement any quality control measures?
