## Peer Review: Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of a Wikipedia article's main concept (MC) – a specific instance of coreference resolution tailored to the unique characteristics of Wikipedia. Recognizing that general CR systems perform poorly on this genre, the authors propose a binary classification approach using features extracted from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. They evaluate their approach against several baselines built on state-of-the-art CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref) and demonstrate improved performance, further showing that integrating their classifier into Dcoref enhances coreference chain detection.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A Focused Dataset & Evaluation:** The creation of the WCR dataset, specifically designed for evaluating MC resolution in Wikipedia, is a valuable contribution.  The publicly releasing of this dataset will facilitate further research in this area.
2. **Demonstration of Genre-Specific Adaptation:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that adapting CR techniques to the specific characteristics of Wikipedia (markup, links, Freebase integration) yields significant performance gains over generic CR systems.
3. **Effective Feature Engineering:** The carefully designed feature set, leveraging Wikipedia’s structure and external knowledge, is a strong aspect of the work and contributes to the performance improvements.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of adapting CR to Wikipedia and provides a compelling justification for its importance. The connection to downstream tasks like OIE highlights the practical relevance.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The comparison against multiple baselines, including strong state-of-the-art systems, is well executed. The use of 10-fold cross-validation and standard evaluation metrics (CoNLL scorer) adds to the robustness of the results.
3. **Detailed Feature Analysis:**  The ablation study in Section 6.3, demonstrating the contribution of each feature family, provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of their approach. This level of detail helps understand *why* the model works.
4. **Integration with Existing System:** The successful integration of the classifier into Dcoref and subsequent performance gain is a positive result, showing practical applicability.
5. **Commitment to Public Release:** The authors' intent to release the dataset and data dump is highly commendable and will facilitate reproducibility and further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** While the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution, its size (30 documents) is relatively small. This limits the generalizability of the results and the statistical significance of the performance improvements.  While the authors acknowledge this, it remains a significant concern.
2. **Over-Reliance on Wikipedia Structure:** The approach is heavily reliant on the well-structured nature of Wikipedia.  The generalizability of these techniques to less structured text remains unclear. While the authors touch on this, exploring this limitation further would be beneficial.
3. **Classifier Complexity & Explainability:** The paper describes numerous features.  While the ablation study helps understand their impact, the overall complexity of the classifier makes it difficult to interpret the model's decision-making process. Providing examples of misclassifications and analyzing the contributing features would be helpful.
4. **Baseline Selection:** While the selected baselines are strong, exploring a wider range of approaches, particularly those explicitly designed for Wikipedia (beyond the two cited in the introduction), might provide a more comprehensive comparison.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Expansion:**  Do you have plans to expand the WCR dataset in the future? What are the challenges in scaling up the annotation effort?
2. **Generalizability:** Have you explored the performance of your approach on other, less structured text genres? If so, what were the results? If not, what steps could be taken to improve the generalizability of the system?
3. **Error Analysis:** Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, including specific examples of misclassifications and the features that contributed to those errors?  This would help understand the limitations of the current approach.
4. **Feature Selection:**  Given the large number of features, have you considered feature selection techniques to identify the most informative features and potentially simplify the model?
5. **Freebase Dependency:** How robust is the system to changes or unavailability of Freebase data? Is there a fallback mechanism in place?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The figures are helpful in illustrating the representation of mentions and the main concept.
*   The inclusion of results for Dcoref++ is valuable and demonstrates the practical benefits of the approach.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of coreference resolution, particularly in the context of Wikipedia. The creation of the WCR dataset and the demonstration of the effectiveness of genre-specific adaptation are significant achievements. While the limited dataset size and reliance on Wikipedia structure are weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions raised above.
