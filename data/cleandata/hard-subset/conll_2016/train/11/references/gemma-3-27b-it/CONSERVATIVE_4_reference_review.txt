This paper addresses the task of main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles, framing it as a binary classification problem to identify mentions referring to the article’s central topic. The authors present a testbed (the WCR dataset) and compare several coreference resolution systems, ultimately demonstrating that their approach, leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase, outperforms existing methods. The primary contribution appears to be the creation and evaluation of the WCR dataset, providing a focused benchmark for main concept coreference, alongside a classification approach showing modest improvements over existing systems when integrated into a rule-based coreference system. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that existing generic coreference systems perform poorly on this specific task, highlighting the need for specialized approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Dataset Creation:** The introduction of the WCR dataset is a significant positive. A dedicated, annotated dataset focusing on main concept coreference within Wikipedia is a valuable resource for the community. The reported statistics suggest a challenging but feasible dataset for evaluation.
2. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the task of main concept resolution and justifies its importance, particularly in the context of information extraction from Wikipedia. Framing it as a binary classification problem is a reasonable and practical approach.
3. **Systematic Evaluation:** The authors demonstrate a systematic approach to evaluating multiple baseline systems (both statistical and rule-based) and their own method. The comparison against established coreference engines provides a useful benchmark, clearly demonstrating the shortcomings of generic systems.
4. **Feature Engineering:** The feature set, combining Wikipedia markup, Freebase attributes, and linguistic features, appears well-considered and demonstrates an understanding of the relevant information sources. The ablation study (Table 2) provides some insight into the importance of different feature groups.
5. **Integration with Existing System:**  Showing improvement in a rule-based system (Dcoref++) demonstrates a practical application of their classifier and provides a more substantial result than simply reporting classification accuracy.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Approach:** While the results are positive, the core method – a binary classifier with manually engineered features – lacks significant novelty.  It’s an application of well-established machine learning techniques to a specific task, rather than a methodological breakthrough. The reliance on external knowledge sources (Freebase, Wikipedia) is also not new in coreference resolution.
2. **Small Dataset Size:** The WCR dataset, with 30 documents and approximately 7000 mentions, is relatively small.  The reported performance gains, while statistically significant within the cross-validation framework, may not generalize robustly to larger, more diverse datasets. A larger, more representative dataset is crucial for validating the effectiveness of the approach.
3. **Over-reliance on Heuristics:** The baselines, particularly the aggregation strategies (B3, B4), rely on heuristics that seem fragile and potentially prone to errors. While they establish a lower bound for performance, they don’t represent truly state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems adapted to this task.  A more rigorous comparison would involve fine-tuning existing systems specifically for main concept resolution.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis.  While Figure 3 shows a few examples of errors, a more systematic analysis of the types of errors made by the system would provide valuable insights for future improvement. Understanding *why* the system fails is crucial.
5. **Limited Generalizability:** The focus solely on Wikipedia articles limits the generalizability of the findings. Wikipedia has a specific structure and style. It's unclear whether the proposed approach would perform well on other text genres.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What are the annotation guidelines used for the WCR dataset, and how were annotators trained to ensure consistency? Providing access to these guidelines would be beneficial for reproducibility.
2.  You mention a 10-fold cross-validation. Was the data split stratified to ensure a balanced representation of mentions referring to the MC in each fold?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by your system (e.g., incorrect pronoun resolution, ambiguity due to multiple potential MCs)?
4.  What is the computational cost of your feature extraction process? Is it feasible to apply this approach to large-scale Wikipedia datasets?
5.  You mention that the WCR dataset and extracted data will be available. When will this data be released, and what format will it be in?
