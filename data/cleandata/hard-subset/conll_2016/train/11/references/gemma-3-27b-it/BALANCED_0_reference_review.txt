This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of the main concept (MC) within Wikipedia articles, a challenging coreference resolution problem often overlooked in favor of news-like text. The authors revisit this task, providing a testbed (WCR dataset) and demonstrating that existing state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems perform poorly on this genre. They propose a binary classification approach, leveraging features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, which outperforms baselines and improves a rule-based system (Dcoref). 

**Main Contributions:**

1. **The WCR Dataset:** The creation and release of a dedicated dataset for evaluating coreference resolution specifically tailored to the characteristics of Wikipedia text is the strongest contribution. The dataset provides a valuable resource for the community, enabling focused research on this under-explored area. The annotation details are promising (following OntoNotes guidelines) and the dataset size appears reasonable for initial experimentation.
2. **Demonstration of Genre Dependence:** The empirical results clearly demonstrate that coreference resolution systems trained on news data degrade significantly when applied to Wikipedia. This underscores the importance of genre-specific adaptation and provides a strong motivation for the work.
3. **Effective Feature Engineering:** While not groundbreaking in itself, the careful selection and combination of features derived from Wikipedia structure (hyperlinks, redirects) and knowledge bases (Freebase) are crucial to the success of the approach. The feature engineering process seems thoughtful and well-motivated.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of adapting coreference resolution to Wikipedia and convincingly argues for its importance. The introduction effectively frames the gap in existing research.
2. **Comprehensive Baseline Comparison:**  The authors evaluate their approach against several strong baselines, including recent state-of-the-art coreference systems (Dcoref, Scoref). This thorough comparison provides a solid foundation for assessing the effectiveness of the proposed method.
3. **Detailed Feature Description:** The paper provides a detailed and well-organized description of the features used in the classifier. This transparency allows for reproducibility and facilitates further research.  The breakdown of feature families and their individual impact is particularly valuable.
4. **Positive Impact on Existing System:** Showing improvement when integrating the classifier with Dcoref demonstrates practical applicability and potential for broader impact.  The 4% F1 increase is significant.
5. **Commitment to Reproducibility:** The authors’ intention to release the dataset and data dump is commendable and will further enhance the value of the work for the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** While the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution, its size (30 documents) is relatively small. This limits the generalizability of the results and raises concerns about potential overfitting, even with cross-validation. A larger, more diverse dataset would significantly strengthen the findings.
2. **Classifier Complexity:** The use of separate classifiers for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions, while potentially effective, adds complexity. The justification for this separation could be made more explicit. It's not immediately clear why a single model couldn't be trained with appropriate features to distinguish between mention types.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The error analysis section (Figure 3) is brief and doesn't provide a comprehensive understanding of the types of errors the system makes. A more in-depth analysis, categorizing errors and identifying patterns, would be highly beneficial.  Specifically, a discussion on *why* certain errors occur, beyond simply stating they are “difficult cases”, is needed.
4. **Dependency on External Resources:** The reliance on Wikipedia structure and Freebase introduces potential dependencies and vulnerabilities. The performance could be affected by changes or inaccuracies in these external resources. This is not thoroughly discussed.
5. **Evaluation Metric Focus:** The primary evaluation focuses on F1 score.  While a standard metric, it may not fully capture the nuances of coreference resolution in this context. Considering other metrics (e.g., precision/recall specifically for pronominal mentions) could provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What steps were taken to mitigate potential overfitting given the small dataset size? (e.g., regularization, feature selection).
2.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for using separate classifiers for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions? Were there specific performance considerations that drove this decision?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by the system and identifying the most frequent error patterns?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of the system to inaccuracies or changes in the Wikipedia structure and Freebase data? Have you considered methods for mitigating these dependencies?
5.  Beyond F1 score, did you consider other evaluation metrics (e.g., precision/recall for specific mention types) to provide a more nuanced assessment of performance?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is good, but could benefit from including more recent advances in coreference resolution that incorporate external knowledge.
*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, but some sections (e.g., section 4.2) are quite dense and could be restructured for improved clarity.
*   The link to the data availability webpage ("www.somewhere.country") should be replaced with a concrete URL in the final version.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of coreference resolution. The creation of the WCR dataset is a significant achievement, and the empirical results demonstrate the importance of adapting coreference models to specific genres. While the relatively small dataset size and limited error analysis are weaknesses, the strengths of the work outweigh these concerns. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions** to address the questions and suggestions outlined above. The authors have made a substantial contribution to the community, and I believe this work will stimulate further research in this important area.
