## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of the "main concept" (MC) within Wikipedia articles. Recognizing the poor performance of general coreference resolution (CR) systems on Wikipedia text, the authors propose a binary classification approach to identify MC mentions, leveraging features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. They demonstrate their approach outperforms existing CR systems on a newly created dataset (WCR) and improves a rule-based CR system when integrated.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A Dedicated Dataset (WCR):** The creation of a manually annotated dataset specifically for evaluating main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles is the most significant contribution. This addresses a clear gap in resources for this task.
2. **A Feature-Rich Classification Approach:**  The authors present a well-motivated and comprehensive set of features, integrating information from Wikipedia’s structure (hyperlinks, redirects) and external knowledge (Freebase), demonstrating the potential of leveraging these resources for MC resolution.
3. **Empirical Validation and Integration:** The paper provides solid empirical evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach, both as a standalone classifier and when integrated into an existing CR system.



**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Relevance of the Task:** Focusing on main concept resolution within Wikipedia is a relevant and practical problem. Wikipedia’s unique structure makes it a challenging but valuable testbed for CR techniques, and this work correctly identifies the limitations of existing systems.
2. **Strong Baseline Comparison:**  The authors thoughtfully compare their approach to several state-of-the-art CR systems (both rule-based and machine learning-based) and provide a fair evaluation demonstrating clear improvements. The inclusion of baselines built on top of existing systems is a good practice.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:**  The paper thoroughly describes the feature set used, providing clear justifications for each feature's inclusion. The exploration of different feature families and their impact on performance is particularly insightful (see Table 2).
4. **Practical Integration and Improvement:**  Successfully integrating the classifier into an existing rule-based system (Dcoref++) and demonstrating performance gains highlights the practical applicability of the research.
5. **Dataset Availability:** The authors commit to releasing the dataset and related resources, which will be invaluable for future research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** While the WCR dataset is a valuable contribution, its size (30 documents, ~60k tokens) is relatively small. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the results and the statistical significance of the improvements reported.  The reported gains, while statistically tested within the paper, could be inflated due to the dataset size.
2. **Classifier Complexity:** While the feature engineering is detailed, the choice of SVM with a linear kernel feels somewhat basic given the complexity of the feature set. Exploring more advanced classifiers (e.g., neural networks) could potentially yield further improvements.  The reasoning for choosing SVM over other models isn't thoroughly explained.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis Depth:** While the authors mention some error cases (Figure 3), a more in-depth qualitative error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *why* the classifier makes certain errors could reveal further avenues for improvement.  Specifically, the challenges of handling ambiguous mentions and coreference across sentences deserve deeper examination.
4. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation focuses primarily on identifying mentions *of* the main concept. Evaluating the full CR pipeline (i.e., correctly chaining all mentions of the MC) could provide a more holistic assessment of the system’s performance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Expansion:** What are your plans for expanding the WCR dataset?  Will you be releasing the annotation guidelines to facilitate community contributions?
2. **Classifier Justification:**  Why was a linear SVM chosen over more complex models, given the rich feature set? Did you perform any preliminary experiments with different classifiers?
3. **Error Analysis Details:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the common error cases?  Specifically, what types of mentions are most frequently misclassified, and why?
4. **End-to-End Evaluation:** Have you evaluated the performance of your system on a full coreference resolution task, including chaining all mentions of the MC? If not, what are the challenges of doing so?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The related work section is comprehensive and provides good context for the research.
* The reference to making the data and code available is very positive.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that addresses a relevant problem with a well-motivated approach. The creation of the WCR dataset is a significant contribution, and the empirical results are promising. While the dataset size and the choice of classifier are areas for potential improvement, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of coreference resolution and warrants acceptance.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily addressing the points raised in the "Questions to Authors" section.
