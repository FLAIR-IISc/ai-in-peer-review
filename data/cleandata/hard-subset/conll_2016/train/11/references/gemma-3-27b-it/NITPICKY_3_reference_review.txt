This paper addresses the task of main concept resolution (MCR) in Wikipedia articles, framing it as a binary classification problem: determining whether a given mention refers to the article’s main concept. The authors revisit work by Nakayama (2008) and present a testbed, along with a feature-rich classifier, demonstrating improved performance over existing coreference resolution systems and enhancing a rule-based system (Lee et al., 2013). The primary contribution is a focused evaluation and a feature engineering effort specifically tailored to MCR in Wikipedia, along with a small dataset (WCR). A secondary contribution is the demonstration of performance gains when integrating their classifier into an existing rule-based system.

**Strengths:**

1. **Focused Task Definition:** The paper clearly defines and isolates the MCR task, which is a useful specialization of coreference resolution with specific applications in information extraction and knowledge base construction from Wikipedia. This focus allows for targeted development and evaluation.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate a thorough exploration of features, combining characteristics of mentions, Wikipedia markup, and external knowledge from Freebase. The division into pronominal and non-pronominal feature sets is sensible and shows an understanding of the nuances of the task. The detailed description of features (Sections 4.2.1 and 4.2.2) is commendable.
3. **Empirical Validation & Baseline Comparison:** The experimental setup includes several baselines built upon existing coreference systems, providing a solid benchmark for evaluating the proposed approach.  The comparison to state-of-the-art systems, even with the limitations of the small dataset, is valuable. The reported improvements, particularly the 4% F1 score gain when integrated into Dcoref, are noteworthy.
4. **Resource Creation:** The creation of the WCR dataset, while small (30 documents), is a positive contribution. The authors state they will release the dataset and mention predictions, facilitating future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Small Dataset Size & Generalizability:** The WCR dataset is critically small (30 documents).  The results, while statistically significant within the confines of the dataset, raise serious concerns about generalizability to larger, more diverse sets of Wikipedia articles. The performance differences between systems (Table 1) are substantial, yet potentially driven by the limited sample.  The paper acknowledges this but doesn’t adequately address the implications for the broader applicability of their findings.
2. **Overly Complex Feature Set & Limited Ablation:** While the feature engineering is thorough, the paper doesn’t provide a compelling justification for *every* feature. The ablation study (Table 2) focuses only on adding features incrementally; it does not investigate the *necessity* of any feature.  Removing redundant or uninformative features could simplify the model and potentially improve performance on unseen data.  The sheer number of features (hundreds) raises the risk of overfitting to the small training set.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis Depth:** The error analysis (Figure 3) is superficial. While it points out some failure cases, it lacks a systematic categorization of errors and a discussion of the underlying reasons for those errors. A deeper analysis is needed to understand the limitations of the approach and guide future improvements.  For example, the failure to resolve pronouns based on surrounding context (example f) is a significant issue that deserves more attention.
4. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** There are numerous minor formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper. For instance, inconsistent spacing, improper hyphenation ("co-referent"), and occasional grammatical errors distract from the content. The figure captions are also somewhat lacking in detail. While not fatal, these issues suggest a lack of careful attention to detail. The reference list could also benefit from more consistent formatting.
5. **Baseline Implementation Details:** The description of the baselines (Section 3) is somewhat vague. It would be helpful to have more detail about how these baselines were implemented and tuned. Specifically, the heuristics for aggregating chains seem somewhat ad hoc, and a more systematic exploration of these heuristics might have yielded better results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What steps were taken to mitigate the risk of overfitting given the small dataset size and the large number of features? Did you employ any regularization techniques or feature selection methods beyond the incremental feature addition in Table 2?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by your system and discussing the potential causes of those errors?
3.  What is the computational cost of your approach, both in terms of training time and inference time? How does this compare to the baselines?
4.  The paper mentions sharing the predictions on all of Wikipedia. What are the practical considerations for deploying your system at this scale (e.g., memory requirements, processing time)?
5. The baseline implementations seem somewhat heuristic. Were different variations of these heuristics explored, and if so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The link to the dataset (www.somewhere.country) is a placeholder and needs to be replaced with a valid URL.
*   Consider providing a more detailed discussion of related work, including recent advances in entity linking and knowledge graph completion, which are relevant to the MCR task.
*   The paper would benefit from a concluding section that explicitly outlines future research directions.



Overall, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the task of main concept resolution in Wikipedia. However, the small dataset size and the lack of in-depth error analysis raise concerns about the generalizability of the findings. The numerous minor formatting issues, while not critical, detract from the overall quality of the paper. I recommend **rejection** at this time, pending substantial revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly a significant expansion of the dataset or a more rigorous justification for the robustness of the results with the current dataset size.
