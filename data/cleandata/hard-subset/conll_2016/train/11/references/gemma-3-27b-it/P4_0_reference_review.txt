This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution in Wikipedia, specifically focusing on identifying mentions of the “main concept” (MC) of an article. The authors revisit this task, framing it as a binary classification problem and comparing state-of-the-art systems on a dedicated testbed. The primary contribution is the creation and evaluation of a dedicated testbed (WCR) for this task, along with a feature-rich binary classifier for identifying MC mentions that outperforms existing systems. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is demonstrating that integrating this classifier into a rule-based coreference system improves performance on full coreference resolution.

**Strengths:**

1. **Creation of a Dedicated Dataset (WCR):** The creation of the WCR dataset is a significant strength. Existing coreference resources are largely focused on news-like text, and Wikipedia presents unique characteristics. Having a dedicated, annotated dataset allows for more meaningful evaluation of systems designed for this genre. The authors provide details on dataset size and characteristics, indicating a thoughtful approach to its creation.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistently strong empirical results. The proposed approach outperforms existing coreference systems on the WCR dataset across multiple metrics. The ablation study (Table 2) provides valuable insights into the contribution of different feature families, demonstrating the importance of features tailored to the Wikipedia context.  The 13-point F1 gain over baselines is substantial.
3. **Integration with Existing System:** Showing that the classifier can be integrated into an existing rule-based system (Dcoref) and improve its performance is a practical and valuable result. This highlights the potential for using the proposed approach to enhance existing coreference pipelines. The 4% improvement in CoNLL F1 score confirms this benefit.
4. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors present a thoughtful and comprehensive set of features, drawing inspiration from existing work but also incorporating features specifically designed for Wikipedia’s markup and structure (hyperlinks, redirects, Freebase information).  The clear distinction between features for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the task.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** While the creation of WCR is a major strength, its relatively small size (30 documents, ~60k tokens) raises concerns about the generalizability of the results. The 10-fold cross-validation helps, but a larger dataset would provide more confidence in the findings. The authors acknowledge this limitation but further discussion of its potential impact is warranted.
2. **Reliance on External Resources (Freebase):** The approach heavily relies on external knowledge sources like Freebase. While leveraging external knowledge is common in coreference resolution, the dependence on Freebase introduces a potential point of failure and limits portability to languages or domains where such resources are unavailable or incomplete.  The paper does acknowledge potential issues with inaccuracies in external resources, but a more in-depth discussion of strategies for mitigating this dependency would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis Depth:** While the authors provide some example errors (Figure 3), a more systematic and in-depth error analysis would be valuable. Identifying common error patterns and their underlying causes could inform future improvements to the approach. The brief mention of difficulties with pronoun resolution and the example provided are good starting points, but a more comprehensive analysis would be stronger.
4. **Limited Comparison to More Recent Coreference Systems:** The comparison is largely to systems published in 2013-2015. While these are important baselines, including more recent and potentially competitive coreference models would strengthen the evaluation and demonstrate the continued relevance of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What are the plans for expanding the WCR dataset?  Is there a publicly available roadmap or intention to collect more annotated data?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the system to the quality and completeness of the Freebase data?  Have you explored any techniques for handling missing or inaccurate Freebase information?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors the system makes and identifying the key challenges in resolving coreference for Wikipedia's main concept?
4.  Have you experimented with more recent coreference resolution systems (e.g., those leveraging transformers) as baselines? If so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The link to the datasets and code will be crucial for reproducibility and further research.
*   The notation in Table 4 is a bit dense, and could benefit from clearer labeling (e.g., specifying what "P," "R," and "F1" refer to).
*   The description of the baselines in section 3 is clear and concise.
