## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of a Wikipedia article's main concept. While the problem itself is relevant – adapting coreference resolution (CR) to the specific characteristics of Wikipedia – the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, methodological shortcomings, and a general lack of rigor that, cumulatively, outweigh its contributions. The primary contribution, framing the task as binary classification and providing a testbed, is weakened by the relatively small dataset and the overly simplistic baseline comparisons.

**Summary & Contributions (as assessed):** The paper proposes a binary classification approach for identifying mentions of a Wikipedia article's main concept. A small, newly created dataset (WCR) is presented for evaluation. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms existing generic CR systems on this specific task and modestly improves a rule-based system when integrated. The most significant contribution lies in the creation of the WCR dataset, though its size is a limitation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance of the Task:** Adapting CR to Wikipedia is a worthwhile pursuit, given the platform’s unique structure and widespread use. The authors correctly identify the limitations of systems trained on newswire data when applied to Wikipedia.
2. **Dataset Creation:** The WCR dataset, while small, provides a focused resource for evaluating main concept resolution systems. The annotation guidelines based on OntoNotes are a positive aspect.
3. **Feature Engineering:** The features used, combining Wikipedia markup, Freebase information, and linguistic characteristics, demonstrate a thoughtful consideration of the task’s specific challenges. The attempt to leverage the hyperlink structure is commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Small Dataset & Limited Generalizability:** The WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. This drastically limits the statistical power of the experiments and raises concerns about the generalizability of the results. The authors acknowledge this but downplay the severity. A more substantial evaluation dataset is crucial for establishing the validity of the findings.
2. **Overly Simplistic Baselines:** The baselines (B1-B4) are rudimentary and do not represent a strong challenge to the proposed approach.  Simply picking the longest chain or aggregating based on substring matches feels more like proof-of-concept than a rigorous comparison to existing techniques. The choice to only compare against two full CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref) is insufficient. The paper needs to demonstrate superiority over more sophisticated baselines specifically tailored for Wikipedia or knowledge-rich CR.
3. **Lack of Rigorous Error Analysis:** The error analysis (Figure 3) is superficial and provides only anecdotal evidence. A systematic categorization of errors and a detailed discussion of the challenges faced by the classifier are missing. It’s not enough to *show* errors; the authors need to *explain* why they occur and suggest potential solutions.
4. **Methodological Concerns - Data Preparation:** The paper adds automatically extracted mentions to the annotated data. While attempting to enrich the data, it introduces noise. The lack of discussion of the quality of automatically extracted mentions and their potential impact on the results is concerning.
5. **Formatting & Presentation:** The paper suffers from numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of italics, spacing issues) and a generally clunky writing style. The organization is somewhat disjointed, and certain sections (e.g., 4.2.1 & 4.2.2) are overly verbose and repetitive. The numerous references to figure numbers without actual figures is also a major issue.
6. **Incremental Improvement:** The reported improvement in the Dcoref++ system is modest, and the claim that this demonstrates the value of the classifier is weak. The improvement could be due to factors other than the classifier itself, and a more thorough ablation study is necessary to isolate its contribution.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What steps have been taken to assess the quality and impact of the automatically extracted mentions added to the WCR dataset? Please provide metrics (precision/recall) and discuss the potential bias introduced.
2.  Why were only Dcoref and Scoref chosen for comparison? Have you experimented with other state-of-the-art CR systems, particularly those designed for knowledge-rich scenarios?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed and systematic error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by your classifier and discussing potential solutions?
4.  How sensitive are your results to the weighting applied to the negative class during SVM training? Have you explored other regularization techniques?
5.  Please provide the actual figures referenced throughout the text.

**Additional Comments:** The paper needs substantial revision. While the task is interesting and the WCR dataset potentially useful, the current presentation lacks the rigor and depth required for publication at a competitive venue like CoNLL. The focus on numerous minor feature engineering details overshadows the limitations of the experimental setup and the relatively modest contributions. A larger dataset, stronger baselines, a more thorough error analysis, and improved clarity of presentation are essential for addressing these concerns.



**Recommendation:** **Reject.** The flaws are too numerous and significant to warrant acceptance in its current form. A major revision addressing the weaknesses outlined above would be necessary for reconsideration.
