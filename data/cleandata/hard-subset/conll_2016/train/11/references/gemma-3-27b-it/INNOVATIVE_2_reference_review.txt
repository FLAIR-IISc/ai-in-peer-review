This paper tackles the interesting and under-explored problem of coreference resolution specifically tailored to Wikipedia articles, focusing on identifying mentions of the “main concept” of an article. The primary contribution is framing this task as a binary classification problem and demonstrating superior performance compared to generic CR systems, alongside a positive impact when integrated into a rule-based system. A secondary contribution is the creation and release of a dedicated Wikipedia coreference resource (WCR), albeit relatively small.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Task:** While coreference resolution is a well-studied area, focusing on the “main concept” in Wikipedia introduces a valuable constraint and a specific evaluation scenario. This moves beyond generic CR and targets a real-world need in knowledge base construction and information retrieval from Wikipedia. The authors correctly identify the shortcomings of applying generic CR systems to this unique text genre.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms established coreference systems on the WCR dataset, providing concrete evidence of the efficacy of their approach. The improvements are substantial (13-15 F1 points), suggesting the carefully designed features are effectively capturing relevant information for this task. The improvement to the Dcoref system is also promising.
3. **Feature Engineering is Insightful:** The feature set, combining Wikipedia markup, Freebase information, and linguistic characteristics, appears well-motivated and thoughtfully designed. The ablation study in Section 6.3 clearly demonstrates the importance of each feature family, adding credibility to the approach. The use of Wikipedia’s inherent structure (hyperlinks, redirects) as a source of less ambiguous information is clever.
4. **Resource Contribution:** The WCR dataset, while small, is a valuable contribution to the community. Providing a focused resource encourages further research in this specific area and facilitates comparative evaluation.  The promise to release the dataset and identified MC mentions is excellent.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** The biggest concern is the small size of the WCR dataset (30 documents). While the authors acknowledge this, it raises questions about the generalizability of the results. The performance gains might be specific to this particular dataset and not translate well to larger, more diverse Wikipedia corpora.  More extensive evaluation is crucial.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the authors mention a few error examples, a more systematic error analysis would be valuable. Understanding *why* the system fails in certain cases could guide future improvements.  What types of mentions are consistently misclassified? Are there patterns related to article topic or structure?
3. **Classifier Complexity & Explainability:** The paper focuses on feature engineering for the classifiers but lacks details on the classifiers themselves. The choice of SVM with a linear kernel is reasonable, but discussing the feature importance ranking within the SVM model would add valuable insight. Also, a more nuanced discussion of how features interact would be useful.
4. **Limited Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While comparing to state-of-the-art CR systems is good, some of the selected systems (e.g., Durrett & Klein 2013) are a few years old. Including more recent, competitive baselines – especially those incorporating knowledge graph information – would strengthen the evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What steps are you taking to expand the WCR dataset? What are the challenges in creating a larger, high-quality annotated dataset for this task?
2. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors made by the system? Are there specific types of mentions (e.g., complex noun phrases, ambiguous pronouns) that pose a significant challenge?
3. Can you provide a feature importance ranking from your SVM models? Which features are most predictive of a mention referring to the main concept?
4. Have you experimented with more sophisticated classifier architectures (e.g., neural networks) for this task? If so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
* The references are relevant and appropriately cited.
* The code and dataset release is highly encouraged and will be valuable for the community.



**Overall:**

Despite the limitations of the dataset size, I believe this paper presents a novel and promising approach to coreference resolution in Wikipedia. The strong empirical results, insightful feature engineering, and resource contribution outweigh the weaknesses. I lean towards **acceptance**, with the strong encouragement that the authors prioritize expanding the dataset and conducting a more thorough error analysis in future work. The boldness of tackling a specific, underserved problem in a creative way is commendable.
