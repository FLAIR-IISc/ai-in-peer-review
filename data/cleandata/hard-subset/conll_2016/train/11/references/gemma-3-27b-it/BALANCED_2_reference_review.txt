This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution specifically tailored to Wikipedia articles, focusing on identifying mentions of the “main concept” (MC) of an article. The authors revisit this task, providing a testbed (the WCR dataset) and comparing several coreference resolution systems, along with a novel classifier designed for this specific challenge.

**Summary of the Paper and Contributions:**

The paper effectively identifies a gap in the coreference resolution landscape: a lack of resources and systems specifically adapted to the characteristics of Wikipedia text. The authors tackle this by creating a dataset, framing the MC resolution problem as a binary classification task, and developing a feature-rich classifier. 

I see three main contributions:

1. **The WCR Dataset:** The creation of the WCR dataset is a significant contribution. While relatively small (30 documents), it's specifically annotated for the MC resolution task and provides a valuable benchmark for evaluating systems on Wikipedia data. The detailed annotation guidelines aligning with OntoNotes further enhance its usability.
2. **A Feature-Rich Classifier for MC Resolution:** The authors demonstrate a well-designed classifier leveraging Wikipedia markup, Freebase information, and features inspired by existing CR systems. This shows careful consideration of the unique features of Wikipedia text.  The separation of classifiers for pronominal vs. non-pronominal mentions is a sensible approach.
3. **Demonstration of Adaptation Benefit:** The paper convincingly shows that adapting coreference resolution techniques to Wikipedia, rather than directly applying generic systems, yields performance improvements.  Integrating their classifier into Dcoref is a concrete example of this. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem – the poor performance of general CR systems on Wikipedia – and motivates the need for a dedicated approach.
2. **Thorough Experimentation:**  The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation, comparing their approach to multiple baselines (including state-of-the-art CR engines) and performing ablation studies to analyze feature importance. The cross-validation setup is sound.
3. **Effective Feature Engineering:** The chosen features, leveraging Wikipedia’s structured data (links, redirects, Freebase) appear to be well-suited to the task. The distinction between pronominal and non-pronominal feature sets is insightful.
4. **Publicly Available Resources:** The commitment to releasing the dataset and the identified MC mentions is excellent and will facilitate future research.
5. **Improvements to Existing System:** Demonstrating a positive impact on an existing coreference system (Dcoref++) strengthens the significance of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** The biggest weakness is the relatively small size of the WCR dataset. While the authors acknowledge this, the conclusions drawn about the superiority of their approach should be interpreted cautiously. Performance gains might not generalize to larger, more diverse Wikipedia corpora.
2. **Baseline Selection:** While the authors use strong baselines, it's not entirely clear *why* Dcoref performed better than other state-of-the-art statistical systems on the WCR dataset. More investigation into this discrepancy would be valuable. Is it the rule-based nature, or specific characteristics of the implementation?
3. **Feature Ablation Detail:** While ablation studies are performed, the report of these results is a bit shallow. Showing the specific gains (or losses) for *each* feature in a table (as is done for pronominal mentions, but not non-pronominal) would give more insight.  A more detailed analysis of error cases linked to specific features would also be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** Beyond mentioning some difficult cases in Section 6.3, a more systematic error analysis is missing.  What types of mentions are consistently misclassified? Are there specific Wikipedia article structures that pose challenges?  This would inform future improvements.
5. **Limited Generalizability Discussion:** The paper focuses heavily on the MC resolution task. While valuable, the authors could strengthen the work by discussing how the lessons learned about adapting CR to Wikipedia might generalize to other NLP tasks or datasets.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Given the small dataset size, how confident are you that the observed performance differences between your approach and the baselines are statistically significant?  Could you provide confidence intervals?
2.  Why did Dcoref outperform the other statistical baselines (Durrett & Klein, Martschat & Strube, Clark & Manning) on the WCR dataset? Was any specific tuning applied to Dcoref for this task?
3.  Could you provide a table detailing the feature ablation study results for *non-pronominal* mentions, similar to Table 2 for pronominal mentions?
4.  Can you elaborate on the specific types of errors observed in the classifier? Are there particular categories of mentions or article structures that consistently lead to misclassifications?
5.  Beyond the MC resolution task, how do you envision the insights gained from this work being applied to other NLP tasks utilizing Wikipedia data (e.g., relation extraction, knowledge base completion)?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   Figure 3 is helpful in illustrating example classifications and errors.
*   The reference to the availability of the dataset and MC mentions at “www.somewhere.country” should be replaced with a real URL.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of coreference resolution. The creation of the WCR dataset and the demonstrated success of their feature-rich classifier are commendable. While the small dataset size is a concern, the work provides a strong foundation for future research on adapting CR to the unique characteristics of Wikipedia text. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The authors should prioritize a more detailed error analysis and provide additional justification for the baseline performance discrepancies.
