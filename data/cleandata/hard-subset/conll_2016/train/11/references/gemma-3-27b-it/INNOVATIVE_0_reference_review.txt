This paper tackles the interesting and under-explored problem of coreference resolution specifically within Wikipedia, focusing on identifying mentions of the “main concept” of an article. While coreference resolution is well-studied, its adaptation to specific genres like Wikipedia is surprisingly lacking, making this a valuable direction. The primary contribution is framing this task as a binary classification problem and demonstrating that a classifier leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase knowledge outperforms generic CR systems and improves a rule-based system. A secondary, but still significant, contribution is the creation and release of the WCR dataset, offering a valuable resource for future research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Task:** The focus on “main concept resolution” within Wikipedia is genuinely novel. Most CR research targets news or conversational text. Recognizing the specific characteristics of Wikipedia – its structure, markup, and link density – and tailoring a system to exploit them is a strong approach. This isn’t simply applying existing CR to a new domain; it *defines* a new, arguably more constrained and useful, CR subtask.
2. **Effective Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the unique information available in Wikipedia. The features leveraging markup (redirects, internal links) and Freebase connections are well-motivated and demonstrably improve performance. The feature ablation study (Table 2) is compelling, clearly showing the value of each feature family.
3. **Positive Results & Integration:** The consistent outperformance of their approach compared to strong baselines (including state-of-the-art generic CR systems) is impressive. The successful integration into the Dcoref system (leading to further improvements) further validates the approach's utility.
4. **Resource Contribution:** The creation and planned release of the WCR dataset is a substantial contribution to the community. This dataset will facilitate further research and allow for more standardized evaluation of Wikipedia-specific CR systems. The size, though not massive, seems appropriate for initial exploration.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper mentions a few example errors, a more thorough error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding *why* the classifier fails in certain cases could guide future improvements. Specifically, a detailed categorization of error types (e.g., ambiguity due to multiple concepts, difficulty with pronoun resolution) would be valuable. The brief mention of example (f) is a start, but more depth is needed.
2. **Dataset Size & Representativeness:** The WCR dataset, at 30 documents, is relatively small. While the authors acknowledge this, it raises concerns about the generalizability of the results. The selection of articles (various characteristics mentioned) is reassuring, but more detail on the selection process would strengthen this point. Could the results be skewed towards certain types of Wikipedia articles?
3. **Classifier Complexity:** The reliance on SVMs feels somewhat dated, given the recent advances in neural network-based coreference resolution. While the performance is strong, exploring more modern architectures might yield further improvements. This isn’t a critical flaw, but a missed opportunity.
4. **Lack of Discussion of Scalability:** The paper doesn’t address the scalability of the approach to the entirety of Wikipedia. The preprocessing steps (accessing WikipediaMiner, querying Freebase) could be computationally expensive for the entire corpus.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, categorizing the types of errors your system makes?
2. What were the specific criteria used to select the 30 articles for the WCR dataset? What steps were taken to ensure representativeness?
3. Have you considered experimenting with neural network-based architectures for this task? If not, why?
4. What is the estimated computational cost of preprocessing a large number of Wikipedia articles using your method? Are there plans to optimize this process for scalability?

**Additional Comments:**

* The references are well-cited and relevant.
* The paper is generally well-written and clear, though some sections (particularly Section 4.2) could benefit from further simplification and clarification.
* The availability of the dataset and code would be highly appreciated.



**Overall Recommendation:**

Despite the minor weaknesses, I recommend **accepting** this paper. The novelty of the task, the strong performance, the thoughtful feature engineering, and the valuable contribution of the WCR dataset outweigh the limitations. This work represents a significant step forward in adapting coreference resolution to the unique characteristics of Wikipedia and opens up exciting avenues for future research. I prioritize novel ideas and under-explored areas, and this paper delivers on both fronts.
