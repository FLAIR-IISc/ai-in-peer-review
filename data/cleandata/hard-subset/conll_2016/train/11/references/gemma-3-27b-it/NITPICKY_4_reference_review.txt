This paper addresses the task of identifying mentions of the main concept (MC) within Wikipedia articles, framing it as a binary classification problem. The authors claim contributions in: 1) revisiting the MC resolution task, providing a testbed and comparisons to existing systems; 2) proposing a feature-rich classifier leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase; and 3) demonstrating improvements to a rule-based coreference system via integration of their classifier.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines a focused coreference task – identifying mentions of the main concept within a Wikipedia article – which is a valuable and under-explored area. This contrasts with broad, general coreference resolution.
2. **Comprehensive Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate a thorough effort in feature engineering, drawing on both intrinsic mention characteristics and external knowledge sources (Wikipedia markup, Freebase). The detailed descriptions of feature families (base, title, tag, main, etc.) are commendable, even if their individual contributions vary.
3. **Empirical Evaluation:** The inclusion of a dedicated dataset (WCR) and comparison to several state-of-the-art coreference systems provide a solid empirical grounding.  The results demonstrating improvement over baselines and integration with Dcoref are positive. The comparison to systems on the OntoNotes dataset, while showing the difficulty of the task, is helpful context.
4. **Detailed Description of System Components:** The description of the pre-processing steps and feature extraction process are detailed and well-structured. This allows for reproducibility and potential extension of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size & Representativeness:** The WCR dataset, comprised of only 30 documents, is critically small. While the authors acknowledge this, the conclusions drawn from such a limited corpus are questionable. The claim that the dataset “samples Wikipedia articles of various characteristics” is unsubstantiated and requires significantly more justification. A dataset of this size is susceptible to biases, and results may not generalize.
2. **Incremental Improvement, Not Breakthrough:** While the performance gains are statistically significant, the absolute F1 scores achieved (around 89%) are not dramatically higher than existing systems, especially considering the dedicated task and extensive feature engineering. The improvement feels incremental rather than a fundamental advance in coreference resolution.
3. **Feature Importance Analysis Lacks Rigor:** The feature ablation study (Table 2) is presented superficially. Simply stating that a feature family “improves” performance without detailed statistical analysis or effect sizes is insufficient. Were the improvements statistically significant? Were there any feature interactions?
4. **Overly Complex Feature Set:** The sheer number of features (hundreds) raises concerns about overfitting, especially given the small dataset. A more parsimonious feature set, perhaps identified through feature selection techniques, would likely be more robust.
5. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the authors briefly mention errors (e.g., example (f) in Figure 3), a more systematic error analysis would be invaluable. Understanding *why* the system fails in specific cases would provide insights for future improvements and highlight the limitations of the approach.  The discussion of error examples is anecdotal and unconvincing.
6. **Formatting and Typographical Errors:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies and typographical errors detract from the professionalism of the paper (e.g., “Dcoref++” appearing in Table 5 before being formally introduced, inconsistent spacing, awkward phrasing). While individually minor, these issues accumulate and indicate a lack of meticulousness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed justification for the representativeness of the WCR dataset, including specific characteristics that were considered during the selection process?
2. Have you performed any feature selection experiments to identify a more parsimonious feature set? If so, what were the results?
3. Can you present a more rigorous analysis of the feature ablation study, including statistical significance tests and effect sizes?
4. Provide a more in-depth error analysis, categorizing common error types and discussing potential solutions.
5. Please address the numerous minor formatting and typographical errors throughout the paper.



**Additional Comments:**

*  The reference list is generally well-formatted, but check for completeness and consistency.
* The description of the baselines is clear, but could benefit from a more concise summary table.
*  The link to the "somewhere.country" data repository is placeholder text and needs to be updated.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a valuable task definition and a reasonably well-engineered system, the small dataset size and incremental improvements are significant weaknesses. The lack of rigorous analysis and numerous minor errors further detract from the overall quality. **I recommend rejection.**  The authors should significantly expand the dataset, conduct a more thorough analysis of feature importance and errors, and carefully address the formatting issues before resubmitting. The potential of the task is there, but this submission does not currently demonstrate a substantial contribution to the field.
