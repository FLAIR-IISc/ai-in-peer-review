Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper targets “main concept” resolution (MCR) in Wikipedia: given an article, identify all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the article’s subject. The task is framed as binary classification over detected mentions.
- Contributions, as I see them:
  1) A feature-based classifier that leverages Wikipedia markup and Freebase attributes to identify MC mentions, with separate models for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions; and an ablation over feature families.
  2) A small Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR) and an evaluation showing that off-the-shelf CR systems degrade substantially on this genre; construction of four baselines from Dcoref/Scoref outputs for the MC task.
  3) Integration of the MC classifier as a sieve in Dcoref (Dcoref++), yielding a 4-point CoNLL F1 improvement on WCR.

Strengths
- Clear, focused subtask: Framing main-concept linking within a document as a classification problem is well motivated for Wikipedia and practically useful (e.g., for downstream IE on Wikipedia).
- Sensible use of Wikipedia-specific signals: The use of links, redirects, and first-sentence type induction is natural and strong for this domain; the ablation for pronouns shows consistent gains from position/tag/distance features.
- Empirical indication of genre mismatch: The table contrasting multiple state-of-the-art CR systems on WCR vs OntoNotes convincingly supports the claim that newswire-trained systems struggle on Wikipedia, justifying genre-specific approaches.
- Simple integration into a CR pipeline: The Dcoref++ experiment is straightforward and shows measurable overall coreference gains, suggesting the MC sieve has useful precision.

Weaknesses
- Dataset scale and documentation are insufficient. WCR has only 30 articles and ~60k tokens. There is no inter-annotator agreement, no annotation protocol details, no topic breakdown beyond a brief statement, and no release plan beyond placeholders (anonymized links, “www.somewhere.country”). For a new task/dataset, this is a critical omission. With 30 documents, variance across folds could be high; results may not generalize.
- Cross-validation protocol and potential leakage are not clearly specified. It is vital to state whether 10-fold CV is by document (folds contain disjoint articles) or by mention. Because features exploit article-level signals (title, redirects, paragraph/section positions), mention-level CV would leak article-specific patterns from train to test. For Dcoref++, you say predictions come from 10-fold CV, but the interaction between the sieve and fold training is unclear. Without document-level CV guarantees, reported gains may be inflated.
- Labeling and use of automatically added mentions are unclear. You augment the training pool with ~13.4k automatically extracted singleton mentions, but WCR “does not contain singletons.” How are labels for these added mentions obtained? If labels are heuristic or derived from links, this introduces noise/confirmation bias that is not quantified. If gold labels were created, that contradicts the earlier guideline statement. This is a major methodological gap.
- Class weighting appears incorrect. For non-pronominal mentions, positives are only ~16%. You state you “penalized the negative class with a weight of 2.0.” With an imbalanced dataset (more negatives), standard practice is to upweight the minority (positive) class, not the majority. Penalizing negatives more heavily biases the model toward positive predictions and can artificially inflate recall/overall F1 for this specific task. This choice needs justification and sensitivity analysis; as written it reads as a mistake or at best ad hoc tuning.
- Baselines and comparisons are limited and not entirely fair. You compare with heuristics built on Dcoref/Scoref chains (B1–B4), but do not include a strong non-learning heuristic that exploits the same markup used by your model (e.g., title/redirect/name-variant matching with POS/type filters and paragraph/section positions). Your ablation suggests title and variants dominate; a rule-based system using those alone might be competitive. You also do not adapt or train any statistical CR system on WCR (even partially), nor compare to a simple logistic regression over your features.
- Incomplete reporting and missing elements. Several referenced Figures/Tables are absent or underspecified (Figure 1/2 not shown; Table 3 is mentioned but not present; Figure 3 is partial). Important feature engineering details are missing: how Wu & Palmer similarity is computed for proper names and multiword titles; what “compatibility” means in the pronoun distance features; which POS tagset and mapping yield the “34×4” and “2380” binary features; how entity types from Freebase are normalized; how name-variant noise is mitigated. Hyperparameter selection is vaguely described (“selected the C-SVC with linear kernel”).
- External knowledge dependency and longevity. Heavy reliance on Freebase (now deprecated) threatens reproducibility and long-term utility. Alternatives (Wikidata) and a discussion of portability beyond Wikipedia are not provided.
- Evaluation scope and robustness. The MCR evaluation reports micro-averaged precision/recall across mentions, but no per-article variance or error bars; with only 30 documents this is important. For CR, only Dcoref is augmented; analogous integration into Scoref is not attempted. The Dcoref++ sieve “prevents other sieves from appending new mentions to the MC chain,” which could suppress recall on difficult coreference cases; the trade-off is insufficiently analyzed.

Questions to the authors
- CV splits: Are folds by document (i.e., each article appears in exactly one fold), both for the MCR classifier and for Dcoref++ integration? If not, please rerun with document-level CV and report results.
- Singleton labeling: How did you obtain gold labels for the 13.4k automatically extracted singletons? If heuristically labeled, what is the estimated noise rate? If annotated, please provide IAA and protocol.
- Class weighting: Why penalize the negative class in SVM training given class imbalance in the opposite direction? Please provide a comparison with standard class weights (e.g., inverse frequency) and show performance sensitivity.
- Feature details: How is Wu–Palmer similarity computed for multiword titles and named entities? What constitutes “compatibility” in the pronoun distance features? Which POS tagset and exact binary feature construction yield the reported dimensions?
- Baselines: Can you provide a markup-aware heuristic baseline using only title/redirect/name variants (and possibly inferred type), without using Dcoref/Scoref chains, and report its performance? Also, can you integrate the MC sieve into Scoref for parity?
- Dcoref++ protocol: When you say “we use predicted results from 10-fold cross-validation,” do you retrain the MC classifier per fold and run Dcoref++ on held-out documents only, with no overlap in training data? Please clarify.

Given the number of methodological and reporting issues, especially around dataset construction, CV leakage risk, class weighting, and incomplete baselines, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form despite the promising idea and initial gains.
