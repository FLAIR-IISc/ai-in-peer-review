Summary
This paper targets “Main Concept (MC) resolution” in Wikipedia articles: identifying all mentions (named, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the article’s subject. The authors frame the problem as binary mention classification, engineer features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, train separate SVMs for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions, and report strong gains over baselines built from state-of-the-art coreference systems (Dcoref, Scoref). They also integrate their MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref, improving CoNLL F1 on their Wikipedia dataset (WCR).

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A focused formulation and empirical study of MC resolution in Wikipedia, with a supervised classifier that leverages article structure and external knowledge to substantially outperform repurposed generic CR systems on this task.
2) An integration of MC resolution into a rule-based CR pipeline (Dcoref), demonstrating measurable gains in overall CoNLL F1 on Wikipedia data.
3) A small Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR) and an evaluation testbed for MC resolution (though details and release are not fully available in the anonymized submission).

Strengths
- Clear task focus with a practical payoff: MC resolution is directly useful for downstream tasks on Wikipedia (e.g., OIE) where conflating MC mentions harms utility. Framing it as mention-level classification is sensible and yields interpretable improvements over general CR pipelines.
- Effective use of Wikipedia structure and external knowledge: The feature design (title, redirects, name variants, inferred types, Freebase attributes) is well-aligned with the domain. Ablations show these sources are additive, especially for pronominals where position and local compatibility features help.
- Strong empirical improvements on the target task: On MC identification with 10-fold CV, the approach improves F1 by ~13–15 points over baselines derived from Dcoref/Scoref, and notably outperforms nontrivial heuristics (e.g., chain aggregation plus pronoun boosting).
- Sensible system integration and end-to-end gains: Incorporating the classifier as the first sieve in Dcoref produces a +4 CoNLL F1 improvement on WCR. This suggests MC resolution can guide downstream sieves and improve precision without major pipeline changes.
- Diagnostic analysis: The paper includes ablations (especially for pronouns) and illustrative error cases that highlight where features help or fail (e.g., inferred type aiding “the team” -> Houston Texans; difficulties with ambiguous place/team mentions).

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and representativeness: WCR contains only 30 Wikipedia documents (≈60k tokens) and 1.8k MC mentions. While the topic mix is said to vary, the small scale raises concerns about robustness, variance in 10-fold CV, and overfitting to idiosyncrasies of Wikipedia markup. No significance tests are reported for the observed gains.
- Baselines may be too weak for the new task: All baselines are derived from generic CR outputs with heuristics (longest chain, title-based aggregation, pronoun spreading). There is no supervised mention-level baseline using simple lexical/positional features, nor comparison to strong entity-linking–assisted baselines beyond the authors’ features. This makes it hard to assess how much of the gain stems from supervision versus domain-specific signals.
- Limited evaluation scope: Improvements are shown only on WCR. The Dcoref++ sieve is not evaluated on OntoNotes or broader Wikipedia to assess generalization or potential adverse effects in non-MC chains. Also, per-topic breakdowns (people, orgs, events) and coverage analyses (e.g., Freebase missingness) are absent.
- Reproducibility and anonymity gaps: The dataset and code are not available during review (“will be described in the non-anonymized version” and “www.somewhere.country”). Critical implementation details (e.g., exact method for “inferred type,” definition of “compatible” entities for pronoun distance features, cross-validation split protocol by document) are not fully specified.
- Dependence on Freebase: The approach depends on Freebase attributes and notable types, with limited discussion of fallback behavior/coverage and how noise in redirects or missing entries affects performance. Given ecosystem shifts, a discussion of porting to Wikidata or alternatives would strengthen the contribution.

Questions to Authors
1) Cross-validation protocol: Are folds split strictly by document? Please clarify to rule out any within-document leakage of article-specific features.
2) Inferred type extraction: How exactly is the noun type induced from the first sentence? What precision/recall does this heuristic achieve, and how often is it absent?
3) Freebase coverage and noise: What fraction of mentions/MCs receive Freebase attributes? How do results change when Freebase info is missing or incorrect? Any results using only Wikipedia-internal signals?
4) Baselines: Can you add a supervised mention-level baseline (e.g., linear model with string match, head match, frequency, position, POS) without Wikipedia/Freebase features to quantify the incremental value of external knowledge?
5) Generalization: Do the MC classifier and Dcoref++ improvements hold across different Wikipedia topics (PER/ORG/LOC/EVENT) and longer articles with dense links? A per-topic breakdown would be helpful.
6) Broader CR impact: Does constraining the MC chain early reduce recall on non-MC chains? Any error analysis of non-MC chains in Dcoref++?
7) Resource release: Can you provide (even anonymized) artifacts during review? At minimum, detailed schema, feature extraction scripts, and fold assignments.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper addresses a useful, under-explored subtask with a well-motivated feature set and shows compelling gains on that task and in a downstream sieve. However, the dataset is small, baselines are limited for the supervised setting, and evaluation is confined to a non-public Wikipedia set without significance testing or broader generalization. Strengthening the baselines, expanding and releasing the dataset, and providing more comprehensive evaluation would make this a solid contribution.
