Summary and main contributions
- Task and testbed: The paper revisits “main concept (MC) resolution” in Wikipedia—identifying all mentions that refer to the article’s subject—and frames it as a binary mention-level classification task. It provides a small but reasonably diverse annotated Wikipedia dataset (WCR) and shows that state-of-the-art news-trained CR systems underperform on it.
- Method and features: Two linear SVMs (separate for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) leveraging Wikipedia-native signals (title, redirects, internal links) and Freebase attributes, plus simple document/positional and POS-based features, outperform adapted CR-based baselines by a large margin on MC resolution.
- Impact on full CR: Integrating the MC classifier as a high-precision first sieve into Dcoref yields a +4 CoNLL F1 improvement on WCR, indicating downstream benefits for coreference in Wikipedia.

Strengths
- Well-motivated, practically useful task with clear scope: Identifying MC mentions is highly relevant for Wikipedia-centric IE and downstream tasks (e.g., OIE, relation extraction), and isolates a core subproblem where Wikipedia’s structure can be exploited. The paper demonstrates that newswire-trained CR models degrade on Wikipedia (Table 1), reinforcing the need for genre-specific methods.
- Strong empirical gains with simple, interpretable modeling: The MC classifiers achieve ~89 F1 on the classification task, improving by 13–15 F1 over adapted baselines (Table 4). The approach is lightweight (linear SVMs), transparent, and deployable without heavy EL pipelines.
- Effective exploitation of Wikipedia markup: Using human-curated internal links, redirects, and title/type cues reduces ambiguity and avoids low-recall named-entity linking. The feature ablations for pronouns and non-pronominals clearly justify how each Wikipedia-derived feature family contributes (Tables 2–3).
- Demonstrated downstream benefit to full CR: Incorporating the classifier as an initial sieve in Dcoref improves CoNLL F1 by +4 on WCR (Table 5). This establishes that solving MC resolution early can help broader CR, not just a niche subtask.
- Analysis and baselines: The paper designs multiple chain-selection baselines from two strong CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref), includes pronoun propagation heuristics, and provides qualitative error examples illustrating both successes and failure modes.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset size and external validity: WCR contains only 30 documents (~60k tokens). While the authors sampled for diversity, this is a very small basis to claim robustness. It is unclear how results generalize across broader Wikipedia topics, article lengths, and writing styles (e.g., stubs, lists), or to other languages. At minimum, held-out document-level splits and variance across folds should be reported.
- Potential evaluation leakage and split clarity: The 10-fold cross-validation is over mentions, but it is not stated whether folds are split by document. Because features include document-level statistics (mention frequency) and article-specific attributes (title, redirects, internal-link-derived attributes), splitting by mentions could leak article-specific signals into both train and test, inflating performance. Explicitly confirm document-level CV and provide per-fold variance.
- Baseline adequacy and fairness: While adapting Dcoref/Scoref chains is reasonable, stronger direct heuristics could be competitive (e.g., purely string-based matching to title/redirects/types without invoking a CR system, or a simple learning baseline over these features). It would help to include such non-CR baselines. Also, Scoref baselines perform quite poorly on WCR; including a tuned/simple supervised baseline using only title/name-variant signals would strengthen the case for the classifier.
- Dependence on Freebase and resource availability: Several features use Freebase attributes; Freebase is deprecated, and availability/replication may be problematic. The paper promises to release data and outputs but provides “anonymized” placeholders; at submission time, reproducibility is unclear. An ablation quantifying performance without Freebase (Wikipedia-only) would be valuable and more future-proof.
- Novelty is incremental: The task has been explored (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008), and the method is largely feature engineering plus linear SVMs. The main novelty lies in assembling Wikipedia/Freebase features and demonstrating improvements on a new testbed. This is solid but not conceptually groundbreaking.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Are folds split by document (not by mention)? Please confirm and report mean ± stdev across folds.
- Resource release: Will WCR (documents, annotations, guidelines), feature extraction code, and trained models be released at camera-ready? Given Freebase’s status, will you provide a Wikipedia-only feature configuration and its performance?
- Baselines: How does a simple heuristic that tags mentions as MC if they match title/redirects/name variants (with basic head/prenorm matching) perform? Likewise, what is the performance of a logistic regression or linear SVM using only these string-match features?
- Generalization: Do results hold for different Wikipedia article types (e.g., organizations vs people vs locations) and lengths? Can you provide per-topic breakdowns or learning curves? Any results on another held-out Wikipedia slice?
- Integration details: In Dcoref++, preventing later sieves from appending mentions to the MC chain increases precision more than recall. What is the recall delta on MC mentions and the impact on non-MC chains? Could a softer constraint (allowing later merges with confidence) improve recall?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses a clearly useful and underexplored subtask, shows substantive gains using simple, well-justified features that exploit Wikipedia structure, and demonstrates downstream CR benefits. The main concerns are dataset size, clarity of the evaluation split, and reliance on Freebase/resources not yet released. If the authors can confirm document-level splits, release the resources (or provide Wikipedia-only alternatives), and strengthen baselines with a simple string-match learner, I would lean toward acceptance. As it stands, this is a solid, practically impactful contribution with modest methodological novelty.
