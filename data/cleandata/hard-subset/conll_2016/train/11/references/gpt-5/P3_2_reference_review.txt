Summary and main contributions
- The paper targets “main concept resolution” (MC): given a Wikipedia article, identify all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the article’s subject. The authors cast this as binary mention classification and engineer features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, training two linear SVMs (pronouns vs. non-pronouns).
- They introduce a small Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR; 30 documents) annotated with OntoNotes-style mentions and links to Freebase, and show that state-of-the-art CR systems trained for news perform poorly on this genre.
- They show that their MC classifier outperforms several baselines derived from Dcoref and Scoref, and that inserting a high-precision MC sieve into Dcoref improves overall coreference on WCR.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation with practical utility. MC resolution is a well-motivated subtask for Wikipedia-centric IE and OIE, where collapsing mentions of the page subject is highly valuable. Framing it explicitly as a mention-level classification task is clean and reusable.
- Effective use of Wikipedia structure without full-blown entity linking. Mining redirects, aliases, and in-article hyperlinks to populate types, gender, number, and variant names offers high-precision knowledge that avoids cascading NEL errors. The “inferred type from first sentence” is a particularly pragmatic and useful signal for nominal mentions.
- Solid empirical gains on the target task. On WCR, the proposed classifier achieves around 89 F1 on MC mention identification, substantially outperforming baselines built on two strong CR systems. The feature ablation shows consistent, interpretable improvements: title and inferred type for nominals; coarse attributes, position, and local compatibility for pronouns.
- Demonstrated impact on full CR within Wikipedia. Integrating the MC classifier as an early sieve (Dcoref++) yields a notable CoNLL F1 gain (+3–4 points) over the strong Dcoref baseline on WCR, suggesting that early, high-precision consolidation of the page subject benefits downstream sieves.
- Resource contribution and transparency. WCR is a welcome step toward genre diversification for CR, and the promise to release the dataset and the MC mention dump will enable reproducibility and future work.

Weaknesses
- Dataset scale and robustness. WCR has only 30 documents (~60k tokens). Ten-fold cross-validation on such a small set risks high variance and topic leakage across folds. The large reported gains may not generalize across the breadth of Wikipedia. Stronger evidence would include a larger, more diverse test set and/or leave-one-article-out evaluation.
- Baseline strength. The baselines depend on the clustering quality of off-the-shelf CR systems and simple chain aggregation heuristics, which may underutilize Wikipedia-specific cues. Missing is a direct, mention-level deterministic baseline that uses exactly the same sources as your model (e.g., label positive if the mention matches title/redirects/aliases/inferred type and is attribute-compatible), akin to the rule-based approaches of Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008). Such a baseline might substantially narrow the gap.
- Potential feature leakage and bias from Wikipedia links. The approach enriches candidate mentions by matching them to anchor strings of in-article hyperlinks and importing Freebase types. While this is realistic at inference time, hyperlinks often correlate with “being another entity, not the MC.” Even if a “linked/unlinked” flag is not used directly, the presence of rich types for non-MC mentions can implicitly encode that distinction. An explicit analysis quantifying how much performance depends on link-derived features (vs. title/alias/type-only) would address this concern.
- Limited evaluation of Dcoref++ and no significance testing. The improved CoNLL F1 on WCR is encouraging, but it is unclear whether: (i) the gains are statistically significant; (ii) the sieve harms performance on non-Wikipedia genres (e.g., OntoNotes news); and (iii) precision/recall trade-offs are stable across topics. Reporting significance tests and out-of-domain checks would strengthen the claim.
- Dependency on Freebase and missing implementation details. Freebase is deprecated; mapping to Wikidata or DBpedia would improve longevity. Some important details are underspecified: how the “inferred type” is extracted (pattern, heuristics, error rate), how Wu & Palmer similarity is computed (sense inventory/disambiguation), the exact pronoun-compatibility test using Freebase, and hyperparameter selection beyond class weighting. Clarifying these would aid reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Can you report a rule-based mention-level baseline using the same signals available to your model (title/aliases/redirects/inferred type/coarse attributes), without relying on external CR clusters? How close does it get?
- How exactly do you infer the MC type from the first sentence? Please provide patterns, coverage, and accuracy estimates, and how you handle appositives or atypical leads.
- What is the contribution of link-derived features? Please ablate features that depend on in-article hyperlinks (including any attributes imported via matched anchors) to quantify their effect relative to title/aliases/type alone.
- Did you evaluate Dcoref++ on OntoNotes or other non-Wikipedia genres to assess whether introducing the MC sieve harms general-domain CR? If not, can you at least report on WCR-style vs. non-WCR within OntoNotes (e.g., encyclopedic or web text portions)?
- Are the improvements statistically significant across folds for the MC classifier and for Dcoref++? Please include appropriate tests (e.g., stratified bootstrap over documents).
- How are hyperparameters chosen (C, class weights)? Was any tuning performed outside the CV loop to avoid optimistic estimates?

Additional comments
- Consider replacing Freebase with Wikidata and documenting the mapping; this will increase impact and longevity.
- Provide per-genre/topic breakdown within WCR (people, organizations, locations, events) and per-mention-type breakdown (named, nominal, pronoun), to assess where the method shines or struggles.
- A discussion of failure cases involving metonymy (e.g., team vs. city) and how to mitigate them (contextual cues, section headers) would be valuable.
- Minor: clarify whether gold mention boundaries are ever used at training/inference; the text suggests union of gold and predicted, but evaluation is on predicted mentions only. Please be explicit.
