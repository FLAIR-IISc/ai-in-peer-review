Summary
This paper targets a focused variant of coreference in Wikipedia: identifying all mentions in an article that refer to the article’s main concept (MC). The authors frame this as a binary mention classification task and train two SVMs (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase (title, redirects, link anchors, first-sentence type, coarse attributes), plus standard lexical/syntactic cues. They introduce WCR, a 30-article Wikipedia dataset annotated under OntoNotes guidelines, and show: (i) off-the-shelf CR systems degrade sharply on WCR; (ii) their MC classifier substantially outperforms baselines derived from Dcoref and Scoref; and (iii) integrating the classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref improves overall CR on WCR by ~4 CoNLL F1.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) An evaluation testbed for “main concept resolution” in Wikipedia (WCR), and an empirical study demonstrating that general-purpose CR systems underperform on this genre/task.
2) A simple, effective MC mention classifier leveraging Wikipedia/Freebase features that outperforms heuristics built on state-of-the-art CR outputs.
3) A practical integration of the MC classifier into a sieve-based CR pipeline (Dcoref), yielding measurable end-to-end gains on Wikipedia coreference.

Strengths
- Clear, well-scoped problem formulation with practical relevance: The MC resolution task is both useful (e.g., for Wikipedia-based IE/OIE) and cleanly defined for encyclopedic articles. Establishing it as a mention-level binary classification problem is sensible and reproducible.
- Strong empirical signal that CR trained on news does not transfer to Wikipedia: The side-by-side comparison across five CR systems on WCR vs OntoNotes is informative, shows large degradation, and motivates Wikipedia-specific modeling.
- Effective exploitation of Wikipedia structure: The feature design makes judicious, high-precision use of internal links, redirects, and first-sentence typing, avoiding full-blown entity linking while still capturing salient attributes. The ablation shows these sources drive performance, especially for pronouns (position/distance) and nominal mentions (title/type/variants).
- End-to-end impact via sieve integration: Embedding the MC classifier as an early sieve in Dcoref leads to consistent gains across MUC, B3, and CEAF on WCR. This demonstrates that targeted, high-precision early decisions can help rule-based pipelines beyond the MC chain itself.
- Reasonable experimental hygiene for the main task: The baselines are derived from two strong CR systems, and the analysis includes per-mention-type results and a feature ablation sequence that clarifies where improvements come from.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset scale and lack of anonymized release details: WCR contains only 30 documents (~60k tokens). This raises concerns about robustness, topic diversity, and statistical significance. The paper defers dataset details and release info to a “non-anonymized” version and a placeholder URL, which complicates reproducibility under double-blind review.
- Potential overreliance on Wikipedia markup and knowledge bases: Many features derive from redirects, link anchors, and Freebase types. This risks trivializing the task in settings where links are sparse or unevenly distributed (Wikipedia often links only the first mention). It also limits portability to non-Wikipedia text or to articles with atypical markup. An explicit analysis split by link density and by availability of Freebase attributes is missing.
- Baseline strength and fairness: The baselines B1–B4 are heuristic chain-selection/aggregation strategies grafted onto CR outputs; they do not include a learned mention-level classifier using simple lexical/contextual features without knowledge bases. A stronger non-KB baseline (e.g., shallow lexical + POS + position window) would help calibrate the added value of Wikipedia/Freebase signals.
- Cross-validation setup and potential leakage: It is unclear whether folds are defined at the document level. If mentions from the same article appear in both train and test folds, the model may benefit from article-specific regularities (e.g., MC title/type) learned during training. A leave-one-document-out (or leave-article-out) evaluation would better reflect generalization to unseen articles.
- End-to-end CR evaluation scope: While Dcoref++ gains are encouraging, the MC chain constitutes a large portion of mentions (25%). It’s unclear whether improvements arise mostly from consolidating the MC chain versus improving non-MC chains. A breakdown by chain type/size and an error analysis of non-MC chains would strengthen the claim.

Questions to Authors
- Evaluation protocol: Are CV folds defined by document/article, or by mention? Please report results with leave-one-article-out splits to rule out intra-article leakage.
- Dependence on markup: How does performance vary with link density? What fraction of positive mentions are linked versus unlinked? Can you provide results when suppressing internal links (or using only first-mention links)?
- Knowledge base reliance: What coverage do Freebase attributes have on WCR? How do results change when Freebase-derived features are ablated, or replaced with Wikidata?
- Baselines: Can you include a learned mention-level classifier without Wikipedia/Freebase features (lexical n-grams around the mention, POS, sentence/section position), and a heuristic “match MC title or redirect variants” baseline?
- Integration effects: In Dcoref++, how do metrics change when excluding the MC cluster from scoring, or when reporting performance specifically on non-MC chains? Does preventing later sieves from appending to the MC chain ever harm recall on difficult MC mentions?
- Generalization: Have you tested across broader topics or more articles? Any indication the approach scales to thousands of pages, or to other languages where markup conventions differ?

Overall assessment
This is a well-written, practically motivated paper with a neat problem formulation, thoughtful feature design, and clear empirical gains on the proposed task and in a sieve-based CR pipeline. However, the dataset is quite small, the reliance on Wikipedia-specific markup/KBs limits generality, and the evaluation protocol and baselines need strengthening to establish robustness. I lean toward rejection in its current form for a major conference, but the work is promising. With a larger, properly anonymized/released dataset, document-level evaluation splits, stronger learned baselines, and analyses of link-density/KB coverage and non-MC chain impact, this could be a solid contribution.
