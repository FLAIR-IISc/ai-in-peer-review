Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper defines “Main Concept (MC) resolution” for Wikipedia articles: identifying all mentions in an article that refer to the article’s subject. The authors formulate it as a binary classification problem (per mention), engineer features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, and train separate SVMs for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. On a small Wikipedia dataset (WCR), they report clear gains over heuristics derived from state-of-the-art CR systems (Dcoref, Scoref).
- They integrate the MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref (Dcoref++), obtaining a +4 CoNLL F1 improvement on their WCR data.
- They claim to release a new Wikipedia-CR dataset and a large dump of MC mentions for English Wikipedia.

Strengths
- Clearly scoped and practically motivated task. Focusing on the article’s main concept is useful for Wikipedia-centric IE/OIE pipelines and avoids full CR complexity while addressing a frequent source of extraction noise.
- Sensible use of Wikipedia structure. Leveraging human-curated links and redirects to avoid explicit entity linking ambiguity is reasonable and often more robust on Wikipedia than generic EL pipelines. The feature design is transparent, and the ablation on pronouns shows consistent, interpretable gains (base → +main → +position → +tag → +distance).
- Stronger baselines than strawmen. The authors adapt Dcoref and Scoref outputs to the MC task with several heuristics (including chain aggregation and a pronoun-boosting trick). This is a reasonable way to repurpose established CR systems to the new task.
- Positive downstream effect within a CR pipeline. Integrating the MC classifier as an early sieve yields measurable improvements on WCR (+4 CoNLL F1), suggesting that high-precision MC decisions can help subsequent sieves.
- Empirical observation that state-of-the-art CR systems underperform on Wikipedia compared to OntoNotes is valuable, reinforcing genre-specific challenges.

Weaknesses
- Very small, unreleased dataset at review time. WCR contains only 30 documents (60k tokens), with 1,800 MC mentions. This size raises concerns about statistical reliability, overfitting to idiosyncrasies of a few pages, and the robustness of the reported +13–15 F1 gains. The paper provides no significance testing or variance estimates across folds. Since the dataset is anonymized/unavailable during review, reproducibility and external validation are limited.
- Potential evaluation leakage. The 10-fold cross-validation protocol is not clearly specified. If folds split at the mention level rather than at the document (article) level, training and test could share the same article’s content and MC attributes, inflating results. Given that features directly encode the article’s MC title, variants, Freebase attributes, and section positions, document-level folds are essential. This is a critical methodological point that must be clarified.
- Baselines and comparisons are incomplete. While the Dcoref/Scoref-derived heuristics are reasonable, the paper does not (i) train or adapt statistical CR baselines on WCR (even simple domain adaptation), (ii) compare against a strong, simple MC-specific classifier that uses only Wikipedia title/redirect/anchor features without Freebase, or (iii) provide significance tests. The ablation for non-pronominal mentions is discussed but not fully tabulated; more systematic analysis would strengthen claims.
- Heavy reliance on external knowledge specific to Wikipedia/Freebase. The approach depends on internal links and Freebase-derived attributes; performance on pages with sparse linking or missing KB entries is unclear. Freebase is also deprecated; no alternative (e.g., Wikidata/DBpedia) is evaluated. The portability beyond Wikipedia (or even to under-linked Wikipedia pages) is not addressed; yet the paper’s broader CR claims would require such evidence.
- Limited general CR validation. Dcoref++ is only evaluated on WCR, where MC structure is salient. There is no assessment on OntoNotes (or other genres) to demonstrate that the added sieve does not hurt general performance, nor any analysis of failure modes when MC is less central. The claim that this improves “coreference resolution” would be more convincing with evidence that it generalizes or at least does not degrade on standard benchmarks.
- Dataset construction details are sparse. The selection criteria, annotation process, inter-annotator agreement, and how singleton augmentation interacts with gold spans are under-specified. This limits interpretability of results, especially given the task’s skew (30 MC chains out of 1,469).

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Were folds created at the document (article) level? Please confirm and report mean±std across folds. If not, can you re-run with document-level folds? Also, were hyperparameters tuned on held-out data that does not overlap with test folds?
- Dataset details: Please provide selection criteria, annotation procedure, inter-annotator agreement (for mention spans and MC labels), and alignment between gold and predicted mentions. How many articles have sparse/no internal links? Can you report results by link density and by article type (PER/ORG/LOC/etc.)?
- Baseline parity: Are all systems (baselines and your classifier) evaluated on exactly the same candidate mention set produced by the same detector? If pronoun-boosting is allowed for baselines, can you quantify its effect separately?
- Ablations: Please provide a complete ablation for non-pronominal mentions, including variants without Freebase (Wikipedia-only) and without KB features (string-only). How much do gains persist with Wikidata instead of Freebase?
- Generalization: Does adding the MC sieve to Dcoref affect performance on OntoNotes (CoNLL-2012 test)? At least report “no harm” results. Also, how does the classifier perform on low-link-density articles?
- Significance: Are the improvements over the best baseline statistically significant for both pronouns and non-pronouns? Please include tests (e.g., randomization/bootstrap at document level).
