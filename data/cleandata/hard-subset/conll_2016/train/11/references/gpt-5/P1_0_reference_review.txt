Summary and main contributions
The paper studies main concept (MC) resolution in Wikipedia: given an article, identify all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the article’s main concept. The authors:
1) Propose a simple, feature-based approach (two linear SVMs for pronominal vs non-pronominal mentions) leveraging Wikipedia markup (titles, redirects, internal links) and Freebase attributes, plus POS/position/distance features, to classify whether a mention refers to the MC.
2) Construct a Wikipedia coreference resource (WCR) of 30 articles with OntoNotes-style coref, and show that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on OntoNotes degrade substantially on this genre. They derive baselines from Dcoref and Scoref by heuristically extracting/aggregating the MC chain(s).
3) Demonstrate that their MC classifier strongly outperforms baselines (F1 ≈ 89 vs 73–76), and that integrating the MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref yields a 4-point CoNLL F1 gain on WCR for full coreference.

Strengths
- Clear task formulation with practical value. MC resolution is a well-scoped subtask highly relevant for Wikipedia and downstream IE/OIE, where pruning MC mentions improves tuple utility. Framing it as binary classification is intuitive and effective.

- Strong empirical gains and informative analysis. The method delivers large, consistent improvements over multiple baselines for both pronominal and non-pronominal cases (up to +13–15 F1). Feature ablations are thoughtful and reveal which information sources matter (e.g., title/variants and coarse attributes; position and distance for pronouns).

- Simple, reproducible modeling with smart use of structure. The approach uses linear SVMs and lightweight features, avoiding heavy pipelines or entity linking. Leveraging human-annotated Wikipedia markup for supervision signals is a pragmatic, robust idea likely to transfer across articles and potentially languages.

- Impact on full CR. Integrating the MC classifier as a sieve in Dcoref improves CoNLL F1 by ~4 on WCR, indicating that early, high-precision MC decisions can help broader clustering—not only the MC chain.

- Dataset and benchmarking perspective. The paper documents a realistic domain shift: top systems trained on OntoNotes drop markedly on WCR, underscoring the need for domain-adapted CR and the utility of Wikipedia-specific features.

Weaknesses
- Small, not clearly available dataset and possible evaluation leakage. WCR has only 30 documents; the split protocol for 10-fold cross-validation is unspecified. If folds are over mentions rather than documents, features derived from article-level information (title/variants) risk leakage and inflated scores. The paper also refers to the dataset being described in the “nonanonymized version” and to a placeholder URL; reproducibility is unclear at submission time.

- Baseline strength and fairness. Baselines are derived from general CR systems plus simple chain heuristics. However, there is no supervised MC-resolution baseline (e.g., logistic regression/SVM using only title/variant/string match and frequency features) or a rule-based baseline that uses the same Wikipedia markup and redirects the authors exploit. This makes it hard to gauge how much of the gain is due to learning vs access to strong, task-specific signals.

- Dependence on Freebase and internal links. The approach assumes access to Freebase (deprecated) and internal links; coverage and robustness when Freebase is unavailable or for low-link-density articles is not evaluated. The paper shows benefits from “inferred type” from the first sentence but does not quantify performance with Wikipedia-only vs combined sources.

- Labeling of automatically added mentions. The training set includes ~13k automatically extracted singletons added to 7k annotated mentions, but the paper does not explain how these singletons were labeled for MC vs non-MC. If treated as negative by default, this could introduce substantial label noise and bias.

- Limited scope of full-CR evaluation and ablation. The Dcoref++ sieve is evaluated only on WCR; effects on standard OntoNotes are unknown. The sieve forbids later sieves from appending mentions to the MC chain—this could reduce recall elsewhere. No ablation is provided to quantify the specific contribution of the sieve vs other Dcoref components, and no significance testing is reported.

Questions to Authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Were folds created at the document level (i.e., entire articles held out), or over mentions? Please clarify to rule out leakage from article-level features.

- Data availability: Will WCR (texts, annotations, derived features) and your code be released upon acceptance? Can you provide an anonymized link for the review phase?

- Labeling of added singletons: How were the 13,400 automatically extracted mentions labeled as MC/non-MC for training? If unmatched to gold, were they assumed negative? What fraction might be false negatives?

- Resource dependence: What is performance with Wikipedia-only features (titles/redirects/anchors/inferred type) vs Freebase-only vs combined? Given Freebase deprecation, can Wikidata be used, and how sensitive are results to KB coverage?

- Stronger baselines: Did you try a supervised baseline that uses only simple string/frequency features (e.g., title/variant exact/substring matches, head frequency) without POS/position/distance? What is its F1?

- Dcoref++ generalization: Does adding the MC sieve help or harm on OntoNotes or other Wikipedia subsets? Any evidence that forbidding later sieves to attach mentions to the MC chain reduces recall elsewhere?

- Inferred type extraction: How exactly do you infer the MC type from the first sentence? How do you handle appositives, multiple types, or noisy lead sentences?

Additional comments
- Consider reporting confidence intervals or significance tests, given the small number of documents.
- Provide error breakdowns by entity type (PER/ORG/LOC/MISC) and by link density to better characterize robustness.
- Clarify the “position tier” feature definition and the “distance” feature computation and thresholds.

Overall, the paper addresses a valuable, underexplored task with a simple and effective method and promising improvements. Clarifying the evaluation protocol, strengthening baselines, and ensuring data/code availability would substantially improve the submission.
