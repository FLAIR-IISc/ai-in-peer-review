Summary and claimed contributions
This paper targets a Wikipedia-specific subtask of coreference: Main Concept (MC) Resolution, i.e., identifying all mentions in an article that refer to the article’s subject. The authors (i) introduce a dedicated evaluation testbed (WCR) with manually annotated Wikipedia articles, (ii) present a supervised approach using two linear SVMs (separate models for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) with features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase (title, name variants, inferred type, entity type, coarse attributes, positional and POS features), and (iii) show that integrating the MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into a deterministic CR system (Dcoref) improves end-to-end coreference on Wikipedia.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A task definition and evaluation testbed for MC resolution in Wikipedia, including a comparative evaluation showing that newswire-trained CR systems underperform on Wikipedia and that MC-focused modeling helps.  
2) A simple, effective supervised model leveraging Wikipedia/Freebase features that substantially outperforms baselines for MC resolution.  
3) A practical integration into an existing sieve-based CR system (Dcoref) that improves CoNLL F1 on WCR by 4 points.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical gains on the target task with clear ablations. The proposed classifier achieves 88.79 F1 on identifying MC mentions, an absolute gain of 13–15 F1 over baselines (Table 4). The feature ablation for pronouns (Table 2) is informative: base → +main → +position → +tag → +distance shows monotonic improvements, highlighting the value of Wikipedia-specific and positional cues. For non-pronominals, features tied to article title, inferred type, name variants, and entity type each contribute.

2) Wikipedia-aware design avoids noisy entity linking and leverages human-curated signals. Using internal links, redirects, and first-sentence patterns for types circumvents common precision-recall tradeoffs in entity linking pipelines. This design choice is well-motivated for Wikipedia and substantiated by the performance deltas versus CR-derived baselines.

3) Demonstrated practical impact on full CR via sieve integration. The new first sieve that merges MC mentions improves Dcoref’s CoNLL score from 51.77 to 55.11 on WCR (Table 5), with consistent gains across MUC, B3, and CEAF. This indicates the MC chain can anchor subsequent sieves’ decisions and reduce fragmentation.

4) Clear evidence of domain mismatch and value of domain-specific modeling. The comparison of state-of-the-art CR systems trained on OntoNotes to their performance on WCR (Table 1) quantifies a notable drop, motivating the specialized MC module and dataset.

5) Reproducibility intent. The paper states plans to release the dataset, features, and a Wikipedia-wide MC mention dump, which would enable benchmarking and follow-on work.

Weaknesses
1) Dataset size and split methodology risk overestimating performance. WCR consists of only 30 Wikipedia articles (~60k tokens, ~7k non-singleton mentions). The paper reports 10-fold cross-validation but does not specify whether folds are by document. If folds split mentions within the same article, strong article-specific features (e.g., title, name variants) can leak across folds, inflating results. A document-level CV or leave-one-article-out setup is needed to ensure proper generalization.

2) Limited novelty in modeling; main contribution is the task/testbed. The approach uses conventional linear SVMs with largely standard string-matching, POS, positional, and KB-derived attribute features. Similar signals (title/aliases/types) have been used in prior CR work. The novelty resides primarily in task focus and Wikipedia-specific engineering rather than algorithmic advances.

3) Heavy reliance on Freebase and limited analysis of its contribution. Several features rely on Freebase (entity types, gender, number, notable type, aliases). Freebase is no longer maintained; portability to current KBs (e.g., Wikidata) is not explored. An explicit ablation contrasting “Wikipedia-only” vs “Wikipedia+Freebase” features, and robustness when KB attributes are missing or noisy, is not reported.

4) Baselines and comparative scope. The baselines depend on two CR systems’ chains with heuristics (B1–B4) and a pronoun recall heuristic. While reasonable, a stronger baseline could be a learning-based model using only string/positional features (no Freebase), or a rule-based system directly matching title/aliases/types without CR pipelines. Also, the Dcoref++ integration is evaluated only for Dcoref; assessing integration with a statistical CR system (e.g., Scoref) would strengthen generality claims.

5) Incomplete dataset and annotation details. The paper states WCR details are to appear in a non-anonymized version. Inter-annotator agreement, annotation protocol, topic distribution, and how Freebase links were verified are not reported. These are important for assessing data quality and the reliability of the reported results.

Questions to authors
1) How were CV folds constructed? Were folds by document or by mention? If not by document, can you report document-level CV or leave-one-article-out results to rule out leakage from article-specific features?

2) What is the inter-annotator agreement for MC and coreference annotations in WCR? Please provide IAA metrics and annotation guidelines summary.

3) What is the contribution of Freebase vs. Wikipedia-only features? Can you add an ablation removing Freebase-derived attributes and aliases? How often are Freebase attributes available/missing in WCR?

4) How does performance vary by article type (PER/ORG/LOC/MISC), article length, and link density? Are gains consistent across categories?

5) Can the MC sieve be integrated into Scoref or other CR systems, and with what effect? Also, does adding the sieve affect performance on non-Wikipedia domains (e.g., OntoNotes)?

6) For reproducibility under anonymity, can you include more concrete dataset statistics (topic distribution, span length distributions) and precise feature definitions (e.g., thresholds for “partial match,” token overlap rules)?

Overall, the work is clearly presented, technically sound for the stated scope, and addresses a practically useful Wikipedia-specific task, with strong empirical gains on the proposed testbed. The primary concerns are potential data split leakage, small dataset size, and limited novelty beyond the task/testbed.
