Summary and claimed contributions:
- The paper defines “Main Concept Resolution” (MCR) for Wikipedia: deciding, for each detected mention, whether it refers to the article’s main concept (MC). The authors build mention-level SVM classifiers (separate for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, and show gains over baselines derived from state-of-the-art coreference systems. They also add a sieve using their classifier to Dcoref and report CoNLL F1 improvements on a small Wikipedia dataset (WCR).
- Contributions as I see them:
  1) A Wikipedia-specific mention-level classifier for MC detection that leverages internal links, redirects, and Freebase attributes, with reported improvements over heuristic baselines and two CR systems’ outputs.
  2) A small annotated Wikipedia dataset (WCR) and a testbed for the MCR task.
  3) An integration of the MCR classifier into Dcoref as a high-precision sieve, yielding modest CoNLL F1 gains on WCR.

Strengths:
- Targeted task definition with practical relevance: Identifying the main-concept chain in Wikipedia articles is a concrete, useful subproblem for IE/OIE and downstream tasks. Framing it as binary mention classification is clean.
- Effective exploitation of Wikipedia-specific signals: Using human-authored internal links, redirects, and title/type cues is sensible and avoids the brittleness of noisy entity linking. The feature design is straightforward and interpretable.
- Empirical gains on the defined task: On MCR, the classifier substantially outperforms the proposed baselines on F1 (up to ~13–15 points), for both pronominal and non-pronominal mentions, and the ablation for pronouns is informative.
- Integration into a standard CR pipeline: The sieve addition to Dcoref yields consistent improvements across MUC, B3, CEAFφ4, with ~4 CoNLL F1 gain on WCR, suggesting the main-concept decision can help early clustering.
- Clarity of the setup: The paper is generally well written, features are described, and the relationship to prior attempts at Wikipedia-aware CR is laid out.

Weaknesses:
- Very small dataset and limited validation: WCR contains only 30 documents (~60K tokens). Ten-fold CV on 30 docs (≈3 docs per fold) with hundreds of features risks high variance and overfitting; no statistical significance is reported. The generalization of the reported gains is unclear.
- Comparisons are not fully fair or comprehensive: The main MCR classifier is trained on WCR, whereas the baseline CR systems are off-the-shelf models trained on OntoNotes/news; this favors the proposed method. Missing are (i) a strong non-Wikipedia baseline trained on WCR with conventional CR features, and (ii) simple but competitive string/redirect/type-only heuristics (e.g., “mark as MC if string matches title/redirect or first-sentence type”) not mediated by third-party CR outputs.
- Potential leakage/unrealistic assumptions: The method uses within-article internal links and Freebase attributes to enrich mentions. While leveraging links is reasonable, it raises concerns about circularity (human links often disambiguate the very mentions to be decided) and about applicability to unlinked mentions or domains without such markup. Ablations that remove internal links and/or Freebase are needed to demonstrate robustness and isolate their contribution.
- Evaluation protocol clarity: It is not fully clear that all compared methods operate over the exact same mention set. Baselines are derived from Dcoref/Scoref chains, while the proposed classifier uses mentions from the same detection algorithm plus added singletons. If mention universes differ, per-mention precision/recall/F1 are not directly comparable. Details on fold construction (document-level splits), hyperparameter tuning, and feature selection on held-out data are insufficient.
- Reproducibility and resource availability: The dataset is anonymized and not currently accessible; the URL is a placeholder. The paper relies on Freebase (defunct) and WikipediaMiner, but does not specify exact versions/snapshots. Without released data/code and with external resources that may change, reproducibility is at risk.

Questions to Authors:
- Data splits and comparability: Are the 10 CV folds strictly document-level? Do all baselines and the proposed method evaluate on the exact same set of detected mentions per fold? Please clarify the mention detection pipeline used for each baseline and confirm identical mention universes for fair scoring.
- Stronger baselines: How does a simple heuristic baseline that marks a mention as MC if it matches the title, any redirect/alias, or the inferred first-sentence type (without invoking a CR system) perform? Similarly, what is the performance of an SVM trained on WCR with only standard CR features (no internal links/Freebase) to quantify the incremental value of Wikipedia-specific features?
- Ablations: Please provide ablations removing (a) internal links; (b) Freebase; (c) each of the four non-pronominal feature families (title, inferred type, name variants, entity type) to isolate contributions, and report results for non-pronominals analogous to Table 2 for pronouns.
- Robustness and generalization: How does the approach perform on a larger, more diverse Wikipedia sample and on articles with sparse linking? What fraction of mentions receive enriched attributes via internal links? Do results hold if cross-validated at the article-topic level (e.g., leave-entity-type-out)?
- Dcoref++ experimental setup: Was the sieve’s threshold tuned on held-out data separate from the CV folds? Does preventing later sieves from appending to the MC chain ever harm recall for non-MC chains? Please report error analysis and the impact on non-MC chains.

Additional comments:
- Please release WCR, code, and exact resource snapshots. Report statistical significance. Clarify how Wu & Palmer similarity is applied to strings (which ontology, sense inventory, and mapping strategy?).
