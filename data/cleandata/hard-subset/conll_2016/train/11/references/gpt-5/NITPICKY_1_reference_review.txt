Summary and contributions
- Defines and evaluates “Main Concept” (MC) resolution in Wikipedia: a binary classification problem to identify mentions coreferring to the article’s subject.
- Proposes two SVM classifiers (pronominal vs. non-pronominal) leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase-derived attributes; shows large gains over simple chain-selection baselines and modest gains when inserted as an early sieve into Dcoref.
- Introduces a small Wikipedia coreference resource (WCR) and reports performance of several state-of-the-art CR systems on it, highlighting domain mismatch.

Strengths
- Clear, useful task formulation: MC resolution in Wikipedia is pragmatic and has downstream utility (IE/OIE), and the focus on exploiting Wikipedia’s structure is well motivated.
- Simple method with strong empirical effect on this task: the feature design is sensible, and the classifiers substantially outperform the provided baselines on MC identification (F1 ≈ 89), especially for pronouns where generic CR struggles.
- Integration into a full CR pipeline: adding an MC sieve to Dcoref yields a non-trivial +4 CoNLL F1 on WCR, indicating the idea can benefit broader CR decisions.
- Some (partial) analysis: pronominal ablations show incremental gains from feature families and include qualitative examples.

Weaknesses
- Experimental design risk: potential leakage across folds. The 10-fold cross-validation appears to split by mentions, not by document. Because features include article-level counts/positions and a document-specific MC representation, training and test likely share the same article context, inflating results. Proper evaluation should leave articles out (leave-one-document-out CV). This also affects the out-of-fold predictions used in Dcoref++.
- Dataset is very small (30 articles, ~60k tokens) and heterogeneous; improvements may not generalize. No evaluation on a larger or different Wikipedia sample, and no statistical significance testing. The reported +4 CoNLL F1 on WCR could be within variance given the tiny test size.
- Baselines are weak and potentially unfair. They rely on chain length/title substring heuristics; however, the proposed model exploits redirects, Freebase aliases, inferred types, etc., while baselines do not. A stronger rule-based Wikipedia-specific baseline (e.g., match mentions to redirects/aliases/types; use first-sentence apposition; boldface cues) is required. Moreover, the pronoun “add-all” heuristic is only applied to baselines, not to the proposed method, confounding comparisons.
- Labeling and data preparation unclear. You augment gold WCR mentions with ~13.4k predicted singletons; how are MC labels assigned to these additional mentions (especially when unannotated in WCR)? This can introduce substantial noise or bias. Also, feature extraction uses within-article hyperlinks; the assumption that these are “high quality” should be quantified (many guidelines link only first occurrence, anchors can be ambiguous).
- Methodological details missing/underspecified:
  - Inferred type extraction from the first sentence: exact algorithm, error rates, and coverage not described.
  - Wu-Palmer similarity requires sense inventory and disambiguation; mapping procedure is unspecified.
  - “Compatibility” in the pronominal distance features via Freebase is not defined.
  - SVM hyperparameters (C, regularization strength) and tuning protocol are not detailed; “selected on a held-out dataset” is vague for such a tiny corpus.
- Reproducibility/anonymity issues:
  - WCR is referenced as “details in the non-anonymized version”; URLs include placeholders (www.somewhere.country) and “http://www.anonymized.org”; Freebase is deprecated. It is impossible to assess reproducibility now.
  - Missing tables/figures or misnumbered references: Table 3 is referenced but absent; Figures 1–3 are textual placeholders; footnotes are misplaced; small inconsistencies in reported scores (e.g., Scoref CoNLL F1 46.39 vs 46.58 across tables).

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation split: Did you ensure folds are grouped by document (no article appears in both train and test)? If not, can you re-run with leave-one-article-out CV and report results?
- Label assignment for added predicted singletons: How did you determine which of these refer to the MC? What is the estimated labeling noise? How sensitive are results to including these added mentions?
- Baselines: Can you add a stronger rule-based baseline that uses redirects, Freebase aliases/notable types, and first-sentence type extraction (i.e., the same sources your model uses but without learning)? Also, apply the “add-all pronouns” heuristic equally (or remove it) to avoid bias.
- Dcoref++ evaluation: Are the sieve decisions based strictly on out-of-fold predictions from a document-held-out model? Please clarify the training/prediction protocol and provide per-metric deltas with confidence intervals.
- Feature details: Provide precise algorithms for (a) inferred type extraction, (b) Wu-Palmer mapping/disambiguation, and (c) Freebase-based compatibility for pronouns. What is coverage when Freebase info is missing?
- Generalization: How does the classifier perform on a larger, random Wikipedia sample (e.g., 200–500 articles) with leave-article-out evaluation? Any per-topic breakdown?

Additional comments
- Clarify the evaluation metric for MC identification (micro vs macro averaging).
- Provide a complete non-pronominal ablation table (Table 3?) analogous to Table 2.
- Consider significance tests (bootstrap over documents) for both MC identification and CR metrics.
- Clean up formatting: fix footnotes, missing tables/figures, inconsistent references/URLs, and typos; ensure anonymity (no deferred “non-anonymized” details).
- Reliance on Freebase is problematic; discuss replacement (e.g., Wikidata) and whether results are robust without Freebase.

Overall, the task is interesting and the idea is promising, but the experimental protocol (likely leakage), tiny dataset, weak baselines, and reproducibility gaps undermine the claims. I cannot recommend acceptance without a document-level evaluation, stronger baselines, and cleaned-up, reproducible experiments.
