Summary and contributions
- Defines and operationalizes the task of Main Concept Resolution (MCR) in Wikipedia: identify all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the article’s subject. Frames it as binary mention classification and proposes tailored features that exploit Wikipedia markup and Freebase.
- Builds a dedicated Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR), annotated with OntoNotes guidelines and Freebase links, and shows that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on newswire degrade substantially on this genre, motivating genre-specific methods.
- Demonstrates a practical pipeline contribution: a simple SVM-based MCR classifier that significantly outperforms strong baselines (incl. those derived from Dcoref/Scoref) and, when integrated as an early sieve in Dcoref, yields a 4-point CoNLL F1 improvement on Wikipedia CR.

Strengths
- Novel and useful task framing for Wikipedia. Focusing on the article’s main concept is distinct from standard CR and directly applicable to knowledge extraction, indexing, and summarization in Wikipedia. The paper articulates the task clearly and turns it into a testable benchmark.
- Strong empirical gains with a simple, well-motivated approach. The MCR classifier achieves ~89 F1, an absolute gain of 13–15 points over baselines across pronouns and non-pronouns. The feature design smartly leverages human-curated structure (titles, redirects, anchors) to sidestep brittle entity linking.
- A meaningful testbed and diagnostic result for genre shift. The WCR dataset and the comparison showing sharp drops for news-trained systems on Wikipedia are valuable to the community and highlight an underexplored domain for CR.
- End-to-end impact via integration into a state-of-the-art CR pipeline. The Dcoref++ sieve experiment convincingly shows that recognizing MC mentions early benefits full CR on Wikipedia, not just the specialized subtask.
- Sensible analysis and ablations. The greedy feature addition experiments (especially for pronouns) and targeted examples help illuminate where the gains come from (e.g., title/type/name-variant families and position/distance cues).

Weaknesses
- Dataset scale and generalization. WCR contains only 30 articles (~60k tokens, ~7k non-singleton mentions). Ten-fold cross-validation over so few documents risks overfitting and makes it hard to assess robustness across topics and writing styles. The paper would be stronger with more articles or an additional held-out set.
- Heavy reliance on Wikipedia/Freebase markup without coverage reporting. Freebase is deprecated, and the approach’s dependence on entity types, gender, and redirects may limit longevity and portability. There is no ablation quantifying performance with only Wikipedia markup, without Freebase, or text-only features, nor coverage of how often attributes are available.
- Baselines are close to the proposed feature families but may still be weak. While B1–B4 and pronoun propagation are reasonable, a learned baseline using only title/redirect/anchor signals (no Freebase), or a modern EL-based MC resolver, would help isolate the value of the richer features and reduce the chance that improvements stem from leveraging near-trivial cues in Wikipedia.
- Methodological clarity and potential leakage. It is not fully clear whether main results use gold, predicted, or union mentions at test time. The enrichment step applies attributes to mentions whose string matches an internal link’s surface form; if anchors include the MC title and variants, this could give the model very strong nearly-supervised signals. More detail is needed to ensure no leakage from gold MC identity via redirect/anchor features.
- Limited analysis of side effects in full CR. In Dcoref++, later sieves are prevented from appending to the MC chain; while precision gains are reported, the trade-off in recall and potential negative impact on non-MC chains is underexplored. More qualitative error analysis for non-pronominal cases would also help.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- What mention inventory is used at test time for MCR evaluation: gold mentions, predicted mentions, or their union? How are P/R/F1 computed relative to that inventory? Please report results separately if possible.
- What is the coverage of attribute enrichment? Specifically, what percentage of mentions receive (a) any Wikipedia-derived attributes, (b) any Freebase-derived attributes? Can you provide an ablation with: only Wikipedia markup; only Freebase; neither (text-only)?
- How do you handle ambiguous surface strings when assigning attributes from internal links (same string linking to different pages in different contexts)? Are attributes propagated uniformly to all matches, and could this leak MC identity via redirects?
- Can you report per-topic breakdowns (PER/ORG/LOC/EVENT) and by article length/link density to gauge robustness?
- In Dcoref++, how often does the “freeze MC chain” rule block correct attachments from later sieves? Do you have an oracle or ablation showing the precision/recall trade-off of this design?

Additional comments
- Replacing Freebase with Wikidata and reporting the corresponding results would future-proof the approach.
- Publishing more articles (even +100) and a small out-of-sample test split would materially strengthen the generalization claim.
- Please release precise feature definitions and string similarity heuristics to ensure reproducibility.
- A future neural baseline (e.g., a BERT-based mention–title classifier) would be a natural comparator; not required for acceptance given the task’s novelty, but worth noting.
