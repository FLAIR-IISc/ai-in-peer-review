Summary and main contributions
- The paper reframes “main concept” (MC) resolution in Wikipedia articles as a binary mention classification task: determine whether a mention refers to the article’s subject. It leverages Wikipedia markup (titles, redirects, internal links) and Freebase attributes to engineer feature-rich SVM classifiers for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions.
- It introduces a small annotated Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR, 30 docs) and shows that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on news perform markedly worse on this genre. The authors design baselines by post-processing outputs of Dcoref and Scoref to isolate the MC chain.
- Integrating the MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref yields a +4 CoNLL F1 improvement on WCR, suggesting that early, high-confidence MC decisions can benefit full-document CR in Wikipedia.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of a practically useful subtask. Identifying the article’s main entity is relevant for Wikipedia-specific IE and downstream tasks (e.g., OIE, tuple extraction) and is more tractable than full CR while still impactful.
- Effective use of Wikipedia-native signals. Exploiting internal links, redirects, and first-sentence type heuristics avoids the brittleness of external EL pipelines; features derived from titles, variants, and Freebase attributes are intuitive and strong for Wikipedia.
- Consistent, substantial empirical gains on the target task. The proposed classifiers outperform multiple baselines derived from two competitive CR systems by large margins (up to +13–15 F1), for both pronominal and non-pronominal mentions, with an informative ablation highlighting which feature families matter.
- Demonstrates genre mismatch in CR. The evaluation of several state-of-the-art CR systems on WCR (vs. OntoNotes) is a useful reminder of domain sensitivity, with interesting observations (rule-based Dcoref outperforming statistical systems on Wikipedia).
- Integration into a full CR pipeline. The sieve-based insertion into Dcoref (“Dcoref++”) is simple and yields +4 CoNLL F1 on WCR, indicating that MC recognition can serve as a strong, early precision anchor for subsequent sieves.

Weaknesses
- Dataset scale and representativeness. WCR is very small (30 articles, ~60K tokens). Results may not generalize across Wikipedia’s topic diversity and style variations; the MC task can be artificially easy in some pages (e.g., lead sections with many surface repeats). No inter-annotator agreement is reported, and the selection procedure (topics, link density) is only briefly described.
- Evaluation design risks leakage/overfitting. The 10-fold cross-validation granularity is not clearly specified. If folds are not by document, MC-specific attributes (title, variants) can leak across train/test. Even with document-level CV, using CV predictions for integration into Dcoref and evaluating on the same 30 documents risks optimistic estimates. Statistical significance is not reported.
- Baselines may be too weak or too tailored. The baselines select/aggregate chains by heuristics from Dcoref/Scoref, and include an “all-pronoun” heuristic. Missing are stronger deterministic baselines that directly use the same Wikipedia/Freebase signals (e.g., exact/partial title match + redirects + first-sentence type + simple pronoun rules) without learned models. This would better isolate the value of the classifier vs. the signal source.
- Feature/source ablations are incomplete. The paper mixes signals from Wikipedia and Freebase but does not quantify coverage, noise, or their relative contributions (e.g., performance when Freebase is absent, or when internal links are sparse). Redirect noise is acknowledged but not characterized. The “distance” features depend on Freebase compatibility; robustness without Freebase is unclear.
- Limited novelty beyond engineering. Prior work (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008) targeted similar goals with rules. The main technical novelty is a supervised classifier with handcrafted features leveraging Wikipedia/Freebase, which is incremental. The integration into a sieve-based system is straightforward. The contribution is primarily empirical/engineering rather than methodological.
- Some methodological ambiguities. The class weighting for SVMs is described as “penalized the negative class with weight 2.0,” which seems counterintuitive given class imbalance; clarification is needed. Details on mention detection quality after adding 13.4k singletons, impact on labeling noise, and error propagation are limited.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Are folds done by document (i.e., all mentions from a document in the same fold)? If not, how do you avoid leakage from MC attributes across train/test? For Dcoref++ evaluation, are sieve predictions strictly out-of-fold per document?
- Inter-annotator agreement: What were the annotation guidelines for WCR, and what are the IAA statistics (mention detection, coreference links, MC mentions)? How were copular/attributive links handled vis-à-vis MC?
- Baseline strengthening: How would a deterministic baseline that uses your MC representation (title, redirects, inferred type, Freebase aliases/types) plus simple pronoun rules perform? Please report this to separate the effect of utilizing Wikipedia/Freebase from the classifier itself.
- Source ablations and coverage: What fraction of mentions receive attributes via internal links? What fraction of MCs lack Freebase entries? Provide ablations: no Freebase, no redirects, no inferred type, no internal-link propagation.
- Generalization: How does performance vary by article type (PER/ORG/LOC/MISC), article length, and link density? Do pronoun-heavy vs. nominal-heavy pages behave differently?
- SVM class weighting: Did you intend to upweight positives or negatives? Please clarify the rationale and any tuning.
- Dcoref++ behavior: Your sieve prevents later sieves from appending to the MC chain. Does this harm recall on MC or on non-MC chains? Provide per-metric (precision/recall) breakdowns per chain type and an error analysis of cases where later sieves would have correctly added mentions.

Additional comments
- Please clarify whether “average precision” means macro-averaged precision across documents or the IR-style AP metric. Report confidence intervals or significance tests where possible.
- Some examples suggest that pronouns overwhelmingly refer to the MC (the “always positive” baseline gets 63.7 F1). Reporting class priors per mention type would contextualize results.
- Freebase is now deprecated; consider discussing portability to Wikidata and the minimal set of Wikipedia-only features required.
