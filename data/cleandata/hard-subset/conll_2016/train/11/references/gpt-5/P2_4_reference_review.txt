Summary and main contributions
- Task and benchmark: The paper formalizes “main concept (MC) resolution” in Wikipedia—deciding whether a mention refers to the article’s subject—and proposes a testbed (WCR) with Wikipedia articles annotated under OntoNotes guidelines. It also shows that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on newswire degrade substantially on this genre.
- Method: A pair of linear SVM classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. The approach treats MC resolution as binary classification per mention.
- Integration into CR: Incorporates the classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref, improving overall CoNLL F1 on WCR.

Strengths
- Clear problem definition and motivation: MC resolution is a practically useful subtask for Wikipedia-based IE/OIE. The paper demonstrates a large domain shift for CR systems on Wikipedia (substantial drops in CoNLL F1), justifying a dedicated approach.
- Strong empirical gains on the target task: The classifier achieves about 89 F1 on MC mention identification, with 13–15 absolute F1 gains over baselines derived from Dcoref/Scoref. The feature ablations are informative (e.g., title, inferred type, alias, and coarse attribute families each add value).
- Effective use of Wikipedia structure: Leveraging internal links, redirects, aliases, and the lead sentence to infer types is simple, effective, and genre-appropriate. Avoiding full-blown entity linking reduces pipeline brittleness while still injecting valuable knowledge.
- Impact on end-to-end CR (within-domain): Adding the MC sieve to Dcoref improves CoNLL F1 by about 4 points on WCR across MUC/B3/CEAF, suggesting that early, high-precision MC decisions help downstream sieves.
- Clarity and partial reproducibility: System design, features, and baselines are described in reasonable detail, with some qualitative error analysis. The authors commit to releasing data and outputs.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and openness: WCR comprises only 30 documents (~60k tokens, ~7k non-singleton mentions). This is small for training and robust evaluation; 10-fold CV over 30 docs risks high variance. Details are deferred to a non-anonymized version, and current availability is unclear. The paper’s impact hinges on releasing WCR and code.
- Potential leakage and split protocol: The paper uses 10-fold cross-validation but does not explicitly state whether folds are document-level. If folds mix sentences from the same article across train/test, or if MC aliases/redirects derived from the test article leak into training features, results would be inflated. Given that many features are article-specific (title, redirects, lead sentence type), a strict document-level split (one article wholly in a single fold) is essential; please clarify and, if needed, re-run.
- Baselines may be too favorable or mismatched: All baselines are built on coreference outputs (Dcoref/Scoref). Missing are direct, genre-informed rule baselines that exploit the same Wikipedia signals as the model, e.g., “all mentions matching title/redirects/aliases/inferred type + simple positional/pronoun rules.” Such a baseline could be strong and is closer to a method-free upper bound for Wikipedia. As shown in ablations, string/type features drive much of the performance; a deterministic alias/type heuristic baseline would better contextualize the gains from learning.
- Dependency on Freebase and external resources: Freebase is now deprecated; the paper neither explores alternatives (e.g., Wikidata) nor ablates Freebase-dependent features. This affects reproducibility and future utility. A variant using only Wikipedia signals (or Wikidata) would strengthen claims and adoption.
- Generalization and scope: Improvements to end-to-end CR are reported only on WCR. It remains unclear whether inserting the MC sieve helps on other Wikipedia topics or harms performance on non-MC chains or other genres (e.g., OntoNotes). A per-chain analysis (MC vs. non-MC) and a cross-domain check would help assess side effects and generality.

Questions to authors
- Splits and leakage: Are CV folds strictly document-level (entire articles in a single fold)? Are MC attributes (title, redirects, aliases, inferred type) computed solely from the test article at test time, with no cross-article/shared resource contamination from training folds? Please detail how you prevented leakage.
- Rule baseline: How would a deterministic baseline that uses title/redirects/aliases/inferred type and simple heuristics (e.g., pronouns in lead/section starts) perform? Can you add this comparison?
- Freebase ablation: What is performance using only Wikipedia-derived features (title, redirects, inferred type, categories) or substituting Wikidata? Which feature families are most robust without Freebase?
- Impact on other chains: When integrating the MC sieve, does performance on non-MC chains degrade or improve? Can you report MC-chain F1 vs. non-MC-chain F1 and typical error modes introduced by freezing the MC chain early?
- Resource release: Will WCR, code, and the Wikipedia-mention dump be released at camera-ready, with documentation sufficient for reproduction?

Additional comments
- Reporting significance: Consider statistical significance tests for main improvements.
- Efficiency: A brief note on runtime (feature extraction + classification) would help practitioners.
- Broader applicability: The approach seems adaptable to other encyclopedic or structured corpora; a short discussion on portability (and on replacing Freebase) would increase the paper’s longevity.

Overall assessment
This is a well-motivated, cleanly executed study with clear utility for Wikipedia-centric NLP. The empirical gains on MC resolution and the positive impact on end-to-end CR within Wikipedia are convincing. However, the small dataset, potential leakage concerns, lack of a strong Wikipedia-heuristic baseline, and reliance on Freebase limit robustness and reproducibility. If the authors clarify splits, add a rule baseline using the same Wikipedia signals, and provide an ablation without Freebase (or with Wikidata), I would be inclined to recommend acceptance.
