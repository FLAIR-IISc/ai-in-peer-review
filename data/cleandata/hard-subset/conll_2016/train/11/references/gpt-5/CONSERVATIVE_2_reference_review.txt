Summary and claimed contributions:
This paper targets “Main Concept Resolution” (MCR) in Wikipedia: given an article, identify all mentions coreferring to the article’s main concept. The authors (i) construct a small Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR) and show that off-the-shelf CR systems trained on news perform poorly on it; (ii) propose two SVM classifiers (pronominal and non-pronominal) with features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase to identify MC mentions, substantially outperforming baselines derived from Dcoref and Scoref; and (iii) integrate the MC classifier as an early sieve into Dcoref (Dcoref++), reporting a CoNLL F1 improvement on WCR.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A focused formulation and evaluation of MC mention identification in Wikipedia, with a feature-based classifier leveraging Wikipedia/Freebase metadata that outperforms heuristic baselines.
2) Empirical evidence that standard CR systems underperform on Wikipedia and that seeding a high-precision MC sieve can improve rule-based CR on this genre.
3) Release (claimed) of a Wikipedia CR dataset and resources for reproducibility.

Strengths:
- Clear, well-scoped task with practical motivation. Identifying the article’s main entity is a reasonable subproblem for Wikipedia-centric IE and CR, and the paper gives a concrete framing as binary classification over mentions.
- Leverages established, interpretable features and resources. The approach is technically simple and grounded in prior work (surface forms, redirects, aliases, first-sentence types, Freebase attributes). The ablation for pronouns is informative, showing incremental gains from position/tag/distance features.
- Strong gains on the targeted subtask relative to adapted CR baselines. On their WCR set, the classifier achieves ~89 F1 vs ~76–84 F1 for the best baselines, with gains for both pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. This suggests the approach captures Wikipedia idiosyncrasies better than generic systems.
- Demonstrates downstream impact in a complete CR pipeline on this domain. Integrating the classifier as a leading sieve yields a 4-point CoNLL F1 improvement over Dcoref on WCR, with precision gains across MUC/B3/CEAF. This is an encouraging sign that the MC signal can help broader CR decisions.
- Addresses cross-genre robustness. Table 1 quantifies the drop of several CR systems on WCR vs OntoNotes, reinforcing the need for domain-aware methods.

Weaknesses:
- Limited dataset scale and insufficient validation. WCR is only 30 articles (~60k tokens). All results, including the Dcoref++ improvement, are confined to this set via 10-fold CV. There is no held-out Wikipedia set, no cross-article split that guards against leakage of article-specific aliases/redirects across folds, and no evaluation on other Wikipedia pages or categories. The risk of overfitting feature design to this small collection is high, and statistical significance of reported gains is not provided.
- Baseline coverage and fairness. The baselines depend on CR outputs (chain selection/aggregation) plus a pronoun-propagation heuristic. However, stronger non-CR baselines that use the same supervision as the proposed method are missing, e.g., (i) a direct rule-based system using Wikipedia title/redirect/first-sentence type and hyperlink spans; (ii) a simple learned baseline such as logistic regression over the same features; (iii) a named-entity linking or wikification-based baseline (even high-precision, low-recall), as discussed in related work. As a result, it is unclear how much of the gain stems from obvious Wikipedia markup heuristics versus the classifier itself.
- Reliance on Freebase and link structure threatens reproducibility and robustness. Freebase is deprecated, and coverage varies; many Wikipedia articles have sparse links beyond the first mention. The paper asserts “human-made links” are high-quality but does not quantify coverage, error rates, or the model’s performance when Freebase or links are absent. No analysis of failure modes in low-link-density pages is provided, and no ablation removing Freebase is reported.
- Methodological clarity gaps. Key details are missing: inter-annotator agreement for WCR; exact sampling criteria and topic distribution; how negative labels were assigned to the 13.4k automatically added singletons; how redirects/aliases were handled across cross-validation folds to avoid leakage; and how parameters (e.g., SVM C, class weights) were tuned without overfitting. The pronoun “distance” features rely on “compatibility” per Freebase—this requires more precise definition.
- Generality and novelty are limited. Identifying an article’s main entity references is a known target in Wikipedia IE (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008), and the feature set is largely an aggregation of known signals. The integration into Dcoref is a straightforward sieve addition. Without broader validation (e.g., Wikidata instead of Freebase, larger evaluation, other domains), it is hard to endorse the approach beyond the narrow WCR testbed.

Questions to Authors:
- Dataset and splits: How were the 10 CV folds constructed? Were articles kept intact within folds? Were redirect/alias resources and first-sentence type induction constructed solely from the training folds, or do test folds benefit from global resources (risking leakage)? Please report inter-annotator agreement for WCR and provide more details on annotation guidelines and adjudication.
- Singletons and labels: You add ~13.4k automatically extracted mentions. How are these labeled for MC vs non-MC? Could this introduce label noise that biases precision/recall? What fraction of MC mentions are singletons in the annotation (if any)?
- Baselines: Can you add (i) a pure markup/redirect/title/type rule-based baseline (no CR dependency), and (ii) a simple logistic regression baseline over your features? Also, compare against a high-precision wikification/EL baseline as in Ratinov and Roth (2012).
- External resources: What is the model’s performance when Freebase features are removed? Can you substitute Wikidata and report comparable results? How often are Freebase attributes available in WCR?
- Robustness: How does performance vary by article type (PER/ORG/LOC), length, and link density? Please provide per-genre breakdowns and an analysis for pages with minimal internal links.
- Significance: Are the improvements over baselines and Dcoref statistically significant? Please include appropriate tests.

Additional comments:
- Please clarify whether the promised data/resources will include code for mention detection and feature extraction, and whether any third-party APIs (e.g., WikipediaMiner) require specific versions to reproduce results.
