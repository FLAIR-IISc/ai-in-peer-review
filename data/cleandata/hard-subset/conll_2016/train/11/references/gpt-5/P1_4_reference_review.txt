Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper targets “Main Concept (MC) Resolution” in Wikipedia: identifying all mentions in an article that refer to the article’s main concept. It frames this as a binary classification task over mentions (pronominal vs non-pronominal handled by separate SVMs) with features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase.
- It constructs heuristics on top of two state-of-the-art CR systems (Dcoref, Scoref) as baselines for MC identification, and shows large improvements over these baselines on a new Wikipedia Coreference Resource (WCR).
- It integrates the MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref, improving overall coreference on WCR.
- It introduces WCR: 30 Wikipedia documents (≈60k tokens), OntoNotes-style annotation, with MC labels and Freebase links.

My view of the main contributions (in order of significance)
1) A simple but effective, Wikipedia-aware MC mention classifier with strong gains over reasonable baselines and a practical integration into a full CR pipeline (+4 CoNLL F1).
2) A focused evaluation protocol and analysis demonstrating the genre gap: state-of-the-art CR systems trained on OntoNotes degrade substantially on Wikipedia, motivating genre-specific approaches.
3) A small but valuable Wikipedia-genre dataset (WCR) annotated for coreference and linked to Freebase.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation and practical relevance: Framing MC identification as a binary classification task is well-scoped, useful for downstream tasks (IE, wikification, page summarization), and sidesteps brittle global clustering. The demonstrated downstream impact via Dcoref integration is a plus.
- Effective exploitation of Wikipedia/KB signals: Features leveraging title, inferred type (from the lead sentence), redirects/aliases, and Freebase notable types and coarse attributes are well-justified for Wikipedia and empirically potent. The ablation-style feature addition shows consistent improvements, especially for pronominals (F1 up to 88.99).
- Strong empirical gains over baselines for the target task: On all mentions, the best classifier reaches ≈89 F1, with +13–15 absolute F1 over baselines built on Dcoref/Scoref. The pronominal case is particularly compelling, outperforming aggressive heuristics.
- Integration into a full CR system with measurable benefit: Adding the classifier as a high-precision sieve leads to +4 CoNLL F1 (from 51.77 to 55.11), with improvements across MUC, B3, CEAFφ4. This shows utility beyond the narrow task and suggests that early high-precision MC decisions can guide subsequent sieves.
- Diagnostic analysis: The paper provides insight into what features help (e.g., title/inferred type for nominals, position/distance for pronouns), and gives qualitative examples of both successes and typical errors.

Weaknesses
- Dataset scale and statistical robustness: WCR contains only 30 documents. Results are reported via 10-fold CV, but there is no variance or significance testing. With small N, high-dimensional binary features, and cross-validation-based feature selection, overfitting risks are nontrivial. The generality of the reported gains across topics and article structures remains uncertain.
- Baselines are limited and potentially weak for this task: All baselines are heuristic post-processing of off-the-shelf CR outputs. Stronger task-specific baselines are missing, e.g., (i) a simple rule-based system leveraging MC title + redirects/aliases + first-sentence appositives/types; (ii) a supervised linear classifier with only string/position/frequency features; (iii) a replication of Nguyen/Nakayama’s heuristics. The current comparison may understate how far well-tuned rule-based methods can go in Wikipedia.
- Heavy dependence on KB resources and Wikipedia-wide signals: The approach leans on Freebase (entity types, gender/number, profession) and Wikipedia redirects/anchor text to the MC. This may inflate performance on well-linked concepts and reduces applicability when markup sparsity or KB coverage is limited. While some feature ablations are reported, the paper lacks a clear decoupling analysis (e.g., performance with/without Freebase; with only local-page markup vs global anchor text). Reproducibility is also hampered as Freebase is deprecated.
- Reproducibility and anonymization gaps: Details about WCR are withheld (“non-anonymized version”), and the promised release link is a placeholder. Critical implementation details are missing or terse (exact feature inventories, SVM hyperparameters/regularization choices, thresholds). Without accessible data/code and with reliance on external KBs, replicability is unclear.
- Data preparation and labeling clarity: For MC detection, the training pool is the union of gold WCR mentions and ≈13.4k automatically extracted singletons. The labeling of these auto singletons (and any noise therein) is not described, yet they constitute the majority of negatives and can materially affect measured precision/recall.

Questions to authors
- Dataset and statistical robustness: Can you provide per-fold variance (or confidence intervals) for the main metrics and significance testing vs baselines? Also, how are the 10 folds defined—by article (i.e., no sentence-level mixing)? Any topic stratification across folds?
- Ablations on KB dependency: What is performance when (a) Freebase features are removed; (b) only local-page markup is used (no global anchor text/redirects); (c) both are removed (string-only)? This would clarify robustness on low-coverage concepts and the marginal utility of each source.
- Baseline strengthening: Can you add a rule-based baseline that uses MC title + redirects + first-sentence type/appositive patterns, and a minimal supervised baseline using only string/position/frequency features? If possible, compare to heuristics in Nguyen (2007) and Nakayama (2008).
- Labeling of automatically extracted singletons: How were labels assigned to the added 13.4k mentions? What is the estimated noise rate, and how sensitive are results to this augmentation?
- Dcoref++ effects beyond MC chain: Does prioritizing the MC sieve harm non-MC chains (e.g., over-merging or blocking later sieves from correcting)? Please provide per-chain or per-entity-type analysis, and error breakdowns on precision vs recall changes.

Additional comments
- The paper is clearly written and well-motivated. Adding more detail on feature definitions and providing concrete release artifacts (data/code or processed features) will greatly improve impact.
- Discussing the applicability of MC resolution to non-entity MCs (events, works, abstract concepts) would help scope the method’s limits.
