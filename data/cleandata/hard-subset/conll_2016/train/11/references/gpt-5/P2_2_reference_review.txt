Summary and contributions
- The paper defines and studies “Main Concept (MC) Resolution” in Wikipedia: given an article, identify all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the article’s subject. It frames this as binary classification per mention and exploits Wikipedia markup and Freebase attributes to build features, with separate models for pronominal vs non-pronominal mentions.
- It introduces WCR, a small Wikipedia coreference dataset (30 documents; ~60k tokens), annotated with OntoNotes guidelines, and uses it to show that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on news perform poorly on Wikipedia.
- It demonstrates strong gains over baselines tailored from Dcoref/Scoref outputs for MC detection (F1 ~88.8 vs best baseline ~75.8), and shows that integrating the MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref yields a non-trivial improvement on full CR on WCR (+3.34 CoNLL F1, from 51.77 to 55.11).

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated task with practical value. Identifying mentions of the page’s subject is directly useful for downstream tasks such as OIE and entity-centric analysis in Wikipedia. The paper convincingly argues that generic CR trained on newswire struggles on Wikipedia, motivating specialized solutions.
- Effective, simple method that leverages Wikipedia structure. The feature design is pragmatic: using article title, redirects, intra-Wiki anchors, inferred types from lead sentence, and Freebase attributes. This sidesteps noisy NEL and capitalizes on human-curated links. The ablation (especially for pronouns) shows incremental gains from position, POS, and local distance features, giving insight into what works on Wikipedia.
- Strong empirical improvements on the target task. On MC detection, the proposed classifiers outperform all Dcoref/Scoref-derived baselines by large margins (overall F1 88.79 vs 75.77/73.69; pronominal F1 88.99 vs 84.17 best baseline). The gains are consistent across precision and recall, indicating the features improve both identification and filtering.
- Demonstrated impact on full CR. Integrating the MC classifier as the top sieve in Dcoref improves CoNLL F1 by 3–4 absolute points on WCR across MUC/B3/CEAFφ4. This shows the MC signal is not just an isolated subtask but also helpful for broader CR decisions.
- Reproducibility intent and resource promise. The authors state they will release WCR, features, and a Wikipedia-wide dump of predicted MC mentions, which would be valuable to the community.

Weaknesses
- Dataset is very small and limited in diversity. WCR contains only 30 articles (1469 chains; 1800 MC mentions). While the topic variety is claimed, the sample size makes it difficult to assess robustness and generalization. The reported gains might be sensitive to idiosyncrasies of the sample, and variance across folds/documents is not reported.
- Limited baseline breadth and fairness. The strongest baselines rely on outputs from existing CR systems plus heuristics, but there is no comparison to a strong “markup-only” baseline that ignores CR (e.g., all anchors linking to the page or redirects + propagation via simple rules for unlinked mentions/pronouns). Such a baseline could be very competitive on Wikipedia and would better isolate the contribution of the proposed features beyond link/title matching. Also, statistical CR systems were not retrained on WCR; the paper notes this but it limits fairness.
- Heavy reliance on Wikipedia/Freebase signals reduces generality. Many features depend on internal links, redirects, and Freebase types. Wikipedia editorial style discourages overlinking, so many mentions (especially repeated nominal mentions) are unlinked; the paper does not quantify how often Freebase/type info is available. It is unclear how performance degrades on low-link-density pages or sections. The dependency on Freebase (now deprecated) also raises maintainability concerns; no results with Wikidata are provided.
- Insufficient methodological detail in places. The semantic similarity feature (Wu & Palmer) is mentioned without specifying sense inventories or disambiguation; how similarity is computed between multiword strings and titles is unclear. For non-pronominal ablation, results are described narratively without a table analogous to Table 2, limiting interpretability. No statistical significance testing is reported for improvements.
- Risk of bias/label leakage and limited external validation. Features such as title/redirects are derived from the same article content; while appropriate at inference, it can lead to overly optimistic estimates when coupled with a tiny dataset and 10-fold CV. An external test set (e.g., a held-out Wikipedia subset or more documents) would strengthen claims. The Dcoref++ evaluation is only on WCR; it is unknown whether MC-first sieves harm CR on other genres or even on different Wikipedia topics.

Questions to authors
- Can you report a strong link-only baseline (e.g., all anchors pointing to the page or its redirects as positive; propagate to exact/substring matches and pronouns by simple rules) without using Dcoref/Scoref? This would clarify the added value of the classifier beyond exploiting markup.
- What fraction of mentions in WCR have available Freebase types/attributes? How does MC detection performance vary with link density and availability of Freebase info?
- Please detail how the Wu & Palmer similarity is computed between strings without word sense disambiguation. What resource and mapping are used for multiword mentions and titles?
- Can you provide non-pronominal ablation results in a table and include statistical significance tests for key improvements?
- Did you try retraining a statistical CR model on WCR or augmenting it with your Wikipedia/Freebase features to compare against Dcoref++? If not, what are the expected challenges?

Overall, the paper addresses an important gap with a focused, effective approach and shows convincing gains on the defined task. The main concerns are dataset scale/scope, baseline coverage, and generality. Addressing these would strengthen the submission. I lean toward acceptance given the practical utility and clear improvements, contingent on clarifying baselines and adding analysis on dependency to Wikipedia/Freebase signals.
