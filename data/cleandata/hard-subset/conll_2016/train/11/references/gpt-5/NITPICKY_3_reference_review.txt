Summary and contributions
- The paper revisits “main concept” (MC) resolution in Wikipedia: given an article, identify all mentions (nominal, pronominal, named) that refer to the article’s subject. The authors frame this as per-mention binary classification with two SVMs (pronominal/non-pronominal), leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase attributes.
- They build a small Wikipedia coreference resource (WCR: 30 docs, ~60k tokens), show that off-the-shelf CR systems underperform on this genre, design baselines derived from Dcoref/Scoref outputs, and report sizable gains of their classifier over those baselines. They also add a high-precision sieve to Dcoref (Dcoref++) and report +4 CoNLL F1 on WCR.
- Contributions as I see them: (1) a focused task definition and simple, markup-aware classifier for MC resolution in Wikipedia; (2) an empirical study highlighting genre mismatch for CR systems on Wikipedia; (3) a small annotated dataset and a demonstration that injecting MC knowledge can help a rule-based CR pipeline.

Strengths
- Clear, useful subtask with strong motivation. The MC chain is indeed central in Wikipedia articles and important for downstream extraction tasks; framing as binary classification is sensible.
- Effective exploitation of Wikipedia-specific signals. The feature design (title/aliases/redirects/types, plus Freebase attributes) is pragmatic, easy to reproduce in principle, and yields substantial gains over generic CR baselines.
- Solid pronoun modeling. The pronominal feature ablation (Table 2) is informative; the progressive gains (+main, +position, +tag, +distance) are convincing and outperform heuristic “always positive” and Dcoref-derived baselines by a healthy margin.
- Integration into a CR system shows promise. The Dcoref++ sieve that consolidates the MC chain improves CoNLL F1 by 4 points on WCR, indicating that early, high-precision MC decisions can help downstream sieves.
- Empirical evidence of genre mismatch. The comparison in Table 1 (large drops from OntoNotes to WCR) is timely and reinforces the need for genre-specific adaptation.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size, availability, and anonymity. WCR is extremely small (30 docs) for training and 10-fold CV, risking instability and overfitting. The paper withholds details (“nonanonymized version”) and provides no anonymized download. This raises reproducibility and fairness concerns and may violate anonymity policy. No variance/confidence intervals or significance tests are reported.
- Baseline design may be uncompetitive/unfair. All baselines are built on outputs of CR systems ill-suited to Wikipedia and then filtered/aggregated. A much stronger, task-specific baseline (simple string match to title/aliases/redirects/types plus light heuristics) is conspicuously absent and likely to be very competitive for non-pronominal mentions. The current baselines may underestimate attainable performance without learning.
- Possible mis-specification of class weighting. The dataset is heavily imbalanced for non-pronominal mentions (~16% positive), yet the authors “penalized the negative class with a weight of 2.0.” This seems backwards and would exacerbate imbalance; please verify. No details on how class weights were chosen (tuning protocol) are provided.
- Incomplete ablations and reporting. The non-pronominal feature ablation is only described qualitatively; no table akin to Table 2 is provided. It is difficult to assess the individual contribution of title, inferred type, name variants, entity type, and main features quantitatively. Also, “average precision” is mentioned but only P/R/F1 are reported; no PR curves or AP values.
- Risk of information leakage and overfitting to the page. Features use the article’s own title/aliases/redirects and internal links to assign coarse attributes. While this is permitted by task design, it makes non-pronominal decisions nearly trivial (string/alias inclusion), and may inflate results on such a small CV. The pronoun “distance” features depend on detecting compatible entities within the same sentence using Freebase; details on how candidates are obtained without full NEL are sparse.
- Comparisons across systems not fully controlled. Dcoref/Scoref use the same mention detector as the authors’, but the authors add ~13.4k singleton mentions; are baselines re-run with the same mention universe? It is unclear whether all systems operate on identical mention sets. Also, in Table 1, “Scoref 46.39” vs Table 5 “C&M (2015) 46.58” inconsistency.
- Reproducibility gaps and outdated resources. Precise versions/snapshots of Wikipedia, WikipediaMiner, Freebase, and Jena are not specified; Freebase is deprecated. Hyperparameters beyond C-SVC/linear are not reported; no development split is described beyond “heldout dataset.” The new sieve’s exact decision thresholds and tie-breaking are not specified.
- Numerous presentation/formatting issues. Typos and inconsistencies (e.g., “Queen s,” “nonanonymized,” “co-referent/co-refer,” “Grammatical words are not considered for matching.” as a stray footnote), awkward phrasing (“imputable”), and orphaned URLs/footnotes (“Jena2,” “http://www.anonymized.org,” “www.somewhere.country”). Table/figure referencing is uneven; Figure 1/2/3 are described but not shown here; the CEAF metric is denoted CEAFφ4 without specifying the alignment variant beyond φ4.

Questions to authors
- Class weights: Did you intend to upweight the positive class? Please clarify the exact class weights used, the tuning protocol, and the sensitivity of results to these weights and to the linear kernel choice.
- Baselines: Please add a strong non-CR baseline: exact/partial string match against title + redirects + aliases + inferred type, with simple heuristics for section-initial pronouns. Report its performance and include a full ablation table for non-pronominal features (akin to Table 2).
- Mention sets: Do all baselines and systems use the identical set of predicted mentions (including added singletons)? If not, please normalize and re-run to ensure fair comparison.
- Data and splits: Is 10-fold CV done at document level (no sentence-level mixing)? Provide per-fold variance or confidence intervals. Will you release an anonymized download link for WCR, code, and all feature resources for reproducibility?
- Resource versions: Which Wikipedia dump, WikipediaMiner, Freebase snapshot, Jena version, and Stanford CoreNLP models did you use? How are Freebase attributes mapped to mentions without a full NEL step, and how robust is this when links are sparse?
- Dcoref++: What is the precise sieve logic (thresholds, conflict resolution), and how does it affect non-MC chains? Please report MC-chain-specific precision/recall as well as the impact on the rest of the document’s chains.

Overall, while the task is useful and the approach yields promising gains, the paper’s experimental design, baseline completeness, dataset size/availability, and clarity issues prevent a confident assessment. I lean toward rejection unless the authors can substantially strengthen baselines, clarify/class-correct the training setup, and ensure reproducibility under anonymity.
