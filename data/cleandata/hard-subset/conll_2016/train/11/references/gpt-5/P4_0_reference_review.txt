Summary
The paper targets a focused variant of coreference in Wikipedia: identifying all mentions that refer to the article’s main concept (MC). The task is framed as binary classification over detected mentions (positive if referring to the MC). The authors build two SVM classifiers (pronominal and non-pronominal) with features derived from Wikipedia markup (title, redirects, internal links), Freebase attributes (types, aliases, gender/number), and local linguistic/contextual signals. They construct baselines by post-processing outputs from state-of-the-art coreference systems (Dcoref and Scoref) via heuristics to select/aggregate chains hypothesized to be the MC chain. On a small, annotated Wikipedia dataset (WCR), the proposed approach outperforms all baselines for MC resolution, and integrating the classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref improves end-to-end coreference metrics on WCR.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A dedicated, feature-based approach for Main Concept Resolution in Wikipedia that leverages Wikipedia/Freebase signals and substantially outperforms strong baselines built on state-of-the-art coreference systems.

2) An annotated Wikipedia dataset (WCR) and an empirical study documenting the performance drop of widely used coreference systems on Wikipedia compared to OntoNotes.

3) A simple integration of the MC classifier into a rule-based coreference system (Dcoref), yielding consistent improvements on end-to-end coreference evaluation on Wikipedia.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation aligned with Wikipedia usage: The MC resolution task is well-scoped, realistic for downstream extraction/IE, and exploits the fact that the article’s topic is known. Framing it as per-mention classification is technically sound and matches the available supervision (article title and markup).

- Effective use of Wikipedia/Freebase supervision without a full NEL step: The method capitalizes on human-authored internal links, redirects, and Freebase notable types/aliases to derive high-precision features. This addresses the precision–recall trade-offs and cascading errors typical of pipeline NEL approaches, and is reflected in consistent gains across pronominal and non-pronominal mentions.

- Strong empirical gains with informative ablations: On WCR, the approach achieves F1≈89 for MC resolution, an absolute +13–15 F1 over baselines derived from Dcoref/Scoref. Feature ablations are meaningful: for pronouns, adding MC attributes, positional, POS, and distance features steadily improves both precision and recall; for non-pronominals, title/inferred-type/name-variant/entity-type features materially increase performance, demonstrating the value of each external-knowledge family.

- Downstream impact on coreference resolution: Embedding the classifier as a top sieve (Dcoref++) produces a +4 CoNLL F1 improvement on WCR, with gains across MUC, B3, and CEAF. This shows that accurate MC identification can beneficially constrain subsequent sieves.

- Baselines and evaluation choices are reasonably strong: Baselines consider multiple heuristics (longest chain, title matching, chain aggregation, pronoun augmentation) built on top of two competitive CR systems, reducing the likelihood that gains are due to weak comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and representativeness: WCR comprises 30 documents (~60k tokens), with ~7k coreferent mentions. While varied topics are claimed, the absolute size is small for training, feature selection, and robust evaluation. No statistical significance testing is provided. The reliability of absolute improvements and generalizability to broader Wikipedia (or other languages) are uncertain.

- Cross-validation protocol clarity and potential leakage: The paper uses 10-fold cross-validation but does not state whether folds are at the document level. If mentions from the same article appear in both train and test folds, distributional overlap could inflate performance. For Dcoref++, the use of “predicted results from 10-fold cross-validation” is mentioned but still lacks clarity on document-wise isolation. A leave-one-document-out or document-level CV would better validate generalization to unseen articles.

- Dependence on deprecated/limited resources and markup availability: The approach relies on Freebase (now retired) and assumes access to Wikipedia internal links/redirects. Coverage gaps (unlinked mentions, missing/misleading redirects) and portability to settings without such markup (or to non-Wikipedia corpora) are not evaluated. The paper does not quantify how often features derived from markup are available at test time.

- Incomplete methodological details: Key training details are under-specified: exact SVM C values, feature scaling/normalization, total feature dimensionality, and whether thresholds or class weights differ by mention type. The use of Wu & Palmer semantic similarity is mentioned without specifying sense inventories or disambiguation. The greedy feature selection process appears to use the same data used for reporting, which risks overfitting.

- Baseline scope and comparisons: While CR-based heuristics are relevant, there is no comparison to simple, strong non-CR baselines (e.g., purely alias/redirect-based classifiers, or string/NER-type matching baselines leveraging the same Wikipedia/Freebase sources), which would better isolate the added value of the SVM and non-trivial features.

Questions to Authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Are folds defined at the document level? If not, can you report results with document-level CV or leave-one-document-out to assess generalization to unseen articles?

- Feature availability and coverage: What proportion of mentions have usable Wikipedia/Freebase-derived attributes (e.g., types, aliases) at test time? How does performance vary with/without internal links present?

- Training details: What C values and class weights were used for each SVM? Were features standardized? What is the total dimensionality per mention type? Were thresholds tuned on a held-out development set separate from CV?

- Wu & Palmer feature: How is semantic similarity computed between mention/title strings? Are tokens mapped to WordNet synsets, and how is sense ambiguity handled?

- Baseline breadth: Can you include a non-CR baseline that uses only article title/redirects/aliases and simple string/NER-type matching to classify mentions, to quantify the incremental value of the full feature set and SVM?

- Dcoref++ evaluation: Does the sieve prevent adding true MC mentions found later by other sieves, potentially reducing recall? Can you report per-chain precision/recall for the MC chain to complement global MUC/B3/CEAF changes?

Overall, the paper addresses a useful, clearly defined subtask with a simple, effective approach and demonstrates tangible gains, but would benefit from stronger validation on document-held-out splits, more methodological detail, and broader baselines.
