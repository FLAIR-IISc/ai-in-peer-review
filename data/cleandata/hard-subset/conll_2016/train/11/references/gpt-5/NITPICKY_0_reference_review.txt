Summary and contributions
- Task and dataset: The paper revisits “main concept” (MC) resolution in Wikipedia articles—identifying mentions that refer to the page’s subject—and introduces a small annotated dataset (WCR; 30 articles, ~60k tokens). It shows that off-the-shelf CR systems underperform on this genre.
- Method: A pair of SVM classifiers (pronominal vs non-pronominal) with features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, framed as per-mention binary classification for MC membership.
- System integration: A sieve added to Dcoref that merges chains predicted as MC, yielding higher CoNLL F1 on WCR.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and empirical evidence that genre mismatch hurts CR: Table 1 shows substantial drops of multiple state-of-the-art systems from OntoNotes to WCR. This supports the importance of adapting CR to Wikipedia.
- Practical, simple approach: Leveraging human-curated Wikipedia links and redirects (plus Freebase) is sensible and avoids a full NEL pipeline. The feature design for pronouns (position, local compatibility, tag context) is reasonable and yields strong gains (Table 2).
- Strong gains on the defined task: On MC identification, the proposed model substantially outperforms chain-aggregation baselines derived from Dcoref and Scoref (Table 4), with F1 ≈ 89 on both pronouns and non-pronominals.
- Pipeline impact: Integrating the classifier as a high-precision sieve improves Dcoref’s overall coreference metrics on WCR by ~4 CoNLL F1 (Table 5), suggesting the approach is useful beyond the auxiliary task.

Weaknesses
- Data size and labeling concerns: WCR is extremely small (30 articles). More critically, the paper states OntoNotes-style annotation without singletons, but then “considers the union of WCR mentions and all predicted mentions” for training, yielding 20,362 mentions. It is unclear how gold labels were obtained for the 13.4k added singletons. If they are unlabeled (as per OntoNotes practice), training and evaluation on their union risks label noise or evaluation bias. The paper must clarify whether evaluation excludes added singletons or how they were labeled. As written, the setup is ambiguous and threatens validity of reported P/R/F1.
- Evaluation fairness and baseline strength: Baselines B1–B4 are chain-level heuristics, not per-mention classifiers, and do not include strong lexical per-mention baselines (e.g., exact/substring match to MC title/variants/types alone). The “pronoun propagation” heuristic added to baselines may inflate recall in ways orthogonal to the task definition. Also, the model is cross-validated, while baselines appear to be single runs of Dcoref/Scoref; it is unclear whether all systems use the same mention set (gold vs predicted) and whether baselines are penalized by poorer mention detection. This muddles the comparison.
- Methodological underspecification: Several key components are not described with sufficient precision to reproduce: (i) “inferred type” extraction from the first sentence (algorithm? patterns? error rate?); (ii) how Wu & Palmer relatedness is computed between spans (especially proper names without WordNet senses); (iii) exact construction and dimensionality of tag features (e.g., “34×4 binary features” – where does 34 come from?), and 2,380 pronominal tag-bigram features on only 1,334 pronouns risks overfitting, but no regularization, scaling, or C selection details are given; (iv) which WikipediaMiner and Jena/Freebase versions; (v) how “compatible entities” for the pronoun distance feature are detected using Freebase (coverage in Wikipedia where self-links to the page subject are discouraged).
- Potential leakage/overfitting: MC representation includes redirects and aliases for the page itself; the feature families compare mention strings directly to MC title/variants/types. On a small dataset and with Wikipedia’s rich metadata, this can become close to trivial matching on many pages. No robust out-of-domain test or article-level leave-one-article-out scheme is shown to guard against dataset artifacts. No significance testing is reported.
- Incomplete and inconsistent reporting: Table 3 (non-pronominal feature ablation) is discussed but missing. Table values are slightly inconsistent (Scoref CoNLL F1 differs between Tables 1 and 5). There are multiple formatting and editorial issues (broken/unnumbered footnotes, typos, “nonanonymized version” mention, placeholder URLs such as “www.somewhere.country,” inconsistent references and notation). These issues hinder reproducibility and clarity.
- System integration details: For Dcoref++, the sieve is ranked first and prevents later sieves from appending new mentions to the MC chain. This design choice could trade off recall and adversely affect non-MC chains; the paper reports overall gains but provides no analysis of impact on non-MC chains or error propagation. Also, the sieve uses predictions from 10-fold CV on the same small dataset, raising concerns of optimistic estimates.

Questions to authors
- Labeling: How are gold labels obtained for the 13.4k automatically added mentions (singletons)? If they are unlabeled, what was the evaluation protocol for P/R/F1? If labels were heuristically assigned, please detail the procedure and quantify noise.
- Data splits: Are folds constructed at article level (i.e., no sentence/mention leakage across folds)? Please confirm that MC variants/redirects for an article are not used to train a model that is evaluated on the same article.
- Baseline parity: Do all systems (your classifier and the baselines) operate over the exact same mention set at evaluation time? Are baselines evaluated under cross-validation as well?
- Inferred type and Wu-Palmer: Describe the algorithm to infer types from the first sentence and how Wu & Palmer similarity is computed for proper nouns. What is the coverage and accuracy?
- Hyperparameters and regularization: What are the SVM C values, feature scaling, and class weights for both models? Any feature selection beyond the greedy ablation?
- Dcoref++: Does preventing later sieves from appending to the MC chain hurt recall? Please provide per-metric changes for MC vs non-MC chains, and significance tests.

Overall, while the motivation is sound and initial results are encouraging, the paper has substantial methodological ambiguities, weak baselines, a very small dataset, and numerous clarity/formatting issues. I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
