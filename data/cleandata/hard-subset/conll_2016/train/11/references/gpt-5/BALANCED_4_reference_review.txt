Summary and contributions
This paper revisits “main concept (MC) resolution” in Wikipedia: given a Wikipedia article, identify all mentions coreferring to the article’s main concept (proper, nominal, and pronominal). The authors:
1) Propose a supervised mention-level classifier (separate SVMs for pronominal vs. non-pronominal mentions) that exploits Wikipedia markup (anchors, redirects), Freebase attributes, and shallow linguistic features to decide whether a mention refers to the MC. On a 10-fold CV over a small Wikipedia corpus, the approach substantially outperforms baselines adapted from state-of-the-art CR systems.
2) Provide a comparative testbed: they show that several strong, newswire-oriented CR systems degrade markedly on Wikipedia (Table 1), construct MC-specific baselines from Dcoref and Scoref, and analyze feature groups and error cases.
3) Integrate the MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref, yielding consistent improvements on full coreference metrics (+4 CoNLL F1 on their WCR dataset).

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains on the target task: The approach achieves 89 F1 on MC identification, a +13–15 absolute F1 improvement over Dcoref/Scoref-derived baselines (Table 4). For pronouns specifically, the best model reaches 85.5/92.8/89.0 P/R/F1, greatly surpassing heuristics (e.g., “always positive” 63.7 F1, Table 2) and CR-chain baselines (e.g., Dcoref B4 77.1 F1).
- Effective use of Wikipedia structure without full entity linking: The method leverages human-created links and redirects to obtain reliable name variants, types, and coarse attributes, reducing ambiguity and cascading errors common in wikification pipelines. The feature ablation for pronouns (Table 2) shows principled gains from Wikipedia-aware features (e.g., +position, +distance).
- Demonstrated domain mismatch and practical benefit for CR: The paper quantifies the degradation of state-of-the-art systems on Wikipedia (Table 1), reinforcing the need for genre adaptation. Integrating the MC classifier as a sieve in Dcoref improves MUC/B3/CEAF and +4 CoNLL F1 (Table 5), indicating the MC decision benefits downstream clustering.
- Sensible problem formulation and feature design: Separating pronominal from non-pronominal mentions is reasonable. The MC representation (title, inferred type from lead sentence, variants via redirects/anchors, Freebase notable types) is straightforward and interpretable, and the analysis links feature families to concrete examples (e.g., “team” type helps for sports articles).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and scope: MC resolution for Wikipedia has precedents (Nguyen et al., 2007; Nakayama, 2008). The main advance is a more systematic evaluation and richer featureization rather than a new algorithmic paradigm. The sieve integration into Dcoref is incremental.
- Small, insufficiently documented dataset: WCR comprises only 30 documents (≈60k tokens), with details deferred to the non-anonymized version. There is no report of annotation quality (e.g., inter-annotator agreement), topic balance, or per-article/link-density breakdowns. This limits confidence in generalization and the robustness of the reported gains.
- Baseline design may be too tied to off-the-shelf CR and not competitive enough: All main baselines depend on CR chains (B1–B4). A strong mention-level string/semantic baseline that directly matches to MC title/redirects/inferred type without CR (plus simple pronoun heuristics) is missing; this could be competitive given the strength of Wikipedia markup (and seems close to the features used by the proposed classifier).
- Evaluation protocol clarity: The 10-fold CV splitting unit is not specified. If folds are not at the document level, information leakage is likely. Moreover, the use of Freebase/Wikipedia-derived attributes during training/test should be clarified to ensure no leakage from gold annotations or test articles into training features. Reproducibility is also complicated by reliance on Freebase (deprecated) and unspecified mapping details (e.g., Wu & Palmer similarity on mention strings—how are synsets obtained?).
- Incomplete reporting and analysis: The paper references Table 3 for non-pronominal feature ablation but does not show it; only a narrative is provided. A more thorough ablation (including on non-pronominals), per-article-type breakdown (PER/ORG/LOC/etc.), and analysis by link density would strengthen the claims. Error analysis suggests challenging cases (e.g., metonymy: “Houston” city vs. team), but mitigation strategies are not explored.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Are folds strictly at the document level? Please confirm to rule out leakage across folds (e.g., mentions or features from the same article appearing in both train and test).
- Wu & Palmer similarity: How do you map mention spans and MC fields to WordNet synsets for W&P scores? What coverage do you achieve; what is the fallback when mapping fails?
- “Distance/compatibility” features for pronouns: How exactly is compatibility defined using Freebase (e.g., gender/number/type constraints)? What happens when Freebase attributes are missing or conflicting?
- Inferred type extraction: How is the head type robustly induced from the first sentence (pattern rules, parser-based)? What proportion of articles fail this extraction?
- Baselines: Can you include a mention-level string-matching baseline using only title/redirects/inferred type on all candidate mentions (without CR chains), plus the pronoun heuristic? This seems an essential comparator for the proposed classifier.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Release plan: Given Freebase’s deprecation, consider switching to Wikidata for types/aliases and documenting mappings to sustain reproducibility.
- Generalization and robustness: Report performance by topic (biographies vs. organizations vs. locations) and by internal link density. Include an error typology (metonymy, appositives, ambiguous common nouns) and potential remedies (e.g., discourse cues, local syntax, or light entity linking).
- Integration analysis: Provide an ablation showing Dcoref++ with and without the “lock MC chain” constraint, and report how often the sieve merges incorrect chains; quantify precision/recall impact on the MC chain specifically.
- Clarity: Ensure all referenced tables/figures (e.g., Table 3) are included and fix minor typos/formatting. Clearly list all feature families and counts for both pronoun and non-pronoun models in one place.

Overall, the paper addresses a useful, underexplored Wikipedia-specific subtask and shows convincing gains with a simple, well-engineered approach. Strengthening the evaluation (stronger baselines, clearer protocol, fuller ablations) and improving dataset documentation would substantially increase its impact.
