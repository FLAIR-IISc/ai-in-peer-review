Summary and main contributions
- The paper defines and studies “Main Concept (MC) Resolution” in Wikipedia: given an article, identify all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the article’s subject. It frames this as a binary mention-level classification task and proposes feature-rich SVM models (separate for pronouns and non-pronouns) leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase attributes.
- It introduces a Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR) of 30 articles with OntoNotes-style annotations, shows that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on news underperform on this genre, and provides baselines derived from Dcoref and Scoref outputs to extract the MC chain(s).
- It demonstrates substantial improvements over baselines on the MC task (≈+13–15 F1), and shows that integrating the MC classifier as an early sieve into Dcoref yields an absolute +4 CoNLL F1 on full CR for WCR.

Strengths
- Clear, focused task formulation with practical utility. Wikipedia is pervasive in NLP; reliably recovering mentions of the article’s subject is valuable for IE and downstream tasks where intra-article references to the page topic are ubiquitous yet genre-specific. The paper isolates this subproblem and argues convincingly that generic CR systems struggle in this genre (Table 1).
- Effective use of Wikipedia structure and external knowledge without full entity linking. The feature design sensibly leverages human-created markup (anchors, redirects, title variants) and Freebase types/gender to avoid the noise/recall issues of full EL pipelines. The pronominal feature families (position, tag, distance) are intuitive and empirically useful (Table 2).
- Strong empirical gains on the defined task. On a 10-fold CV over 30 articles, the proposed classifier achieves ≈89 F1 vs best baselines in the mid-70s (Table 4), with consistent improvements for both pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. The incremental feature analysis for pronouns shows clear, reasoned contributions of each family, not just a monolithic model.
- Positive impact on full CR within a competitive system. Integrating the classifier as a high-precision sieve improves Dcoref by ≈4 CoNLL F1 points on WCR (Table 5), suggesting the MC decision helps subsequent sieves and offering a practical path to better Wikipedia CR.
- Dataset contribution and genre analysis. The WCR set, though small, is carefully sampled and used to document the drop in performance of news-trained systems on Wikipedia (Table 1), reinforcing the need for genre-adaptive approaches.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and potential train/test leakage. With only 30 documents, 10-fold CV must be done at the document level; otherwise, mention-level splits would leak article-specific features (title, variants, redirects) from train to test, inflating results. The paper does not explicitly state the fold unit, and the high F1 suggests possible leakage. Clarification and, if needed, corrected evaluation are necessary.
- Reproducibility and missing details. The dataset is “anonymized” with promised release “somewhere,” and critical preprocessing details (e.g., exact matching policy for linking mentions to hyperlink-derived attributes; coverage statistics of Freebase attributes; proportions of mentions enriched) are sparse. Table 3 referenced for non-pronominal ablations appears missing. These gaps hinder verification.
- Baseline strength and fairness. The baselines derived from Dcoref/Scoref are reasonable but may not represent the best readily-available heuristics for Wikipedia (e.g., leveraging bolded lead mentions, infobox fields, first-sentence appositives, redirects/aliases beyond substring matching). Since the proposed model exploits rich Wikipedia-specific signals, stronger Wikipedia-aware baselines would better contextualize gains.
- Model novelty is incremental. The approach is a well-engineered application of linear SVMs with handcrafted features from Wikipedia/Freebase. While effective and valuable, the methodological novelty is limited; the main contribution is empirical and resource-oriented.
- Class weighting confusion. The text states the dataset is imbalanced (non-pronominal positives ≈16%) yet “penalized the negative class with a weight of 2.0,” which is counter to standard practice (typically upweight the minority/positive class). This may be a typo or a nonstandard choice; either way, it needs justification or correction.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Are folds constructed at the document level (i.e., no article appears in both train and test within a fold)? If not, please re-run with document-level splits and report results.
- Class weighting: Did you intend to upweight the positive class? Please clarify the exact class weights used for both pronoun and non-pronoun models.
- Coverage and noise: What fraction of mentions receive attributes from hyperlink→Freebase propagation? For pronouns, how often does the “distance” feature find a compatible nearby entity? Please report coverage and error rates of these enrichment steps.
- Baselines: Did you try Wikipedia-specific heuristics such as using bolded name variants from the lead, infobox values, or exact/alias matches restricted to lead section? How do those fare vs B1–B4?
- Dcoref++ integration: Is the gain robust to different sieve orderings? Does preventing later sieves from adding to the MC chain trade off recall elsewhere (e.g., more singleton chains)? Any error analysis of changes in precision vs recall across MUC/B3/CEAF?
- Statistical significance: Are improvements in Table 4 and Table 5 statistically significant? Please report tests.

Additional comments
- The idea of inferring a common noun “type” from the first sentence is promising; detailing the extraction and its accuracy would be useful.
- Please include the full non-pronominal ablation (Table 3) and a few more failure cases. Also clarify the heuristic that propagates pronouns in baselines and whether similar global heuristics were considered for your model.
- Freebase is now deprecated; please comment on portability to Wikidata or other KBs.

Overall, this is a well-motivated, clearly executed study with strong empirical evidence on a meaningful Wikipedia-specific subtask and a tangible benefit to full CR. The main reservations are evaluation rigor (folding), dataset size/availability, and baseline strength. Addressing these would make a solid case for acceptance.
