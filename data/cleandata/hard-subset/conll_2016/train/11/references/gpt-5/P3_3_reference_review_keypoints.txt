- Reframes “main concept” (MC) resolution in Wikipedia as a binary mention classification task using Wikipedia markup and Freebase features with SVMs for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions.
- Introduces a small annotated Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR, 30 docs) and shows news-trained CR systems perform poorly on Wikipedia; designs baselines by post-processing Dcoref and Scoref outputs.
- Integrating the MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref yields a +4 CoNLL F1 improvement on WCR, showing early MC decisions help full-document CR.

- Clearly defines a practically useful, Wikipedia-specific subtask that is more tractable than full CR but beneficial for downstream IE.
- Effectively exploits Wikipedia-native signals (titles, redirects, internal links, first-sentence heuristics) and Freebase attributes for intuitive, strong features.
- Consistently large empirical gains: classifiers outperform baselines by up to +13–15 F1 for pronouns and nominals, with informative ablations on feature importance.
- Demonstrates genre mismatch: state-of-the-art CR systems trained on news degrade on Wikipedia; rule-based Dcoref can outperform statistical systems in this genre.
- Simple sieve-based integration (“Dcoref++”) is practical and yields substantial improvement (+4 CoNLL F1) by acting as a high-precision early sieve.

- WCR is very small (30 articles, ~60K tokens); limited scale and unclear representativeness across Wikipedia topics and styles; no IAA reported.
- Cross-validation design may leak MC-specific signals if not done by document; using CV predictions for integration and evaluation on same 30 docs risks optimistic estimates and lacks significance testing.
- Baselines may be weak/tailored; missing stronger deterministic baselines that use the same Wikipedia/Freebase signals to isolate classifier value from feature sources.
- Feature/source ablations are incomplete: no quantification of Freebase coverage/noise, redirect noise, or robustness when internal links/Freebase are absent.
- Technical novelty is limited—mainly a supervised, feature-engineered classifier and straightforward sieve integration—so contribution is largely empirical/engineering.
- Methodological ambiguities: unclear SVM class-weighting rationale, limited details on mention detection after adding many singletons, and possible labeling/noise impacts.
- Potential error propagation from preventing later sieves from modifying the MC chain is not fully analyzed for recall/precision trade-offs.

- Clarify cross-validation protocol: are folds by document to prevent train/test leakage, and were Dcoref++ predictions strictly out-of-fold per document?
- Provide inter-annotator agreement, annotation guidelines, and handling rules for copular/attributive links and mention detection.
- Report a deterministic baseline using title/redirects/first-sentence type/Freebase aliases plus simple pronoun rules to separate signal utility from classifier gains.
- Provide source ablations and coverage stats: fraction of mentions with internal-link attributes, fraction of MCs without Freebase, and performance without Freebase/redirects/inferred type/internal-link propagation.
- Report generalization analyses: performance by article type (PER/ORG/LOC/MISC), article length, link density, and pronoun-heavy vs nominal-heavy pages.
- Clarify SVM class-weighting intent and tuning (should positives or negatives be upweighted?).
- Analyze Dcoref++ effects: does preventing later sieves from appending to MC chains hurt recall or non-MC chains? Provide per-metric breakdowns and error analysis.

- Clarify whether “average precision” refers to macro-averaged precision or IR-style AP, and report confidence intervals/significance tests.
- Report class priors per mention type to contextualize strong baselines (e.g., “always positive” pronoun baseline).
- Discuss portability given Freebase deprecation (e.g., Wikidata) and identify minimal Wikipedia-only feature set required.
