Summary and main contributions
- The paper defines and studies Main Concept (MC) Resolution in Wikipedia: given a page, identify all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) referring to the page’s main concept. It reframes this as a binary mention-level classification problem.
- It proposes a lightweight, Wikipedia-aware approach: two linear SVMs (pronoun vs non-pronoun) using features derived from Wikipedia markup (title, redirects, internal links), Freebase attributes, inferred type from the lead sentence, and local/contextual cues.
- It provides a new small annotated dataset (WCR) of 30 Wikipedia articles and shows that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on OntoNotes degrade substantially on this genre. It further shows that injecting the MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref raises end-to-end coreference F1 on WCR.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated task with practical value. MC mentions are crucial for downstream IE/OIE on Wikipedia and for genre adaptation. The task is carefully framed to exploit Wikipedia’s structure directly rather than generic NEL, avoiding substantial ambiguity.
- Solid gains with simple models. The two SVMs, with interpretable features, deliver strong improvements over multiple baselines built from two state-of-the-art CR systems. On MC identification, +13–15 F1 points over Dcoref/Scoref-derived baselines and an extrinsic +4 CoNLL F1 on WCR when integrated into Dcoref.
- Effective use of Wikipedia signals. Leveraging titles, redirects, internal links, and lead-sentence hypernyms is sensible and largely high-precision for Wikipedia. The feature families are thoughtfully designed for pronouns (position, local compatibility) vs non-pronouns (string/variant/type matches).
- Diagnostic value for genre mismatch. The comparison of several recent CR systems on WCR versus OntoNotes quantifies the drop in performance and underscores the need for genre-specific methods; the finding that the deterministic system fares better than statistical ones on WCR is informative.
- Initial ablation and analysis. The pronoun feature ablation shows meaningful, incremental contributions; examples illustrate both successful cases and typical errors.

Weaknesses
- Small dataset and limited validation. WCR has only 30 documents; 10-fold CV implies ~3 docs per fold, which risks high variance and overfitting to idiosyncrasies of specific pages/topics. No statistical significance tests are reported for the gains. Results are presented only on WCR; there is no demonstration of robustness across a broader Wikipedia sample or other encyclopedic genres.
- Potentially uneven comparison to baselines. The paper augments the mention set by taking the union of gold WCR mentions and predicted singletons (Raghunathan et al.). It’s unclear whether baseline systems are evaluated on exactly the same candidate mention inventory or are constrained to their own detected mentions and chains. If the latter, recall disadvantages could inflate the apparent gains. This needs clarification and, ideally, a setting where all methods classify the same mention set.
- Baselines are tied to CR outputs and may be weak. The B1–B4 chain-aggregation heuristics are reasonable but depend on upstream CR quality (known to be poor on WCR). Stronger, CR-independent rule baselines (e.g., direct title/redirect/type matching with simple heuristics for pronouns and section starts) would be informative. Also, a learning baseline using only Wikipedia features (no Freebase) would help isolate the value of external KBs.
- Reliance on Freebase and noisy attribute propagation. Freebase is now deprecated; reproducibility and longevity would be better served by Wikidata. The method assigns attributes to any mention whose surface matches any internal link’s anchor text, which can be ambiguous within a page (e.g., “Houston” city vs team). A quantitative analysis of attribute noise and coverage (how many mentions receive attributes, error rates) is missing.
- Incomplete methodological clarity. Several details need tightening: how “inferred type” is extracted from the first sentence and its coverage; how Wu & Palmer similarity (WordNet-based) is applied to proper names and multiword mentions; coverage of Freebase/Wikipedia-derived attributes across entity types; per-entity-type performance (PER/ORG/LOC/MISC) and by page/link density. The non-pronominal ablation is only discussed narratively; a table comparable to the pronoun ablation would strengthen claims.
- Dcoref++ evaluation scope. The modified sieve ordering (and blocking later sieves from appending to the MC chain) may have broader implications for precision/recall trade-offs; results are only on WCR. It would be useful to know whether this harms performance on non-MC chains or in other domains, and whether improvements extend beyond the MC chain.

Questions to authors
- Evaluation protocol: Do all methods (your classifier and the CR-derived baselines) make predictions over the same set of mentions at evaluation time? If not, please provide results where all systems classify the identical mention inventory to control for mention detection differences.
- Inferred type extraction: What exact heuristic parses the first sentence (e.g., is-a patterns)? What’s the coverage/accuracy across WCR? How do you back off when the pattern fails?
- Wu & Palmer similarity: How are synsets assigned to mentions and MC fields, especially for proper names? What proportion of comparisons have a valid score?
- Attribute assignment from internal links: When multiple links share the same anchor or the same surface occurs with different referents in the same page, how do you prevent incorrect propagation? Can you report precision/recall of the coarse attributes (gender/number/NER) assigned via this mechanism?
- Freebase dependence: What fraction of mentions/MCs lack Freebase attributes? Can you report an ablation using only Wikipedia-derived features (title/redirects/lead type) and the relative drop?
- Generalization: Do results hold when increasing the number/diversity of pages? Any breakdown by entity type, article length, or link density? Are the Dcoref++ gains statistically significant and do they generalize beyond WCR?

Additional comments
- Consider replacing Freebase with Wikidata and exploiting infobox types as complementary signals.
- A stronger, CR-independent rule baseline and a full non-pronominal ablation table would improve the empirical case.
- The work is timely and practical; with clarifications and strengthened evaluation, it could be a solid contribution to genre-aware coreference and Wikipedia IE.
