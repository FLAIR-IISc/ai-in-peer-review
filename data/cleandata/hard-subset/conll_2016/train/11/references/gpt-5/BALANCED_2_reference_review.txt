Summary and contributions
This paper targets “main concept resolution” (MCR) in Wikipedia: given an article, identify all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the article’s main concept (MC). The authors (a) construct a small but annotated Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR), (b) train two SVM classifiers (separate for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, and (c) integrate the MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into a deterministic coreference system (Dcoref), reporting gains on WCR.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A focused formulation and evaluation of MC resolution in Wikipedia, with strong feature-engineered models that substantially outperform baselines derived from state-of-the-art coreference systems on WCR.
2) An integration of MC classification into a sieve-based coreference pipeline (Dcoref++), improving CoNLL F1 by 4 points on WCR.
3) A small, annotated Wikipedia dataset (WCR) and a feature analysis showing the value of leveraging Wikipedia’s markup and Freebase attributes for MCR.

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation with practical utility: Identifying the MC chain in Wikipedia is useful for downstream tasks (e.g., OIE cleaning, entity-centric summarization). The binary MCR framing is simple and well-motivated for Wikipedia, where an article’s main concept is unique and prominent.

- Strong empirical gains on the defined task: The proposed classifiers achieve ~89 F1 on MCR, substantially outperforming robust baselines derived from Dcoref and Scoref (absolute gains of 13–15 F1 points overall). The pronominal classifier ablation shows consistent improvements with each feature family (+base, +main, +position, +tag, +distance), suggesting the features capture meaningful regularities.

- Sensible exploitation of Wikipedia-specific signals: Using internal links, redirects, and first-sentence “type” along with Freebase attributes is a principled way to avoid noisy entity linking and to ground mentions in high-quality human-curated markup. The name-variant and type features are especially apt for Wikipedia.

- Integration into a full CR system: The Dcoref++ sieve indicates that early, high-precision MC decisions can beneficially structure the rest of the pipeline, yielding +4 CoNLL F1 on WCR. This demonstrates utility beyond the binary MCR task.

- Domain mismatch analysis: The comparison in Table 1 underscores how OntoNotes-trained systems degrade on Wikipedia, justifying a genre-specific approach and dataset.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and generalizability: WCR comprises only 30 documents (≈60k tokens), leading to 10-fold CV with extremely small folds. The reported gains may not generalize. There are no confidence intervals or significance tests. The diversity claims (topics, sizes, link density) are qualitative; a richer characterization and larger sample are needed for CoNLL-level claims.

- Potential leakage and reliance on markup: The method uses internal links within the same article to enrich mention attributes and to compile MC name variants and types. This can make the task easier than real-world inference, since many MC mentions (especially early or most frequent ones) may be directly or indirectly linked. The paper does not quantify link coverage (what fraction of positive mentions are linked or share surface variants with links/redirects) nor test robustness when links are sparse or removed. Similarly, Freebase attributes are not always available; coverage and fallbacks are not reported.

- Baseline adequacy: The baselines are built by post-processing outputs of general CR systems (with heuristics B1–B4). Missing is a strong dictionary/markup-only baseline that labels a mention as MC if it matches title/redirects/anchored variants and simple type heuristics, plus a pronoun heuristic. Given the power of Wikipedia markup, such a baseline may be competitive. The paper partially approximates this in features but does not report it as a baseline, limiting the interpretability of gains.

- Limited comparison to modern methods: The modeling approach is classic SVM with manual features. No comparison to stronger neural mention classifiers (e.g., contextual encoders) or to neurally enhanced CR systems, even in a simple fine-tuning setup on WCR. While prior work cited is relevant, the absence of modern baselines weakens the case for acceptance.

- Clarity on training labels and pipeline details: The authors augment gold mentions with ~13.4k predicted singletons. It is unclear how labels were assigned to these automatically extracted mentions (risk of noisy negatives/positives), whether any heuristic filtering was used, and how this impacts class imbalance and evaluation. For Dcoref++ evaluation, “predicted results from 10-fold cross-validation” are used, but more details are needed to confirm no fold leakage and how the sieve interacts with later sieves (e.g., blocking behavior and its effect on recall).

Questions to authors
- What is the coverage of internal links and Freebase attributes for mentions and MCs in WCR? Please report per-document statistics (percentage of MC mentions linked; percentage of mentions with Freebase-derived attributes; proportion of articles with reliable inferred types).

- Can you add a strong markup-only baseline: label as MC if the mention matches title/redirects/anchor variants (with token-normalization) and simple inferred-type matching; plus the pronoun-expansion heuristic? How does it compare?

- How were labels assigned to automatically extracted mentions (singletons) added to training? If a predicted mention overlaps with a gold MC mention, is it positive? Otherwise negative? What is the estimated noise rate?

- Provide variance estimates/significance tests for Table 4 and Table 5, and error bars across folds.

- How robust is the method when internal links are sparse? Can you ablate the use of internal links and Freebase to show performance degradation and quantify dependency?

- For Dcoref++, please clarify the exact sieve order, the criteria to prevent later sieves from appending mentions to the MC chain, and the impact on recall. Could a softer constraint (e.g., allowing appends above a confidence threshold) improve recall?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Expand WCR beyond 30 documents, and release it with clear licenses. Include topic splits to test cross-topic generalization and report per-topic performance.

- Report a more detailed error analysis, including pronoun resolution errors where local NP cues mislead the classifier, and errors due to ambiguous name variants.

- Consider a neural baseline (e.g., a simple contextual mention encoder with binary classification) to strengthen the empirical case.

- Provide a breakdown of performance by mention type (PER/ORG/LOC/MISC) and by link density tiers.

Recommendation
Weak reject. The task is well-motivated and the results on WCR are promising, but the dataset is too small, baselines miss a strong markup-only comparator, dependency on Wikipedia markup is not quantified, and modern baselines are absent. With a larger dataset, stronger baselines, robustness analyses, and significance testing, this work could become a solid contribution.
