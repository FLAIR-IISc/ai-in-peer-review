Summary and contributions
- The paper defines and studies Main Concept Resolution (MCR): within a Wikipedia article, identify all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the article’s main concept (MC). It frames MCR as a binary classification problem over detected mentions.
- It introduces a Wikipedia-specific feature design that leverages intrinsic markup (title, redirects, anchor text) and Freebase attributes, and trains separate SVMs for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. The approach substantially outperforms baselines derived from state-of-the-art CR systems (Dcoref, Scoref) adapted to this task.
- It provides a small but curated Wikipedia coreference resource (WCR; 30 docs, 60k tokens) and shows (i) generic CR systems suffer large drops on Wikipedia vs. OntoNotes, and (ii) integrating the MCR classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref yields a +4 CoNLL F1 improvement on WCR.

I see the primary contributions as:
1) Defining and operationalizing MCR as a task with an accompanying testbed, and showing that genre-tailored models can substantially improve over news-trained CR systems.
2) A simple, effective method that exploits Wikipedia structure directly (instead of a full EL pipeline) to recognize MC mentions, with strong gains on pronominal and nominal mentions.
3) A practical integration of the MC classifier into a full CR pipeline that improves overall CR quality on Wikipedia.

Strengths
- Novel task focus with clear utility: Elevating “main concept resolution” to a first-class task for Wikipedia is a crisp, actionable idea. Many downstream applications on Wikipedia (IE, summarization, linking, QA) will benefit from reliably picking out MC mentions, especially pronouns at section/paragraph boundaries.
- Leverages Wikipedia-native signals instead of brittle EL: The design taps title/redirects/anchors and Freebase attributes to produce high-precision features without the typical cascading errors of a separate entity linker. This is a well-motivated, bold simplification that pays off empirically.
- Strong, clear empirical gains on the target problem: The method achieves ~89 F1 on MCR with double-digit absolute gains over baselines derived from two strong CR systems, across both pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. The ablative analysis for pronouns is informative, showing cumulative benefits of MC attributes, position, and local distance features.
- Demonstrated benefit to end-to-end CR: The Dcoref++ sieve integration is simple yet effective, yielding a +4 CoNLL F1 on WCR. This shows MCR predictions can be productively injected into a broader CR pipeline.
- Genre mismatch evidence: The results in Table 1 convincingly confirm the drop of newswire-trained systems on Wikipedia, reinforcing the need for genre-specific methods and resources.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and validation: WCR has only 30 articles. While curated and diverse, this is small for a new task definition and risk of overfitting is real. There is no report of inter-annotator agreement, nor variance/confidence intervals. It is also unclear whether the 10-fold CV is by article (it should be) to avoid leakage; please clarify the split protocol. The gains are compelling, but robustness remains uncertain.
- Potential leakage through markup features: Many non-pronominal gains stem from title/redirect/anchor matching. This risks making MCR largely a string-matching task for named/nominal mentions in Wikipedia, where links and redirects are abundant. The paper does compare to baselines that use title/substrings, but a stronger ablation would quantify performance when links/redirects are masked or restricted, or when Freebase is unavailable, and how much pronominal performance depends on these signals.
- External resource dependence and longevity: Relying on Freebase (now deprecated) and WikipediaMiner may limit reproducibility and future utility. Wikidata is a more sustainable alternative. Please evaluate a setting without Freebase (or replaced by Wikidata/YAGO), and report the drop.
- Baselines and comparisons: The baselines derived from CR outputs are reasonable, but miss stronger alternatives: (i) a supervised learning baseline using only simple string/TF features without external KBs; (ii) a “wikification-only” heuristic that maps mention spans against page’s redirects/anchors; (iii) recent neural mention encoders. The work shows clear improvements over adapted CR chains, but the gap to a direct string/structure baseline is not fully established.
- Limited scope: The study is English-only and Wikipedia-specific. It is not clear whether the approach generalizes across languages (redirects and first-sentence type inference may transfer) or to other knowledge-rich encyclopedic corpora. Also, the full-CR improvement is only shown on WCR, not on a broader Wikipedia benchmark.

Questions to authors
- Splitting protocol: Is the 10-fold cross-validation done by article (i.e., no article appears in both train and test folds)? If not, please revisit to prevent leakage across mentions in the same article.
- Inter-annotator agreement: What is the IAA for mention annotations and MC coreference labels? Any adjudication protocol?
- Resource disclosure: When and where will WCR be released? What exactly is included (raw text, stand-off annotations, features)? Can you replace Freebase-related features with Wikidata and report results?
- Ablations: Please provide ablations removing (a) all link/redirect features, (b) all Freebase features, and (c) both, to quantify dependence on external structure and knowledge. For pronouns, how much performance remains without external KB-derived attributes?
- Dcoref++ sieve behavior: How often does the sieve erroneously “lock in” a wrong MC chain that blocks later corrections? Can you show precision/recall deltas per metric (MUC/B3/CEAF) and an error analysis?
- Generalization: Have you tried the classifier on other Wikipedia pages or a larger held-out set? Any preliminary multilingual experiments?

Overall, I find the paper’s core idea—elevating main concept resolution in Wikipedia and exploiting native markup—novel and useful, with a straightforward method that delivers strong gains and a promising integration into CR. The dataset is small and some evaluations could be strengthened (leakage checks, ablations, Freebase dependence), but given the novelty and potential to spur follow-on work, I lean positive, contingent on clarifications and minor extensions.
