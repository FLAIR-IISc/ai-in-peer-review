Summary and contributions
- The paper targets a focused subtask of coreference in Wikipedia: identifying all mentions of the article’s main concept (MC). It frames MC resolution as binary mention-level classification (MC vs. not), using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, with separate SVMs for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions.
- It introduces a small annotated dataset (WCR) of 30 Wikipedia articles (≈60k tokens), reports that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on news perform poorly on WCR, and builds baselines on top of those systems by heuristically selecting/aggregating chains to represent the MC.
- It shows substantial gains over baselines for MC identification (F1 ≈ 89) and demonstrates that adding a high-precision sieve based on the MC classifier to Dcoref improves full CR on WCR by +4 CoNLL F1.

In my view, the primary contributions are:
1) A practical, high-precision method for Wikipedia MC resolution leveraging Wikipedia/Freebase signals, with clear empirical gains over strong, adapted baselines.
2) An empirical study showing cross-genre degradation of CR systems on Wikipedia and a simple, effective way to improve a widely used rule-based system via an MC sieve.
3) A new annotated Wikipedia resource (WCR) for coreference and MC evaluation (pending release).

Strengths
- Clear problem formulation with impact: MC resolution is highly relevant for downstream tasks (e.g., Open IE, KB population). The binary formulation with rich Wikipedia-derived features is well-motivated and exploits domain structure few CR systems use, which is likely why it outperforms news-trained systems. The rationale is coherent and borne out in results.
- Strong empirical improvements with ablations: The paper reports consistent, sizeable gains over baselines derived from two strong CR systems (Dcoref, Scoref). For pronouns, the feature ablation is informative: adding MC coarse attributes, positional cues, and local compatibility features each brings meaningful increments, which justifies the feature design rather than a single monolithic gain.
- Integration into a full CR system: Incorporating the MC classifier as an early sieve in Dcoref yields +4 CoNLL F1 on WCR. This is a nontrivial, end-to-end improvement on a standard scorer, suggesting the signal learned for MC mentions propagates beneficially within a broader CR pipeline.
- Sensible use of Wikipedia structure to avoid noisy entity linking: Assigning attributes using human-curated hyperlinks and Freebase reduces ambiguity relative to full NEL, with plausible high precision. The approach is simple and reproducible in principle.
- Useful negative/positive qualitative analysis: The examples illustrate both where the model succeeds (e.g., leveraging inferred types) and typical failure modes (local ambiguity, section-internal references), giving credibility to the claims and pointing to future enhancements.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and representativeness: WCR has only 30 documents. While the authors state they sampled for diversity, conclusions—especially the full CR improvements—may not generalize across Wikipedia’s breadth. The small size also limits robust training of the SVMs and risks variance in cross-validation estimates. A larger testbed or evaluation on standard Wikipedia subsets would strengthen claims.
- Potential evaluation leakage and unclear cross-validation protocol: The MC features are article-level (title, redirects, inferred type). If folds split mentions rather than documents, training could see many mentions from the same article/MC as those in test, inflating performance. The paper says “10-fold cross-validation” but does not state explicitly it is document-level. This is critical given the article-specific features. Without this clarification, the reported 89 F1 may overestimate real generalization.
- Baselines may not be fully competitive for the task: All non-trivial baselines are derived from CR outputs, which we know underperform on Wikipedia. A stronger heuristic baseline would be a direct rule-based MC detector that matches title/redirects/aliases and simple type cues, plus anaphor rules for pronouns (akin to Nguyen et al., 2007), without relying on chains. This would isolate the added value of the classifier over straightforward Wikipedia-aware heuristics.
- Reproducibility details and reliance on deprecated resources: Key preprocessing decisions are under-specified (e.g., how inferred type is extracted; hyperparameters beyond class weight; exact POS/tag features). Freebase is now deprecated; practical reproduction would need Wikidata or alternative KBs. The promise of releasing data is good, but at review time, URLs are placeholders and some WCR details are deferred to a non-anonymized version.
- Limited error analysis and statistical testing: While examples are shown, there is no quantified breakdown of error types (e.g., ambiguity due to redirects, errors from attribute propagation via hyperlinks). No significance testing is provided; with small data, this matters for judging robustness.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation splits: Are the 10 folds by document or by mention? If by mention, can you re-run with document-level folds and report the MC identification metrics? This is crucial to rule out leakage from article-specific features.
- Stronger heuristic baseline: How would a purely rule-based MC resolver that uses title+redirect+aliases+inferred type matching and simple pronoun heuristics perform? Can you report it to better contextualize the classifier’s gain?
- Hyperlink-based attribute propagation: When you assign attributes from hyperlink targets to all mentions that match the link’s surface form, how do you handle ambiguity (e.g., “Houston” city vs. team)? Do you restrict propagation by context or NER type? What is the observed precision of this step?
- Resource availability and replacements: Since Freebase is deprecated, can the method straightforwardly port to Wikidata? What fields map to your “notable types,” gender, and profession features?
- Document statistics and generalization: Can you provide per-topic performance and variance across the 30 articles? Do gains hold similarly for people vs. organizations vs. locations?

Additional comments
- Table for non-pronominal feature ablation is discussed but not shown; adding it would improve transparency.
- Please report whether improvements in Dcoref++ are statistically significant under standard bootstrap or permutation tests on WCR.
- If possible, include a small-scale evaluation on held-out Wikipedia articles beyond WCR to support generalization.
