Summary and contributions
This paper targets a focused subtask of coreference in Wikipedia: identifying all mentions that refer to the article’s main concept (MC). The authors:
1) Propose a supervised mention-level classifier (separate models for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) that leverages Wikipedia markup (titles, redirects, internal links) and Freebase attributes to decide whether a detected mention refers to the MC.
2) Construct a small Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR) and show that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on OntoNotes degrade markedly on this genre; they design baselines for isolating MC chains from CR outputs and report that their classifier substantially outperforms these.
3) Integrate their MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref, yielding a 4-point CoNLL F1 improvement on WCR.

Strengths
- Clear, useful task formulation with practical impact: Framing “main concept resolution” as a binary mention classification problem is well-scoped and immediately valuable for Wikipedia-centric IE/OIE and downstream tasks where the article subject is pervasive but not always trivially identifiable (especially for pronominal and nominal mentions).
- Effective exploitation of Wikipedia structure: The system cleverly replaces noisy end-to-end entity linking with high-precision supervision from internal links, redirects, and title/alias variants. The first-sentence type heuristic and Freebase/Wikipedia types are well-motivated, and the feature families are thoughtfully designed (e.g., position within sections, local compatibility for pronouns).
- Strong empirical gains on the target task: On WCR, the model achieves large F1 improvements over multiple baselines derived from two strong CR engines (Dcoref and Scoref), particularly for pronouns. The feature ablations for pronouns are informative, showing consistent gains from main attributes, position, tags, and distance features.
- End-to-end benefit within a CR system: Incorporating the classifier as an early sieve in Dcoref (and freezing the MC chain) improves the overall CoNLL F1 across MUC/B3/CEAF, suggesting the MC chain is influential and early high-precision decisions help later sieves.
- Diagnostic evaluation of genre mismatch: The paper’s measurement of performance degradation of popular CR systems on WCR reinforces the need for genre-specific adaptation and provides a testbed for future work.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size, representativeness, and potential leakage: WCR comprises only 30 documents (60k tokens, ~7k non-singletons), which is small for supervised learning and for drawing robust conclusions. The 10-fold CV protocol is not described at document level; given that features are article-specific (title, aliases, redirects, internal links), split-by-mention folds could leak article-level supervision across folds and inflate results. Please clarify fold construction and any precautions to avoid leakage. The sampling strategy and topic diversity are asserted but not quantified.
- Baselines are weak relative to available signals: While B1–B4 probe simple chain heuristics, they underutilize the same powerful sources the model exploits. A stronger non-learning baseline would directly label a mention as MC if it matches title/redirects/aliases (partial and head matches), inferred/type-compatible nominals, plus simple pronoun rules with gender/number/section-position heuristics—i.e., a deterministic system leveraging the same markup. This comparison is essential to establish the added value of the classifier beyond string/markup matching.
- Limited analysis and incomplete reporting: Feature ablation is shown only for pronouns; non-pronominal ablations are alluded to but not reported (Table 3 is mentioned but missing). There is no breakdown by MC type (PER/ORG/LOC/MISC), by article link density, or by presence/absence of Freebase/Wikipedia types. No significance testing is provided. Error analysis is anecdotal and small-scale. These omissions reduce interpretability of where the gains come from and when the method may fail.
- Dependence on Freebase and Wikipedia-specific signals: The approach is tightly coupled to Wikipedia markup and Freebase (now deprecated), which limits portability and long-term maintainability. It is unclear how performance changes when Freebase is removed or replaced (e.g., Wikidata), or when internal link density is low (common outside lead sections). Generalization to non-Wikipedia corpora is out of scope and not discussed.
- Dcoref++ design choice and scope: Freezing the MC chain prevents later sieves from attaching mentions that the classifier missed; although precision gains dominate recall loss on WCR, the design may be brittle, and its effect on non-Wikipedia domains is unknown. It would help to quantify how many mentions are prevented from attachment and whether a softer mechanism (e.g., confidence thresholds, allowing later attachments) trades precision/recall more gracefully.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Are folds created at the document level (i.e., entire articles in either train or test)? If not, results likely suffer from leakage because article-level features (title, aliases, internal-link-derived attributes) are shared across mentions. Please clarify and, if necessary, re-run with doc-level splits.
- Stronger rule-based baseline: How does a deterministic system that uses title/redirect/alias matching, inferred type, and simple pronoun heuristics (gender/number compatibility, section-start bias) perform? This would better contextualize the learning gains.
- Ablations and coverage: Please report ablations for non-pronominal features analogous to Table 2, and provide statistics on coverage—what fraction of mentions receive attributes via internal-link matches, inferred types, or Freebase types?
- Type- and link-density-specific results: Can you break down performance by MC NER type and by article internal link density? How robust is the method on sparsely linked pages?
- Resource choices: Given Freebase deprecation, can your pipeline operate with Wikidata? What performance change do you observe if Freebase features are removed or replaced?
- Significance and variance: Are improvements over baselines and over Dcoref statistically significant across CV folds?

Overall assessment
Moderate accept if leakage is ruled out and stronger baselines/ablations are added. The task is well-motivated, the method is practical and effective within Wikipedia, and the integration into a full CR system is a meaningful contribution. However, the small dataset, potential evaluation leakage, and lack of stronger baselines/analysis currently weaken the empirical case. Addressing the methodological concerns and reporting more complete diagnostics would substantially strengthen the paper.
