Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies “Main Concept (MC) resolution” in Wikipedia articles: identifying all proper, nominal, and pronominal mentions that refer to the article’s subject. The work (i) constructs a Wikipedia coreference resource (WCR) and shows state-of-the-art (SOTA) coreference systems degrade substantially on it; (ii) proposes a supervised binary classifier (separate models for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) that leverages Wikipedia markup and Freebase-derived attributes to identify MC mentions, outperforming baselines built from SOTA CR outputs; and (iii) integrates the classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref, improving overall coreference performance on WCR.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical testbed and analysis: A dedicated Wikipedia dataset (WCR) and systematic evaluation showing large performance drops of newswire-trained CR systems on Wikipedia, along with strong baselines for MC resolution constructed atop Dcoref and Scoref.
2) MC mention classifier: A simple, effective supervised approach using features from Wikipedia structure and Freebase that achieves substantial gains over baselines, especially for pronominal mentions.
3) Downstream impact: Incorporation of the MC classifier as an early sieve in Dcoref that yields a 4-point absolute CoNLL F1 improvement on WCR.

Strengths
- Clear task definition with practical relevance and strong empirical gains
  The MC resolution task is well-scoped and useful for Wikipedia-centric NLP and IE. The proposed classifier achieves F1 ≈ 88.8 on identifying MC mentions, improving over the strongest baselines by 13–15 points (Table 4). The breakdown by pronoun vs non-pronoun and the ablation (Table 2) support the effectiveness of the features, particularly for challenging pronominal cases.

- Thoughtful use of Wikipedia markup to avoid noisy entity linking
  The method leverages human-curated hyperlinks, redirects, and aliases to derive high-precision attributes (entity type, gender/number, name variants, inferred type from the first sentence), sidestepping the precision–recall trade-offs and cascading errors common in full named-entity linking pipelines. This design choice is justified and borne out by the results.

- Comprehensive and fair baselines built from strong CR systems
  The paper constructs multiple baselines (B1–B4) over two competitive CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref), including chain aggregation and a pronoun expansion heuristic, yielding diverse precision/recall trade-offs. The proposed model outperforms these across pronominal and non-pronominal mentions, strengthening the claim of effectiveness.

- Demonstrated downstream benefit to full CR
  Integrating the classifier as the first sieve in Dcoref (Dcoref++) improves CoNLL F1 from 51.77 to 55.11 on WCR, with gains observed on MUC, B3, and CEAF as well (Table 5). This indicates that early, accurate MC decisions can positively influence subsequent sieves.

- Useful analysis and feature ablations
  The incremental feature study for pronouns (Table 2) shows steady improvements from base to +distance features and contrasts with naive “always positive” behavior. Qualitative examples illustrate both successes and typical failure modes (e.g., local NP context around pronouns).

Weaknesses
- Dataset size, representativeness, and statistical rigor
  WCR comprises only 30 documents (~60k tokens). Ten-fold CV over such a small collection (≈3 docs per fold) risks high variance and limited genre/topic coverage. No significance testing is reported for improvements over baselines or for Dcoref++, and there is limited characterization of topic distribution, article length, or link density beyond a brief statement.

- Labeling and training data construction for singletons is under-specified
  The training pool adds ~13.4k automatically extracted singleton mentions to the ~7k annotated mentions. It is unclear how labels were assigned to these singletons, given WCR annotates only non-singleton mentions. Treating unmatched predicted singletons as negative may introduce label noise and bias (e.g., missed coreferent mentions or annotation gaps). Clarification is needed to assess potential contamination and class imbalance effects.

- Limited comparison to alternative learning baselines and adaptation strategies
  The baselines rely on off-the-shelf CR outputs with heuristics; there is no comparison to simple supervised MC baselines (e.g., logistic regression with only title/name-variant features), nor to fine-tuning or domain-adapting existing CR models on WCR. This makes it hard to disentangle the contribution of Wikipedia/Freebase features versus supervised learning per se.

- Dependence on Freebase and Wikipedia-specific markup
  The approach heavily exploits Freebase attributes and Wikipedia-specific structures (redirects, internal links). Portability to other domains, to less well-linked Wikipedia pages, or to languages where markup is sparser is not evaluated. An ablation excluding Freebase (Wikipedia-only) would clarify the relative importance and robustness of external KBs.

- Missing or anonymized implementation details reduce reproducibility
  Key details are deferred to the “non-anonymized version” (e.g., WCR specifics), URLs are placeholder/anonymized, and some feature computations (e.g., title/type matching feature definitions, semantic relatedness computation) are only sketched. Document-level CV splitting and potential cross-article leakage in feature induction are not explicitly confirmed. Hyperparameter selection and any tuning protocol are only briefly mentioned.

Questions to authors
- How are labels assigned to automatically added singleton mentions in training? Are any of them positives (MC mentions) and how is that determined, given WCR annotates only non-singletons?
- Is cross-validation strictly document-level (i.e., no article appears in both train and test folds)? Are feature resources (e.g., name variants inferred from the article) computed using only the target article to avoid leakage?
- Can you report statistical significance (e.g., paired bootstrap or randomization tests) for the improvements over the strongest baselines and for Dcoref++ vs Dcoref?
- What is the contribution of Freebase vs Wikipedia-only features? Please provide an ablation removing Freebase-derived attributes.
- How sensitive is the classifier to sparse or noisy linkage (e.g., articles with few internal links)? Do results vary by article type (person vs organization vs location)?
- Could you compare against a simple supervised baseline using only title and name-variant string match features? This would clarify how much the more advanced features contribute.
- Are there plans to release WCR and code at camera-ready? Please detail dataset composition (topics, article lengths, link density) and fold splits.

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important, well-motivated Wikipedia-specific coreference subtask, provides a small but useful testbed, and demonstrates clear gains from leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase. The approach is technically straightforward and appears sound, with meaningful downstream benefits. The main limitations concern dataset scale/representativeness, incomplete methodological details, and limited comparative baselines. Addressing these would strengthen the claims and impact.
