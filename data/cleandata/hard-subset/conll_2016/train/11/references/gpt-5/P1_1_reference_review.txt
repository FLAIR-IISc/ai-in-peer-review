Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper targets “Main Concept Resolution” (MCR) in Wikipedia: given an article, identify all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the article’s main concept (MC). The authors frame MCR as mention-level binary classification with two SVMs (pronoun vs non-pronoun), leveraging features from Wikipedia markup (title, redirects, internal links), an inferred type from the first sentence, and Freebase attributes.
- They introduce a small OntoNotes-style annotated Wikipedia dataset (WCR; 30 docs, ~60k tokens, ~7k non-singleton mentions; ~1.8k MC mentions), show that off-the-shelf CR systems degrade markedly on WCR, and build baselines (B1–B4) by post-processing outputs of Dcoref and Scoref to isolate/aggregate MC chains.
- Their classifier substantially outperforms baselines on MCR (F1 ~89 vs best baselines ~76), and integrating the classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref yields a +4 CoNLL F1 improvement on full CR over WCR.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength
1) A strong empirical approach to MCR in Wikipedia that exploits Wikipedia-specific signals (markup, redirects, first-sentence type) and Freebase attributes to deliver large gains over reasonable chain-based baselines across pronouns and non-pronouns.
2) Empirical evidence and analysis that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on OntoNotes underperform on Wikipedia, plus showing how an MCR module can improve a full CR system on Wikipedia.
3) A curated Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR) and a plan to release MC-labeled mentions across English Wikipedia.

Strengths
- Clear, practically meaningful problem formulation and strong results. Identifying MC mentions is valuable for downstream IE/OIE and article understanding. The proposed models achieve sizable F1 gains (≈+13–15 absolute over Dcoref/Scoref-based baselines), with sensible ablations showing where the gains come from (e.g., title, inferred type, name variants for non-pronouns; position/distance for pronouns).
- Effective use of Wikipedia-specific supervision without full entity linking. Replacing a noisy NEL step with in-article markup and redirects is a sound design choice that mitigates pipeline errors while providing high-precision cues. The “inferred type from first sentence” is an insightful, low-cost signal and appears to complement Freebase types.
- Thorough baseline design and evaluation setup. The authors adapt two strong CR systems and propose multiple heuristics (longest chain, title-aware, chain aggregation, pronoun proliferation) that are non-trivial and stress both precision and recall. Using predicted mentions and 10-fold CV lends robustness.
- Integration into a full CR pipeline shows utility beyond the focused task. The +4 CoNLL F1 improvement of Dcoref++ on WCR demonstrates that early, high-precision MC decisions can benefit downstream sieves, not just the focused MCR metric.
- Domain insight: convincingly demonstrates significant genre mismatch effects (large drops from OntoNotes to WCR across all systems). This motivates further domain-specific CR research and provides a testbed for it.

Weaknesses
- Dataset scale and statistical rigor. WCR is very small (30 docs), raising concerns about overfitting, variance, and generality. The paper lacks statistical significance testing, error bars, or per-fold variance, and does not present learning curves. The large gains are promising but need stronger statistical support.
- Potential leakage/bias from Wikipedia links in feature construction. The method enriches mentions by matching surface strings to in-article links and transfers Freebase attributes. This can create optimistic conditions if many positive mentions share anchor strings with links to the MC or if internal links overly correlate with MC mentions. The paper should quantify: (i) the proportion of positive mentions that are linked vs unlinked; (ii) coverage of attributes from links; (iii) whether links pointing to the MC page within the article are used (Wikipedia style often discourages self-links, but redirects/aliases may still be present). Without this, it is hard to assess how much the gains rely on “gold-like” in-document supervision.
- Baseline fairness and breadth. The strongest comparisons are against post-processed outputs of CR systems that underperform on Wikipedia. Missing are stronger string/alias/type matchers that use exactly the same Wikipedia/Freebase cues as the proposed model (e.g., title/alias exact/substring match + inferred type + coarse attributes), or replication of earlier Wikipedia-specific heuristics (e.g., Nguyen et al. 2007; Nakayama 2008) as baselines. This would better isolate the incremental value of the SVM and feature interactions beyond relatively weak CR outputs.
- Missing details and clarity. The non-pronominal ablation table is only described qualitatively; full numbers would help. The algorithm to infer the first-sentence type is underspecified (patterning, error handling, constraints to ensure common nouns). Hyperparameter selection is minimally described; no info on C values, feature scaling, or per-class weighting for pronoun model. It is unclear if folds are strictly document-disjoint and how parameters were tuned without leakages across folds.
- Generality and external validity. The approach is tightly coupled to Wikipedia and Freebase (now deprecated); portability to Wikidata or other encyclopedic corpora is not discussed. The Dcoref++ result is only shown for Dcoref; no attempt to plug into Scoref or other systems. Error analysis is anecdotal; no breakdown by entity type/topic length, or by linked vs unlinked mentions.

Questions to authors
- Link-based features: What fraction of MC-positive mentions in WCR are (a) exact matches to the title/redirects/aliases, (b) covered by in-article hyperlinks (to MC or others), and (c) unlinked? How often do you copy attributes from links pointing specifically to the MC page? Please quantify coverage and potential bias.
- Inferred type extraction: Describe the exact algorithm and heuristics to derive the common-noun type from the first sentence (patterns used, POS constraints, handling of appositives and parentheticals), and its precision/recall against a small gold set.
- Statistical significance: Are the MCR gains over the best baselines statistically significant across folds? Please report variance/confidence intervals.
- Sensitivity to mention detection: How do results change with gold mentions vs predicted mentions? What is the coverage and error rate of the Raghunathan et al. (2010) mention detector on WCR?
- Dcoref++ design: Did you try allowing later sieves to append mentions to the MC chain after seeding it with your classifier (vs freezing it)? How do results change? Can you integrate the sieve into Scoref or another ML-based system?
- Resource availability: Since Freebase is discontinued, will you release derived features/attributes or scripts to query Wikidata instead? Please clarify licensing and the exact content of the planned data release (WCR, MC mention dump, feature caches).
