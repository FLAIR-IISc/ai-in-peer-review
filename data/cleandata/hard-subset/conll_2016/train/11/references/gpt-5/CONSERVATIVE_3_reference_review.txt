Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper revisits “main concept (MC) resolution” in Wikipedia articles: deciding whether a mention refers to the article’s subject. The authors frame this as binary classification and build separate SVMs for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase.
- They introduce a small Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR; 30 docs, ~60k tokens) annotated with OntoNotes-style guidelines, and show that off-the-shelf CR systems trained on newswire perform poorly on it.
- They report that their MC classifier outperforms baselines derived from two CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref) and that adding a sieve driven by their classifier to Dcoref improves CoNLL F1 by ~4 points on WCR.

Strengths
- Clear, practically motivated task within a widely used genre. Wikipedia is pervasive in downstream pipelines; showing that standard CR models degrade substantially here is useful and consistent with prior observations on genre mismatch.
- Sensible use of Wikipedia’s internal links and Freebase attributes as high-precision signals to sidestep noisy entity linking. The feature design is straightforward and leverages well-established cues (title/aliases/types/gender/number/position).
- Empirical improvements on the defined MC task. On mention-level MC identification, the proposed classifier yields sizable gains over the presented baselines (e.g., ~13–15 F1 points) for both pronominal and non-pronominal mentions, with ablation indicating which feature families contribute.
- Integration into an established CR system with measurable effect. The Dcoref++ sieve that merges MC chains yields consistent improvements across MUC/B3/CEAF on WCR, suggesting the MC signal can inform broader CR decisions.
- The paper emphasizes evaluation on Wikipedia and promises resource release, which could encourage more genre-diverse CR research.

Weaknesses
- Very small dataset and unclear experimental protocol risk overestimation. WCR has only 30 documents. The 10-fold cross-validation split is not specified as document-level; if mentions from the same document appear in both training and test folds, heavy leakage is likely, especially given that features are built from article-specific markup (title/aliases/links). This is critical to clarify, as within-document CV would substantially inflate results.
- Baselines are weak and not fully fair for the defined task. The B1–B4 baselines are indirect (chain-level heuristics derived from generic CR outputs). Simple, strong mention-level baselines are missing, e.g., “label a mention positive if it exactly/partially matches title/redirects/aliases/inferred type.” Given the feature space, such baselines might close much of the reported gap. Moreover, the authors explicitly avoid training modern statistical CR models on WCR, stating this is left for future work—this undermines the comparative claims.
- Labeling and class weighting details are problematic. The training set includes ~13.4k automatically extracted singleton mentions added to gold coreferent mentions, but the labeling of these added mentions is not described. Are all added singletons assumed negative unless matched to MC? If so, noise is likely. Further, the paper states “we penalized the negative class with a weight of 2.0” despite negatives being the majority—this seems reversed from standard practice and requires justification or correction.
- Heavy reliance on external structured resources and potential brittleness. The approach depends on Wikipedia’s internal links/redirects and Freebase attributes. Coverage, noise (e.g., redirect quality), and failure modes when attributes are missing are not quantified. It is unclear how robust the system is on articles with sparse linking or entities with poor Freebase coverage.
- Incomplete reporting and statistical rigor. No statistical significance testing is reported. Table 3 is referenced for non-pronominal feature ablations but not included. The “inferred type” extraction from the first sentence is underspecified. Error analysis is anecdotal and limited. The Dcoref++ improvement is shown only on the small WCR set; impact on broader CR benchmarks is unknown.

Questions to authors
- How were CV folds constructed? Please confirm that folds are by document, with no cross-document leakage of mentions or article-level features (title/aliases/links/types) across train/test. If not, please re-run with document-level splits.
- How were the automatically added singleton mentions labeled for training and evaluation? What proportion of these are positives/negatives, and what is the estimated noise?
- Class weights: why penalize the negative class 2.0 when it is the majority? Is this a typo (did you mean positive)? Please report sensitivity to class-weighting.
- Mention-level baselines: what are the results of simple string/alias/type matching at the mention level, independent of a CR system (e.g., exact/substring match to title/redirects/aliases; match to inferred/common noun type)? These seem necessary to contextualize your gains.
- Resource dependence: what percentage of mentions lack usable Freebase attributes or internal link-derived attributes? How do results change when Freebase-derived features are ablated?
- Dcoref++ sieve: does freezing the MC chain prevent correct attachments by later sieves in some cases? Any analysis of errors introduced by this constraint?

Additional comments
- Please include the missing Table 3 and provide full details on the inferred-type induction algorithm.
- Report statistical significance for key improvements and provide per-article variance to assess robustness on this small dataset.
- Consider retraining an established CR model on WCR (even a simple mention-ranking baseline) to better assess the contribution of MC features relative to learned baselines.
- Clarify reproducibility: exact versions of Wikipedia/Freebase used, preprocessing, and code to derive features (especially inferred type and alias extraction).
- If possible, add a small held-out set from different Wikipedia topics to evaluate generalization.

Overall, the problem is relevant and the approach is practical, but the current evaluation suffers from scale, potential leakage, and weak baselines. Strengthening the experimental protocol and comparisons is necessary to support the claims.
