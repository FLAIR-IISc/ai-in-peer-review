Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles Main Concept (MC) Resolution in Wikipedia: given an article, identify all mentions (named, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the article’s subject. The authors (i) construct a testbed (WCR) of 30 Wikipedia articles annotated with OntoNotes-style coreference and Freebase links, (ii) propose simple SVM classifiers (separate for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) that leverage Wikipedia markup and Freebase-derived attributes to decide whether a mention refers to the MC, and (iii) demonstrate that plugging their MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Stanford’s deterministic coreference system (Dcoref) improves end-to-end coreference on Wikipedia.

Strengths
- Well-scoped, practically motivated task with clear downstream utility. Framing “subject-of-the-page” resolution as a binary, per-mention decision is crisp and aligned with many information extraction and reading tasks on Wikipedia. The paper convincingly shows that off-the-shelf coreference systems trained on news underperform on Wikipedia (Table 1), underlining the need for domain-adapted approaches.

- Effective use of high-precision, low-cost knowledge. The approach cleverly exploits human-curated structures (titles, redirects, internal links) and coarse attributes from Freebase to avoid the error-prone, low-recall entity linking step used in prior CR+KB works. The ablations are informative: for non-pronominals, title/inferred type/variants/entity type features provide substantial gains; for pronouns, document structure (position) and local-compatibility features help substantially (Tables 2–4).

- Strong empirical gains on the target task. The proposed classifier outperforms carefully designed baselines derived from two strong CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref) by 13–15 absolute F1 points on MC identification, with especially high recall on pronouns and high precision on non-pronominals (Table 4). The analysis includes qualitative examples illustrating both successes and failure modes.

- Integration into a full CR pipeline yields measurable improvements. Introducing the MC sieve first in Dcoref improves CoNLL F1 by 4 points on WCR (Table 5), with precision increases across MUC, B3, and CEAF. This demonstrates that early, high-confidence MC decisions can beneficially steer subsequent sieves.

- Dataset and planned release. A new Wikipedia-genre resource annotated with OntoNotes-style guidelines and Freebase links is valuable; the promise to release data and an MC-mention dump would facilitate follow-up work.

Weaknesses
- Small dataset and unclear split protocol. WCR has only 30 documents (∼60k tokens). More critically, the 10-fold cross-validation for MC classification appears to be at the mention level; if folds are not split by document, per-article features (e.g., title, inferred type, name variants) may leak across train/test, inflating performance. The Dcoref++ experiment also appears to use CV predictions on the same 30 docs. Results should be reported with leave-one-article-out or document-level CV; significance tests would strengthen claims.

- Potentially weak or incomplete baselines. All baselines are built on top of CR outputs, which are known to be poor on Wikipedia in this setting. Stronger, CR-independent baselines should be included: e.g., mark a mention as MC if its head or span overlaps the title/redirects/aliases; a simple mention-level classifier using only lexical and positional features; or a high-precision alias-matching heuristic. Since title-based features dominate the gains, a direct string/alias baseline is a crucial comparison point.

- Reliance on Freebase and internal links limits portability and may bias evaluation. Freebase is deprecated and coverage is uneven; although the authors show gains from “inferred type” from text, the approach still leans heavily on Freebase and internal links to assign attributes to mentions (Section 4.1). The fraction of linked vs unlinked mentions is not reported; performance on unlinked mentions should be broken out. The method is tightly bound to Wikipedia and may not generalize to other encyclopedic sources or low-link-density pages.

- Some methodological details are underspecified. It is unclear how “name variants” are assembled and whether anchor texts within the same article are included, potentially blurring the line between features and supervision. The use of Wu–Palmer similarity requires sense inventories; how is this computed for arbitrary surface strings? Feature dimensionality vs sample size and regularization choices (beyond class weighting) are not discussed; risk of overfitting is non-negligible given 20k mentions and thousands of binary features.

- Limited analysis of impact on non-MC chains. Dcoref++ freezes the MC chain early and prevents later sieves from appending mentions. While overall CoNLL F1 improves, more granular analysis is warranted: how often are non-MC chains harmed? What proportion of the gain is attributable to improved MC chains versus collateral effects on other entities? An oracle MC sieve upper bound would contextualize headroom.

Questions to authors
- How are the CV folds constructed? Please confirm that folds are at the document level (no article appears in both train and test). If not, can you report leave-one-article-out results for both MC classification and Dcoref++?

- What exactly goes into “name variants”? Do you include anchor texts from the same article when compiling variants for the MC? If so, how do you avoid circularity where the presence of a link surface essentially supervises the label?

- What is the proportion of linked vs unlinked mentions in WCR? Can you report performance separately on linked and unlinked mentions for both pronominal and non-pronominal cases?

- How is Wu–Palmer similarity computed between the MC fields and mention spans (sense inventory, disambiguation)? What is its incremental contribution in ablations?

- Can you add CR-independent baselines, especially: (i) pure title/redirect/alias string overlap; (ii) “first NP in each sentence” or “first NP in a paragraph” heuristics; and (iii) a local lexical+position classifier without KB features?

- For Dcoref++, are the classifier decisions produced on held-out folds for each article? Could you provide an error analysis of cases where mislabeling MC mentions causes incorrect chain merges, and an oracle experiment where the true MC chain is fixed to quantify potential gains?

Additional comments
- Consider replacing Freebase with Wikidata and evaluating the system’s sensitivity to KB coverage. Report results with/without KB features.

- The task and resource are useful; scaling WCR beyond 30 articles, adding per-topic splits, and reporting significance would materially strengthen the empirical claims.

- Releasing code and data (with clear licensing) is essential for impact.
