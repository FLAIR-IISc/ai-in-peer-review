Summary and main contributions
- This paper targets a Wikipedia-specific subtask of coreference: identifying the set of mentions in an article that refer to its main concept (MC). The authors frame MC resolution as binary classification over mentions (pronominal vs. non-pronominal handled by separate SVMs) using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, and show that this approach outperforms baselines derived from state-of-the-art general CR systems.
- They introduce a small annotated Wikipedia coreference resource (WCR; 30 docs, ~60k tokens) and demonstrate that off-the-shelf coreference systems trained for newswire perform substantially worse on this domain (e.g., CoNLL F1 drops from ~64 on OntoNotes to ~46–52 on WCR).
- They show that incorporating their MC classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref improves end-to-end coreference on WCR (+3.34 CoNLL F1, 51.77 to 55.11).

Strengths
- Clear task formulation and practical relevance. Framing “main concept resolution” as a binary classification problem for Wikipedia is well-motivated and practically useful (e.g., improving OIE subject/object quality). The paper provides a concrete, testable definition and evaluation setup.
- Effective use of Wikipedia-specific signals. The feature design smartly leverages high-quality human-curated signals (internal links, redirects, first-sentence type, Freebase notable types/attributes). This avoids the precision/recall pitfalls of generic entity linking pipelines and yields strong gains: overall MC F1 88.79 vs. best baselines 75.77 (Dcoref-derived) and 73.69 (Scoref-derived), and large improvements for pronouns (88.99 F1).
- Thorough comparisons and ablations. The paper constructs reasonable baselines from two strong CR systems (rule-based Dcoref and statistical Scoref) with multiple heuristics (B1–B4), and provides family-wise feature ablations. For pronouns, F1 increases steadily from 74.11 (base) to 88.99 (+main, +position, +tag, +distance). For non-pronominals, title, inferred type, name variants, entity type, and main features cumulatively increase both precision and recall with plausible explanations.
- Empirical evidence of domain mismatch. Table 1 convincingly demonstrates the degradation of general CR systems on Wikipedia (e.g., Scoref 63.61 on OntoNotes vs. 46.39 on WCR), underscoring the need for genre-adapted approaches. Interestingly, rule-based Dcoref outperforms statistical systems on WCR, which the paper notes and explores.
- Integration into a full CR pipeline. The Dcoref++ sieve that consolidates MC chains shows consistent gains across MUC, B3, and CEAFφ4 (e.g., CoNLL F1 +3.34 absolute), demonstrating that high-precision early MC decisions can beneficially constrain later sieves.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset size and diversity. WCR comprises only 30 documents (~7k nonsingleton mentions; ~1.8k MC mentions). While the authors sampled across topics, the small size raises concerns about generalization, overfitting to Wikipedia conventions, and robustness of cross-validation. No statistical significance tests are reported for improvements across folds.
- Reliance on Wikipedia-specific markup and Freebase. The approach heavily depends on internal links, redirects, first-sentence type, and Freebase attributes. This limits portability to other domains or less richly annotated wikis. Freebase is deprecated; the paper does not discuss mapping to Wikidata/DBpedia or handling missing attributes. The reported gains may partially reflect the presence of explicit links to the MC (style guidelines often link the first occurrence), which risks making parts of the task trivial without analyzing how often links alone suffice.
- Possible leakage or trivial cues not fully controlled. The MC representation includes name variants “extracted from the markup of Wikipedia (redirects, text anchored in links)” and the mention preprocessor assigns attributes to mentions that match link surface forms. It is unclear whether mentions that are explicitly linked to the MC page are included in evaluation and how much performance depends on those trivial cases versus unlinked mentions. A controlled analysis excluding anchor-linked mentions to the MC would strengthen the claims.
- Baselines could be stronger and more varied. All baselines are heuristic aggregations of chains from Dcoref/Scoref plus a heuristic for pronoun proliferation. There is no supervised, genre-adapted baseline that uses only shallow features (e.g., linear classifier over counts/lexical features without KBs) nor a pipeline with a modern entity linker to Wikipedia (to compare against the authors’ “no-EL” design). This makes it harder to isolate the benefit of the specific feature families.
- Incomplete methodological details. Important implementation specifics are missing or brief: how “inferred type” is extracted from the first sentence (patterns, coverage, error rates); details of the “distance” feature for pronouns (compatibility function, reliance on Freebase types); handling of errors in mention detection (the union of predicted and gold mentions may bias training); and whether cross-validation folds were document-stratified to avoid leakage across redirects/aliases.

Questions to authors
- How large is the subset of MC mentions that are explicitly linked (anchor text) to the MC page? What is performance when those mentions are excluded from evaluation or when features derived from anchors pointing to the MC are disabled? This would quantify dependence on markup.
- Please clarify the “inferred type” extraction procedure from the first sentence: pattern rules, coverage, and error rate. How often does it disagree with Freebase notable types, and which source is used when they conflict?
- For the pronoun “distance” features: how is compatibility determined (exact type match, gender/number, profession)? Do these rely on Freebase attributes for nearby mentions, and how robust is this when Freebase info is missing?
- Cross-validation: are folds by document, ensuring no redirect/alias leakage across train/test for the same MC? Did you conduct any significance testing on F1 improvements across folds?
- Dcoref++ sieve: beyond MC chain quality, does the sieve harm recall or precision on non-MC chains? Can you report per-chain metrics or error analysis showing typical changes to other entities’ clusters?
- Freebase dependency: have you tried replacing Freebase with Wikidata/DBpedia? If not, what proportion of your features would remain available, and how would you expect performance to change?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider releasing a larger WCR variant and reporting results on subsets by topic and article length. Include significance tests.
- Add a supervised baseline without KB features (e.g., lexical counts, TF-IDF similarity to title) and/or with a lightweight entity linker to better isolate the contribution of Wikipedia/Freebase signals.
- Provide an analysis separating performance on unlinked vs. linked MC mentions, and by mention type (proper, nominal, pronominal).
- Discuss how to port the method to Wikidata and to other encyclopedic or domain-specific wikis.

Overall, the paper addresses a useful, underexplored task, shows solid engineering and clear gains, and offers actionable insights for Wikipedia-domain CR. Strengthening the experimental controls and clarifying dependencies on markup/Freebase would further solidify the contribution.
