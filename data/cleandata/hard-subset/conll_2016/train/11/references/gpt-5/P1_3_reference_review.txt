Summary and contributions
This paper targets “Main Concept Resolution” (MCR) in Wikipedia: identifying all mentions (proper, nominal, pronominal) that refer to the subject of the page. The authors (i) construct a small but carefully annotated Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR), and show state-of-the-art CR systems degrade substantially on it; (ii) propose a simple, feature-based binary classifier (separate models for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) that leverages Wikipedia markup and Freebase attributes to decide whether a mention refers to the page’s main concept; and (iii) integrate the classifier as an early sieve in a rule-based CR system (Dcoref), improving overall CoNLL F1 on WCR.

Main contributions as I see them:
1) Empirical demonstration and testbed: a new Wikipedia-focused CR dataset (WCR) and evidence that off-the-shelf CR models trained on newswire underperform on Wikipedia.
2) A practical, high-performing MCR model that exploits Wikipedia/Freebase signals, substantially outperforming baselines built from two strong CR systems.
3) A simple integration of the MCR classifier into Dcoref that improves full coreference metrics on Wikipedia.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated task with practical impact. Resolving the page’s main concept is a frequent subroutine in IE/KBP on Wikipedia. Framing it as a binary mention-level classification task is crisp and actionable. The demonstrated gains (overall F1 ≈ 88.8 vs ≈ 75 for baselines) suggest strong utility.

- Effective use of Wikipedia structure and external KB attributes. The feature design is sensible and grounded in prior work: title/aliases/redirects, inferred types from first sentence, Freebase notable types/gender/number, plus positional and local distance features for pronouns. Ablations show these features matter and complement each other (e.g., title and type families boost precision/recall as expected).

- Solid empirical comparisons and ablation. The authors construct multiple baselines from two strong CR systems (Dcoref, Scoref) with reasonable heuristics for isolating the MC chain and pronoun propagation. They also present stepwise feature additions with clear gains, and error examples to illustrate failure modes.

- Integration into a broader CR pipeline. Incorporating the classifier as a high-precision sieve in Dcoref improves CoNLL F1 by about 3.3–4 points on WCR (51.77 → 55.11), with consistent gains across MUC/B3/CEAFφ4, showing that early, targeted decisions about the MC can help full-document CR.

- Reproducibility intent. The authors plan to release WCR and a large dump of predicted MC mentions and associated features, enabling follow-up work and fair comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and scope are limited. WCR contains only 30 documents (~60k tokens), with 1,800 MC mentions among ~7,000 coreferent mentions. While the pages are said to be varied, conclusions and stability of improvements on larger or more diverse Wikipedia subsets (and other languages) are unclear. Reported gains could be sensitive to this small sample.

- Ambiguity in training labels for added singletons and potential leakage. The authors add ~13.4k automatically extracted singleton mentions to training but WCR lacks singleton annotations. It is unclear how these additional mentions were labeled (e.g., all as negative unless aligned?) and whether this introduces bias. Moreover, the 10-fold cross-validation setup is not specified as document-level; if folds are at the mention level, training and test mentions from the same article could leak article-specific distributions and inflate results.

- Baselines may be weaker than necessary given available knowledge. The classifier exploits redirects, aliases, inferred types, and Freebase attributes. The baselines only partially tap similar knowledge (exact/substring title matches; limited chain aggregation; pronoun propagation). A stronger, rule-based baseline that fully uses redirects/aliases/types (including first-sentence type and Freebase attributes) without learning would be a fairer comparator to isolate the benefit of learning vs knowledge.

- Dependence on Freebase and existing markup. The approach assumes high-quality internal links and relies on Freebase for attributes (gender, type), which can be incomplete or noisy; Freebase is also deprecated. Robustness to missing/erroneous links, redirects noise (noted by the authors), and alternative KBs (e.g., Wikidata) is not evaluated.

- Limited analysis and statistical testing. While some error cases are discussed, deeper breakdowns (by mention type, entity class, presence/absence of links, page length/link density) and significance testing of improvements are missing. The Dcoref++ changes (e.g., prohibiting later sieves from appending to the MC chain) warrant more analysis on precision/recall trade-offs and potential adverse effects on non-MC chains.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Are folds split by document (i.e., leave-whole-articles-out) to prevent training and test mentions from the same article? If not, please re-run with document-level splits and report results.

- Labeling of added singletons: How were the ~13.4k automatically extracted mentions labeled for training/evaluation, given WCR lacks singleton annotations? Were any heuristics used to assign ground-truth MC labels to these singletons?

- Stronger knowledge-driven baseline: Can you provide a rule-based system that uses exactly your knowledge sources (title + redirects/aliases + inferred type + Freebase coarse attributes) to classify mentions as MC, without SVM, to quantify the added value of learning?

- Robustness to missing/noisy KB signals: What happens if Freebase attributes are removed or replaced with Wikidata? Can you report ablations without Freebase and/or without internal links?

- Split of pronominal vs non-pronominal performance across entity categories: Do gains hold uniformly across PER/ORG/LOC/MISC, and across pages with sparse vs dense linking?

Additional comments
- Please clarify whether pronoun-propagation heuristics were applied only to baselines and not to your classifier; if so, explain why this does not disadvantage baselines or provide a variant for parity.
- Consider reporting per-article variance and correlation with page link density/length. 
- If possible, add significance tests for main comparisons. 
- Freebase deprecation: a brief note on substituting Wikidata would improve longevity.
