Summary and main contributions
- Defines and operationalizes a focused subtask: Main Concept (MC) Resolution in Wikipedia articles (identify all mentions referring to the article’s subject), arguing for its utility and distinctiveness from generic CR.
- Builds a dedicated Wikipedia coreference resource (WCR), and a testbed for MC resolution. Demonstrates that state-of-the-art CR systems trained on newswire degrade substantially on Wikipedia.
- Proposes a simple, feature-driven SVM classifier (separate for pronominal vs. non-pronominal) that leverages Wikipedia markup and Freebase-derived attributes to decide whether a mention refers to the MC; achieves large gains over strong baselines and improves a full CR system (Dcoref) when integrated as a high-precision sieve.

Strengths
- Clear, novel task definition with practical value: Framing MC resolution as a binary mention-level classification problem in Wikipedia is crisp and actionable. The task is motivated by downstream uses (IE/OIE, knowledge extraction), and the paper shows how MC resolution can plug into broader CR systems to yield end-to-end gains.
- Effective use of structured knowledge inherent to Wikipedia: The method smartly leverages internal links, redirects, titles, and Freebase attributes to build rich features without a full-blown entity linker. This reduces ambiguity and appears robust for this domain. The feature families are well-motivated (title, type, variants, entity type, positional cues, local distance signals).
- Strong empirical gains on a targeted benchmark: On MC resolution, the approach reaches ~89 F1, a +13–15 absolute improvement over baselines derived from two competitive CR systems (Dcoref/Scoref). Ablations on pronominal mentions show consistent improvements as feature families are added. The method also yields a meaningful +4 CoNLL F1 improvement when inserted as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref on WCR.
- Useful resource and insight about domain shift: WCR, though small, covers multiple article types and shows an important result: SOTA CR trained on OntoNotes performs much worse on Wikipedia (e.g., Scoref 63.6 on OntoNotes vs. 46.4 on WCR), reinforcing the need for domain-targeted approaches.
- Simplicity and reproducibility: A linear SVM with interpretable features that can be integrated into existing pipelines is attractive. The promise to release the dataset and large-scale outputs (MC mentions across English Wikipedia) should catalyze further work.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and split protocol: WCR is only 30 documents. It is unclear whether the 10-fold cross-validation splits are by document or by mention. If mentions from the same article appear in both train and test, scores may be inflated due to within-article regularities and shared MC features. A leave-one-article-out or document-level CV would be a stronger evaluation.
- Potential leakage via intra-article links: The preprocessing assigns attributes to mentions based on internal hyperlinks. Many MC mentions in Wikipedia are linked (especially the first occurrence). Using link-derived attributes during classification could make some cases trivial and may correlate strongly with gold labels. The paper does not quantify performance separately for linked vs. unlinked mentions or show ablations that remove link-derived features.
- Limited baselines and contemporaneity: Baselines are largely heuristics on top of Dcoref/Scoref outputs. There is no learning baseline trained on WCR for the same MC task (e.g., a simple logistic regression with string overlap and frequency features, or a neural mention classifier), nor a direct entity-linking-based heuristic baseline. Given the reliance on markup, such baselines are important to establish the added value of the full feature set. Also, Freebase is now deprecated; portability to Wikidata or beyond Wikipedia isn’t addressed.
- Incomplete clarity and minor presentation issues: Table numbering is inconsistent (Table 3 referenced but not shown), and some feature details are underspecified (e.g., how Wu & Palmer similarity is computed between arbitrary strings; how “inferred type” is extracted from the first sentence; exact normalization and stopword handling in substring/overlap matches). Error analysis is anecdotal; more systematic breakdowns (by entity type, article type, link density) would help.
- Generalization beyond Wikipedia: The approach is highly tailored to Wikipedia’s structure. While this is the point of the task, discussion of transferability (e.g., to Wikidata-based corpora, other encyclopedic sites, or Wikipedia in other languages) is minimal.

Questions to authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- How are the CV folds constructed? Are they by document or by mentions pooled across documents? Please report document-level CV and/or leave-one-article-out results.
- What fraction of MC mentions are anchor-linked vs. unlinked? What is performance on linked vs. unlinked mentions? Does removing link-derived features substantially change results?
- For mentions whose anchor links point directly to the MC page, do you mask this information to avoid trivial identification? If not, can you provide ablations that exclude features derived from links to the MC itself?
- Can you detail how the “inferred type” from the first sentence is extracted and normalized, and how Wu & Palmer similarity is computed for mention/title strings (synset disambiguation, coverage)?
- Can you add a learning baseline trained on WCR using only string overlap/frequency and positional features (no Freebase/Wikipedia attributes), and an entity-linking-based heuristic baseline, to better isolate the gains from your feature design?
- Since Freebase is deprecated, can your pipeline be adapted to Wikidata? If so, what changes are needed, and how would that affect attribute coverage?

Overall recommendation
This paper proposes a focused, useful task with a simple, well-engineered solution that leverages Wikipedia structure and yields strong gains, plus a small but valuable dataset and an integration that improves full CR. Despite concerns about dataset size and potential intra-article leakage, the direction is novel and promising. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying evaluation splits and mitigating/link-ablating analyses.
