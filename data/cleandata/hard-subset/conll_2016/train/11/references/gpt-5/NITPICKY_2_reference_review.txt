Summary and main contributions
- The paper targets “Main Concept Resolution” (MCR) in Wikipedia: identifying all mentions in an article that refer to the article’s main concept (MC). It frames this as a binary classification task over mentions.
- It proposes two linear SVM classifiers (for pronominal and non‑pronominal mentions) that leverage Wikipedia markup (title, redirects, internal links) and Freebase metadata as features, and shows large gains over baselines derived from state‑of‑the‑art CR systems (Dcoref, Scoref) on a new Wikipedia dataset (WCR).
- It integrates the MC classifier as a high‑precision sieve into Dcoref, reporting improvements on standard CoNLL coref metrics on WCR.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated task: The paper isolates a practically relevant subtask specific to Wikipedia, where generic CR systems underperform. Quantifying the performance drop of several CR systems on WCR is informative and motivates the need for domain‑specific adaptation.
- Simple but effective approach: The feature design capitalizes on Wikipedia’s rich markup and yields strong F1 for MC identification, especially for pronouns. The incremental ablation for pronominal features is sensible and shows consistent gains.
- Demonstrated downstream impact: Incorporating the MC classifier as a sieve into Dcoref improves CoNLL metrics on WCR by ~4 F1 points, suggesting useful interactions with a full CR pipeline.
- Use of readily available signals: Leveraging internal links, redirects, and Freebase attributes avoids noisy entity linking and appears to provide robust cues for Wikipedia‑specific CR.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage in evaluation: The paper performs 10‑fold cross‑validation but does not specify whether folds are at the document level. Given heavy reliance on article‑level signals (title, internal links, MC attributes), any cross‑article mixing at the mention level would lead to severe train/test contamination and inflated results. This is a critical methodological gap that must be clarified and, if needed, corrected with document‑level CV or leave‑one‑article‑out evaluation.
- Unfair comparison to baselines: The proposed classifiers exploit human-curated internal links and Freebase attributes at test time; the baselines (Dcoref/Scoref and their variants) operate on raw text without access to the same signals. This is not an apples-to-apples comparison. Stronger baselines are needed, e.g., (i) a purely string/redirect/alias baseline (title + redirects + section headings + anchor texts) without any CR, (ii) Dcoref/Scoref augmented with the same wiki-derived attributes, or (iii) a simple rule system that uses the same Wikipedia signals.
- Small dataset with limited evidence: WCR has only 30 documents (~60k tokens). There is no external test set, no significance testing, and limited error analysis. Generalization beyond this small sample is unclear. The claim that the sample is “representative” is not substantiated (no statistics on topic distribution, link density, entity types).
- Reproducibility concerns: The dataset is “to be described in the non‑anonymized version,” links are placeholders, and many implementation details are underspecified: exact feature definitions (the 10 boolean string-comparison features), how MC “inferred type” is extracted, how Wu & Palmer similarity is computed (sense disambiguation?), SVM hyperparameters (C value, regularization, class weights beyond “2.0”), and the precise construction and labeling of added singleton mentions. Freebase is deprecated; no guidance on replacing it (e.g., Wikidata). The promised data dump link is a placeholder.
- Missing/inconsistent content and formatting issues: Figures 1–2 are referenced but not present; Table 3 (non‑pronominal ablation) is referenced but missing; minor inconsistencies between reported numbers for Scoref; numerous typos, awkward phrasing, and intrusive footnotes in the text break readability. These collectively raise concerns about the completeness of the submission.

Questions to authors (to be addressed in rebuttal)
- What is the unit of cross-validation splitting? Please confirm that folds are strictly at the document level (no mentions from the same article in both train and test). Provide exact fold assignments or statistics.
- What fraction of mentions receive attributes via internal links/Freebase? Report coverage for entity type, gender, number, aliases, etc., separately for pronominal and non‑pronominal mentions.
- How are the 13.4k automatically extracted singleton mentions labeled for training? Are any MC mentions present as singletons in WCR? Could your labeling introduce systematic bias?
- Provide a baseline using only title/redirects/aliases (no CR engine) and report its P/R/F1 versus your classifier. Also evaluate an ablated version of your model without internal links (text-only) to quantify the contribution of Wikipedia signals.
- Detail the “inferred type” extraction from the first sentence, the 10 boolean string similarity features, and the Wu & Palmer computation (resource, sense selection). Specify SVM C and class weighting.
- For Dcoref++, do you prevent adding mentions only for the MC chain, or for all chains touched by the new sieve? Provide precision/recall impacts per metric and per mention type.

Overall recommendation
While the task is interesting and the approach promising, the experimental design and fairness concerns (especially potential cross‑article leakage and non‑comparable baselines) undermine the validity of the reported gains. Coupled with the small dataset, missing details, and reproducibility gaps, I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form. A revised version with document‑level evaluation, stronger baselines using the same signals, fuller methodological detail, and released resources would be much stronger.
