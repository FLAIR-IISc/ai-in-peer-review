## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

This paper revisits the task of identifying mentions of the main concept within a Wikipedia article, a task surprisingly underexplored despite Wikipedia’s prominence in NLP. The authors address this gap by framing the problem as a binary classification task and leveraging Wikipedia’s markup and links to external knowledge bases (Freebase) to build a classifier. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that this approach significantly outperforms baseline coreference resolution systems on this specific task, and improves performance when integrated into a rule-based coreference pipeline.

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The problem itself is well-motivated – adapting coreference resolution to Wikipedia is crucial given its widespread use. The experimental setup, including the creation of a dedicated dataset (WCR), is commendable. The feature engineering, drawing from both coreference literature and Wikipedia-specific characteristics, appears thoughtful and effective. The results clearly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach, with substantial F1 gains over baselines. Furthermore, the ablation study (Table 2) provides valuable insight into the contribution of different feature families. The inclusion of results on a full coreference resolution pipeline (Dcoref++) further strengthens the practical relevance of the work.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The dataset size (30 documents) is relatively small, raising concerns about the generalizability of the results. While the authors acknowledge this, a larger, more diverse dataset would significantly bolster the findings. The description of the dataset creation process is somewhat limited; more details on annotation guidelines and inter-annotator agreement would be beneficial. The paper could benefit from a more thorough error analysis, beyond the few examples provided in Figure 3. Understanding *why* the classifier fails in certain cases would inform future improvements. Finally, the writing style is occasionally dense and could be streamlined for clarity.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the annotation process for the WCR dataset, including inter-annotator agreement scores?
2.  What is the size of the dataset of mentions you plan to share, and what format will it be in?
3.  The paper mentions noisy redirects in Wikipedia impacting performance. Have you considered any techniques to filter or weight these redirects differently?



Overall, this is a solid paper that addresses an important problem with a well-designed approach. Despite the limitations related to dataset size, the clear results and insightful analysis make a compelling case for the effectiveness of the proposed method. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions above.
