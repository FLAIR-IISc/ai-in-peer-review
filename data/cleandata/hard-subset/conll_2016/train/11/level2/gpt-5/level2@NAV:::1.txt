Summary
The paper targets “main concept resolution” in Wikipedia: identifying all mentions in an article that refer to the article’s main concept (MC). The authors propose two SVM classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. They compare against baselines built from state-of-the-art coreference systems (Dcoref, Scoref) adapted via heuristics, and show sizable gains on a new Wikipedia Coreference (WCR) dataset. They also integrate their MC classifier as a sieve in Dcoref, improving end-to-end coreference.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A focused formulation of MC resolution with a feature-based classifier leveraging Wikipedia structure and Freebase, yielding strong gains over heuristics derived from SOTA CR systems.
2) Empirical evidence of domain mismatch for CR on Wikipedia (large drops for several systems) and a practical integration that improves a full CR pipeline (Dcoref++).
3) A specialized Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR) and analysis of feature families for MC resolution.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains: The classifier achieves ~89 F1 on MC mentions, improving 13–15 F1 points over best adapted baselines; integrating it into Dcoref raises CoNLL F1 by ~4 points (51.77 to 55.11). These are meaningful, practical improvements.
- Effective use of Wikipedia/Freebase signals: Features from titles, redirects, inferred types, and markup substantially boost both precision (exact/partial matches) and recall (type cues, position).
- Useful task and downstream impact: MC identification is valuable for Wikipedia-centric NLP (IE, summarization, EL). The sieve integration demonstrates usefulness beyond a standalone task.
- Thoughtful ablation for pronouns and qualitative analysis: The stepwise feature inclusion shows consistent gains, and error examples are informative about remaining challenges.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and reporting: WCR has only 30 documents; inter-annotator agreement and detailed annotation protocol are deferred to the non-anonymized version. Statistical significance is not reported.
- Baseline fairness and breadth: Comparisons rely on heuristic adaptations of CR systems; missing are stronger learned baselines for MC detection (e.g., simple learned string/EL-based classifiers) or end-to-end entity linking baselines.
- Dependence on Freebase and markup: Freebase is deprecated; reliance may limit reproducibility and portability. Robustness to sparse/erroneous markup is only partially analyzed.
- Generality: Experiments are English-only, single-domain (Wikipedia), with no evidence of transfer to other encyclopedic corpora or broader CR tasks beyond Dcoref++.

Questions to authors
- Please report annotation guidelines and inter-annotator agreement for WCR, and clarify train/dev/test splits and significance testing.
- How sensitive is performance to missing or noisy links/redirects? Can you quantify results when markup is ablated?
- Can Freebase features be replaced by Wikidata, and do results hold?
- Could you include a learned “title/variants-only” baseline and an EL-based baseline to strengthen comparisons?
