Summary and contributions
The paper targets a Wikipedia-specific coreference subtask: identifying, in a Wikipedia article, all mentions that refer to the article’s main concept (MC). The task is framed as binary classification at the mention level, with two SVMs (pronominal and non-pronominal) using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase. The authors: (1) introduce a small but annotated Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR), (2) design and evaluate an MC-resolution classifier that clearly outperforms baselines derived from state-of-the-art CR systems (Dcoref, Scoref), and (3) demonstrate gains in a full CR pipeline by adding a high-precision sieve to Dcoref.

As I see it, the main contributions, in order of significance:
1) A simple, effective MC-resolution classifier leveraging Wikipedia/Freebase features with strong gains over competitive baselines.
2) A practical demonstration that injecting MC-resolution early in a sieve-based CR system improves overall CR performance on Wikipedia.
3) A curated Wikipedia coreference dataset and a task formulation that foregrounds Wikipedia-specific phenomena.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains on a relevant, underexplored genre: On the MC-resolution task, the proposed classifier achieves F1 ≈ 88.8, improving over the best Dcoref-derived baseline by ~13 F1 points (Table 4). The improvement holds for both pronouns and non-pronominal mentions and across precision and recall.
- Wikipedia-aware feature design: The feature families effectively exploit Wikipedia markup (titles, redirects, internal links) and Freebase attributes. Ablations for pronominal mentions (Table 2) show meaningful, interpretable contributions from position, tag, and local compatibility (distance) features, suggesting robustness beyond pure string overlap.
- Impact on full coreference: The Dcoref++ sieve yields a +3.3 to +4 CoNLL F1 improvement over Dcoref on WCR (Table 5), with gains across MUC, B3, and CEAFϕ4. This supports the claim that early, high-precision MC decisions benefit subsequent sieves.
- Solid baselines and context: The paper carefully adapts two strong CR systems (rule-based and statistical) to the MC task with multiple heuristics (B1–B4, pronoun augmentation), and first shows that all off-the-shelf CR systems degrade sharply on WCR relative to OntoNotes (Table 1), motivating the need for genre adaptation.
- Constructive analysis: The paper provides illustrative examples and qualitative error discussion (e.g., noise from redirects, type induction complementing Freebase types), and a greedy feature-addition analysis clarifying design choices.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and representativeness: WCR includes only 30 documents (~60k tokens), with 1469 chains and 1800 MC mentions. While the sample is said to span topics and sizes, 10-fold cross-validation on so few articles risks instability and overfitting to Wikipedia idiosyncrasies. No variance/confidence intervals or significance tests are reported for the sizable improvements.
- Incomplete ablations for non-pronominal mentions: The text references a stepwise addition of feature families for non-pronominal mentions (e.g., +title, +inferred type, +name variants, +entity type, +main), but the corresponding table (Table 3) is missing. Quantitative contributions of these critical features are therefore not verifiable.
- Potential leakage and fairness of supervision: The approach assigns attributes to mentions by matching mention strings to surface forms of hyperlinks in the same article, then importing attributes from the linked target. This is attractive but might advantage the MC resolution task disproportionately (e.g., if many surface forms directly match MC title/redirects), compared to baselines that cannot use such Wikipedia-internal supervision. It would help to control for “title/alias obviousness” with stricter splits or feature-ablation baselines (e.g., no title/redirect/alias features).
- Baseline scope: The baselines are derived from CR system outputs plus heuristics. Stronger mention-level baselines that use the same information sources as the proposed model (e.g., pure string/alias matching and inferred type matching without learning) would better isolate the added value of the classifier and non-trivial features (e.g., position, distance).
- External KB dependency and generality: The approach relies on Freebase attributes; as Freebase is deprecated, it is unclear how performance would translate to Wikidata-only setups. An experiment without Freebase (Wikipedia-only features) would clarify portability.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: Are folds split by article (i.e., no sentences from the same article appear in both train and test)? Given the use of per-article internal links for features, article-level separation is crucial to avoid leakage.
- Non-pronominal feature ablations: Can you provide the missing quantitative table for non-pronominal mentions to substantiate claims about each feature family’s contribution?
- Robustness without Freebase: What is performance when removing Freebase-derived features (entity type, gender/number, aliases)? Conversely, how much gain is due solely to Freebase vs. Wikipedia markup?
- Statistical significance and variability: Do the MC-resolution improvements and Dcoref++ gains hold across multiple random CV splits, and are they statistically significant?
- Error characterization: Can you provide a breakdown of major remaining error types (e.g., metonymy, section-local salience shifts, noisy redirects), and how often redirects vs. inferred types cause errors?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider adding a simple, high-precision mention-level baseline: predict MC for a mention if it exactly matches any of {title, redirects, anchor texts to the MC page}; optionally add “head matches inferred type.” This would calibrate how much the classifier adds beyond direct Wikipedia signals.
- Clarify how singletons (automatic mentions) are labeled and balanced during training, and whether this skews the class distribution for non-pronominal mentions.
- Report per-article performance variance to show stability across topics and article lengths; consider adding a few more articles to reduce sampling noise.
- Since you show improvements when integrating the classifier as a sieve, it would be useful to analyze changes specifically on the MC chain metrics (precision/recall of the MC chain) vs. non-MC chains, to confirm that precision gains are not offset elsewhere.
- Given Freebase’s deprecation, a brief discussion of mapping to Wikidata properties would improve the paper’s longevity.

Overall, the paper addresses a meaningful and practical subtask in a high-impact genre, with a simple and effective method and clear empirical gains. Addressing the ablation gaps, robustness, and leakage concerns would strengthen the case for acceptance.
