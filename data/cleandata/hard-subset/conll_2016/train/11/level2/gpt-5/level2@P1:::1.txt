Summary and contributions
This paper revisits the task of identifying, within a Wikipedia article, all mentions that refer to the article’s main concept (MC). The authors frame MC resolution as binary mention classification and propose two SVMs (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions), driven by features extracted from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, plus simple surface and contextual cues. They construct baselines by extracting the MC chain(s) from the outputs of state-of-the-art coreference systems (Dcoref, Scoref) using several heuristics, and show large gains in F1 for MC identification. They also integrate the classifier as a high-precision first sieve into Dcoref and report an absolute +4 CoNLL F1 improvement on a Wikipedia coreference testbed (WCR).

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) A simple, effective MC resolution model for Wikipedia that leverages Wikipedia structure and Freebase attributes, with strong gains over robust baselines on a clearly defined task.
2) A small, annotated Wikipedia dataset (WCR) and an empirical study demonstrating that off-the-shelf CR models degrade markedly on this genre, motivating adaptation.
3) A practical integration of MC resolution into a full CR pipeline (Dcoref++) yielding measurable improvements on Wikipedia CR.

Strengths
- Clear task formulation and strong empirical results. The MC mention identification task is well specified; using a binary classifier decoupled by mention type is sensible. Reported gains are substantial: ~+13–15 F1 over strong baselines on MC resolution (overall ~89 F1) and +4 CoNLL F1 when injected into Dcoref. The ablations (especially for pronouns) are informative and show that each family of Wikipedia/Freebase-derived features contributes.
- Makes effective use of Wikipedia structure while avoiding noisy NEL. Exploiting human-placed links, redirects, and first-sentence types provides high-precision signals without a full entity linker. The approach is simple, interpretable, and appears robust for pronouns (position/distance cues) and nominals (title/variants/type).
- Relevance and practicality. MC resolution is directly useful for downstream IE/OIE on Wikipedia; demonstrating benefits in a full CR sieve pipeline strengthens the case for impact.
- Genre-focused evaluation and insight. The comparison of several recent CR systems on WCR, showing consistent degradation relative to OntoNotes, is valuable and highlights the need for genre adaptation.

Weaknesses
- Dataset size and evaluation protocol. WCR contains only 30 documents (~60k tokens). Ten-fold CV on so few documents risks high variance; no held-out test set or significance testing is reported. It is hard to gauge generalization across topics or MC types. A type-wise breakdown (PER/ORG/LOC/MISC) and per-article variance would strengthen the claims.
- Dependence on Wikipedia links and Freebase. The approach assumes high link coverage; many mentions in Wikipedia are unlinked. The paper does not quantify how often attributes are unavailable, nor the performance when Freebase/Wikipedia-derived attributes are missing or noisy (e.g., redirects). Freebase is being deprecated; an analysis with Wikidata or a link-only variant would improve robustness claims.
- Baseline design may underplay stronger string/alias options. The baselines based on CR chains are reasonable, but a purely alias/redirect-based baseline (using title, redirects, and link-anchored variants plus head matching) is not reported and might close some of the gap for non-pronominals. The heuristic of adding all occurrences of a pronoun once it appears in the chain could inflate recall in ways that differ from the proposed classifier’s behavior.
- Limited methodological detail for reproducibility. The feature set is described at a family level, but a complete inventory (including the exact string-similarity predicates and thresholds), hyperparameter selection, and decision thresholds are not fully specified. It is unclear whether any calibration was used, how class weights were tuned, or whether any nested CV was used for model selection. The paper also does not report inter-annotator agreement for WCR.
- Scope of end-to-end evaluation. The Dcoref++ improvement is shown only on WCR, with precision gains larger than recall. It would be useful to compare against a variant that uses MC predictions as features (rather than freezing the MC chain) and to explore sensitivity to sieve order. No comparison is made to retraining a statistical CR model on WCR or adding MC cues as features.

Questions to authors
- Coverage: What fraction of mentions and MCs lacked Freebase entries or Wikipedia link-derived attributes? How does performance change for those cases?
- Breakdown: Can you report MC resolution performance by MC type (PER/ORG/LOC/MISC) and by mention type (proper vs nominal vs pronoun) per MC type?
- Resources: Given Freebase deprecation, have you tried Wikidata as a drop-in replacement? Any differences?
- Baselines: Could you add a stronger alias-based baseline using title+redirects+link-anchored variants and head matching (without CR chains)? For pronouns, a simple majority-pronoun-per-document baseline with gender/number compatibility might be informative.
- Dcoref++: Once the MC chain is built, you prevent later sieves from adding to it. Did you try allowing high-precision later sieves to append mentions? How sensitive are results to sieve order and to the MC classifier’s confidence threshold?
- Evaluation: Did you run significance tests for the reported gains? What is the per-fold variance? Any results on a larger held-out set?

Additional comments
- Please release WCR (with guidelines and IAA), code, and an immutable URL as promised. Clarify licensing.
- An error taxonomy (e.g., section-initial pronouns, noisy redirects, ambiguous heads like “Houston”) and per-topic analysis would further clarify limits.
- The paper makes a good case for Wikipedia-specific CR; scaling the dataset and replacing Freebase with Wikidata would make the contribution more durable.
