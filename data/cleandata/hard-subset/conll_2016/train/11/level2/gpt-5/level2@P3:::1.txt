Summary and claimed contributions
The paper revisits “main concept” resolution in Wikipedia: identifying all mentions in an article that refer to the article’s primary subject. The work (a) formulates a supervised per-mention classifier leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase-derived attributes to decide whether a mention refers to the main concept; (b) presents a small Wikipedia coreference dataset (WCR) and evaluates the proposed approach against baselines derived from two state-of-the-art CR systems (Dcoref and Scoref); and (c) integrates the classifier as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref, showing improvements on full coreference metrics in Wikipedia articles.

In this reviewer’s view, the primary contribution is the effective, feature-based main concept classifier that substantially outperforms strong CR-derived baselines on Wikipedia. Secondary contributions are (i) the WCR testbed and empirical analysis showing the genre mismatch of news-trained CR systems on Wikipedia, and (ii) an integration demonstrating downstream gains in a full CR pipeline.

Strengths
- Clear problem focus and motivation with demonstrated genre gap. Results in Table 1 show a notable drop in CoNLL F1 for several recent CR systems when moving from OntoNotes to the WCR Wikipedia data (e.g., Scoref 63.61→46.39), underscoring the importance of adapting CR to Wikipedia.

- Effective exploitation of Wikipedia and KB signals. The approach leverages human-curated hyperlinks, redirects, and Freebase attributes without running a noisy entity linker. Ablations are informative: for pronominals, incremental feature families improve F1 from 74.1 (base) to 89.0 (base+main+position+tag+distance; Table 2). For non-pronominals, adding title, inferred type, name variants, entity type, and main features successively raises precision/recall, with detailed discussion of why each helps (e.g., inferred type enables mapping mentions like “the team” to Houston Texans).

- Strong empirical gains on the target task. On 10-fold cross-validation over WCR mentions, the classifier achieves 88.79 F1 overall, improving by 13–15 absolute points over baselines derived from Dcoref/Scoref (Table 4). The baselines include reasonable heuristics (longest chain, title-aware, chain aggregation, and pronoun-boosting), making the comparison meaningful.

- Demonstrated downstream impact. Incorporating the classifier as the earliest sieve in Dcoref (Dcoref++) yields consistent gains across MUC, B3, CEAFφ4, and CoNLL F1 on WCR (e.g., CoNLL F1 51.77→55.11 in Table 5). This supports the claim that making early, high-precision MC decisions can help a full CR pipeline on Wikipedia.

- Useful resource contribution and reproducibility intent. The paper commits to releasing the WCR dataset and a Wikipedia-wide dump of predicted MC mentions with extracted attributes, which can foster further work on Wikipedia-specific CR.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset size and potential generalization concerns. WCR contains 30 documents (~60k tokens, ~7k non-singleton mentions). While the paper sampled diverse topics and lengths, the small size raises concerns about robustness and overfitting, especially given high-dimensional feature families (e.g., 2,380 POS-based binary features for pronominals). The 10-fold cross-validation setup is reasonable, but explicit confirmation that folds are by document (to avoid cross-article leakage) would strengthen the evidence.

- Baselines may under-represent simple, strong Wikipedia-specific heuristics. While B1–B4 are reasonable chain-based heuristics on top of CR outputs, Wikipedia affords very high-precision cues. A competitive non-CR baseline that labels as positive (i) exact title/redirect matches, (ii) anchor texts that link to the article’s page, plus (iii) pronouns filtered by article-level gender/number, might be strong for non-pronominals. Reporting such a baseline would help quantify the added value of the learning approach beyond straightforward exploitation of markup.

- Possible feature leakage via intra-article linking. The preprocessing assigns Freebase-derived types to unlinked surface forms whenever that surface string appears anywhere as a hyperlink in the article. If surface strings that sometimes anchor to the MC are used to type all occurrences, the model may partially inherit signals tantamount to oracle entity disambiguation for those strings. An analysis excluding mentions that exactly match the title/redirects and excluding mentions with surface strings that appear as MC anchors would clarify the true difficulty and the model’s gains on genuinely ambiguous nominals and pronouns.

- Scope and external validity of the downstream gains. Dcoref++ improves CoNLL F1 by 4 points on WCR, but the experiment is limited to this small dataset. It remains unclear whether the sieve negatively affects non-MC chains (e.g., by preventing later sieves from appending legitimate mentions) or how it behaves on longer, denser articles. A per-chain or per-entity breakdown, and results on a larger Wikipedia sample, would make the case more compelling.

- Details missing for full reproducibility. Several implementation choices could be specified more precisely: exact fold construction; feature scaling/normalization; prevalence and coverage of Freebase attributes (and behavior when absent); and the exact lists and thresholds for string similarity features (the “10 boolean” comparisons). The anonymized dataset link is also a blocker for verification during review.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- How were the 10-fold splits constructed—by document or by mention? If by mention, please re-report with document-level folds to exclude cross-article leakage.

- What fraction of positive non-pronominal MC mentions are exact title/redirect/name-variant matches? What is the classifier’s F1 on the subset excluding those matches?

- How often do surface strings of mentions appear as hyperlinks to the MC within the same article? What is performance when removing Freebase/Wikipedia attributes assigned via intra-article hyperlinks for the same surface string?

- In Dcoref++, how often does the new sieve block later sieves from correctly adding non-MC mentions to non-MC chains? A per-entity error analysis or diagnostics would help.

- What is the coverage of Freebase notable types and coarse attributes in WCR? How does performance change when Freebase information is missing or removed?

Suggestions for improvement
- Strengthen baselines with a high-precision, Wikipedia-specific string/link heuristic independent of CR outputs, and report ablations excluding direct title/redirect/anchor matches.

- Expand WCR or supplement with a larger, held-out Wikipedia test set; ensure document-level splits and report learning curves to assess overfitting.

- Provide clearer feature specifications and release code/configuration for feature extraction and model training; include results without Freebase to quantify dependence on external KBs.

- Extend Dcoref++ evaluation with per-chain analyses and results on a broader Wikipedia sample to demonstrate scalability and stability.
