

Summary: The paper introduces a novel task of identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) in Wikipedia articles, distinct from general coreference resolution. The authors propose a binary classification approach using features derived from Wikipedia markup and Freebase to determine if a mention refers to the MC. They develop two classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) that significantly outperform baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference systems. The main contributions, in order of strength, are: (1) a well-defined task and evaluation framework for main concept resolution in Wikipedia; (2) a feature-rich classifier that leverages Wikipedia's unique structure and external knowledge bases; and (3) demonstration that integrating this classifier into a full coreference pipeline improves overall performance.

Strengths:
1. The paper identifies a meaningful gap in the literature: coreference resolution systems trained on news data perform poorly on Wikipedia, yet Wikipedia is a widely used resource in NLP. The authors provide compelling evidence through experiments showing significant performance drops for state-of-the-art systems on their WCR dataset.

2. The feature engineering is thoughtful and well-justified. The authors effectively leverage Wikipedia's unique structure (hyperlinks, redirects, Freebase attributes) to create features that are less ambiguous than those used in general coreference systems. The incremental feature analysis (Table 2) clearly demonstrates the value of each feature family.

3. The results are impressive: the proposed classifier achieves 88.79% F1 on the main concept resolution task, representing an absolute 13-15 point improvement over the best baseline. The integration into Dcoref (Dcoref++) also yields a 4% absolute F1 improvement on the full coreference task, showing practical utility beyond the specific task.

4. The paper makes a valuable contribution to reproducibility by sharing the dataset and classifier results. This will enable future work on Wikipedia-specific coreference resolution.

Weaknesses:
1. The dataset (WCR) is quite small (30 documents, 60k tokens), which limits the statistical significance of the results. The authors acknowledge this but don't adequately address how this impacts the reliability of their conclusions or the generalizability of their approach to larger Wikipedia corpora.

2. The paper doesn't sufficiently justify why the main concept resolution task is more valuable than general coreference resolution for Wikipedia applications. The authors mention benefits for information extraction (citing Nguyen et al. 2007), but don't clearly demonstrate how identifying MC mentions specifically improves downstream tasks beyond what general coreference would provide.

3. The comparison to state-of-the-art systems is limited to only two systems (Dcoref and Scoref), despite the paper claiming to use "state-of-the-art" systems. This omission makes it difficult to assess how competitive their approach is with the broader state of the art in coreference resolution.

4. The paper doesn't adequately address scalability concerns. With only 30 documents annotated, it's unclear how the proposed approach would scale to the full Wikipedia corpus, which contains millions of articles.

5. The evaluation metrics for the main concept resolution task could be more clearly justified. The authors use average precision, recall, and F1, but don't explain why these are the most appropriate metrics for this specific task.

Questions to Authors:
1. Given the small size of the WCR dataset (30 documents), how confident are you in the statistical significance of your results? Could you provide error bars or confidence intervals for your F1 scores?

2. Could you clarify why identifying main concept mentions is more valuable than general coreference resolution for Wikipedia applications? Specifically, how does this task enable improvements in downstream NLP tasks that general coreference resolution wouldn't provide?

3. Why did you choose to compare only to Dcoref and Scoref, rather than including other recent state-of-the-art coreference systems (such as those described in the CoNLL 2012 or 2015 shared tasks)?
