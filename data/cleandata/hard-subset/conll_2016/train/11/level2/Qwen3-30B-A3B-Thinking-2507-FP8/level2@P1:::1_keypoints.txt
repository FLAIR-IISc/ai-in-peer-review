- Introduces a novel task: identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) in Wikipedia articles, distinct from general coreference.
- Proposes a binary classification approach using features from Wikipedia markup and Freebase to detect MC mentions.
- Develops two classifiers (pronominal and non-pronominal) that significantly outperform baselines built on state-of-the-art coreference systems.
- Main contributions: well-defined task and evaluation framework for MC resolution in Wikipedia.
- Main contributions: a feature-rich classifier leveraging Wikipedia structure and external KBs.
- Main contributions: integration of the classifier into a full coreference pipeline improves overall performance.
- Strength: Identifies a real gap—news-trained coreference systems perform poorly on Wikipedia—and shows empirical evidence of performance drops.
- Strength: Thoughtful, well-justified feature engineering using hyperlinks, redirects, and Freebase; incremental feature analysis demonstrates value.
- Strength: Strong empirical results—88.79% F1 on MC task, ~13–15 point gain over best baseline; Dcoref++ yields ~4% absolute F1 gain on full coref.
- Strength: Releases dataset and classifier results, supporting reproducibility.
- Weakness: WCR dataset is very small (30 documents, 60k tokens), limiting statistical significance and generalizability.
- Weakness: Insufficient justification or demonstration that MC resolution is more valuable than general coreference for downstream tasks.
- Weakness: Comparisons limited to only Dcoref and Scoref, omitting other recent state-of-the-art coreference systems.
- Weakness: Scalability to the full Wikipedia corpus is not addressed.
- Weakness: Choice of evaluation metrics (precision, recall, F1 averages) is not clearly justified for this task.
- Question for authors: Given the small dataset, can you provide error bars or confidence intervals to support statistical significance?
- Question for authors: Clarify why MC identification yields unique benefits for downstream tasks beyond general coreference.
- Question for authors: Why were only Dcoref and Scoref used as baselines instead of broader recent state-of-the-art systems (e.g., CoNLL shared-task models)?
