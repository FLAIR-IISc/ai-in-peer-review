

Summary of contributions: The paper introduces "Main Concept Resolution" as a distinct task for Wikipedia texts, framing it as identifying all mentions of the article's primary subject rather than full coreference chains. It proposes a binary classifier using Wikipedia-specific features (markup, Freebase attributes) and demonstrates significant performance gains over baselines. The work contributes a new dataset (WCR) and classifier outputs for future research.

Strengths: The paper provides a compelling demonstration of Wikipedia's unique challenges for coreference resolution, with Table 1 showing a 15-17 point F1 drop for state-of-the-art systems on WCR compared to OntoNotes. The binary classification approach for Main Concept Resolution is well-motivated and achieves substantial gains (13-15 F1 points over baselines in Table 4), with the pronominal classifier reaching 88.99% F1. The feature ablation study (Table 2) clearly demonstrates the incremental value of each feature family, particularly the title and main attributes. The integration of the classifier into Dcoref (Dcoref++) yields a 4-point CoNLL F1 improvement (Table 5), showing practical utility for full coreference pipelines.

Weaknesses: The WCR dataset size (30 articles, 60k tokens) is extremely small for coreference research, potentially limiting the statistical significance of the results and raising concerns about generalizability to Wikipedia as a whole. The comparison to prior work on Wikipedia coreference is incomplete; the paper cites Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008) but does not directly compare results to their systems on the same task or dataset. The error analysis is limited to a single example (Figure 3f), with no systematic examination of failure modes or error patterns. Baseline comparison is restricted to two systems (Dcoref and Scoref) despite the paper mentioning four state-of-the-art systems in Section 5.

Questions to Authors: How was the WCR dataset sampled to ensure representativeness across Wikipedia article types, given its small size? What evidence supports the claim that the WCR dataset is representative when it contains only 30 articles? Why were Nguyen et al. (2007) and Nakayama (2008) not directly compared on the Main Concept Resolution task using the WCR dataset?
