- Revisits the task of identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) in Wikipedia articles.  
- Designs a classifier that outperforms baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference systems.  
- Demonstrates benefits of this classifier in a full coreference pipeline applied to Wikipedia texts, emphasizing a Wikipedia-specific design.  
- Identifies a research gap: coreference systems trained on news drop 10–15 F1 points on Wikipedia (WCR) compared to OntoNotes.  
- Leverages Wikipedia markup and Freebase to avoid named-entity linking errors, using redirects/links for attribute extraction.  
- Strong empirical results: ~88.99% F1 for pronominal and ~88.67% for non-pronominal mentions, outperforming baselines by 13–15 points; Dcoref++ gives ~4-point CoNLL F1 gain.  
- Provides the WCR dataset (30 articles, ~60k tokens) and shares classifier outputs for reproducibility.  
- Makes a convincing argument that Wikipedia’s structure differs from news and is a valuable NLP resource.  
- Dataset size is very small, limiting statistical robustness and representativeness across Wikipedia.  
- Task definition (main concept resolution) is narrower than standard coreference and its broader significance is underexplained.  
- Baselines (B1–B4) are weakly motivated and heuristics like substring aggregation (B4) likely introduce false positives.  
- Evaluation is limited to two SOTA systems (Dcoref, Scoref), omitting comparisons to other relevant systems.  
- Paper lacks discussion of limitations, e.g., handling multiple or ambiguous main concepts and scalability concerns.  
- How confident are you that results generalize across Wikipedia given the small dataset, and what additional analyses do you recommend?  
- How would the approach handle articles where the main concept is unclear or articles about events/multi-interpretation concepts?  
- Why focus on main concept resolution instead of standard coreference, and how does this task relate to broader coreference problems?  
- What are the limitations for articles with low internal link density or different structural properties, and how would the approach adapt?
