

Reviewer for CONLL_2016

Summary of Contributions:
The paper claims three main contributions: (1) revisiting the task of identifying all mentions of the main concept (MC) in Wikipedia articles; (2) designing a classifier that outperforms baselines built on top of state-of-the-art coreference resolution systems; and (3) demonstrating the benefits of this classifier in a full coreference resolution pipeline applied to Wikipedia texts. The most significant contribution appears to be the development of a classifier specifically designed for Wikipedia's unique structure.

Strengths:
1. The paper identifies a valuable research gap: coreference resolution systems trained on news data perform poorly on Wikipedia texts, and there's limited work adapting CR specifically to Wikipedia. The authors provide convincing evidence through experiments showing significant performance drops (10-15 F1 points) of standard CR systems on their WCR dataset compared to OntoNotes.

2. The approach of leveraging Wikipedia's markup and Freebase to avoid the inaccuracies of named-entity linking pipelines is well-motivated and represents a meaningful technical contribution. The use of Wikipedia's internal structure (redirects, links) for attribute extraction is particularly clever and avoids cascading errors common in other approaches.

3. The evaluation demonstrates clear practical benefits: the classifier achieves 88.99% F1 for pronominal mentions and 88.67% for non-pronominal mentions, outperforming baselines by 13-15 points. The integration into Dcoref (Dcoref++) shows a meaningful 4-point improvement in CoNLL F1 on the full coreference task, which is a strong practical validation of their approach.

4. The authors provide a dedicated dataset (WCR) with 30 Wikipedia articles annotated for coreference, which is valuable for future research in this niche. Their sharing of the dataset and classifier outputs will facilitate reproducibility and further research.

5. The paper makes a strong case for Wikipedia as a valuable resource for NLP, citing relevant literature and explaining why Wikipedia's structure differs from news articles in ways that affect coreference resolution.

Weaknesses:
1. The dataset size (30 articles, 60k tokens) is extremely small for a coreference resolution study, making it difficult to draw statistically robust conclusions. The authors acknowledge this but don't sufficiently justify why such a small dataset is representative of Wikipedia's diversity. The paper lacks analysis of how results would scale with larger datasets or vary across different Wikipedia article types.

2. The task definition ("main concept resolution") is significantly narrower than standard coreference resolution. The paper doesn't adequately explain how this task relates to the broader coreference resolution problem or why it's a meaningful problem in its own right. This limits the broader significance of the work.

3. The baselines (B1-B4) are poorly motivated and lack theoretical grounding. B4's heuristic of aggregating chains based on substring matching ("Barack Obama" matching "Obama" or "Barack") is likely to introduce many false positives as noted in the paper. The authors don't provide strong justification for why these specific baselines are meaningful comparisons.

4. The evaluation is limited to only two state-of-the-art systems (Dcoref and Scoref) despite citing multiple others. This weakens the claim that their approach "drastically outperforms fair baselines" when they don't compare against the most relevant systems. The comparison to other CR systems in Table 1 shows Dcoref outperforming statistical systems, but they don't explore why.

5. The paper lacks a thorough discussion of limitations. For instance, it doesn't address how the approach would handle Wikipedia articles with multiple main concepts or ambiguous main concepts, nor does it discuss potential scalability issues with their approach for larger Wikipedia articles.

Questions to Authors:
1. Given the small dataset size (30 articles), how confident are you that the results generalize across the diverse Wikipedia domain? What additional analysis would you recommend to address this concern?

2. How would your approach handle Wikipedia articles where the "main concept" is not clearly defined (e.g., articles about events or concepts with multiple interpretations)?

3. Why did you choose to focus on "main concept resolution" rather than standard coreference resolution, and how does this task relate to the broader coreference resolution problem in NLP?

4. Could you elaborate on the potential limitations of your approach for Wikipedia articles with low internal link density or different structural properties?
