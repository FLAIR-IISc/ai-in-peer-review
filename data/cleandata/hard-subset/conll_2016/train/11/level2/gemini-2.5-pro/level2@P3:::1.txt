**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of coreference resolution on Wikipedia articles, a domain where standard systems trained on newswire text are known to perform poorly. The work focuses on a specific and important sub-problem: identifying all mentions of the "Main Concept" (MC), which is the subject of the Wikipedia article. The proposed method frames this as a binary classification task, training two separate SVM classifiers for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions. The key insight is to leverage features derived directly from Wikipedia's structure (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked knowledge bases (Freebase) to obtain high-quality information about entities without relying on a separate, potentially error-prone, entity linking step. The work introduces a new annotated dataset, WCR, and uses it to demonstrate that the proposed classifier substantially outperforms baselines constructed from state-of-the-art coreference systems. Finally, it shows that integrating this MC classifier into a rule-based coreference system improves its overall performance on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia text.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The introduction and evaluation of a new dataset (WCR) for coreference on Wikipedia. This includes a valuable benchmark of existing state-of-the-art systems, which empirically confirms the domain adaptation challenge for this genre.
2.  A highly effective, feature-engineered classification model for the MC resolution task. The model skillfully exploits domain-specific markup and knowledge base information to achieve a large performance improvement over strong baselines.
3.  A demonstration that solving the MC resolution task as a high-precision preliminary step can be successfully integrated into a full coreference resolution pipeline, leading to significant gains on the WCR dataset.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and Focused Problem:** The paper clearly identifies a known gap in coreference resolution research—the poor performance of standard systems on non-newswire genres like Wikipedia. Instead of tackling the entire, complex problem of domain adaptation at once, it wisely narrows the focus to MC resolution. This is a well-justified choice, as the MC constitutes a significant fraction of mentions in any given article. This focus allows for a thorough and convincing analysis.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Rigorous Evaluation:** The proposed classifier drastically outperforms a range of well-designed baselines built from state-of-the-art systems (Table 4), with an absolute F1 improvement of over 13 points against the best baseline. The evaluation is further strengthened by a detailed feature ablation study (Tables 2 and 3), which clearly demonstrates the contribution of the different Wikipedia- and Freebase-derived feature groups. The utility of the MC classifier is also demonstrated in a downstream task, where its integration into the Dcoref system yields a 4-point F1 improvement on the full coreference task (Table 5).

3.  **Creation of a Valuable New Resource:** The paper introduces the WCR dataset. By providing new annotations for Wikipedia text and benchmarking several modern coreference systems, it provides a valuable resource for future research on coreference in encyclopedic text. The finding that an older rule-based system (Dcoref) outperforms more recent statistical systems on this new dataset is an interesting and useful result in its own right.

4.  **Clever Use of Domain-Specific Information:** The approach makes excellent use of the inherent structure of Wikipedia. By using internal hyperlinks to map mentions to Freebase entities, it effectively bypasses the need for a standard Wikification or named entity linking pipeline. This is a simple but powerful design choice that avoids a common source of cascading errors, and it is perfectly suited to the target domain.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of the Downstream Integration Experiment:** The MC classifier is integrated into a single, rule-based system (Dcoref). While the results are positive, it is not clear whether this approach would provide similar benefits for modern statistical coreference systems (e.g., Scoref). The sieve-based architecture of Dcoref is a natural fit for adding a high-precision first pass. How the classifier's output could be integrated as features or constraints into an end-to-end neural system is less obvious and remains an open question. Showing a successful integration with a statistical system would strengthen the claim of general utility.

2.  **Small Dataset Size:** The new WCR dataset consists of only 30 documents. While the paper states that care was taken to sample a variety of articles, this is a small sample size from which to draw broad conclusions about performance on Wikipedia as a whole. The robustness and generalizability of both the trained classifier and the experimental findings might be limited. A discussion of the potential limitations imposed by the dataset size would be beneficial.

3.  **Dependence on a Deprecated Resource:** The approach relies on Freebase for extracting entity attributes. As Freebase has been discontinued and superseded by Wikidata, the long-term reproducibility of this exact method could be a concern for other researchers wishing to build on this work. While the underlying principles would likely transfer to Wikidata, a brief comment on this migration path would strengthen the paper's forward-looking perspective.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The integration into the `Dcoref++` system involves adding a new first sieve and also preventing subsequent sieves from modifying the MC chain ("prevent other sieves from appending new mentions to the MC coreference chain"). Could this be clarified? Does this hard constraint mean that no other mentions can be added to the MC cluster at all, or that mentions identified as part of the MC cluster cannot be moved to other clusters? This seems like a strong constraint that might hurt recall. Were alternative, softer integration strategies considered (e.g., using the classifier's output as a strong feature or preference in later sieves rather than a hard decision)?

2.  The results in Table 1 show that existing statistical models perform poorly on WCR when used off-the-shelf. As a point of comparison for the `Dcoref++` system, was any attempt made to fine-tune a statistical model like Scoref on the WCR training data? This would help determine whether the performance gap is due to the inherent superiority of the rule-based system on this data, or if the statistical models simply need in-domain training data to adapt and potentially match or exceed the performance of the proposed `Dcoref++` system.
