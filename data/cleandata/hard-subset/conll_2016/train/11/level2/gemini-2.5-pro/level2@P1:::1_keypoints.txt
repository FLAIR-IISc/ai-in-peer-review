- Introduces WCR, a 30-article Wikipedia coreference dataset and analysis showing newswire-trained systems perform poorly on this domain.  
- Proposes two SVM classifiers (pronominal and non-pronominal) using Wikipedia markup and Freebase-derived features to identify an article’s Main Concept (MC).  
- Classifier significantly outperforms strong baselines and ablations; integrating it as a high-precision sieve into Dcoref (Dcoref++) improves full-coreference performance on WCR.  
- Well-motivated task formulation: MC resolution is a practical, well-defined subtask addressing an under-explored domain.  
- Thorough empirical evaluation with fair, varied baselines and clear feature ablation studies demonstrating feature contributions.  
- Valuable community resource: release of the WCR dataset enables reproducible, domain-specific coreference research.  
- Clever exploitation of Wikipedia structure (hyperlinks, redirects) to obtain semantic signals without a noisy entity-linking step.  
- Methodological novelty limited: linear SVM with hand-crafted features rather than contemporary neural approaches; rationale for this choice is not fully discussed.  
- Heavy reliance on deprecated Freebase raises concerns about long-term reproducibility and portability to Wikidata.  
- Brief analysis of Dcoref++ integration; placing the new sieve first is a strong hard constraint and its error trade-offs are not explored.  
- Small dataset (30 documents) limits confidence in generalization despite 10-fold cross-validation.  
- Question: Why choose SVMs over recent neural models—was dataset size the main factor?  
- Question: Can the feature pipeline be migrated from Freebase to Wikidata, and what challenges would that entail?  
- Question: What is the impact of locking the MC chain in Dcoref++—how would performance change if later sieves could modify it?
