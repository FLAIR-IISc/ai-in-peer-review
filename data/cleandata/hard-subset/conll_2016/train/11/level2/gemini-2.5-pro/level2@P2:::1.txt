**Summary of the paper and its main contributions**

This paper tackles the task of coreference resolution within Wikipedia articles, focusing on a specific sub-problem: identifying all mentions of the article's Main Concept (MC). The authors frame this "Main Concept Resolution" as a binary classification task for each mention. The core of their contribution is a supervised approach using two SVM classifiers (for pronominal and non-pronominal mentions) powered by a rich set of features. These features are cleverly extracted from Wikipedia's internal structure (e.g., hyperlinks, redirects) and linked knowledge bases (Freebase), largely bypassing the need for a separate, potentially noisy, entity linking step. The authors introduce a new, annotated dataset of 30 Wikipedia articles (WCR) to evaluate their approach. They show that their method significantly outperforms several strong baselines constructed from state-of-the-art coreference systems. Finally, they demonstrate the practical utility of their classifier by integrating it as a high-precision sieve into the Dcoref system, leading to improved performance on the full coreference resolution task for Wikipedia text.

The main contributions of this work are, in order of strength:
1.  A simple yet highly effective feature-based model for Main Concept Resolution in Wikipedia. The strength of the model lies in its well-designed features that directly leverage the rich, structured information available in Wikipedia and Freebase, achieving a substantial performance gain over strong baselines.
2.  The creation of a new annotated dataset (WCR) for coreference in Wikipedia. This resource is valuable for future research, and the paper effectively uses it to demonstrate the significant performance gap when applying standard newswire-trained coreference systems to the Wikipedia genre, thus strongly motivating the need for domain-specific adaptation.
3.  A proof-of-concept demonstrating that a specialized MC resolver can be successfully integrated into a general-purpose coreference system (Dcoref) to improve its performance on Wikipedia articles.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Thoughtful Baselines:** The proposed model achieves an F1 score of 88.79 on the MCR task, an absolute improvement of over 13 points compared to the best-performing baseline. The baselines themselves are not strawmen; they are intelligently constructed by applying several reasonable heuristics to the output of two different state-of-the-art systems (one rule-based, one learning-based). This makes the performance gains of the proposed method highly convincing.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Elegant Approach:** The paper identifies a clear and useful sub-problem. The decision to exploit Wikipedia's internal hyperlink structure as a substitute for a full named-entity linking pipeline is a key insight. It allows the model to access high-quality, human-curated semantic information (via Freebase) with much lower noise than typical automated linking, simplifying the pipeline and reducing cascading errors.
3.  **Valuable Resource and Genre Analysis:** The introduction of the WCR dataset is a contribution in itself. The analysis in Table 1, which shows a drop of 10-17 F1 points for state-of-the-art systems when moving from OntoNotes to WCR, provides compelling evidence for the genre-adaptation problem in coreference resolution and strongly justifies the work.
4.  **Thorough Analysis:** The paper provides detailed ablation studies (Tables 2 and 3) that clearly demonstrate the contribution of each feature family. This analysis offers valuable insights into which sources of information are most critical for the task (e.g., the `title` and `inferred type` features). The inclusion of qualitative examples in Figure 3 further helps in understanding the model's behavior and its remaining error types.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Dependence on a Deprecated Resource:** The method relies heavily on Freebase for extracting crucial entity attributes (types, gender, aliases, etc.). As Freebase was shut down in 2016, this presents a significant challenge for the reproducibility and future applicability of this work. While the methodology could likely be adapted to use a successor like Wikidata, the paper does not mention this limitation or discuss potential migration paths, which is a notable omission.
2.  **Limited Scale of Evaluation:** The evaluation is conducted on a newly created dataset of only 30 articles. While the authors state that care was taken to sample diverse articles, this is a very small sample size from which to draw general conclusions about a resource as vast and varied as Wikipedia. A larger-scale evaluation would be needed to fully establish the robustness and generalizability of the proposed model and its features.
3.  **Methodology is Somewhat Dated:** The paper presents a classic feature engineering approach. While effective, the field has been steadily moving towards end-to-end neural models that learn feature representations automatically. The comparison in the full coreference task is made by integrating the classifier into Dcoref, a rule-based system from 2013. A more forward-looking experiment would have been to use the classifier's predictions as features for a more recent statistical system (like Scoref) to see if this "best of both worlds" approach yields even greater gains.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Given that Freebase has been deprecated, have you considered adapting your approach to use its successor, Wikidata? Could you comment on the feasibility of this and whether the same types of attributes (notable types, aliases, gender, etc.) are readily available through Wikidata?
2.  The Dcoref++ experiment shows a promising direction. Did you consider or experiment with integrating your MC classifier's predictions into a learning-based system like Scoref? For instance, the binary prediction could serve as a powerful feature for mention-pair scoring, or the resulting MC chain could be used to constrain the final clustering.
3.  Could you provide more detail on the composition of the 30-document WCR corpus? Specifically, what is the distribution of article topics (e.g., number of articles on people, organizations, locations, events) to better assess its diversity?
