## Review of "Coreference in Wikipedia: Main Concept Resolution"

This paper revisits the task of identifying mentions of the main concept in Wikipedia articles. The authors argue that existing coreference resolution systems, trained primarily on news data, perform poorly on Wikipedia and propose a binary classification approach leveraging Wikipedia markup and Freebase links to improve performance on this specific task. The paper presents a dataset, baseline comparisons, and experiments showing improvements over state-of-the-art systems, and demonstrates a positive impact when integrated into a rule-based coreference system.

**Contributions:**

1. **A Dedicated Dataset (WCR):** The creation and release of the WCR dataset for main concept resolution in Wikipedia is a significant contribution. While the size is modest (30 documents), the dataset provides a valuable resource for evaluating systems specifically designed for this task, and the authors mention making the dataset and extracted mentions publicly available, which is highly commendable.
2. **Effective Feature Engineering:** The authors demonstrate that carefully engineered features derived from Wikipedia’s markup (links, redirects) and external knowledge bases (Freebase) significantly improve performance on the main concept resolution task. The combination of features – leveraging both structural information and semantic attributes – is well-motivated and effective.
3. **Improved Performance on a Specific Task:** The paper demonstrates substantial improvements (up to 13 F1 points) over baseline coreference systems, and a moderate improvement when integrated with a rule-based system (Dcoref++). This establishes the effectiveness of the proposed approach for identifying mentions of the main concept.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the task of main concept resolution and motivates its importance in the context of Wikipedia as a resource for NLP applications.
2. **Strong Baseline Comparisons:** The authors rigorously compare their approach against several state-of-the-art coreference systems, providing a strong benchmark for future research.  The inclusion of both rule-based (Dcoref) and statistical (Scoref) baselines is particularly valuable.
3. **Detailed Feature Analysis:** The ablation study (Table 2) provides valuable insights into the contribution of different feature families, allowing for a better understanding of what drives the performance gains.
4. **Practical Integration:**  Demonstrating the integration of the classifier into a larger coreference system (Dcoref++) shows the practical applicability of the approach beyond just the main concept resolution task.
5. **Availability of Resources:** The stated intention to release the dataset and extracted mentions is crucial for reproducibility and further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset Size:** The WCR dataset, while valuable, is relatively small (30 documents). This limits the generalizability of the results and the robustness of the models trained on it.  The authors acknowledge this, but it remains a significant concern. Performance gains might not be sustained on a larger, more diverse dataset.
2. **Over-reliance on Wikipedia Structure:** The approach heavily relies on the unique characteristics of Wikipedia’s markup and links. While this is a reasonable strategy given the target domain, it raises questions about the portability of the method to other text genres.  The authors don’t sufficiently address the potential limitations of this reliance.
3. **Classifier Complexity & Feature Engineering:** The feature engineering process seems somewhat ad-hoc and relies on a large number of features.  A more systematic feature selection or dimensionality reduction technique could potentially simplify the model and improve its robustness. The description of feature engineering could be more structured and concise.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper provides some examples of classification errors (Figure 3), a more comprehensive error analysis would be beneficial. Understanding the types of errors the system makes could provide valuable insights for future improvements. Specifically, the analysis points to difficulty with broader context (example f) - this area needs further exploration.
5. **Missing Related Work:** The related work section is somewhat limited.  Recent advances in entity linking and knowledge graph-enhanced coreference resolution (beyond the cited papers) could be discussed to better position the work.


**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What are the plans for expanding the WCR dataset?  Would it be feasible to scale the annotation process to include a larger number of Wikipedia articles?
2.  How robust is the approach to noisy or incomplete Wikipedia markup? Have you considered strategies for handling situations where links are broken or Freebase information is unavailable?
3.  Could you provide more details on the feature selection process? Were any techniques used to reduce the dimensionality of the feature space?
4.  What is the computational cost of feature extraction, and how does it scale with the size of the input document?
5. How does the performance change when evaluating on different categories of Wikipedia articles (e.g., biographical articles vs. articles on scientific concepts)?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a discussion of potential ethical considerations related to the use of knowledge bases like Freebase, which may contain biases.
*  The formatting of Table 4 could be improved for readability.
* Typo: "co-referent" should consistently be "coreferent".
