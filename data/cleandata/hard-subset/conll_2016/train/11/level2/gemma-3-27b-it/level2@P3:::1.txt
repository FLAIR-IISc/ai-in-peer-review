This paper revisits the task of main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles, framing it as a binary classification problem. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that leveraging Wikipedia markup and external knowledge bases (Freebase) through a carefully designed classifier significantly outperforms baseline coreference resolution systems on this specific task. An additional contribution is the provision of a dataset (WCR) and resources for this task, and demonstrating a benefit to integrating the classifier into a full coreference resolution pipeline.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Task Focus:** While coreference resolution is a well-studied area, applying it specifically to the task of identifying mentions of the main concept within a Wikipedia article is a valuable and relatively unexplored area. The paper successfully highlights the limitations of generic CR systems when applied to this specific domain.
2. **Effective Feature Engineering:** The features designed, drawing from Wikipedia markup and Freebase, are well-motivated and demonstrably improve performance. The detailed breakdown of feature families and their individual impacts (as shown in Table 2 and Table 3) is particularly strong. The ablation study is a strong point.
3. **Clear Experimental Setup and Evaluation:** The authors establish clear baselines and use a dedicated dataset (WCR) for evaluation. The inclusion of comparison to state-of-the-art coreference systems, along with performance metrics on both the WCR dataset and the CoNLL 2012 dataset, provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach. The experiment integrating the classifier into Dcoref demonstrates practical utility.
4. **Resource Contribution:** The creation and planned release of the WCR dataset and extracted mention data represents a valuable contribution to the research community. This allows for reproducibility and future work building upon the presented results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dataset Size and Representativeness:** While the creation of WCR is commendable, its size (30 documents) is relatively small. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't sufficiently address the potential limitations regarding the generalizability of the results. More information about the selection criteria for these 30 articles would be beneficial.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** While examples of errors are provided in Figure 3, a more systematic error analysis would be helpful. Identifying common failure cases and potential areas for improvement could strengthen the work. The discussion of noisy redirects is a good starting point, but more detail would be appreciated.
3. **Classifier Detail:** While the authors state the use of a linear kernel SVM, further details regarding parameter tuning and the exact settings used would be useful for reproducibility. Specifying the penalty parameter weighting for the negative class would be helpful.
4. **Dcoref++ Improvement Nuance:** The improvement demonstrated by Dcoref++ (4% F1) feels relatively small given the reported performance gains of the primary classifier. A deeper dive into *how* the sieve integration affects performance, and what kinds of errors remain, would be beneficial. Is the improvement primarily in precision or recall?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What criteria were used to select the 30 documents for the WCR dataset? How representative is this sample of the broader range of Wikipedia articles?
2. Can you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process for the SVM classifiers, including the specific values tested and the criteria used for selection?
3. Could you expand on the error analysis, identifying common types of errors made by the classifier and discussing potential strategies for mitigating these errors?
4. Could you clarify which metrics (MUC, B3, CEAF, CoNLL F1) are most relevant for evaluating performance on this specific task (main concept resolution), and why?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The phrase “somewhere.country” in the acknowledgements should be replaced with the actual URL when the paper is finalized.
*  Consider briefly discussing potential biases present in Wikipedia content and how these biases might affect the performance of the classifier.
* The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
