This paper addresses the task of main concept resolution in Wikipedia articles – identifying all mentions of the central topic of an article. This is a valuable task, particularly given the widespread use of Wikipedia as a resource in NLP. The authors revisit this task, framing it as a binary classification problem and leveraging Wikipedia markup and external knowledge from Freebase to improve performance.

**Contributions:**

1. **Dataset and Testbed:** The creation of the WCR dataset and associated evaluation framework provides a valuable resource for researchers interested in coreference resolution specifically tailored to the Wikipedia domain. While the size is relatively small (30 documents), the annotation following OntoNotes guidelines and the availability of the data (promised in the conclusion) are significant.
2. **Feature Engineering:** The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of carefully designed features, combining information from Wikipedia markup, Freebase, and traditional coreference features. The feature ablation studies (Table 2 & discussion) are particularly strong in illustrating the relative importance of different feature families.
3. **Improved Performance:** The proposed approach significantly outperforms baseline systems (including state-of-the-art coreference resolvers) on the main concept resolution task and demonstrates a modest improvement when integrated into a rule-based coreference system (Dcoref++).

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the task and motivates its importance, particularly highlighting the gap in existing coreference resources focused on Wikipedia.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct comprehensive experiments, comparing their approach to several baselines and performing feature ablation studies. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of their method.
3. **Good Use of External Knowledge:** The clever integration of Wikipedia markup and Freebase information is a key strength. Exploiting readily available, human-created links is a smart design choice.
4. **Practical Application:** The integration into Dcoref++ and the observed improvement demonstrate the potential for real-world impact of the proposed approach.
5. **Detailed Feature Description:** The description of the features is detailed and well-organized, making it easy to understand the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dataset Size:** The relatively small size of the WCR dataset is a limitation. While the authors acknowledge this, it impacts the generalizability of the results. A larger, more diverse dataset would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Baseline Selection:** While the choice of Dcoref and Scoref is reasonable, the paper could benefit from comparing against more recent and/or specialized coreference systems. The comparison to systems primarily evaluated on news-like data (as noted in the paper) may not be entirely fair, but a broader comparison would be helpful.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the authors provide some examples of errors (Figure 3), a more thorough error analysis would provide deeper insights into the limitations of the approach and potential areas for improvement.  Specifically, investigating why the system struggles with examples like (g) in Figure 3 would be beneficial.
4. **Over-reliance on Freebase:** The method's performance is heavily reliant on the quality and availability of information in Freebase.  The paper briefly acknowledges this, but a discussion of potential strategies for handling cases where Freebase information is missing or inaccurate would be valuable.
5. **Presentation of Dcoref++ Results:** The presentation of the Dcoref++ results in Table 5 is somewhat difficult to interpret without more context.  It would be helpful to include the individual scores for Dcoref and Dcoref++ for a clearer comparison.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What steps were taken to ensure the quality and consistency of the annotations in the WCR dataset?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the classifier to the quality of the Freebase information? Have you explored strategies for handling missing or inaccurate Freebase data?
3. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, focusing on the types of errors the system makes and the reasons behind them?
4. Given the reliance on Wikipedia markup, how robust is the system to variations in Wikipedia's formatting or structure?
5. The paper mentions sharing the dataset and a dump of MC mentions. When and where will these resources be available?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   Consider expanding the discussion of related work to include recent advances in knowledge graph embedding and their potential applications to coreference resolution.
*   The citation style is generally correct, but please double-check for consistency.
