## Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

This paper investigates the prediction of compound noun compositionality by comparing different vector representations – neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors – and exploring the impact of “aligned” versus plain vectors for dependents in dependency parsing. The central finding is that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings, while alignment of vectors alone hinders performance, though a tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields slight gains.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the empirical demonstration that carefully constructed, syntactically-informed count vectors can still be competitive, and even outperform, neural embeddings in the specific task of compositionality detection. This is a valuable reminder that despite the current dominance of neural methods, traditional distributional semantics approaches still have merit, particularly when leveraging linguistic structure. A secondary contribution is the exploration of aligned dependency vectors, although the novelty here is questionable (see below).

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Research Question:** The paper tackles a well-defined and interesting problem – understanding how well compositional models can detect non-compositional phrases.
2. **Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a series of experiments comparing different approaches, providing quantitative results to support their claims.
3. **Focus on Syntactic Structure:** The emphasis on incorporating syntactic information through dependency parsing is a strength, as it acknowledges the importance of linguistic structure in semantic composition.
4. **Comparison to Baseline:** The comparison to Reddy et al.’s (2011) baseline provides a useful point of reference and demonstrates improvement.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset & Reporting:** The reliance on Reddy et al.’s (2011) 90-compound dataset is a significant limitation. Crucially, the paper lacks details about the dataset’s selection criteria, frequency ranges of the compounds, and the number of human judges involved, making it difficult to assess potential biases. The small size makes the marginal gains reported fragile.
2. **Questionable Novelty:** The “aligned” vector approach appears to be a rehash of previously published work by the authors (suppressed reference), diminishing the paper’s innovative contribution.
3. **Methodological Inconsistency & Justification:** The paper utilizes higher-order dependency features generally but restricts similarity measurement to first-order features, without sufficient justification. The explanation for the unreliability of second-order paths is unconvincing.
4. **Clarity & Readability:** The paper suffers from confusing terminology ("packed anchored tree"), unclear explanations (e.g., what "not properly aligned" means), and convoluted sentences. The lack of running examples hinders understanding.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide detailed information about the dataset used: selection criteria for the 90 compounds, frequency ranges in the corpus, and the number of human judges involved in the compositionality ratings.
2.  Can you elaborate on the suppressed reference and clearly articulate the novelty of the current work compared to your previous publication?
3.  Please justify the use of only first-order dependency features for similarity measurement, given the use of higher-order features in the APT construction.
4.  Could you provide running examples illustrating the difference between "compound phrase token" and constituent representations?
5.  Please clarify the definition of "elementary APTs" and confirm whether "shifted PMI" corresponds to equation (3).



**Additional Comments:**

*   Table 4’s legend is ambiguous regarding the “add” combination.
*   Consider including results using word2vec vectors for compound phrases, not just components.
*   The intuition behind the FREQ baseline is unclear and potentially indicative of dataset bias.
