Summary
The paper evaluates compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by comparing observed compound vectors with composed component vectors, using cosine similarity and reporting Spearman correlation with human judgments. It contrasts sparse, syntactic-context count vectors (including higher-order dependency paths) with neural embeddings, and studies “aligned” APTs versus unaligned dependents, plus a hybrid mix. On the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, count vectors outperform embeddings; alignment alone often hurts, while a tuned hybrid yields small gains.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Primary: An empirical finding that syntactic-context count vectors (with typed dependency features) outperform neural embeddings on compound compositionality detection.
- Secondary: Evidence that a hybrid of aligned and unaligned features provides slight improvements over either alone.
- Tertiary: A replication-style evaluation that surfaces strong frequency–compositionality correlations and competitive simple baselines.

Strengths
- Clear, task-focused comparison showing sparse syntactic features can beat embeddings on this benchmark, an informative empirical result for the community.
- Inclusion of intuitive baselines (RAND, FREQ) and cross-validation enhances interpretability; the FREQ baseline highlights potential dataset artifacts.
- Systematic exploration of APT variants (aligned, unaligned, hybrid) and composition operators gives a reasonably broad empirical picture.
- Practical takeaway: simple unaligned syntactic count vectors with additive composition are strong and robust.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is limited: the key APT/“alignment” idea appears previously published by the authors (reference suppressed), and innovation over that work is unclear.
- Dataset concerns: only 90 compounds; selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of judges are not reported; possible bias (FREQ performs well). Treatment of ties in human scores and implications for Spearman are not addressed.
- Methodological inconsistency: the model relies on higher-order dependency features broadly, yet similarity is computed on first-order features only; justification (note 3) is weak and unconvincing.
- Reporting gaps: hyperparameters h and q (Eqs. 8–9) and their contribution to gains are not disclosed; Table 4 legend is ambiguous (was “add” used?); α=0.75’s “small positive effect” is not clearly supported by Table 3.
- Exposition issues: “packed anchored tree” vs. “elementary APTs” terminology is opaque; Section 3’s discarded features/order>3 and incompatible types need concrete examples; several sentences are confusing/contradictory; alignment rationale (“not properly aligned,” why NMOD overlap increases) is unclear; paras 558–563 and 575–580 are puzzling.

Questions to Authors
- What were the selection criteria for the 90 compounds, their frequency ranges, and how many human judges contributed? How were ties handled in Spearman?
- Please define “elementary APTs” precisely and clarify whether “shifted PMI” corresponds to Eq. (3) with α or another shift k.
- Why restrict similarity to first-order features while advocating higher-order paths elsewhere? Provide examples showing unreliability of 2nd-order paths post-composition.
- Report tuned values and ablations for h and q; how much does hybridity contribute?
- Clarify alignment: what constitutes “not properly aligned,” and why should alignment increase overlap, especially for NMOD?
- Include results with word2vec for compound phrases (holistic vectors), and disambiguate Table 4’s composition operator (“add”).
