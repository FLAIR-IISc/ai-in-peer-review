Summary and claimed contributions
The paper investigates compositionality detection for noun-noun compounds by comparing “composed” vectors (built from constituents) to “observed” holistic compound vectors and correlating cosine similarities with human compositionality judgments (Reddy et al., 2011). It contrasts neural word embeddings (word2vec) with sparse, syntactic-context count vectors based on “anchored packed trees” (APTs), and studies three composition settings for APTs: aligned, unaligned, and a hybrid linear combination. Results on the 90-compound dataset show that APT-based models outperform neural baselines; the hybrid APT variant slightly outperforms aligned or unaligned alone.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical result: typed dependency count vectors with higher-order features outperform word2vec embeddings on noun-compound compositionality detection (ρ up to 0.79 vs 0.74 for cbow and 0.71 in Reddy et al.).
2) A hybrid composition strategy combining aligned and unaligned APTs yields small but consistent gains over either alone (0.79 vs 0.78/0.75).
3) A parameter study indicating that, for this task, cbow > skip-gram, higher subsampling is detrimental, and that weighting parameters tend to slightly favor the modifier and unaligned features (optimal h, q in [0.3, 0.5]).

Strengths
- Clear empirical comparison with strong baselines. The paper evaluates multiple families of models on the same corpus: word2vec (cbow/skip-gram, multiple dimensions and subsampling) and APT variants (aligned, unaligned, hybrid; PPMI and smoothed PPMI). The quantitative summary (Table 5) is easy to interpret: hybrid APT (0.79) > unaligned APT (0.78) > aligned APT (0.75) > cbow (0.74) > skip-gram (0.68) > Reddy (0.71) > FREQ (0.63). This gives a persuasive empirical ranking.
- Methodological care around evaluation. The use of repeated 3-fold cross-validation, reporting of small SEs (≤0.0015), and a clear threshold for meaningful differences (>0.005) improves confidence in the comparisons. Preprocessing that replaces compounds with single tokens prevents information leakage from holistic contexts into constituent vectors.
- Useful task-specific insights. The observation that the modifier and unaligned features get slightly higher weight (optimal h and q around 0.3–0.5) is informative for designing future composition functions for compounds. The finding that aggressive subsampling (analogous to shifted PMI) hurts low-frequency compounds is plausible and practically relevant.
- Practical improvements over prior work. The top APT results substantially exceed the original untyped Reddy et al. baseline (0.79 vs 0.71), supporting the value of typed dependency features for compositionality detection.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and clarity of the APT framework. The aligned APT idea appears to have been introduced previously by the authors (cited as “reference suppressed”), making the present conceptual novelty unclear. Section 3 struggles to define “elementary APTs,” “alignment,” “reduction,” and the treatment of higher-order/incompatible types; illustrative derivations and concrete examples are needed. The new terminology “anchored packed trees” seems superfluous without a crisp formalization and running example.
- Dataset and evaluation fragility. The dataset is tiny (90 compounds), with insufficient reporting: no summary of selection criteria, frequency ranges, or number of annotators for the human judgments. The strong FREQ baseline (ρ=0.63) signals potential frequency–compositionality confounds; the paper does not analyze this bias. The handling of ties in human scores (common on 0–5 scales) and the exact Spearman implementation are not described.
- Methodological inconsistencies. Although the model constructs higher-order dependency features and composition can generate higher-order paths, similarity to the observed compound is computed only over first-order features. The footnote justification (“unreliable second-order paths”) is unconvincing given that elementary APTs include up to order-2 paths; this choice risks favoring unaligned models and obscures where the gains come from. A principled ablation is needed (e.g., evaluate using 1st vs 1st+2nd order features).
- Ambiguities and contradictions. Several passages are confusing: the discussion of “not properly aligned” vs “overlap increases for NMOD,” the explanation of why shifted PMI hurts (with pronoun references that obscure the argument), and the claim that α=0.75 yields “small positive effect” is not consistently supported by Table 3. Table 4’s legend is ambiguous about composition (only “add”? any weighting?) and whether compound tokens’ own word2vec vectors were also evaluated as a baseline.
- Missing details on hyperparameters and contributions. The paper notes optimal h and q lie in [0.3,0.5] but does not report the chosen values per setting, nor quantify how much the hybrid’s gain derives from the aligned vs unaligned part (e.g., by varying q). Statistical testing methodology (paired tests across CV splits) is not specified.

Questions to authors
- APT clarity and novelty: Please provide a succinct, formal definition of “elementary APTs,” alignment, and reduction with a complete running example (e.g., for “spelling bee”), and clarify what is new here relative to your prior APT work.
- Feature orders: Why is evaluation restricted to first-order features when APTs include 2nd-order and composition can produce higher-order paths? Can you report results including 2nd-order features and an ablation showing their impact?
- Alignment rationale: In Section 3, you argue that unaligned modifier features can overlap with head features under NMOD; please illustrate with explicit feature examples and explain why alignment reduces overlap in that case.
- Hyperparameters and hybrid contributions: Please report the exact optimal values of h and q for each APT variant and quantify the hybrid’s gains as a function of q (e.g., plot ρ vs q). Do the best settings differ by dependency type (NMOD vs AMOD)?
- Dataset reporting: Provide the number of annotators, inter-annotator agreement, selection criteria for the 90 compounds, frequency distributions for compounds and constituents, and how ties in human scores were handled in Spearman’s ρ.
- Word2vec baselines: Did you also evaluate holistic compound vectors produced by word2vec (given that compounds were re-tokenized) vs composed constituent vectors? If not, please add this baseline.
- Statistical testing: Specify the exact significance tests and units of analysis (per-fold/per-repetition), and provide confidence intervals for ρ.

Overall, the empirical results are promising and suggest typed dependency contexts help compositionality detection, but the paper’s clarity, novelty positioning, and evaluation rigor need strengthening to make the claims compelling.
