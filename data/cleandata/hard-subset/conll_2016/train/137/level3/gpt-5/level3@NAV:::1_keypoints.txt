Summary: Compares composed component vectors vs. observed compound vectors using cosine similarity/Spearman on Reddy et al. (2011), finding sparse syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings and a tuned hybrid gives small gains; alignment alone often harms.

Primary contribution: Empirical finding that typed dependency, sparse count vectors beat neural embeddings on compound compositionality detection.

Secondary contribution: Evidence that a hybrid of aligned and unaligned features yields slight improvements over either alone.

Tertiary contribution: Replication-style analysis revealing strong frequency–compositionality correlations and confirming simple baselines are competitive.

Strength: Clear, task-focused comparison showing sparse syntactic features can outperform embeddings on this benchmark.

Strength: Inclusion of intuitive baselines (RAND, FREQ) and cross-validation, exposing potential dataset artifacts via the FREQ baseline.

Strength: Systematic exploration of APT variants (aligned, unaligned, hybrid) and composition operators gives broad empirical coverage.

Strength: Practical takeaway that unaligned syntactic count vectors with additive composition are strong and robust.

Weakness: Limited novelty—alignment/APT idea appears previously published by the authors and incremental here.

Weakness: Small/dubious dataset—only 90 compounds with unspecified selection, frequency ranges, judge counts, and tie handling; FREQ baseline suggests bias.

Weakness: Methodological inconsistency—advocates higher-order dependency features but computes similarity on first-order features with weak justification.

Weakness: Reporting gaps—tuned hyperparameters h and q not disclosed, ambiguous Table 4 legend, and unclear support for α=0.75 effects.

Weakness: Exposition problems—opaque terminology (packed anchored tree vs. elementary APTs), unclear rationale for alignment behavior, and several confusing/contradictory passages.

Question: What were selection criteria, frequency ranges, number of human judges, and how were ties handled for Spearman?

Question: Precisely define “elementary APTs” and clarify whether “shifted PMI” equals Eq. (3) with α or a different shift k.

Question: Why limit similarity to first-order features while using higher-order paths elsewhere; provide examples showing 2nd-order paths become unreliable after composition.

Question: Report tuned values and ablations for h and q and quantify how much the hybrid contributes.

Question: Clarify what “not properly aligned” means and why alignment should increase overlap (especially for NMOD).

Question: Provide results using holistic word2vec vectors for compounds and confirm Table 4’s composition operator (was it “add”?).
