- Paper compares APT (typed dependency count) vs word2vec embeddings for noun–noun compositionality on Reddy et al.’s 90 compounds, using cosine similarity vs human judgments (Spearman’s ρ).
- Main result: APTs outperform neural embeddings (best ρ≈0.79 vs ≈0.74 for best cbow), aligned APTs underperform unaligned, and a tuned aligned+unaligned hybrid gives a small extra gain.
- Explores hyperparameters (h, q) and PPMI smoothing (α), plus composition operators (add/min) and weighting choices.
- Contribution: empirical evidence that typed syntactic-context count vectors predict compound compositionality better than standard neural embeddings on this benchmark.
- Contribution: observation that offset/alignment alone can hurt performance, but a tuned hybrid of aligned and unaligned representations yields modest, significant improvements.
- Strength: systematic, fair empirical comparison across representation families and multiple word2vec settings.
- Strength: strong benchmark performance—hybrid APTs substantially exceed prior Reddy baseline and tested neural configs.
- Strength: provides useful negative/nuanced findings about alignment effects and hybridization.
- Strength: transparent evaluation protocol (repeated 3-fold CV, averaged ρ) and analysis of composition operators, weighting, and smoothing.
- Weakness: limited novelty—core APT/alignment ideas appear previously published by the authors; paper needs to state what is new.
- Weakness: dataset limitations—reliance solely on Reddy’s 90 compounds with missing selection, frequency, and judge/tie reporting; strong FREQ baseline (ρ=0.63) raises bias concerns.
- Weakness: methodological inconsistency—similarity is computed using only first-order features despite building higher-order dependency features; explanation and examples for excluding higher orders are unconvincing.
- Weakness: clarity and terminology issues—“anchored packed trees,” “elementary APTs,” and constituent/component distinctions are poorly defined; several paragraphs are confusing and need reworking.
- Weakness: reporting gaps and missing ablations—unclear PPMI benefit, missing tuned h/q values per setting, ambiguous table legends, no explicit holistic word2vec (compound token) results, and other reproducibility details omitted.
- Question: precisely what is novel relative to prior APT/offset alignment work—please cite and delineate new contributions.
- Question: provide selection criteria for the 90 compounds, raw/effective frequency distributions for compounds and components, number of human judges, and tie statistics; state how ties are handled in Spearman’s ρ.
- Question: why restrict similarity to first-order features—provide ablations with/without higher-order features and concrete examples showing unreliability of 2nd/3rd-order composed paths.
- Question: define “elementary APTs” and give a running example of alignment/offset, reduction, and resulting overlaps; explain what “not properly aligned” means and why alignment reduces NMOD performance.
- Question: report tuned h and q values for best-performing configurations and quantify how much the hybrid adds beyond the best unaligned model; clarify composition operators tested for embeddings (add only or others).
- Question: confirm whether holistic word2vec vectors for compound tokens were used; if not, add those results.
- Question: clarify the PPMI smoothing claim—provide per-row statistical tests to support α=0.75 effects.
- Additional: unify terminology, define terms at first use, and fix confusing sentences and pronoun references.
- Additional: improve §3 exposition with step-by-step running examples showing feature generation, reduction, discarded/incompatible types, and order>2 effects.
- Additional: explain the FREQ baseline intuition and discuss possible dataset bias toward frequent/literal compounds.
- Additional: provide code, preprocessing scripts, dependency inventory, feature pruning thresholds, and all hyperparameters to enable reproducibility.
