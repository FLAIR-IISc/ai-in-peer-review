Summary
The paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by comparing a compound’s observed vector to a composed vector built from its components, and correlating cosine similarities with human judgments (Spearman’s ρ) on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds). It contrasts count-based typed dependency vectors (APTs) with neural word embeddings (word2vec), and investigates “aligned” (offset) vs plain dependent representations, as well as a hybrid mixture. Results: APTs outperform neural embeddings on this dataset; fully aligned APTs underperform unaligned APTs; a tuned hybrid of aligned and unaligned gives a small further gain. The paper also explores weighting (h, q) and PPMI smoothing.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical finding: Typed syntactic-context count vectors (APTs) predict compound compositionality better than standard neural embeddings on the Reddy dataset, with a best ρ≈0.79 vs ≈0.74 for the best cbow setting.
- Observation about alignment: Alignment alone hurts, but a tuned hybrid of aligned and unaligned representations yields small, statistically significant gains over either alone.

Strengths
- Clear empirical comparison across families of representations. The study systematically contrasts APTs vs several word2vec settings (cbow/skip-gram, dimensions, subsampling), providing a fairer baseline than prior work that used untyped counts.
- Strong performance on the standard benchmark. Best APT results (hybrid) substantially exceed the Reddy baseline and the best neural embedding configuration reported here, lending support to the utility of typed dependency features for compositionality detection.
- Useful negative/nuanced findings. The result that fully aligned APTs underperform unaligned for NMOD compounds, yet a hybrid helps, is informative for the community and suggests nontrivial interactions between dependency-typed features and composition.
- Transparent evaluation protocol. Using repeated 3-fold CV and reporting averaged ρ helps mitigate variance on a small dataset, and the comparison of composition operators (add/min), weighting, and PPMI smoothing is valuable.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty. The core APT framework and alignment idea appear to be previously published by the same authors (reference suppressed), making the innovation here largely an application to compositionality detection and a simple hybrid mixing. The paper should clarify which ideas are new.
- Dataset limitations and reporting gaps. The study relies exclusively on Reddy et al.’s 90 compounds. Selection criteria, frequency ranges, and the number of human judges are not reported for the instances used here after preprocessing; ties in human scores and their handling in Spearman correlation are not discussed. The strong FREQ baseline (ρ=0.63) suggests the dataset may be biased toward frequent, more literal compounds, making conclusions fragile.
- Methodological inconsistency and insufficient justification. While the model builds higher-order dependency features, similarity between composed and observed vectors is computed using only first-order features. The note explaining unreliability of 2nd/3rd-order paths in composed APTs is unconvincing and lacks examples. If higher-order features drive the approach, excluding them at evaluation requires stronger evidence.
- Clarity/terminology issues hinder understanding. The introduction of “anchored packed trees” (sometimes called “packed anchored” in the text) and “elementary APTs” is insufficiently defined. Section 3’s discussion of reduction, “incompatible co-occurrence types,” and discarding features of order >3 needs concrete examples. The distinction between “constituent,” “component,” “compound phrase token,” and constituent representations is often blurry. Several sentences in §5.1 are confusing (e.g., low positive PMI, “these phrases” pronouns); the explanation of alignment and what “not properly aligned” means (and why alignment should increase overlap for NMOD) is unclear. Paragraphs 558–563 and 575–580 need reworking.
- Reporting ambiguities and missing ablations. The claimed small benefit of PPMI smoothing (α=0.75) is not clearly supported by Table 3 (mixed effects). Exact tuned values or ranges for h and q are not reported per setting; how much “hybridity” contributes beyond the best unaligned model remains unclear. Table 4’s legend is ambiguous (was “add” the only composition for embeddings?). The request to include results using word2vec vectors for compound phrases (holistic) needs to be satisfied explicitly in the tables. Overall, reproducibility is hampered by missing details (e.g., feature pruning, thresholds, exact dependency inventory, treatment of low counts, shifted PMI settings).

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Novelty: What, precisely, is new in this submission relative to your prior APT/offset alignment work? Please cite clearly and delineate contributions.
- Data and evaluation: Provide (a) selection criteria for the 90 compounds used, (b) raw and effective frequency distributions for compounds and components, and (c) number of human judges and tie statistics. How are ties handled in Spearman’s ρ?
- Features and similarity: Why restrict similarity to first-order features when composed and observed vectors contain higher-order paths? Provide empirical evidence (ablation with/without higher-order features in similarity) and concrete examples illustrating “unreliability” of 2nd-order paths in composed APTs.
- Alignment: Define “elementary APTs” precisely and give a small running example showing alignment/offset, reduction, and the resulting overlaps for NMOD. What does “not properly aligned” exactly mean? Why does alignment reduce performance for NMOD?
- Hyperparameters: Report the tuned h and q for best-performing rows (per table/config). Quantify the gain from the hybrid over the best unaligned model after controlling for h. Clarify whether embeddings used only addition; if so, why not test multiplication or learned weights?
- Neural baselines: Confirm whether holistic word2vec vectors for compound tokens (not just composed embeddings) were used in the compositionality scoring; if not, please add those results.
- PPMI smoothing: The text claims a small positive effect of α=0.75, but Table 3 appears mixed. Provide statistical tests per row and clarify the claim.

Additional Comments
- Unify terminology (“anchored packed tree,” “elementary APT”), define all terms at first use, and maintain consistent use of “constituent” vs “component.”
- Improve exposition in §3 with step-by-step, sentence-level running examples showing feature generation, reduction, discarded/incompatible types, and order >2 effects.
- Tighten confusing sentences in §5.1 (PMI shifting rationale) and fix pronoun references.
- Explain the intuition and implications of the FREQ baseline: why should frequency correlate with compositionality? Discuss possible dataset bias.
- Provide code and preprocessing scripts, including compound recombination, dependency inventory, feature pruning, and all hyperparameters.
