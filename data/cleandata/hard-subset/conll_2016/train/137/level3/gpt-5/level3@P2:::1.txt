Summary and contributions
This paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by comparing “composed” representations of constituent words to “observed” (holistic) compound representations and correlating cosine similarities with human compositionality judgments on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. It compares (a) neural word embeddings composed by simple addition and (b) count-based, syntactic-context vectors built from higher-order dependency paths (“anchored packed trees”, APTs), with variants that align modifier spaces to the head before composition, do not align, or mix the two.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) Empirical finding that typed, higher-order dependency count vectors outperform neural embeddings for compound compositionality detection on ukWaC and Reddy’s dataset (hybrid APT ρ≈0.79 vs cbow ρ≈0.74; prior state-of-the-art 0.71).
2) Evidence that a simple hybrid mixture of aligned and unaligned APT representations slightly but consistently outperforms either alone, and that modifiers contribute slightly more than heads to predicting compositionality (optimal weights h, q in ~[0.3,0.5]).
3) A broad parameter sweep for both count-based PPMI (with/without smoothing) and neural embeddings (cbow/skip-gram, dimensions, subsampling), yielding practical guidance for this task.

Strengths
- Strong empirical signal on a standard benchmark. The APT-based methods consistently beat both the previous best (0.71) and well-tuned neural baselines (cbow up to 0.74). The hybrid APT variant reaches 0.79; unaligned APT 0.78; aligned APT 0.75. This is a meaningful improvement in this setting.
- Systematic comparison of modeling choices. The paper covers (i) typed vs untyped contexts, (ii) alignment vs no alignment, and (iii) additive vs intersective composition, alongside a reasonable grid of neural hyperparameters. This breadth helps isolate what matters for this task.
- Useful qualitative insights. Findings such as “unaligned typed dependency features are more informative than aligned ones for NMOD compounds,” “modifiers contribute slightly more than heads,” and “cbow benefits from lighter subsampling here” are actionable for practitioners.
- Thoughtful preprocessing to avoid leakage. Collapsing compounds into single tokens and excluding compound-derived contexts from constituent representations avoids trivial overlap that would inflate intersective composition methods.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and unclear positioning. The APT framework and alignment idea appear to be from prior work by the authors (cited as “reference suppressed”). The main novelty is applying them to compositionality detection on a single small dataset and mixing aligned/unaligned variants. The paper should clearly articulate what is new relative to prior APT publications and why the hybrid is conceptually warranted.
- Small, potentially biased evaluation and underreported details. The Reddy dataset has only 90 compounds. The paper does not report per-compound frequencies, frequency ranges, or how ties in human scores are handled in Spearman ρ. The strong FREQ baseline (ρ≈0.63) suggests a frequency confound; controlling for frequency (e.g., partial correlation) is not attempted. Reliance on a single dataset makes the conclusions fragile.
- Methodological clarity issues around APTs and alignment. Key notions (“anchored packed trees,” “elementary APTs,” alignment, reduction, zero-th order features, elimination of “incompatible types”) are not defined with concrete, end-to-end examples. It is unclear what “not properly aligned” means operationally, why alignment is expected to increase overlap in certain NMOD cases, and how higher-order features are generated and truncated in practice.
- Inconsistency between feature design and evaluation. While the approach emphasizes higher-order dependency features, the similarity between composed and observed representations is computed using only first-order features. The justification that second-order paths in composed vectors are “unreliable” is asserted but not demonstrated. This design choice directly affects the main result and needs empirical support (e.g., ablations including 1st+2nd order).
- Reporting and reproducibility gaps. The paper states that differences >0.005 are statistically significant at 95% but does not specify the number of cross-validation repetitions, the test used, or variance estimates. The exact optimal values of h and q per model, and the hybrid’s gain over pure aligned (q=1) and pure unaligned (q=0) under the same h, are not reported. Table 4’s legend and the use of additive composition for embeddings are not fully explicit. Results using “holistic” neural vectors of the compound itself as the observed target should be reported as a baseline alongside APT-based “observed” representations.

Questions to authors
- Clarify and exemplify APTs: Provide a precise definition of an “elementary APT,” the alignment operation (path offset and reduction), how zero-th order features arise, and which “incompatible” types are eliminated. Please include a full running example (e.g., spelling bee, red herring) showing constituent APTs, alignment, composition, and the resulting feature sets.
- Justify the first-order-only evaluation. Why are composed second-order (and higher) features “unreliable” given elementary APTs up to order 2? Please provide results with (i) 1st-order only, (ii) 1st+2nd-order, and (iii) all available orders on both sides, with a discussion of failure cases.
- Hybrid ablation and hyperparameters. Report the best h and q per variant, and show ablations at q∈{0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1} with the same h to quantify the hybrid’s contribution. How often does alignment help vs hurt across compounds?
- Ties and statistics. How many distinct ranks exist in the human judgments? How are ties handled in Spearman ρ? How many CV repetitions were run, and what statistical test supports the 0.005 threshold?
- Frequency confound. Provide per-compound frequency statistics (compound and constituents), the correlation between frequency and human compositionality, and results controlling for frequency (e.g., partial ρ or regression).
- Neural “observed” baseline. You state neural representations were trained for words and compounds. Please report results where composed neural vectors are compared to the holistic compound neural vectors (and to APT holistic vectors), mirroring the APT setup.

Additional comments
- Several passages are hard to follow (e.g., PMI shifting discussion, pronoun references to “these phrases,” the motivation around NMOD overlap). Careful editing with a running example will improve readability.
- The claim that PPMI smoothing (α=0.75) yields a “small positive effect” is not consistently visible in Table 3; please quantify the average gain and where it helps/hurts.
- Consider releasing code, preprocessing scripts, and parameter files to facilitate reproducibility.
