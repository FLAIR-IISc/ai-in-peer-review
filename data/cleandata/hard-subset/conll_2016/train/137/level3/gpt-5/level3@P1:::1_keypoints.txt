- Paper compares composed vs. holistic compound vectors to predict noun-compound compositionality, contrasting word2vec with APT-based sparse dependency vectors across aligned, unaligned, and hybrid APT composition.
- Main claimed contributions: typed dependency count vectors outperform word2vec (ρ up to 0.79 vs 0.74 cbow), a hybrid APT composition gives small consistent gains, and a parameter study on cbow/skip-gram, subsampling, and weighting favoring modifier/unaligned features.
- Strength: clear empirical comparison with strong baselines and an easy-to-interpret quantitative ranking (hybrid APT > unaligned > aligned > cbow > skip-gram > prior work).
- Strength: careful evaluation protocol (repeated 3-fold CV, small SEs, explicit threshold for meaningful differences) and preprocessing to avoid leakage.
- Strength: task-specific insights about modifier/unaligned feature weights and the negative effect of aggressive subsampling on low-frequency compounds.
- Strength: practical improvement over prior untyped baselines (APT results exceed Reddy et al.).
- Weakness: conceptual novelty and clarity of the APT framework are unclear; key terms (elementary APTs, alignment, reduction) lack crisp formalization and examples.
- Weakness: very small dataset (90 compounds) with missing details on selection, annotators, frequencies, ties handling, and potential frequency–compositionality confounds.
- Weakness: methodological inconsistency—similarity is computed only over first-order features despite APTs and composition producing higher-order paths, with an unconvincing justification.
- Weakness: confusing or contradictory explanations and ambiguous table legends (composition method, baselines like compound word2vec unclear).
- Weakness: missing hyperparameter reporting (exact h and q per setting) and lack of ablations showing how much hybrid gains come from aligned vs unaligned components.
- Weakness: statistical testing methodology is not specified (paired tests across CV splits, confidence intervals).
- Question: provide a succinct formal definition and running example of elementary APTs, alignment, and reduction, and clarify novelty over prior APT work.
- Question: why restrict evaluation to first-order features—please report results including 2nd-order features and an ablation on their impact.
- Question: illustrate with explicit feature examples how alignment reduces overlap between modifier and head features under NMOD.
- Question: report exact optimal h and q per variant and quantify hybrid gains as a function of q (e.g., plot ρ vs q); do settings differ by dependency type?
- Question: provide dataset details—number of annotators, inter-annotator agreement, selection criteria, frequency distributions, and handling of tied human scores in Spearman’s ρ.
- Question: did you evaluate holistic compound word2vec vectors as a baseline (given compounds were re-tokenized)? If not, add that baseline.
- Question: specify the exact significance tests, units of analysis, and provide confidence intervals for reported ρ.
- Overall: empirical results are promising that typed dependency contexts help compositionality detection, but clarity, novelty positioning, dataset detail, and methodological rigor need strengthening.
