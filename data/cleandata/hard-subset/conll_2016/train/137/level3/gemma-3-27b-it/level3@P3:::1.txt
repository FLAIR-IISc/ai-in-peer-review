This paper investigates predicting compound noun compositionality by comparing vector representations of compounds to those of their components, utilizing both neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors. The core idea revolves around the impact of contextual features – specifically, typed versus untyped dependency relations, and the use of “aligned” vectors – on compositional accuracy. The authors find that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings, aligned vectors alone hinder performance, but a tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors offers slight gains.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of Count Vector Superiority:** The most significant contribution is the finding that, for this specific task and dataset, traditional syntactic-context count vectors consistently outperform neural embeddings. This challenges the prevailing trend in distributional semantics towards neural methods and highlights the continued value of carefully engineered feature spaces.
2. **Detailed Exploration of Alignment Strategies:** The systematic investigation of aligned vs. unaligned dependency features provides valuable insights into how syntactic structure can be leveraged for compositional modeling. While alignment alone doesn’t improve results, the benefit of a hybrid approach suggests that capturing both the intended and broader contextual relationships is crucial.
3. **Dataset & Reproducibility (Potential):** Although the dataset is small and has limitations (detailed below), the use of Reddy et al.’s benchmark provides a common ground for comparison and encourages further research. The detailed description of the experimental setup, despite clarity issues (addressed in weaknesses), *potentially* allows for replication.

**Strengths:**

* **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The paper conducts a comprehensive set of experiments, varying vector types, composition operations, and parameter settings. The use of 3-fold cross-validation strengthens the reliability of the findings.
* **Focus on Syntactic Structure:** The emphasis on incorporating syntactic dependencies through anchored packed trees (APTs) is a valuable contribution, pushing beyond simple bag-of-words approaches.
* **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of compositionality detection as a testbed for compositional models and the need to address non-compositional phrases.
* **Comparison to Baseline:** Including Reddy et al.’s original results and a frequency baseline provides a solid benchmark for evaluating the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Dataset and Reporting:** The primary weakness is the reliance on Reddy et al.’s small (90 compounds) dataset. Crucially, the paper *fails* to report vital details about the dataset’s construction: selection criteria for the compounds, the frequency range of the compounds in the corpus, and the number/expertise of human judges involved in the compositionality scoring. This makes it difficult to assess the generalizability of the findings and introduces concerns about potential bias.
* **Fragile Gains & Statistical Significance:** The reported gains (particularly with the hybrid APT model) are marginal. Given the small dataset size, these gains might not be statistically robust. The authors acknowledge small error rates but don't provide confidence intervals.
* **Unclear Methodology & Terminology:** The concept of "packed anchored trees" (APTs) feels unnecessarily complex and isn’t clearly explained. The meaning of "elementary APTs" remains obscure. Section 3 lacks concrete examples illustrating how discarded features and the order >3 limitation impact the feature space.
* **Insufficient Justification for First-Order Similarity:** The paper utilizes higher-order dependency features for representation building but measures similarity using only first-order features. The justification provided in note 3 is unconvincing, and this disconnect raises concerns about the effectiveness of the chosen similarity metric.
* **Ambiguous Explanations & Pronoun Issues:** Several passages are difficult to follow due to confusing sentence structure and unclear pronoun references. The explanation of alignment – specifically, what “not properly aligned” means and why alignment *should* increase overlap, especially for NMOD relations – is inadequate. Paragraphs 575–580 and 558–563 require substantial clarification.
* **Questionable Baseline Intuition:** The observed correlation between compound frequency and compositionality (the FREQ baseline) suggests potential bias in the dataset. Why should frequently occurring compounds be *more* likely to be compositional?
* **Suppressed Reference:** The authors admit a prior publication on APTs but suppress the reference. This raises concerns about novelty.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Details:** Please provide comprehensive details regarding the selection criteria for the 90 compounds in the Reddy et al. dataset, the frequency range of those compounds in the ukWaC corpus, and information about the human judges (number, linguistic background, instructions).
2. **APT Clarity:** Can you provide a step-by-step example illustrating the construction of an APT for a given sentence, clarifying the meaning of "elementary APTs" and the reduction process?
3. **Alignment Explanation:** Please elaborate on what "not properly aligned" signifies in the context of dependency relations. Why should alignment specifically *increase* overlap for NMOD relations?
4. **Similarity Metric:** Justify the use of first-order similarity when higher-order dependency features are used in representation building. Are there plans to explore similarity metrics that leverage the full complexity of the APT representations?
5. **Hyperparameter Impact:** Report the optimal values of hyperparameters *h* and *q* (Equations 8-9) and quantify the contribution of the hybrid component (aligned + unaligned vectors) to the overall performance gain.
6. **Table 4 Clarification:** Confirm whether the “add” combination was used in the experiments reported in Table 4.
7. **Word2Vec Baseline:** Please include results using word2vec vectors for *compound phrases* (not just the individual components) as a baseline.



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit significantly from more running examples throughout, particularly to illustrate the concepts of "compound phrase token" versus constituent representations and the application of the APT framework.
* Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the study and potential directions for future research, such as exploring larger and more diverse datasets.
* Improving the overall readability of the paper through clearer writing and more concise explanations is strongly recommended.
