This paper investigates the detection of non-compositional phrases, specifically compound nouns, by comparing compositional distributional models based on sparse, high-dimensional representations with approaches applying simple composition operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings. The primary contribution of this work lies in the detailed exploration of incorporating syntactic structure – dependency paths – into distributional representations and demonstrating that such representations, particularly a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned dependency features, outperform standard neural embeddings on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for compositionality detection. A secondary contribution is the empirical analysis revealing that, for this specific task and dataset, smoothing PMI with α=0.75 provides a small but consistent improvement. Finally, the study offers a potentially valuable methodology for evaluating compositional methods by focusing on compositionality detection as distinct from phrase similarity.

**Strengths:**

1. **Superior Performance with Syntactic Context:** The strongest argument for acceptance is the demonstrated improvement in compositionality detection accuracy achieved by leveraging dependency-based contextual features. The results consistently show that models incorporating syntactic information outperform those based solely on neural word embeddings, suggesting that syntactic structure is crucial for accurately modeling phrase meaning, particularly in non-compositional cases. This finding is significant, reinforcing the importance of linguistic knowledge in distributional semantics.
2. **Novel Hybrid Approach:** The authors’ exploration of a hybrid model, combining aligned and unaligned dependency features, is a valuable contribution. The slight performance gain achieved by this combination suggests that both perspectives – considering strict dependency alignments and broader contextual relationships – are important for capturing the nuances of phrase compositionality. This nuanced approach moves beyond simple application of either aligned or unaligned methods.
3. **Clear Experimental Setup and Comparison:** The paper presents a well-defined experimental setup, directly adapting the methodology from Reddy et al. (2011) to facilitate a fair comparison. The clear description of the corpus, preprocessing steps, and evaluation metric (Spearman’s rank correlation) enhances the reproducibility of the study and allows for easy benchmarking against other approaches. The explicit comparison to baseline methods (RAND, FREQ, Reddy et al.) provides important context for the reported results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset and Generalizability:** A major concern is the reliance on the relatively small Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compound nouns). While a standard benchmark, the paper lacks sufficient detail regarding the dataset's construction, selection criteria, frequency distribution of the compounds, and the number of human judges involved. This limits the generalizability of the findings; the observed performance gains might be specific to this particular dataset and not reflective of broader trends in compositionality detection. The paper needs to address potential biases inherent in the dataset.
2. **Lack of Novelty in Alignment Method:** The authors allude to a previously suppressed reference detailing the “anchored packed trees” (APT) approach, raising concerns about the novelty of the alignment method. The paper needs to more clearly articulate the specific innovations of this work *relative* to their prior research, clarifying what aspects of the approach are truly new and contribute to the improved performance. The unexplained terminology (e.g. "elementary APTs") further obscures this.
3. **Methodological Inconsistency and Unclear Justification:** The use of higher-order dependency features for representation building, coupled with a similarity measurement based *only* on first-order features (as noted in footnote 3), is a methodological inconsistency that lacks sufficient justification. The rationale for discarding higher-order feature information during the final comparison step needs to be clearly explained. Furthermore, the explanation for why 2nd-order paths in the composed representations are “unreliable” is not convincing and requires elaboration with concrete examples.
4. **Readability and Clarity Issues:** Several sections of the paper suffer from a lack of clarity and contain confusing or contradictory statements. The argumentation in paragraphs 575–580 and 558–563 is particularly puzzling and needs to be significantly revised for better comprehension. Pronoun references are ambiguous in several instances. The writing would greatly benefit from illustrative examples and a more pedagogical approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the construction of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, including the selection criteria for the 90 compound nouns, the frequency distribution of these compounds in the corpus, and the number of human judges involved in the compositionality ratings?
2. Please clarify the specific novelty of the “anchored packed trees” (APT) approach presented in this paper compared to your previously suppressed work. What new aspects of the method contribute to the observed performance improvements?
3. Can you provide a more compelling justification for restricting the similarity measurement to first-order dependency features, given that higher-order features are used during the representation building phase?
4. Please elaborate on the reasoning behind the claim that 2nd-order paths in composed representations are “unreliable,” and provide concrete examples to illustrate this point.
5. Could you include results using the word2vec model to generate vectors *directly for the compound phrases* (rather than just for the constituent words) as another baseline for comparison?



**Additional Comments:**

- The significance of the hyperparameters 'h' and 'q' (equations 8-9) and their impact on the hybrid model's performance should be discussed more thoroughly.
- The ambiguous legend in Table 4 needs clarification. Specifically, indicate whether the additive combination was used in the reported results.
- The interpretation of the FREQ baseline is unclear. A more detailed explanation of why frequent compounds might be considered more compositional is required.
- Consider rephrasing sections of the paper with illustrative examples and a clearer, more accessible writing style.
