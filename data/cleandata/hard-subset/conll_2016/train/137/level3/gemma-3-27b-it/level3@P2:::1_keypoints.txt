- Primary contribution: Detailed exploration of incorporating dependency-path syntactic structure into sparse distributional representations; a hybrid aligned+unaligned dependency model outperforms standard neural embeddings on the Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality detection dataset.

- Secondary contribution: Empirical finding that smoothing PMI with α=0.75 gives a small but consistent improvement.

- Evaluation contribution: Proposes focusing on compositionality detection as a distinct evaluation methodology for compositional methods, separate from phrase similarity.

- Strength: Demonstrates that syntactic (dependency-based) contextual features improve compositionality detection accuracy over neural embeddings, highlighting the importance of linguistic structure.

- Strength: Introduces a hybrid aligned/unaligned dependency feature model that captures both strict alignments and broader contextual relationships, yielding modest gains.

- Strength: Presents a clear, reproducible experimental setup adapting Reddy et al. (2011) with explicit corpus/preprocessing details and Spearman correlation evaluation, plus baseline comparisons.

- Weakness: Heavy reliance on the small Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds) with insufficient detail on dataset construction, selection criteria, frequency distribution, and annotator information, limiting generalizability.

- Weakness: Novelty concerns around the “anchored packed trees” (APT) alignment method—unclear what is new relative to prior suppressed work and terminology (e.g., “elementary APTs”) is unexplained.

- Weakness: Methodological inconsistency in using higher-order dependency features to build representations but computing similarity using only first-order features; the dismissal of 2nd-order paths as “unreliable” lacks convincing justification and examples.

- Weakness: Readability and clarity problems in several sections (notably paragraphs 575–580 and 558–563), ambiguous pronouns, and a need for more illustrative examples and clearer exposition.

- Question: Provide details on the Reddy et al. dataset construction—selection criteria for the 90 compounds, their corpus frequency distribution, and number of human judges.

- Question: Clarify the specific novelty of the APT approach in this paper compared to the authors’ prior suppressed work and specify which aspects drive performance improvements.

- Question: Justify why similarity measurement is restricted to first-order dependency features despite building representations with higher-order features.

- Question: Elaborate on why 2nd-order paths in composed representations are considered “unreliable” and provide concrete examples illustrating this claim.

- Question: Include results where word2vec (or similar) is used to generate vectors directly for the complete compound phrases as an additional baseline.

- Additional comment: Discuss the roles and impact of hyperparameters h and q (eqs. 8–9) on hybrid-model performance.

- Additional comment: Clarify the ambiguous legend in Table 4 and explicitly state whether additive combination was used in reported results.

- Additional comment: Explain the interpretation of the FREQ baseline and why higher frequency might be taken as an indicator of compositionality.

- Additional comment: Revise problematic sections with more illustrative examples and a more pedagogical, accessible writing style.
