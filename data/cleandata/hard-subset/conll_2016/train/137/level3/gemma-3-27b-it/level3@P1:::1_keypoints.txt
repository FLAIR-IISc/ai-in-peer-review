- Paper investigates detection of non-compositional compound nouns by comparing dependency-based distributional models and neural embeddings, showing dependency models with higher-order features outperform standard neural embeddings.
- Main contribution: dependency-based distributional models leveraging syntactic context yield higher correlation with human judgments of compound compositionality.
- Secondary contribution: a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned dependency features gives marginal improvements over either alone.
- Empirical strength: thorough evaluation on Reddy et al. (2011) dataset with systematic parameter exploration, cross-validation, and baseline comparison.
- Methodological strength: detailed exploration of composition operations (INT, UNI), PPMI smoothing, and alignment strategies.
- Strength: investigation of aligned vs. unaligned dependency features reveals nuanced benefits of combining syntactic structure and semantic similarity.
- Weakness: dataset is small (90 compounds) with insufficient detail on selection criteria, frequency distributions, and annotator background, raising generalizability concerns.
- Weakness: contribution is incremental; aligned dependency vectors appear previously explored and improvements are modest.
- Weakness: clarity/readability issues—poor explanations of “elementary APTs,” feature order truncation, notation inconsistencies, and vague alignment justification.
- Question: provide detailed dataset information—selection criteria, compound/component frequency in a large corpus (e.g., ukWaC), and annotator counts/background.
- Question: clarify and exemplify the alignment strategy, especially why alignment for NMOD might decrease vector overlap.
- Question: report optimized hyperparameter values (h, q), their sensitivity, and results when training word2vec on compounds as single tokens.
- Question: revise unclear sections (3, 4, 5.1) and add running examples for concepts like “compound phrase token” and “elementary APTs.”
- Additional: define the APT acronym clearly at first use and consider visualizations for dependency paths and alignment.
- Additional: address potential dataset bias indicated by FREQ baseline and discuss study limitations and future directions.
