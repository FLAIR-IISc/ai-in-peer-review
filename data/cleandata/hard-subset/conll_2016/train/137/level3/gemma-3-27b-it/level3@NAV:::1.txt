## Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

This paper investigates the performance of compositional methods for predicting the compositionality of noun compounds, comparing syntactic-context count vectors with neural embeddings. The core finding is that the syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings, and while simply “aligning” vectors hurts performance, a tuned mixture of aligned and unaligned vectors offers slight gains.

The primary contribution, as I see it, lies in the empirical demonstration of the continued strength of count-based methods, specifically those leveraging typed dependency information, in a task focusing on compositional semantics. While not a novel method *per se*, the work provides a focused comparison and highlights the benefits of a more linguistically informed approach against the current trend toward neural methods. A secondary contribution is the exploration of combining aligned and unaligned dependency features, yielding a minor performance boost.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Experimental Setup:** The paper follows the established methodology of Reddy et al. (2011) for evaluating compositionality, allowing for a direct comparison.
2. **Interesting Results:** The finding that count-based methods outperform embeddings in this specific task challenges the prevailing view of embeddings’ superiority and warrants further investigation.
3. **Detailed Feature Engineering:**  The exploration of higher-order dependency features and the proposed “aligned” vs. unaligned vector approach demonstrate a thoughtful engagement with the problem of compositional representation. 
4. **Comprehensive Baseline:** The inclusion of several baselines (RAND, FREQ, Reddy et al.) provides a solid foundation for evaluating the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Innovation:** The novelty of the approach is questionable. The “aligned” vector method appears to be a revisiting of previously published work by the authors (suppressed reference), diminishing the perceived contribution. 
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The reliance on the small (90 compounds) Reddy et al. dataset raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings. Crucially, the paper lacks essential details about the dataset’s selection criteria, frequency ranges of compounds, and the number of human judges involved – making it difficult to assess potential biases.
3. **Methodological Inconsistency & Justification:** The use of higher-order dependency features generally, contrasted with similarity measurement relying only on first-order features, lacks sufficient justification. Note 3 is unconvincing in addressing this.
4. **Clarity and Readability:** The paper suffers from confusing terminology ("packed anchored tree"), unclear explanations (e.g., "elementary APTs," what constitutes a "compound phrase token"), and convoluted sentences throughout. The argumentation in sections 3, 5 (paragraphs 558-563 and 575-580) is particularly puzzling.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide details about the selection criteria for the 90 compounds in the Reddy et al. dataset, the frequency range of these compounds in the corpus, and the number of human judges used for annotation?
2.  Please clarify the definition of “elementary APTs” and elaborate on whether “shifted PMI” (mentioned in Section 5.1) directly corresponds to equation (3).
3.  Can you provide a running example illustrating the difference between a “compound phrase token” and its constituent representations?
4.  What constitutes "not properly aligned" in the context of vector alignment, and why would alignment *increase* overlap, particularly for NMOD relations?
5.  Please provide the values of the hyperparameters 'h' and 'q' (equations 8-9), and quantify the contribution of the hybrid approach to the observed gains.
