- Empirical finding: syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings for this compound compositionality task and dataset.
- Systematic analysis: investigated aligned vs. unaligned dependency features and found alignment alone hurts but a tuned hybrid yields slight improvements.
- Dataset/reproducibility: uses Reddy et al.’s benchmark and details experimental setup sufficiently to potentially replicate, despite dataset limits.

- Rigorous experiments: comprehensive evaluation across vector types, composition ops, and parameters with 3-fold cross-validation.
- Emphasis on syntax: leverages syntactic dependencies (APTs) to move beyond bag-of-words representations.
- Clear motivation: frames compositionality detection as a meaningful testbed and compares to baseline methods.

- Small/dubious dataset: reliance on Reddy et al.’s 90-compound dataset with missing details on selection, frequency, and annotator info undermines generalizability.
- Marginal gains: hybrid improvements are slight and may lack statistical robustness given dataset size and absent confidence intervals.
- Opaque methodology: APTs and “elementary APTs” are not clearly explained and lack examples showing feature reductions and order limitations.
- Similarity mismatch: using higher-order dependency features for representation but first-order similarity for comparison is insufficiently justified.
- Poor clarity: confusing sentence structure, unclear pronoun references, and inadequate explanation of alignment concepts (e.g., “not properly aligned”).
- Questionable baseline: correlation of compound frequency with compositionality suggests dataset bias and unclear intuition for FREQ baseline.
- Suppressed prior work: authors acknowledge a prior APT publication but omit its reference, raising novelty concerns.
