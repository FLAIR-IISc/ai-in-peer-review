This paper investigates the detection of non-compositional phrases, specifically compound nouns, by comparing different approaches to representing and composing word meaning. The authors explore both traditional distributional models based on dependency parsing and more recent neural word embeddings, focusing on the impact of incorporating syntactic context and aligning vector spaces during composition. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating that dependency-based distributional models, leveraging higher-order features, outperform standard neural embeddings in predicting human judgments of compound noun compositionality. A secondary, albeit smaller, contribution is the finding that a hybrid approach – combining aligned and unaligned dependency features – offers marginal gains over using either alone. Finally, the work presents a thorough empirical evaluation on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, providing a comparative analysis of different composition methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Superior Performance of Dependency-Based Models:** The key strength of this work is the clear demonstration that dependency-based distributional models, encoding syntactic relationships, achieve better performance in compositionality detection than methods relying solely on neural word embeddings. This supports the argument for incorporating richer linguistic information into semantic representations, particularly when dealing with non-compositional language. The consistently higher Spearman correlation coefficients across various configurations of the APT model are strong evidence for this claim.

2. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive experimental setup, systematically exploring different parameters and configurations for both the dependency-based and neural embedding approaches. The use of 3-fold cross-validation and the comparison against a baseline (Reddy et al., 2011) provide a robust framework for evaluating the proposed methods.  The exploration of PPMI smoothing and different composition operations (INT, UNI) shows a good level of methodological rigor.

3. **Detailed Exploration of Alignment Strategies:** The investigation of aligned vs. unaligned dependency features is a valuable contribution. The finding that a hybrid approach, judiciously combining both, yields the best results suggests a nuanced interplay between syntactic structure and semantic similarity. This finding moves beyond a simple binary choice and highlights the potential benefits of integrating different sources of information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset & Potential Bias:** The primary weakness concerns the dataset. The Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, consisting of only 90 compound nouns, is relatively small and the paper lacks crucial details about its construction. The selection criteria for the compounds, the frequency distribution of these compounds and their components in a larger corpus, and the number and background of the human judges are not reported.  This raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings and the potential for dataset-specific biases.  The observation that frequency correlates with human judgments (reflected in the FREQ baseline) suggests a possible issue.

2. **Incremental Contribution & Lack of Novelty:** While the results are positive, the innovation is incremental. The concept of using aligned dependency vectors seems to have been previously explored by the authors (referenced as a "suppressed reference"), and the improvement over existing methods, while statistically significant, is modest. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a breakthrough in compositionality detection. 

3. **Clarity and Readability Issues:** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in several sections. The explanation of “elementary APTs” and the rationale behind discarding features of order >3 is unclear.  Several sentences are confusing or contain contradictory statements. The notation (e.g., Eqs. 8-9) and terminology are not consistently explained, hindering understanding. The explanations regarding "proper alignment" and the impact on vector overlap are particularly vague and need substantial clarification. The justification for using only first-order features for similarity calculation (note 3) while utilizing higher-order features for representation is weak.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Details:** Please provide detailed information regarding the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. Specifically, what were the selection criteria for the 90 compound nouns? What are the frequency distributions of the compounds and their constituents in a large corpus like ukWaC? How many human judges were involved and what was their linguistic background?

2. **Alignment Strategy Justification:**  Can you elaborate on the intuition behind the alignment strategy? Why should alignment, particularly for NMOD relations, *decrease* overlap, as the current explanation seems counterintuitive? Provide concrete examples illustrating the alignment process.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper mentions optimizing hyperparameters 'h' and 'q', but does not report the optimal values or their impact on performance. Please provide these details.  Also, can you report the results when running the word2vec model on the *compounds* themselves as single tokens, instead of just their components?

4. **Clarification of Confusing Sections:**  Please revise sections 3, 4, and 5.1 to address the clarity issues identified above. Provide running examples to illustrate key concepts like "compound phrase token" and "elementary APTs."

**Additional Comments:**

- The acronym "APT" is used frequently without adequate initial definition and explanation.
- The paper could benefit from a visual representation of the dependency paths and the alignment process.
- The discussion of the FREQ baseline suggests the dataset may be biased; address this possibility more thoroughly.
- Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the study and future research directions.
