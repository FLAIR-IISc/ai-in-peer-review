This paper proposes a novel approach to predicting compound compositionality by leveraging compositional methods and comparing compound vectors to composed component vectors using cosine similarity and Spearman correlation. The authors explore the effectiveness of syntactic-context count vectors versus neural embeddings and examine the impact of using "aligned" versus plain vectors for dependents.

The main contributions of this work can be summarized as follows:

1. **Syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings**: The authors demonstrate that syntactic-context count vectors, which incorporate syntactic information, achieve better performance than neural embeddings in predicting compound compositionality.
2. **Tuned mix of aligned and unaligned vectors gives slight gains**: The authors show that a tuned mix of aligned and unaligned vectors for dependents can lead to slight improvements in performance, suggesting that both types of vectors capture complementary information.
3. **Compositional methods can improve compositionality detection**: The authors demonstrate that compositional methods can improve compositionality detection, which is an essential task in natural language processing.

The strengths of this paper include:

1. **Novel approach to predicting compound compositionality**: The authors propose a new approach to predicting compound compositionality, which leverages compositional methods and compares compound vectors to composed component vectors.
2. **Systematic evaluation of different methods**: The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of different methods, including neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors, to determine their effectiveness in predicting compound compositionality.
3. **Use of a well-established dataset**: The authors use the Reddy et al. dataset, which is a well-established benchmark for evaluating compositionality detection methods.

However, there are also some weaknesses and areas for improvement:

1. **Lack of clarity in terminology and notation**: The authors introduce new terminology, such as "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APTs," which may be unclear to readers. Additionally, the notation used in the paper could be improved for better readability.
2. **Dataset issues**: The authors use a relatively small dataset, and the selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of human judges are not reported. This may raise concerns about the generalizability of the results.
3. **Methodological inconsistencies**: The authors use higher-order dependency features generally but similarity measurement uses only first-order features. This inconsistency needs to be addressed and justified.
4. **Lack of transparency in experimental settings**: The authors do not provide sufficient details about the experimental settings, such as the hyperparameters used for the neural embeddings and the tuned mix of aligned and unaligned vectors.

To address these concerns, the authors could provide more details about the dataset, clarify the terminology and notation, and justify the methodological inconsistencies. Additionally, the authors could consider using a larger dataset and providing more transparency in the experimental settings.

Questions to authors:

1. Can you provide more details about the dataset, including the selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of human judges?
2. Can you clarify the terminology and notation used in the paper, particularly with regards to "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APTs"?
3. Can you justify the methodological inconsistency of using higher-order dependency features generally but similarity measurement uses only first-order features?
4. Can you provide more transparency in the experimental settings, including the hyperparameters used for the neural embeddings and the tuned mix of aligned and unaligned vectors?
5. Have you considered using a larger dataset to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed approach?
