This paper investigates the effectiveness of compositional methods in predicting compound compositionality. The authors use the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, which consists of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of their compositionality. The paper compares two approaches: a neural embedding-based approach and a syntactic-context count vector approach. The authors also explore the use of "aligned" vs. plain vectors for dependents.

The main finding of the paper is that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings. The authors also find that using a tuned mix of aligned and unaligned vectors gives slight gains. However, the innovation of the paper is unclear, as the use of aligned vectors seems to have been previously published by the authors.

The paper has several limitations. The dataset used is small and possibly biased, which makes the findings fragile. The authors do not report the selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of human judges used to create the dataset. Additionally, the paper does not address the issue of ties in human scores and their impact on Spearman correlation.

The terminology used in the paper is sometimes unclear, such as the use of the term "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APTs." The authors could have provided more explanations and examples to clarify these terms.

The paper's readability suffers in places, and some sentences are confusing or contradictory. The authors could have rephrased these sentences to make them clearer.

The methodology used in the paper has some inconsistencies. The authors use higher-order dependency features generally, but the similarity measurement uses only first-order features. The authors do not provide sufficient justification for this choice.

The paper's contributions are:

1. The authors demonstrate that syntactic-context count vectors can be used to predict compound compositionality, and that they outperform neural embeddings.
2. The authors explore the use of aligned and unaligned vectors for dependents and find that a tuned mix of both gives slight gains.

The strengths of the paper are:

1. The authors provide a thorough comparison of two approaches to compositional methods, which is useful for understanding the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
2. The authors provide a clear explanation of their methodology and results, which makes it easy to follow their argument.

The weaknesses of the paper are:

1. The dataset used is small and possibly biased, which makes the findings fragile.
2. The authors do not report the selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of human judges used to create the dataset.
3. The paper does not address the issue of ties in human scores and their impact on Spearman correlation.

Questions to authors:

1. Can you provide more information about the selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of human judges used to create the dataset?
2. How do you address the issue of ties in human scores and their impact on Spearman correlation?
3. Can you provide more explanations and examples to clarify the terminology used in the paper, such as "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APTs"?

Additional comments:

* The paper could benefit from more examples and illustrations to help explain the concepts and methodology.
* The authors could have provided more discussion on the implications of their findings and how they relate to other research in the field.
* The paper's readability could be improved by breaking up long sentences and paragraphs into shorter ones.
