- Proposes predicting compound compositionality by comparing compound vectors to composed component vectors using cosine similarity and Spearman correlation.  
- Compares neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors, and examines "aligned" vs plain dependent vectors.  
- Main finding: syntactic-context count vectors (APTs) outperform neural embeddings; a tuned mix of aligned and unaligned vectors yields slight improvements.  
- Contribution: provides a comprehensive evaluation of compositionality detection methods across vector types.  
- Contribution: introduces anchored packed trees (APTs), a new syntactic-context-aware representation that boosts performance.  
- Contribution: investigates vector alignment for dependents and shows a tuned alignment mix gives small gains.  
- Strength: thorough, systematic evaluation across methods and representations.  
- Strength: novel APT representation that captures syntactic context effectively.  
- Strength: careful experiments with clearly presented results.  
- Weakness: parts of the paper (notably Section 3) lack clarity and need more examples/explication of APTs.  
- Weakness: dataset is small (90 compounds) and selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of human judges are not reported, harming generalizability.  
- Weakness: methodological inconsistency—paper uses higher-order dependency features generally but similarity measurement relies only on first-order features without justification.  
- Weakness: readability issues; sections would benefit from running examples and clearer exposition.  
- Question: please provide more examples and clarification on APTs, especially in Section 3.  
- Question: report dataset selection criteria, frequency ranges, number of human judges, include word2vec results for compound phrases, justify the first-order feature choice, and clarify the FREQ baseline intuition (why frequent compounds are deemed more compositional).
