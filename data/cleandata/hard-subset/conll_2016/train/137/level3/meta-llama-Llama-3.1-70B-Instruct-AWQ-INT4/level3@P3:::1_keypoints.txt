- Task: compares neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors for predicting compound compositionality using Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds).
- Main result: syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings.
- Secondary result: a tuned mix of aligned and unaligned dependent vectors yields slight additional gains.
- Novelty concern: use of aligned vectors appears previously published by the authors, so innovation is unclear.
- Dataset limitation: very small and possibly biased, making results fragile.
- Missing dataset details: selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of human judges are not reported.
- Evaluation issue: paper does not discuss ties in human scores or their impact on Spearman correlation.
- Terminology unclear: terms like "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APTs" need clearer definitions and examples.
- Readability problems: some sentences are confusing or contradictory and need rephrasing.
- Methodology inconsistency: higher-order dependency features are used generally but similarity measurements use only first-order features without sufficient justification.
- Contribution claim 1: shows syntactic-context count vectors can predict compound compositionality and outperform neural embeddings.
- Contribution claim 2: investigates aligned vs. unaligned dependent vectors and finds small gains from mixing them.
- Strength: thorough comparison of two compositionality approaches, clarifying relative strengths/weaknesses.
- Strength: methodology and results are presented clearly enough to follow the argument.
- Weakness summary: small/biased dataset, missing dataset metadata, and unaddressed ties in human scores.
- Questions for authors: provide dataset selection/frequency/judges info; explain handling of ties and Spearman correlation; clarify terminology with examples.
- Suggested improvements: include more examples/illustrations, discuss implications and relation to prior work, and improve readability (shorter sentences/paragraphs).
