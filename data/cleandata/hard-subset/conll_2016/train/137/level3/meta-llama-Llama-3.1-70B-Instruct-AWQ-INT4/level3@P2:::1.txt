**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach to predicting compound compositionality by comparing compound vectors to composed component vectors using cosine similarity and Spearman correlation. The authors compare two types of vector representations: neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors. They also investigate the use of "aligned" vs plain vectors for dependents. The main finding is that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, and a tuned mix of aligned and unaligned vectors gives slight gains.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Evaluation of compositionality detection methods**: The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation of different compositionality detection methods, including neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors. This evaluation is a significant contribution to the field of distributional semantics.
2. **Introduction of anchored packed trees (APTs)**: The authors propose a new representation, APTs, which takes into account the syntactic context of words. This representation is shown to outperform neural embeddings in predicting compound compositionality.
3. **Investigation of alignment in composition**: The paper investigates the effect of aligning vectors for dependents in composition. The results show that a tuned mix of aligned and unaligned vectors gives slight gains.

**Strengths**

1. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation of different compositionality detection methods, including neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors.
2. **Novel representation**: The introduction of APTs is a significant contribution to the field of distributional semantics. APTs take into account the syntactic context of words, which is shown to be important in predicting compound compositionality.
3. **Careful experimentation**: The authors have conducted careful experiments to evaluate the performance of different methods. The results are presented in a clear and concise manner.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of clarity in some sections**: Some sections of the paper, such as Section 3, are unclear and need further explanation. The authors should provide more examples and clarification on the use of APTs.
2. **Dataset issues**: The paper uses a small dataset of 90 compounds, and the selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of human judges are not reported. This makes it difficult to evaluate the generalizability of the results.
3. **Methodological inconsistency**: The paper uses higher-order dependency features generally, but similarity measurement uses only first-order features. The authors should provide a clear justification for this inconsistency.
4. **Readability**: The paper's readability suffers in places, and the authors should consider rephrasing some sections with running examples and clearer exposition.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more examples and clarification on the use of APTs, especially in Section 3?
2. Can you report the selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of human judges for the dataset used in the paper?
3. Can you provide a clear justification for using only first-order features in similarity measurement, despite using higher-order dependency features generally?
4. Can you include results using word2vec vectors for compound phrases, not just components?
5. Can you clarify the baseline FREQ intuition and why frequent compounds are more compositional?
