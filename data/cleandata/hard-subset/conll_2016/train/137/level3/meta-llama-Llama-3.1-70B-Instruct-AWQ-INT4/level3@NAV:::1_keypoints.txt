- Paper proposes using compositional methods to detect non-compositional compound nouns, comparing integrated sparse high-dimensional representations to simple neural embedding compositions.
- New integrated compositional distributional model using sparse high-dimensional representations is proposed and claimed to outperform existing models.
- Paper evaluates multiple compositional models, including neural word embeddings, comparing their strengths and weaknesses.
- Uses the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human compositionality judgments for evaluation.
- Proposed model achieves strong performance (Spearman’s rho = 0.79), outperforming prior approaches.
- Evaluation on the dataset is presented as robust evidence of the model’s effectiveness.
- Provides a comprehensive comparison of compositional models highlighting relative merits.
- Explanations of the proposed model and its components lack clarity and detail.
- Evaluation is limited to a single dataset; broader testing on other datasets is needed.
- Methodological inconsistencies (different metrics/methods across models) hinder fair comparisons.
- Requests clarification on model details such as “packed anchored tree” and “elementary APTs.”
- Asks how authors will address methodological inconsistencies in evaluations.
- Seeks more dataset details (selection criteria, frequency ranges, number of human judges).
- Overall, the paper contributes to compositional semantics but would benefit from clearer explanations and more consistent, broader evaluation.
