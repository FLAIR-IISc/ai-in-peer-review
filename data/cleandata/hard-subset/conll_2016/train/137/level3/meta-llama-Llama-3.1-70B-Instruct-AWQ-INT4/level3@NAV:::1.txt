**Summary of the Paper**

This paper explores the use of compositional methods to improve the detection of non-compositional phrases, specifically in the context of compound nouns. The authors propose an integrated compositional distributional approach, using sparse high-dimensional representations, and compare it to an ad-hoc compositional approach using simple composition operations on state-of-the-art neural embeddings. The paper uses the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality and evaluates the performance of different compositional models using Spearman's rank correlation coefficient.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Proposal of a new compositional model**: The authors propose an integrated compositional distributional approach using sparse high-dimensional representations, which outperforms existing compositional models.
2. **Evaluation of different compositional models**: The paper evaluates the performance of different compositional models, including neural word embeddings, and provides a comparison of their strengths and weaknesses.
3. **Use of a large dataset**: The paper uses a large dataset of compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality, which provides a robust evaluation of the proposed compositional model.

**Strengths**

1. **Improved performance**: The proposed compositional model outperforms existing models, achieving a Spearman's rank correlation coefficient of 0.79.
2. **Robust evaluation**: The use of a large dataset provides a robust evaluation of the proposed model, demonstrating its effectiveness in detecting non-compositional phrases.
3. **Comparison of compositional models**: The paper provides a comprehensive comparison of different compositional models, highlighting their strengths and weaknesses.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of clarity**: The paper could benefit from clearer explanations of the proposed compositional model and its components.
2. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the proposed model on a single dataset, and it would be beneficial to evaluate its performance on other datasets as well.
3. **Methodological inconsistencies**: The paper uses different evaluation metrics and methods for different compositional models, which can make it difficult to compare their performance.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the proposed compositional model and its components, including the "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APTs"?
2. How do you plan to address the methodological inconsistencies in the evaluation of different compositional models?
3. Can you provide more information on the dataset used in the paper, including the selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of human judges?

**Additional Comments**

The paper could benefit from clearer explanations of the proposed compositional model and its components. Additionally, the evaluation of different compositional models could be improved by using consistent evaluation metrics and methods. Overall, the paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics, and its findings have implications for the development of more effective compositional models.
