- Task: Predicting compositionality of English compound nouns using cosine similarity and Spearman correlation.
- Methods compared: neural embeddings vs syntactic-context count vectors; hybrid of aligned and unaligned vector spaces performs best.
- Use of APTs: anchored packed trees encode syntactic structure, emphasizing syntactic dependencies for compositionality detection.
- Empirical result: syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings and prior state-of-the-art.
- Hybrid contribution: combining aligned and unaligned vectors yields a small but consistent improvement.
- Presentation: clear introduction and motivation situating the work in distributional semantics.
- Evaluation: comprehensive testing of configurations (PPMI smoothing, composition ops) with statistically significant results.
- Dataset limitation: small size (90 compounds) with insufficient detail on selection and potential frequency biases.
- Clarity issues: dense terminology (e.g., “packed anchored tree,” “elementary APTs”) and hard-to-follow sections; needs examples/diagrams.
- Innovation concern: reliance on prior work for aligned vectors makes the hybrid approach incrementally novel rather than groundbreaking.
- Baseline issue: unclear rationale for FREQ baseline (assumes frequent compounds are compositional), suggesting possible dataset bias.
- Reporting gaps: key hybrid parameters (h, q) and the effect of PPMI smoothing (α = 0.75) are underreported and unexplained.
- Question: clarify how the 90 compounds were selected and whether frequency or linguistic thresholds were applied.
- Question: explain how ties in human compositionality judgments were handled during evaluation.
- Question: provide the intuition behind the FREQ baseline and how its potential bias is addressed.
- Question: give more details on parameter optimization for h and q in the hybrid model.
- Question: discuss how the APT approach could scale to larger datasets or more complex phrases.
- Recommendation: accept with major revisions focused on clarity, dataset robustness, and stronger justification/reporting of methods.
