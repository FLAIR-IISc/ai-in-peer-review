

This paper investigates compositionality detection for compound nouns, comparing an integrated compositional distributional approach (using sparse high-dimensional representations based on typed dependency paths) with applying simple composition operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings. The main contribution is demonstrating that syntactic-context count vectors significantly outperform neural embeddings for this specific task, with a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned representations yielding the best results (Spearman ρ=0.79 vs. previous state-of-the-art 0.71). A second contribution is showing that higher-order typed dependency paths provide more informative features than untyped representations. The third contribution is the novel method of evaluating compositionality through direct comparison of composed and observed phrase representations, avoiding confounding with phrase similarity judgments.

Strengths: The paper addresses a meaningful problem in distributional semantics where compositionality detection provides a clean evaluation metric for compositional methods. The results are clear and significant, with the best model achieving 0.79 Spearman correlation with human judgments, a notable improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.71). The hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned representations) provides nuanced insight into how different contextual features contribute to compositionality. The use of the same Reddy et al. dataset and evaluation protocol enables direct comparison with prior work.

Weaknesses: The novelty of the approach is questionable as the "aligned" vector space method appears to be previously published by the authors (suppressed reference), making the claimed innovation unclear. The dataset is small (90 compounds) and lacks critical details: selection criteria, frequency ranges of compounds and constituents, number of human judges, and how ties in human scores were handled, which undermines the robustness of the findings. The paper has significant clarity issues, particularly regarding the APT method, alignment process, and why higher-order features are used during composition but only first-order features are used in evaluation. The justification for using only first-order features in evaluation despite using higher-order features during composition is insufficient (note 3 is unclear). The marginal gains on a small, potentially biased dataset make the findings fragile. The FREQ baseline (using compound frequency as predictor) performs better than RAND, suggesting a possible dataset bias where frequent compounds are more compositional, but this is not properly addressed.

Questions to authors: Please clarify the novelty of the "aligned" vector space method compared to your previous work. Provide detailed information about the dataset: selection criteria, frequency ranges of compounds and constituents, and number of human judges. Explain why the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned representations) provides the best results, including the contribution of each component. Justify the use of only first-order features in evaluation despite using higher-order features during composition. Explain why the FREQ baseline performs well and how this might indicate dataset bias. Provide running examples to clarify the "compound phrase token" versus constituent representations and the "elementary APTs" concept.

The paper's methodology is promising but its presentation and evaluation lack sufficient rigor. The small dataset size combined with unclear methodology details and questionable novelty make the claimed improvements less convincing. The paper would benefit from a more thorough description of the APT method with concrete examples, better justification for methodological choices, and more comprehensive dataset details. The marginal gains on a small dataset suggest the findings may not generalize well, and the paper's claims about the importance of syntactic structure would be stronger with more extensive evaluation on larger, more diverse datasets. The paper's readability suffers from unclear explanations of key concepts like "elementary APTs" and the alignment process, which would benefit from additional examples and clearer exposition.
