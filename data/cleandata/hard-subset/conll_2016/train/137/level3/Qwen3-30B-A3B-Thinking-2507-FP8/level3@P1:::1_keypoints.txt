- Main contribution: syntactic-context count vectors (typed dependency APTs) significantly outperform neural embeddings for compound noun compositionality detection.  
- Best result: hybrid aligned+unaligned representations achieve Spearman ρ=0.79 vs prior SOTA 0.71.  
- Contribution: higher-order typed dependency paths yield more informative features than untyped/first-order representations.  
- Contribution: novel evaluation method comparing composed and observed phrase representations to directly assess compositionality.  
- Strength: addresses a meaningful, clean evaluation problem in distributional semantics.  
- Strength: clear, significant empirical improvement on the Reddy et al. dataset.  
- Strength: hybrid approach provides insight into complementary roles of aligned and unaligned features.  
- Strength: uses the same dataset and protocol as prior work, enabling direct comparison.  
- Weakness: novelty is unclear—“aligned” vector space method appears previously published by the authors.  
- Weakness: dataset is very small (90 compounds) and lacks crucial metadata (selection criteria, frequency ranges, number of judges, tie handling).  
- Weakness: methodological clarity issues around the APT method, alignment process, and the distinction between compound token vs. constituent representations.  
- Weakness: inconsistent use of feature orders—higher-order features used in composition but only first-order used in evaluation without sufficient justification.  
- Weakness: marginal gains on a small, potentially biased dataset raise concerns about robustness and generalizability.  
- Weakness: FREQ baseline outperforming RAND suggests dataset bias (frequent compounds more compositional) that is not properly addressed.  
- Questions for authors: clarify novelty of aligned method and differences from prior work.  
- Questions for authors: provide full dataset details (selection, frequencies, judge counts, tie handling).  
- Questions for authors: explain why the hybrid model outperforms—quantify each component’s contribution.  
- Questions for authors: justify evaluation using only first-order features despite higher-order composition.  
- Questions for authors: explain the strong FREQ baseline and potential dataset bias.  
- Request: include running examples to clarify “compound phrase token” vs constituent representations and the “elementary APTs” concept.
