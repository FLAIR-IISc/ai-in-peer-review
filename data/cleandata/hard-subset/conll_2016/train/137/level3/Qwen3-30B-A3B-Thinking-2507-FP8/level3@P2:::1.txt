

# Review for "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates compositionality detection for compound nouns by comparing observed phrase representations with those obtained through composition of constituent representations. The main contributions are: (1) A demonstration that syntactic-contextual distributional features significantly outperform neural embeddings for compositionality detection, (2) A novel hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned APT representations that provides marginal improvements over previous methods, and (3) A detailed analysis of how dependency structure affects compositional modeling. The authors use Reddy et al.'s 90-compound dataset with human compositionality judgments to evaluate their approach.

## Strengths
1. **Clear problem framing**: The paper effectively argues that compositionality detection provides a better testbed for compositional methods than phrase similarity tasks, as it avoids confounding compositionality judgments with similarity judgments. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field.

2. **Meaningful performance improvement**: The paper achieves a Spearman correlation of 0.79 with human judgments, which is a statistically significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.71). This represents a meaningful contribution to the field, particularly given the difficulty of the task.

3. **Insightful analysis of compositionality**: The paper provides valuable insights about the relationship between syntactic structure and compositionality, demonstrating that higher-order dependency paths encode important compositional information. The finding that the modifier contributes slightly more to compositionality than the head is an interesting linguistic insight.

## Weaknesses
1. **Questionable novelty**: The core method (APTs with alignment) appears to be a refinement of the authors' previous work (suppressed reference), with insufficient explanation of how this differs from prior publications. This undermines the paper's contribution claim.

2. **Poorly described dataset**: The paper uses Reddy et al.'s 90-compound dataset without providing essential details: selection criteria, frequency ranges of compounds, number of human judges, or how ties in human scores were handled. This makes the results appear fragile and potentially biased.

3. **Methodological inconsistency**: The paper uses higher-order dependency features in the model but restricts similarity computation to first-order features (note 3 in Section 5.1). This inconsistency is not adequately justified, undermining the validity of the comparison.

4. **Unaddressed baseline issue**: The FREQ baseline (using compound frequency) performs much better than RAND (0.63 vs 0.02), suggesting a potential dataset bias where frequent compounds are more likely to be compositional. The authors fail to address this fundamental question about their dataset.

5. **Readability and clarity issues**: The paper suffers from unclear terminology ("packed anchored tree," "elementary APTs"), contradictory explanations, and insufficient examples. Section 3 in particular is confusing regarding discarded features and order >3, making it difficult to replicate or understand the method.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please clarify the novelty of the APT approach compared to your previous work (suppressed reference), and explain why this constitutes a significant advance.

2. Provide details about the 90 compounds: selection criteria, frequency ranges, number of human judges, and how ties in human scores were handled.

3. Justify the use of only first-order features for similarity computation when the model uses higher-order features. Why isn't the full higher-order representation used?

4. Explain why the FREQ baseline performs significantly better than RAND. Is this due to a dataset bias where frequent compounds are more likely to be compositional?

5. Please provide a running example illustrating how "compound phrase token" representations differ from constituent representations, and clarify the meaning of "elementary APTs."

## Overall Recommendation
The paper makes a meaningful contribution to compositionality detection by demonstrating that syntactic-contextual features significantly outperform neural embeddings. However, the questionable novelty, poorly described dataset, methodological inconsistencies, and clarity issues significantly undermine the paper's impact. With substantial revisions addressing these concerns, particularly the novelty claims, dataset description, and methodological inconsistencies, this paper could be a strong contribution. As currently written, it is borderline for acceptance.
