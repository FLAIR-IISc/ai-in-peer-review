- Contributions: shows syntactic-context count vectors beat neural embeddings for compositionality prediction.  
- Contributions: reports marginal gains from a hybrid of aligned and unaligned vectors.  
- Contributions: presents a new dataset of 90 compounds for compositionality evaluation.  
- Strength: Highlights the importance of syntactic structure for compositionality detection.  
- Strength: Provides a useful comparison of different vector representations for practitioners.  
- Strength: Treats compositionality detection as a sensible testbed for compositional methods.  
- Weakness: Innovation is questionable because "aligned vectors" seem previously published by the authors.  
- Weakness: Dataset is small (90 compounds), poorly described, and potentially biased, making results fragile.  
- Weakness: Key methodological details are missing or unclear (e.g., definition of "elementary APTs", what "alignment" means).  
- Weakness: No justification for using only first-order features in similarity measures.  
- Weakness: Several contradictory statements (e.g., about low positive PMI) undermine credibility.  
- Weakness: The FREQ baseline intuition is unclear and may indicate dataset bias.  
- Weakness: Readability suffers from ambiguous terminology and lack of concrete examples.  
- Question: Please define "elementary APTs" and confirm whether "shifted PMI" corresponds to Eq. (3).  
- Question: Provide dataset selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of human judges.  
- Question: Explain why the hybrid model's gains are marginal and quantify how much hybridity contributes.  
- Question: Clarify the relationship and consistent usage of "constituent" vs "component" terminology.
