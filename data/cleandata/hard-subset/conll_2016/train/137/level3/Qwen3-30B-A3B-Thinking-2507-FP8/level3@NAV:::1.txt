

This paper investigates compound compositionality prediction by comparing compound vectors to composed component vectors using cosine similarity and Spearman correlation. The main contributions, as I see them, are: 1) demonstrating that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings for compositionality prediction, 2) showing that a hybrid of aligned and unaligned vectors provides marginal gains over either approach alone, and 3) presenting a dataset of 90 compounds for compositionality evaluation.

Strengths: The paper makes a valid point about the importance of syntactic structure in compositionality detection, which is valuable for the field. The comparison between different vector representations provides useful insights for practitioners. The focus on compositionality detection as a testbed for compositional methods is conceptually sound.

Weaknesses: The innovation is questionable as "aligned vectors" appear previously published by the authors (suppressed reference). The dataset is small (90 compounds), poorly described (missing selection criteria, frequency ranges, number of human judges), and potentially biased, making findings fragile despite marginal gains. Key methodological details are missing or unclear: the definition of "elementary APTs" is absent, justification for using only first-order features in similarity measurement is insufficient, and the explanation of "alignment" is confusing. Several contradictory statements undermine credibility (e.g., about low positive PMI). The baseline FREQ intuition is unclear and may indicate dataset bias. The paper's readability suffers from ambiguous terminology and lack of concrete examples.

Questions to Authors: Please clarify the definition of "elementary APTs" and whether "shifted PMI" corresponds to Equation (3). Provide the selection criteria, frequency ranges, and number of human judges for the dataset. Explain why the hybrid model's gains are marginal and how much hybridity contributes to performance. Clarify the relationship between the "constituent" and "component" terminology throughout the paper.
