- Summary: Compares syntactic-context count vectors (anchored packed trees) vs neural embeddings for detecting compositionality on Reddy et al.'s 90 compound noun dataset.

- Contribution 1: Provides an empirical benchmark for compositionality detection methods.

- Contribution 2: Shows syntactic-context count vectors can outperform neural embeddings on this task.

- Contribution 3: Explores alignment techniques for composition, though alignment novelty is limited.

- Strength  1: Tackles an important task that directly evaluates compositionality rather than just phrase similarity.

- Strength  2: Valuable empirical comparison between count-based and neural approaches amid the neural-dominant trend.

- Strength  3: Clearly demonstrates limits of naive composition with neural embeddings for practitioners.

- Weakness 1: Claimed "aligned vectors" approach appears previously published by the authors, undermining novelty and attribution.

- Weakness 2: Dataset usage is poorly reported (selection criteria, frequencies, number of judges, tie handling), harming interpretability and reproducibility.

- Weakness 3: Methodological inconsistencies: uses higher-order dependency features for composition but evaluates similarity on first-order features without convincing justification or examples.

- Weakness 4: Improvements are marginal (0.01–0.02 Spearman) on a small, possibly biased dataset (strong FREQ baseline), limiting generalizability.

- Weakness 5: Paper has confusing terminology and unclear explanations (e.g., "packed anchored tree," "elementary APTs," alignment rationale).

- Question 1: Define "elementary APTs" and clarify differences from standard dependency features and whether this is in the suppressed reference.

- Question 2: Why does compound frequency (FREQ) correlate so strongly with compositionality (0.63)—does this reflect dataset bias?

- Question 3: Provide concrete examples showing why second-order paths are unreliable after composition and why measuring only first-order similarity is justified.

- Question 4: What specific effect does the hybrid APT method (0.79) add over the unaligned APT (0.78)?

- Question 5: How much of the reported hybrid performance gain is due to the hybrid method itself versus simply using unaligned vectors?
