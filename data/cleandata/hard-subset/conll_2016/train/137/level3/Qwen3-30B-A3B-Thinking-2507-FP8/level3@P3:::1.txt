

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary
This paper investigates methods for detecting compositionality in compound nouns by comparing compound phrase vectors to vectors composed from their constituent parts. The authors compare syntactic-context count vectors (using "anchored packed trees" with various alignment techniques) against neural embeddings with naive compositional operations. They use the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human compositionality judgments to evaluate their methods.

## Main Contributions
1. The paper's strongest contribution is the empirical evaluation of compositional methods for compositionality detection, which provides a useful benchmark for future work in this specific evaluation task.
2. The secondary contribution is the demonstration that syntactic-context count vectors can outperform neural embeddings for this specific task, challenging the common assumption that neural embeddings are universally superior.
3. The tertiary contribution is the exploration of alignment techniques for compositional methods, though this is undermined by the lack of novelty in the alignment approach.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses an important problem: compositionality detection provides a more nuanced evaluation of compositional methods than simple phrase similarity tasks, as it directly assesses whether compositionality can be modeled rather than just measuring similarity.

2. The empirical comparison between count-based and neural embedding approaches for this specific task is valuable, especially given the current trend toward neural embeddings in NLP research.

3. The paper provides a clear demonstration of the limitations of naive composition methods when applied to neural embeddings, which is useful for practitioners.

## Weaknesses
1. **Innovation and Novelty**: The paper's "aligned vectors" approach appears to be previously published by the authors (with a suppressed reference), making the claimed innovation questionable. The paper fails to adequately distinguish their approach from prior work, which undermines the contribution.

2. **Dataset Description**: The paper uses Reddy et al.'s 90 compounds but fails to report critical details including selection criteria, frequency ranges for compounds and components, number of human judges, and how ties in human scores were handled. This makes the results difficult to interpret and reproduce, and raises questions about potential dataset bias.

3. **Methodological Inconsistencies**: The paper uses higher-order dependency features for composition but then measures similarity only on first-order features without justification. The explanation for why second-order paths are unreliable in composed representations is not convincing and lacks examples. The paper also uses "elementary APTs" without clear definition or examples.

4. **Marginal Results**: The claimed improvements are marginal (0.01-0.02 in Spearman correlation) on a small, poorly described dataset. This makes the findings fragile and their generalizability questionable, especially given the dataset's possible bias (as evidenced by the strong FREQ baseline).

5. **Clarity and Presentation**: The paper suffers from confusing terminology ("packed anchored tree," "elementary APTs"), contradictory statements, and unclear explanations. For instance, the explanation of alignment ("not properly aligned") and the rationale for using unaligned vectors as part of the hybrid approach are not adequately clarified.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims to use "elementary APTs" without clearly defining them. Can you clarify how they differ from standard dependency features, and whether this concept is defined in the suppressed reference?

2. The FREQ baseline (using compound frequency as a predictor) achieves 0.63, significantly better than RAND (0.02). What is the rationale for this correlation between frequency and compositionality? Does this indicate a dataset bias?

3. The paper uses higher-order dependency features for composition but only measures similarity on first-order features. Can you provide specific examples to illustrate why second-order paths are unreliable in composed representations, and why this is sufficient for the similarity measurement?

4. Table 5 shows that the hybrid APT method (0.79) slightly outperforms the unaligned APT method (0.78). What is the specific contribution of the hybrid approach, and why is it better than simply using unaligned vectors?

5. The paper states that "the unaligned model outperforms the aligned model" but then claims a "small but significant performance gain" with the hybrid method. How much of this gain comes from the hybrid approach versus simply using unaligned vectors alone?
