- Measures compositionality by cosine similarity between an observed compound vector and a vector composed from its constituents, comparing word2vec and dependency-based APT representations; hybrid APT wins on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.  
- Contribution: Empirically shows sparse, dependency-based vectors with syntactic features outperform standard neural embeddings for noun-compound compositionality.  
- Contribution: Proposes and evaluates a syntax-aware alignment mechanism for modifier-to-head vector composition and shows a hybrid of aligned/unaligned features yields small gains.  
- Strength: Strong empirical comparison and new state-of-the-art result on the benchmark, outperforming word2vec and prior baselines.  
- Strength: Linguistically motivated modeling that integrates syntactic structure into composition beyond simple vector addition.  
- Strength: Thorough baseline comparisons and reasonable hyperparameter tuning for both APT and word2vec models.  
- Weakness: Poorly explained methodology—key concepts like APTs, elementary APTs, and alignment/reduction lack clear definitions and examples, hurting reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Evaluation relies on a single small dataset (90 compounds) with missing dataset details and a surprisingly strong frequency baseline, raising concerns about bias and significance.  
- Weakness: Unclear novelty relative to the authors’ prior APT work; contribution may be incremental (application + interpolation).  
- Question: Clarify the novel contributions over prior APT work—alignment method vs. application/hybrid formulation?  
- Question: Justify restricting final similarity to 1st-order dependency features and provide examples showing composed higher-order features are unreliable.  
- Question: Report actual learned interpolation parameters h and q and quantify how much gain comes specifically from the hybrid component (q=1 vs. optimal q).  
- Question: Explain why the FREQ baseline performs strongly and analyze dataset properties (frequency distribution, inter-annotator agreement) and potential bias.  
- Question: Provide a step-by-step running example (e.g., “spelling bee”) showing elementary APTs, aligned modifier APT, final hybrid composed APT, and the observed compound APT.
