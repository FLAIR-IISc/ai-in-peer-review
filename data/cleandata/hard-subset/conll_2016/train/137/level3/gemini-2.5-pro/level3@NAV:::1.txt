This paper investigates the prediction of compound noun compositionality by comparing observed compound vectors with vectors composed from their constituents. The primary contribution is the proposed "Anchored Packed Tree" (APT) model, a sparse, count-based representation using higher-order dependency paths. The authors claim that a hybrid version of their APT model, which combines "aligned" and "unaligned" component vectors, achieves a new state-of-the-art on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, outperforming both neural embeddings and previous methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  The paper provides a valuable and direct comparison between structured, count-based distributional models and popular neural embeddings for the task of compositionality prediction. This systematic evaluation addresses an important question in distributional semantics.
2.  The central finding—that sparse, syntax-aware count vectors outperform dense embeddings—is significant. It serves as a compelling reminder that for tasks requiring fine-grained syntactic awareness, explicit dependency features can be more powerful than the implicitly learned features of models like word2vec.
3.  The exploration of different composition strategies within the APT framework (aligned, unaligned, and a hybrid model) is methodologically interesting. The finding that a tuned mixture of aligned and unaligned features yields the best results is a nuanced contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  The empirical claims are built on a fragile foundation. The experiments rely exclusively on a small (90 compounds) and poorly described dataset. The authors do not report the original selection criteria, frequency distribution, or details about the human judges, making it difficult to assess potential biases. The strong performance of a simple frequency baseline further suggests the dataset may have confounding properties. The marginal gains reported are therefore not convincing.
2.  The paper suffers from significant clarity issues that hinder reproducibility and understanding. The core alignment method and key terms like "elementary APTs" are poorly explained. The justification for a major methodological choice—using higher-order features to build vectors but only first-order features for the final similarity comparison—is unconvincing and relegated to an unclear footnote. The exposition would benefit greatly from running examples to illustrate the concepts.
3.  The novelty of the core method is questionable. The "aligned" vector approach appears to be from the authors' own previously published (suppressed) work, reducing the innovative contribution of this submission. Furthermore, key details about the best-performing hybrid model, such as the hyperparameters controlling the mixture, are not reported, making it impossible to assess the specific contribution of the proposed combination.
