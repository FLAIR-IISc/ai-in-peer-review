### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates methods for detecting the compositionality of noun compounds. The core approach is to measure the similarity (using cosine) between the distributional vector of an observed compound phrase (e.g., "red herring") and a vector composed from the representations of its constituent words (e.g., "red" and "herring"). The correlation (Spearman's ρ) between this similarity score and human compositionality judgments is used as the evaluation metric. The study compares two main types of vector representations: dense, low-dimensional neural word embeddings (word2vec) and sparse, high-dimensional, count-based vectors derived from typed dependency paths (which the authors term Anchored Packed Trees or APTs). For the dependency-based vectors, the authors also explore a syntax-aware composition method that "aligns" the modifier's vector space to the head's space before combination. The main finding is that the dependency-based APT models outperform neural embedding models, with a hybrid model that interpolates between aligned and unaligned modifier vectors achieving the best results on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.

### Contributions

1.  **An empirical demonstration of the value of explicit syntactic features for compositionality detection.** The paper provides a strong comparative analysis showing that sparse vector models using typed higher-order dependency paths significantly outperform standard, state-of-the-art neural word embeddings (word2vec) on this task. This finding highlights the importance of rich, structured syntactic information for fine-grained semantic tasks like compositionality modeling, which is a valuable contribution.
2.  **An investigation of a syntax-aware alignment mechanism for vector composition.** The paper introduces and evaluates a method for aligning vector spaces based on the syntactic relationship between a modifier and a head. While the results show that this alignment procedure alone does not improve performance, the analysis thoughtfully explores its combination with unaligned features, showing that a hybrid model can yield small but consistent gains.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Comparison and Results.** The paper presents a well-designed comparison between two dominant paradigms in distributional semantics: dense prediction-based embeddings and sparse count-based vectors. The results convincingly show that for the task of modeling noun compound compositionality, the linguistically-informed features from dependency parses provide a much stronger signal than the more general contexts captured by word2vec. The proposed hybrid APT model achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the benchmark dataset, surpassing the original baseline by a significant margin.
2.  **Linguistically Motivated Modeling.** The APT approach, which attempts to integrate syntactic structure directly into the composition process through feature alignment, is a well-motivated and interesting research direction. While simple vector addition remains a powerful baseline, the paper’s exploration of more structured compositional operations is a valuable step towards more sophisticated models of meaning.
3.  **Thorough Investigation of Baselines and Parameters.** The authors compare their method against several relevant baselines, including the original result from Reddy et al. (2011), a frequency baseline, and a strong baseline using modern word2vec embeddings. The hyperparameter tuning for both the APT and word2vec models appears reasonably thorough, strengthening the validity of the final comparison.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Methodological Exposition.** The paper's central weakness is the poor explanation of its core method. Key concepts like "Anchored Packed Trees" and "elementary APTs" are not clearly defined, and the crucial vector alignment and reduction process described in Section 3 is opaque without concrete examples. This makes it very difficult for the reader to understand precisely what the model is doing, which hinders reproducibility and evaluation of the technical contribution. Similarly, the justification for key methodological choices, such as using all dependency orders to build representations but only first-order features for the final similarity comparison (footnote 3), is unconvincing and not adequately explained.
2.  **Concerns Regarding the Evaluation Dataset.** The experiments are conducted on a single, small dataset of 90 noun compounds. The paper does not provide important details about this dataset, such as the frequency distribution of the compounds or the inter-annotator agreement on the human judgments. The surprisingly high performance of the frequency (FREQ) baseline (ρ=0.63) suggests that the dataset may be biased, where more frequent phrases tend to be more compositional. This potential bias, combined with the small dataset size, makes it difficult to assess the significance of the relatively marginal gains of the best model (0.79) over simpler variants (0.78).
3.  **Unclear Novelty and Contribution.** The core APT method, involving higher-order dependency paths and an alignment step, appears to have been proposed in previous work by the authors (a suppressed reference is cited). The paper should explicitly state what the novel contributions are relative to that work. As presented, the main contribution seems to be the application of an existing method to a new task and the addition of a simple interpolation (the hybrid model), which feels incremental.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the specific novel contributions of this paper over your previous work on APTs (the suppressed reference)? Is the core alignment method novel, or is the novelty in its application and the hybrid formulation?
2.  The justification for restricting the final similarity computation to only 1st-order dependency features (footnote 3) is not clear. You state that higher-order paths in the composed representations are "not reliable". Could you provide a concrete example of why a composed 2nd-order feature is less reliable than an elementary 2nd-order feature used to create it? This decision seems to discard potentially valuable information that your model is designed to capture.
3.  The optimal values for the interpolation parameters `h` and `q` are crucial for understanding the behavior of your best-performing models, but you only report a general range. What were the actual learned values? How much of the performance gain comes from the hybrid component (i.e., what is the result when `q=1` vs. the optimal `q` in the hybrid model)?
4.  The strong performance of the FREQ baseline is counter-intuitive and suggests a potential dataset artifact. Why should more frequent compounds be more compositional? Could you provide more analysis on the dataset properties and discuss how this potential bias might affect the interpretation of your results?
5.  To improve the paper's clarity, could you walk through a running example (e.g., for "spelling bee") showing: (a) the elementary APT vectors for "spelling" and "bee", (b) the aligned APT for "spelling", (c) the final composed hybrid APT vector, and (d) the observed APT for the compound? This would make the mechanics of your model in Sections 3 and 5 much easier to understand.
