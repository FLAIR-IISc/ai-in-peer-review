### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the task of predicting the compositionality of noun compounds. The core method involves creating a vector representation for a compound by composing the vectors of its constituent words and then measuring the similarity (using cosine) between this composed vector and the observed, holistic vector of the compound phrase derived directly from the corpus. The resulting similarity score is correlated (using Spearman's rho) with human judgments of compositionality. The study's primary focus is a comparison between two types of vector representations: dense neural embeddings (word2vec) and sparse, high-dimensional count-based vectors derived from typed dependency paths (which the authors term Anchored Packed Trees, or APTs). The paper also explores a novel composition technique for APTs that involves "aligning" the vector space of the modifier to that of the head, and evaluates a hybrid model combining aligned and unaligned features.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that sparse, dependency-based vector representations (the APT model) significantly outperform standard dense word embeddings (word2vec) on the task of predicting noun compound compositionality. The results in Table 5 show a notable gap between the best APT model (ρ=0.79) and the best word2vec model (ρ=0.74). This provides strong evidence for the value of explicit syntactic information in modeling this specific semantic phenomenon.

2.  An analysis of a composition method based on feature space alignment for dependency-based vectors. While the proposed alignment process alone does not improve performance, the paper shows that a hybrid model, which combines both aligned and unaligned modifier features, yields a small but statistically significant improvement over using unaligned features alone. This suggests that both local (unaligned) and structurally transformed (aligned) contexts contribute complementary information.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackles a aundamental problem in compositional semantics:** The paper addresses the important and challenging issue of non-compositionality, which is a known weakness of many distributional semantic models. By framing the problem as a testbed for compositional methods, the authors provide a clear and direct way to evaluate a model's ability to distinguish between literal and idiomatic phrases, moving beyond simple phrase similarity tasks.

2.  **Valuable comparison of representation types:** The head-to-head comparison between sparse, syntax-aware count vectors (APTs) and popular dense neural embeddings (word2vec) is a significant strength. The finding that the structured information in the APTs is more effective for this task is an important result. It contributes to the ongoing discussion in the field about the relative merits of different vector space models, highlighting that for tasks requiring fine-grained relational understanding, explicit syntactic features can be superior to the more implicit features learned by models like word2vec.

3.  **Systematic exploration of compositional models:** The paper presents a thorough experimental investigation. Within the APT framework, it systematically compares different weighting schemes (PPMI vs. smoothed PPMI), composition operations (intersective vs. additive), and model variations (aligned, unaligned, and hybrid). This detailed analysis provides valuable insights into the design choices that are most effective for this task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Claims are based on a small and poorly described dataset:** The experiments are conducted on a dataset of only 90 noun compounds from Reddy et al. (2011). This small size makes the findings, particularly the small performance gains of the hybrid model, potentially fragile and difficult to generalize. The paper omits crucial details about the dataset, such as the criteria for selecting the 90 compounds, their frequency range in the corpus, the number of human annotators, and inter-annotator agreement. The surprisingly strong performance of the frequency baseline (FREQ, ρ=0.63), which is not well-justified, may also indicate a frequency bias in the dataset that confounds the results. Without more details, it is difficult to assess the reliability of the conclusions.

2.  **Lack of clarity in methodology and terminology:** The paper's exposition is often difficult to follow. The introduction of the term "Anchored Packed Trees (APTs)" seems to be unnecessary jargon for what are essentially typed, higher-order dependency path features. The term "elementary APTs" is used repeatedly but never clearly defined. Section 3, which describes the core alignment mechanism, is particularly dense and lacks concrete examples to illustrate the path reduction process (Eq. 1) and the elimination of "incompatible co-occurrence types." This lack of clarity makes it hard to fully grasp the mechanics of the proposed method and hinders reproducibility.

3.  **Methodological inconsistency in feature usage:** A key methodological choice is insufficiently justified. The models use up to second-order (or higher) dependency features to construct and compose representations, but the final similarity between the composed and observed vectors is calculated *only* using first-order features. The justification in footnote 3—that composed higher-order paths are "not reliable"—is vague and unconvincing. This seems to discard precisely the information that the compositional model is designed to generate. A more rigorous explanation or empirical justification for this decision is needed.

4.  **Unclear novelty of the core method:** The paper references a suppressed publication for the APT approach, suggesting that the core compositional method of using aligned, higher-order dependency features has been previously proposed by the authors. This makes the primary contribution of the current work an application of that method to a new task (compositionality detection) and the introduction of the hybrid model. While this is a valid contribution, the novelty of the underlying framework itself appears limited.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the methodology behind APTs with a concrete, running example? Specifically, please define "elementary APTs" and walk through how the vectors for a compound like "spelling bee" would be constructed, aligned (showing the effect of Eq. 1), and composed.

2.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for restricting the final similarity computation to only first-order dependency features? Why exactly are the composed second-order features considered "not reliable," and have you experimented with including them in the similarity calculation?

3.  Can you provide more details about the dataset? What is the frequency distribution of the 90 compounds in the ukWaC corpus? Furthermore, what is the intuition behind the strong FREQ baseline? Does this suggest a correlation in the dataset where more common phrases are also judged to be more compositional?

4.  What were the optimal values for the hyperparameters *h* (head weight) and *q* (alignment weight) in your best-performing hybrid model (Table 5, ρ=0.79)? Reporting these values would provide insight into the relative importance of the modifier vs. head and the aligned vs. unaligned features.

5.  The human judgments are on a 0-5 integer scale, which likely leads to many ties. How does the Spearman correlation calculation handle these ties, and have you considered how this might affect the stability of the reported correlation scores?
