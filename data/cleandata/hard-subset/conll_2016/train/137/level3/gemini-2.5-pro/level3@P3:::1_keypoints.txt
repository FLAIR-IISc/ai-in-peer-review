- Paper investigates predicting noun-compound compositionality by comparing holistic compound vectors with composed constituent vectors, contrasting sparse syntactic-count APTs vs dense word2vec and testing an "alignment" composition method.  
- Contribution: Empirically compares structured count vectors and neural embeddings for compositionality prediction, showing count-based APTs can outperform word2vec.  
- Contribution: Proposes and evaluates an APT "alignment" method for composing dependency-based vectors, finding a tuned hybrid of aligned/unaligned gives small additional gains.  
- Strength: Tackles an important problem—detecting non-compositional phrases is crucial for semantic modeling.  
- Strength: Strong empirical result: APTs achieve high Spearman ρ (0.79), outperforming prior SOTA (0.71) and a word2vec baseline (0.74).  
- Strength: Systematic evaluation across vector types, weighting schemes, alignment strategies, and composition functions strengthens the empirical claims.  
- Weakness: Results are fragile because experiments use only the small Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds with missing dataset details and potential selection/frequency biases.  
- Weakness: Small performance differences (e.g., 0.78 vs 0.79) on a tiny dataset may not be robust or generalizable; strong frequency baseline (ρ=0.63) is concerning.  
- Weakness: Paper exposition is unclear and hinders reproducibility; APT terminology is poorly defined and “elementary APTs” are unexplained.  
- Weakness: Section 3 is hard to follow—no concrete examples for path reduction, incompatible co-occurrence types, or what “not properly aligned” means.  
- Weakness: Methodological inconsistency: model uses higher-order dependency features but final similarity is restricted to first-order features without convincing justification.  
- Weakness: Novelty of the alignment method is unclear due to a "suppressed reference" suggesting prior publication of the idea.  
- Weakness: Key experimental details missing—optimal hyperparameter values (h and q) are reported as ranges, not the specific best settings.  
- Weakness: Neural-embedding experiments omit an important baseline: holistic word2vec vectors for full compounds to compare directly with corpus-derived compound vectors.  
- Question: Clarify the novelty of the APT alignment method versus the suppressed reference and whether the main contribution is the mechanism or its application.  
- Question: Provide a running example (e.g., "spelling bee") showing component features, construction of aligned vs unaligned composed vectors, and how dependency-path reduction works.  
- Question: Given N=90 and strong frequency baseline, demonstrate whether the hybrid model’s small gains are statistically significant and generalizable.  
- Question: Justify restricting final similarity to first-order features—explain why higher-order paths are unreliable and how that choice affects results.
