- Paper studies noun-compound compositionality by comparing composed constituent vectors to holistic compound vectors (cosine) and correlating with human judgments; compares dense word2vec vs sparse dependency-based APTs and proposes an alignment-based composition plus a hybrid model.

- Contribution: APTs (sparse, dependency-based vectors) significantly outperform word2vec on this task (best APT ρ=0.79 vs best word2vec ρ=0.74), highlighting the value of explicit syntactic information.

- Contribution: Proposes and analyzes a feature-space alignment for dependency vectors; alignment alone fails, but a hybrid of aligned+unaligned modifier features yields a small but significant improvement.

- Strength: Addresses a fundamental problem in compositional semantics and provides a clear evaluation for distinguishing literal vs idiomatic phrases.

- Strength: Valuable head-to-head comparison showing structured, syntax-aware count vectors can outperform popular dense embeddings for fine-grained relational semantics.

- Strength: Systematic experimental exploration of weighting schemes, composition operations, and model variants within the APT framework.

- Weakness: Experiments use a small (90-item) and poorly described dataset—selection criteria, frequency ranges, annotator counts, and agreement are omitted—raising concerns about generalizability and dataset bias (e.g., strong FREQ baseline).

- Weakness: Exposition and terminology are unclear; APT jargon (e.g., "elementary APTs") is not well defined and the alignment/path-reduction description (Sect.3, Eq.1) lacks concrete examples, harming reproducibility.

- Weakness: Methodological inconsistency: models construct higher-order dependency features but compute final similarity using only first-order features, with an unconvincing justification that higher-order composed paths are "not reliable."

- Weakness: Novelty concerns: core APT compositional method appears previously published, so the paper's main novelty is its application to compositionality detection and the hybrid model rather than a wholly new framework.

- Question: Please provide a concrete running example defining "elementary APTs" and showing how a compound like "spelling bee" is constructed, aligned (Eq.1), and composed.

- Question: Please justify restricting final similarity to first-order features, explain why composed second-order features are "not reliable," and report experiments including them if available.

- Question: Provide dataset details: frequency distribution of the 90 compounds in ukWaC and an explanation/intuition for the surprisingly strong FREQ baseline (possible correlation between frequency and judged compositionality).

- Question: Report the optimal hyperparameter values h (head weight) and q (alignment weight) for the best hybrid model (Table 5, ρ=0.79).

- Question: How are ties handled in Spearman correlation given 0–5 integer human judgments, and has the effect of ties on correlation stability been considered?
