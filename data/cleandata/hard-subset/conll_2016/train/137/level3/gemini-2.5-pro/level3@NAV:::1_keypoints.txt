- Investigates predicting compound noun compositionality by comparing observed compound vectors to vectors composed from constituents.  
- Proposes the Anchored Packed Tree (APT), a sparse, count-based representation using higher-order dependency paths.  
- Claims a hybrid APT (aligned + unaligned) achieves state-of-the-art on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, outperforming neural embeddings and prior methods.

Strengths:
- Provides a direct, valuable comparison between structured count-based models and popular neural embeddings for compositionality prediction.  
- Shows sparse, syntax-aware count vectors can outperform dense embeddings for tasks needing fine-grained syntactic awareness.  
- Methodologically interesting exploration of composition strategies within APT, with a tuned mixture of aligned and unaligned features yielding best results.

Weaknesses:
- Experiments rely on a small (90 compounds), poorly described dataset with missing selection criteria, frequency distribution, and judge details, undermining empirical claims.  
- Paper has clarity and reproducibility issues: core alignment method, terms like "elementary APTs," and key methodological justifications are poorly explained and lacking examples.  
- Novelty is questionable: aligned approach appears previously published by the authors, and details/hyperparameters of the best hybrid model are not reported.
