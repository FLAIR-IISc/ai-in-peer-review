General Notes
=============================
This paper reports experiments on predicting the level of structurality of compounds in English.

The dataset used is a pre-existing set of 90 compounds, ranked 1 to 5 in terms of structurality (by non-expert judges).

The general approach for each experiment is to calculate a cosine similarity between the vector of the compound (considered as a token) and the vector of the components.

The evaluation is done using the Spearman correlation between the cosine similarity and human judgments.

The experiments are different:
- For the vectors used: neural embedding vs syntactic-reference counting vector
- And for the later case, a simple or "aligned" vector should be used for the dependent component of the compound. Alignment tries to capture a shift from the dependent to the head. Alignment was proposed in a pre-cited reference.

The results indicate that syntactic-reference counting vectors perform better than embeddings, and only the aligned vector performs less well than the non-modified vector, and an over-tune combination of aligned and unaligned vectors provides a slight improvement.

Regarding the form of the paper, I found the introduction to be quite well-written, but other parts (such as section 5.1) are difficult to read, although the underlying assumptions are not very complex. Rewriting with running examples may help.

Regarding the summary, I have several concerns:
- Regarding Reddy et al., the innovation seems to be the use of aligned vectors, but they are published by the authors in a pre- "cited reference".
- The dataset is small, and not described adequately. In particular, the limits of frequencies are likely to significantly affect the results.
Since the improvements using aligned vectors are minor, on a small dataset, in which it is not clear how the compounds were chosen, I think the conclusions in the paper are quite fragile.

I think it is a simple extraction of paths between two literal objects in a dependency tree,
i.e. a simple extension traditionally done in syntactic distribution representation of words
(which usually use paths with length one, or length two, compressed prepositions).

Section 4:
Since the Reddy et al. dataset is central to your work, it seems necessary to state how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency limits of the compounds/components etc.? It is very likely that the results vary depending on the frequency limits.
How many judgments are provided for a given compound? Are there many compounds with the same final structurality score? Is it not a problem to rank them to calculate the Spearman correlation?
Obviously you use "components" for a component of an N N sequence. I would suggest "components", because "components" also mean "phrases" (syntagms).
"...the intuition that if a component is used literally within a phrase, it is highly likely that the compound and component co-occur"
: Note that the intuition is certainly correct if the component is the head of the phrase, otherwise much less correct (for example in "spelling B" there is no distribution of "spelling").
