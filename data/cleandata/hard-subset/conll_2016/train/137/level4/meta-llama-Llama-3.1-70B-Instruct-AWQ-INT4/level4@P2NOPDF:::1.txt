**General Comments**

The paper presents experiments on predicting the level of compositionality of compounds in English. The dataset used is a previously existing set of 90 compounds, with compositionality rankings from 1 to 5 provided by an unspecified number of judges. The experiments involve computing cosine similarity between the compound vector and a composition of the component vectors, with evaluation performed using Spearman correlation with human judgments. The experiments vary in terms of the vectors used (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors) and the use of plain or "aligned" vectors for the dependent component of the compound.

The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, while the use of aligned vectors alone performs less well than non-modified vectors. A highly-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors provides a slight improvement. However, the improvements using aligned vectors are marginal, and the findings are based on a small dataset with unclear compound selection criteria.

The introduction is well-written, but other sections, such as 5.1, are difficult to read despite the underlying notions being straightforward. Rephrasing with running examples could improve clarity.

**Concerns and Suggestions**

1. The innovation with respect to Reddy et al. appears to be the use of aligned vectors, which have been previously published by the authors. The contribution of the paper is unclear.
2. The dataset is small and not adequately described. Frequency ranges of the compounds and components are likely to impact the results.
3. The use of aligned vectors provides only marginal improvements, and the findings are fragile due to the small dataset and unclear compound selection criteria.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

**Section 3**

* The term "packed anchored tree" is unclear and seems to refer to a plain extraction of paths between two lexical items in a dependency tree.
* The term "elementary APTs" is confusing and requires clarification.

**Section 4**

* The selection criteria for the 90 compounds in the Reddy et al. dataset are unclear. Frequency ranges of the compounds and components should be provided.
* The number of judgments provided for each compound is unclear. Are there many compounds with the same final compositionality score? How does this affect the Spearman correlation?
* The term "constituent" is used to refer to a component of the N N sequence. Consider using "component" instead to avoid confusion with the sense of "phrase" or "syntagm."

**Section 5**

* A running example would help clarify the explanation of the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase.
* The term "compound phrase token" is unclear. Does it refer to the merged components of the compound?

**Section 5.1**

* The definition of "elementary APTs" is unclear. Are they a triplet of target word, dependency path, and other word?
* The term "shifted PMI" is unclear. Does it refer to PMI as defined in equation (3)?
* The explanation of removing features with low positive PMI is contradictory and requires clarification.

**Section 6**

* The use of first-order features only in measuring similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors is puzzling. Why are higher-order dependency features not used?
* The explanation in Note 3 for not using second-order paths in composed representations is unclear.
* The optimal values for h and q in equations 8 and 9 are unclear. Providing these values would help estimate the impact of "hybridity" on the results.
* Consider adding the results from the word2vec vectors for the compound phrases.
* The intuition behind the FREQ baseline is unclear. Why would a frequent compound tend to be compositional? This suggests a potential bias in the dataset.
