- Paper proposes a compositional approach using distributional semantics (anchored packed trees, APTs) and compares it to ad-hoc composition on neural embeddings, evaluated on 90 compound nouns with human compositionality judgments.
- Contribution: introduces an APT-based compositional method that captures syntactic structure to detect non-compositional phrases.
- Contribution: empirical evaluation on a 90-compound dataset shows the APT approach outperforms state-of-the-art neural embedding baselines.
- Contribution: analyzes alignment of modifier and head vector spaces and finds alignment can improve performance.
- Strength: novel application of APTs to model syntactic compositionality for phrase-level non-compositionality detection.
- Strength: thorough empirical comparison against strong neural baselines.
- Strength: providing analysis of vector-space alignment effects adds valuable insight.
- Weakness: dataset is small (90 compounds), limiting statistical power and generalizability.
- Weakness: some sections (e.g., Section 5.1) are hard to follow and would benefit from running examples.
- Weakness: key concepts like "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APTs" are not clearly explained.
- Question: request details on dataset statistics, including frequency ranges of compounds and components.
- Question: ask for clearer explanations of "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APTs."
- Question: request clarification on optimal values for hyperparameters h and q in equations 8 and 9.
- Question: ask for results using word2vec vectors for the compound phrases.
- Question: request clarification of the intuition behind the FREQ baseline and why higher frequency would indicate compositionality.
