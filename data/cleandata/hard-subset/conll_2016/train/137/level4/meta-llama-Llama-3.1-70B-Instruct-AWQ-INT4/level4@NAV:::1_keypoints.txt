- Study predicts English compound compositionality using vector representations, evaluating cosine similarity between compound vectors and composed component vectors with Spearman correlation.
- Uses a pre-existing dataset of 90 compounds with human compositionality scores (1–5).
- Experiments compare neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors; count vectors outperform embeddings.
- "Aligned" syntactic-context vectors alone perform worse; combining aligned and unaligned vectors gives only slight improvements.
- Paper’s introduction is clear, but other sections (notably 5.1) are hard to follow; running examples are recommended for clarity.
- Limited novelty over Reddy et al.; methodological innovation is marginal.
- Dataset is small and poorly described, raising concerns about result reliability and generality.
- Missing frequency information for compounds and constituents; frequency could strongly affect results.
- Number of human judgments per compound and score distribution are not reported, undermining confidence in Spearman correlations.
- Terminology issues: “constituent” vs. “component” may cause confusion with syntactic constituents.
- The term “packed anchored tree” seems unnecessary and is essentially path extraction from dependency trees, like existing syntactic distributional representations.
- Use of the term “tree” and the notion of “elementary APTs” (Section 5.1) are unclear and need better definition.
- Section 5 lacks a running example to clarify “elementary representation” and the meaning of “compound phrase token.”
- Definition and naming of “elementary APTs” (triplet of target word + dependency path + other word) are confusing and should be clarified.
- The concept of “shifted PMI” and its relation to standard PMI (eq. 3) is not explained clearly.
- The sentence about removing features with low positive PMI is contradictory/confusing and needs rephrasing and justification.
- The phrase “not properly aligned” APTs is vague; the authors should define what proper alignment means and its expected overlap.
- Inconsistency in feature order: higher-order dependency features are used elsewhere, but first-order features are suddenly used for similarity comparisons.
- Note 3’s explanation for unreliability of second-order paths in composed representations is insufficient.
- Key hyperparameters (h and q in eqs. 8–9) and how they were chosen are not reported, obscuring the claimed “hybridity” effect.
- Table 4’s results likely correspond to the add combination but this is not stated in the legend and should be clarified.
- Authors should report compound-phrase vectors from word2vec (or similar) for comparison.
- The FREQ baseline’s intuition is unclear — why should frequent compounds be more compositional — and this could indicate bias in the dataset.
