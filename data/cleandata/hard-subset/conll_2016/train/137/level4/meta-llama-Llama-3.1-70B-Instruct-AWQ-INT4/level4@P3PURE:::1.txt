General Comments
================

This paper reports experiments on predicting the level of compositionality of compounds in English. The dataset used is a previously existing set of 90 compounds, whose compositionality was ranked from 1 to 5 by a non-specified number of judges. The general form of each experiment is to compute a cosine similarity between the vector of the compound (treated as one token) and a composition of the vectors of the components. Evaluation is performed using a Spearman correlation between the cosine similarity and the human judgments.

The experiments vary in two aspects: the vectors used (neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors) and, for the latter case, whether plain or "aligned" vectors should be used for the dependent component of the compound. The alignment aims to capture a shift from the dependent to the head, as proposed in a previous reference by the authors. The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, and the use of aligned vectors alone performs less well than non-modified vectors. A highly-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors provides a slight improvement.

Regarding the form of the paper, the introduction is well-written, but other parts, such as Section 5.1, are difficult to read, despite the underlying notions not being very complicated. Rephrasing with running examples could help improve clarity.

Regarding the substance, several concerns arise:

- The innovation with respect to Reddy et al. seems to be the use of aligned vectors, but they have been published in a previous reference by the authors.
- The dataset is small and not sufficiently described. In particular, frequency ranges are likely to impact the results.
- Since the improvements using aligned vectors are marginal, over a small dataset, in which it is unclear how the choice of compounds was performed, the findings in the paper appear quite fragile.

More Detailed Comments/Questions
================================

Section 3
----------

The need for the new name "packed anchored tree" is unclear. It seems to be a plain extraction of the paths between two lexical items in a dependency tree, namely a plain extension of what is traditionally done in syntactic distributional representations of words (which typically use paths of length one or two, with collapsed prepositions). Furthermore, why is it called a tree? What are "elementary APTs" (Section 5.1)?

Table 2: Did the authors forget to mention that they discard features of order more than 3? (This is why, for instance, NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ does not appear in the leftmost bottom cell of Table 2.) Or does it have to do with the elimination of some incompatible types, for which an example should be provided (the explanation was not clear)?

Section 4
----------

Since the Reddy et al. dataset is central to this work, it seems necessary to explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components? There is a high likelihood that results vary depending on the frequency ranges.

How many judgments were provided for a given compound? Are there many compounds with the same final compositionality score? Isn't this a problem when ranking them to compute the Spearman correlation?

The term "constituent" is used to refer to a component of the N N sequence. It would be more accurate to use "component," as "constituent" also has the sense of "phrase" (syntagm).

The statement "... the intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase, then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences" is true if the constituent is the head of the phrase, but much less true otherwise (e.g., "spelling bee" does not have the distribution of "spelling").

Section 5
----------

The sentence "Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context" would benefit from a running example to help the reader follow the object being referred to.

Does "compound phrase token" refer to the merged components of the compound?

Section 5.1
------------

It is assumed that "elementary APTs" are a triplet target word w + dependency path r + other word w'. The name is confusing and should be clarified.

The term "shifted PMI" should be clarified to indicate whether it refers to PMI as defined in equation (3).

The sentence "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts" seems contradictory. It should be clarified whether "these phrases" co-refer with "things" in this context.

The statement "In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned" is unclear. What does "not properly aligned" mean? Does it mean not aligned at all?

The paragraph spanning lines 558-563 is unclear. Why should the potential overlap be considerable in the particular case of the NMOD relation between the two components?

The paragraph spanning lines 575-580 is puzzling. Why does the whole paper make use of higher-order dependency features and then suddenly, at the critical point of actually measuring the crucial metric of similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors, use first-order features only? Note 3 is supposed to provide an answer, but the explanation of why the second-order paths in the composed representations are not reliable is unclear and should be clarified.

Section 6
----------

The statement "Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect" does not seem obvious from Table 3.

What are the optimal values for h and q in equations 8 and 9? These are important for estimating how much "hybridity" provides the slight gains with respect to the unaligned results.

It would be helpful to have the results from the word2vec vectors for the compound phrases. Additionally, it could be beneficial to include a legend in Table 4 indicating that the results correspond to using the add combination.

The intuition behind the FREQ baseline is unclear. Why would a frequent compound tend to be compositional? This suggests maybe a bias in the dataset.
