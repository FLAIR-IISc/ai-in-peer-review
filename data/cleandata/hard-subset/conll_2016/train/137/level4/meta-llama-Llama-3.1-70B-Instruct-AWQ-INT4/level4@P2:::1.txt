**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents experiments on predicting the level of compositionality of compounds in English using a dataset of 90 compounds with human judgments of compositionality. The authors compare the performance of two types of vector representations: neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors. They also investigate the effect of using aligned vectors for the dependent component of the compound, which is a novel approach proposed in a previous work by the authors. The results show that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings, and the use of aligned vectors provides a slight improvement over non-aligned vectors.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a new approach to compositionality detection using aligned vectors, which is a novel contribution to the field.
2. They demonstrate the effectiveness of syntactic-context count vectors over neural embeddings for compositionality detection.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach using a dataset of 90 compounds with human judgments of compositionality.

**Strengths**

1. The paper presents a clear and well-written introduction to the problem of compositionality detection.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach using a dataset of 90 compounds with human judgments of compositionality.
3. The results show that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses**

1. The innovation of the paper is limited to the use of aligned vectors, which has been previously proposed by the authors.
2. The dataset used is small and not well-described, which may impact the generalizability of the results.
3. The improvements using aligned vectors are marginal, which may not be significant enough to justify the use of this approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more information about the dataset used, including the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components?
2. How many judgments were provided for each compound, and are there many compounds with the same final compositionality score?
3. Can you clarify the intuition behind the FREQ baseline and why a frequent compound would tend to be compositional?
4. Can you provide more information about the optimal values for h and q in equations 8 and 9, and how they contribute to the slight gains in performance?
5. Can you provide results for the word2vec vectors for the compound phrases, and how they compare to the syntactic-context count vectors?
