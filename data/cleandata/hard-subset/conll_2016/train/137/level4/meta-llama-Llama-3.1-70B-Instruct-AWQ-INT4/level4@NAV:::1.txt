**General Comments**

This paper presents an experimental study on predicting the level of compositionality of English compounds using vector representations. The authors utilize a pre-existing dataset of 90 compounds with human-annotated compositionality scores, ranging from 1 to 5. The experimental setup involves computing cosine similarity between the compound vector and a composition of its component vectors, with evaluation performed using Spearman correlation.

The experiments investigate two types of vector representations: neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors. For the latter, the authors also examine the impact of using "aligned" vectors, which aim to capture the shift from the dependent component to the head. The results suggest that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, while the use of aligned vectors alone yields inferior performance. However, a combination of aligned and unaligned vectors provides a slight improvement.

Regarding the paper's structure, the introduction is well-written, but other sections, such as 5.1, are difficult to follow. Rephrasing with running examples could enhance clarity. Concerns regarding the substance of the paper include the limited innovation compared to Reddy et al., the small and poorly described dataset, and the marginal improvements achieved using aligned vectors.

**More Detailed Comments/Questions**

**Section 3**

1. The term "packed anchored tree" seems unnecessary, as it appears to be a straightforward extraction of paths between lexical items in a dependency tree, similar to traditional syntactic distributional representations.
2. The use of the term "tree" is unclear, and the concept of "elementary APTs" (Section 5.1) requires further explanation.

**Section 4**

1. The selection process for the 90 compounds in the Reddy et al. dataset is unclear, and frequency ranges for the compounds and their components are not provided. This information is crucial, as results may vary depending on these factors.
2. The number of judgments per compound and the distribution of compositionality scores are not specified. This lack of information raises concerns about the reliability of the Spearman correlation.
3. The term "constituent" is used to refer to a component of the N N sequence, but "component" might be a more suitable term to avoid confusion with the phrase "constituent" (syntagm).

**Section 5**

1. A running example would help clarify the statement regarding the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase.
2. The term "compound phrase token" is unclear; does it refer to the merged components of the compound?

**Section 5.1**

1. The definition of "elementary APTs" as a triplet target word w + dependency path r + other word w' is assumed, but the name remains confusing.
2. The concept of "shifted PMI" and its relation to PMI as defined in equation (3) requires clarification.
3. The sentence "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts" seems contradictory and requires further explanation.
4. The statement "In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned" is unclear; what does "not properly aligned" mean?

**Section 6**

1. The use of higher-order dependency features throughout the paper is inconsistent with the sudden use of first-order features when measuring similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors.
2. Note 3 does not provide a clear explanation for the unreliability of second-order paths in composed representations.
3. The optimal values for h and q in equations 8 and 9 are not specified, which is crucial for understanding the "hybridity" that yields slight gains over unaligned results.
4. The results in Table 4 appear to correspond to the add combination, but this should be explicitly stated in the legend.
5. Providing results from word2vec vectors for compound phrases would be beneficial.
6. The intuition behind the FREQ baseline is unclear; why would a frequent compound tend to be compositional, and could this suggest a bias in the dataset?
