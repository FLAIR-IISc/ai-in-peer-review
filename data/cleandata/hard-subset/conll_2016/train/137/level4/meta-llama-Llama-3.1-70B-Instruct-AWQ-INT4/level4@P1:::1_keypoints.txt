- Task and setup: Predict compound compositionality by computing cosine similarity between compound vectors and composed component vectors, evaluated with Spearman correlation on a 90-compound dataset.  
- Experimental variables: Compare neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors and aligned vs. non-aligned composition methods.  
- Key result: Syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings.  
- Alignment result: Using aligned vectors alone performs worse than unmodified vectors; a carefully tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors gives only slight improvement.  
- Writing clarity: Introduction is good, but sections (notably 5.1) are hard to follow and need clearer explanations and running examples.  
- Novelty concern: The main innovation (aligned vectors) appears previously published by the authors, so new insights over prior work are unclear.  
- Dataset concern: Dataset is small (90 items) and poorly described; frequency ranges of compounds and components are not reported.  
- Fragility: Reported improvements are marginal and findings seem fragile given dataset size and unclear compound selection.  
- Main contributions claimed: Evaluation of syntactic-context count vectors vs embeddings, investigation of aligned vs non-aligned composition, and empirical insights into composition methods.  
- Strengths: Novel application of syntactic-context count vectors and generally well-designed experiments yielding useful comparisons.  
- Weaknesses: Small/under-described dataset, marginal gains from alignment, and limited novelty beyond prior aligned-vector work.  
- Questions for authors: How were the 90 compounds selected and what are their frequency ranges? Please detail the aligned composition method and how it differs from prior work, and how you will address dataset size and marginal improvements in future work.
