**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a compositional approach to improve the detection of non-compositional phrases using distributional semantics. The authors compare an integrated compositional distributional approach using sparse high-dimensional representations with the ad-hoc compositional approach of applying simple composition operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings. They use a dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality to evaluate the effectiveness of their approach.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Proposal of a new compositional approach**: The authors propose a compositional approach that uses anchored packed trees (APTs) to capture the syntactic structure of phrases. They demonstrate that this approach can improve the detection of non-compositional phrases.
2. **Evaluation of the approach**: The authors evaluate their approach using a dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality. They compare their approach with state-of-the-art neural embeddings and demonstrate that their approach outperforms the baselines.
3. **Analysis of the effect of alignment**: The authors investigate the effect of aligning the vector spaces of the modifier and head nouns in compound nouns. They demonstrate that alignment can improve the performance of their approach.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel compositional approach**: The authors propose a novel compositional approach that uses APTs to capture the syntactic structure of phrases. This approach has the potential to improve the detection of non-compositional phrases.
2. **Thorough evaluation**: The authors evaluate their approach using a dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality. They compare their approach with state-of-the-art neural embeddings and demonstrate that their approach outperforms the baselines.
3. **Analysis of the effect of alignment**: The authors investigate the effect of aligning the vector spaces of the modifier and head nouns in compound nouns. They demonstrate that alignment can improve the performance of their approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited dataset**: The authors use a dataset of 90 compound nouns, which is relatively small compared to other datasets in the field.
2. **Lack of clarity in some sections**: Some sections of the paper, such as Section 5.1, are difficult to read and could benefit from rephrasing with running examples.
3. **Unclear explanation of some concepts**: Some concepts, such as the "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APTs", are not clearly explained and could benefit from further clarification.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more information about the dataset used in the paper, such as the frequency ranges of the compounds and the components?
2. Can you clarify the explanation of the "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APTs" concepts?
3. Can you provide more information about the optimal values for h and q in equations 8 and 9?
4. Can you provide the results from the word2vec vectors for the compound phrases?
5. Can you clarify the intuition behind the FREQ baseline and why a frequent compound would tend to be compositional?
