1. Paper compares APT-based sparse compositional distributional methods to ad-hoc composition on neural embeddings for predicting compound compositionality.  
2. Uses a dataset of 90 English compound nouns with human compositionality judgments and evaluates via Spearman correlation between composed vs observed vector cosine similarity.  
3. Proposes using anchored packed trees (APTs) to capture syntactic structure and dependencies in composition.  
4. Empirically compares APT methods against neural word embeddings for compound compositionality prediction.  
5. Examines the effect of alignment and hybrid methods on APT composition performance.  
6. Thorough evaluation using human-judged dataset.  
7. Shows benefits of incorporating syntactic structure in compositional distributional semantics versus standard neural embeddings.  
8. Analysis of alignment and hybrid methods provides insight into the role of syntactic structure.  
9. Dataset is small and under-described, limiting generalizability.  
10. Aligned-vector technique may lack novelty due to prior (suppressed) publication by the authors.  
11. Performance gains from aligned vectors are marginal and possibly fragile given dataset size.  
12. Request for more dataset details, including frequency ranges of compounds and components.  
13. Ask how many judgments per compound and whether many compounds share the same final compositionality score.  
14. Ask for clarification on why 2nd-order paths in composed representations are considered unreliable.  
15. Request results using word2vec vectors for the compound phrases.  
16. Ask for intuition and justification for the FREQ baseline and why frequent compounds would tend to be compositional.
