- Paper predicts compound compositionality by correlating cosine similarity between compound vectors and composed component vectors with human judgments.
- Experiments vary vector types (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors) and whether dependent-component vectors are "aligned."
- Main empirical findings: syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings; aligned vectors alone perform worse than unaligned; a tuned hybrid of aligned+unaligned yields marginal gains.
- Novelty concern: use of aligned vectors seems incremental and previously published by the authors.
- Dataset concern: only 90 compounds used; selection criteria and frequency ranges are not described and likely affect results.
- Annotation details missing: number of judges per compound and the prevalence of tied compositionality scores are not reported, which matters for Spearman correlation.
- Results may be fragile due to small dataset, marginal improvements, and unclear compound selection procedure.
- Paper clarity issues: Sections (notably 5.1) are hard to read and would benefit from running examples.
- Terminology problems: the new name "packed anchored tree" is unclear and seems to be a standard dependency-path extraction; "tree" and "elementary APTs" are not explained.
- Table 2 ambiguity: unclear if features of order >3 are discarded or if incompatible types are removed; examples and clarification needed.
- Terminology precision: use "component" instead of "constituent" to avoid syntactic ambiguity.
- The intuition linking constituent literal use and shared co-occurrences is inaccurate when the constituent is not the head (e.g., "spelling bee").
- Section 5 ambiguities: unclear what "compound phrase token" refers to and how elementary representations relate; a running example is requested.
- Section 5.1 confusions: definition of "elementary APTs" and "shifted PMI" needs clarification and explicit reference to equation (3).
- Contradictory sentence about removing low-PMI features needs rewording to resolve co-reference confusion.
- Alignment explanation unclear: what "not properly aligned" means and why alignment/non-alignment affects overlap is not made explicit.
- Unclear reasoning about why NMOD relation should show considerable potential overlap (lines 558–563).
- Methodological inconsistency: paper uses higher-order dependency features generally but seems to use only first-order features for final similarity computations; rationale is unclear and Note 3 insufficient.
- Statistical details omitted: effect of PPMI smoothing (α=0.75) is not clearly supported by Table 3.
- Hyperparameters h and q (equations 8 and 9) are not reported/justified, yet are central to the hybrid gains.
- Missing baselines/results: results for word2vec vectors computed for compound phrase tokens are not shown, and Table 4 lacks a legend indicating use of the add composition.
- FREQ baseline intuition is unclear and may indicate dataset bias if frequency correlates with compositionality.
- Overall recommendation: clarify methodology, provide dataset statistics (selection, frequencies, annotator counts), justify hyperparameters, include running examples, and present additional baselines and results to support claims.
