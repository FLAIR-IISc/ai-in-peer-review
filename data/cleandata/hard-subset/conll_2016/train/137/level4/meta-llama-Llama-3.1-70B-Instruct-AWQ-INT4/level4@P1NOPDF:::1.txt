General Comments
================

The paper reports on experiments predicting the level of compositionality of compounds in English. The dataset used is a previously existing set of 90 compounds, whose compositionality was ranked from 1 to 5 by a non-specified number of judges. The general form of each experiment involves computing a cosine similarity between the vector of the compound (treated as one token) and a composition of the vectors of the components. Evaluation is performed using a Spearman correlation between the cosine similarity and the human judgments.

The experiments vary in two ways: (1) the vectors used, comparing neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors, and (2) for the latter case, whether plain or "aligned" vectors should be used for the dependent component of the compound. The alignment aims to capture a shift from the dependent to the head, a concept previously proposed by the authors in a suppressed reference. The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, and the use of aligned vectors alone performs less well than non-modified vectors. However, a highly-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors provides a slight improvement.

Regarding the form of the paper, I found the introduction well-written, but other sections (such as 5.1) are difficult to read, despite the underlying notions not being very complicated. Rephrasing with running examples could help improve clarity.

Regarding the substance, I have several concerns:

* The innovation with respect to Reddy et al. appears to be the use of aligned vectors, but these have been published in a previous suppressed reference by the authors.
* The dataset is small and not sufficiently described. In particular, frequency ranges are likely to impact the results.
* Since the improvements using aligned vectors are marginal, over a small dataset, in which it is unclear how the choice of compounds was performed, I find that the findings in the paper are quite fragile.

More Detailed Comments/Questions
================================

Section 3
---------

I do not understand the need for the new name "packed anchored tree." It seems to be a plain extraction of the paths between two lexical items in a dependency tree, which is a straightforward extension of what is traditionally done in syntactic distributional representations of words. Typically, these representations use paths of length one or two, with collapsed prepositions (e.g., Lin, 1998).

Further, why is it called a tree? What are "elementary APTs" (Section 5.1)?

Table 2: Did you forget to mention that you discard features of order more than 3? This would explain why, for instance, NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ does not appear in the leftmost bottom cell of Table 2. Alternatively, does it have to do with the elimination of some incompatible types you mention, for which an example should be provided? I did not find it very clear.

Section 4
---------

Since the Reddy et al. dataset is central to your work, it seems necessary to explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components? There is a high likelihood that results vary depending on the frequency ranges.

How many judgments were provided for a given compound? Are there many compounds with the same final compositionality score? Isn't this a problem when ranking them to compute the Spearman correlation?

Apparently, you use "constituent" for a component of the N N sequence. I would suggest using "component," as "constituent" also has the sense of "phrase" (syntagm).

The intuition that "if a constituent is used literally within a phrase, then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences" is certainly true if the constituent is the head of the phrase, but much less true otherwise (e.g., "spelling bee" does not have the distribution of "spelling").

Section 5
---------

Please provide a running example to help the reader follow which object you're talking about in the sentence: "Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context."

Does "compound phrase token" refer to the merged components of the compound?

Section 5.1
------------

I assume that "elementary APTs" are a triplet consisting of a target word w, a dependency path r, and another word w'. I find the name confusing.

Clarify whether "shifted PMI" refers to PMI as defined in equation (3).

The sentence "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts" seems contradictory. Do "these phrases" co-refer with "things" here?

The sentence "In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned" is unclear. What does "not properly aligned" mean? Do you mean not aligned at all?

I do not understand the paragraph spanning lines 558-563. Why should the potential overlap be considerable in the particular case of the NMOD relation between the two components?

The paragraph spanning lines 575-580 is puzzling. Why does the whole paper make use of higher-order dependency features and then suddenly, at the critical point of actually measuring the crucial metric of similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors, use first-order features only? Note 3 is supposed to provide an answer, but I do not understand the explanation of why the 2nd-order paths in the composed representations are not reliable. Please clarify.

Section 6
---------

The statement "Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect" does not seem obvious from Table 3.

What are the optimal values for h and q in equations 8 and 9? These are important in order to estimate how much "hybridity" provides the slight gains with respect to the unaligned results.

It would be helpful to have the combination method used in the results (e.g., "add") in the legend of the tables.

Also, could you provide the results from the word2vec vectors for the compound phrases?

I do not understand the intuition behind the FREQ baseline. Why would a frequent compound tend to be compositional? This suggests maybe a bias in the dataset.
