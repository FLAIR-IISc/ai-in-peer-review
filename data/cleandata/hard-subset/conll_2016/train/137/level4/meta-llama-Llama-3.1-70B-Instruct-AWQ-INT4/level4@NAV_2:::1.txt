Summary of the Paper
--------------------

This paper presents experiments on predicting the level of compositionality of compounds in English using compositional distributional methods. The authors compare an integrated compositional distributional approach using sparse high-dimensional representations with the ad-hoc compositional approach of applying simple composition operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings. They use a dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality and evaluate their methods using a Spearman correlation between the cosine similarity of composed and observed representations and human judgments.

Main Contributions
-----------------

1. The authors propose a compositional distributional approach using anchored packed trees (APTs) that captures the syntactic structure of words and their dependencies.
2. They compare the performance of APT-based methods with neural word embeddings in predicting the compositionality of compound nouns.
3. They investigate the effect of alignment and hybrid methods on the performance of APT-based composition.

Strengths
----------

1. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their methods using a dataset with human judgments of compositionality.
2. They compare their approach with state-of-the-art neural word embeddings and demonstrate the benefits of using syntactic structure in compositional distributional semantics.
3. They investigate the effect of alignment and hybrid methods on the performance of APT-based composition, providing insights into the importance of syntactic structure in compositional distributional semantics.

Weaknesses
----------

1. The dataset used is small and not well-described, which may impact the generalizability of the results.
2. The innovation of the paper, the use of aligned vectors, has been published in a previous "suppressed reference" by the authors, which may reduce the novelty of the contribution.
3. The improvements using aligned vectors are marginal, and the results may be fragile due to the small size of the dataset.

Questions to Authors
-------------------

1. Can you provide more details about the dataset, including the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components?
2. How many judgments were provided for a given compound, and are there many compounds with the same final compositionality score?
3. Can you clarify the explanation of why the 2nd order paths in the composed representations are not reliable?
4. Can you provide the results from the word2vec vectors for the compound phrases?
5. Can you explain the intuition behind the FREQ baseline and why a frequent compound would tend to be compositional?
