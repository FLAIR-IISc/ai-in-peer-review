- Paper experiments on predicting compound compositionality for 90 English compounds using cosine similarity between compound vectors and compositions of component vectors.
- Uses two vector types: neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors, and compares plain vs. "aligned" vectors for the dependent component.
- Evaluation is Spearman correlation with human judgments on a 1–5 compositionality scale from an unspecified number of judges.
- Main result: syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings on this task.
- Aligned vectors alone perform worse than unmodified vectors; a highly tuned combination of aligned and unaligned gives only slight improvement.
- Overall improvements from alignment are marginal and fragile given the small, poorly described dataset.
- Introduction is clear, but several sections (notably 5.1) are hard to read and would benefit from running examples and clearer exposition.
- Novelty is unclear: aligned vectors appear to be the main innovation but were previously published by the authors.
- Contribution to prior work (Reddy et al.) is not clearly established.
- Dataset issues: the set of 90 compounds is small and selection criteria are not described.
- Frequency ranges of compounds and components are not reported but likely affect results.
- Number of human judgments per compound is unspecified; many compounds may share identical scores, impacting Spearman correlation.
- Terminology problems: "constituent" should be replaced by "component" to avoid confusion with syntactic phrases.
- Section 3: the term "packed anchored tree" is unclear and seems to refer simply to dependency paths between two words.
- Section 3: definition of "elementary APTs" is confusing and needs clarification.
- Section 5: explanation of elementary representations lacks a running example; the term "compound phrase token" is ambiguous.
- Section 5.1: unclear whether elementary APTs are triplets of target word, dependency path, and co-occurring word.
- Section 5.1: "shifted PMI" is not clearly defined; the relation to equation (3) is unclear.
- Section 5.1: contradictory explanation about removing low positive PMI features requires clarification.
- Section 6: puzzling choice to use only first-order features when measuring similarity; question why higher-order dependency features are not used.
- Section 6: Note 3’s explanation for not using second-order paths in composed representations is unclear.
- Section 6: optimal values for hyperparameters h and q in equations (8) and (9) are not reported, hindering assessment of hybridity effects.
- Section 6: recommend including results for word2vec vectors for compound phrases.
- Section 6: intuition for the FREQ baseline is unclear and suggests potential dataset bias if frequent compounds are assumed more compositional.
