The paper reports on experiments aimed at predicting the level of compositionality of compounds in English. The dataset used is a previously existing set of 90 compounds, whose compositionality was ranked from 1 to 5 by a non-specified number of judges. The general form of each experiment is to compute a cosine similarity between the vector of the compound (treated as one token) and a composition of the vectors of the components. Evaluation is performed using a Spearman correlation between the cosine similarity and the human judgments.

The experiments vary in two main aspects: the vectors used (neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors) and, for the latter case, whether plain or "aligned" vectors should be used for the dependent component of the compound. The alignment attempts to capture a shift from the dependent to the head, as proposed in a previous suppressed reference. The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, and the use of aligned vectors alone performs less well than non-modified vectors. However, a highly-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors provides a slight improvement.

Regarding the form of the paper, I found the introduction to be well-written, but other parts (such as section 5.1) are difficult to read, despite the underlying notions not being very complicated. Rephrasing with running examples could help improve clarity.

Regarding the substance, I have several concerns. Firstly, the innovation with respect to Reddy et al. seems to be the use of aligned vectors, but these have been published in a previous suppressed reference by the authors. Secondly, the dataset is small and not sufficiently described. In particular, the frequency ranges of the compounds are likely to impact the results. Since the improvements using aligned vectors are marginal over a small dataset, in which it is unclear how the choice of compounds was performed, I find that the findings in the paper are quite fragile.

More detailed comments and questions are as follows:

Section 3: I do not understand the need for the new name "packed anchored tree." It appears to be a plain extraction of the paths between two lexical items in a dependency tree, which is a traditional approach in syntactic distributional representations of words. Further, why is it called a tree? What are "elementary APTs" (section 5.1)? 

Table 2: Did you forget to mention that you discard features of order more than 3? Or does it have to do with the elimination of some incompatible types you mention (for which an example should be provided, as it is not very clear)?

Section 4: Since the Reddy et al. dataset is central to your work, it seems necessary to explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components? There is a high chance that results vary depending on the frequency ranges. How many judgments were provided for a given compound? Are there many compounds with the same final compositionality score? Isn't this a problem when ranking them to compute the Spearman correlation?

Apparently, you use "constituent" for a component of the N N sequence. I would suggest using "component," as "constituent" also has the sense of "phrase" (syntagm). The intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase, then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences is certainly true if the constituent is the head of the phrase, but less true otherwise (e.g., "spelling bee" does not have the distribution of "spelling").

Section 5: Please provide a running example to help the reader follow which object you're talking about. Does "compound phrase token" refer to the merged components of the compound? 

Section 5.1: I assume that "elementary APTs" are a triplet target word w + dependency path r + other word w'. I find the name confusing. Clarify whether "shifted PMI" refers to PMI as defined in equation (3). The sentence "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts" seems contradictory. Do "these phrases" co-refer with "things" here?

The whole sentence seems puzzling, and the paragraph from 558 to 563 is unclear. What does "not properly aligned" mean? Do you mean not aligned at all? I do not understand why the potential overlap should be considerable in the particular case of the NMOD relation between the two components.

Paragraph 575 to 580 is quite puzzling. Why does the whole paper make use of higher-order dependency features and then suddenly, at the critical point of actually measuring the crucial metric of similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors, use first-order features only? Note 3 is supposed to provide an answer, but I do not understand the explanation of why the 2nd order paths in the composed representations are not reliable. Please clarify.

Section 6: The statement "Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect" does not seem obvious from table 3. What are the optimal values for h and q in equations 8 and 9? They are important in order to estimate how much "hybridity" provides the slight gains with respect to the unaligned results.

It would be helpful to have the results from the word2vec vectors for the compound phrases. I do not understand the intuition behind the FREQ baseline. Why would a frequent compound tend to be compositional? This suggests maybe a bias in the dataset.

In the legend of table 4, it could be helpful to specify that the results correspond to using the add combination.
