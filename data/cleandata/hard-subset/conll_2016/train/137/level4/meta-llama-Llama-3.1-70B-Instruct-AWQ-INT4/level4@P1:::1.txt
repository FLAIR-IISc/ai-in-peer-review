The paper reports experiments on predicting the level of compositionality of compounds in English using a previously existing dataset of 90 compounds with human judgments of compositionality. The general form of each experiment is to compute a cosine similarity between the vector of the compound and a composition of the vectors of its components. Evaluation is performed using a Spearman correlation between the cosine similarity and the human judgments. The experiments vary in the vectors used (neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors) and the composition method (aligned versus non-aligned vectors). The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, and the use of aligned alone performs less well than non-modified vectors, while a highly-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors provides a slight improvement.

The introduction is well-written, but other parts, such as section 5.1, are difficult to read due to the underlying notions not being clearly explained. Rephrasing with running examples could help.

Regarding the substance, several concerns arise:

1. The innovation with respect to Reddy et al. seems to be the use of aligned vectors, but they have been published in a previous reference by the authors. It is unclear what new insights this paper provides.

2. The dataset is small and not well-described. In particular, the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components are not provided, which could impact the results.

3. The improvements using aligned vectors are marginal, and the findings in the paper are quite fragile due to the small dataset and unclear choice of compounds.

Main Contributions:

1. The paper explores the use of syntactic-context count vectors and neural embeddings for predicting the level of compositionality of compounds in English.

2. The experiments investigate the effect of aligned versus non-aligned vectors on the composition method.

3. The results provide insights into the performance of different composition methods and vector representations for this task.

Strengths:

1. The paper explores a novel approach to predicting compositionality using syntactic-context count vectors.

2. The experiments are well-designed, and the results provide valuable insights into the performance of different composition methods.

Weaknesses:

1. The dataset is small, and the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components are not well-described.

2. The improvements using aligned vectors are marginal, and the findings in the paper are quite fragile.

3. The paper does not provide new insights beyond the use of aligned vectors, which has been previously published by the authors.

Questions to Authors:

1. How were the 90 compounds selected for the dataset, and what are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components?

2. Can you provide more details on the aligned vector composition method and how it differs from previous work?

3. How do you plan to address the limitations of the small dataset and marginal improvements using aligned vectors in future work?
