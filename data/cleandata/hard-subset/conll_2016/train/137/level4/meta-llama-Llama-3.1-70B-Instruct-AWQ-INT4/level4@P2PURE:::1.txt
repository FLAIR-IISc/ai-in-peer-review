General comments
=============================

The paper presents experiments on predicting the compositionality of English compounds using a previously existing dataset of 90 compounds, whose compositionality was ranked from 1 to 5 by an unspecified number of judges. The general experimental setup involves computing the cosine similarity between the vector of the compound (treated as a single token) and the composition of the vectors of its components. Evaluation is performed using a Spearman correlation between the cosine similarity and human judgments. The experiments vary in terms of the vectors used (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors) and, for the latter case, whether plain or "aligned" vectors should be used for the dependent component of the compound. The alignment aims to capture the shift from the dependent to the head, as proposed in a previous suppressed reference by the authors. The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, and the use of aligned vectors alone performs less well than non-modified vectors, while a highly-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors provides a slight improvement.

Regarding the paper's form, I found the introduction to be well-written, but other sections (such as 5.1) are difficult to read, despite the underlying notions being relatively straightforward. Rephrasing with running examples could improve clarity.

Regarding the substance, I have several concerns:

- The innovation with respect to Reddy et al. appears to be the use of aligned vectors, which have been previously published by the authors in a suppressed reference.
- The dataset is small and not sufficiently described. In particular, frequency ranges are likely to impact the results.
- Given that the improvements using aligned vectors are marginal over a small dataset, where the choice of compounds is unclear, I find the paper's findings to be fragile.

More detailed comments/questions
================================

Section 3
I do not understand the need for the new term "packed anchored tree." It seems to be a plain extraction of the paths between two lexical items in a dependency tree, which is a straightforward extension of traditional syntactic distributional representations of words (e.g., Lin, 1998).

Furthermore, why is it referred to as a tree? What are "elementary APTs" (section 5.1)?

Table 2: Did you omit mentioning that you discard features of order higher than 3, which is why, for instance, NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ does not appear in the leftmost bottom cell of Table 2? Alternatively, does this have to do with the elimination of some incompatible types you mention (for which an example should be provided, as I found the explanation unclear)?

Section 4
Since the Reddy et al. dataset is central to your work, it seems necessary to explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components? It is likely that results vary depending on the frequency ranges.

How many judgments were provided for a given compound? Are there many compounds with the same final compositionality score? Isn't this a problem when ranking them to compute the Spearman correlation?

Apparently, you use "constituent" to refer to a component of the N N sequence. I would suggest using "component," as "constituent" also refers to a phrase (syntagm).

The statement "... the intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase, then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences" is certainly true if the constituent is the head of the phrase, but less true otherwise (e.g., "spelling bee" does not have the distribution of "spelling").

Section 5
The statement "Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context" could benefit from a running example to help the reader follow the object being referred to.

Does "compound phrase token" refer to the merged components of the compound?

Section 5.1
I assume that "elementary APTs" are triplets consisting of a target word w, a dependency path r, and another word w'. I find the name confusing.

Please clarify whether "shifted PMI" refers to PMI as defined in equation (3).

The statement "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts" seems contradictory. Do "these phrases" co-refer with "things" here?

The statement "In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned" is unclear. What does "not properly aligned" mean? Do you mean not aligned at all?

I do not understand the paragraph spanning lines 558-563.

Why should the potential overlap be considerable in the particular case of the NMOD relation between the two components?

The paragraph spanning lines 575-580 is puzzling.

Why does the paper use higher-order dependency features throughout, only to suddenly use first-order features when measuring the crucial metric of similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors? Note 3 is supposed to provide an explanation, but I do not understand the reasoning behind the unreliability of second-order paths in the composed representations.

Section 6
The statement "Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect" is not obvious from Table 3.

What are the optimal values for h and q in equations 8 and 9? These values are crucial for estimating the extent to which "hybridity" provides the slight gains with respect to the unaligned results.

It would be helpful to include this information in the legend of Table 4, which appears to correspond to using the add combination.

Additionally, could you provide the results from the word2vec vectors for the compound phrases?

I do not understand the intuition behind the FREQ baseline. Why would a frequent compound tend to be compositional? This suggests a possible bias in the dataset.
