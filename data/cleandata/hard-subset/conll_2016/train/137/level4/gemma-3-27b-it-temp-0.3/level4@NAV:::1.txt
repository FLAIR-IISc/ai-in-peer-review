Review- General comments
=============================
The paper investigates the prediction of compositionality in English compounds. It utilizes a pre-existing dataset of 90 compounds, each assigned a compositionality score from 1 to 5 by an unspecified number of human judges. The core experimental approach involves calculating the cosine similarity between a compound’s vector representation (treated as a single token) and the composition of its component vectors. Evaluation is based on Spearman correlation between these cosine similarities and the human-assigned compositionality scores. The experiments explore different vector representations – neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors – and, for the latter, the impact of “aligned” vectors designed to capture a dependency shift between components (previously described in a non-public reference). Results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, while using aligned vectors alone yields poorer performance than unaligned vectors. A carefully tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors offers a marginal improvement.

Regarding the paper’s presentation, the introduction is well-written, but certain sections (e.g., 5.1) are difficult to follow despite the underlying concepts being relatively straightforward. Incorporating illustrative examples would likely enhance clarity. 

Concerning the substance, my primary concerns relate to the novelty of the work and the robustness of the findings. The key innovation appears to be the use of aligned vectors, but these were previously presented in a “suppressed reference” by the authors. Furthermore, the dataset is small and lacks sufficient description. The range of frequencies within the dataset is likely to influence the results. Given the marginal improvements achieved with aligned vectors and the small dataset size, coupled with uncertainty regarding compound selection, I consider the conclusions to be somewhat fragile.

More detailed comments/questions
================================
Section 3
The motivation for introducing the term "packed anchored tree" is unclear. It appears to be a standard extraction of dependency paths between lexical items, representing a straightforward extension of existing techniques in syntactic distributional representations of words (e.g., Lin 98, which typically employs paths of length one or two with collapsed prepositions). What defines it as a “tree,” and what are “elementary APTs” (section 5.1)?

Table 2: Is it accurate to omit the mention that features of order greater than 3 are discarded (explaining the absence of, for example, NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ in the bottom-left cell)? Or does this relate to the exclusion of incompatible types, for which a clarifying example would be helpful?

Section 4:
Given the centrality of the Reddy et al. dataset, a detailed explanation of the compound selection process is necessary. What are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components? Variations in frequency ranges could significantly impact the results. How many judgments were elicited for each compound? Are there numerous compounds sharing the same compositionality score, and if so, how does this affect the Spearman correlation ranking? 

The term "constituent" is used to denote a component of the N N sequence. I suggest using "component" instead, as "constituent" can also refer to a phrase (syntagm). 

Regarding the statement “…the intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences,” this intuition is more strongly applicable when the constituent is the head of the phrase; it is less reliable otherwise (e.g., “spelling bee” does not exhibit the same distribution as “spelling”).

Section 5
“Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.” Please provide a concrete example to aid reader comprehension of the referenced objects. Does “compound phrase token” refer to the combined components of the compound?

Section 5.1
I presume “elementary APTs” represent a triplet consisting of a target word *w*, a dependency path *r*, and another word *w’*? The terminology is confusing and should be clarified. 

Please clarify whether “shifted PMI” refers to the PMI as defined in equation (3).

“Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.” Do “these phrases” co-refer with “things” here? The sentence appears contradictory and requires clarification.

“In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned.” What does “not properly aligned” signify? Does this mean not aligned at all? I find paragraphs 558-563 difficult to understand.

Why should potential overlap be considerable specifically in the case of the NMOD relation between the two components?

Paragraphs 575-580 are puzzling. Why does the paper employ higher-order dependency features throughout, only to revert to first-order features when measuring the crucial similarity metric between composed and observed phrasal vectors? Note 3 attempts to explain this, but the rationale for the unreliability of second-order paths in composed representations remains unclear.

Section 6
“Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect” is not readily apparent from Table 3. 

What are the optimal values for *h* and *q* in equations 8 and 9? These values are important for assessing the contribution of “hybridity” to the slight gains observed compared to the unaligned results.

Table 4’s results appear to correspond to the additive combination method; this should be clarified in the legend. Additionally, could you provide the results for word2vec vectors applied to the compound phrases?

I do not understand the rationale behind the FREQ baseline. Why would a frequent compound be more likely to be compositional? This suggests a potential bias within the dataset.
