- Paper evaluates compositionality prediction on 90 English compounds using cosine similarity and Spearman correlation with human scores.  
- Syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings in the experiments.  
- Aligned vectors alone perform worse than unmodified vectors; a carefully tuned mix of aligned and unaligned vectors yields only slight improvement.  
- Main novelty (use of aligned vectors) is limited and was previously published by the authors; overall innovation is modest compared to Reddy et al.  
- Dataset is small (90 compounds), inadequately described, and compound selection/frequency ranges are not provided, making results fragile.  
- Number of human judgments per compound and ranking methodology are not reported, raising evaluation concerns.  
- Introduction is well written, but other sections (notably 5.1) are hard to follow and need clearer exposition and running examples.  
- Terminology is unclear: "packed anchored tree," "tree," "elementary APTs," and "compound phrase token" are insufficiently explained.  
- Table 2 seems to omit features of order >3; an example and clarification about incompatible types are needed.  
- The paper’s description of the elementary representation for compound constituents and "shifted PMI" (relation to Eq. 3) requires clearer explanation and examples.  
- Definition and naming of "elementary APTs" (target word, dependency path, other word) are confusing and should be clarified.  
- Contradictory or unclear statements about removing features with low positive PMI need resolution.  
- Expectation of little overlap between unaligned APTs is questionable, especially for NMOD relations where overlap is plausible.  
- Use of higher-order dependency features throughout but switching to first-order features for similarity measurement is unexplained.  
- Claim that smoothing PPMI with α = 0.75 helps is not clearly supported by Table 3.  
- Optimal hyperparameters (h and q in Eqs. 8–9) are not reported, obscuring the contribution of hybridity.  
- Table 4’s results seem to correspond to the add combination but the legend doesn’t state this; clarity needed.  
- Results for word2vec vectors representing compound phrases are missing.  
- The FREQ baseline’s intuition is unclear and may indicate dataset bias.
