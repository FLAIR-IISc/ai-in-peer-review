General Comments
=============================
This paper is a report on an experiment to predict the structural level of compounds in English.
The dataset used here is a set of 90 pre-existing compounds, whose structural dimensions are transferred (by an unspecified number of judges) from 1 to 5.
The general form of each test is to calculate cosine similarity between a vector of compounds (considered as a token) and a combination of vectors of elements.
The cosine similarity is evaluated using the Spearman correlation between human judgment.
The tests are different:
- For the vectors used: neural embedding versus syntactic-context computation vector
- And in the latter case, whether a plain or "aligned" vector should be used for the dependent element of the compound. The alignment tries to capture a change from dependent to head. Alignment was previously proposed in a hidden reference.
The results indicate that syntactic-context computation vector performs better than embedding, and using only aligned vectors performs less well than non-aligned vectors, and a highly tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors provides a slight improvement.
Regarding the structure of the paper, I found the introduction to be quite well-written, but other parts (such as paragraph 5.1) are difficult to read, although the underlying concepts are not very complex. Rewriting with ongoing examples may help.
Regarding the content, I have several concerns:
- The innovation related to Reddy et al seems to use aligned vectors,
But they were published by the authors in an earlier "hidden reference".
- The dataset is small, and not described adequately. In particular, the frequency range may affect the results.
Since the improvements using aligned vectors are minor, in a small dataset where the choice of compounds is unclear, I think the paper's results are quite fragile.

(which usually uses one or two-length paths until (Lin 98), including contracted propositions).

Paragraph 4:
Since Reddy et al's dataset is the focus of your work, it is necessary to explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What is the frequency range / elements, etc. of the compounds? Depending on the frequency range, the probability of the results being different is very high.
How many judgments were provided for a specific compound? Are there many compounds with the same final structural score? Isn't it a problem to determine their position when calculating the Spearman correlation?
It seems you use "element" for an element of sequence N N. I would suggest "element", because the "phrasal" (synonym) of "element" also has meaning.
"... the idea that if an element is literally used within a phrase, it is highly likely that the compound and the element share a co-occurrence"
: Remember that the idea must be true if the element is the head of the phrase, otherwise it is much less true (e.g., the "spelling" of "spelling" does not have a distribution).
