- Empirical study comparing syntax-based APTs and neural embeddings (word2vec) for predicting compositionality of 90 English compound nouns.
- Main contribution: detailed empirical comparison showing structured, count-based APTs can outperform neural embeddings on this task.
- Main contribution: introduction and evaluation of a hybrid APT model combining aligned and unaligned features, yielding modest gains.
- Strength: tackles a core problem (compositionality) and uses a well-motivated evaluation that avoids standard phrase-similarity confounds.
- Strength: comprehensive experimental comparison against strong baselines (CBOW, Skip-gram, RAND, FREQ, REDDY) with varied settings.
- Strength: potentially impactful result that syntax-based count vectors outperform dense neural embeddings for syntax-sensitive tasks.
- Weakness: limited novelty — key APT alignment idea appears pre-existing and the hybrid yields only marginal improvement (ρ 0.79 vs 0.78).
- Weakness: conclusions may be fragile due to small dataset (90 compounds) and lack of analysis of frequency or constituency distributions; frequency baseline performs strongly.
- Weakness: unclear exposition in key sections (APT description, model parameters) and insufficient justification for restricting similarity to first-order dependency features.
- Question: provide frequency ranges of the 90 compounds and components in ukWaC to rule out frequency-driven correlations with the FREQ baseline.
- Question: justify why higher-order dependency features are discarded in final similarity and clarify the claim that they are "not reliable."
- Question: report optimal values for weighting parameters h (head vs modifier) and q (aligned vs unaligned) in the best hybrid model.
- Question: clarify what is novel about "Anchored Packed Trees" versus conventional dependency-path feature extraction and explain the "tree" terminology.
- Question: rephrase and clarify the explanation for why shifted PMI performs poorly, especially the sentence about removing low-positive-PMI features.
