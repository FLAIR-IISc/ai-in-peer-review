**General Comments**
=============================
This paper presents a series of experiments aimed at predicting the degree of compositionality for English compounds. The study utilizes an existing dataset of 90 compounds, each rated on a 1-to-5 compositionality scale by an unspecified number of human annotators. The core methodology involves calculating the cosine similarity between the vector representation of the entire compound (as a single token) and a vector derived from the composition of its individual components. Performance is evaluated by computing the Spearman correlation between the model's cosine similarity scores and the gold-standard human judgments.

The experiments explore several variables, primarily the type of vector representation (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors). For the count-based vectors, the authors investigate the impact of using either standard vectors or "aligned" vectors for the compound's dependent component. This alignment, which aims to model the semantic shift from the dependent to the head, was introduced in a previously published work by the authors (cited as a suppressed reference).

The reported results show that syntactic-context count vectors are more effective than neural embeddings. Furthermore, using aligned vectors alone underperforms the standard, non-aligned vectors, while a finely-tuned combination of both aligned and unaligned vectors yields a marginal performance gain.

In terms of presentation, the introduction is clear and well-written. However, other sections, notably 5.1, are difficult to follow despite the relative simplicity of the concepts being described. The clarity could be significantly improved by incorporating running examples to illustrate the key ideas.

Regarding the scientific contribution, I have several major concerns:
- The primary novelty compared to Reddy et al. appears to be the application of aligned vectors. However, this technique was developed and published by the authors in a prior work (the "suppressed reference"), which diminishes the novelty of the current contribution.
- The dataset is small (90 compounds) and insufficiently described. Key statistics, such as the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components, are missing. These factors are highly likely to influence the results.
- Given that the performance gains from using aligned vectors are marginal, achieved on a small dataset whose selection criteria are not specified, the paper's conclusions appear to be fragile and may not generalize.

**More Detailed Comments/Questions**
================================
**Section 3**
I do not see the justification for introducing the new term "packed anchored tree". This seems to be a straightforward extraction of dependency paths between two lexical items, which is an extension of standard practice in syntactic distributional semantics (e.g., Lin 98) that uses short, sometimes collapsed, paths. Furthermore, the rationale for calling this structure a "tree" is unclear. Could the authors also define what is meant by "elementary APTs" (mentioned in section 5.1)?

Regarding Table 2, it appears that features of an order greater than 3 have been discarded. Is this correct? If so, this should be explicitly stated. This would explain, for instance, the absence of NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ in the bottom-left cell. Alternatively, is its absence related to the elimination of "incompatible types"? If the latter, this concept needs to be clarified with an example, as the current explanation is not clear.

**Section 4**
Given its central role, the Reddy et al. dataset requires a more thorough description. How were the 90 compounds selected? What are the frequency distributions for the compounds and their components? Results are likely to be sensitive to these frequency effects.

How many annotators provided judgments for each compound? Is there a high degree of score collision (i.e., many compounds with the same final score)? This could potentially be problematic for a ranking-based evaluation like Spearman correlation.

The term "constituent" is used to refer to a component of the N-N compound. I suggest using "component" instead to avoid ambiguity, as "constituent" also refers to a syntactic phrase.

The paper states the intuition that "if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences." This intuition holds primarily for the head of a phrase, but is much less true for the dependent (e.g., "spelling bee" does not share the distribution of "spelling"). This should be qualified.

**Section 5**
The sentence "Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context" is difficult to parse. A running example would greatly aid reader comprehension. For instance, what does "compound phrase token" refer to here? Is it the vector for the compound treated as a single unit?

**Section 5.1**
My understanding is that "elementary APTs" refers to a triplet of (target word w, dependency path r, context word w'). Is this correct? The term itself is confusing.

Does "shifted PMI" refer to the PMI definition in Equation (3)? Please clarify.

The sentence "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts" is contradictory. Does "these phrases" refer back to the "features" or the "things"? The logic of this sentence needs to be rephrased for clarity.

What is meant by "APTs which have not been properly aligned"? Does this simply mean unaligned vectors, or something else?

The reasoning in the paragraph from line 558 to 563 is hard to follow. Why would the potential overlap be "considerable" specifically for the NMOD relation between components?

The paragraph from 575 to 580 is perplexing. The paper leverages higher-order dependency features throughout, but then, at the critical step of measuring similarity, it reverts to using only first-order features. Footnote 3 is meant to explain this, but its justification for why second-order paths in the composed representations are unreliable is not clear. This needs further explanation.

**Section 6**
The claim that "Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect" is not immediately evident from Table 3. This observation could be substantiated.

What were the optimal values for the hyperparameters h and q in Equations 8 and 9? These values are crucial for understanding the contribution of the "hybrid" model that achieves the slight performance gains over the unaligned baseline.

The results in Table 4 appear to be for the "add" combination method. This should be explicitly mentioned in the caption or legend for clarity.

Could the authors also provide the results using word2vec vectors for the compound phrases themselves (treated as single tokens)? This seems like a relevant baseline.

The intuition behind the FREQ baseline is unclear. Why would a more frequent compound necessarily be more compositional? This correlation might suggest an inherent bias in the dataset.
