### Summary of the paper and main contributions

This paper presents an empirical study on predicting the compositionality of 90 English compound nouns. The authors compare two main approaches: a compositional method using state-of-the-art neural word embeddings (word2vec) and an integrated compositional distributional model based on sparse, high-dimensional, syntax-based vectors (Anchored Packed Trees or APTs). The core experimental setup involves computing the similarity between a compound's observed vector and a composed vector derived from its constituents, and then correlating this similarity score with human judgments. The results suggest that the syntax-based APT models, particularly a hybrid version combining aligned and unaligned features, outperform the neural embedding models on this task.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A detailed empirical comparison of syntax-based count vectors and neural word embeddings for the specific task of compositionality detection. The finding that structured, count-based models outperform popular neural embeddings is a valuable data point for the field.
2.  The introduction and evaluation of a hybrid compositional model (the hybrid APT) that combines different feature alignments. Although the performance gains are modest, this demonstrates a potentially fruitful direction for integrating different types of contextual information.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses an Important Problem**: The paper tackles the challenging and fundamental problem of compositionality, which is a known limitation of many distributional models. Using compositionality detection as a testbed for compositional methods is a well-motivated and insightful evaluation strategy, as it avoids confounds present in standard phrase similarity tasks.
2.  **Thorough Experimental Comparison**: The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments comparing their proposed APT models against strong baselines, including state-of-the-art neural embeddings (CBOW and Skip-gram) with varied parameter settings. The inclusion of multiple baselines (RAND, FREQ, REDDY) helps to contextualize the results effectively.
3.  **Interesting and Potentially Impactful Results**: The finding that carefully constructed, syntax-based count vectors (APTs) outperform popular, powerful, and more commonly used neural embeddings is significant. This result contributes to the ongoing discussion about the relative merits of explicit, structured representations versus dense, prediction-based ones, particularly for syntax-sensitive tasks.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty and Marginal Gains**: The primary methodological contribution, the APT model with alignment, is attributed to a "suppressed reference," suggesting it is not novel to this paper. The main finding is that a highly-tuned hybrid model, combining this prior work with a simpler unaligned model, yields a marginal improvement (0.79 vs 0.78 Spearman's ρ) over the unaligned version. This small gain raises questions about the significance of the contribution.
2.  **Fragility of Conclusions due to Small Dataset**: The experiments are conducted on a small dataset of 90 compounds from Reddy et al. (2011). The paper lacks a detailed analysis of this dataset, such as the frequency distribution of the compounds or their constituents, which is known to heavily influence the performance of distributional models. Conclusions drawn from a small, potentially biased dataset might not generalize well. The strong performance of the frequency baseline (FREQ) further suggests a possible frequency bias in the dataset that is not adequately discussed.
3.  **Lack of Clarity in Key Sections**: Several key descriptions are difficult to follow. Section 3 introduces "Anchored Packed Trees" without clearly distinguishing them from standard dependency path extraction in distributional semantics. Section 5.1, which describes the APT model parameters, is particularly dense and would benefit greatly from a running example. Furthermore, the critical decision to restrict the final similarity computation to only first-order dependency features (Section 5.1, footnote 3) is not well-justified; the provided explanation that higher-order paths are "not reliable" is vague and undermines the paper's emphasis on using these very features for composition.

### Questions to Authors

1.  **Dataset Details**: Could you provide more details about the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset? Specifically, what are the frequency ranges for the 90 compounds and their components in the ukWaC corpus? Given the strong performance of the FREQ baseline, how can you be sure your model is capturing compositionality rather than simply correlating with frequency effects present in the data?
2.  **Justification for Discarding Higher-Order Features**: The core motivation for the APT model is the use of higher-order dependency features for composition. Why, then, is the final similarity between the composed and observed vectors calculated using only first-order features? Please clarify the claim in footnote 3 that higher-order paths in the composed representations are "not reliable". This seems counter-intuitive and weakens the argument for the model's design.
3.  **Model Parameter Interpretation**: What were the optimal values for the weighting parameters `h` (head vs. modifier) and `q` (aligned vs. unaligned) in the best-performing hybrid model (Eq. 9)? These values are crucial for interpreting the relative importance of the different components of your model.
4.  **Clarity on Terminology**: In Section 3, could you clarify what is novel about "Anchored Packed Trees" compared to conventional methods of extracting dependency paths as features for distributional models (e.g., as in Padó & Lapata, 2007)? The name itself is also unclear; why is it a "tree"?
5.  **Confusing Statement on Shifted PMI**: The explanation in Section 5.1 for why shifted PMI performs poorly is difficult to parse: "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts." Could you please rephrase this to clarify the reasoning?
