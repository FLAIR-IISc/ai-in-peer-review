### General Comments

This paper presents an experimental study on predicting the compositionality of English compounds. The work uses a pre-existing dataset of 90 compounds, each rated for compositionality on a scale of 1 to 5 by an unspecified number of judges. The general methodology involves computing the cosine similarity between the vector for a compound and a composed vector derived from its components. The system's performance is evaluated using Spearman's rank correlation between these similarity scores and the human judgments.

The experiments systematically vary two main factors:
1.  The type of vector representation: neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors.
2.  For the count vectors, the use of standard vectors versus "aligned" vectors for the dependent component of the compound. This alignment technique, designed to capture a semantic shift from the dependent to the head, was introduced in a previous publication by the authors, which is cited as a "suppressed reference."

The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings. The alignment method used alone performs worse than non-modified vectors, while a highly-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields a slight improvement.

In terms of presentation, the introduction is well-written. However, other parts of the paper, such as Section 5.1, are difficult to follow, despite the relative simplicity of the underlying concepts. The addition of running examples would significantly improve readability.

Substantively, I have several major concerns:

*   **Novelty:** The primary technical contribution appears to be the application of aligned vectors. However, since these were introduced in a previous, suppressed reference by the same authors, the novelty of the present work is questionable.
*   **Dataset:** The dataset is small (90 compounds) and inadequately described. Key details, such as the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components, are omitted. This is a significant concern, as these factors are likely to influence the results.
*   **Fragility of Findings:** Given that the performance gains from the proposed method are marginal and are demonstrated on a small dataset with unclear selection criteria, the paper's conclusions appear fragile.

### More Detailed Comments/Questions

**Section 3**
*   The motivation for introducing the new term "packed anchored tree" (PAT) is unclear. This method appears to be a standard extraction of paths between lexical items in a dependency tree, extending traditional syntactic distributional representations that use paths of length one or two. The rationale for calling this structure a "tree" is also not explained.
*   The term "elementary APTs," used later in Section 5.1, requires a clear definition here.
*   Regarding Table 2, it is unclear why features of order greater than 3 (e.g., NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ) are absent from the bottom-left cell. Is this due to a maximum feature order, or does it relate to the "elimination of some incompatible types" mentioned in the text? If the latter, an example of such an elimination would be helpful.

**Section 4**
*   Given the centrality of the Reddy et al. dataset to this work, a more detailed description is necessary. Specifically, how were the 90 compounds selected? What are the frequency ranges for the compounds and their components? Results are likely to vary significantly across different frequency bands.
*   How many human judgments were collected per compound? Furthermore, what is the distribution of the final compositionality scores? A high number of ties could complicate the interpretation of the Spearman correlation.
*   The paper uses the term "constituent" to refer to a component of the N-N compound. I suggest using "component" throughout to avoid ambiguity, as "constituent" also refers to a syntactic phrase.
*   The intuition that "if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences" needs refinement. While this is likely true when the component is the head of the compound, it is much less certain for the dependent (e.g., "spelling" in "spelling bee" does not share the distribution of the compound).

**Section 5**
*   The sentence "Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context" is difficult to parse. A concrete running example would greatly help the reader understand which vector representations are being discussed. For instance, what does "compound phrase token" refer to? Is it the vector for the compound treated as a single unit?

**Section 5.1**
*   What exactly are "elementary APTs"? Are they triplets consisting of a target word, a dependency path, and a context word?
*   Please clarify if "shifted PMI" refers to the PMI as defined in Equation (3).
*   The sentence "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts" is confusing and appears contradictory. Do "these phrases" co-refer with "things"? Please rephrase to clarify the intended meaning.
*   What is meant by "APTs which have not been properly aligned"? Does this simply mean unaligned APTs?
*   The reasoning in lines 558-563 is hard to follow. Why would the potential overlap of features be "considerable" specifically for the NMOD relation between components?
*   The methodology described in lines 575-580 is puzzling. The paper's representation is built on higher-order dependency features, yet at the critical step of measuring similarity between the composed and observed vectors, only first-order features are used. Footnote 3 is meant to justify this, but its explanation of why second-order paths in composed representations are unreliable is not convincing. Please provide a clearer justification for this choice.

**Section 6**
*   The claim that smoothing PPMI with α = 0.75 "generally has a small positive effect" is not clearly supported by the results in Table 3. This observation requires further justification.
*   To better understand the contribution of the hybrid model, what were the optimal values for the hyperparameters *h* and *q* (equations 8 and 9)? The degree of "hybridity" that yields the reported slight gains over the unaligned model is an important detail.
*   The legend for Table 4 should explicitly state that the results correspond to the `add` combination function.
*   For completeness, could you provide the results from using word2vec vectors for the observed compound phrases themselves? This would serve as a valuable baseline.
*   The intuition behind the FREQ baseline is unclear. Why would a more frequent compound be expected to be more compositional? The strong performance of this baseline may suggest a potential bias in the dataset.
