- Paper studies compound compositionality by cosine similarity between observed compound vectors and composed constituent vectors, comparing word2vec vs. syntactic-context count vectors.  
- Main empirical claims: syntactic-context vectors beat neural embeddings, and a tuned hybrid of aligned+unaligned syntactic vectors gives only marginal gains.  
- Primary novelty concern: main technical ingredient (aligned vectors) appears pre-existing; authors must clarify novel contributions beyond applying it to this task.  
- Dataset concern: reliance on a small (90 compounds) and under-described Reddy et al. dataset raises generalizability questions.  
- Request dataset details: need selection criteria, frequency ranges of compounds and components, and discussion of frequency effects on results.  
- Annotation transparency: clarify number of annotators per compound, annotation procedure, and prevalence of ties affecting Spearman evaluation.  
- Marginal gains: reported improvements are small and may be fragile given dataset size and unclear composition.  
- Presentation issues: several sections (notably 5.1) are hard to follow and require rephrasing and running examples to illustrate data structures and methods.  
- Terminology: unclear need for the new term "packed anchored tree" (PAT) and why it's called a "tree" rather than a standard dependency-path extraction.  
- Define terms earlier: provide a clear definition of "elementary APT" and related terminology before section 5.1.  
- Feature truncation question: Table 2 suggests discarding features of order >3—state this explicitly and give examples of "incompatible types."  
- Clarify shifted PMI: specify whether "shifted PMI" refers to the PMI in Equation (3) and explain choices around PPMI smoothing (α=0.75) and its reported effects.  
- Confusing PMI sentence: rephrase the claim linking removal of low-PMI features to phrases appearing in a small number of informative contexts.  
- Alignment terminology: clarify whether "not properly aligned" simply means "unaligned."  
- NMOD overlap reasoning: explain why overlap potential is "considerable" specifically for the NMOD relation between components.  
- Higher-order features vs. final similarity: justify why higher-order dependency features are emphasized earlier but the final similarity uses only first-order features; clarify footnote 3’s reasoning.  
- Hybrid model details: report optimal hyperparameter values (h and q) used to produce results to assess aligned vs. unaligned contributions.  
- Table/reporting clarity: specify combination function used (e.g., add) in Table 4 caption and include results using word2vec vectors for observed compound phrases for completeness.  
- FREQ baseline: clarify the intuition for why more frequent compounds would be more compositional and discuss whether the strong baseline performance indicates a frequency-related dataset bias.  
- Terminology suggestion: use "component" instead of "constituent" to avoid syntactic ambiguity.
