### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents an empirical study on predicting the compositionality of English noun compounds. The authors evaluate their method on a pre-existing dataset of 90 compounds, each with human-provided compositionality ratings. The core experimental method involves computing the cosine similarity between a vector representation of a full compound (treated as a single token) and a composed vector derived from its constituent parts. Performance is measured by the Spearman correlation between these similarity scores and the human judgments.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A comparison between different vector representations for compositionality detection, namely state-of-the-art neural embeddings (word2vec) and high-dimensional, count-based vectors derived from syntactic dependency paths (the authors' APT model).
2.  An investigation into a compositional model (APT) that uses higher-order dependency paths. The authors compare a version where the modifier's vector space is "aligned" to the head's space against a simpler, unaligned version, as well as a hybrid model combining both.
3.  The empirical finding that the syntax-aware, count-based APT models, particularly the unaligned and hybrid versions, significantly outperform both the previous state-of-the-art and standard word2vec-based compositional models on this task.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses an Important Problem**: The paper tackles the challenging and important problem of non-compositionality, which remains a significant hurdle for compositional distributional semantics. The task of predicting compositionality serves as an excellent testbed for evaluating the capabilities of compositional models.
2.  **Thorough Empirical Comparison**: The authors provide a detailed comparison between two major families of distributional models: dense neural embeddings and sparse, explicit syntactic vectors. This comparison is valuable, and the finding that the syntax-aware model outperforms the word2vec baseline is a significant result.
3.  **Well-Motivated Approach**: The core idea of using richer, syntax-based features to model composition is well-motivated. The distinction between aligned and unaligned composition within this framework allows for a fine-grained analysis of how different types of syntactic information contribute to the task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Method**: The primary methodological innovation over the baseline (Reddy et al., 2011) is the use of the APT model and its vector alignment technique. However, the paper states that this method was introduced in a "suppressed reference," presumably a previous publication by the authors. This raises questions about the novelty of the current submission, which appears to be primarily an application of an existing method to a known task.
2.  **Fragile Conclusions Due to Small Dataset**: The experiments are conducted on a small dataset of only 90 compounds. The paper provides insufficient detail about this dataset, particularly the criteria for its construction and the frequency distribution of the compounds and their components. As the results show a strong correlation between frequency and compositionality (the FREQ baseline is surprisingly high), the findings may be influenced by dataset-specific biases. The marginal improvements of the best-performing hybrid model over simpler baselines are therefore difficult to interpret and may not generalize.
3.  **Clarity and Presentation**: While the introduction is well-written, other parts of the paper, particularly the description of the APT model and the experimental setup (e.g., Section 5.1), are difficult to follow. The underlying concepts are not overly complex, but the lack of clear definitions and running examples makes the methodology opaque. This significantly hinders the reader's ability to understand the precise mechanics of the model.

### Questions to Authors

1.  **Motivation for APT Terminology**: The term "Anchored Packed Trees" (APTs) seems unnecessarily complex for what appear to be standard dependency paths. Could you clarify the motivation for this new terminology and explain what makes the representation a "tree"? Furthermore, the term "elementary APT" is used without a clear definition.
2.  **Dataset Details**: The Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is central to your evaluation. Could you provide more details about it? Specifically:
    *   What were the selection criteria for the 90 compounds?
    *   What are the frequency ranges for the compounds and their components in your corpus? This is crucial given the strong performance of the FREQ baseline.
    *   How many human judgments were collected per item, and what was the inter-annotator agreement? Are there many ties in the gold-standard ranks, and how might this affect the reliability of the Spearman correlation metric?
3.  **Use of Higher-Order Features**: A key motivation for your model is the use of higher-order dependency features. However, at the critical evaluation step, you discard these features and compute similarity using only first-order paths. Footnote 3 states that higher-order paths in the composed vectors are "not reliable," which seems to undermine the central premise of the model. Could you please elaborate on why these features are unreliable and discuss the implications of discarding them?
4.  **Details of the APT Model**:
    *   In Table 2, it seems features beyond a certain order are discarded. Is this due to a maximum path length, or is it related to the "elimination of some incompatible co-occurrence types"? If the latter, please provide a clear example of an incompatible type.
    *   The explanation for why shifted PMI performed poorly is confusing. In the sentence "Removing features... means that these phrases appear...", what does "these phrases" refer to? Please clarify this argument.
5.  **Hyperparameter Values and Baselines**:
    *   What were the optimal values for the hyperparameters `h` and `q` in your best-performing hybrid models? These values are essential for understanding the relative contributions of the aligned and unaligned components.
    *   The strong performance of the FREQ baseline is counter-intuitive; one might expect frequent phrases to be more idiomatic. Does this correlation suggest a bias in the dataset? Please comment.
