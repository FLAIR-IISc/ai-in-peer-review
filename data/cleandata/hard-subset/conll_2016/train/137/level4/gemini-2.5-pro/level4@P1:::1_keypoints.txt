- Presents an empirical study predicting compositionality of 90 English noun compounds using cosine similarity between compound token vectors and composed constituent vectors, measured by Spearman correlation with human ratings.
- Compares dense neural embeddings (word2vec) against sparse, syntax-aware count-based APT vectors for compositionality detection.
- Investigates APT composition variants: aligned modifier-to-head, unaligned, and a hybrid model combining both.
- Reports that syntax-aware APT models (especially unaligned and hybrid) significantly outperform word2vec and prior state-of-the-art on this dataset.
- Addresses the important problem of detecting non-compositionality as a testbed for compositional distributional semantics.
- Provides a thorough empirical comparison between neural embeddings and explicit syntactic vectors, yielding a valuable finding favoring syntax-aware models.
- Uses a well-motivated, syntax-based approach and analyzes the impact of alignment vs. unaligned composition.
- Novelty is limited because the APT method and alignment technique appear previously published by the authors, raising questions about the paper’s originality.
- Conclusions may be fragile due to the very small dataset (90 compounds) and lack of detail on dataset construction and frequency distributions, with a strong FREQ baseline suggesting dataset bias.
- Presentation and clarity are weak: descriptions of the APT model and experimental setup lack clear definitions and running examples, making the methodology hard to follow.
- Asks authors to justify the APT terminology (why “Anchored Packed Trees”), define “elementary APT,” and explain what makes the representation a tree.
- Requests detailed dataset information: selection criteria for the 90 compounds, frequency ranges of compounds and components, number of human judgments per item, inter-annotator agreement, and effect of ties on Spearman correlation.
- Questions why higher-order dependency features—central to the model—are discarded at evaluation and why they are deemed “not reliable,” plus implications of discarding them.
- Seeks clarification on APT feature pruning: whether features are discarded due to maximum path length or “incompatible co-occurrence types,” and asks for an example of an incompatible type.
- Asks for clarification on the argument about why shifted PMI performed poorly, specifically what “these phrases” refers to in the paper’s explanation.
- Requests the optimal hyperparameter values (h and q) for the best hybrid models and commentary on the surprising strength of the FREQ baseline and possible dataset bias.
