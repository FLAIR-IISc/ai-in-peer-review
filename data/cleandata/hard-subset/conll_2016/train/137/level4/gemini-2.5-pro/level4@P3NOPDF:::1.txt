### Review

**Summary**

This paper investigates methods for predicting the compositionality of English noun-noun compounds. The core approach involves computing the cosine similarity between a vector representing the compound (treated as a single token) and a composed vector derived from its constituents. The experiments compare two types of vector representations: neural embeddings (word2vec) and syntactic-context count vectors based on dependency paths. For the latter, the authors explore a previously proposed "alignment" technique designed to model the syntactic shift from a modifier to a head noun. Using a dataset of 90 compounds with human compositionality ratings, the authors evaluate their models by calculating the Spearman correlation between the model's similarity scores and the human judgments. The main findings are that syntactic-context vectors outperform neural embeddings, and that a fine-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned syntactic vectors yields a marginal improvement over unaligned vectors alone.

**General Comments**

The paper is well-motivated, and the introduction clearly frames the problem of modeling compound compositionality. However, I have several significant concerns regarding the substance and presentation of the work.

My primary concern regards the paper's novelty. The main technical contribution appears to be the application of "aligned" vectors, which the authors state were introduced in a previous, suppressed reference. It would be helpful for the authors to clarify the specific novel contributions of the present work beyond the application of this pre-existing method to a new (though related) task.

A second major concern is the reliance on a small and insufficiently described dataset of 90 compounds from Reddy et al. The paper would be substantially stronger if the authors provided more details about this dataset, particularly regarding the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components, and the criteria used for their selection. Without this information, it is difficult to assess the generalizability of the findings, as frequency effects are known to strongly influence distributional models.

Combined with the above, the reported improvements from the proposed methods are marginal. Given the small scale of the dataset and the lack of clarity on its composition, these slight gains make the paper's central findings appear somewhat fragile.

Regarding the presentation, while the introduction is clear, several key sections (notably 5.1) are difficult to follow. The underlying concepts do not seem overly complex, but the exposition would benefit significantly from rephrasing and the inclusion of running examples to illustrate the data structures and methods being discussed.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

**Section 3**
*   The term "packed anchored tree" (PAT) is introduced, but its necessity is unclear. The structure appears to be a standard extraction of dependency paths between two lexical items, extending what is common in syntactic distributional semantics (e.g., using paths of length one or two, as in Lin, 1998). It would be helpful to clarify why this new terminology is needed and how it differs from established representations. Furthermore, the name is somewhat confusing: in what sense is this structure a "tree"?
*   What exactly are "elementary APTs" (mentioned in section 5.1)? A clear definition is needed earlier.
*   In Table 2, it seems that features of order higher than 3 are discarded (e.g., `NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ` does not appear). Is this correct? If so, this should be stated explicitly. Additionally, the paper mentions eliminating "incompatible types"; an example would be very helpful to clarify what this entails.

**Section 4**
*   Given the centrality of the Reddy et al. dataset, a more thorough description is necessary. How were the 90 compounds selected? What are the frequency ranges for the compounds and their components? This is crucial, as results may be highly sensitive to frequency.
*   Could the authors clarify the human annotation process? For instance, how many annotators provided judgments for each compound? Also, are there many ties in the final compositionality scores? A high number of ties could potentially affect the reliability of a rank-based evaluation metric like Spearman correlation.
*   A minor terminological suggestion: the paper uses "constituent" to refer to a component of the compound (e.g., 'car' in 'car key'). To avoid ambiguity with the syntactic sense of "constituent" (i.e., a phrase), I would suggest using the more specific term "component" throughout.
*   The paper states the intuition that "if a constituent is used literally...the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences." This intuition holds well for the head of a compound, but less so for the modifier (e.g., the distribution of "spelling bee" is very different from that of "spelling"). The authors may wish to refine this statement.

**Section 5 & 5.1**
*   This section would greatly benefit from a concrete running example. For instance, when the paper states, "Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token...", it is difficult to track what "compound phrase token" and "constituent" refer to without an example.
*   What is the precise definition of an "elementary APT"? Is it a triplet of (target word, dependency path, context word)? The name is confusing and should be clarified.
*   Does "shifted PMI" refer to PMI as defined in Equation (3)? Please clarify.
*   The sentence "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts" is confusing. The connection between removing low-PMI features and the phrases appearing in a "small number of contexts" seems contradictory. Please rephrase this point.
*   In the phrase "APTs which have not been properly aligned," does "not properly aligned" simply mean "unaligned"?
*   The reasoning in lines 558-563 is difficult to follow. Why is the potential for overlap considered "considerable" specifically for the NMOD relation between components?
*   A critical point in lines 575-580 is puzzling. After emphasizing the value of higher-order dependency features, the final similarity calculation seems to rely only on first-order features. Footnote 3 attempts to justify this by claiming second-order paths are unreliable, but the explanation itself is unclear. Could the authors provide a more detailed justification for this seemingly significant simplification at the most crucial step of the method?

**Section 6 (Results)**
*   The claim that PPMI smoothing (α = 0.75) "generally has a small positive effect" is not immediately obvious from Table 3. Could the authors elaborate on this interpretation?
*   The hybrid model (Equations 8 and 9) introduces hyperparameters *h* and *q*. What were the optimal values used to generate the results? This information is essential for understanding the contribution of the aligned vs. unaligned vectors in the final, slightly improved model.
*   For clarity, the caption for Table 4 should specify which combination function (e.g., 'add') was used. Furthermore, for a more complete comparison, could the results using word2vec vectors for the observed compound phrases also be included?
*   The intuition behind the FREQ baseline is unclear. Why would a more frequent compound be expected to be more compositional? The strong performance of this baseline might suggest a frequency-related bias in the dataset, which warrants discussion.
