### **General Comments**

This paper reports on experiments predicting the compositionality of English compounds. The work uses a pre-existing dataset of 90 compounds, each rated on a 1-5 compositionality scale by an unspecified number of human judges. The general experimental paradigm involves computing the cosine similarity between the vector for a compound (treated as a single token) and a vector derived from the composition of its components. The primary evaluation metric is the Spearman correlation between these computed similarities and the human judgments.

The experiments compare different vector representations (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors) and, for the count vectors, explore the use of "aligned" vectors for the dependent component of the compound. The alignment, proposed in a previously suppressed reference by the authors, attempts to capture a syntactic shift from the dependent to the head.

The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings. The use of aligned vectors alone performs worse than non-modified vectors, while a highly-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields a slight improvement.

Regarding the paper's presentation, the introduction is well-written, but other sections, notably 5.1, are difficult to follow despite the relative simplicity of the underlying concepts. The clarity could be significantly improved by incorporating running examples to guide the reader.

Substantively, I have several concerns:

1.  **Novelty:** The main innovation over Reddy et al. appears to be the use of aligned vectors. However, the paper states these were introduced in a 'suppressed reference' by the same authors, which diminishes the novelty of the current work.
2.  **Dataset:** The dataset is small and insufficiently described. In particular, information about the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components is missing; this is a critical detail, as frequency is likely to impact the results.
3.  **Fragility of Findings:** Given that the improvements from using aligned vectors are marginal, are demonstrated on a small dataset, and the selection criteria for this dataset are unclear, the paper's findings appear fragile.

### **Detailed Comments and Questions**

**Section 3**
*   The necessity for the new term "packed anchored tree" (PAT) is unclear. This method appears to be a straightforward extraction of dependency paths between two lexical items, a direct extension of traditional syntactic distributional representations (e.g., Lin, 1998), which typically use paths of length one or two, sometimes with collapsed prepositions.
*   Could the authors clarify why this structure is called a "tree"? Additionally, the term "elementary APTs," introduced in Section 5.1, requires a clear definition.
*   Regarding Table 2: Was it omitted that features of order greater than three are discarded? This might explain why, for instance, `NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ` is absent from the bottom-left cell. Alternatively, is this absence due to the "elimination of some incompatible types" mentioned by the authors? If so, this process is unclear and should be illustrated with an example.

**Section 4**
*   The Reddy et al. dataset is central to this work, yet its selection process is not described. It is crucial to detail how the 90 compounds were chosen. What are the frequency ranges for the compounds and their components? The results are likely to be sensitive to these frequency distributions.
*   For a given compound, how many human judgments were collected? Are there many instances of different compounds receiving the same final compositionality score? The presence of many ties could be problematic for the Spearman correlation calculation.
*   The paper uses the term "constituent" to refer to a component of the N-N sequence. I suggest using "component" instead to avoid ambiguity, as "constituent" also commonly refers to a syntactic phrase.
*   The paper states the intuition that "if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences." This intuition holds more strongly when the constituent is the head of the phrase. For a dependent component, it is far less certain (e.g., the distribution of "spelling bee" is very different from that of "spelling").

**Section 5**
*   The sentence "Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context" is difficult to parse. Please provide a running example to clarify the different vector representations being discussed. Does "compound phrase token" refer to the vector for the compound treated as a single unit?

**Section 5.1**
*   It appears that "elementary APTs" are triplets of the form (target word w, dependency path r, context word w'). If this is correct, the name remains confusing and could be clarified.
*   Please clarify if "shifted PMI" refers to the PMI definition in Equation (3).
*   The sentence "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts" is contradictory. It states that removing features with low PMI (that "go with lots of things") results in phrases that seem to have been observed in a "very small number" of contexts. Please clarify this reasoning. Does "these phrases" refer back to the "things" with which the features co-occur?
*   What is meant by "not properly aligned" (line 558)? Does this simply mean "unaligned"?
*   The reasoning in lines 558-563 is unclear. Why is the potential feature overlap expected to be "considerable" specifically when the two components are in an NMOD relation?
*   The methodology described in lines 575-580 is puzzling. The paper builds its representations on higher-order dependency features, but at the critical step of measuring the similarity between the composed and observed vectors, it reverts to using only first-order features. This seems inconsistent.
*   Footnote 3 is intended to justify this choice, but its explanation of why second-order paths in the composed representations are "not reliable" is not convincing. Please elaborate on this point.

**Section 6**
*   The claim that "Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect" is not clearly supported by the results in Table 3.
*   To properly assess the contribution of the hybrid model, the optimal values for the hyperparameters *h* and *q* (Equations 8 and 9) should be reported. These values are needed to understand the degree of "hybridity" that leads to the slight gains over the unaligned model.
*   The results in Table 4 appear to be for the additive composition function. This should be explicitly stated in the caption or legend.
*   For a more complete comparison, could the authors also report the results of using word2vec vectors for the compound phrases themselves (i.e., the observed vector)?
*   The intuition behind the frequency (FREQ) baseline is unclear. Why would a more frequent compound be expected to be more compositional? This correlation might suggest a potential bias in the dataset.
