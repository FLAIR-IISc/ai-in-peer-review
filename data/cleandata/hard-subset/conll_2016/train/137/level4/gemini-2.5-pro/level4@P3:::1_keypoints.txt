- Paper investigates predicting compositionality of English noun compounds by comparing observed compound vectors to composed vectors, evaluating word2vec vs. dependency-based APTs and finding a tuned hybrid APT model best.  
- Contribution: comprehensive empirical comparison showing syntactically-informed, sparse (APT) vectors can outperform neural embeddings on compositionality detection.  
- Contribution: systematic exploration of composition operations, weighting schemes, and alignment strategies (aligned, unaligned, hybrid), yielding a new state-of-the-art on Reddy et al. (2011).  
- Strength: thorough empirical evaluation across vector types, parameters, and composition functions, providing convincing evidence.  
- Strength: strong results and baselines—hybrid APT achieves ρ=0.79 vs. prior ρ=0.71 and tuned word2vec ρ=0.74, with multiple baselines included.  
- Strength: clear motivation and well-written background situating compositionality detection as a testbed for compositional models.  
- Weakness: limited novelty—APT/alignment appears based on the authors’ prior work and may rebrand established dependency-path features.  
- Weakness: fragility due to small, under-specified dataset (90 compounds), marginal gains vs. simpler models, and potential dataset bias (high FREQ baseline at ρ=0.63).  
- Weakness: unclear methodological details—restricting final similarity to first-order features and other steps (Section 5.1) lack adequate justification or examples.  
- Question: please justify using only first-order dependency features for final similarity and why composed higher-order features are considered unreliable.  
- Question: provide frequency statistics for the 90 compounds in ukWaC to assess potential bias related to the strong FREQ baseline.  
- Question: report the optimal weighting parameters h and q for the best hybrid model to clarify head vs. modifier and aligned vs. unaligned contributions.  
- Question: clarify how "Anchored Packed Trees" fundamentally differ from standard typed dependency-path feature models in prior literature.
