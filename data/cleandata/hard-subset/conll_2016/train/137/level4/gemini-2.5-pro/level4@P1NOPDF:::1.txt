General comments
=============================
This paper presents experiments on predicting the compositionality of English noun compounds. The authors use an existing dataset of 90 compounds, each rated for compositionality on a scale of 1 to 5 by an unspecified number of human judges. The experimental setup involves computing the cosine similarity between the vector representation of the compound (treated as a single token) and a composed vector derived from its components. The evaluation metric is the Spearman correlation between these computed similarities and the human judgments.

The experiments compare two types of vector representations: neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors. For the count vectors, the authors further investigate the use of plain versus "aligned" vectors for the dependent component of the compound. This alignment, proposed in a previous work by the authors (cited as a suppressed reference), aims to capture the syntactic shift from the dependent to the head.

The results show that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings. The alignment method on its own performs worse than using unmodified vectors, although a highly-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields a slight improvement.

Regarding the paper's presentation, the introduction is well-written, but other sections (e.g., Section 5.1) are difficult to follow, despite the underlying concepts being relatively straightforward. The inclusion of running examples would significantly improve readability.

On a more substantive level, I have several concerns:
- The primary innovation over Reddy et al. appears to be the use of aligned vectors. However, this method was introduced by the authors in a previous publication (the "suppressed reference"), which diminishes the novelty of the current work.
- The dataset is small and insufficiently described. In particular, information about the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components is missing. This is a critical omission, as frequency is likely to have a significant impact on the results.
- Given that the improvements from using aligned vectors are marginal, the dataset is small, and the compound selection criteria are unclear, I find the paper's conclusions to be fragile.

More detailed comments/questions
================================
**Section 3**
- The motivation for introducing the new term "packed anchored tree" (PAT) is unclear. The concept appears to be a straightforward extraction of paths between two lexical items in a dependency tree. This seems to be a simple extension of traditional syntactic distributional representations (e.g., Lin, 1998), which typically use paths of length one or two, sometimes with collapsed prepositions.
- Furthermore, the justification for calling this structure a "tree" is not provided. The term "elementary APTs" (Section 5.1) also requires clarification.

**Table 2**
- In Table 2, is the feature `NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ` absent from the bottom-left cell because you discard features of order higher than 3? If so, this should be stated explicitly. Alternatively, is its absence due to the "elimination of some incompatible types" you mention? This point was unclear, and an example of an incompatible type would be helpful.

**Section 4**
- Given the centrality of the Reddy et al. dataset to this work, it is essential to describe how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency ranges for the compounds and their components? The results are likely to vary significantly across different frequency bands.
- How many judgments were collected for each compound? Are there many ties in the final compositionality scores? Ties can be problematic when computing a rank-based metric like Spearman correlation.
- The term "constituent" is used to refer to a component of the N-N compound. I would suggest using "component" instead, as "constituent" has a broader meaning in syntax (i.e., a phrase or syntagm) and could cause confusion.
- Regarding the intuition that "if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences": this holds true primarily when the component is the head of the compound. For the dependent, it is much less certain (e.g., "spelling bee" does not share the distribution of "spelling").

**Section 5**
- The sentence beginning "Note that the elementary representation..." is difficult to parse. A concrete example would greatly help the reader understand which objects are being discussed. For instance, does "compound phrase token" refer to the vector for the compound treated as a single unit (e.g., `spelling_bee`)?

**Section 5.1**
- Am I correct in assuming that "elementary APTs" are triplets of the form (target word w, dependency path r, context word w')? If so, the name is confusing and could be clarified.
- Please clarify if "shifted PMI" refers to the PMI calculation defined in Equation (3).
- The sentence "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things..." is confusing. It appears contradictory: removing features with low PMI (which occur with many items) is said to result in phrases that appear in a *small* number of contexts. Do "these phrases" refer to the "things"? Please rephrase and clarify this point.
- What is meant by APTs that have "not been properly aligned"? Does this simply mean unaligned APTs, or is there a distinction between improper and proper alignment?
- The reasoning in lines 558-563 is unclear. Why would the potential overlap between the feature sets of the components be "considerable" specifically when they are in an NMOD relation?
- The paragraph on lines 575-580 is puzzling. The paper emphasizes the use of higher-order dependency features, yet at the critical step of measuring the similarity between the composed and observed compound vectors, only first-order features are used. This seems inconsistent. Footnote 3 is meant to justify this, but its explanation of why second-order paths in the composed representations are "not reliable" is not convincing. Please elaborate on this point.

**Section 6**
- The claim that smoothing PPMI with α = 0.75 "generally has a small positive effect" is not clearly supported by the results in Table 3. Could you elaborate?
- What were the optimal values for the hyperparameters *h* and *q* (Equations 8 and 9)? These values are crucial for understanding how much the "hybrid" model's slight performance gain owes to tuning.
- The results in Table 4 appear to be for the additive combination model. It would be helpful to specify this in the table's caption.
- For a more complete comparison, could you also report the results of using pre-trained word2vec vectors for the compound phrases themselves (i.e., the "observed" vectors)?
- The intuition behind the frequency (FREQ) baseline is unclear. Why would a more frequent compound be expected to be more compositional? This correlation, if it holds, might suggest a frequency bias in the dataset.
