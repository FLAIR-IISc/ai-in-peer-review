- Paper studies predicting English compound compositionality using cosine similarity between compound vectors and composed component vectors, evaluated by Spearman rank correlation.
- Uses a small pre-existing dataset of 90 compounds rated 1–5, but number of annotators and selection criteria are unspecified.
- Experiments compare neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors, and standard vs. “aligned” count vectors for dependents.
- Results: syntactic-context count vectors beat neural embeddings; aligned vectors alone perform worse than unaligned; a tuned hybrid of aligned+unaligned gives slight improvement.
- Intro is well-written, but several sections (notably 5.1) are hard to follow; running examples would improve readability.
- Novelty concern: aligned vectors are a previously published method by the authors, calling the technical contribution into question.
- Dataset concerns: sample is small and inadequately described—missing frequency ranges, selection criteria, and likely sensitivity to frequency bands.
- Fragility of findings: marginal performance gains on a small, poorly characterized dataset make conclusions unstable.
- Section 3: motivation for the new term “packed anchored tree (PAT)” is unclear and seems to repurpose standard dependency-path extraction.
- Section 3: “elementary APTs” is used without a clear definition and must be defined earlier.
- Section 3/Table 2: absence of features of order >3 is unexplained—unclear whether due to explicit feature-order cap or elimination of incompatible types.
- Section 4: needs a fuller description of the Reddy et al. dataset—how the 90 compounds were chosen and frequency ranges for compounds and components.
- Section 4: unspecified number of human judgments per compound and unclear distribution of compositionality scores; ties could affect Spearman’s interpretation.
- Section 4: term “constituent” is ambiguous—recommend using “component” to avoid syntactic confusion.
- Section 4: the intuition linking literal use of a component to shared co-occurrences is oversimplified and fails for dependents (e.g., “spelling” in “spelling bee”).
- Section 5: several sentences are hard to parse; the notion of “compound phrase token” and which vector representations are meant require concrete running examples.
- Section 5.1: clarify whether “elementary APTs” are triplets (target, dependency path, context) and whether “shifted PMI” equals the PMI in Eq. (3).
- Section 5.1: one sentence about removing low-PMI features is confusing/contradictory and needs rephrasing for clarity.
- Section 5.1: define “APTs which have not been properly aligned” (i.e., unaligned APTs?) and explain why feature overlap would be “considerable” for NMOD relations.
- Section 5.1/method: puzzling why similarity is measured only on first-order features when representations use higher-order dependency features; footnote justification is unconvincing and needs clearer rationale.
- Section 6: claim that PPMI smoothing with α=0.75 helps is not clearly supported by Table 3 and needs stronger justification.
- Section 6: report optimal hyperparameters h and q for the hybrid model to show how much hybridity produced the gains.
- Section 6/Table 4: legend should state results use the `add` combination function for clarity.
- Section 6: provide results using word2vec vectors for the observed compound phrases themselves as a baseline.
- Section 6: the FREQ baseline’s intuition is unclear—why would higher-frequency compounds be more compositional—and its strong performance may indicate dataset bias.
