**General Comments**

The paper presents a set of experiments on predicting the compositionality of English noun-noun compounds. The work utilizes a pre-existing dataset of 90 compounds, each rated on a 1-5 compositionality scale by an unspecified number of annotators. The core experimental method involves computing the cosine similarity between a vector representing the compound as a single unit and a vector derived from the composition of its components' vectors. The system's output is evaluated against the human judgments using Spearman correlation.

The experiments explore two main variables:
1.  The type of vector representation: neural word embeddings versus count-based vectors derived from syntactic contexts.
2.  For the count-based vectors, the use of either standard vectors or "aligned" vectors for the modifier component. This alignment technique, which aims to model the semantic shift from the modifier to the head, was introduced by the authors in a previous publication (cited as a suppressed reference).

The primary findings are that syntactic count-based vectors outperform neural embeddings, and that using aligned vectors alone is less effective than using the original, unaligned vectors. However, a fine-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields a slight improvement over the unaligned baseline.

In terms of presentation, the introduction is well-written and provides a clear overview. However, other sections, such as 5.1, are difficult to follow despite the relative simplicity of the underlying concepts. The clarity of these sections could be significantly improved by incorporating running examples.

In terms of substance, I have several concerns:
-   **Novelty:** The main technical contribution appears to be the application of aligned vectors to this task. However, this method has been published previously by the authors. The novelty of the current work should be articulated more clearly.
-   **Dataset:** The dataset is small (N=90), and its properties are not sufficiently described. Information regarding the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components is crucial, as these factors are likely to influence the results.
-   **Robustness of Findings:** Given that the reported improvements from using aligned vectors are marginal and are demonstrated on a small, under-specified dataset, the conclusions of the paper appear somewhat fragile.

**Detailed Comments and Questions**

**Section 3: Packed Anchored Trees (PATs)**
-   The motivation for introducing the new term "packed anchored tree" is unclear. The method appears to be a standard extraction of paths between two words in a dependency tree, which is a direct extension of common practice in syntactic distributional models (e.g., Lin, 1998) that use paths of length one or two. Furthermore, the rationale for calling this structure a "tree" is not obvious.
-   Could you clarify the definition of "elementary APTs" mentioned in Section 5.1?
-   **Table 2:** It seems that dependency paths of order higher than three have been discarded (e.g., `NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ` does not appear in the bottom-left cell). If this is the case, it should be explicitly stated. Additionally, the text mentions eliminating "incompatible types." An example would be helpful to clarify what this means.

**Section 4: Dataset and Experimental Setup**
-   Given the centrality of the Reddy et al. dataset to this work, a more detailed description is necessary. How were the 90 compounds selected? What are the frequency ranges for the compounds and their components? Results could vary significantly across different frequency bands.
-   How many human judgments were collected for each compound? It would be useful to know the distribution of the final compositionality scores. Were there many compounds with tied scores, and if so, how might this affect the Spearman correlation calculation?
-   The paper uses the term "constituent" to refer to a component of the N-N compound. I would suggest using "component" to avoid confusion with the broader syntactic sense of "constituent" (i.e., a phrase).
-   The intuition that "if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences" is not universally true. It holds for the head of the compound, but much less so for the modifier (e.g., the distribution of "spelling bee" is very different from that of "spelling").

**Section 5: Compositional Models**
-   The sentence "Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context" is difficult to parse. A running example would greatly help the reader understand what is meant by "elementary representation," "contextual features," and "compound phrase token" (which I assume refers to the vector for the unanalyzed compound).

**Section 5.1: Vector Alignment**
-   The term "elementary APTs" is confusing. Does it refer to a triplet of (target word *w*, dependency path *r*, context word *w'*)?
-   Please clarify whether "shifted PMI" refers to the PMI definition in Equation (3).
-   The sentence "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts" seems contradictory. Please rephrase this to clarify the intended meaning.
-   What is meant by APTs that "have not been properly aligned"? Does this simply mean unaligned APTs?
-   The reasoning in paragraph 558-563 is difficult to follow. Why is the potential overlap of APTs considered particularly high for the NMOD relation between the two components?
-   A central methodological choice that requires further justification is the use of only first-order features when calculating the similarity between the composed and observed phrasal vectors (lines 575-580). The paper builds its representations on higher-order dependency features, so this sudden restriction to first-order features at the most critical step is puzzling. The justification in Footnote 3 is not sufficiently clear; could the authors elaborate on why second-order paths in the composed representations are considered unreliable?

**Section 6: Results**
-   The claim that smoothing PPMI with α = 0.75 "generally has a small positive effect" is not immediately obvious from Table 3. Could the authors highlight the specific comparisons that support this?
-   The optimal values for the hyperparameters *h* and *q* in Equations (8) and (9) are not reported. These are crucial for understanding the contribution of the hybrid model and for reproducibility.
-   The caption for Table 4 should specify that the results shown are for the `add` combination function.
-   For a more complete comparison, could the authors provide the results for a baseline using word2vec vectors for the compound phrases themselves, rather than just for the components?
-   The intuition behind the FREQ baseline is unclear. Why would more frequent compounds necessarily be more compositional? The strong performance of this baseline might suggest a frequency bias in the dataset, which warrants discussion.
