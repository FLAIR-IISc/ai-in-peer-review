- Paper studies noun-compound compositionality by comparing whole-compound vectors to vectors composed from constituents, using word2vec vs syntax-based APT representations with unaligned, aligned, and hybrid composition.
- Experiments on the Reddy et al. (2011) 90-compound dataset show syntax-based APTs outperform word2vec, with a hybrid APT reaching a new state-of-the-art on that benchmark.

- Contribution: extensive empirical comparison between syntax-based (sparse, dependency-path) and embedding-based (dense) compositional models.
- Contribution: demonstrates a new SOTA on Reddy et al. (2011), arguing explicit syntactic features can beat neural embeddings for this task.

- Strength: thorough empirical evaluation exploring multiple representations, composition operations, and integration methods.
- Strength: strong and interesting result that syntax-informed, higher-order dependency features outperform a strong word2vec baseline.
- Strength: well-motivated problem and clear positioning in computational semantics literature.

- Weakness: limited novelty since APT framework was introduced previously by the authors, making this primarily an application paper.
- Weakness: fragility of claims due to tiny dataset (90 compounds), marginal gains between hybrid and simpler models, and a strong frequency baseline that suggests potential dataset bias.
- Weakness: unclear methodological descriptions (opaque terminology, lack of examples) and unexplained removal of higher-order features before final similarity computation, hurting reproducibility and intuition.

- Question: clarify the novelty relative to the prior (suppressed) APT paper and specify new contributions beyond applying APT to this task.
- Question: justify why higher-order features are discarded when computing final similarity and explain why composed higher-order paths are considered unreliable.
- Question: report the optimal weighting parameters (h and q) found in cross-validation to interpret head vs modifier and aligned vs unaligned contributions.
- Question: provide details on the Reddy et al. dataset construction and frequency distributions to assess potential frequency-compositionality bias.
