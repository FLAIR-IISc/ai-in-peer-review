### Summary of the paper

This paper investigates methods for predicting the degree of compositionality of English noun compounds. Using the established dataset from Reddy et al. (2011), the authors evaluate compositionality by measuring the cosine similarity between an observed vector for a compound (treated as a single token) and a composed vector derived from its constituent parts. The primary focus is a comparison between two types of word representations: standard neural word embeddings (word2vec) and high-dimensional, sparse vectors derived from dependency parse contexts (termed Anchored Packed Trees or APTs). The APT method involves composing vectors by "aligning" the feature space of the modifier to match that of the head. The results indicate that the dependency-based vectors outperform the neural embeddings, and that a fine-tuned hybrid model combining aligned and unaligned dependency vectors achieves the highest correlation with human judgments.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A comprehensive empirical comparison of dependency-based count vectors against neural embeddings for the task of compositionality detection.** The paper provides a strong, direct comparison, showing that syntactically-informed, sparse vectors can outperform dense embeddings on this fine-grained semantic task.
2.  **An extensive exploration of compositionality functions for dependency-based vectors.** The authors systematically test different composition operations (intersective vs. additive), feature weighting schemes (PPMI vs. smoothed PPMI), and vector alignment strategies (aligned, unaligned, and a novel hybrid model), leading to a new state-of-the-art result on the Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark.

### Strengths

1.  **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a robust set of experiments. It methodically compares different vector types (APT vs. word2vec), explores a variety of parameters for each (e.g., PPMI smoothing, embedding dimensions, subsampling thresholds), and tests multiple composition functions (aligned, unaligned, hybrid, weighted addition). This comprehensive approach provides convincing evidence for its conclusions.
2.  **Strong Results and Baselines:** The proposed hybrid APT model achieves a new state-of-the-art on this benchmark (Spearman's ρ = 0.79), outperforming the previous result from Reddy et al. (ρ = 0.71) as well as a strong, well-tuned baseline using word2vec embeddings (ρ = 0.74). The inclusion of multiple baselines (RAND, FREQ, REDDY) effectively contextualizes the significance of the results.
3.  **Clear Motivation and Background:** The introduction and related work sections are well-written, providing a clear context for the problem of non-compositionality and a solid overview of previous approaches to distributional composition. The motivation for using compositionality detection as a testbed for compositional models is well-argued.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Method:** The central technical concept, the alignment of dependency vectors via what the paper calls Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), appears to be based on previously published work by the authors (as indicated by the suppressed references). While its application to this specific task is new, the method itself is not. Furthermore, the term "Anchored Packed Trees" seems to be a new name for the established concept of using dependency paths of varying lengths as contextual features, which could cause confusion.
2.  **Fragility of Results due to Small and Under-specified Dataset:** The conclusions are drawn from experiments on a small dataset of only 90 noun compounds. The paper does not provide crucial details about this dataset, such as the frequency distribution of the compounds in the corpus or the original criteria for their selection. The performance gains of the best hybrid model over the simpler unaligned model are marginal (0.79 vs. 0.78). On such a small dataset, it is difficult to be confident that these minor improvements are statistically robust and would generalize to a wider range of phenomena. The high performance of the frequency baseline (FREQ, ρ=0.63) may also suggest a bias in the dataset.
3.  **Lack of Clarity in Critical Methodological Details:** Several key aspects of the methodology are difficult to follow. In particular, the decision to restrict the final similarity computation to only first-order dependency features (Section 5.1, footnote 3) is counter-intuitive, given that the model's novelty lies in using higher-order features for composition. The explanation provided—that higher-order paths in the composed representation are "not reliable"—is insufficient and requires a much clearer justification, as this is a critical step in the evaluation. Section 5.1 is generally dense and would benefit from a running example to clarify the different vector manipulation steps.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed justification for why only first-order dependency features were used for the final similarity calculation? Why are the composed higher-order features considered "unreliable" when the elementary vectors from which they are composed contain paths up to order 2? This seems to discard the very information that the alignment process is designed to leverage.
2.  Could you provide more details on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, specifically the frequency range of the 90 compounds in the ukWaC corpus? The strong performance of the FREQ baseline (correlation of 0.63) suggests a potential bias where more frequent compounds are rated as more compositional. Could you comment on this?
3.  What were the optimal values for the weighting parameters `h` and `q` in the best-performing hybrid model (Eq. 9)? This information is crucial for understanding the relative contributions of the head vs. modifier and the aligned vs. unaligned feature sets.
4.  The paper introduces the term "Anchored Packed Trees" (APTs). How does this concept fundamentally differ from the well-established practice of using typed dependency paths of varying lengths as features in distributional models? A clarification would help position the work with respect to prior literature.
