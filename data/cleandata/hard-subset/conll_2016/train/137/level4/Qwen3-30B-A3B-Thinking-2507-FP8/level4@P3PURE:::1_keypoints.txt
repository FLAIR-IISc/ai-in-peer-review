- Paper predicts compositionality of 90 English compounds using cosine similarity between compound vectors and composed component vectors, evaluated by Spearman correlation with human 1–5 ratings.
- Experiments compare neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors and test an "alignment" transform for dependents; syntactic-context counts outperform embeddings.
- Aligned vectors alone perform worse than unmodified vectors; a highly tuned hybrid of aligned and unaligned yields only marginal improvements.
- Introduction is clear, but methodological sections (especially 5.1) are hard to follow and would benefit from running examples.
- Main claimed innovation (aligned vectors) was previously published by the authors in a suppressed reference, undermining novelty.
- Dataset is small (90 compounds), poorly characterized, and selection criteria/frequency ranges for compounds and components are missing, making results fragile.
- Section 3: the term "packed anchored tree" (PAT) seems unnecessary and should be justified; "elementary APTs" need definition.
- Table 2: clarify why features beyond order 3 are excluded and whether exclusions are due to order limits or incompatible types, with examples.
- Section 4: report compound/component frequency ranges, number of human judgments per compound, whether tied compositionality scores occur, and avoid the ambiguous term "constituent" (use "component").
- Section 5: the intuition about literal use of constituents is only valid for heads; provide running examples and define "compound phrase token" and the contextual feature scope.
- Section 5.1: clarify if elementary APTs are triplets (w, r, w'), confirm "shifted PMI" refers to Eq. (3), and resolve contradictory wording about removing low-PMI features.
- Section 5.1: define "not properly aligned," explain why unaligned APT overlap is expected for NMOD relations, and justify why similarity uses only first-order features despite claiming higher-order features.
- Section 5.1: Note 3’s rationale for discarding second-order paths in composed representations is unclear and needs explicit justification.
- Section 6: the claim that PPMI smoothing with α=0.75 helps is not clearly supported by Table 3 and needs clarification.
- Section 6: report optimal hyperparameters h and q (Eqns 8–9) to assess the contribution of "hybridity" to marginal gains.
- Section 6/Table 4: clarify that reported results use the "add" combination in the legend.
- Section 6: provide word2vec results for compound phrases to enable direct comparison with count-based methods.
- Section 6: justify the FREQ baseline—explain why more frequent compounds would be expected to be more compositional and address potential dataset bias.
