- Goal: predicts compositionality of English compound nouns using vector space models and cosine similarity against composed component vectors.

- Contribution 1: Syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings for compositionality detection.

- Contribution 2: A hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned vector composition gives marginal improvement.

- Contribution 3: Detailed analysis of how dependency structure affects compositionality prediction.

- Strength: Clear evaluation framework that isolates compositionality detection from phrase similarity, avoiding prior confounds.

- Strength: Thorough experimental design comparing multiple composition methods and vector types with 3-fold cross-validation and parameter analysis.

- Strength: Empirical improvement over prior work (best hybrid APT ρ=0.79 vs. 0.71), statistically significant (p<0.05).

- Weakness: Claimed vector-alignment innovation appears to reuse the authors' prior (suppressed) work, weakening novelty.

- Weakness: Dataset is very small (90 compounds) and lacks frequency/selection details, limiting generalizability and raising concerns about the modest ρ improvements.

- Weakness: Inconsistent evaluation—composition uses higher-order dependency paths, but final similarity only uses first-order features without sufficient justification.

- Weakness: FREQ baseline outperforms RAND without explanation, suggesting possible dataset bias (frequent compounds may be more compositional).

- Question 1: Clarify the specific contribution relative to the referenced "suppressed" prior work and why the current alignment is a meaningful advancement.

- Question 2: Provide compound and component frequency ranges and correlations with compositionality scores to assess robustness.

- Question 3: Explain why second-order features are used in composition but deemed unreliable for final evaluation.

- Question 4: Justify why compound frequency predicts compositionality and whether this indicates dataset bias.

- Question 5: Report the exact optimal h and q values (reported range [0.3,0.5]) to clarify hybrid versus unaligned contributions.
