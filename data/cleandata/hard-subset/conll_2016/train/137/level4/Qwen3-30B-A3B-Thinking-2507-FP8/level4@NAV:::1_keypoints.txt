- Paper experiments predict English compound compositionality using a 90-compound dataset with human 1–5 rankings, but number of judges is unspecified.  
- Method: compute cosine between compound token vector and composition of component vectors, evaluate with Spearman correlation against human judgments.  
- Variations: neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors; for count vectors, test plain vs. "aligned" dependent vectors per a prior (suppressed) reference.  
- Results: syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings; aligned vectors alone underperform unmodified vectors; a highly tuned mix of aligned+unaligned gives only marginal improvement.  
- Intro is clear, but sections like 5.1 are hard to follow; reviewer recommends adding running examples for clarity.  
- Major concern: claimed innovation (aligned vectors) is derived from the authors’ earlier suppressed work, undermining novelty.  
- Major concern: dataset is small and poorly described (no frequencies for compounds/components), making findings fragile and possibly biased.  
- Clarify whether multiple compounds share identical compositionality scores and how many human judgments per item, since this affects Spearman correlation validity.  
- Terminology: “packed anchored tree” seems unnecessary—appears equivalent to standard dependency path extraction; define why it’s called a “tree” and define “elementary APTs.”  
- Table 2: explain why features with path order >3 are discarded and give an example of incompatible feature elimination.  
- Section 4: detail selection criteria for the 90 compounds, including frequency ranges of compounds and components.  
- Language: avoid “constituent” for compound parts; use “component”; correct intuition that distributional similarity mainly holds when the constituent is the head.  
- Section 5: provide a concrete running example to illustrate “elementary representation … will not contain contextual features associated with the compound phrase token.” Clarify “compound phrase token.”  
- Section 5.1: define “elementary APTs” (e.g., triplet form), specify whether “shifted PMI” refers to Eq. (3), and resolve the contradiction about removing low-PMI features increasing apparent informativeness.  
- Clarify “not properly aligned” (does it mean unaligned?) and explain why NMOD relations between components are expected to overlap.  
- Address the abrupt methodological shift from higher-order dependency features in modeling to first-order features when measuring similarity; Note 3’s rationale for discarding second-order paths is unclear.  
- Section 6: claim that smoothing PPMI with α=0.75 helps is not supported by Table 3—clarify evidence.  
- Specify optimal values for h and q (Eqs. 8–9) to evaluate hybridity’s contribution and clarify that Table 4 uses the “add” combination in its legend.  
- Include compound-phrase word2vec results and justify the FREQ baseline—explain why higher compound frequency would imply greater compositionality or whether this indicates dataset bias.
