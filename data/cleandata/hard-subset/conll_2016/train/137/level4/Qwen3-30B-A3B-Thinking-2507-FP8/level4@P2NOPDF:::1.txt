

General Comments  
=============================  
This paper investigates compositionality prediction for English noun compounds using vector-based similarity metrics. The authors evaluate cosine similarity between compound vectors (treated as single tokens) and compositional vectors of their components, correlating results with human judgments (ranked 1–5) on a 90-compound dataset. Key variations include: (1) neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors, and (2) for count vectors, comparing plain versus "aligned" vectors (which model head-dependent shifts, previously proposed by the authors). Results show count vectors outperform embeddings, plain vectors surpass aligned ones, and a tuned hybrid of aligned/unaligned vectors yields minimal improvement.  

The introduction is well-written, but sections like 5.1 suffer from poor clarity despite straightforward concepts. Concrete examples would significantly enhance readability.  

Substantive concerns outweigh the paper’s modest contributions:  
1. **Novelty**: The use of aligned vectors is not new, as the authors’ prior work (previously published) introduced this method. The claimed innovation lacks sufficient differentiation from Reddy et al.  
2. **Dataset limitations**: The 90-compound dataset is small and inadequately described. Critical details—such as frequency ranges of compounds/components, number of human judgments per compound, and score distribution—are missing. Given that frequency heavily influences compositional behavior (e.g., low-frequency compounds may resist prediction), these omissions undermine the findings. Marginal gains from aligned vectors on this fragile dataset further weaken conclusions.  

Detailed Comments  
=============================  
**Section 3**  
The term "packed anchored tree" appears unnecessary. It describes standard dependency path extraction between lexical items (e.g., paths beyond length-2, as in Lin 1998), yet is mislabeled as a "tree." Clarify "elementary APTs" (Section 5.1) and explain why this structure is novel.  

**Table 2**  
The omission of features like *NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ* likely stems from discarding paths longer than order 3. Explicitly state this in the caption. Additionally, the paper mentions eliminating "incompatible types" but provides no examples—this requires clarification.  

**Section 4**  
The Reddy et al. dataset is central, yet its selection criteria are undefined. Specify:  
- Frequency ranges for compounds and components (e.g., are low-frequency compounds overrepresented?).  
- Number of human judgments per compound (e.g., 1–5 judges) and whether ties in compositionality scores affect Spearman correlation.  
Use "component" instead of "constituent" (to avoid confusion with syntagmatic phrases). The intuition that "literal use implies shared co-occurrences" is misleading: it holds only if the component is the *head* (e.g., "spelling" in "spelling bee" shares distribution with "bee," not "spelling bee").  

**Section 5**  
Clarify the phrase "compound phrase token" (e.g., does it denote the merged compound? Provide a running example for the elementary representation statement: *"The constituent’s representation excludes contextual features exclusive to the compound phrase unless observed elsewhere."*).  

**Section 5.1**  
- Define "elementary APTs" explicitly (e.g., as triplets: target word, dependency path, and associated word).  
- Clarify "shifted PMI" (does it reference Equation 3?). The claim that "low positive PMI removes features co-occurring with many things" contradicts itself—rephrase to: *"Features with low positive PMI (indicating broad co-occurrence) are filtered, leaving only high-informativeness contexts."*  
- Explain "not properly aligned" (does it mean "unaligned"?) and justify why NMOD relations show significant APT overlap post-alignment (per paragraphs 558–563).  
- Address the critical inconsistency: Why do Sections 3–4 use higher-order dependency features, yet Section 5.1 relies *only* on first-order features for similarity calculation? Note 3’s explanation for discarding second-order paths is unclear.  

**Section 6**  
- The claim that "α = 0.75 smoothing has a small positive effect" (p. 663) is not evident from Table 3—support this assertion.  
- Specify optimal *h* and *q* values (Equations 8–9), as they determine the "hybridity" contribution to gains over unaligned vectors.  
- Confirm Table 4 uses the "add" combination (state this in the legend).  
- Include word2vec results for compound phrases to strengthen comparisons.  
- The FREQ baseline (higher frequency → higher compositionality) lacks justification. This may indicate dataset bias (e.g., frequent compounds are more predictable by chance).  

Overall, the paper’s core methodology is sound but its claims are overstated. The dataset’s small size and poor characterization, coupled with the non-novel use of aligned vectors, render the results fragile. Prior work should be acknowledged more transparently, and methodological gaps (especially regarding feature selection and dataset curation) must be resolved before the findings can be considered robust. The paper would benefit from concrete examples, clearer definitions, and a more cautious interpretation of the marginal improvements observed.
