- Dataset: 90 English noun compounds with human 0–5 compositionality judgments; evaluation via Spearman correlation between cosine similarities of composed and observed compound vectors.  
- Methods compared: syntactic-context count vectors (typed dependency paths) versus neural embeddings.  
- Contribution 1: Syntactic-context count vectors significantly outperform neural embeddings for compositionality detection.  
- Contribution 2: Unaligned vectors generally outperform aligned vectors for compositionality prediction.  
- Contribution 3: A hybrid of aligned and unaligned vectors yields marginal improvement.  
- Strength: Positions compositionality detection as a useful testbed for compositional modeling.  
- Strength: Empirical results consistently favor dependency-based count vectors; hybrid achieves highest correlation (0.79).  
- Strength: Detailed analysis explaining the importance of syntactic structure and dependency paths for predicting compound meanings.  
- Strength: Evaluation framework separates compositionality detection from phrase similarity, avoiding confounds.  
- Weakness: Novelty is marginal over Reddy et al. (2011) and relies on previously published alignment methods.  
- Weakness: Small dataset (90 compounds) with insufficient reporting of compound and component frequency ranges.  
- Weakness: Hybrid gains are tiny (0.01–0.02 correlation), raising concerns about fragility on larger/different data.  
- Weakness: Paper fails to explain selection criteria for the 90 compounds and the number of human judgments per compound.  
- Question: How were the 90 compounds selected and what are the frequency ranges of compounds and their components?  
- Question: How many human judgments per compound were collected and how were scores aggregated?  
- Question: Why use only first-order dependency features for evaluation when composition uses higher-order features, and why are higher-order paths in composed representations deemed unreliable?  
- Question: What is the relationship between compound frequency and compositionality scores, given FREQ outperforms RAND and may indicate dataset bias?
