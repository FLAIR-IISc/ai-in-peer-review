- Core idea (compositionality prediction with vector-based similarity) is plausible but undermined by methodological and presentation issues.  
- Claimed novelty (using "aligned" vectors) is unclear and relies on unpublished prior work; scholarly context and novelty must be clarified.  
- Dataset of 90 compounds is too small and poorly specified, threatening result reliability and generalizability.  
- No information on compound/component frequency ranges; results may be biased toward high- or low-frequency items.  
- Judgment protocol is underspecified: number of judges per compound, score distributions, and ties are not reported, affecting validity of Spearman correlations.  
- Reported improvements from novel methods are marginal and possibly statistically insignificant given the small dataset.  
- The term "packed anchored tree" (PAT) is redundant and appears to re-describe standard dependency path extraction; differences from established syntactic distributional methods are not justified.  
- "Elementary APTs" is not defined explicitly.  
- Compound selection procedure (random, frequency-stratified, etc.) is unspecified.  
- Terminology confusion: use "component" instead of ambiguous "constituent/phrase"; intuitions about co-occurrence sharing depend on head vs. dependent status.  
- Path-handling is unclear: rationale for discarding paths >3 and examples of incompatible path elimination are missing.  
- "Compound phrase token" is ambiguous; a running example (e.g., "bookshelf") is needed.  
- Alignment logic is vague: what "not properly aligned" or "little overlap" precisely means is not explained.  
- Inconsistency: higher-order dependency paths are used for vector construction but only first-order paths are used for similarity—this contradiction must be resolved.  
- Smoothing effect (α=0.75) is claimed but not quantified; reported gains (e.g., +0.02 Spearman) should be given.  
- Hybridity parameters (*h*, *q*) are not reported; need to show optimal values to assess contribution of aligned+unaligned combination.  
- Missing baselines: include compound-phrase Word2Vec results and justify or remove the FREQ baseline (frequency-compositionality intuition may reflect dataset bias).  
- Presentation is opaque in places (e.g., Section 5.1); more examples and clearer explanations are required.  
- Overall: contribution is overstated; results lack robustness and the paper should be rejected unless major revisions addressing dataset, novelty, parameter reporting, path-inconsistency, and clearer exposition are made.
