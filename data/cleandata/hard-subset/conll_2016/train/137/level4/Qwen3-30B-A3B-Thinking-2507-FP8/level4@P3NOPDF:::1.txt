

**Review for CONLL_2016**  

This paper investigates compositionality prediction for English noun compounds using vector-based similarity measures. While the core idea is plausible, significant methodological and presentation issues undermine the contribution. The work builds on Reddy et al.’s dataset of 90 compounds (ranked 1–5 by unspecified judges) but lacks critical details about compound selection (e.g., frequency ranges of compounds/components) and judgment consistency (e.g., number of judges per compound, score distribution). The small, poorly characterized dataset—combined with marginal improvements from novel methods—renders the findings fragile.  

**Major Concerns**  
1. **Innovation and Prior Work**: The claimed novelty (using "aligned" vectors for dependent components) is based on previously unpublished work by the authors. This constitutes a significant gap in scholarly context. The paper must clarify how this aligns with Reddy et al. and justify its novelty.  
2. **Dataset Limitations**: The 90-compound dataset is underspecified. Without frequency data for compounds/components, it is unclear whether results generalize beyond low-frequency items (where distributional models often fail). High-frequency compounds may bias compositionality rankings (e.g., "butterfly" is frequent *and* compositional), yet the paper dismisses this with no evidence.  
3. **Marginal Results**: Syntactic-context vectors outperform embeddings, but the "aligned vectors" approach (supposedly novel) underperforms plain vectors. The slight improvement from *combining* aligned/unaligned vectors (Table 4) is statistically insignificant given the small dataset. This weakens the core claim that alignment enhances compositionality prediction.  

**Technical and Presentation Issues**  
*Section 3*: The term "packed anchored tree" (PAT) is redundant. It describes standard dependency path extraction (e.g., paths of length ≤3 between words), not a new structure. The paper should clarify why PATs differ from established syntactic distributional methods (e.g., Lin 1998). Define "elementary APTs" explicitly.  

*Section 4*: Critical gaps in dataset description:  
- How were compounds selected? (e.g., random sample, frequency-stratified?)  
- How many judges rated each compound? Are scores tied (e.g., multiple compounds at rank 3)? This affects Spearman correlation validity.  
- Clarify "constituent" (avoid "phrase" ambiguity; use "component" for NN sequence parts). The intuition that "constituents share co-occurrences" only holds if the constituent is the *head* (e.g., "spelling" in "spelling bee" ≠ "bee"), not the dependent.  

*Section 5*:  
- **Table 2**: Why are paths like *NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ* absent? The text claims paths >3 are discarded, but this should be explicitly stated. Provide an example of incompatible path elimination.  
- **"Compound phrase token"**: Clarify if this refers to the merged compound (e.g., "bookshelf" as a single token). A running example would help (e.g., "For 'bookshelf', the compound token is 'bookshelf', while 'book' is the constituent").  
- **Alignment logic**: The claim that "unaligned APTs have little overlap" (p. 558) is vague. What does "not properly aligned" mean? (e.g., not aligned *at all*?) Why would NMOD relations show high overlap without alignment?  
- **Critical inconsistency**: The paper uses higher-order dependency paths (e.g., paths of length 3) for vector construction (Section 5), yet relies *only* on first-order paths for similarity computation (Section 5.1). Note 3 cites unreliability of second-order paths, but this contradiction needs resolution.  

*Section 6*:  
- **Smoothing (α=0.75)**: The "small positive effect" (p. 663) is not evident from Table 3. Quantify the improvement (e.g., +0.02 Spearman).  
- **Hybridity parameters**: Report optimal *h* and *q* (Equations 8–9) to assess how much "hybridity" (aligned + unaligned vectors) drives gains over unaligned baselines.  
- **Word2Vec baseline**: Include compound-phrase word2vec results for comparison (Table 4 only shows syntactic-context vectors).  
- **FREQ baseline**: The intuition that frequent compounds are compositional is unsupported. This may reflect dataset bias (e.g., frequent compounds like "toothpaste" are more likely compositional). Justify this baseline or remove it.  

**Overall Assessment**  
The paper’s core method is sound but its contribution is overstated. The small, underspecified dataset, marginal results, and reliance on unpublished work limit impact. The presentation (e.g., Section 5.1) is opaque without examples. To strengthen the paper, the authors must:  
1. Specify compound selection criteria, frequency ranges, and judgment protocols.  
2. Clarify the novelty of "aligned vectors" and cite prior work properly.  
3. Resolve the path-order inconsistency (Section 5) and provide examples for key terms (e.g., APTs, "compound phrase token").  
4. Report critical parameters (*h*, *q*) and add word2vec results.  

Without these revisions, the paper’s findings lack robustness. The results could easily reverse with a larger or better-specified dataset. While the topic is relevant, the current work does not advance the state of the art sufficiently for CONLL. **Recommendation: Reject (with major revisions if resubmitted)**.
