

General Comments  
=============================  
This paper investigates predicting compositionality levels of English noun compounds using vector-based similarity measures. The authors employ a previously established dataset of 90 compounds with human-rated compositionality scores (1–5), though the number of judges remains unspecified. Each experiment computes cosine similarity between the compound vector (treated as a single token) and a composition of its component vectors, evaluated via Spearman correlation with human judgments. Key variations include: (1) neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors, and (2) for count vectors, whether to use plain or "aligned" vectors for the dependent component (where alignment shifts the dependent toward the head, as proposed in the authors’ prior work). Results show syntactic-context vectors outperform embeddings, aligned vectors alone underperform relative to unmodified vectors, and a carefully tuned combination of aligned/unaligned vectors yields marginal gains.  

The introduction is well-written, but sections like 5.1 lack clarity despite straightforward concepts. Adding running examples would significantly improve readability.  

Substantive concerns center on novelty and robustness:  
- The key innovation (aligned vectors) is derived from the authors’ own previously published work (cited as "suppressed reference"), raising questions about novelty.  
- The dataset is small (90 compounds) and insufficiently described. Critical gaps include frequency ranges for compounds/components (which likely influence results) and details on compound selection. Given the marginal gains from aligned vectors and unclear selection criteria, the findings appear fragile.  

Detailed Comments  
================================  
**Section 3**  
The term "packed anchored tree" (PAT) is redundant. It describes a standard dependency path extraction between lexical items—essentially extending traditional syntactic distributional methods (e.g., Lin 1998, which uses paths of length 1–2 with collapsed prepositions). Why is it termed a "tree"? Clarify "elementary APTs" (Section 5.1).  

**Table 2**  
The table omits mention of discarding features beyond order 3 (e.g., why NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ is absent from the leftmost bottom cell). Is this due to incompatible feature elimination? An example of such elimination would clarify.  

**Section 4**  
The Reddy et al. dataset is central but lacks methodological detail. Explain:  
- How the 90 compounds were selected (e.g., frequency ranges of compounds/components).  
- How many judgments per compound were provided, and whether compounds share identical compositionality scores (which could skew Spearman correlation).  
Avoid "constituent" for compound components; use "component" instead (to prevent confusion with syntagmatic phrases). The intuition that "a constituent used literally within a phrase shares co-occurrences with the compound" holds only if the constituent is the head (e.g., "spelling" in "spelling bee" is the head; "bee" is not).  

**Section 5**  
Clarify the phrase: "the elementary representation for the constituent... will not contain contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent elsewhere." Provide a running example. Define "compound phrase token" (does it refer to the merged compound token?).  

**Section 5.1**  
- "Elementary APTs" is confusing. If these are triplets (target word + dependency path + other word), clarify the definition.  
- Specify whether "shifted PMI" refers to Equation (3).  
- The sentence "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts" is contradictory. "These phrases" and "things" are ambiguous.  
- "Not properly aligned" is unclear (does it mean "unaligned"?) and requires definition.  
- The claim about "considerable overlap" for NMOD relations (lines 558–563) needs justification.  
- The sudden shift from higher-order dependency features (used throughout) to first-order features for similarity measurement (lines 575–580) is unexplained. Note 3’s rationale for discarding second-order paths in composed representations is unclear—please clarify.  

**Section 6**  
- The statement that "smoothing PPMI with α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect" (line 663) is not evident from Table 3.  
- Report optimal values for *h* and *q* (Equations 8–9), as they are critical to assessing the "hybridity" driving marginal gains over unaligned vectors.  
- Specify in Table 4’s legend whether results use the "add" combination (as implied).  
- Include word2vec results for compound phrases to strengthen comparisons.  
- The FREQ baseline’s intuition (frequent compounds are compositional) seems questionable; this may reflect dataset bias. Clarify.
