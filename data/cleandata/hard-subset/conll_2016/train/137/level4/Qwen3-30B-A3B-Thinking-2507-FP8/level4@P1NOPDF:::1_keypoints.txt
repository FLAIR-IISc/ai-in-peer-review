- Paper predicts compositionality of English noun compounds by comparing compound vectors to compositions of component vectors using cosine similarity and Spearman correlation on a 90-compound dataset.  
- Main results: syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings; aligned vectors alone underperform unmodified vectors; a tuned hybrid of aligned/unaligned vectors gives only marginal gains.  
- Introduction is clear, but parts like Section 5.1 are unclear; running examples would improve readability.  
- Novelty concern: the key "aligned vectors" idea appears inherited from the authors’ prior work, raising questions about originality.  
- Dataset concerns: 90 compounds is small and underspecified; selection criteria and frequency ranges for compounds/components are not reported, making results fragile.  
- Missing annotation detail: number of human judgments per compound is unspecified and potential identical scores across compounds (which would affect Spearman) are not reported.  
- Terminology issue: use "component" instead of "constituent" to avoid confusion with syntagmatic phrases.  
- The intuition that a component’s literal use shares co-occurrences with the compound only holds when the component is the head; this caveat is not acknowledged.  
- Section 3: the term "packed anchored tree (PAT)" is redundant/misleading; clarify why it’s called a "tree" and define "elementary APTs."  
- Table 2: omission of features beyond order 3 is unexplained—explain if/elimination of incompatible features and give an example.  
- Section 5: clarify statements about when constituent representations contain contextual features of the compound and define "compound phrase token"; provide a running example.  
- Section 5.1: define "elementary APTs" (are they triplets?), explicitly state whether "shifted PMI" refers to Eq. (3), and resolve contradictory/ambiguous sentences about removing low-PMI features.  
- Section 5.1: clarify "not properly aligned"/"unaligned", justify the claim of "considerable overlap" for NMOD relations, and explain the switch from higher-order dependency features to first-order features for similarity.  
- Note 3 and the rationale for discarding second-order paths in composed representations need clearer justification.  
- Section 6: claim that PPMI smoothing (α = 0.75) helps is not clearly supported by Table 3—clarify evidence.  
- Report optimal hyperparameters h and q (Eqs. 8–9) since they critically affect the hybrid results.  
- Table 4: explicitly state in the legend which composition method ("add" etc.) was used.  
- Comparisons: include word2vec results for compound phrase tokens (not just components) to strengthen embedding comparisons.  
- Baseline issues: the FREQ baseline’s assumption (frequent compounds are more compositional) is questionable and may reflect dataset bias—clarify or investigate.
