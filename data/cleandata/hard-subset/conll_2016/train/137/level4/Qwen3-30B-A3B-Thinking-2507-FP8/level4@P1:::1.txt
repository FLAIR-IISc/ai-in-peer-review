

General comments
=============================
This paper investigates methods for predicting the compositionality of English noun compounds, using a dataset of 90 compounds with human judgments of compositionality on a scale of 1-5. The authors compare an integrated compositional distributional approach using syntactically-annotated count vectors (based on dependency paths) against applying simple composition operations to neural embeddings. The evaluation measures Spearman correlation between computed compositionality scores and human judgments.

The paper's main contributions are:
1. Demonstrating that syntactically-annotated count vectors significantly outperform neural embeddings for compositionality prediction
2. Showing that a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned vector representations provides modest but statistically significant improvements
3. Providing evidence that typed dependency paths contain valuable information for compositionality detection

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses an important problem in compositional semantics - distinguishing compositional from non-compositional phrases - which is often overlooked in standard phrase similarity evaluations. The authors correctly identify that phrase similarity metrics can be confounded by compositionality.
2. The comparison between syntactic-contextual count vectors and neural embeddings is well-motivated, and the results consistently show the former outperforming the latter. This finding is significant given the current dominance of neural embeddings in NLP.
3. The authors provide a clear, principled approach to handling syntactic structure through their anchored packed tree (APT) framework, with thoughtful analysis of how dependency paths affect compositionality prediction.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper's key innovation (the use of aligned vectors) appears to be based on prior work by the authors ("suppressed reference"), which undermines the novelty claim. The paper should clearly acknowledge this prior work and explain how the current approach differs or extends it.
2. The dataset is small (90 compounds) and lacks critical details about compound frequency distributions and the number of human judgments per compound. The authors do not address whether the selection of compounds might introduce bias, which is especially concerning given the marginal performance gains observed.
3. The paper uses higher-order dependency paths during composition but only evaluates against first-order features in the final similarity computation (note 3). The justification for this is unclear and creates a potential inconsistency in the methodology. The authors should clarify why second-order paths in composed representations are considered unreliable for evaluation.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please clarify the relationship between the current work and the "suppressed reference" regarding aligned vectors. How does the current approach advance beyond prior work?
2. The paper does not describe how the 90 compounds were selected. Please provide details about the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components, and explain how many human judgments were provided per compound.
3. The authors claim that "the modifier is slightly more informative than the head" (q ≈ 0.3-0.5). However, for compounds like "spelling bee," the modifier ("spelling") is not used compositionally. Please explain how the model handles such cases where the modifier is non-compositional.
4. The justification for using only first-order features in the evaluation (note 3) is insufficient. Why are second-order paths in composed representations considered unreliable, while they were used during composition? Please provide a clear explanation.
5. The FREQ baseline (using compound frequency as a predictor) performs significantly better than RAND. However, the authors do not explain why frequent compounds would be more likely to be compositional. This suggests potential dataset bias that should be addressed.

Additional comments:
- The paper would benefit from clearer explanations of technical concepts like "elementary APTs" and "alignment" with concrete examples. Section 5.1 is particularly difficult to follow without such examples.
- The claim that "smoothed PPMI (α=0.75) generally has a small positive effect" is not clearly supported by the data in Table 3.
- The authors should clarify whether "shifted PMI" refers to the PMI calculation in equation (3), and provide a clear explanation of the apparent contradiction in the statement about removing features with low positive PMI.
- The paper uses "constituent" to refer to components of noun-noun compounds, which is confusing since "constituent" typically refers to phrase structure. The authors should use "component" instead.
- The paper does not provide results for word2vec vectors specifically for compound phrases, which would help contextualize the comparison.
