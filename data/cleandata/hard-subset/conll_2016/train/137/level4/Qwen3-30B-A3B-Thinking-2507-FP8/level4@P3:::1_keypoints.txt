- Paper investigates English noun-compound compositionality detection using anchored packed trees (APTs) that encode higher-order dependency paths.  
- Main contributions: APT-based method (Spearman ρ=0.79) outperforms prior work (0.71), and a hybrid aligned/unaligned composition shows syntactic structure is more informative than neural embeddings.

- Strength: Uses compositionality detection as a cleaner diagnostic evaluation to avoid confounds of phrase similarity tasks.  
- Strength: Strong empirical gains—APT and hybrid models consistently outperform state-of-the-art baselines.  
- Strength: Clear methodological details on APT construction, composition operations, and parameter tuning, with useful comparison of aligned vs. unaligned composition.

- Weakness: Novelty is questionable because the aligned-vector technique was previously published by the authors and not explicitly acknowledged.  
- Weakness: Dataset is small (90 compounds) and poorly characterized—frequency ranges, number of judgments, and scoring aggregation are unclear, raising robustness concerns.  
- Weakness: Claims to use higher-order dependency paths but evaluation uses only first-order features without sufficient justification, contradicting the paper’s emphasis on higher-order features.

- Question: How does this work advance the authors’ previously described aligned-vector approach and what new insights are provided?  
- Question: Please report dataset details: (a) frequency ranges of compounds/components, (b) number of human judgments per compound, and (c) how compositionality scores were aggregated.  
- Question: Why are second-order paths in composed representations considered unreliable, and how does that reconcile with the paper’s focus on higher-order features?

- Comment: Technical sections use ambiguous terminology (e.g., “elementary APTs,” “not properly aligned”); concrete examples would improve clarity.  
- Comment: The FREQ baseline (ρ=0.63) suggests compositionality judgments correlate with frequency, indicating potential dataset bias that should be discussed.  
- Comment: The claim that neural embeddings outperform count-based vectors is inconsistent with results—clarify comparisons where cbow (ρ=0.74) underperforms APT models but marginally beats some baselines.
