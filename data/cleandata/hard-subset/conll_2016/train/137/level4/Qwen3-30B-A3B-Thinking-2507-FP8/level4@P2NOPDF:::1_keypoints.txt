- Paper studies compositionality prediction for English noun compounds using cosine similarity between compound vectors and compositional component vectors.  
- Main results: count-based vectors outperform neural embeddings; plain (unaligned) count vectors beat aligned vectors; a tuned hybrid yields only minimal gains.  
- Introduction is good, but several sections (notably 5.1) lack clarity and would benefit from concrete examples.  
- Low novelty: aligned vectors replicate the authors’ prior work and are not sufficiently differentiated from Reddy et al.  
- Dataset is small (90 compounds) and poorly described; missing frequency ranges, number of human judgments per item, and score distribution.  
- Frequency effects are important and currently unreported, undermining conclusions (low-frequency compounds may skew results).  
- Section 3: the term “packed anchored tree” is misleading/unnecessary and “elementary APTs” need clear definition and justification.  
- Table 2: explain omission of long dependency-path features (e.g., NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ) and give examples of discarded “incompatible types.”  
- Section 4: selection criteria for the Reddy et al. dataset are unspecified; report frequency ranges and judge counts, and use “component” instead of “constituent.”  
- The intuition that literal use implies shared co-occurrences is misleading—this holds mainly when the component is the head.  
- Section 5: clarify what “compound phrase token” means and provide a running example illustrating the elementary representation rule.  
- Section 5.1: explicitly define “elementary APTs” (e.g., triplets) and clarify “shifted PMI” and which equation it refers to.  
- Rephrase/conflict: explain how low positive PMI filtering works and why NMOD relations show strong APT overlap after alignment.  
- Critical inconsistency: earlier sections use higher-order dependency features, yet similarity computation in 5.1 apparently uses only first-order features—justify this discrepancy.  
- Section 6: the claim that α=0.75 smoothing helps is not clearly supported by Table 3; provide evidence.  
- Report optimal h and q values (Equations 8–9) to show how much hybrids contribute over unaligned vectors.  
- Clarify Table 4’s combination method (confirm “add”), include word2vec results for compound phrases, and justify or examine the FREQ baseline (possible dataset bias).  
- Overall: methodology is reasonable but claims are overstated given dataset issues and limited novelty; recommend clearer definitions, more transparent dataset curation, concrete examples, and tempered interpretation of marginal improvements.
