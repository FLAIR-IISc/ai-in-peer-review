

Summary
The paper investigates the prediction of compositionality levels for English noun compounds using a dataset of 90 compounds with human judgments on a 0-5 scale. The authors compare compositional methods using syntactic-context count vectors (with typed dependency paths) against neural embeddings. They evaluate by measuring the Spearman correlation between cosine similarity scores of composed and observed compound vectors and human judgments. The main contributions as assessed by this reviewer are: (1) demonstrating that syntactic-context count vectors significantly outperform neural embeddings for compositionality detection, (2) showing that unaligned vectors generally outperform aligned vectors for compositionality prediction, and (3) providing a detailed analysis of the hybrid approach that combines aligned and unaligned vectors for marginal improvement.

Strengths
The paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of compositionality detection as a testbed for compositional modeling. The results consistently show that syntactic-context count vectors with typed dependency paths outperform neural embeddings across all tested configurations, with the hybrid approach achieving the highest correlation (0.79). The paper provides a thorough analysis of why syntactic structure is crucial for compositionality detection, particularly explaining the role of dependency paths in predicting compound meanings. The evaluation framework, which separates compositionality detection from phrase similarity evaluation, avoids confounding factors that plague other approaches in the field.

Weaknesses
The innovation with respect to Reddy et al. (2011) is marginal and heavily relies on previously published work (the "suppressed reference" for the alignment method), which undermines the novelty claim. The dataset is small (90 compounds) and lacks sufficient description of compound frequency ranges, which could significantly impact results as low-frequency compounds are particularly challenging for distributional models. The improvement from the proposed hybrid approach is marginal (only 0.01-0.02 increase in correlation over unaligned vectors), making the findings potentially fragile when applied to larger or different datasets. The paper fails to adequately explain how the 90 compounds were selected or the number of human judgments per compound, which is critical for evaluating the robustness of the results.

Questions to Authors
How were the 90 compounds selected, and what are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components? The paper states that the compounds are from the Reddy et al. dataset, but does not provide details on selection criteria or frequency distributions that could influence results.
How many human judgments were provided for each compound, and how were they aggregated? The paper mentions "human judgments" but does not specify the number of judges or how scores were averaged, which is essential for evaluating the reliability of the dataset.
Why does the evaluation use only first-order dependency features (while composition uses higher-order features) for measuring similarity? The paper states that "the second-order (and third-order) paths in the composed representations are not reliable" but does not provide a clear explanation for this claim.
What is the relationship between compound frequency and compositionality scores? The FREQ baseline performs better than RAND, suggesting that frequency may be correlated with compositionality, which could indicate a dataset bias that needs to be addressed.
