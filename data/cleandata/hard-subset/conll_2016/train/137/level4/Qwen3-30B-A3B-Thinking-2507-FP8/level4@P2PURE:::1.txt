

General comments
=============================
The paper presents experiments predicting compositionality levels of English compounds. It utilizes a pre-existing dataset of 90 compounds, with compositionality scores ranked from 1 to 5 by an unspecified number of judges. Each experiment computes cosine similarity between the compound vector (treated as a single token) and a vector composition of its components. Evaluation employs Spearman correlation between these similarities and human judgments. Experiments vary by:  
- Vector type (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors)  
- For count vectors, whether plain or "aligned" vectors are used for the dependent component. Alignment (proposed in a previous suppressed reference by the authors) aims to capture a shift from dependent to head.  
Results show syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings. Using aligned vectors alone performs worse than unmodified vectors, while a highly-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields only marginal improvement.  

The introduction is well-written, but sections like 5.1 are difficult to follow despite straightforward concepts. Rephrasing with running examples would enhance clarity.  

Regarding substance, I have significant concerns:  
1. The claimed innovation over Reddy et al. centers on aligned vectors, yet these were published in the authors' suppressed reference.  
2. The dataset is small and inadequately described. Frequency ranges of compounds/components likely impact results. Given the marginal gains from aligned vectors over this small dataset (with unclear compound selection), the findings appear fragile.  

More detailed comments/questions
================================
Section 3  
The term "packed anchored tree" is unnecessary. It appears to be a standard extraction of dependency paths between lexical items—merely extending traditional syntactic distributional representations (e.g., Lin 98, which uses paths of length 1 or 2 with collapsed prepositions). Why is it called a "tree"? What are "elementary APTs" (Section 5.1)?  

Table 2: Did you omit mentioning the discard of features with order >3? This explains the absence of *NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ* in the leftmost bottom cell. Alternatively, does this relate to your elimination of incompatible types (which require an example for clarity)?  

Section 4:  
As the Reddy et al. dataset is central, clarify how the 90 compounds were selected. Provide frequency ranges for compounds and components—results likely vary significantly with these. How many judgments per compound? Are many compounds assigned identical compositionality scores? This could invalidate Spearman correlation ranking.  
The term "constituent" for a compound component (e.g., in N N) is confusing; "component" is preferable, as "constituent" implies a phrase (syntagm).  
The intuition—"if a constituent is used literally within a phrase, it likely shares co-occurrences with the compound"—holds only if the constituent is the head (e.g., "spelling" in *spelling bee* has a different distribution than *spelling bee* itself).  

Section 5:  
Clarify: "the elementary representation for the constituent... will not contain contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent elsewhere." Provide a running example specifying which objects are referenced (e.g., does "compound phrase token" mean the merged compound?).  

Section 5.1:  
Assuming "elementary APTs" are triplets (target word *w* + dependency path *r* + other word *w'*), the term is confusing. Clarify if "shifted PMI" refers to Equation (3).  
The sentence "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts" is contradictory. Do "these phrases" refer to "things"? Clarify.  
"In general, we would expect little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned." What does "not properly aligned" mean? (Not aligned at all?)  
The discussion from line 558 to 563 is unclear. Why would overlap be considerable specifically for NMOD between components?  
Paragraphs 575–580 are puzzling: Why use higher-order dependency features throughout, then revert to first-order features only when measuring similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors? Note 3 claims 2nd-order paths are unreliable, but the explanation is insufficient.  

Section 6:  
"Smoothing PPMI with α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect" (line 663) is not evident from Table 3.  
Specify optimal *h* and *q* values in Equations (8) and (9)—they are critical for assessing the "hybridity" contributing to marginal gains over unaligned results.  
Table 4 results appear to use the "add" combination; state this in the legend.  
Include word2vec results for compound phrases.  
The FREQ baseline's intuition is unclear: Why would frequent compounds be more compositional? This suggests potential dataset bias.
