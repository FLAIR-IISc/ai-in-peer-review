

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper investigates the detection of compositionality in English noun compounds using distributional semantic models. The authors propose an integrated compositional approach based on "anchored packed trees" (APTs), which encode higher-order dependency paths between words. They compare this method against neural embeddings and ad-hoc composition operations. The primary contribution is demonstrating that APT-based compositionality detection significantly outperforms prior work (Spearman ρ=0.79 vs. Reddy et al.'s 0.71), particularly through a hybrid composition model that combines aligned and unaligned contextual features. A secondary contribution is establishing that syntactic structure (via typed dependency paths) is more informative than neural embeddings for compositionality tasks. The authors claim that their approach better captures the compositional nature of phrases by maintaining syntactic relationships in vector representations.

**Strengths**  
1. **Novel evaluation framework**: The paper effectively uses compositionality detection as a diagnostic tool to evaluate compositional methods, avoiding confounding effects present in standard phrase similarity tasks. This provides a clearer metric for assessing compositional models.  
2. **Strong empirical results**: The APT approach consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods (0.79 vs. 0.71), with the hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned features) yielding the best results. This demonstrates that syntactic structure significantly enhances compositionality detection.  
3. **Methodological clarity**: The authors provide detailed technical specifications for APT construction, composition operations, and parameter tuning. The comparison between aligned and unaligned composition, along with the hybrid approach, offers valuable insights into how syntactic features influence compositional modeling.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Questionable novelty**: The key innovation—using aligned vectors for composition—was previously published by the authors (in a "suppressed reference"). This undermines the paper's claimed contribution, as the core technique is not new. The paper should explicitly acknowledge prior work and clarify how this study advances the previous approach.  
2. **Small, poorly characterized dataset**: The Reddy et al. dataset (90 compounds) is small and lacks critical details (e.g., frequency ranges of compounds/components, number of human judgments per compound). Given that compositionality judgments correlate with frequency (FREQ baseline ρ=0.63), the dataset's selection criteria and compositionality scoring methodology require clarification to assess result robustness.  
3. **Inconsistent feature usage**: The paper uses higher-order dependency paths for feature extraction but restricts evaluation to first-order features (per Note 3). The justification for this limitation ("second-order paths in composed representations are unreliable") is insufficiently explained and contradicts the paper's emphasis on higher-order features. This undermines confidence in the results.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. The "aligned" vector approach was previously described in the authors' suppressed work. How does this study advance that prior method, and what new insights does it provide?  
2. The dataset's selection criteria and compositionality scoring methodology are unclear. Please specify: (a) the frequency ranges of the 90 compounds and their components, (b) the number of human judgments per compound, and (c) how compositionality scores were aggregated (e.g., mean vs. median).  
3. The paper uses higher-order dependency paths in feature extraction but evaluates only on first-order features. Please clarify why second-order paths in composed representations are unreliable, and how this aligns with the paper's emphasis on higher-order features.  

**Additional Comments**  
- The paper's technical sections (e.g., Section 5.1) are difficult to follow due to ambiguous terminology (e.g., "elementary APTs," "not properly aligned"). Providing concrete examples would improve readability.  
- The FREQ baseline (ρ=0.63) outperforms RAND (ρ=0.02), suggesting compositionality judgments correlate with compound frequency. This raises questions about dataset bias that warrant discussion.  
- The claim that "neural word embeddings consistently outperform count-based vectors" (Section 2) appears inconsistent with the paper's results (Table 4), where cbow embeddings (ρ=0.74) marginally outperform Reddy et al.'s untyped approach (ρ=0.71) but underperform APT models. Clarify this comparison.
