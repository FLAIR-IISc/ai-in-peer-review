

General Comments
================
This paper investigates the prediction of compositionality levels for English compounds. The authors utilize a pre-existing dataset of 90 compounds, with compositionality ranked on a 1–5 scale by an unspecified number of judges. Each experiment computes cosine similarity between the compound vector (treated as a single token) and a vector composition of its components, evaluating results via Spearman correlation against human judgments. Experiments vary by vector type (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors) and, for the latter, whether plain or "aligned" vectors are used for the dependent component (where alignment captures a shift from dependent to head, as proposed in a previously suppressed reference by the authors).

Results indicate syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, and aligned vectors alone perform worse than unmodified vectors. A highly-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields only marginal improvement. While the introduction is well-written, sections like 5.1 are difficult to follow despite the underlying concepts being straightforward; incorporating running examples would enhance clarity.

Substantive Concerns
====================
1.  **Innovation and Prior Work**: The primary innovation appears to be the use of aligned vectors, yet this method was previously published in a suppressed reference by the authors. This significantly undermines the paper's novelty claim.
2.  **Dataset Limitations**: The dataset is small (90 compounds) and insufficiently characterized. Crucially, frequency ranges of compounds and components—which likely impact results—are not provided. Given the marginal gains from aligned vectors and the unclear compound selection criteria, the findings are fragile.

Detailed Comments
=================
**Section 3**: The term "packed anchored tree" (PAT) appears unnecessary. It describes a standard extension of dependency path extraction (e.g., paths of length >2, as in Lin 1998), not a novel structure. Clarify why it is termed a "tree" and define "elementary APTs" (Section 5.1).

**Table 2**: The exclusion of features beyond order 3 (e.g., why `NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ` does not appear in the leftmost bottom cell) requires explicit mention. Is this due to the feature order limit or the elimination of incompatible types? An example of the latter would clarify.

**Section 4**: The selection criteria for the 90 compounds are critical but missing. Frequency ranges for compounds and components must be reported, as results likely depend on them. Clarify: (a) the number of human judgments per compound; (b) whether multiple compounds share identical compositionality scores (a potential issue for Spearman correlation); (c) the term "constituent" (suggest "component" to avoid confusion with syntactic phrases).

**Section 5**: The statement *"the intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences"* is only valid if the constituent is the head (e.g., "spelling" in "spelling bee" shares distribution with "spelling bee," not "bee"). A running example is needed to clarify "compound phrase token" (referring to the merged compound) and the scope of contextual features.

**Section 5.1**:  
- Clarify if "elementary APTs" denote triplets (target word *w*, dependency path *r*, other word *w'*) and justify the confusing terminology.  
- Confirm whether "shifted PMI" refers to Equation (3).  
- The sentence *"Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts"* is contradictory. "These phrases" ambiguously co-refers with "things"; clarify.  
- Define "not properly aligned" (does this mean "not aligned at all"?).  
- Explain why overlap between unaligned APTs is expected to be considerable *specifically for NMOD relations* (pp. 558–563).  
- Resolve the inconsistency: The paper uses higher-order dependency features (Section 5), yet the similarity metric (Section 5.1) relies solely on first-order features. Note 3’s explanation for discarding second-order paths in composed representations is unclear; provide justification.

**Section 6**:  
- The claim that "smoothing PPMI with α=0.75 generally has a small positive effect" (p. 663) is not evident from Table 3; clarify.  
- Specify optimal values for *h* and *q* (Equations 8–9), as they are essential for assessing the contribution of "hybridity" to the slight gains over unaligned results.  
- Clarify in Table 4’s legend that results correspond to the "add" combination.  
- Provide word2vec results for compound phrases to enable direct comparison.  
- Justify the FREQ baseline: Why would frequent compounds be more compositional? This suggests potential dataset bias.
