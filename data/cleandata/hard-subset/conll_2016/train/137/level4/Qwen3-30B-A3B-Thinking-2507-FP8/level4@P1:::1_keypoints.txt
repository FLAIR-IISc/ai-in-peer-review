- Paper studies prediction of English noun-compound compositionality using 90 compounds and Spearman correlation with human judgments.
- Compares syntactically-annotated count vectors (APT/dependency paths) against simple composition of neural embeddings.

Main contributions:
- Syntactically-annotated count vectors outperform neural embeddings for compositionality prediction.
- A hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned representations yields modest but statistically significant improvements.
- Typed dependency paths provide valuable signals for compositionality detection.

Strengths:
- Addresses an important and under-studied problem (distinguishing compositional vs. non-compositional phrases).
- Well-motivated comparison showing count-based syntactic-context models beat neural embeddings.
- Clear, principled handling of syntactic structure via the APT framework and analysis of dependency-path effects.

Weaknesses:
- Key innovation (aligned vectors) appears derived from the authors' prior work and novelty is not clearly established.
- Small dataset (90 compounds) with missing details on frequency distributions and number of human judgments; potential selection bias.
- Methodological inconsistency: uses higher-order dependency paths during composition but evaluates only first-order features without clear justification.

Questions to authors:
- Clarify relationship to the "suppressed reference" and how the current aligned-vectors approach advances prior work.
- Describe how the 90 compounds were selected, frequency ranges of compounds/components, and number of human judgments per compound.
- Explain how the model handles cases where the modifier is non-compositional (e.g., "spelling bee") despite claiming modifiers are more informative.
- Justify using only first-order features in evaluation while employing second-order paths in composition and explain why second-order paths are unreliable for evaluation.
- Explain why compound frequency (FREQ baseline) predicts compositionality better than RAND and whether this indicates dataset bias.

Additional comments:
- Provide clearer explanations and concrete examples for technical concepts like "elementary APTs" and "alignment" (section 5.1 is hard to follow).
- Evidence for the claimed positive effect of smoothed PPMI (α=0.75) is not clearly supported by Table 3.
- Clarify whether "shifted PMI" refers to equation (3) and reconcile the statement about removing low positive PMI features.
- Use "component" instead of "constituent" to avoid confusion with phrase-structure terminology.
- Report results for word2vec (or other neural) phrase vectors specifically to better contextualize comparisons.
