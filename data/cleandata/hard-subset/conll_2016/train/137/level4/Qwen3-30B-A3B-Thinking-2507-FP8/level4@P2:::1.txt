

Summary
The paper investigates methods for predicting compositionality in English compound nouns using vector space models. It leverages Reddy et al.'s benchmark dataset of 90 compounds with human judgments of compositionality (ranging from 1-5) and evaluates cosine similarity between observed compound vectors and composed vectors from their components. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings for compositionality detection; (2) showing that a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned vector composition provides marginal improvement; and (3) providing detailed analysis of how dependency structure affects compositionality prediction.

Strengths
1. The paper offers a clear, well-structured evaluation framework that isolates compositionality detection from phrase similarity evaluation, addressing an important limitation in prior work. By focusing specifically on compositionality rather than phrase similarity, the authors avoid confounding effects that plague other approaches.

2. The experimental design is thorough, systematically comparing multiple composition methods (aligned/unaligned, intersective/additive) and vector representations (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors). The use of 3-fold cross-validation and detailed parameter analysis (including optimal h/q values in [0.3,0.5]) demonstrates methodological rigor.

3. The results significantly advance the state-of-the-art, with the best hybrid APT model achieving ρ=0.79 versus Reddy et al.'s 0.71. This represents a statistically significant improvement (p<0.05) that demonstrates the value of incorporating syntactic structure into compositional models.

Weaknesses
1. The claimed innovation (vector alignment) appears to be based on previously published work by the authors (referenced as "suppressed"), undermining the novelty claim. The paper does not clearly distinguish their contribution from prior work, which is a significant issue for a conference paper.

2. The dataset is too small (only 90 compounds) and lacks critical details about compound frequencies, component frequencies, and the selection process. Without this information, it's impossible to assess whether the results generalize beyond this specific sample. The marginal improvements (0.08-0.09 on ρ) are especially concerning given the dataset's size.

3. The paper makes an inconsistent choice in the evaluation metric: while composition uses higher-order dependency paths, the final similarity calculation only considers first-order features. The justification (Note 3) is unclear and insufficient to explain why second-order features would be unreliable in composed representations but not in the observed phrasal vectors.

4. The FREQ baseline (using compound frequency as a predictor) outperforms RAND but is not well-justified. The paper suggests "more frequent compounds tend to be compositional," but this is not explained or supported, potentially indicating dataset bias.

Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims innovation in "aligned" vectors but references a "suppressed" prior work. Please clarify the specific contribution relative to this prior work and explain why the current implementation constitutes a meaningful advancement.

2. Please provide details about the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components in the dataset. How do these frequencies correlate with compositionality scores? Without this information, the results' robustness cannot be properly assessed.

3. The final evaluation uses only first-order features despite using higher-order features in composition. Please clarify why second-order features in composed representations are considered unreliable for evaluation while being used in the composition process itself.

4. The FREQ baseline (using compound frequency) outperforms RAND. Please explain why frequent compounds would tend to be more compositional and whether this might indicate a dataset bias that affects the results' interpretation.

5. The paper states optimal h and q values were in [0.3,0.5] but does not report specific values. Please provide these values to better understand the contribution of hybrid composition versus unaligned approaches.
