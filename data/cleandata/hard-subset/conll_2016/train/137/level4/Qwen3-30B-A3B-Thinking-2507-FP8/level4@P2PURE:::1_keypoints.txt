- Paper experiments predict compositionality of 90 English compounds using cosine similarity between compound vectors and composed component vectors, evaluated with Spearman correlation against human ratings.  
- Experiments vary vector type (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors) and use an "alignment" technique for dependent components.  
- Main result: syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings; aligned vectors alone perform worse than unaligned, and a tuned hybrid yields only marginal gains.  
- Introduction is clear, but some sections (notably 5.1) are confusing and would benefit from running examples.  
- Major concern: claimed novelty (aligned vectors) is undermined because that method appears in the authors’ previously suppressed reference.  
- Major concern: dataset is small (90 items), poorly described, and compound/component frequency likely affects results—findings may be fragile.  
- Need clarity on how the 90 compounds were selected and the frequency ranges of compounds and components.  
- Report the number of human judgments per compound and whether many compounds share identical compositionality scores (which would affect Spearman correlation validity).  
- Terminology issue: “constituent” is misleading for compound components; “component” is preferable.  
- The intuition that literal constituent usage implies shared co-occurrences with the compound only reliably holds when the constituent is the head.  
- Question the use of the term “packed anchored tree” — it appears to be standard dependency-path extraction; explain why it’s called a “tree” and define “elementary APTs.”  
- Clarify whether features with order >3 were discarded (explains missing feature examples) and provide an example for “incompatible types.”  
- Section 5 needs a concrete running example clarifying “elementary representation for the constituent” and what “compound phrase token” refers to.  
- Section 5.1: if elementary APTs are triplets (w, r, w′), clarify this and define “shifted PMI” and its equation reference.  
- Fix confusing sentence about removing low-positive-PMI features—identify referents (do “these phrases” mean “things” or something else?).  
- Clarify what “not properly aligned” means and why aligned overlap would be considerable specifically for NMOD relations.  
- Explain why higher-order dependency features are used generally but only first-order features are used when measuring similarity between composed and phrasal vectors; Note 3’s explanation about 2nd-order paths is insufficient.  
- Section 6: claim that smoothing PPMI with α=0.75 helps is not clearly supported by Table 3—clarify evidence.  
- Report optimal h and q values from Equations (8) and (9) since they are critical to assessing the hybrid/combined method’s contribution.  
- State which combination method (e.g., add) is used for the results in Table 4 in the table legend.  
- Include word2vec results for compound phrase tokens (not only constituents).  
- Clarify the intuition and validity of the FREQ baseline—why would more frequent compounds be more compositional, and could this indicate dataset bias?
