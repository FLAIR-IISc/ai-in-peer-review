

The paper presents experiments on predicting compositionality levels of English compounds using a previously established dataset of 90 compounds, with human judgments ranking compositionality from 1 to 5 (without specifying the number of judges). Each experiment computes cosine similarity between the compound vector (treated as a single token) and a composition of its component vectors, evaluating results via Spearman correlation against human judgments. Variations include vector types (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors) and, for the latter, whether plain or "aligned" vectors (designed to capture a shift from dependent to head, per a prior suppressed reference) are used for the dependent component. Results show syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings; aligned vectors alone perform worse than unmodified vectors, while a highly-tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields marginal improvement.  

The introduction is well-written, but sections like 5.1 are difficult to follow despite straightforward concepts; incorporating running examples would enhance clarity.  

Key substantive concerns:  
- The claimed innovation (aligned vectors) stems from a prior suppressed reference by the authors.  
- The dataset is small and inadequately described, particularly regarding frequency ranges of compounds/components, which likely influence results. Given the marginal gains from aligned vectors over a limited dataset with unclear compound selection, the findings are fragile.  

Detailed comments:  
**Section 3**  
The term "packed anchored tree" is unnecessary; it appears equivalent to standard dependency path extraction (e.g., extending traditional syntactic distributional methods beyond length-1/2 paths). Clarify why it is termed a "tree" and define "elementary APTs" (Section 5.1).  

**Table 2**  
Explain why features with path order >3 are discarded (e.g., why NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ is absent in the leftmost bottom cell). Provide an example of incompatible feature elimination.  

**Section 4**  
Detail the selection criteria for the 90 compounds (e.g., frequency ranges of compounds/components). Clarify the number of human judgments per compound and whether multiple compounds share identical compositionality scores (impacting Spearman correlation). Avoid "constituent" for compound components; use "component" (to distinguish from syntagmatic constituents). Correct the intuition: the distributional similarity holds primarily if the constituent is the head (e.g., "spelling" in "spelling bee" vs. "bee").  

**Section 5**  
Provide a running example for: "elementary representation for the constituent... will not contain contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless observed elsewhere." Clarify if "compound phrase token" refers to the merged compound.  

**Section 5.1**  
Define "elementary APTs" (e.g., triplet: target word + dependency path + other word). Clarify if "shifted PMI" refers to Equation (3). Resolve the contradiction in: "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts." Clarify "not properly aligned" (does it mean unaligned?). Explain why overlap is expected for NMOD relations between components (paragraphs 558–563).  

**Section 5.1 (cont.)**  
Address the abrupt shift from higher-order dependency features (used throughout) to first-order features only when measuring similarity (paragraphs 575–580). Note 3’s explanation for discarding second-order paths in composed representations is unclear.  

**Section 6**  
The statement "Smoothing PPMI with α=0.75 generally has a small positive effect" (p. 663) is not evident from Table 3. Specify optimal values for *h* and *q* (Equations 8–9) to assess hybridity’s contribution. Clarify in Table 4’s legend that results use the "add" combination. Include word2vec results for compound phrases. Justify the FREQ baseline: why would frequent compounds be more compositional? This suggests potential dataset bias.
