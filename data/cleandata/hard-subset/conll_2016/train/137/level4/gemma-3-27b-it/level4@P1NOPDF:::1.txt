The paper reports experiments on predicting the degree of compositionality of English compounds. The dataset used is a pre-existing set of 90 compounds, with compositionality ranked on a scale of 1 to 5 by an unspecified number of human judges. The core experimental approach involves calculating the cosine similarity between the vector representation of the compound (treated as a single token) and a composition of the vectors representing its constituent parts. Evaluation is performed using Spearman correlation between these cosine similarities and the human compositionality judgments. The experiments vary in terms of the vector representations used – neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors – and, in the latter case, whether “aligned” vectors are used for the dependent component of the compound. This alignment attempts to capture a shift in meaning from the dependent to the head word, and was previously proposed in a related, unpublished work by the authors. 

The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings. Using aligned vectors alone performs less well than using unaligned vectors, but a carefully tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors provides a slight improvement.

Regarding the paper's presentation, I found the introduction to be well-written, but other sections (such as 5.1) are difficult to follow despite the underlying concepts being relatively straightforward. Incorporating running examples would likely improve clarity. 

Concerning the substance of the work, I have several concerns. The primary innovation appears to be the use of aligned vectors, yet these were previously presented in a “suppressed reference” by the authors. Furthermore, the dataset is small and not thoroughly described. The range of frequencies of the compounds and their components is likely to influence the results. Given the marginal improvements achieved using aligned vectors, coupled with the small dataset size and uncertainty surrounding the compound selection process, I consider the findings presented in the paper to be somewhat fragile.

**More Detailed Comments/Questions**

**Section 3**

I don’t understand the rationale for introducing the term “packed anchored tree.” It appears to be a straightforward extraction of dependency paths between two lexical items, a common technique in syntactic distributional representations of words (as seen in Lin, 1998, and subsequent work, which typically uses paths of length one or two, often with collapsed prepositions). Further, why is it referred to as a “tree”? What are “elementary APTs” (Section 5.1)?

**Table 2:** Did you omit mentioning that features of order greater than 3 were discarded (which explains why, for instance, `NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ` doesn't appear in the bottom-left cell of Table 2)? Or does this relate to the elimination of incompatible types you mention, for which a clarifying example would be helpful?

**Section 4:**

Given that the Reddy et al. dataset is central to your work, it is important to explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components? Results are highly susceptible to variation based on frequency. How many judgments were provided for each compound? Are there many compounds with identical compositionality scores? If so, does this pose a problem when ranking them to compute the Spearman correlation?

You use “constituent” to refer to a component of the N N sequence. I suggest using “component,” as “constituent” often implies a phrase or syntagm. 

“…the intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences”: This intuition holds true if the constituent is the head of the phrase, but is much less reliable otherwise (e.g., “spelling bee” does not exhibit the same distribution as “spelling”).

**Section 5**

“Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.” Please provide a running example to help the reader understand which element you are referring to. Does “compound phrase token” refer to the combined representation of the compound’s components?

**Section 5.1**

I believe that “elementary APTs” represent a triplet consisting of a target word `w`, a dependency path `r`, and another word `w'?`. I find this terminology confusing. Clarify whether “shifted PMI” refers to PMI as defined in equation (3).

“Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.” Do “these phrases” co-refer to “things” here? The sentence appears contradictory and requires clarification.

“In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned.” What does “not properly aligned” mean? Do you mean not aligned at all?

I find paragraphs 558 to 563 difficult to understand. Why should the potential overlap be particularly considerable in the case of an NMOD relation between the two compound components?

Paragraphs 575 to 580 are puzzling. Why does the paper utilize higher-order dependency features throughout, only to revert to first-order features when measuring the crucial metric of similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors? Note 3 attempts to explain this, but I don’t fully understand why second-order paths in the composed representations are considered unreliable. Please clarify.

**Section 6**

“Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect” does not appear clearly demonstrated in Table 3. What are the optimal values for `h` and `q` in equations 8 and 9? These are important for understanding the extent to which “hybridity” contributes to the slight gains over the unaligned results. 

It appears that the results in Table 4 correspond to the additive combination method; this could be explicitly stated in the caption. Furthermore, could you provide results for the word2vec vectors trained on the compound phrases?

I don’t understand the rationale behind the FREQ baseline. Why would frequent compounds tend to be compositional? This suggests a potential bias within the dataset.
