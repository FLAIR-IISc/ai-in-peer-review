The paper reports experiments on predicting the level of compositionality of compounds in English using a dataset of 90 compounds with compositionality ranked from 1 to 5 based on human judgments. The experiments compare cosine similarity between a compound’s vector and the composition of its components, varying the vector type (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors) and whether vectors are aligned to capture dependencies. Results suggest syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, alignment alone performs worse than non-modified vectors, and a tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors offers slight improvement. 

The introduction is well-written, but sections like 5.1 are difficult to follow; running examples could help. The core innovation—aligned vectors—was previously published by the authors (“suppressed reference”), and the dataset is small with unclear selection criteria and frequency ranges, potentially impacting results. Given the marginal improvements and small dataset, I find the findings somewhat fragile.

**Strengths:**

1. **Methodological Rigor:** The paper systematically explores different compositional approaches and parameter settings, providing a comprehensive comparison between neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors. The thoroughness of the experimental design strengthens the validity of the findings.
2. **Novel Application of Dependency Paths:** Utilizing higher-order dependency paths to build contextual features demonstrates a thoughtful approach to incorporating syntactic information into distributional semantics. This expands upon traditional methods that often rely on simpler co-occurrence patterns.
3. **Clear Research Question:** The paper addresses a well-defined research question – how to better detect the compositionality of noun compounds – and provides a clear evaluation framework based on human judgments and Spearman correlation. This focus enhances the clarity and impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The primary contribution – the use of aligned vectors – appears to have been previously presented in a “suppressed reference” by the authors. This diminishes the novelty of the work and raises concerns about incremental progress.
2. **Dataset Concerns:** The dataset of 90 compounds is relatively small, and details regarding its creation (selection criteria, frequency ranges of compounds and components) are lacking. This limits the generalizability of the results and introduces potential biases. The impact of compound/component frequencies is particularly concerning given the observation that frequent compounds tend to be more compositional (as indicated by the frequency baseline).
3. **Unclear Justification for Methodological Choices:** Several methodological choices lack sufficient justification. For instance, the rationale for discarding higher-order dependency features when computing the crucial similarity metric (Section 5, Note 3) is unclear and weakens the overall argument. The explanation about unreliable second-order paths is not persuasive without further elaboration.
4. **Readability Issues:** Section 3 and particularly Section 5.1 are difficult to follow due to abstract terminology and a lack of concrete examples. The notation is dense, and key concepts like "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APT" are not adequately explained.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the novelty of this work compared to your previous “suppressed reference” detailing the aligned vector approach? What specific aspects have been improved or expanded upon?
2. Please provide a more detailed description of the dataset, including the frequency distribution of the compounds and their constituent parts. How might variations in frequency influence the observed results?
3. Can you clarify the rationale for restricting the similarity calculation to first-order dependency features after incorporating higher-order features in the vector construction (Section 5, Note 3)? Why are the second-order paths considered unreliable in this specific context?
4. Please provide concrete examples illustrating the "alignment" process and how it addresses the potential issues of feature space misalignment. A visual representation or step-by-step explanation would be beneficial.
5. What are the optimal values for the parameters *h* and *q* in equations 8 and 9? Reporting these values would allow for a better understanding of the contribution of the aligned and unaligned components in the hybrid model.
