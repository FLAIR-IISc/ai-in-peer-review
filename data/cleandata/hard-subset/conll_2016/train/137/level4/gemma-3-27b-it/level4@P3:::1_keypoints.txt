- Summary: Compares an APT dependency-based composition method (with aligned features) to neural embeddings for noun–noun compositionality on Reddy et al. (2011); hybrid APT slightly outperforms embeddings with modest gains.

- Contribution: Empirical evaluation of dependency-based composition methods specifically for compositionality detection, benchmarked against strong neural baselines.

- Contribution: Investigation of aligned dependency features and finding that a hybrid of aligned and unaligned features gives the best performance.

- Contribution: Replication and extension of the Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark, providing updated results and comparisons.

- Strength: Clear motivation and problem definition framing compositionality detection as a useful evaluation.

- Strength: Rigorous experimental setup using a standard dataset (Reddy et al., 2011), Spearman correlation, and systematic parameter variation.

- Strength: Insightful comparison showing benefits of incorporating syntactic structure and that a hybrid approach can outperform pure distributional methods.

- Strength: Detailed methodological description of the APT model and helpful example dependency paths that aid reproducibility.

- Weakness: Incremental innovation—aligned dependency features were previously introduced and overall improvements are marginal.

- Weakness: Small dataset (90 compounds) limits generalizability and risks dataset bias; frequency distributions and selection criteria are not provided.

- Weakness: Clarity/readability problems in places (e.g., Section 5) and unexplained terminology that hinder understanding.

- Weakness: Several design choices lack justification (e.g., using first-order features for final similarity, rationale for optimal h and q).

- Weakness: Insufficient error analysis—no breakdown of compound types or patterns where methods fail.

- Question: Provide a detailed description of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, including frequency distributions and selection criteria for the 90 compounds.

- Question: Explain intuitively why aligning dependency features improves performance and which syntactic phenomena the alignment captures.

- Question: Why use first-order dependency features for the final similarity calculation despite using higher-order features during model construction?

- Question: What explains the observed optimal parameter values for h and q in equations 8 and 9?

- Question: Provide an error analysis identifying compound types or patterns where the proposed methods perform poorly.
