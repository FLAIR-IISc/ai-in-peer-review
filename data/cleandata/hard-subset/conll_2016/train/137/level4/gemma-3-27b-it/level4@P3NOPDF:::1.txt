## Review for CONLL_2016

**General Comments**

This paper investigates the prediction of compositionality in English compounds, employing both neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors. The evaluation leverages a Spearman correlation between cosine similarity – computed between a compound’s vector representation and the composition of its components’ vectors – and human judgments of compositionality. The core innovation appears to be the introduction of “aligned” vectors designed to capture semantic shifts between the dependent and head components of the compound, previously described in a suppressed reference. The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors generally outperform embeddings, and that a carefully tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields a modest improvement.

While the introduction is well-written and clearly outlines the task, several sections, particularly 5.1, present challenges in terms of readability. The underlying concepts are not inherently complex, but benefit significantly from illustrative examples. Regarding the substance of the work, my primary concerns revolve around the dataset’s limited size, insufficient description, and the marginal gains achieved with aligned vectors. Given these limitations, I find the conclusions of the paper somewhat fragile. The reliance on a small dataset, coupled with an unclear selection process for the compounds, weakens the generalizability of the findings.

**Detailed Comments/Questions**

**Section 3**

The introduction of the term “packed anchored tree” is unclear. It seems to represent a straightforward extraction of dependency paths between lexical items – a standard technique in syntactic distributional semantics (e.g., Lin, 1998, utilizing paths of length one or two with collapsed prepositions). Clarification is needed regarding why a new term is introduced. Furthermore, the definition of “elementary APTs” in section 5.1 is opaque.

**Table 2:** The table should explicitly state that features of order greater than 3 are discarded, explaining the absence of entries like `NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ`. Alternatively, clarify if this is related to the elimination of incompatible types, and if so, provide a concrete example.

**Section 4**

Given the centrality of the Reddy et al. dataset, a more detailed explanation of its composition is crucial.  Specifically, details about the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components are needed, as these factors likely influence the results. How many independent judgments were collected for each compound? Are there significant clusters of compounds sharing the same compositionality score? This could introduce bias when calculating the Spearman correlation. 

The paper uses "constituent" to refer to a component of the N N sequence. I recommend using "component" instead, as "constituent" carries the connotation of a syntactic phrase.

Regarding the statement “…the intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences,” this intuition primarily holds when the constituent is the *head* of the phrase.  This is less true when it is a modifier (e.g., “spelling bee” does not exhibit the same distribution as “spelling”).

**Section 5**

“Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.”  Please provide a concrete example to clarify which objects are being discussed and facilitate understanding. Is "compound phrase token" referring to the combined vector representation of the compound's components?

**Section 5.1**

I interpret “elementary APTs” as a triplet consisting of a target word *w*, a dependency path *r*, and another word *w’*. However, the terminology is confusing and should be clarified.  Is “shifted PMI” defined as PMI in equation (3)?

“Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.”  This sentence appears contradictory: do “these phrases” co-refer to “things”?  The logic requires clarification.

“In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned.”  What does “not properly aligned” mean – is it synonymous with “not aligned at all”?

Paragraphs 558-563 are difficult to follow.  Why should overlap be particularly considerable in cases where the NMOD relation exists between the two components?

Paragraphs 575-580 are puzzling. Why is the paper employing higher-order dependency features throughout, only to revert to first-order features when measuring the crucial similarity between composed and observed vectors? Note 3 attempts to justify this, but the explanation regarding the unreliability of second-order paths in composed representations lacks clarity.

**Section 6**

“Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect” is not readily apparent from Table 3. What are the optimal values for *h* and *q* in equations 8 and 9? These parameters are essential for quantifying the contribution of “hybridity” to the slight gains observed over the unaligned results.

The table legend should clarify that the results in Table 4 correspond to using the additive combination method. Furthermore, could you provide the results obtained using word2vec vectors for the compound phrases?

I do not understand the rationale behind the FREQ baseline.  Why would a frequent compound tend to be more compositional? This suggests a potential bias within the dataset.
