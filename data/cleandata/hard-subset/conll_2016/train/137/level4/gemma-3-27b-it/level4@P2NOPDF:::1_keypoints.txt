- Study predicts compositionality of 90 English compounds by comparing compound vectors to composed component vectors via cosine similarity.  
- Explored neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors; count vectors outperform embeddings.  
- Introducing “aligned” syntactic-context vectors yields only a slight improvement when combined with unaligned vectors.  
- Primary contribution appears to be applying aligned vectors, but these were previously described in a suppressed publication.  
- Dataset is small (90 compounds) and compound selection criteria are unclear, making results fragile.  
- Impact of compound and component frequency on results is not analyzed or reported.  
- Unclear how many human judgments per compound and whether many compounds share identical compositionality scores, complicating Spearman correlation.  
- Introduction is strong, but some sections (notably 5.1) are hard to follow; running examples would improve clarity.  
- The term “constituent” is potentially misleading; “component” would be clearer for NN sequences.  
- Assumption that co-occurrence relies on the head being the component is flawed (e.g., “spelling bee”).  
- Framing dependency-path extraction as “packed anchored trees” (APTs) seems unnecessary and misnamed; elementary APTs are not clearly defined.  
- Table 2 omits that features of order >3 are discarded; clarification on feature elimination or incompatibility is needed and examples of incompatible types should be given.  
- Section 5’s description of contextual features and “elementary representation” needs concrete examples to be understandable.  
- Ambiguity over whether “compound phrase token” denotes the compound vector or the composed component vectors.  
- In Section 5.1, terminology around elementary APTs, “shifted PMI,” and feature removal is confusing and sometimes contradictory.  
- The meaning of “not properly aligned” is not explained—does it mean no alignment or partial alignment?  
- Paragraphs 558–563 are unclear about why overlap matters, especially for NMOD relations between components.  
- Paragraphs 575–580 are puzzling: higher-order dependency features are used elsewhere but similarity is computed with first-order features without sufficient justification.  
- Reported small positive effect of PPMI smoothing (α = 0.75) is not clearly supported by Table 3.  
- Optimal hyperparameters h and q (eqs. 8–9) are not reported, obscuring the contribution of hybridity.  
- Table 4 legend should explicitly state that results use the additive combination method.  
- Suggest including results from word2vec applied to compound phrase tokens.  
- Rationale for the FREQ baseline is unclear; claiming frequent compounds are more compositional may reflect dataset bias rather than a principled baseline.
