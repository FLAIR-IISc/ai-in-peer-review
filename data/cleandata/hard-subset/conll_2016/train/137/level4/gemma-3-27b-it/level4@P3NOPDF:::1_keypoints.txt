- Paper studies English compound compositionality using neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors, introducing “aligned” vectors to capture semantic shifts.  
- Syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, and a tuned mix of aligned/unaligned vectors gives only modest improvements.  
- Introduction is clear, but sections (notably 5.1) are hard to read and need illustrative examples.  
- Dataset is small, poorly described, and compound selection is unclear, making conclusions fragile and limiting generalizability.  
- The term “packed anchored tree” is unexplained and seems to rebrand standard dependency-path extraction.  
- Definition of “elementary APTs” (section 5.1) is opaque and needs clarification.  
- Table 2 should state that features of order >3 are discarded or explain absence of certain feature entries.  
- More details required about the Reddy et al. dataset: frequency ranges of compounds/components, number of judgments per compound, and potential score clusters that could bias Spearman correlation.  
- Recommend using “component” instead of “constituent” to avoid syntactic ambiguity.  
- The claim that constituents share co-occurrences with compounds mainly holds when the constituent is the head, not the modifier (example: “spelling bee”).  
- Section 5’s statement about elementary representations lacking compound-context features needs a concrete example and clarification of “compound phrase token.”  
- In 5.1, clarify whether an “elementary APT” is a (w, r, w’) triplet and whether “shifted PMI” refers to PMI in equation (3).  
- Sentence about removing low-positive-PMI features is contradictory and requires rephrasing for logical clarity.  
- Define “not properly aligned” versus “not aligned at all” when discussing APT overlap.  
- Paragraphs 558–563 are unclear on why overlap should be considerable when an NMOD relation exists.  
- Paragraphs 575–580 are puzzling: justify using higher-order dependency features generally but reverting to first-order features for the key similarity measurement.  
- Claim that PPMI smoothing (α=0.75) helps is not evident from Table 3; report optimal h and q values to quantify hybridity’s contribution.  
- Table legends should specify additive combination usage; report results using word2vec vectors for compound phrases.  
- Rationale for the FREQ baseline is unclear—why would higher-frequency compounds be more compositional, and does this reflect dataset bias?
