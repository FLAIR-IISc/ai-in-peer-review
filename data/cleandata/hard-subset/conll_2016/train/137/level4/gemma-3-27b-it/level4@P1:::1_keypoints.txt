- Paper studies predicting compositionality of English noun–noun compounds using vector-based similarity.  
- Uses an existing dataset of 90 compounds with human compositionality ratings (1–5).  
- Method: compute cosine similarity between a compound token vector and a composition of its component vectors.  
- Evaluation metric: Spearman rank correlation between similarity scores and human judgments.  
- Experiments compare neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors.  
- For count vectors, experiments test plain versus “aligned” vectors for the dependent component (alignment models dependent→head shift).  
- Results: syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings.  
- Results: aligned vectors alone perform worse than non-aligned vectors.  
- Results: a tuned hybrid of aligned and unaligned vectors yields only slight improvement.  
- Presentation: introduction is clear, but other sections (e.g., 5.1) are hard to follow and need rephrasing and examples.  
- Main concern: primary novelty (aligned vectors) was presented previously by the authors.  
- Main concern: dataset is small (90 items) and not sufficiently described, making findings fragile.  
- Need to report frequency ranges of compounds and their components, since frequency likely affects results.  
- Need to state how the 90 compounds were selected from the original dataset.  
- Need to report number of human judgments per compound and prevalence of tied compositionality scores (affects Spearman).  
- Terminology: prefer “component” over “constituent” to avoid syntactic ambiguity.  
- The intuition about constituent–compound co-occurrence is stronger when the constituent is the head; counterexamples exist (e.g., “spelling bee”).  
- Section 3 critique: term “packed anchored tree” is unclear, tree structure and “elementary APTs” need definition and examples.  
- Table 2 critique: paper omits that features are limited to order ≤3; clarify exclusion of incompatible types with examples.  
- Section 5 critique: give a running example to explain elementary representations and clarify “compound phrase token.”  
- Section 5.1 critique: clarify what an “elementary APT” is, whether “shifted PMI” matches Eq. (3), and resolve ambiguous/contradictory sentences.  
- Sections 5.1 paragraphs questioned: explain “not properly aligned,” why NMOD relation should show considerable overlap, and why higher-order features are used but similarity measured with first-order features.  
- Section 6 critique: smoothing effect of α=0.75 is not clearly supported by the table; report optimal h and q to assess hybridity contribution.  
- Clarify whether Table 4 uses additive combination and provide compound phrase vectors from word2vec as well.  
- Question rationale for the FREQ baseline and whether dataset bias explains why frequent compounds appear more compositional.
