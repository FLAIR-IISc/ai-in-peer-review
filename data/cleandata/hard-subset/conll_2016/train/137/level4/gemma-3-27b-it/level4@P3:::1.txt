## Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates methods for predicting the compositionality of English noun-noun compounds. The authors compare two main approaches: a novel integrated compositional distributional approach based on dependency-parsed representations (APT – Anchored Packed Trees) and applying simple composition operations to state-of-the-art neural word embeddings. The core innovation lies in the use of "aligned" dependency features, intended to account for syntactic structure during composition, though this was previously introduced in a suppressed reference. Experiments are conducted on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, evaluating performance using Spearman’s rank correlation between predicted compositionality scores and human judgments. Results indicate that the APT-based methods, particularly a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned features, slightly outperform neural embeddings, though improvements are modest.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation of Dependency-Based Composition:** The primary contribution is a systematic empirical evaluation of compositional methods leveraging dependency parsing for noun-noun compounds. While dependency-based distributional semantics isn’t novel, applying it specifically to *compositionality detection* and comparing it against strong neural embedding baselines provides valuable insight.
2. **Exploration of Aligned Dependency Features:** The investigation of aligned dependency features, while previously introduced, represents a focused effort to incorporate syntactic structure into the composition process.  The demonstration that a hybrid approach, balancing aligned and unaligned features, yields the best performance is a noteworthy finding.
3. **Replication and Extension of Reddy et al. (2011):** The work replicates and extends the benchmark established by Reddy et al. (2011), providing updated results and a comparison with a different compositional methodology. This builds on existing work in the field and provides a current performance baseline.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenge of evaluating compositional models in the presence of non-compositional phrases and frames compositionality detection as a valuable testbed.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The use of a standardized dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) and established evaluation metric (Spearman correlation) ensures comparability and reproducibility. The systematic variation of parameters within both the APT and neural embedding models demonstrates thoroughness.
3. **Insightful Comparison of Approaches:** The comparison between APT-based methods and neural embeddings offers valuable insight into the benefits of incorporating syntactic structure into compositional semantics. The finding that a hybrid approach performs best suggests a nuanced interplay between structured and distributional representations.
4. **Detailed Methodological Description:** The paper provides a relatively detailed description of the APT model and its various configurations, enabling other researchers to understand and potentially replicate the work.  The inclusion of example dependency paths (Table 1) is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Innovation:** The core innovation (aligned dependency features) was previously introduced in a “suppressed reference,” diminishing the novelty of the work.  The improvements over existing methods are marginal, raising concerns about the significance of the findings.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The small size of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds) limits the generalizability of the results. A more detailed characterization of the dataset, including frequency distributions of compounds and their components, is crucial for assessing the robustness of the findings. The potential for dataset bias (as suggested by the FREQ baseline) is not adequately addressed.
3. **Clarity and Readability:** Certain sections of the paper (particularly Section 5) are dense and difficult to follow. The use of specialized terminology (e.g., "elementary APTs") without sufficient explanation hinders understanding.  Providing concrete examples throughout the paper would greatly improve clarity.
4. **Unclear Justification for Design Choices:** Several design choices lack clear justification. For example, the decision to use first-order dependency features for the final similarity calculation (despite utilizing higher-order features during model construction) is not adequately explained. The rationale behind the optimal parameter values (h and q) is also missing.
5. **Insufficient Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying specific types of compounds where the proposed methods fail would provide valuable insights and directions for future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Dataset Details:** Please provide a detailed description of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, including the frequency distribution of compounds and their components.  Were there any specific criteria used for selecting the 90 compounds?
2. **Alignment Rationale:**  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of why aligning dependency features improves performance, particularly for noun-noun compounds? What specific syntactic phenomena are captured by this alignment process?
3. **First-Order Feature Choice:** Why were first-order dependency features used for the final similarity calculation, despite using higher-order features during model construction?
4. **Parameter Optimization:**  What is the reasoning behind the observed optimal values for the parameters *h* and *q* in equations 8 and 9?
5. **Error Analysis:**  Can you provide an analysis of the types of compounds where the proposed methods perform poorly? Are there any patterns or characteristics that distinguish these compounds from those that are correctly classified?

**Additional Comments:**

* The term "constituent" should be replaced with "component" throughout the paper to avoid ambiguity.
* Clarify whether "shifted PMI" refers to PMI as defined in equation (3).
* The sentence in Section 5.1 beginning "Removing features which tend to go with..." is unclear and should be rephrased.
* In Table 4, clarify that the results correspond to using the additive composition method in the legend.
* Consider including the results from the word2vec vectors *for the compound phrases* themselves in Table 4.
