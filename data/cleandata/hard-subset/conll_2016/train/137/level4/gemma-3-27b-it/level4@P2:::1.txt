The paper reports experiments on predicting the level of compositionality of compounds in English, utilizing a dataset of 90 compounds with compositionality scores ranging from 1 to 5, assigned by an unspecified number of human judges. The core methodology involves calculating the cosine similarity between a compound’s vector representation and the compositional combination of its component vectors, then evaluating this correlation with human judgments using Spearman’s rank correlation. Experiments manipulate the vector type (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors) and, for the latter, whether vectors are “aligned” to account for dependency relationships. Results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, aligned vectors perform worse than non-aligned vectors, and a carefully tuned combination of both yields a slight improvement.

The introduction is well-written, but some sections, particularly 5.1, are dense and could benefit from clearer explanations and illustrative examples. The primary contribution of the paper, as the authors present it, is the exploration of "aligned" vectors to improve compositionality prediction. However, this method was previously introduced in a suppressed reference by the same authors, diminishing its novelty. Furthermore, the dataset’s small size and limited description raise concerns, particularly regarding the potential impact of compound/component frequencies on the results. Given the marginal improvements achieved with aligned vectors on this small dataset, and uncertainties about compound selection criteria, the findings appear somewhat fragile.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1.  **Empirical Evaluation of Compositional Methods on a Targeted Dataset:** The paper provides a focused empirical comparison of different distributional methods (count-based vs. neural embeddings, aligned vs. non-aligned vectors) specifically for the task of compositionality detection in noun-noun compounds. This dataset-driven analysis is valuable, even if the gains are modest.
2.  **Demonstration of the Effectiveness of Syntactic-Context Count Vectors:** The consistent outperformance of syntactic-context count vectors over neural embeddings for this task reinforces the importance of incorporating syntactic information into compositional models.
3. **Highlighting the Benefit of Combining Aligned and Unaligned Dependency Features:** Showing that a hybrid approach yields slight gains over either aligned or unaligned representations alone suggests a useful direction for future research – exploiting both dependency-aware and dependency-agnostic compositional methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear Experimental Setup:** The experimental design is relatively straightforward and allows for a direct comparison of different approaches. The use of Spearman’s correlation is appropriate for evaluating the ranking of compositionality predictions against human judgments.
2.  **Thorough Exploration of Parameters:** The authors explore a range of parameters for both the APT model and the neural word embeddings, demonstrating a commitment to rigorous experimentation.
3. **Strong Baseline Comparisons**: The comparison to the previous state-of-the-art (Reddy et al.) and the inclusion of a frequency baseline provide valuable context for evaluating the improvements.
4. **Detailed Methodological Description**: The description of the APT construction and composition process, while sometimes dense, is ultimately quite thorough.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The core contribution – aligned vectors – was previously presented in a suppressed reference, limiting the paper’s novelty.
2.  **Small Dataset Size and Description:** The dataset of 90 compounds is relatively small, and the paper lacks a detailed description of its characteristics (frequency ranges, judgment agreement, etc.). This raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.
3. **Unclear Justification for Reliance on First-Order Features in Final Similarity Measurement**: The decision to use only first-order dependency features when calculating the similarity between composed and observed vectors (despite utilizing higher-order features in the earlier stages) is poorly explained and undermines the potential benefits of the higher-order representations.
4. **Dense and Difficult-to-Follow Explanations:** Sections such as 3 and 5.1 contain complex terminology and lack clear examples, making them challenging to understand.
5. **Potentially Confusing Terminology:** Terms like "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APTs" are not intuitively explained and add to the complexity of the paper.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the Reddy et al. dataset, specifically the frequency distribution of the compounds and their components? Was there any assessment of inter-annotator agreement for the compositionality judgments?
2.  Can you elaborate on the rationale for using only first-order dependency features when computing the final similarity score? Why are the higher-order features considered unreliable at this stage?
3.  Please provide a clear example illustrating the concept of "alignment" of vectors and how it impacts the composition process.
4. What are the optimal values for the 'h' and 'q' parameters in equations 8 and 9? Reporting these values would help in understanding the degree of "hybridity" contributing to performance gains.
5.  Could you share the results of applying the word2vec models directly to the compound phrases, rather than just their components? This could provide additional insight into the effectiveness of different approaches.

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding illustrative examples throughout the paper to clarify complex concepts.
*   Re-evaluate the necessity of the terms "packed anchored tree" and "elementary APTs," or provide more intuitive explanations.
*   The intuition behind the FREQ baseline is unclear. Please provide a more compelling explanation for why frequent compounds might be more compositional.
* The statement in Section 5.1, "Removing features which tend to go with lots of things...", is confusing and requires clarification.
