- Paper predicts compositionality of 90 English compounds by comparing compound token vectors to composed constituent vectors using cosine similarity and Spearman correlation with human judgments.

- Main experimental variation: neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors, with “aligned” vectors for dependents; syntactic count vectors outperform embeddings.

- Aligned vectors alone perform worse than unaligned, but a tuned combination yields only slight improvement.

- Primary novelty (aligned vectors) was previously published by the authors (suppressed reference), raising questions about contribution.

- Dataset is small (90 compounds), poorly described, and compound selection/frequency ranges are not reported, undermining result reliability.

- Number of human judges per compound and distribution of identical compositionality scores are not provided, complicating Spearman evaluation.

- Presentation issues: section 5.1 and others are hard to follow; running examples would greatly improve clarity.

- Terminology concerns: “packed anchored tree” seems unnecessary/confusing and should be justified; “elementary APTs” is unclear.

- Clarify what is meant by “tree” in packed anchored tree and give examples of “incompatible types” and discarded high-order features (e.g., order >3).

- Request to report component and compound frequency ranges, and to prefer the term “component” over “constituent” to avoid syntactic confusion.

- The intuition that a constituent’s literal use predicts compound co-occurrences holds mainly when the constituent is the head, not the dependent (example: “spelling bee”).

- Section 5: unclear references—explain “compound phrase token” and give a running example showing which contextual features are/aren’t included.

- Section 5.1: clarify whether elementary APTs are (w, r, w') triplets, whether “shifted PMI” matches equation (3), and reword confusing sentences about low positive PMI and “these phrases.”

- Define what “not properly aligned” means and clarify expected overlap between unaligned APTs; explain why NMOD relation might show considerable overlap (paras 558–563).

- Explain why higher-order dependency features are used throughout but similarity measurement reverts to first-order features at the critical stage (paras 575–580); Note 3’s rationale is insufficient.

- Section 6: the claim that smoothing PPMI with α=0.75 helps is not evident from Table 3—provide clearer evidence.

- Report optimal values for h and q in equations (8) and (9) to assess hybridity’s contribution to gains.

- Clarify Table 4 legend to state results use the additive combination.

- Provide results using word2vec vectors for compound phrases (missing).

- Question the rationale for the FREQ baseline—why would higher frequency indicate higher compositionality, and does this reveal dataset bias?
