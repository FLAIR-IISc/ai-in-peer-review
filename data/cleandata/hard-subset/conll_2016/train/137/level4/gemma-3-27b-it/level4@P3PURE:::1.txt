## Review of CONLL 2016 Paper

**General Comments**

The paper reports experiments on predicting the level of compositionality of English compounds. The dataset used is a pre-existing set of 90 compounds, with compositionality ranked from 1 to 5 by an unspecified number of judges. The core experimental approach involves computing the cosine similarity between the vector representation of the compound (treated as a single token) and a composition of the vectors representing its constituent components. Evaluation is performed using Spearman correlation between the cosine similarity and human judgments. The experiments vary in terms of the vectors used – neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors – and, for the latter, whether plain or “aligned” vectors are employed for the dependent component, with alignment aiming to capture a shift from the dependent to the head (as previously proposed in a suppressed reference). The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings. While aligned vectors alone perform less well than non-modified vectors, a carefully tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields a slight improvement.

Regarding the paper’s presentation, the introduction is well-written, but other sections (such as 5.1) are difficult to follow despite the underlying concepts being relatively straightforward. Incorporating running examples would significantly improve clarity.

In terms of substance, I have several concerns. The primary innovation appears to be the use of aligned vectors, but these were previously published in a “suppressed reference” by the authors. Furthermore, the dataset is small and insufficiently described. The range of frequencies likely impacts the results, and given the marginal improvements observed with aligned vectors, the findings seem fragile due to the small dataset size and unclear compound selection process.

**More Detailed Comments/Questions**

**Section 3**

I question the necessity of introducing the term "packed anchored tree." It appears to be a standard extraction of dependency paths between two lexical items, effectively an extension of established methods in syntactic distributional representations of words (e.g., Lin, 1998, which typically utilizes paths of length one or two, with collapsed prepositions). Further clarification is needed regarding why it’s referred to as a “tree.” What are “elementary APTs” (section 5.1)?

**Table 2:** Did you omit mentioning that features of order greater than 3 are discarded (which explains why, for instance, `NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ` does not appear in the bottom-left cell)? Or does this relate to the elimination of incompatible types you mention (an example of which would be helpful, as the current explanation is unclear)?

**Section 4:**

Since the Reddy et al. dataset is central to your work, a detailed explanation of the compound selection process is crucial. What are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components? Results are likely sensitive to these frequencies. How many judgments were provided for each compound? Are there many compounds with the same final compositionality score? This could pose a problem when ranking them for Spearman correlation.

The paper uses “constituent” for a component of the N N sequence. I suggest using “component” to avoid confusion with the linguistic term "phrase" (syntagm).

Regarding the statement “…the intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences,” this intuition holds strongly if the constituent is the *head* of the phrase, but is less reliable otherwise (e.g., “spelling bee” does not exhibit the distribution of “spelling”).

**Section 5**

“Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.” Please provide a running example to clarify which objects you’re referring to. Does “compound phrase token” refer to the merged components of the compound?

**Section 5.1**

I presume “elementary APTs” represent a triplet: target word *w* + dependency path *r* + other word *w'*? The name remains confusing. Please clarify whether “shifted PMI” refers to the PMI as defined in equation (3).

“Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.” Do “these phrases” co-refer with “things” here? The sentence seems contradictory and requires clarification.

“In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned.” What does “not properly aligned” mean? Do you mean not aligned at all?

I struggle to understand paragraphs 558 to 563. Why should potential overlap be considerable in the specific case of the NMOD relation between the two components?

Paragraph 575 to 580 is puzzling. Why does the paper employ higher-order dependency features throughout, only to revert to first-order features at the critical stage of measuring similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors? Note 3 attempts to address this, but the explanation for the unreliability of second-order paths in composed representations is unclear and requires further clarification.

**Section 6**

“Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect” does not appear evident from Table 3. What are the optimal values for *h* and *q* in equations 8 and 9? These are important for assessing the contribution of “hybridity” to the slight gains observed over the unaligned results.

The table legend could benefit from explicitly stating that the results in Table 4 correspond to using the additive combination.

Could you provide results from the word2vec vectors for the compound phrases?

I do not understand the rationale behind the FREQ baseline. Why would a frequent compound be more likely to be compositional? This suggests a potential bias within the dataset.
