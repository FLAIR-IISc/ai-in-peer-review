- Task and data: Predict compositionality of 90 English noun-noun compounds with human scores (1–5) from an unspecified number of annotators.  
- Method: Compute cosine similarity between compound vectors and composed component vectors, evaluate with Spearman correlation against human judgments.  
- Vector types tested: neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors; for counts, also test “aligned” vs. non-aligned vectors.  
- Main empirical finding: syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings on this task.  
- Alignment result: “Aligned” dependency-aware vectors perform worse than non-aligned vectors on this dataset.  
- Hybrid approach: A carefully tuned combination of aligned and unaligned representations yields a slight improvement over individual methods.  
- Contribution claim: Exploration of aligned vectors for compositionality prediction, but novelty is limited because the alignment method was previously introduced by the authors in a suppressed reference.  
- Dataset concerns: Small size (90 compounds) and sparse description (no frequency ranges or inter-annotator agreement) raise generalizability and robustness concerns.  
- Fragility of results: Marginal gains on a small, poorly-described dataset make findings appear fragile and sensitive to dataset selection and frequency effects.  
- Methodological inconsistency: Authors use higher-order features during APT construction but rely only on first-order dependency features for final similarity without clear justification.  
- Presentation issues: Sections (notably 3 and 5.1) are dense, use complex terminology, and lack illustrative examples, making them hard to follow.  
- Terminology confusion: Terms like “packed anchored tree” and “elementary APTs” are not intuitively explained and add unnecessary complexity.  
- Strength — experimental clarity: Experimental setup is straightforward and Spearman correlation is an appropriate evaluation metric.  
- Strength — parameter exploration: Authors thoroughly explore APT and neural embedding parameters, showing experimental rigor.  
- Strength — baselines: Strong baseline comparisons (including Reddy et al. and a frequency baseline) provide useful context.  
- Strength — method description: APT construction and composition are described in detail, though dense.  
- Weakness — limited novelty: Core method’s prior presentation reduces the paper’s originality.  
- Weakness — dataset description lacking: Missing details about compound/component frequencies and annotator agreement undermine confidence in results.  
- Weakness — unclear rationale for feature choices: No convincing explanation why higher-order features aren’t used in final similarity calculation.  
- Requests for authors: Provide compound/component frequency distributions and inter-annotator agreement statistics.  
- Requests for authors: Explain why only first-order dependency features are used for final similarity and why higher-order features are considered unreliable.  
- Requests for authors: Give a clear example of vector “alignment” and demonstrate its effect on composition.  
- Requests for authors: Report optimal values for parameters h and q (eqs. 8 and 9) to clarify the hybrid contribution.  
- Requests for authors: Show results using word2vec directly on whole compound phrases as an additional baseline.  
- Minor suggestions: Add illustrative examples, simplify or better motivate technical terms, clarify the intuition behind the FREQ baseline, and rewrite confusing statements (e.g., in Section 5.1).
