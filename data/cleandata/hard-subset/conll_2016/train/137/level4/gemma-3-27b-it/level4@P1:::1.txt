The paper reports experiments on predicting the level of compositionality of compounds in English. 
The dataset used is a previously existing set of 90 compounds, with their compositionality ranked from 1 to 5 by human annotators.
The general approach is to compute the cosine similarity between the vector of the compound (treated as a single token) and a composition of the vectors of its components.
Evaluation is performed using Spearman’s rank correlation between the cosine similarity and human judgments.
The experiments vary in the vectors used—neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors—and, in the latter case, whether plain or “aligned” vectors should be used for the dependent component of the compound. The alignment aims to capture a shift from the dependent to the head, as previously proposed by the authors in a separate work.
The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, that using aligned vectors alone performs less well than non-aligned vectors, and that a carefully tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors provides a slight improvement.
Regarding the paper’s presentation, I found the introduction well-written, but other sections (such as Section 5.1) are difficult to follow, despite the underlying concepts being relatively straightforward. Rephrasing with illustrative examples could be helpful.
Regarding the substance, I have several concerns:
- The primary innovation appears to be the use of aligned vectors, but this has already been presented in a previous publication by the authors.
- The dataset is small and not described in sufficient detail. In particular, the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components are likely to influence the results. Since the improvements achieved with aligned vectors are marginal and observed on a small dataset with an unclear compound selection process, I consider the findings to be somewhat fragile.

More detailed comments/questions:

Section 3
I don’t understand the need for the term “packed anchored tree.” It seems to be a straightforward extraction of dependency paths between lexical items, a common extension of traditional syntactic distributional representations of words (as in Lin, 1998, which typically uses paths of length one or two with collapsed prepositions). Further, what defines it as a “tree”? What are “elementary APTs” (Section 5.1)?
Table 2: You didn’t mention that you consider features only up to order 3, which explains why, for instance, `NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ` does not appear in the bottom-left cell of Table 2. Or does this relate to the elimination of incompatible types you mention, for which an example would be helpful?

Section 4:
Given the central role of the Reddy et al. dataset in your work, it's important to explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components? Variations in frequency ranges are likely to affect the results.
How many judgments were provided for each compound? Are there many compounds with the same final compositionality score? This could be problematic when ranking them for Spearman correlation.
You use “constituent” to refer to a component of the noun-noun sequence. I suggest using “component,” as “constituent” also refers to a phrase.
“... the intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase, it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences”: Note that this intuition is more accurate when the constituent is the head of the phrase, and less so otherwise (e.g., “spelling bee” does not exhibit the same distribution as “spelling”).

Section 5
“Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.” Please provide a running example to help the reader understand.
Does “compound phrase token” refer to the merged components of the compound?

Section 5.1
I presume “elementary APTs” are a triplet: target word *w* + dependency path *r* + other word *w’*? I find the name confusing.
Clarify whether “shifted PMI” refers to the PMI as defined in Equation (3).
“Removing features which tend to co-occur with many things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.” Does “these phrases” co-refer with “things” here? The sentence seems contradictory; please clarify.
“In general, we would expect little overlap between APTs that have not been properly aligned.” What does “not properly aligned” mean? Do you mean not aligned at all?
Paragraphs 558-563 are puzzling. Why should potential overlap be considerable in the specific case of the NMOD relation between the two components?
Paragraphs 575-580 are also confusing. Why does the paper use higher-order dependency features, only to switch to first-order features when measuring the crucial similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors? Note 3 attempts to explain this, but the rationale for the unreliability of second-order paths in the composed representations is unclear; please clarify.

Section 6
“Smoothing the PMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect.” This is not readily apparent from Table 3.
What are the optimal values for *h* and *q* in Equations 8 and 9? These are important to assess how much “hybridity” contributes to the slight gains compared to the unaligned results.
It seems the results in Table 4 correspond to using additive combination; this could be clarified in the table legend. Also, could you provide the results from the word2vec vectors for the compound phrases themselves?
I don’t understand the intuition behind the FREQ baseline. Why would a frequent compound tend to be compositional? This suggests a potential bias in the dataset.
