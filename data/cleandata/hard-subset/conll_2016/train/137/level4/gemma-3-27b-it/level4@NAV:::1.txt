Review- General comments
=============================
The paper investigates the prediction of compositionality in English compounds, utilizing a pre-existing dataset of 90 compounds with compositionality scores ranging from 1 to 5 (assigned by an unspecified number of human judges). The core experimental approach involves calculating the cosine similarity between the vector representation of a compound (treated as a single token) and the composition of vectors representing its constituent parts. Evaluation is based on Spearman correlation between these cosine similarities and human judgments. The experiments explore variations in vector representations – neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors – and, for the latter, the effect of “aligning” vectors to capture a shift between dependent and head components, a technique previously introduced in a suppressed publication by the authors. The results demonstrate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, and that while using aligned vectors alone yields poorer results than unaligned vectors, a carefully tuned combination of both provides a marginal improvement.

Regarding the paper’s presentation, the introduction is well-written, but certain sections (e.g., Section 5.1) are difficult to follow despite the underlying concepts being relatively straightforward. Incorporating illustrative examples would likely enhance clarity.  

Substantively, my primary concerns relate to the novelty of the work and the robustness of the findings. The key innovation appears to be the application of aligned vectors, but this technique was previously presented in a "suppressed reference" by the same authors. Furthermore, the dataset is small and lacks detailed description. The influence of component word frequencies on results is a potential confounding factor. Given the marginal improvements achieved with aligned vectors and the dataset’s limited size, coupled with uncertainties regarding the compound selection process, I consider the paper’s conclusions to be relatively fragile.

More detailed comments/questions
================================
Section 3
The rationale for introducing the term "packed anchored tree" is unclear. It appears to be simply an extraction of dependency paths between lexical items, aligning with established techniques in syntactic distributional representations of words (e.g., Lin, 1998, which often employs paths of length one or two, with collapsed prepositions). What defines this as a "tree," and what constitutes "elementary APTs" (Section 5.1)?

Table 2 omits the mention that features of order greater than 3 were discarded (explaining why, for example, NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ is absent from the bottom-left cell). Is this omission related to the elimination of incompatible types mentioned in the text? If so, an example would improve understanding.

Section 4:
Given the centrality of the Reddy et al. dataset, it’s crucial to detail the compound selection process. What are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components? Results are likely sensitive to these distributions.  How many judgments were collected for each compound? How many compounds share the same compositionality score, and does this pose a challenge when calculating Spearman correlation? 

The use of “constituent” to refer to a component of an N N sequence is potentially misleading; “component” would be more precise, as “constituent” also implies a phrasal structure.  The statement regarding co-occurrence suggests a stronger relationship for the head of the phrase: "...the intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences" is more accurate when the constituent *is* the head (e.g., “spelling bee” doesn’t exhibit the distribution of "spelling" alone).

Section 5
“Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.” Providing a concrete example would aid reader comprehension. Does "compound phrase token" refer to the combined vector of the compound’s components?

Section 5.1
Are “elementary APTs” understood as a triplet consisting of a target word, a dependency path, and another word? The term remains confusing. Please clarify if “shifted PMI” corresponds to the PMI definition in Equation (3). 

“Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.”  Do “these phrases” refer to “things” here? The sentence appears contradictory and requires clarification.

“In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned.” Does “not properly aligned” mean *not* aligned at all?  I find paragraphs 558-563 difficult to interpret. Why should overlap be particularly considerable in the case of the NMOD relation between components? 

Paragraphs 575-580 are puzzling. Why does the paper utilize higher-order dependency features generally, yet revert to first-order features when calculating the crucial similarity metric? Note 3 offers an explanation, but the rationale for the unreliability of second-order paths in composed representations remains unclear.

Section 6
“Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect” is not immediately evident from Table 3. What are the optimal values for *h* and *q* in Equations 8 and 9? These are important for quantifying the contribution of “hybridity” to the slight gains over unaligned results.

Table 4’s legend could benefit from explicitly stating that the results correspond to the additive combination method. Furthermore, including the results from word2vec vectors for the compound phrases would be valuable.

I do not fully understand the rationale behind the FREQ baseline. Why would a frequent compound be more likely to be compositional? This potentially indicates a dataset bias.
