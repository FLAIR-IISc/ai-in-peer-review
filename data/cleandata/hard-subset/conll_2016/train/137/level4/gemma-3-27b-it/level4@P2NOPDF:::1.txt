The paper investigates the prediction of compositionality in English compounds, utilizing a dataset of 90 compounds with compositionality ratings from 1 to 5. The core approach involves computing the cosine similarity between vector representations of the compound (treated as a single token) and those derived from composing the vectors of its constituent parts. The study explores different vector types – neural embeddings and syntactic-context count vectors – and, for the latter, variations using “aligned” vectors designed to capture semantic shifts between compound components, previously introduced in a non-publicly available work by the authors. Results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings, with a tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yielding a slight improvement over either alone.

The introduction is well-written; however, some sections, such as 5.1, are difficult to follow despite the concepts being relatively straightforward. Incorporating running examples would significantly improve clarity.

The primary contribution appears to be the application of aligned vectors, though these were previously described in a suppressed publication. The small dataset size and limited description raise concerns about the robustness of the findings. Specifically, the impact of compound and component frequencies on the results is unclear. Given the marginal improvements observed with aligned vectors, and the dataset’s limitations – particularly regarding compound selection criteria – the conclusions feel somewhat fragile.

**Detailed Comments/Questions:**

**Section 3:**

The introduction of “packed anchored trees” (APTs) seems unnecessary. It appears to be a standard extraction of dependency paths between lexical items, aligning with established techniques in syntactic distributional representations (e.g., Lin, 1998), which often utilize paths of length one or two, with collapsed prepositions. Why is it framed as a “tree,” and what are “elementary APTs” (section 5.1)?

Table 2 omits the detail that features of order greater than 3 are discarded, which explains the absence of certain entries like NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ. Clarification is needed; is this due to feature elimination or incompatibility issues? An illustrative example of incompatible types would be helpful.

**Section 4:**

Given the centrality of the Reddy et al. dataset, a detailed explanation of the compound selection process is crucial. What are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their individual components? Variations in frequency are likely to influence the results. 

How many judgments were obtained for each compound? Are there numerous compounds sharing the same compositionality score? This could pose challenges when ranking them for Spearman correlation calculation.

The use of “constituent” to describe a component of the N N sequence is potentially confusing; “component” is more appropriate, as “constituent” can also refer to a syntactic phrase. 

The statement regarding co-occurrence relies on the head of the phrase being the constituent in question. This isn't universally true (e.g., "spelling bee").

**Section 5:**

The sentence about contextual features only being present in the “elementary representation” if they co-occurred with the constituent in other contexts requires a concrete example to aid comprehension.

Is “compound phrase token” intended to represent the combined vectors of the compound’s components?

**Section 5.1:**

Are “elementary APTs” triplets consisting of a target word, a dependency path, and another word? The terminology remains confusing.

Clarify whether “shifted PMI” refers to the PMI calculation defined in equation (3).

The sentence about removing low-positive PMI features (“Removing features which tend to go with lots of things…”) is contradictory. Clarify the relationship between "these phrases" and "things."

What does “not properly aligned” signify? Does it imply a complete lack of alignment?

Paragraphs 558-563 are unclear. Why is a potential overlap particularly significant in the case of NMOD relations between compound components?

Paragraphs 575-580 are puzzling. The paper utilizes higher-order dependency features but then reverts to first-order features for the critical similarity measurement. Note 3 attempts to justify this, but the explanation regarding the unreliability of second-order paths in composed representations is insufficient and requires further clarification.

**Section 6:**

The reported “small positive effect” of smoothing the PPMI calculation with α = 0.75 (line 663) isn’t readily apparent from Table 3.

What are the optimal values for *h* and *q* in equations 8 and 9? These values are essential for gauging the contribution of “hybridity” to the observed gains over unaligned results.

The table legend should explicitly state that the results in Table 4 correspond to using the additive combination method.

Consider including results from word2vec vectors applied to the compound phrases.

The rationale behind the FREQ baseline is unclear. Why would frequent compounds be more likely to be compositional? This might indicate a bias within the dataset.
