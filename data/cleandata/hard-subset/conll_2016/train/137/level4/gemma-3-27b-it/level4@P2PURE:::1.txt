The paper reports experiments on predicting the degree of compositionality of English compounds. The experiments utilize a pre-existing dataset of 90 compounds, each assigned a compositionality score from 1 to 5 by an unspecified number of human judges. The core methodology involves calculating the cosine similarity between the vector representation of the compound (treated as a single token) and a composition of the vectors representing its constituent parts. Evaluation is conducted using Spearman correlation between these cosine similarities and the human compositionality judgments. The study explores variations in both the vector representations used – neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors – and, for the latter, whether to employ “aligned” vectors for the dependent component of the compound. This alignment strategy, previously introduced by the authors in a non-publicly available work, aims to model a semantic shift from the dependent to the head. 

Results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings. Utilizing aligned vectors alone yields poorer performance than using unaligned vectors, while a carefully tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors achieves a modest improvement.

Regarding the paper's presentation, the introduction is well-written, but other sections, such as Section 5.1, are difficult to follow despite the underlying concepts being relatively straightforward. Incorporating illustrative examples could significantly improve clarity.

Concerning the substance of the work, my primary concerns are as follows: the claimed novelty resides largely in the use of aligned vectors, which have already been presented in a previous, unpublished work by the authors. Furthermore, the dataset’s small size and limited description raise concerns. Specifically, the impact of differing term frequencies on the results is unclear. Given the marginal improvements observed with aligned vectors, coupled with the small dataset size and uncertainty surrounding compound selection, I view the findings as potentially fragile.

**More Detailed Comments/Questions**

**Section 3**

The rationale behind the term “packed anchored tree” is unclear. It appears to be a straightforward extraction of dependency paths between lexical items – a common practice in syntactic distributional representations of words (as seen, for example, in Lin, 1998, which often uses paths of length one or two, with collapsed prepositions).  Furthermore, why is it referred to as a “tree”? What are “elementary APTs” as described in Section 5.1?

Table 2 omits the detail that features of order greater than 3 are discarded (explaining, for instance, why NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ does not appear in the bottom-left cell). Alternatively, this omission might relate to the elimination of incompatible types, for which a clarifying example would be helpful.

**Section 4**

Given the central role of the Reddy et al. dataset, a detailed explanation of the 90 compounds’ selection process is crucial. Providing frequency ranges for the compounds and their components is necessary, as results are likely sensitive to these ranges. How many judgments were obtained for each compound? Are there many compounds sharing the same final compositionality score? If so, this could pose a challenge when computing the Spearman correlation.

The use of “constituent” to denote a component of the N N sequence should be replaced with “component,” as “constituent” commonly refers to a phrase or syntagm. 

The statement “…the intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences” is accurate primarily when the constituent is the head of the phrase; it holds less true otherwise (e.g., “spelling bee” does not exhibit the same distribution as “spelling”).

**Section 5**

“Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.” Please provide a concrete example to illustrate which object is being discussed.  Does “compound phrase token” refer to the combined vector of the compound’s components?

**Section 5.1**

Are “elementary APTs” defined as a triplet consisting of a target word *w*, a dependency path *r*, and another word *w'*?  The term remains confusing. Clarify whether “shifted PMI” refers to the PMI calculation as defined in Equation (3).

“Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.” Do “these phrases” co-refer with “things” here? The sentence appears contradictory and requires clarification.

“In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned.” What constitutes “not properly aligned” – does this mean not aligned at all?  I find paragraphs 558-563 difficult to understand.

Why should overlap be considerable specifically in cases of an NMOD relation between the two components?

Paragraphs 575-580 are puzzling. Why does the paper employ higher-order dependency features throughout, only to revert to first-order features when measuring the crucial similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors?  Note 3 attempts to explain this, but the rationale for the unreliability of second-order paths in the composed representations remains unclear.

**Section 6**

“Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect” is not immediately evident from Table 3. 

What are the optimal values for *h* and *q* in Equations 8 and 9? These parameters are important for assessing the contribution of “hybridity” to the slight gains over the unaligned results.

Table 4’s legend should explicitly state that the results correspond to the additive combination method. 

Could you also provide the results from the word2vec vectors for the compound phrases? 

I do not understand the motivation behind the FREQ baseline. Why would frequent compounds tend to be compositional? This suggests a possible bias within the dataset.
