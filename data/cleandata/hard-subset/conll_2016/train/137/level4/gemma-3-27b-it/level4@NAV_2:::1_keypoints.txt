- Task: Predict compositionality of 90 English compounds rated 1–5 by humans.  
- Method: Compare cosine similarity between compound vectors and composed component vectors using neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors.  
- Method variation: Evaluate effect of aligning vectors to capture dependencies vs. leaving them unaligned.  
- Main result: Syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings.  
- Main result: Alignment alone performs worse than unmodified vectors; a tuned hybrid of aligned and unaligned vectors yields slight improvement.  
- Dataset concern: Dataset is small (90 items) with unclear selection criteria and unknown frequency ranges, limiting generalizability.  
- Novelty concern: Core innovation (aligned vectors) appears previously published by the authors, reducing novelty.  
- Methodological concern: Several choices lack justification—e.g., discarding higher-order dependency features when computing similarity.  
- Fragility: Marginal improvements and small dataset make findings appear fragile.  
- Readability issue: Sections (especially 3 and 5.1) are dense and hard to follow; notation and concepts need clearer explanations and examples.  
- Strength: Systematic, thorough exploration of compositional methods and parameter settings.  
- Strength: Thoughtful use of higher-order dependency paths to incorporate syntactic information.  
- Strength: Clear research question and appropriate evaluation framework (human judgments, Spearman correlation).  
- Requested clarifications: novelty vs. prior work, detailed dataset statistics (frequency distributions), rationale for first-order similarity restriction, concrete alignment examples/visuals, and reported optimal h and q parameter values.
