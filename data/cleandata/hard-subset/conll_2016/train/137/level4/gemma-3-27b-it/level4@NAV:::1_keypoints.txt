- Paper studies predicting compositionality of 90 English compounds using cosine similarity between a compound token vector and a composition of its constituents.  
- Dataset has 90 compounds with scores 1–5 but lacks details on number of human judges and selection criteria.  
- Main methods compared: neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors, plus an “alignment” technique previously published by the authors.  
- Syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings in the experiments.  
- Aligned vectors alone perform worse than unaligned vectors; a tuned hybrid of aligned+unaligned gives only marginal improvement.  
- Key innovation (aligned vectors) is not novel given the authors’ prior suppressed publication.  
- Small dataset size and underspecified compound selection make conclusions fragile.  
- Component word frequency is a potential confound that is not controlled or reported.  
- Presentation issues: Section 5.1 and other parts are hard to follow and would benefit from concrete examples.  
- Terminology concern: “packed anchored tree” is unclear and seems equivalent to standard dependency-path extraction; need definition of “elementary APTs.”  
- Table 2 omits that features of order >3 were discarded; this omission and relation to incompatible types needs clarification and examples.  
- Section 4 needs compound and component frequency ranges, number of judgments per compound, and distribution of scores (ties affecting Spearman).  
- Use of “constituent” is potentially misleading; “component” is more precise for NN sequences, and head-dependent distributional differences should be noted.  
- Section 5: clarify whether “compound phrase token” means the compound’s combined vector and give an example showing why constituent vectors lack compound contextual features.  
- Section 5.1: clarify if an “elementary APT” is a (target, dependency path, word) triplet and whether “shifted PMI” equals Equation (3).  
- Several sentences in Section 5.1 are confusing or contradictory (e.g., about removing low-PMI features) and need rewriting.  
- Clarify what “not properly aligned” means and why overlap should be considerable for NMOD relations (paragraphs 558–563 are unclear).  
- Explain why higher-order dependency features are used generally but only first-order features are used for the key similarity metric (paragraphs 575–580 and Note 3 need stronger justification).  
- Section 6: the claimed positive effect of PPMI smoothing (α=0.75) is not obvious from Table 3 and requires more evidence.  
- Report optimal values for h and q (Equations 8 and 9) to quantify hybridity’s contribution.  
- Table 4 should state the combination method (additive) explicitly and include word2vec results for compound phrase vectors.  
- The FREQ baseline rationale is unclear and may indicate a dataset bias toward frequent compounds being labeled more compositional.
