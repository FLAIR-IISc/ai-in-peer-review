- Study predicts compound compositionality using cosine similarity between compound token vectors and composed component vectors, evaluated by Spearman correlation.
- Experiments compare neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors, and use an alignment technique for dependent components.
- Syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings; aligned vectors alone hurt performance while a tuned hybrid of aligned/unaligned vectors yields slight gains.
- Main novelty (aligned vectors) appears already introduced in the authors’ prior suppressed work, reducing apparent originality.
- Dataset is small (90 compounds), insufficiently described, and likely too limited to support the claimed effects.
- Selection criteria and frequency ranges of compounds/components are not reported, though frequency likely affects results.
- Number of human judgments per compound is unspecified; many tied scores could undermine Spearman ranking reliability.
- Terminology issues: "constituent" is misleading for N-N components and "packed anchored tree" seems unnecessary and confusing.
- Definition and role of “elementary APTs” are unclear and need explicit definition or examples.
- Table 2 appears to exclude features of order >3; this exclusion and the elimination of incompatible types need concrete examples.
- The claim that literal use of a constituent implies shared co-occurrences is valid for heads but not for dependents (e.g., "spelling" vs. "spelling bee").
- Section 5 contains opaque statements (e.g., what “compound phrase token” denotes) and would benefit from running examples.
- Ambiguity about whether "shifted PMI" refers to the PMI in Equation (3); terminology should be clarified.
- The sentence about removing low-positive-PMI features is confusing and possibly contradictory; unclear referents need fixing.
- Phrases like “not properly aligned” are ambiguous—does this mean “unaligned” or misaligned?
- Paragraphs 558–563 lack clear reasoning for why significant overlap would occur specifically for the NMOD relation.
- Paragraphs 575–580 are puzzling: paper uses higher-order dependency features but similarity evaluation uses only first-order features, and the justification for this is insufficient.
- The claimed small positive effect from smoothing PPMI with α = 0.75 is not clearly supported by Table 3.
- Optimal hyperparameters h and q for the hybrid method are not reported, yet are crucial to interpret the hybrid gains.
- Table 4 likely shows additive combination results but the legend lacks this information; clarity on combination method is required.
- Results for compound phrases using word2vec vectors are absent and should be provided for comparison.
- The FREQ baseline’s intuition is unclear—why would more frequent compounds be more compositional? This suggests potential dataset bias.
- Overall presentation is uneven: a clear introduction but other sections (notably 5.1) are hard to follow and need examples and clearer notation.
- Given marginal improvements, prior-art overlap, small/poorly-described dataset, and unclear methodology details, the findings appear fragile.
