General comments
The paper presents experiments on predicting the degree of compositionality of English compounds. The dataset is a previously published set of 90 compounds whose compositionality was rated on a 1–5 scale by an unspecified number of judges. In each experiment, the authors compute the cosine similarity between (i) a vector for the compound treated as a single token and (ii) a composed vector derived from its components. Evaluation uses Spearman correlation between these cosine similarities and human judgments.

The experiments vary along two axes:
- The vector representations: neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors.
- For the count vectors, whether to use plain (unaligned) vectors or “aligned” vectors for the dependent component, where alignment is intended to capture a shift from the dependent to the head. These alignments were introduced in a previous (suppressed) reference by the authors.

The results indicate that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings; using only aligned vectors performs worse than unmodified vectors; and a highly tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields a slight improvement.

As for presentation, the introduction is clear and well written, but other parts (e.g., Section 5.1) are hard to follow despite the underlying concepts being relatively straightforward. Rephrasing with running examples would help considerably.

Substantively, I have several concerns:
- Relative to Reddy et al., the main novelty appears to be the use of aligned vectors, but these were already published by the authors in the (suppressed) prior work.
- The dataset is small and insufficiently described. Frequency ranges are likely to affect results.
Given that the gains from aligned vectors are marginal, the dataset is small, and the selection of compounds is not clearly documented, I find the paper’s empirical findings fragile.

More detailed comments and questions

Section 3
- I do not see the need for the new term “packed anchored tree.” It appears to be a straightforward extraction of dependency paths between two lexical items—a direct extension of standard syntactic distributional word representations (as far back as Lin, 1998), which typically use paths of length one or two (often with collapsed prepositions).
- Why is it called a “tree”? What exactly are “elementary APTs” (as referenced in Section 5.1)?
- Table 2: Did you intend to state that you discard features of order greater than 3? Is that why, for example, NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ does not appear in the leftmost bottom cell? Alternatively, is this absence due to your elimination of “incompatible types”? If so, please provide a concrete example of such incompatibility; the current explanation is unclear.

Section 4
- Because the Reddy et al. dataset is central to your study, please explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency ranges for the compounds and their components? Results could vary substantially with frequency.
- How many judgments were collected per compound? Are there many compounds with identical final compositionality scores? If so, does that create ties that affect the Spearman correlation?
- You appear to use “constituent” to refer to a component of the NN compound. I recommend “component,” since “constituent” can also mean a phrasal unit in syntax.
- The intuition that “if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences” is generally valid for the head, but much less so for the modifier (e.g., “spelling bee” does not share the distribution of “spelling”).

Section 5
- “Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.” Please provide a running example to clarify each object being referenced. Also, does “compound phrase token” mean the merged surface token of the compound?

Section 5.1
- I infer that “elementary APTs” are tuples of the form target word w + dependency path r + other word w′. If so, the terminology is confusing; please clarify.
- Please state explicitly whether “shifted PMI” refers to PMI as defined in Equation (3).
- “Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.” Do “these phrases” co-refer with “things” here? The sentence reads as contradictory; please clarify the intended meaning.
- “In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned.” What does “not properly aligned” mean—no alignment at all, or an incorrect alignment? I do not follow the argument in lines 558–563. Why should the potential overlap be “considerable” specifically for the NMOD relation between the two components?
- Lines 575–580 are puzzling. The paper builds representations with higher-order dependency features but, at the critical step of measuring similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors, only first-order features are used. Note 3 is meant to justify this, but I still do not understand why second-order paths in the composed representations are deemed unreliable. Please clarify.

Section 6
- “Smoothing the PPMI calculation with α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect” is not obvious from Table 3.
- What are the optimal values for h and q in Equations (8) and (9)? These are important to assess how much “hybridity” contributes to the small gains over unaligned vectors.
- In Table 4, it appears results are based on the add combination; stating this explicitly in the caption or text would help.
- Could you also report results from word2vec vectors for the compound tokens themselves?
- I do not see the intuition behind the FREQ baseline: why should a more frequent compound tend to be more compositional? This may suggest a dataset bias.
