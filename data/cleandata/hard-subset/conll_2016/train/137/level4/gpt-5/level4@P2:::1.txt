Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper evaluates methods for predicting the compositionality of English noun–noun compounds using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds with human scores on a 0–5 scale. The approach compares the cosine similarity between (i) a holistic vector of the compound (treated as a single token) and (ii) a composed vector from its components, and correlates this similarity with human judgments via Spearman’s ρ.
- Two families of representations are compared: neural embeddings (word2vec) and syntactic-context count vectors based on typed dependency paths (APTs). For APTs, the paper examines both “aligned” composition (offsetting the modifier’s space to the head’s space) and unaligned composition, as well as a weighted hybrid of the two.
- Main empirical findings as I see them:
  1) Typed dependency count vectors outperform neural embeddings on this task.
  2) Unaligned APT composition generally outperforms aligned composition.
  3) A carefully tuned hybrid of aligned and unaligned APTs yields a small additional gain.

Contributions (as assessed by the reviewer)
- Primary: An empirical comparison showing that typed dependency features (and their compositions) are strong predictors of compound compositionality, surpassing neural embeddings and the Reddy et al. baseline.
- Secondary: A demonstration that alignment (as defined in prior work by the authors) is not superior to unaligned composition for noun–noun compounds, while a hybrid yields a marginal improvement.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: compositionality detection is an appropriate testbed for compositional models and avoids confounding with phrase similarity judgments.
- Empirical thoroughness in the space considered: multiple parameter settings for word2vec and for APT composition (including weighting parameters h and q) are explored; results are reported with statistical significance considerations.
- Useful practical finding: simple unaligned composition of typed dependency vectors is robust and competitive; a hybrid with alignment yields a modest boost.
- The paper confirms and extends earlier results by showing improved performance over Reddy et al. using the same corpus (ukWaC), suggesting that typed dependencies and model choices matter.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The central technical ingredient distinguishing this work from Reddy et al.—the alignment of APTs—has appeared in prior (suppressed) work by the same authors. Here, alignment underperforms unaligned composition, and the hybrid gain is small, which weakens the claimed innovation.
- Dataset limitations and under-specification: The dataset is small (90 items), and the paper does not sufficiently describe item selection, frequency ranges of compounds and components, or the number of annotators and agreement/ties. Given this size and potential frequency effects, the findings feel fragile.
- Clarity and terminology: Sections (notably 5.1) are hard to follow despite relatively simple underlying ideas. Key terms (e.g., “anchored packed trees,” “elementary APTs,” “tree,” “properly aligned”) are introduced without sufficiently clear definitions or illustrative running examples, which impedes comprehension.
- Methodological inconsistency: The approach emphasizes higher-order dependency features, yet the final similarity between composed and observed representations is computed using only first-order features. The rationale provided is not fully convincing and needs clearer justification.
- Baselines and intuition: The FREQ baseline’s motivation (frequency implies compositionality) is not well justified and may reflect dataset bias; more discussion is needed.

Questions to the authors
- APT definitions and terminology:
  • Why introduce the term “anchored packed trees”? How does it differ from standard dependency-path-based features used in syntactic distributional models (e.g., Lin, 1998)? Why “tree”?  
  • What exactly are “elementary APTs”? Please provide a precise definition and a simple running example.
- Feature spaces:
  • In Table 2, do you cap path length (e.g., at 3)? If so, please state it explicitly. If some co-occurrence types are eliminated as “incompatible,” provide concrete examples to clarify the constraint τ ∈ R*R* and the reduction rule.
- Dataset description:
  • How were the 90 compounds selected? What are the frequency ranges of compounds and their components?  
  • How many human judgments per compound? How were ties handled in Spearman correlation? Please report inter-annotator agreement or variance if available.
  • You use “constituent” for the two nouns; consider “component” to avoid ambiguity with the syntactic sense of “constituent.”
- Composition and alignment:
  • Please clarify “not properly aligned”: do you mean “not aligned at all”?  
  • Why should NMOD lead to substantial overlap between unaligned modifier and head spaces? A small illustrative example would help.
  • You argue that higher-order features are central, yet you restrict evaluation similarity to first-order features. The note explains that composed higher-order paths may be unreliable because elementary APTs stop at order 2, but this is not entirely clear. Could you elaborate on why including 2nd-order paths in similarity is harmful, and provide empirical evidence or ablations?
- PMI and smoothing:
  • Does “shifted PMI” refer to subtracting a constant from PMI in Eq. (3)? Please specify the shift and its relation to negative sampling.  
  • The sentence “Removing features which tend to go with lots of things … these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts” is hard to parse. What does “these phrases” refer to? Please rephrase and provide a concrete example.
- Results reporting:
  • The benefit of α = 0.75 over α = 1 is described as generally positive; this is not obvious from Table 3. Could you quantify the average gain and its significance?  
  • What are the optimal h and q values for the best APT settings? Please report them (ranges are mentioned later but concrete values per best system would help assess the hybrid’s contribution).  
  • Table 4 seems to use additive composition for embeddings; please state this explicitly in the caption.  
  • Could you also report performance when composing word2vec vectors for compounds themselves (i.e., embeddings trained for the merged compound tokens), to parallel the APT setting?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Readability: Sections 5 and 5.1 would benefit from a running example tracing a single compound (e.g., spelling bee) through preprocessing, elementary APT construction, alignment, and composition. Also clarify “compound phrase token” (i.e., the merged compound treated as one lexical item) and explicitly distinguish compound features from component features.
- The finding that frequency correlates strongly with compositionality (FREQ baseline) should be discussed as a potential dataset bias; consider controlling for frequency or reporting partial correlations.
