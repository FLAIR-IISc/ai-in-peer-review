General comments

This paper presents experiments on estimating the compositionality of English compound nouns. The study uses an existing dataset of 90 compounds with human ratings of compositionality on a 1–5 scale (number of annotators not specified). Each experiment computes the cosine similarity between a vector for the compound (treated as a single token) and a vector obtained by composing the vectors of its two components. Performance is reported as the Spearman correlation between these similarities and the human judgments.

The experiments vary:
- the underlying representations: neural word embeddings versus count-based vectors with syntactic contexts
- for the latter, whether to use “plain” (unaligned) or “aligned” representations for the modifier, where alignment is intended to transform the modifier’s space to the head’s space; this alignment mechanism was introduced in an earlier (suppressed) reference.

The results show that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings; using aligned vectors alone underperforms the unmodified vectors, while a carefully tuned mix of aligned and unaligned vectors yields a small additional gain.

The introduction is clear and well motivated, but other sections (e.g., 5.1) are harder to follow than necessary, despite relatively straightforward underlying ideas. More continuous worked examples would improve readability.

Substantively, I have several concerns:
- The novelty over Reddy et al. appears to center on alignment; however, the alignment mechanism itself has appeared previously (in a suppressed reference by the authors).
- The dataset is small and under-described. Frequency ranges are likely to substantially influence outcomes.
Given that the gains from alignment are slight, obtained on a small set whose selection procedure is unclear, the empirical conclusions feel quite fragile.

More detailed comments/questions

Section 3
- The need for a new name “anchored packed tree” is unclear. The method seems to extract dependency paths between lexical items, i.e., a direct extension of standard syntactic distributional features (going back at least to Lin, 1998) from first- to higher-order paths (with collapsed prepositions).
- Why call it a “tree”? What precisely are “elementary APTs” (as used in section 5.1)?
- Table 2: Did you truncate features above order 3? For instance, is that why NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ is absent in the lower-left cell? Or is this due to your “elimination of incompatible co-occurrence types”? Please provide an explicit example of such incompatibility, as the current explanation is hard to parse.

Section 4
- Since the Reddy et al. dataset is central, please explain how the 90 compounds were chosen. What are the frequency ranges of compounds and their components? Results likely depend on these ranges.
- How many annotators rated each compound? Are there many identical final scores across items? If so, does tie prevalence affect the Spearman correlation?
- You seem to use “constituent” for a component of the NN compound; “component” may be clearer, as “constituent” also commonly denotes a phrase/syntagm.
- The stated intuition that a component used literally should share contexts with the compound is reliable for the head, but much less so for the modifier (e.g., spelling bee does not share the distribution of spelling).

Section 5
- “The elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.” A running example would help track which object is being referred to at each step.
- Does “compound phrase token” mean the recombined merged token of the two components?

Section 5.1
- If “elementary APTs” are triples (target word w, dependency path r, context word w′), please state so explicitly; the current terminology is potentially confusing.
- Please clarify whether “shifted PMI” refers to PMI as defined in equation (3) with an additive shift (as in Levy et al., 2015).
- The sentence “Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.” is confusing. What does “these phrases” refer to—compounds or contexts? As written, it seems contradictory; please rewrite to make the causal mechanism explicit.
- “Not properly aligned”: do you mean “not aligned at all”? Please clarify.
- The claim that there may be considerable overlap without alignment specifically for NMOD needs justification; the preceding discussion suggests little overlap across unaligned APTs in general. Why is NMOD an exception?
- The decision to compute similarity using only first-order features, despite modeling higher-order contexts throughout, is puzzling. Footnote 3 does not fully explain why second-order paths in composed vectors are “unreliable,” while first-order paths are acceptable. Please elaborate.

Section 6
- “Smoothing the PPMI calculation with α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect” is not obviously supported by Table 3; please point to where the effect is consistent or temper the claim.
- What were the optimal h and q values in equations (8) and (9)? These are crucial to understanding how much the hybridization contributes beyond the unaligned model.
- Table 4 results appear to use additive composition; it would help to say this in the caption.
- Could you also report results using word2vec vectors for the compounds themselves (i.e., treating each compound as a token in the embedding model)?
- The intuition behind the FREQ baseline is unclear. Why should more frequent compounds be more compositional? If true here, that may indicate a dataset bias; please comment.
