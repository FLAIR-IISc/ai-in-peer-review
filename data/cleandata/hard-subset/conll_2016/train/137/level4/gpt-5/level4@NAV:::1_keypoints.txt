- Task and evaluation: predicts compositionality of English compounds by comparing compound token vectors to composed component vectors, evaluated with Spearman correlation against human ratings.

- Experimental factors: compares neural embeddings vs syntactic-context count-based vectors, and plain vs “aligned” vectors for the dependent component.

- Main results: syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings.

- Main results: aligned vectors alone underperform unmodified vectors; a heavily tuned hybrid of aligned+unaligned yields only modest gains.

- Presentation: introduction is clear, but several sections (notably 5.1) are hard to follow; running examples would improve readability.

- Novelty concern: alignment method appears previously published by the authors, limiting novelty over Reddy et al.

- Dataset size/description concern: dataset of 90 compounds is small and inadequately described.

- Dataset detail gaps: paper omits selection criteria, frequency ranges of compounds/components, and number of annotators per item—these likely affect results.

- Spearman/Ratings concern: unknown whether many compounds share identical scores, which could affect rank correlation validity.

- Terminology critique: “packed anchored tree” is unnecessary/confusing and resembles standard dependency path representations.

- Terminology critique: “elementary APTs” is undefined/confusing; needs an explicit definition.

- Table 2 / feature-order ambiguity: unclear if features of order >3 were discarded or removed due to “incompatible types”; an example is needed.

- Word choice: recommend using “component” instead of “constituent” to avoid syntactic ambiguity.

- Distributional intuition caveat: claim that literal use of a constituent implies shared co-occurrences is true for heads but not for dependents (e.g., “spelling” vs “spelling bee”).

- Section 5 request: provide a running example and clarify what “compound phrase token” means (merged whole-compound token?).

- Section 5.1 inference: suggests elementary APTs are triples (target w + dependency path r + other word w′), but the paper must clarify terminology and assumptions.

- PMI terminology: clarify whether “shifted PMI” refers to the PMI in Equation (3).

- Ambiguous sentence: clarify the sentence about removing low-PMI features—what “these phrases” and “things” refer to and the intended meaning.

- Alignment clarity: define “not properly aligned” (no alignment vs misalignment) and justify why overlap should be substantial for the NMOD relation.

- Feature-order inconsistency: explain why composed representations use higher-order dependency features but similarity comparisons use only first-order features and why second-order paths are unreliable.

- PPMI smoothing claim: the claim that α=0.75 helps is not clearly supported by Table 3.

- Hybridity transparency: report optimal values of h and q to gauge how much hybridity contributes to gains.

- Table 4 caption: state that results use the “add” combination in the caption.

- Additional experiments requested: report results for word2vec vectors computed for the compound phrases themselves.

- FREQ baseline concern: no intuition given why frequent compounds should be more compositional—this may reveal a dataset bias.
