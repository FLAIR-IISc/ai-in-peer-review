General comments
The paper presents experiments on predicting the degree of compositionality of English compounds. The dataset is an existing collection of 90 compounds whose compositionality was rated on a 1–5 scale by an unspecified number of annotators. Each experiment computes the cosine similarity between a vector for the compound (treated as a single token) and a composed vector derived from its components. Evaluation uses Spearman correlation between the cosine scores and human judgments.

The experimental factors vary along two axes:
- the underlying representations: neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors
- and, for the count vectors, whether to use plain or “aligned” vectors for the dependent component. The alignment is intended to model a shift from dependent to head, based on a previously published (but here suppressed) reference by the authors.

The results show that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings; using only aligned vectors performs worse than using unmodified vectors; and a heavily tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields only a small improvement.

With respect to writing and presentation, the introduction is clear, but other sections (e.g., Section 5.1) are unnecessarily hard to follow given the underlying simplicity of the ideas. Rewriting with running examples would improve readability.

On substance, I have several concerns:
- Relative to Reddy et al., the main novelty appears to be the use of aligned vectors; however, this alignment method has already appeared in the authors’ prior (suppressed) work.
- The dataset is small and insufficiently described. Frequency ranges (for compounds and their components) are likely to affect results.
Given that the improvements from aligned vectors are marginal, obtained on a small dataset with unclear selection criteria, the paper’s empirical conclusions feel fragile.

More detailed comments/questions
Section 3
- I do not see the need for the new term “packed anchored tree.” It appears to be a straightforward extraction of dependency paths between two lexical items—essentially an extension of standard syntactic distributional features (used at least since Lin 98) that typically employ paths of length 1 or 2 (with collapsed prepositions).
- Why call it a “tree”? What exactly are “elementary APTs” (as referenced in Section 5.1)?
- Table 2: Did you omit mentioning that features of order greater than 3 are discarded (hence why, for example, NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ does not appear in the leftmost bottom cell)? Or is this absence due to the elimination of some “incompatible types” you reference? If so, please provide an explicit example; the current explanation is unclear.

Section 4
- Because the Reddy et al. dataset is central to your study, please explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency ranges for the compounds and their components? Results may vary substantially with frequency.
- How many judgments were collected per compound? Are there many ties in the final compositionality scores? If so, does this pose issues for ranking and Spearman correlation?
- You use “constituent” to refer to a component of the N N sequence; I recommend “component,” since “constituent” also denotes a phrase (syntagm).
- Regarding the claim that “if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences”: this is certainly plausible if the constituent is the head, but much less so for the modifier (e.g., “spelling bee” does not share the distribution of “spelling”).

Section 5
- “Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.” Please provide a running example to clarify which object is being referred to at each point. Does “compound phrase token” mean the merged token created from the compound’s components?

Section 5.1
- I infer that “elementary APTs” are triplets of target word w + dependency path r + other word w′. If so, please say so clearly; the term is confusing.
- Please clarify whether “shifted PMI” is exactly the PMI defined in Equation (3).
- “Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.” Do “these phrases” co-refer with “things” here? The sentence reads as self-contradictory; please clarify.
- “In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned.” What does “not properly aligned” mean—simply “not aligned”? I do not follow lines 558–563. Why should the potential overlap be considerable specifically for the NMOD relation between the two components?
- Lines 575–580 are puzzling. The paper throughout leverages higher-order dependency features, yet at the crucial step of measuring similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors you restrict to first-order features only. Note 3 is meant to justify this, but I do not understand why second-order paths in the composed representations would be unreliable. Please explain.

Section 6
- “Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect.” This is not evident from Table 3.
- What are the optimal values of h and q in Equations (8) and (9)? These are necessary to assess how much “hybridity” contributes to the slight gains over the unaligned baseline.
- In Table 4, it appears results correspond to the add combination; please state this explicitly in the caption.
- Could you also report results for compound phrases using word2vec vectors?
- I do not see the intuition behind the FREQ baseline. Why should more frequent compounds be more compositional? This suggests a possible bias in the dataset.
