- Paper studies predicting compositionality of 90 English noun–noun compounds by correlating cosine similarity between compound vectors and composed component vectors with human ratings.
- Experiments compare neural embeddings vs syntactic-context count vectors, and plain vs “aligned” vectors for dependents.
- Main empirical findings: syntactic-context count vectors beat embeddings; aligned vectors alone underperform plain vectors; a tuned hybrid of aligned+unaligned yields minor improvement.
- Presentation is uneven: introduction is clear but other sections (e.g., 5.1) are hard to follow and would benefit from running examples.
- Main novelty is unclear: “aligned vectors” appear to be reused from the authors’ prior (suppressed) work, reducing originality over Reddy et al.
- Dataset concerns: the 90-compound dataset is small, poorly described, and selection criteria are not explained, making results fragile.
- Frequency effects are unreported: compound and component frequency ranges are not given yet likely affect results.
- Annotation details missing: number of judgments per compound, prevalence of tied scores, and tie-handling in Spearman correlations are unspecified.
- Terminology: suggest using “component” instead of “constituent” to avoid ambiguity with phrasal constituents.
- Conceptual clarification needed: the claim that component and compound share co-occurrences assumes head behavior and does not hold universally (e.g., “spelling bee”).
- Section 3 nomenclature issues: the term “packed anchored tree” seems unnecessary and unclear; “elementary APTs” are not defined.
- Table 2 and feature selection: unclear whether features beyond order 3 are discarded or eliminated due to “incompatible types”; provide explicit examples.
- Section 5 clarity: add a running example and define “compound phrase token”/“merged compound token” precisely.
- Section 5.1 technical clarifications: explicitly define elementary APTs (likely triplets), clarify what “shifted PMI” means, and rewrite unclear sentences about low PMI and “these phrases.”
- Alignment discussion needs precision: explain “not been properly aligned,” why NMOD overlap might be substantial, and expand the rationale for relying on only first-order features at evaluation despite using higher-order features in composition.
- Section 6 statistical/reporting gaps: smoothing claim (α = 0.75) is not clearly supported by Table 3—provide stats; report optimal h and q for the hybridity mixture.
- Reporting omissions: state in Table 4 caption that “add” combination is used; provide word2vec results for compound phrases as well.
- Baseline justification: the FREQ baseline’s intuition is unclear and may reflect dataset bias—justify or remove this baseline.
