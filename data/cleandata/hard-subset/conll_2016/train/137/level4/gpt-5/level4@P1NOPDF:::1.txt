General comments
The paper reports experiments on predicting the compositionality of English noun–noun compounds. The dataset is a previously released set of 90 compounds, each rated 1–5 for compositionality by an unspecified number of annotators. Each experiment computes the cosine similarity between a vector for the compound (treated as a single token) and a composed vector derived from its components. Evaluation is via Spearman correlation between these cosine scores and human judgments.

The experiments vary (i) the vector type (neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors) and (ii) for the latter, whether to use “aligned” vs. plain vectors for the dependent component, where alignment is intended to capture a shift from the dependent to the head. Alignment was proposed in a previous (suppressed) reference by the authors.

Results show syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings; using aligned vectors alone underperforms plain vectors; and a heavily tuned combination of aligned and unaligned features yields a slight improvement.

Formally, the introduction is well written, but other sections (e.g., 5.1) are hard to follow despite relatively simple underlying ideas; running examples would help considerably.

Substantively, I have two main concerns:
- Novelty: Relative to Reddy et al., the apparent innovation is the use of aligned vectors, but that technique has already appeared in a prior (suppressed) publication by the authors.
- Data: The dataset is small and insufficiently described. Frequency ranges of compounds and components are likely to affect results.

Given that the gains from alignment are marginal, on a small and under-specified dataset with unclear selection criteria, the findings seem fragile.

Detailed comments and questions
Section 3
- I do not see the need for the new term “packed anchored tree.” This appears to be a straightforward extraction of dependency paths between two lexical items—essentially a standard extension of syntactic distributional representations (e.g., Lin, 1998), which typically use paths of length 1 or 2 with collapsed prepositions.
- Why call it a “tree”? What precisely are “elementary APTs” (mentioned in Section 5.1)?
- Table 2: Did you discard features of order >3? Is that why, for instance, NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ does not appear in the bottom-left cell? Alternatively, is this due to eliminating incompatible types (if so, please provide a concrete example, as this was unclear).

Section 4
- Because the Reddy et al. dataset is central, please explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency ranges for the compounds and their components? Results are very likely to vary with frequency.
- How many judgments were collected per compound? Are there many ties in the final compositionality scores? If so, does that affect the Spearman correlation (e.g., via tied ranks)?
- You use “constituent” for a component of the NN sequence; “component” would be clearer, since “constituent” also denotes a syntactic phrase.
- Regarding the claim that “if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences”: this intuition is more plausible if the constituent is the head; it is much less so for the modifier (e.g., spelling bee does not share the distribution of spelling).

Section 5
- “Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.” A running example would help clarify which object is being discussed at each point. Does “compound phrase token” refer to the merged form of the two components?

Section 5.1
- I infer that “elementary APTs” are triples of the form target word w + dependency path r + other word w′. If so, please say so explicitly; the term is confusing as is.
- Please clarify whether “shifted PMI” is PMI as defined in Equation (3).
- “Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.” What does “these phrases” refer to—“things,” the removed features, or the targets? The sentence reads as contradictory; please rephrase and make the referents explicit.
- “In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned.” What does “not properly aligned” mean here—no alignment at all, or incorrect alignment? I do not follow lines 558–563. Why should the potential overlap be considerable specifically for the NMOD relation between the two components?
- Lines 575–580 are puzzling. The paper builds higher-order dependency features throughout, yet when computing the key similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors, you use only first-order features. Note 3 is intended to justify this, but I do not understand why second-order paths in the composed representations are deemed unreliable. Please explain.

Section 6
- “Smoothing the PPMI calculation with α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect” is not clearly borne out by Table 3.
- What are the optimal values of h and q in Equations (8) and (9)? They are essential to quantify how much “hybridity” contributes to the slight gains over unaligned vectors.
- In Table 4, it appears results use the add combination; please state this in the caption.
- Could you report results using word2vec vectors for the compound phrases (treated as single tokens) as well?
- I do not see the intuition behind the FREQ baseline. Why would more frequent compounds be more compositional? If FREQ performs competitively, it may indicate a frequency-related bias in the dataset.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles a worthwhile problem and offers careful experiments, but its main methodological contribution (alignment) appears to have been published previously; gains are small; and the dataset is both limited and under-documented. Clarifying the definitions (APT terminology), providing running examples, fully specifying the dataset and frequency profiles, and justifying key choices (feature orders, alignment effects, smoothing) would substantially strengthen the work.
