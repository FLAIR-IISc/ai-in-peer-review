- Paper evaluates methods for predicting compositionality of 90 English noun compounds using cosine similarity between holistic compound vectors and composed component vectors, evaluated by Spearman correlation with human ratings.
- Experiments compare neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors and test aligned vs. unaligned modifier vectors plus a tuned hybrid.
- Results: syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings; aligned-only vectors underperform unaligned; a tuned hybrid gives a small gain.
- Introduction is clear but some technical sections (e.g., 5.1) are hard to follow; running examples would help.
- Novelty concern: main contribution (alignment) seems incremental relative to Reddy et al. (2011) and previously published by the authors.
- Dataset concerns: 90-item dataset is small, selection criteria are unclear, and frequency ranges of compounds/components are not reported.
- Robustness concern: observed gains are marginal and may be fragile given small, possibly biased dataset.
- Terminology: “anchored packed tree” is poorly motivated and seems like standard dependency-path features; introducing a new name is unnecessary.
- Clarify why it is called a “tree” and what “elementary APTs” are (term is unclear).
- Table 2: state explicitly whether features were restricted to order ≤ 3 or explain other constraints with a concrete example.
- Section 4: explain how the 90 compounds were selected and report frequency ranges of compounds and components.
- Section 4: report number of human judgments per compound and whether many ties exist that could affect Spearman correlation.
- Use “component” instead of “constituent” to avoid syntactic ambiguity.
- The intuition that constituents share co-occurrences with compounds is stronger for heads than for modifiers; discuss this asymmetry (e.g., “spelling” in “spelling bee”).
- Section 5: provide running examples and clarify what “compound phrase token” refers to (merged surface form?).
- Section 5.1: confirm whether “elementary APTs” are triples (target word, dependency path, context word) and clarify confusing terminology.
- Clarify whether “shifted PMI” means PMI with an additive shift as in Levy et al. (2015).
- Rephrase the sentence about removing low-PMI features that currently reads contradictory and unclear about effects on low-frequency compounds.
- Clarify what “properly aligned” means and whether you mean “not aligned at all” in the discussed sentence.
- Explain why NMOD might be an exception with considerable overlap between component APTs; rationale is unclear.
- Justify why core similarity uses only first-order features when higher-order dependency features are emphasized elsewhere; explain unreliability of second-order paths.
- Section 6: claim that PPMI smoothing (α = 0.75) helps is not obviously supported by Table 3; qualify this statement.
- Report optimal values of hybridization parameters h and q from equations (8)–(9) to quantify hybrid contribution beyond unaligned model.
- Clarify caption of Table 4 to state which composition function (e.g., additive) was used.
- Add results using word2vec vectors trained for compound tokens themselves.
- Explain the intuition behind the FREQ baseline and whether frequent compounds being more compositional indicates dataset bias.
