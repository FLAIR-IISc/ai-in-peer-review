Review- General comments
=============================
This paper presents experiments on predicting the degree of compositionality of English compound nouns. The study uses an existing dataset of 90 compounds whose compositionality was rated on a 1–5 scale (with an unspecified number of annotators). Each experiment computes the cosine similarity between a vector for the compound (treated as a single token) and a composed vector built from the vectors of its components. Performance is assessed via Spearman correlation between these cosine scores and human ratings. The experimental factors vary in two main ways:
- the type of vectors: neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count-based vectors
- and for the latter, whether to use plain vectors or “aligned” vectors for the dependent element of the compound, where alignment is intended to capture a shift from dependent to head. These alignments were introduced by the authors in a prior (suppressed-for-review) publication.

The results suggest that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings; using aligned vectors alone does worse than using unmodified vectors; and a heavily tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields a modest improvement.

As for presentation, the introduction is clear and well-written, but other sections (e.g., 5.1) are hard to follow despite the underlying ideas being fairly straightforward. Running examples would improve readability.

Substantively, I have several concerns:
- the novelty over Reddy et al. appears to lie in the use of aligned vectors, but those were already published by the authors in an earlier suppressed reference
- the dataset is small and insufficiently described. In particular, frequency ranges are very likely to affect outcomes.

Given that the gains from alignment are marginal, the dataset is small, and the selection criteria for the compounds are not clearly explained, I find the paper’s conclusions rather fragile.

More detailed comments/questions
================================
Section 3
I don’t see the need for the new term “packed anchored tree.” It looks like a straightforward extraction of paths between two lexical items in a dependency tree—essentially a direct extension of standard syntactic distributional representations (dating back at least to Lin 1998), which typically use paths of length 1 or 2 with collapsed prepositions. Also, why is it called a “tree”? What exactly are “elementary APTs” (as referenced in Section 5.1)?

Table 2: Did you omit noting that features of order greater than 3 are discarded? Is that why, for example, NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ is absent from the bottom-left cell of Table 2? Or is this due to the removal of “incompatible types” you mention (an example would help; the current explanation is unclear).

Section 4
Because the Reddy et al. dataset is central to the paper, you should explain how the 90 compounds were chosen. What are the frequency ranges of the compounds and their components? Results are likely to vary with frequency. How many ratings were collected per compound? Do many compounds share the same final compositionality score? If so, does that create issues when ranking for Spearman correlation?

You seem to use “constituent” for a component of the N N sequence; I suggest “component,” since “constituent” can also mean a phrase/syntagm.

Regarding the statement “… the intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences”: this is clearly true if the constituent is the head, but much less so otherwise (e.g., “spelling bee” does not share the distribution of “spelling”).

Section 5
“Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.”
Please include a running example to make the referents of these objects explicit. Does “compound phrase token” mean the merged token corresponding to the whole compound?

Section 5.1
I infer that “elementary APTs” are triples of target word w + dependency path r + other word w′. If so, the terminology is confusing; please clarify.

Please specify whether “shifted PMI” refers to the PMI defined in Equation (3).

“Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.”
Do “these phrases” co-refer with “things” here? The sentence reads as self-contradictory; please clarify the intended meaning.

“In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned.”
What does “not properly aligned” mean here—no alignment at all, or misalignment? I do not follow the argument in lines 558–563. Why should potential overlap be substantial specifically for the NMOD relation between the two components?

Lines 575–580 are puzzling. The paper relies on higher-order dependency features throughout, yet at the crucial point of computing similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors, only first-order features are used. Note 3 is meant to justify this, but I do not understand why second-order paths in the composed representations are deemed unreliable; please elaborate.

Section 6
“Smoothing the PPMI calculation with a value of α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect” does not seem evident from Table 3.

What are the optimal values of h and q in Equations (8) and (9)? These are necessary to gauge how much “hybridity” contributes to the slight gains over unaligned baselines.

In Table 4, it appears the results use the add combination; please state this in the caption.

Could you also report results for word2vec vectors computed for the compound phrases themselves?

I do not see the intuition behind the FREQ baseline. Why would a frequent compound be more compositional? This suggests a possible bias in the dataset.
