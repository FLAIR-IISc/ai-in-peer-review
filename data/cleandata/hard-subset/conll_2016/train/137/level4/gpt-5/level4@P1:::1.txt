General comments
The paper evaluates methods for predicting the compositionality of English noun compounds. The dataset is a previously published set of 90 compounds with human compositionality ratings on a 1–5 scale (the number of raters is not specified). Each experiment computes the cosine similarity between a compound’s holistic vector (the compound treated as a single token) and a composition of its component vectors. Spearman’s rank correlation between this similarity and human judgments is used for evaluation.

The experiments vary in:
- Vector type: neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors.
- For count vectors: whether to use “aligned” vectors for the modifier (to shift the modifier into the head’s space using dependency-path alignment from prior work by the authors) or to use unaligned vectors. A tuned hybrid that linearly combines aligned and unaligned representations is also tested.

Results show that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings. Using aligned vectors alone underperforms using unmodified vectors, while a carefully tuned hybrid of aligned and unaligned vectors yields a small gain.

Writing-wise, the introduction is clear, but some technical sections (e.g., 5.1) are hard to follow despite relatively simple underlying ideas. Running examples would help.

Substantively, I have several concerns:
- Novelty: Relative to Reddy et al. (2011), the main addition appears to be alignment, which has already been published by the authors (reference suppressed).
- Dataset: The dataset is small and insufficiently described. Frequency ranges of compounds and components likely affect results.
- Robustness: The gains from aligned vectors are marginal, achieved on a small dataset with unclear selection criteria. The findings feel fragile.

More detailed comments/questions
Section 3
- The term “anchored packed tree” is not well motivated. It appears to be an extraction of dependency paths between lexical items, i.e., a straightforward extension of standard syntactic distributional features (e.g., Lin, 1998), typically using paths of length 1 or 2 (with collapsed prepositions). Why introduce a new name?
- Why is it called a “tree”? What are “elementary APTs” (as referenced in section 5.1)?
- Table 2: Did you restrict features to order ≤ 3? If so, please state this explicitly (e.g., why NMOD.NSUBJ̄.DOBJ does not appear in the bottom-left cell). Alternatively, if this relates to eliminating incompatible co-occurrence types, please provide a concrete example; the current explanation is unclear.

Section 4
- Given that the Reddy et al. dataset is central, please explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency ranges of compounds and their components? Results likely depend on these ranges.
- How many judgments were collected per compound? Are there many ties in the final compositionality scores? If so, does this affect Spearman correlation?
- You use “constituent” to mean a component of an NN compound. I suggest “component,” since “constituent” can denote a phrase in syntax.
- The intuition that “if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences” holds when the constituent is the head; it is much weaker for modifiers (e.g., spelling in spelling bee does not share spelling’s distribution).

Section 5
- “Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.” A running example would help track which object you mean. Does “compound phrase token” refer to the merged surface form of the compound?

Section 5.1
- I infer that “elementary APTs” are triples (target word w, dependency path τ, context word w′). If not, please clarify; the term is confusing.
- Please clarify whether “shifted PMI” refers to PMI as defined in equation (3) with an additive shift (as in Levy et al., 2015).
- “Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.” The pronoun reference is unclear (“these phrases” vs. “things”), and the sentence reads as contradictory. Please rephrase to explain why shifting harms low-frequency compounds.
- “In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned.” Do you mean “not aligned at all”? Please clarify “properly aligned.”
- Why might there be considerable overlap in the specific case of NMOD between the two components? The rationale for the NMOD exception is unclear.
- It is puzzling that the paper emphasizes higher-order dependency features but, when computing the core similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors, restricts to first-order features. Footnote 3 is intended as justification, but I do not understand why second-order paths in composed representations are unreliable, given that elementary APTs include paths up to order 2. Please clarify.

Section 6
- “Smoothing the PPMI calculation with α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect” is not obvious from Table 3. Please qualify.
- What are the optimal values of h and q in equations (8)–(9)? These are crucial to understand how much hybridization contributes beyond the unaligned model.
- Table 4 seems to report results with the additive composition; please state this in the caption.
- Can you report results using word2vec vectors learned for the compound tokens themselves?
- I do not see the intuition behind the FREQ baseline. Why should more frequent compounds be more compositional? This raises the possibility of dataset bias.
