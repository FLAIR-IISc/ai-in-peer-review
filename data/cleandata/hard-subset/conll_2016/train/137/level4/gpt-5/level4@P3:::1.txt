Summary and contributions
The paper studies compositionality detection for English noun–noun compounds by comparing (i) an integrated compositional distributional model using typed dependency features with higher-order paths (anchored packed trees, APTs), against (ii) naïve additive composition of neural word embeddings. The task is to predict human compositionality judgments (Reddy et al., 2011) by correlating the similarity between a composed representation and an observed compound representation.

Contributions as I see them:
1) Empirical finding: Typed, higher-order dependency features substantially outperform untyped neural embeddings on the Reddy et al. dataset. A hybrid of aligned and unaligned APT composition attains ρ≈0.79, exceeding both the prior count-based baseline (ρ=0.71) and strong cbow baselines (ρ≈0.74).
2) Analysis of alignment: Contrary to expectation, unaligned typed features outperform aligned ones; a linear hybrid of the two yields small but consistent gains, suggesting they capture complementary signals.
3) Practical insights on weighting: Observations on PPMI smoothing and subsampling indicate that techniques akin to shifted PMI can hurt in low-frequency regimes typical of many compounds.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains over prior art: The APT-based methods improve substantially over Reddy et al. (2011) and beat well-tuned neural baselines. The results are consistent across PPMI variants and composition choices, with small standard errors and clear statistical significance thresholds.
- Careful experimental controls: The authors preprocess ukWaC to merge target compounds into single tokens and avoid leakage between compound and component contexts. Cross-validated tuning of h and q is appropriate, and comparing aligned, unaligned, and hybrid APT variants is informative.
- Clear takeaways for compositionality: The consistent advantage of typed dependency paths indicates that syntactic structure helps determine compositionality, and that information beyond untyped proximity is important for this task.
- Broad comparison with neural embeddings: The study includes both cbow and skip-gram with multiple dimensionalities and subsampling thresholds, and ties the observed behavior to frequency effects in the data.
- Relevance and timeliness: Compositionality detection is a meaningful testbed for composition methods. The work provides actionable guidance for when typed features and alignment matter.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The main technical apparatus (APT features and alignment) appears to have been introduced previously by the authors (reference suppressed). Here, the incremental novelty is largely empirical: applying APTs to the Reddy et al. dataset and analyzing alignment variants. The conceptual advance beyond prior APT work is not fully articulated.
- Small and underdescribed dataset: The study relies on 90 compounds. The paper does not report how the compounds were selected, frequency ranges of compounds/components, or the number of annotators per item. Given that frequency correlates with compositionality in this dataset (the FREQ baseline is strong), the lack of dataset profiling weakens claims of generality.
- Clarity and terminology: Sections 5/5.1 are difficult to follow. Terms such as “anchored packed tree,” “elementary APT,” and “reduction” are introduced without minimally sufficient examples; the motivation for the “tree” terminology is unclear given the essentially path-based features. Some explanations (e.g., on shifted PMI) are confusing or self-contradictory.
- Evaluation choice undermines the narrative: Although higher-order paths are central to the proposal, the final similarity between composed and observed vectors is computed on first-order dependency features only. The rationale (that higher-order paths in composed representations are unreliable) is asserted but not demonstrated, and it raises questions about how much higher-order structure actually drives the improvements.
- Missing details that affect interpretability: The paper does not report the tuned values (or ranges) of h and q in the best APT configurations; Table legends do not consistently state the composition operator; the intuition for why unaligned overlap should be “considerable” for NMOD is not convincingly argued; and the FREQ baseline’s rationale is not discussed in depth.

Questions to the authors
- Dataset and annotations:
  • How were the 90 compounds selected? Please report frequency statistics (corpus counts) for compounds and components, and the distribution across compositionality scores.
  • How many annotators rated each item? How were ties handled in Spearman correlation? Are many items tied?
- Definitions and examples:
  • Please define “anchored packed tree” and “elementary APT” precisely, with a compact running example that traces a compound through construction, offset/alignment, reduction, and composition.
  • Why “tree,” rather than “set of dependency paths”? What is “packed” and what is “anchored” in formal terms?
  • What does “elimination of incompatible co-occurrence types” mean? Provide concrete examples of types that are disallowed and why.
- Feature orders and evaluation:
  • What maximum path order is used in elementary APTs and during composition? Do you discard paths beyond a certain length?
  • Why is the final similarity computed using only first-order features? Please quantify the impact of including second-order features in the evaluation (even if noisy) or provide ablations showing reliability issues that justify their exclusion.
- Alignment and overlap:
  • In what sense are APTs “not properly aligned”? Do you mean “not aligned” (i.e., no offset) or “misaligned”? Why should NMOD yield substantial overlap between unaligned modifier and head features? Concrete examples would help.
- Parameters and baselines:
  • Please report the best h and q values (means and variance across folds) for each APT variant. How sensitive are results to these hyperparameters?
  • You state that compound phrases also receive word2vec representations. Could you report the performance of composing embeddings vs. using observed compound embeddings alone, and their combination?
  • What is the empirical justification for the FREQ baseline? Is the correlation with compositionality an artifact of this dataset’s selection?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Rewrite Section 5.1 with a single running example to track: (i) elementary APT extraction; (ii) offset and reduction; (iii) aligned vs unaligned composition; (iv) final similarity computation. This would greatly improve readability.
- Clarify the discussion of shifted PMI: what exact shift is used, and does “these phrases” refer to compounds? The current phrasing suggests contradictory effects.
- Report per-item or grouped error analyses (e.g., by frequency or dependency type) to better understand where aligned vs unaligned APTs help.
- If possible, release code or detailed feature templates to facilitate replication.

Overall recommendation
The empirical results are strong and relevant, and the comparison is thoughtfully constructed. However, limited novelty, the small/underdescribed dataset, and clarity issues reduce the impact. With improved exposition and fuller reporting of dataset statistics and hyperparameters, the paper would make a solid empirical contribution to compositionality detection.
