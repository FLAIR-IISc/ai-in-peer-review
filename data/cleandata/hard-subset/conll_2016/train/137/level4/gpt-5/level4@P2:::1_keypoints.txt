- Paper evaluates compound compositionality on Reddy et al.’s 90-item dataset by correlating cosine similarity between holistic compound vectors and composed component vectors with human scores (Spearman’s ρ).
- Compares neural embeddings (word2vec) and typed-dependency count vectors (APTs), testing aligned, unaligned, and hybrid APT composition.
- Main empirical findings: APTs outperform word2vec; unaligned APT > aligned APT; tuned hybrid gives a small extra gain.
- Primary claimed contribution: empirical comparison showing typed dependency features and compositions strongly predict compositionality, surpassing neural embeddings and prior baseline.
- Secondary claimed contribution: demonstration that alignment is not superior to unaligned composition for noun–noun compounds, with a marginal hybrid improvement.
- Strength: clear motivation—compositionality detection is an appropriate testbed distinct from phrase similarity.
- Strength: empirical thoroughness—multiple parameter settings explored and statistical significance considered.
- Strength: practical finding that simple unaligned APT composition is robust and competitive.
- Strength: confirms/extends prior work by improving over Reddy et al. using the same corpus, highlighting the importance of typed dependencies and model choices.
- Weakness: limited novelty—alignment idea largely appeared in prior work by the authors and yields weak gains here.
- Weakness: small dataset (90 items) and under-specified item selection, frequency ranges, annotator counts, and agreement—findings may be fragile.
- Weakness: clarity and terminology problems—terms like “anchored packed trees,” “elementary APTs,” and “properly aligned” lack clear definitions and examples.
- Weakness: methodological inconsistency—paper emphasizes higher-order dependency features but evaluates similarity using only first-order features without convincing justification.
- Weakness: FREQ baseline motivation (frequency implies compositionality) is not well justified and may reflect dataset bias.
- Question: why introduce “anchored packed trees,” how do they differ from standard dependency-path features, and why call them “trees”?
- Question: what are “elementary APTs”? please give a precise definition and a simple running example.
- Question: do you cap path length in Table 2 (e.g., at 3)? If so, state it and clarify incompatible co-occurrence types and the reduction rule τ ∈ R*R* with examples.
- Question: how were the 90 compounds selected, what are frequency ranges for compounds/components, how many human judgments per item, how were ties handled, and what is inter-annotator agreement/variance?
- Question/suggestion: prefer “component” over “constituent” to avoid syntactic ambiguity.
- Question: clarify “not properly aligned” — do you mean “not aligned at all”? Provide an illustrative example for NMOD overlap claims.
- Question: explain why including 2nd-order paths in similarity is harmful and provide empirical ablations supporting this choice.
- Question: clarify “shifted PMI” notation and specify the shift and relation to negative sampling; rephrase unclear sentences and give concrete examples.
- Question: quantify the benefit of α = 0.75 over α = 1, report optimal h and q values for best APT settings, and state composition method used for embeddings in Table 4.
- Question: report performance when composing word2vec vectors for compound tokens (trained merged compound embeddings) to parallel the APT setup.
- Suggestion: add a running example (e.g., “spelling bee”) tracing preprocessing, elementary APT construction, alignment, and composition to improve readability.
- Suggestion: discuss the strong correlation of frequency with compositionality as potential dataset bias and consider controlling for frequency or reporting partial correlations.
