General comments
=============================
The paper presents experiments on predicting the compositionality of English noun–noun compounds. The dataset is a previously published set of 90 compounds with compositionality ratings from 1 to 5 by an unspecified number of annotators. Each experiment computes the cosine similarity between a vector for the compound (treated as a single token) and a composed vector derived from its components. Performance is measured by Spearman correlation between cosine similarity and human judgments.

The experiments vary in:
- the vector type: neural embeddings versus syntactic-context count vectors; and
- for the latter, whether to use plain or “aligned” vectors for the dependent component, where alignment is intended to capture a shift from dependent to head (as proposed in a prior, suppressed reference by the authors).

Results show that syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings. Using aligned vectors alone underperforms plain vectors, while a finely tuned combination of aligned and unaligned vectors yields a small improvement.

Presentation-wise, the introduction is clear, but other sections (e.g., 5.1) are hard to follow despite the underlying ideas being straightforward. Running examples would help substantially.

Substantively, I have several concerns:
- The main novelty over Reddy et al. appears to be the use of aligned vectors, but that technique was introduced by the authors in earlier work (suppressed reference).
- The dataset is small and insufficiently described. Frequency ranges are likely to affect results.

Given that gains from alignment are marginal, the dataset is small, and the selection of compounds is not explained, the findings seem fragile.

More detailed comments/questions
================================
Section 3
- The new term “packed anchored tree” seems unnecessary. It appears to be extraction of dependency paths between two lexical items—an extension of standard syntactic distributional representations (e.g., Lin 1998) using paths of length 1–2 with collapsed prepositions. Why call it a “tree”? What exactly are “elementary APTs” (section 5.1)?
- Table 2: Did you omit stating that features beyond order 3 are discarded (hence why NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ does not appear in the bottom-left cell)? Or is it due to eliminating “incompatible types”? Please provide an explicit example; the current explanation is unclear.

Section 4
- Because the Reddy et al. dataset is central, please explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency ranges for compounds and their components? Results likely depend on these ranges.
- How many judgments per compound? Are there many ties in final compositionality scores? If so, how are ties handled when computing Spearman correlation?
- You use “constituent” to mean a component of the NN compound. I suggest “component,” since “constituent” can also mean “phrase.”
- “... the intuition that if a constituent is used literally within a phrase then it is highly likely that the compound and the constituent share co-occurrences”: this is generally true if the constituent is the head; otherwise less so (e.g., “spelling bee” does not share the distribution of “spelling”).

Section 5
- “Note that the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context.” Please add a running example to make the referents precise. Does “compound phrase token” mean the merged compound token?

Section 5.1
- I infer that “elementary APTs” are triplets (target word w, dependency path r, context word w′). If so, please define explicitly; the current name is confusing.
- Clarify whether “shifted PMI” is PMI as defined in Equation (3), possibly with a shift.
- “Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts.” The antecedents of “these phrases” and “things” are unclear, and the claim seems contradictory. Please restate precisely.
- “In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned.” Do you mean “not aligned at all”? Lines 558–563 are hard to follow. Why would potential overlap be substantial specifically for NMOD between the two components?
- Lines 575–580: The paper relies on higher-order dependency features, yet the crucial similarity between composed and observed phrasal vectors is computed with only first-order features. Why this change at evaluation time? Note 3 attempts to justify unreliability of second-order paths in composed representations, but the rationale is unclear; please elaborate.

Section 6
- “Smoothing the PPMI calculation with α = 0.75 generally has a small positive effect” is not obvious from Table 3. Please qualify or provide supporting statistics.
- What are the optimal values for h and q in Equations (8) and (9)? These are essential to gauge how much the “hybridity” mixture drives the small gains over unaligned vectors.
- In Table 4, please state in the caption that the add combination is used.
- Could you report word2vec results for compound phrases as well?
- The intuition behind the FREQ baseline is unclear: why should frequent compounds be more compositional? This may reflect dataset bias; please justify or reconsider this baseline.
