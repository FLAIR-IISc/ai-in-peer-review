- Paper experiments on predicting compositionality of 90 English noun–noun compounds using cosine between compound vectors and composed component vectors, evaluated by Spearman correlation with human ratings.
- Experiments compare neural embeddings vs. syntactic-context count vectors and aligned vs. plain vectors for the dependent component.
- Main results: syntactic-context count vectors outperform embeddings; aligned vectors alone underperform plain vectors; a tuned hybrid of aligned+unaligned yields only slight improvement.
- Introduction is clear, but other sections (e.g., 5.1) are hard to follow; running examples would improve clarity.
- Novelty concern: alignment technique appears to repeat a prior (suppressed) publication by the authors.
- Data concern: dataset is small (90 compounds), poorly documented, and selection/frequency effects likely influence results.
- Given marginal gains from alignment on a small, under-specified dataset, findings appear fragile.

Section 3:
- The new term “packed anchored tree” seems unnecessary; it resembles standard dependency-path extraction.
- Questioning the use of the term “tree.”
- Request to define “elementary APTs” explicitly.
- Table 2 unclear: did authors discard features of order >3 or incompatible types—please clarify with examples.

Section 4:
- Please explain how the 90 compounds were selected from Reddy et al.’s dataset.
- Report frequency ranges for compounds and their components, since frequency likely affects results.
- How many annotator judgments per compound? Are there many tied scores affecting Spearman correlation?
- Recommend using “component” instead of “constituent” to avoid syntactic ambiguity.
- The claim that literal use of a constituent implies shared co-occurrences is more plausible for heads than modifiers (e.g., spelling bee vs. spelling).

Section 5:
- The statement about elementary representations not containing phrasal context features needs a running example; clarify what “compound phrase token” refers to.

Section 5.1:
- Explicitly state whether “elementary APTs” are triples (target word + dependency path + other word).
- Clarify whether “shifted PMI” equals PMI as in Equation (3).
- Ambiguous sentence about removing low-PMI features—identify referents and rephrase for clarity.
- Clarify “not properly aligned”: no alignment or incorrect alignment; explain lines 558–563 and why overlap is expected for NMOD relations.
- Explain why only first-order features are used for composed vs. observed similarity despite building higher-order dependency features; justify Note 3 and reliability concerns about second-order paths.

Section 6:
- Claim that PPMI smoothing (α=0.75) helps is not clearly supported by Table 3.
- Report optimal values of h and q from Equations (8)–(9) to quantify hybridity’s contribution.
- State in Table 4 caption that results use the additive composition combination if true.
- Request results where compound phrases are represented by word2vec vectors as single tokens.
- Question the intuition behind the FREQ baseline and whether competitive FREQ performance indicates dataset frequency bias.

Overall assessment / recommendations:
- The problem is worthwhile and experiments careful, but the core methodological contribution (alignment) appears previously published and gives small gains.
- Dataset limitations and under-documentation weaken claims; clarify APT terminology, add running examples, fully specify dataset and frequency profiles, and justify choices about feature orders, alignment effects, and smoothing.
