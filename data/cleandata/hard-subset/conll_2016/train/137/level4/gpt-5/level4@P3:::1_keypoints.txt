- Paper compares typed, higher-order dependency APT composition to naïve additive neural embeddings for noun–noun compositionality prediction.
- Empirical finding: APTs with typed higher-order dependency features substantially outperform untyped neural embeddings (hybrid APT ρ≈0.79 vs prior ρ=0.71 and cbow ≈0.74).
- Analysis of alignment: Unaligned typed features unexpectedly outperform aligned ones, and a linear hybrid gives small consistent improvements, indicating complementary signals.
- Practical insight: PPMI smoothing and subsampling (shifted PMI-like techniques) can harm performance in low-frequency regimes typical of many compounds.
- Strength: Strong empirical gains over prior work with statistically significant, consistent results across variants and small standard errors.
- Strength: Careful experimental controls (preprocessing to avoid leakage, cross-validated tuning, and comparison of aligned/unaligned/hybrid APTs).
- Strength: Clear takeaway that typed dependency paths and syntactic structure matter for compositionality detection.
- Strength: Broad comparison with neural embeddings (cbow/skip-gram, multiple dims/subsampling) and linkage of behavior to frequency effects.
- Strength: The task is relevant and provides actionable guidance on when typed features and alignment matter.
- Weakness: Limited novelty—APT apparatus and alignment were introduced previously by the authors; here the contribution is mainly empirical.
- Weakness: Dataset is small (90 compounds) and underdescribed—selection, frequency ranges, and annotator details are missing, weakening generality claims.
- Weakness: Clarity and terminology issues—APT-related terms and motivations (e.g., “tree” terminology) are poorly explained and lack examples.
- Weakness: Evaluation choice undermines narrative—final similarity uses only first-order features despite emphasis on higher-order paths, with no demonstration justifying exclusion.
- Weakness: Missing important details that affect interpretability (tuned h and q values, composition operator legends, intuition for unaligned overlap, FREQ baseline rationale).
- Question: How were the 90 compounds selected and what are frequency statistics and compositionality score distributions for compounds/components?
- Question: How many annotators per item, how were ties handled in Spearman correlation, and are many items tied?
- Question: Define “anchored packed tree” and “elementary APT” with a compact running example showing construction, offset/alignment, reduction, and composition.
- Question: Why call it a “tree” rather than a set of dependency paths, and what do “packed” and “anchored” formally mean?
- Question: What is meant by “elimination of incompatible co-occurrence types”? Provide concrete disallowed types and reasons.
- Question: What maximum path order is used and are long paths discarded during composition?
- Question: Why is final similarity computed using only first-order features—please quantify the impact of including second-order features or show ablations justifying exclusion.
- Question: Clarify in what sense APTs are “not properly aligned” and why NMOD should show substantial overlap unaligned—provide concrete examples.
- Question: Report best h and q values (means/variance across folds) and sensitivity of results to these hyperparameters.
- Question: Report performance of using observed compound embeddings alone versus composed embeddings and their combination.
- Question: What is the empirical justification for the FREQ baseline and could its correlation with compositionality be a dataset artifact?
- Suggestion: Rewrite Section 5.1 with a single running example tracing APT extraction, offset/reduction, aligned vs unaligned composition, and final similarity to improve readability.
- Suggestion: Clarify shifted PMI discussion (exact shift used and what phrases it refers to) and report per-item or grouped error analyses by frequency/dependency type.
- Suggestion: Release code or detailed feature templates to facilitate replication.
- Overall recommendation: Strong and relevant empirical results but limited novelty, small/underdescribed dataset, and clarity issues reduce impact; improved exposition and fuller reporting would make a solid empirical contribution.
