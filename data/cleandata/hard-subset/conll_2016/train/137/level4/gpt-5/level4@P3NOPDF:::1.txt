Summary
The paper investigates methods for predicting the degree of compositionality of English noun–noun compounds. Using a small, previously published dataset of 90 compounds with human compositionality ratings (1–5), the authors compare cosine similarity between (i) distributional vectors for the compound treated as a single token and (ii) composed vectors derived from its components. They evaluate with Spearman correlation against human judgments. The main comparison is between neural embeddings and count-based syntactic-context representations, with an additional proposal to “align” the dependent’s representation toward the head to better capture head–modifier interactions. Results show that count-based syntactic contexts outperform neural embeddings; unaligned dependent vectors outperform aligned ones; and a tuned hybrid of aligned and unaligned features yields a small further gain.

Strengths
- Clear high-level goal and connection to prior work on distributional compositionality.
- Sensible evaluation metric (Spearman correlation) for ranked human judgments.
- Empirical comparison between neural and count-based methods is valuable, and the observation that syntactic-context count vectors can outperform embeddings is interesting.

Main concerns
- Novelty: The paper’s primary contribution appears to be the use of aligned dependent vectors, which, according to the anonymized citation, has already appeared in prior work by the authors. The incremental novelty over Reddy et al. is therefore unclear.
- Dataset limitations and reporting: The dataset is small and insufficiently described. Frequency effects of compounds and components are likely to impact results, yet no frequency analyses are provided. Given the marginal gains from alignment, the findings appear fragile.
- Clarity: While the introduction is well written, several sections (notably 5 and 5.1) are hard to follow. Key terms (e.g., “packed anchored tree,” “elementary APT,” “compound phrase token,” “proper alignment”) are introduced without concrete examples, making the method difficult to reconstruct.
- Methodological consistency: Much of the representation emphasizes higher-order dependency paths, yet the final similarity computation appears to use only first-order features. The justification provided (Note 3) is unclear.

Detailed comments and questions

Section 3 (Representations)
- The term “packed anchored tree” seems to denote the multiset of dependency paths between two lexical items. This looks like a straightforward extension of standard syntactic distributional features (paths of length 1–2, with collapsed prepositions). Why is a new term needed, and in what sense is this a “tree”?
- Please define “elementary APTs.” Are these atomic features corresponding to (target word, dependency path, context word) triplets?
- Table 2: It appears features of order >3 are discarded; is that why patterns such as NMOD.overline(NSUBJ).DOBJ do not appear? If instead this is due to elimination of “incompatible types,” please provide a clear example.
- The alignment operation is central but under-specified. Precisely define what is being aligned, to what, and how the transformation is computed.

Section 4 (Data and evaluation)
- The Reddy et al. dataset is pivotal. Please explain how the 90 compounds were selected, and report frequency ranges for compounds and components. Given known frequency effects in distributional semantics, results may differ substantially across ranges.
- How many annotators contributed to each compound’s score? How are ties handled in Spearman correlation? Are there many tied ranks?
- The paper uses “constituent” to mean component of the N–N compound. Consider “component” to avoid confusion with “phrase” in syntactic theory.
- The stated intuition—if a component is used literally within a phrase, the compound and component should share contexts—holds more strongly for heads than for modifiers. For example, “spelling bee” does not inherit the distribution of “spelling.” Please clarify how head vs. modifier asymmetries are handled.

Section 5 (Method)
- The sentence “the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context” is hard to parse without an example. Provide a running example tracing: component representation → alignment → composition → similarity to the compound-as-token vector. Clarify whether “compound phrase token” means the single-token representation of the compound.
- Please confirm whether “shifted PMI” refers to the PMI variant defined in Equation (3), and specify any smoothing or shift constants.
- The paragraph stating “Removing features which tend to go with lots of things (low positive PMI) means that these phrases appear to have been observed in a very small number of (highly informative) contexts” reads contradictory. If features are low-PMI (un-informative), their removal should increase the relative weight of rare, informative contexts—not imply phrases were observed in few contexts. Please rephrase.
- “Not properly aligned”: Do you mean “not aligned at all,” or “aligned with an incorrect path”? Please clarify the intended failure case and why overlap would be minimal.
- Why is potential overlap “considerable” in the specific case of an NMOD relation between components? Provide a concrete path-based example.

Section 5.1 (Features and similarity)
- The paper promotes higher-order dependency features, yet the final similarity measure relies on first-order features only. Why discard higher-order features at the crucial evaluation step? The brief rationale (Note 3) that composed 2nd-order paths are “not reliable” needs a concrete diagnostic: Are they sparse, noisy, misaligned, or dominated by head contexts? Quantify their contribution and justify the design choice.

Section 6 (Results)
- The claim that PPMI smoothing with α = 0.75 “generally has a small positive effect” is not clearly supported by Table 3. Please highlight where the gain appears and report statistical significance.
- Report the optimal values of the hybridity parameters h and q (Equations 8 and 9), along with sensitivity analyses. This is important to assess how much the small gains derive from mixing aligned and unaligned features.
- In Table 4, specify that the combination function is add.
- Please report word2vec-based results for compound-as-token vectors as well, for completeness and to isolate whether the gap vs. count-based methods is due to representation of compounds, composition, or both.
- The FREQ baseline: Why would higher compound frequency predict higher compositionality? Provide empirical justification or reconsider this baseline. If it correlates with compositionality in this dataset, that suggests potential sampling bias; please analyze.

Presentation
- Several sections would benefit from a compact running example (e.g., “spelling bee,” “coffee cup”) showing extraction of APT features, alignment, composition, and similarity computation.
- Define all acronyms and terms at first use. Ensure consistent terminology (component vs. constituent).
- Improve the flow in Section 5.1; the current prose is dense and contains unresolved pronoun references.

Overall assessment
The empirical finding that syntactic-context count vectors outperform neural embeddings for compound compositionality is of interest. However, the paper’s incremental novelty over prior aligned representations is limited, the dataset is small and under-documented, the gains from alignment are marginal, and important methodological choices are under-explained. I recommend substantial revision focusing on: (i) clearer definitions and examples; (ii) fuller dataset reporting and frequency analyses; (iii) justification and evaluation of higher-order features in the similarity computation; and (iv) a more rigorous comparison and ablations to establish where the observed advantages arise.
