- Task: predict compositionality of 90 English compounds by cosine between compound token vector and composed component vector, evaluated with Spearman correlation to human ratings.
- Experimental axes: vector type (neural embeddings vs syntactic-context count vectors) and use of aligned vs unaligned count vectors for the dependent component.
- Main results: syntactic-context count vectors beat embeddings; aligned-only vectors perform worse than unaligned; a tuned hybrid of aligned+unaligned yields only slight improvement.
- Presentation: introduction is clear but many sections (notably 5.1) are hard to follow and need running examples.
- Novelty concern: aligned vectors appear to be the main claimed contribution but were already published by the authors in prior work.
- Dataset concern: the 90-compound dataset is small and insufficiently described, making empirical findings fragile.
- Missing dataset details: how compounds were selected and the frequency ranges of compounds and their components are not reported.
- Annotation details missing: number of human judgments per compound and prevalence of identical compositionality scores (ties) are not provided.
- Terminology issue: “packed anchored tree” seems unnecessary and unclear; question why it’s called a “tree.”
- Clarify what “elementary APTs” are and whether they denote tuples (target word + dependency path + other word).
- Table 2 ambiguity: unclear if features of order >3 are discarded or if absence is due to “incompatible types”; request concrete example.
- Suggest using “component” instead of “constituent” to avoid syntactic ambiguity.
- The co-occurrence intuition holds for heads but not necessarily for modifiers (example: “spelling bee” vs “spelling”).
- Section 5: request running examples and clarification of what “compound phrase token” refers to (merged surface token?).
- Section 5.1: explicitly state whether “shifted PMI” equals the PMI definition in Equation (3).
- Clarify confusing sentence about removing low-PMI features and whether pronouns like “these phrases” co-refer with “things.”
- Explain “not properly aligned” (no alignment vs incorrect alignment) and why overlap between APTs should be considerable for NMOD relations.
- Justify why higher-order dependency features are built but only first-order features are used for similarity; Note 3’s explanation is insufficient.
- Section 6: claim that PPMI smoothing with α = 0.75 has small positive effect is not clearly supported by Table 3.
- Report optimal h and q in Equations (8) and (9) to assess the contribution of hybridity to gains.
- Clarify that Table 4 results use the add composition method and explicitly state this in caption/text.
- Request reporting results using word2vec vectors for the compound tokens themselves.
- Question the FREQ baseline intuition: why should more frequent compounds be more compositional, and could this indicate dataset bias.
