- Paper goal: predict compositionality of English noun–noun compounds by comparing compound-as-token vectors with composed component vectors using Spearman correlation.
- Methods compared: neural embeddings vs. count-based syntactic-context (APT) representations; proposed alignment of dependent toward head.
- Main results: count-based syntactic contexts outperform neural embeddings; unaligned dependents beat aligned ones; a tuned hybrid yields small extra gain.
- Strength: clear goal, sensible Spearman evaluation, and useful empirical comparison highlighting that syntactic-context counts can outperform embeddings.
- Novelty concern: alignment idea may be incremental and previously published by the authors, so novelty over Reddy et al. is unclear.
- Dataset limitations: tiny (90 compounds), insufficiently described, with no frequency analyses for compounds/components.
- Reporting questions: unclear how compounds were selected, annotator counts, handling of ties in Spearman, and potential sampling bias.
- Clarity issues: many terms (packed anchored tree, elementary APT, compound phrase token, proper alignment) lack concrete definitions or examples.
- Method under-specification: alignment operation is central but not precisely defined (what is aligned, to what, and how computed).
- Terminology consistency: use of “constituent” vs. “component” is confusing; acronyms and terms need first-use definitions.
- Higher-order vs. first-order inconsistency: paper emphasizes higher-order dependency features but similarity uses only first-order features without a clear justification.
- Justify discarding higher-order features: need diagnostics on sparsity/noise and quantitative contribution of 2nd-order paths.
- Section-specific clarity: Sections 5 and 5.1 are hard to follow and require running examples tracing component → alignment → composition → similarity.
- APT terminology: explain why “packed anchored tree” is needed and define “elementary APTs” as atomic feature units if so.
- Table 2/question: clarify whether features of order >3 were discarded or eliminated due to incompatible types, with examples.
- PMI details: confirm definition of “shifted PMI,” including smoothing/shift constants.
- Contradictory phrasing: reword the passage about removing low-PMI features and its interpretation regarding rare informative contexts.
- Ambiguity about misalignment: clarify “not properly aligned” (not aligned vs. incorrectly aligned) and expected effects on overlap.
- NMOD overlap claim: substantiate why NMOD relations yield “considerable” overlap with a concrete path example.
- Results/statistics: provide statistical significance for PPMI α = 0.75 gains and highlight where gains appear in tables.
- Hybrid parameters: report optimal h and q values, sensitivity analyses, and justify how much gains depend on tuning.
- Table clarity: specify combination function (add) in Table 4 and report word2vec compound-as-token vectors to isolate sources of differences.
- FREQ baseline: justify why higher compound frequency would predict compositionality or analyze its correlation to detect sampling bias.
- Presentation fixes: add compact running examples (e.g., “spelling bee,” “coffee cup”), define all acronyms, and improve flow and pronoun references in Section 5.1.
- Overall assessment: interesting empirical finding but limited novelty, fragile/marginal alignment gains, under-documented dataset, and under-explained methodological choices; substantial revision recommended.
