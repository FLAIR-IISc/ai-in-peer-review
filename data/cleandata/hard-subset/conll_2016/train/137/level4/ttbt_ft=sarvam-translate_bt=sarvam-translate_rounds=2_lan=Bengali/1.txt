General Comments
=============================
This paper is a report on an experiment to estimate the structural level of compounds in English.
The dataset used here consists of a set of 90 pre-existing compounds, whose structural dimensions (by an unspecified number of judges) were shifted from 1 to 5.
The general form of each experiment is to calculate cosine similarity between a vector of compounds (considered as a token) and the combination of the vectors of elements.
The cosine similarity is evaluated using the Spearman's correlation of human judgments.
The experiments differ:
- For the vectors used: neural embedding versus syntactic-context computation vector
- And in the latter case, whether a general or "aligned" vector should be used for the dependent elements of the compounds. Alignment tries to capture the change from dependent to head. Alignment was previously proposed in a hidden reference.
The results indicate that syntactic-context computation vector performs better than embedding, and only non-aligned vectors perform less well than aligned vectors, and a highly tuned combination of aligned and non-aligned vectors provides a slight improvement.
Regarding the structure of the paper, I found the introduction to be quite well-written, but other parts (such as paragraph 5.1) are difficult to read, although the underlying concepts are not very complex. Rewriting with running examples may help.
Regarding the content, I have several concerns:
- The innovation related to Reddy et al seems to use aligned vectors,
but the authors expressed them in a previous "hidden reference".
- The dataset is small, and not adequately described. In particular, frequency range may affect the results.
Since the improvements using aligned vectors are slight, in a small dataset where the choice of compounds is unclear, I think the paper's results are quite fragile.

Paragraph 4:
Since Reddy et al's dataset is the focus of your work, it is necessary to explain how the 90 compounds were selected. What are the frequency range / elements etc. of the compounds? According to the frequency range, the results are very likely to differ.
How many judgments were provided for a specific compound? Are there many compounds with the same final structural score? Isn't it a problem to determine their position when calculating the Spearman's correlation?
It seems you use "element" for an element of order N N. I would suggest "element", because "element" also has the meaning of "phrase" (synonyms).
"...the idea that if an element is literally used within a phrase, it is highly likely that the compound and the element share a co-occurrence"
: Remember that the idea must be true if the element is the head of the phrase, otherwise it is much less true (e.g., "spell" has no distribution of "spell").
