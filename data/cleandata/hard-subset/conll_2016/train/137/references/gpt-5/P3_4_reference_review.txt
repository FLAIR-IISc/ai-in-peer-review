Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by contrasting neural and count-based distributional representations, with a focus on typed dependency contexts and a compositional framework based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs). The key ideas are: (i) encode higher-order, typed dependency paths as features; (ii) align constituent spaces via dependency-path offsets before composition; and (iii) mix aligned and unaligned constituent representations (hybrid) to predict the holistic (observed) compound vector, and correlate the similarity with human compositionality judgments (Reddy et al., 2011). On ukWaC, the best APT hybrid model achieves ρ≈0.79, surpassing weighted addition over untyped vectors (ρ≈0.71) and CBOW embeddings (ρ≈0.74).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An empirical demonstration that typed, higher-order dependency features substantially improve noun–noun compositionality detection over untyped window-based representations, with a new hybrid aligned/unaligned APT composition that achieves state-of-the-art correlation on Reddy et al. (2011).
2) A careful experimental setup to avoid leakage (collapsing compounds into single tokens for holistic vectors while excluding those contexts from constituents), and a systematic comparison against tuned word2vec baselines.
3) An analysis showing that mixing aligned and unaligned APTs helps, suggesting that different contextual paths predict complementary aspects of compound behavior.

Strengths
1) Clear, well-motivated modeling of typed contexts and compositional alignment. The offset-and-reduction mechanism to align modifier spaces to the head’s space is principled, and the worked example with higher-order paths clarifies why naive pointwise operations fail without alignment. This is a solid contribution to the ongoing discussion of how syntax should inform composition.

2) Strong empirical gains on a widely used benchmark. The APT hybrid model (ρ≈0.79) clearly improves over: (a) prior untyped count-based methods (ρ≈0.71), and (b) tuned CBOW/SG baselines (best CBOW ≈0.74). The frequency baseline (ρ≈0.63) highlights non-triviality. The ablation across composition operators (add/min), alignment strategies, and PPMI smoothing adds credibility.

3) Thoughtful experimental controls to avoid trivial solutions. By collapsing compounds into single tokens for holistic representations and ensuring constituent vectors do not inherit those same contexts, the evaluation genuinely tests a model’s ability to infer the holistic representation from constituents, rather than rediscovering shared features. The restriction of similarity to first-order features of holistic compounds is defensible given sparsity concerns and is consistently applied.

4) Useful, interpretable analyses. The observation that h and q optima lie near 0.3–0.5 suggests the modifier and unaligned typed features contribute slightly more to predicting compositionality—plausible for noun–noun compounds. The finding that CBOW benefits from weaker subsampling here (contrary to some word similarity settings) is insightful and linked to compound sparsity.

Weaknesses
1) Limited scope and small evaluation set. Results rely solely on 90 noun–noun compounds. While this dataset is canonical, it is very small and narrow. Claims about “improving compositionality detection” would be much stronger with additional datasets (e.g., larger NN compound sets, VPC/VNC datasets, AN phrases, or other MWE compositionality resources) and with analysis by dependency subtype (NMOD vs. others).

2) Surprising result: unaligned > aligned APT, with a small hybrid gain. The core APT novelty is alignment via dependency offsets, yet unaligned APTs often outperform aligned ones, and the best model is a linear mixture. This invites a deeper analysis: is alignment hurting due to sparsity, reduction errors, or the restriction to first-order features when scoring? Without diagnosing why alignment underperforms, it is hard to assess the generality and necessity of APT alignment.

3) Baselines are incomplete for the paper’s central claim. The comparison to CBOW/SG is useful, but a stronger and more directly comparable baseline would be dependency-based SGNS (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) or GloVe trained on dependency contexts, as well as count-based typed dependency vectors with standard dimensionality reduction (e.g., PPMI+SVD) and naive composition. Given the emphasis on typed contexts, these baselines are essential to isolate the effect of the APT-specific alignment and hybrid strategies.

4) Statistical testing and reporting. With only ~30 items per test fold, claiming that differences >0.005 in ρ are significant at 95% due to many CV repetitions is optimistic. Repetitions of the same items do not increase the number of independent observations. Please use paired permutation/bootstrap tests across items to compare systems, and report per-fold/item-wise variability. The tiny standard errors reported (≤0.0015) appear to reflect repetition rather than true across-item uncertainty.

5) Potential confounds from corpus preprocessing. Collapsing compounds increases their token frequency and improves the quality of holistic vectors; while done consistently across models, it changes the task’s difficulty relative to prior work and could favor models that match common first-order contexts. An ablation without collapsing (or reporting holistic token counts) would clarify robustness.

Questions to authors
- Why do aligned APTs underperform unaligned ones? Can you provide a diagnostic: overlap proportions between aligned vs unaligned features, sparsity after reduction, or per-relation (NMOD vs others) breakdown? Does performance change if you evaluate similarity including second-order features for the holistic vector?

- Did you evaluate dependency-based SGNS (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) or GloVe on typed dependency contexts? Given your focus on typed features, this seems a crucial baseline.

- How sensitive are results to collapsing compounds? If you train without collapsing (i) constituents only and (ii) holistic compounds inferred from contexts post hoc, how do scores change?

- Significance testing: can you re-run system comparisons with a paired permutation test over the 90 items (not over CV repetitions) and report p-values?

- Efficiency and scalability: what are the memory/time costs of building and composing APTs with higher-order paths (dimensionality ~80k/230k)? Any pruning strategies, and how do they affect performance?

Additional comments
- Please add an error analysis: examples where the hybrid APT succeeds or fails compared to CBOW; compositional vs idiomatic cases; and per-dependency relation impacts. This would illuminate what the typed paths are capturing.

- Consider releasing code and preprocessed resources for reproducibility.
