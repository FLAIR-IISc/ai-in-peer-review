Summary and main contributions
The paper studies detection of non-compositionality in noun–noun compounds by comparing composed representations of a phrase with its observed (holistic) representation. It contrasts untyped proximity-based models (including neural embeddings) with typed dependency-based representations using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which encode higher-order dependency paths and allow alignment of feature spaces during composition. On the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds, the authors report higher Spearman correlations with human compositionality judgments than prior work, with a hybrid APT method (combining aligned and unaligned composition) performing best.

Contributions (as assessed):
1) Empirical finding that typed, higher-order dependency features significantly improve compositionality detection over untyped proximity-based models, including neural embeddings trained on the same corpus (hybrid APT ρ=0.79 vs Reddy 0.71; cbow 0.74).
2) A hybrid composition method that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APTs outperforms either alone, suggesting complementary signals from dependency-aligned and raw first-order features.
3) An evaluation setup that controls for leakage by recombining compounds as single tokens in the corpus and composing only from constituent representations that exclude compound contexts.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains with controlled comparison: All models are trained on the same parsed ukWaC corpus with compounds recombined. The reported improvements over Reddy et al. (2011) and over neural baselines are substantial (e.g., 0.79 vs 0.71 and 0.74), and the frequency baseline (0.63) highlights that the methods capture more than frequency effects.

- Careful design to avoid leakage: The corpus preprocessing ensures that constituent representations do not inherit contextual features from the compound token. This strengthens the claim that composition recovers phrase behavior rather than memorizing observed contexts.

- Well-motivated use of typed higher-order features: The paper articulates why typed, higher-order dependency paths are useful for composition (e.g., aligning adjective second-order to noun first-order features), and provides explicit alignment/reduction mechanisms. The hybrid results (small but consistent gains over unaligned or aligned alone) support the claim that the dependency structure is informative.

- Systematic parameter exploration for APTs: The study explores weighting schemes (normalized counts vs PPMI with smoothing), composition operators (min vs weighted addition), and mixing weights (h for head/modifier and q for aligned/unaligned). Findings (e.g., smoothed PPMI mild benefits; additive composition superior; optimal h, q ≈ 0.3–0.5) are consistent and informative.

- Fair neural baselines and ablations: Both CBOW and skip-gram are compared under several standard settings (dimensions, subsampling), and the analysis of subsampling thresholds provides a plausible explanation for performance patterns with low-frequency compounds.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope: Results are only on one small dataset (90 compounds), all in English, and only for noun–noun compounds. This limits claims about generality to other phrase types (e.g., adjective–noun, verb–object), languages, or larger-scale settings. The small sample also raises concerns about stability despite repeated CV.

- Missing or incomplete baselines: The neural baselines are proximity-based word2vec models. Stronger and highly relevant baselines are missing, notably dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014) and other compositional models with syntactic awareness. Including these would better contextualize the gains attributed to typed features.

- Incomplete reporting of significance/testing: The paper states that differences > 0.005 are significant because errors are ≤ 0.0015 over repeated 3-fold CV, but does not specify the number of repetitions, the exact test used, or provide confidence intervals. Given N=90, a more standard statistical treatment (e.g., paired permutation or bootstrap on ρ) is warranted.

- Some methodological choices need justification: Similarity is computed “restricted to 1st-order dependency features,” while composition draws on higher-order paths. The rationale is briefly mentioned (reliability), but ablations showing the impact of including/excluding higher orders in the similarity computation are not reported.

- Reproducibility and clarity issues: Several details are missing or under-specified: vocabulary thresholds/min-counts, epochs and negative sampling for word2vec, number of CV repetitions, final chosen h and q, and whether any additional feature pruning was used. Some references are “suppressed,” and the bibliography has formatting issues, hindering replicability.

Questions to authors
- How many repetitions of 3-fold CV were performed? What statistical test was used to assess significance, and can you report confidence intervals for the main correlations?

- What were the final selected ranges or distributions of h and q across folds? How sensitive are the results to these parameters?

- Why is similarity restricted to first-order features? Can you provide ablations showing performance when including second-order features in the similarity computation, and how robust are the findings to this choice?

- Can you compare against dependency-based word embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014) and/or GloVe trained on the same corpus, as additional baselines?

- Could you report more implementation details (min-count thresholds, number of epochs and negative samples in word2vec, feature pruning for APTs) and, if possible, release code or preprocessed resources to facilitate replication?

- How does the approach perform on other compositionality datasets (e.g., adjective–noun or verb–object datasets) or in languages with different morphology/syntax? If not available, can you provide a discussion of expected generalization?

Overall, the work is technically sound within its scope, the empirical improvements are clear and meaningful, and the study highlights the value of typed higher-order dependency features for compositionality detection. The main limitations are the narrow evaluation and missing strong baselines; addressing these would substantially strengthen the paper.
