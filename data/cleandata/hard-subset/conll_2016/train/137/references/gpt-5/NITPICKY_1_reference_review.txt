Summary and claimed contributions
- This paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by comparing the similarity between a composed phrase representation and its observed (holistic) representation. It builds on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which use typed dependency paths (including higher-order paths) and an alignment procedure to compose constituents. It evaluates on the 90-compound Reddy et al. (2011) dataset using Spearman correlation with human compositionality judgments.
- Claimed contributions as I see them:
  1) A hybrid APT-based compositional model that linearly combines aligned and unaligned typed-dependency representations, yielding improved correlations (reported best ρ≈0.79) over prior work and over CBOW/SG neural baselines trained on the same corpus.
  2) An empirical analysis that suggests typed dependency features and higher-order paths help in compositionality detection; unaligned APT composition and aligned APT composition predict complementary contexts.
  3) A careful preprocessing to prevent leakage from compound contexts into constituent representations by collapsing the 90 target compounds into single tokens during corpus processing.

Strengths
- Methodologically motivated use of syntactic structure. The use of typed and higher-order dependency paths and an explicit alignment step directly operationalizes the intuition that composition must account for syntactic roles. The hybrid model’s gains over aligned-only and unaligned-only settings (Table 3; Δρ≈0.01–0.02) support the claim that different sources of syntactic evidence are complementary.
- Competitive empirical results and clear baselines. The reported correlations outperform (a) the Reddy et al. (2011) untyped baseline (0.71) and (b) reasonably tuned CBOW/SG word2vec models (best CBOW 0.74), as well as a strong frequency baseline (0.63). The frequency baseline further contextualizes task difficulty and shows methods add value beyond frequency.
- Evaluation design attempts to avoid leakage. Collapsing compounds to single tokens and ensuring constituent representations do not reuse compound contexts is an appropriate control for evaluating composition quality, and the paper explicitly discusses why modifiers of modifiers are excluded.
- Reasonable parameter exploration within each family. For APTs, the paper examines PPMI α values, composition operators (min vs addition), alignment vs unaligned vs hybrid, and constituent weights (h) and mixing (q). For CBOW/SG, it varies dimensionality and subsampling. The observation that subsampling hurts low-frequency compounds is sensible and practically useful.

Weaknesses
- Missing content and numerous formatting/technical issues. Table 4 (neural word embedding results) is referenced but absent; yet its results are central to the claimed superiority of APTs. Several equations and definitions are hard to parse (e.g., the reduction operator in Eq. (1) uses τ = τ1 r r τ2 twice, likely intending inverses, but is ambiguous). References contain placeholders (“?,” “\Q2010\E,” “reference suppressed”), inconsistent citation formatting, and typos (“siginificantly,” “recommend”). Footnote markers are broken; there is a dangling “1” and an in-text footnote paragraph mid-section. These issues undermine clarity and reproducibility and are not acceptable for a archival venue.
- Limited novelty relative to prior APT work and Weeds et al. (2014). The APT framework and higher-order typed dependencies were previously proposed; here the main new element seems to be applying (and linearly combining) aligned and unaligned APTs to compositionality detection on a small dataset. While empirically useful, the conceptual advance is incremental and not sufficiently distinguished from earlier APT/typed-dependency composition work.
- Incomplete and potentially biased comparison to neural and count baselines. The paper compares APTs (typed dependencies) to window-based CBOW/SG (untyped), which is not apples-to-apples. It omits strong, readily available baselines: dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014), GloVe, SVD on PPMI matrices, or count-based dependency vectors with naive composition. The paper itself argues typed contexts are key; a fair test should compare to typed neural/count models. Without Table 4 and these baselines, the superiority claim is under-supported.
- Evaluation limitations and risk of metric mismatch. The study uses only the 90-item Reddy dataset; no secondary datasets (e.g., other compound lists or VPC compositionality). Moreover, similarity is computed only over first-order dependency features of the observed phrase, while the composed representation may leverage higher-order paths (and aligned offsets). This asymmetry may favor unaligned models whose first-order features overlap more directly with observed first-order features, confounding conclusions about the utility of alignment/higher-order information.
- Statistical methodology and reporting are opaque. The paper states repeated 3-fold CV with parameter tuning on training and claims “errors ≤ 0.0015” and that Δρ>0.005 is significant at 95%, but does not specify the number of repetitions, the test used (e.g., permutation/Steiger/Williams for dependent correlations), or whether multiple comparisons were controlled across many configurations. Given only 90 items and extensive tuning, these claims appear optimistic.

Questions to authors
- Please include the missing Table 4 with full CBOW/SG results (dims, subsampling, windows), and clarify whether compounds were collapsed into single tokens for the neural runs as well.
- What exact statistical test supports the “Δρ>0.005 is significant at 95%” claim? How many CV repetitions were used? How were hyperparameters (α, composition op, h, q) chosen without test leakage?
- Can you report results for dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg, 2014) and/or SVD on dependency PPMI as stronger typed baselines? Also report untyped count-based vectors composed by addition for parity with Reddy et al. on the same preprocessed corpus.
- Please provide an ablation isolating: (a) typed vs untyped contexts, (b) first-order vs first+second order paths, (c) aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid under the same evaluation metric (including computing similarity over higher-order features to check robustness).
- Can you release code and preprocessing scripts, and specify feature pruning thresholds, normalization, and exact reduction rules for APTs?

Recommendation
While the approach is interesting and the reported numbers are promising, the missing core table, numerous formatting/technical issues, limited novelty over prior APT work, and inadequate baselines make the submission below the bar in its current form. I recommend rejection and resubmission after substantial revision and stronger, fairer comparisons.
