Summary
This paper studies the detection of non-compositionality in noun–noun compounds by comparing “naive” compositional operations over standard vector spaces with a compositional distributional model based on anchored packed trees (APTs). APTs encode typed, higher-order dependency paths and introduce an alignment (offset + reduction) procedure so that dependents’ feature spaces are brought into the head’s space before composition. The authors evaluate aligned, unaligned, and hybrid (linear combination) APT compositions on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds, comparing against neural embeddings (word2vec CBOW/SG) composed by weighted addition and prior untyped, window-based baselines. They report best Spearman correlation ρ ≈ 0.79 (hybrid APT), outperforming CBOW (≈0.74) and the Reddy baseline (≈0.71), and argue typed dependency information is crucial for compositionality detection.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: Typed, higher-order dependency features (via APTs) substantially improve compositionality detection of noun compounds over untyped count vectors and over word2vec baselines, with a hybrid of aligned and unaligned APT features performing best.
2) Methodological contribution: Clear formulation of alignment via offsetting and reduction of dependency paths, plus a hybrid composition that mixes aligned and unaligned representations to capture complementary evidence.
3) Experimental analysis: Careful parameter study (PPMI with/without smoothing, composition operators, constituent weights, subsampling in word2vec), and a corpus preprocessing protocol to avoid leakage from observed compound contexts into constituent representations.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains with clear ablations: The paper shows consistent improvements of APT models over both reported prior results and well-tuned neural baselines on the same corpus. The aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid results are diagnostic, and the finding that hybridization helps suggests complementary information, not just overfitting to one mechanism.
- Methodologically principled: The alignment via path offsetting and reduction is well motivated by syntax and provides an elegant way to reconcile feature spaces across parts of speech. The connection to higher-order dependency paths is clear and operationalized.
- Transparent experimental setup: The authors remark on important pitfalls (e.g., avoiding leakage by not letting compound contexts flow back into constituent representations), and give rationale for choices (e.g., not using shifted PMI due to low-frequency compounds; why CBOW subsampling may hurt).
- Competitive baselines and tuning: Including both CBOW and SG with multiple dimensions and subsampling, and reporting that CBOW is hard to beat on related tasks, strengthens the claim that the APT advantage is due to typed dependency structure rather than lack of baseline tuning.
- Insightful observations: The paper highlights that for NN compounds, unaligned (conventional) features can still be predictive (due to the prevalence of NMOD), and that modifiers are slightly more informative than heads for this dataset (optimal h, q in 0.3–0.5).

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope (single small dataset): Results are only on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset with 90 items. With many model choices (PPMI smoothing, h, q, composition operator), repeated 3-fold CV on such a small N risks optimistic estimates. Please report more robust uncertainty (e.g., bootstrap CIs over items) and test on additional datasets (e.g., other NN compounds or MWE compositionality datasets).
- Frequency confound not fully controlled: The FREQ baseline (ρ=0.63) is very strong, indicating compositionality is correlated with frequency. While methods beat this baseline, a partial-correlation or regression analysis controlling for frequency would clarify how much of the gain stems from richer features vs frequency signals. Similarly, reporting performance within frequency bins would be helpful.
- Potential preprocessing and parsing bias: The corpus is modified to merge compounds and to select a single “most frequent” dependency relation per compound; modifiers’ modifiers are removed. There is no sensitivity analysis on (i) parse errors (particularly for multi-noun compounds), (ii) compounds with multiple relation types (AMOD vs NMOD), or (iii) the impact of ignoring modifier-internal modification. An ablation of these choices and an error analysis by relation type would strengthen the claims.
- Missing stronger count-based dependency baselines: Given the claim that typed dependencies are key, it would be fair to include a strong unlexicalized dependency-based count baseline (e.g., first-order dependency contexts with PPMI and cosine; possibly with SVD) without APT alignment to separate gains due to typing from gains due to APT-specific alignment/higher-order modeling.
- Scalability and practicality: The APT spaces (80k–230k dimensions for nouns) are large; there is no runtime/memory analysis, nor discussion of scalability to larger corpora or longer phrases. Since many practitioners favor embeddings for efficiency, a brief complexity assessment and discussion of approximation/feature selection would help.

Questions to authors
- Can you report uncertainty over items (e.g., bootstrap CIs) for the key ρ values, and provide a per-item analysis to show stability of improvements across the 90 compounds?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of “most frequent” dependency relation for compounds that occur with both NMOD and AMOD? What happens if you keep both and marginalize?
- Could you include a dependency-based count baseline without APT alignment (typed first-order contexts with PPMI, cosine) to isolate the contribution of alignment and higher-order paths?
- How do results change if you control for frequency via partial correlation or a regression that includes log frequency and length as covariates?
- What is the computational cost (time/memory) of building APTs and composing them compared to CBOW? Any feature pruning or hashing applied?
- Do the gains of aligned APTs appear for other constructions (e.g., adjective–noun, verb–object) where alignment seems more critical? Any preliminary evidence?

Additional comments
- Please clarify the search ranges/grids for h and q. Report best values to aid reproducibility.
- The “reference suppressed” citations for APTs and smoothing are problematic for replication; please include full references and, ideally, code/data release.
- Consider evaluating with GloVe or dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014b) as additional baselines, given their stronger modeling of syntactic context.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-motivated, carefully executed study with clear empirical gains and useful methodological insights about leveraging typed dependency paths for compositionality detection. The main limitations are the narrow, small-scale evaluation and some untested preprocessing choices. With stronger baselines, uncertainty quantification, and broader evaluation, this would be a solid contribution. I lean toward acceptance if the venue tolerates single-dataset studies and the authors can address analysis concerns in the rebuttal.
