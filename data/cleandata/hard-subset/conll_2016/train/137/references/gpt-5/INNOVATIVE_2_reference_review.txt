Summary and contributions
- The paper studies compositionality detection for noun compounds by leveraging typed, higher-order dependency features within an Anchored Packed Trees (APT) framework. It contrasts three composition regimes: aligned APTs (with path-offset alignment), unaligned APTs, and a hybrid combination, and compares them to naïve additive composition over neural embeddings.
- Main contributions (as I see them):
  1) A principled, syntax-aware compositional approach (APT) for predicting phrasal representations and using composed–observed similarity to infer compositionality, with a novel hybrid of aligned and unaligned typed-dependency spaces. The hybrid achieves the best reported correlation (ρ≈0.79) on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, surpassing prior work (ρ=0.714) and neural baselines.
  2) Empirical evidence that typed higher-order dependency paths materially help compositionality detection, and that combining aligned and unaligned views can be complementary.
  3) A careful experimental setup to avoid trivial overlap between constituents and compounds (by collapsing compounds into single tokens and not letting their contexts leak into constituents), plus a comparative analysis of weighting schemes (PPMI with context smoothing vs normalized counts) and word2vec configurations.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and direction: The paper pushes a syntax-aware, composition-as-alignment view (via APTs) to a task that is often tackled with naïve (order-agnostic) composition. The hybrid treatment of aligned and unaligned typed paths is a neat, non-obvious idea that appears to add complementary evidence. This is an interesting direction beyond incremental tweaks to similarity functions.
- Strong empirical gains on a canonical benchmark: On Reddy et al.’s 90 compounds, the hybrid APT approach achieves ρ≈0.79 vs the prior 0.714 and neural cbow baselines around 0.74. Improvements are consistent across parameterizations (α=0.75/1), with clear trends (⊔UNI generally outperforming ⊔INT; hybrid > unaligned > aligned). This suggests the core idea is robust.
- Careful control to prevent trivial composition: The preprocessing that collapses compounds into single tokens and excludes compound contexts from constituent representations is important and often overlooked; it makes intersective composition non-trivial and strengthens the validity of the evaluation.
- Insightful analysis of design choices: The discussion of alignment vs no alignment for NMOD compounds, the role of higher-order paths, and the effect of PMI smoothing vs shifting shows the authors understand when and why the method works. The finding that h and q fall in [0.3, 0.5] (modifier and unaligned signals slightly more informative) is informative.
- Fair neural baselines and negative results reported: The paper runs cbow/skip-gram with multiple dimensions and subsampling thresholds on the same corpus and reports that cbow is strongest; it also transparently reports that shifted PMI hurt performance, with a plausible explanation tied to compound sparsity.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and dataset size: All results hinge on a single, small dataset (90 compounds). While the gains are sizable, the generality to other compound sets, languages, and other MWE types (e.g., verb–object, phrasal verbs, idioms) is unknown. This is particularly important for an approach that leans on typed dependency paths and higher-order features.
- Potential confounds in preprocessing and labeling: For compounds with multiple observed dependency relations, selecting the most frequent relation may mischaracterize some tokens. The decision to ignore dependency paths involving the modifier’s modifiers (e.g., “recently graduated student”) could bias which contexts the model learns to predict, and may favor the chosen composition setup. A sensitivity analysis would help.
- Statistical testing and tuning on a tiny testbed: The paper reports very small errors (≤0.0015) and declares differences >0.005 as significant, but details on the significance testing procedure are sparse. Given repeated 3-fold CV over 90 items and a large hyperparameter sweep (h, q, alignment mode, PPMI α, counting vs PPMI), there is risk of overfitting to this dataset. Clarifying the resampling protocol and correction for multiple comparisons would strengthen claims.
- Comparisons could be broader: The baselines focus on Reddy’s weighted addition and word2vec. It would be useful to include (or at least discuss) stronger contemporary baselines that also inject syntax (e.g., dependency-based embeddings, GloVe with subword/phrase handling), and models tailored to compositionality detection (e.g., Hermann et al. 2012; more recent MWE-specific methods). This would help isolate whether typed paths per se drive the gains.
- Practicality and scalability not addressed: APTs with typed higher-order dependency features can be large (80k–230k+ dimensions mentioned). The paper would benefit from reporting footprint, runtime, and whether similar gains hold when aggressively pruning features or using compressed representations.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide more detail on the repeated 3-fold CV protocol: number of repetitions, how parameter selection was nested with respect to folds, and how you computed confidence intervals/significance? Did you correct for multiple comparisons across many configurations?
- For compounds with multiple syntactic relations, how often did the most-frequent relation differ from others, and how sensitive are results to this choice? Could you report performance split by relation type (NMOD vs others; AMOD vs NMOD modifiers)?
- How do results change when restricting to first-order paths only vs including second-order paths for the APT features? An ablation isolating the contribution of higher-order paths would clarify their value.
- For the neural baselines, did you train embeddings that include compound tokens as single units (as you did for APT), and if so, what were their frequencies? Could data sparsity of compound tokens be disproportionately hurting neural baselines?
- What is the computational cost and memory footprint of the APT models (aligned, unaligned, hybrid) on ukWaC? Are there pruning/approximation strategies that keep most of the performance?

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept. The paper advances a structurally grounded, compositional approach to detecting non-compositionality and shows clear gains over prior work on a standard dataset. While the evaluation scope is narrow and some methodological details should be tightened, the core idea—typed higher-order dependency composition with alignment/hybridization—is interesting and worth discussion at CoNLL. Expanding evaluation and clarifying the statistical protocol would further strengthen the submission.
