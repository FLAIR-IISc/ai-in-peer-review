Summary
This paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by comparing composed phrase representations to observed (holistic) phrase representations. It evaluates whether maintaining syntactic structure in distributional features (typed dependency paths, including higher-order paths) improves the prediction of human compositionality ratings. The authors use Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) to encode complete dependency paths and explore three composition settings: aligned (offsetting dependents into the head’s feature space), unaligned, and a hybrid linear combination. On the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds) with ukWaC, APT-based models outperform both a replication of the Reddy baseline (weighted addition over untyped windows) and word2vec baselines, achieving up to ρ=0.79 vs 0.71 (Reddy) and 0.74 (best CBOW). They analyze the effects of PPMI smoothing, composition operator (add vs min), and weighting of head/modifier, and discuss why subsampling hurts in this low-frequency phrase setting.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: Typed higher-order dependency features, operationalized via APTs, yield substantial gains for compound compositionality detection over untyped models and over common neural baselines. The hybrid APT (mixing aligned and unaligned) achieves the best ρ=0.79.
2) Methodological refinement for composition: Application and adaptation of APT composition to noun compounds, including offset alignment and a simple but effective hybrid that combines aligned and unaligned features while preventing leakage from compound contexts into constituents.
3) Analysis/ablations: Systematic exploration of weighting, composition operators, PPMI smoothing (α), and subsampling, with informative observations (e.g., modifiers slightly more informative; subsampling analogous to shifted PMI can harm low-frequency compounds).

Strengths
- Clear empirical improvements with justification: The APT models consistently outperform both the prior state-of-the-art (Reddy 0.71) and tuned word2vec baselines (best CBOW 0.74), despite strong frequency correlations (baseline 0.63). This supports the claim that typed dependency information is valuable for compositionality detection, not just for general semantic similarity.
- Careful experimental protocol and ablations: The paper varies composition operators (add/min), alignment vs unaligned vs hybrid, and PPMI smoothing, and reports consistent trends. The repeated 3-fold CV to set h and q adds methodological rigor, and the authors discuss why certain settings (e.g., shifted PMI or aggressive subsampling) underperform given low phrase frequencies.
- Sensible handling of data to avoid trivial leakage: The corpus preprocessing collapses compounds into single tokens to build observed phrase representations while preventing those exact contexts from populating constituent representations. This directly tests the ability of composition to recover observed features from constituent distributions.
- Linguistically motivated features: The use of typed higher-order dependency paths and offset alignment is well-motivated, and the paper gives intuitive examples (e.g., how AMOD-prefixed second-order features of adjectives align with first-order noun features), helping interpretability of what composition is doing.
- Useful ancillary insights: Findings such as “modifier slightly more informative than head” and “frequency correlates with compositionality” are valuable to practitioners and help interpret model behavior.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of the core method: APTs and alignment are cited as prior work by the authors (“reference suppressed”). The primary new elements here are the specific application to noun compounds and the hybrid linear mixture, which is a straightforward heuristic. The methodological novelty is therefore incremental.
- Narrow evaluation scope: Results rest on a single small dataset (90 compounds). Even with repeated 3-fold CV, statistical claims like “differences > 0.005 are significant” seem optimistic given test folds of ~30 items; more detail on the significance testing is needed. Generalization beyond this dataset is unclear.
- Baseline coverage: The neural baselines are untyped CBOW/SGNS with proximity windows. A stronger and highly relevant baseline would be dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg, 2014), which directly encode typed dependency contexts; not including them weakens the comparative claim that typed contexts per se are responsible for the gains.
- Insufficient analysis of key empirical outcome: Unaligned APTs outperform aligned APTs, and the hybrid is best. This is counterintuitive given the alignment rationale. The paper offers limited linguistic or statistical analysis of why this happens (e.g., what types of features each variant captures, or case studies of compounds where alignment helps/hurts).
- Reproducibility and presentation gaps: Some technical details are suppressed (APT prior work), and certain implementation choices (feature pruning, reduction constraints, exact hyperparameter grids, number of CV repetitions) are not fully specified. References are messy, and one table referred to in text appears missing. There is no code or data release plan.

Questions to Authors
- Novelty: Please clarify precisely what is novel here relative to your prior APT work. Is the hybrid mixture new? Are the higher-order path constraints or the reduction procedure different? What is the conceptual contribution beyond empirical validation on compounds?
- Baselines: Can you include dependency-based word embeddings (e.g., SGNS with dependency contexts) as baselines? This would directly test whether typed contexts alone explain the gains, independent of the APT machinery.
- Statistics: How many repetitions of 3-fold CV did you run? How were significance tests conducted on Spearman ρ over small folds? Please report variance/CI and justify the 0.005 significance threshold.
- Analysis of alignment vs unaligned: Can you provide qualitative and quantitative analyses that show which features the aligned vs unaligned models recover, and under what syntactic configurations alignment helps/hurts? Any error breakdown by head/modifier POS, lexicalized relations, or frequency bins?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, preprocessing scripts (compound collapsing and dependency adjustments), and configuration files, to facilitate independent verification? If not, please provide fuller implementation details (feature vocab sizes after pruning, reduction rules, α and shift grids, h and q grids).

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper with clear, well-motivated features and strong results on a recognized benchmark. Its main limitation is the constrained evaluation and incremental methodological novelty. I lean toward weak accept if stronger baselines and additional analysis can be added; otherwise, it remains a valuable contribution but arguably narrow in scope.
