Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), a typed-dependency distributional representation with a composition procedure that aligns modifier and head feature spaces via path offset/reduction. The authors compare aligned, unaligned, and hybrid APT composition against additive composition of neural word embeddings on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.
- The primary empirical claim is that hybrid APT composition achieves higher Spearman correlation with human compositionality judgments (ρ ≈ 0.79) than both the Reddy baseline (0.71) and CBOW/SG (best ≈ 0.74), suggesting typed dependency paths and the hybrid alignment strategy are beneficial.
- A secondary contribution is an exploration of composition operators (union vs. intersection), PPMI smoothing (α), and weighting parameters (h, q), with the observation that unaligned and aligned features capture complementary information.

Strengths
- Focused, well-motivated task. Compositionality detection avoids confounding phrase similarity evaluations with non-compositionality and is appropriate for assessing whether a compositional model recovers observed phrasal behavior.
- Methodological clarity of the APT framework. The path-offset and reduction mechanism makes the alignment rationale explicit, grounded in typed dependency paths. The toy example tables and definitions make the approach understandable.
- Systematic exploration of design choices. The paper compares aligned, unaligned, and hybrid APTs; two composition operators; different PPMI settings; and multiple hyperparameters, offering useful insights (e.g., hybrid > un/aligned alone; union generally better than intersection).
- Empirical improvements on a known benchmark. On Reddy’s dataset, the reported correlations exceed both the original count-based baseline and simple neural additive composition. The addition of a frequency baseline is also helpful, showing a non-trivial task.
- Attempt to mitigate leakage. The authors explicitly prevent compound-derived contexts from populating constituent representations, which is an important detail for a fair composition-vs-observation comparison.

Weaknesses
- Very limited and potentially overfit evaluation. All results are on a single, small dataset (90 compounds). The model involves numerous hyperparameters and design choices selected via repeated 3-fold CV; with such a small test bed, the risk of overfitting and selection bias is high. Claims of significance (differences > 0.005) are not supported by appropriate tests for dependent correlations; reporting tiny standard errors from repeated resampling does not substitute for proper significance testing (e.g., permutation or Steiger tests).
- Missing strong and fair baselines. The comparison to neural models is limited to vanilla CBOW/SG with additive composition on untyped contexts. Given the emphasis on typed dependencies, at minimum, dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg, 2014), and/or other dependency-aware neural models should be included. Likewise, stronger compositional baselines such as lexical function/tensor models, or supervised compositionality models (e.g., Hermann et al., 2012) trained on the same corpus, are missing. As written, it is unclear whether the gains stem from the APT composition per se or simply from using typed contexts.
- Preprocessing and potential confounds. The corpus is modified to merge the 90 target compounds into single tokens and to rewrite dependency paths. This changes the distributional landscape from which both observed and constituent vectors are derived. The choice to keep only the majority dependency relation per compound and to ignore modifier-internal modifiers may materially affect the observed phrasal vectors that the composed vectors are compared against. An ablation without corpus rewrites (or with alternative choices) is needed to rule out artifacts.
- Practicality and robustness concerns. APT spaces are very high-dimensional (80k–230k per noun with first/second-order paths). There is no discussion of memory/runtime costs, pruning thresholds, or scalability to larger vocabularies, longer paths, or broader phrase types. Results rely on automatic dependency parsing (Malt) on web data; the paper acknowledges parsing noise but provides no sensitivity analysis to parse quality or to alternative parsers/schemes.
- Incomplete details and presentation issues. Important implementation details are missing (vocabulary cutoffs, context pruning, exact number of CV repetitions, stopping criteria). Several references are malformed or incomplete. Table 4 is referenced but not shown in the provided text; more comprehensive reporting would help reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Can you include results with dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) and/or other dependency-aware neural baselines, using the same corpus and the same evaluation protocol?
- How many CV repetitions were used, and what statistical tests were applied to compare correlated ρ values across models? Please report p-values or confidence intervals from appropriate tests.
- How sensitive are results to the corpus rewriting step? Provide an ablation where compounds are not merged, and where multiple dependency relations per compound are preserved.
- What pruning thresholds were used for APT feature spaces? Please report average number of active features per word, memory footprint, and training time.
- Can you add an ablation that uses typed constituent-based baselines (e.g., cosine between compound and each constituent in the same typed space) to isolate the benefit of your composition procedure from the benefit of typed contexts?

Overall assessment
While the paper is clearly written and the APT alignment idea is methodologically sound, the empirical validation is too narrow, the choice of baselines is incomplete (particularly for typed/dependency-aware models), and several preprocessing decisions may confound the conclusions. Given the emphasis on established baselines and rigorous validation for new methods, I am not convinced the evidence suffices to support the claimed advantages beyond this small dataset. I recommend rejection in the current form, with a path to strengthening via broader, fairer comparisons, additional datasets, proper significance testing, and clearer scalability and reproducibility details.
