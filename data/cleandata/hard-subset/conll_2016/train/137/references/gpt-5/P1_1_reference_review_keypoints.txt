- Paper shows typed, higher-order dependency features composed via APTs achieve state-of-the-art noun-compound compositionality detection (Spearman ρ up to 0.79) on Reddy et al. (2011).
- Authors compare APT variants (aligned, unaligned, hybrid; intersective vs additive; PPMI vs counts; α-smoothing) against CBOW/skip-gram baselines trained on ukWaC.
- Empirical contribution: typed, higher-order dependency features outperform untyped proximity models and neural embeddings trained on the same data.
- Methodological insight: unaligned typed features are highly informative and combining aligned+unaligned (hybrid) improves performance.
- Evaluation contribution: thorough ablation over weighting, smoothing, composition operators, and neural hyperparameters clarifies important factors (e.g., subsampling harms low-frequency compounds).
- Strength: Clear empirical gains with fair baselines (ρ=0.79 vs 0.71 Reddy and 0.74 CBOW).
- Strength: Well-motivated experiments isolating typing, higher-order paths, alignment, and union vs intersection operations.
- Strength: Preprocessing prevents data leakage by merging compounds into single tokens and protecting constituent representations from phrase contexts.
- Strength: Careful analysis of weighting/smoothing choices; plausible explanation for why shifted PMI hurts due to sparsity.
- Strength: Neural baselines calibrated on same data; finding that subsampling degrades rare compound reps is practically useful.
- Weakness: Limited novelty — APT framework and alignment are mostly from prior work; contribution is mainly application/comparison.
- Weakness: Small, narrow evaluation — only 90 NN compounds limits statistical power and external validity; no tests on constituent literality or other phrase types.
- Weakness: Heavy computational requirements — dependency-parsing 1.9B tokens and large typed feature spaces with no cost/runtime/memory discussion.
- Weakness: Missing comparisons to dependency-based neural embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg) or GloVe trained on ukWaC and prior compound-specific models.
- Weakness: Limited error analysis — no per-item breakdown, frequency-bin analysis, or syntactic-relation breakdown despite strong FREQ baseline (ρ=0.63) suggesting confounds.
- Question: Can you report constituent-level compositionality correlations (head and modifier) and whether aligned vs unaligned APTs differ there?
- Question: What are the APT vocabulary/feature matrix sizes, and what are typical training and inference times? Will you share implementation/code?
- Question: Did you evaluate dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg) as a baseline, and how would they compare to APTs?
- Question: How robust are results to parsing errors or different parsers, given many co-occurring lemmas lacked dependency links?
- Question: Were hybrid weighting parameters q and h tried conditioned on relation type or frequency, or learned per-compound?
- Question: Can the approach generalize to AN or VN compositions where alignment might be more critical, and are there preliminary results beyond NN compounds?
- Additional comment: Clarify whether cross-validation folds were stratified by compound frequency to control for the frequency–compositionality correlation.
- Additional comment: Consider releasing the preprocessed ukWaC with merged compound tokens and retained dependency paths for replication.
- Additional comment: Fix a few malformed bibliographic references.
