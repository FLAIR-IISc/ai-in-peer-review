Summary and main contributions
- Empirical study of compositionality detection for English noun–noun compounds comparing: (i) typed higher-order dependency features via Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) with several composition variants (aligned, unaligned, hybrid; min vs weighted addition; PPMI vs counts), and (ii) neural word embeddings (word2vec CBOW/SG). Evaluation is Spearman correlation with human ratings on the Reddy et al. (2011) 90-compound dataset.
- Introduction of a hybrid composition that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APT representations, yielding small gains over either alone.
- Observation that typed dependency features (higher-order paths) outperform untyped window-based representations for this task, with best reported ρ ≈ 0.79 exceeding the Reddy baseline (0.71) and CBOW (0.74) trained on the same corpus.

Strengths
- Clear task framing and motivation: compositionality detection avoids confounding in phrase similarity evaluation and is appropriate for probing compositional models. The paper articulates why typed syntax should help and operationalizes this through dependency paths.
- Systematic comparison across several reasonable choices: counts vs PPMI (including α-smoothing), composition operators (min/add, weights), alignment vs non-alignment, and neural baselines (CBOW/SG with multiple dims and subsampling). This breadth helps interpret where gains are coming from.
- Use of a large, established corpus (ukWaC) and dependency parsing to build typed contexts; reasonable care to avoid trivial leakage from observed compound contexts into constituent vectors.
- Results improve over an established baseline (Reddy et al. 2011) and over tuned CBOW on the same data, supporting the central claim that typed, structured contexts are useful for this problem.
- The hybrid APT result suggests complementary signals between aligned and unaligned typed features, which is an interesting empirical finding.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope and risk of overfitting: conclusions are based on a single, very small dataset (n=90 compounds) with extensive model and hyperparameter exploration (multiple composition schemes, weighting, smoothing, alignment choices). Even with repeated 3-fold CV, the tiny test sets and multiple comparisons make 0.01–0.03 differences hard to trust as robust. No external validation (e.g., other compound datasets, MWEs, or other languages).
- Missing strong baselines. The comparison omits: (a) dependency-based word embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014), (b) strong count-based typed-dependency baselines without APT alignment (simple PPMI dependency contexts + weighted addition), and (c) prior noun-compound models (e.g., Hermann et al. 2012) on the same corpus. Given the paper’s own finding that the unaligned APT outperforms the aligned version, a simpler typed-dependency model may close most of the gap; this is not established.
- The core methodological novelty (alignment via APTs and path reduction) is not supported by the results: unaligned APT generally beats aligned, and the hybrid’s gain over unaligned is small. This undermines the necessity of APT alignment for compounds, making the main innovation more of an engineering variant than a demonstrated advance.
- Statistical analysis is under-specified. The paper claims errors ≤ 0.0015 and significance for deltas > 0.005, but does not report the number of repetitions, fold splits, or appropriate tests comparing correlations across models. With n=90 and repeated CV on overlapping items, such tight error bars are not credible without more detail. No significance testing is reported against the best neural baseline or between APT variants.
- Preprocessing/design choices may bias results and reduce reproducibility: compounds are recombined into single tokens, the “most frequent” internal dependency relation is chosen, modifier-internal paths are dropped, similarity is computed only on first-order features, and shifted PMI results are omitted. These decisions may advantage the proposed model yet are insufficiently ablated. Implementation details (feature pruning, frequency thresholds, relation inventories) and code/data are not provided.

Questions to authors
- Please include baselines with (i) dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg 2014) trained on the same corpus and (ii) simple count-based typed-dependency PPMI vectors with weighted additive composition. How do these compare to unaligned and hybrid APT?
- How many repetitions were used in repeated 3-fold CV? Provide per-split results, standard deviations, and statistical tests comparing top APT vs top CBOW and among APT variants. Consider a permutation test over correlations.
- What feature pruning thresholds and dependency relation sets were used? Any cutoff on path counts or PMI values? Please release the list of dependency types and path orders retained.
- How sensitive are results to the “compound recombination” step? Can you report performance without recombining compounds, and/or controlling for compounds’ frequency (e.g., stratified by frequency) to show robustness?
- Provide ablations on path order (only 1st vs up to 2nd) and on the decision to restrict the similarity comparison to first-order features. Does the advantage persist under these changes?

Overall recommendation
While the paper presents promising numbers and a well-motivated use of typed dependency contexts, the empirical evidence is too narrow, key baselines are missing, and the main claimed methodological advance (APT alignment) is not convincingly supported for compounds. Given CoNLL’s standards for rigorous validation against strong baselines and concerns about robustness on a very small dataset, I do not recommend acceptance in its current form.
