- Paper compares naive compositional operations on standard vector spaces with anchored packed trees (APTs) for noun–noun compositionality detection.  
- APTs encode typed, higher-order dependency paths and use alignment (offset + reduction) to bring dependents into head feature space.  
- Evaluated aligned, unaligned, and hybrid APTs on Reddy et al. (2011) 90-compound dataset versus word2vec (CBOW/SG) and prior baselines.  
- Best result: hybrid APT achieves Spearman ρ ≈ 0.79, outperforming CBOW (~0.74) and Reddy baseline (~0.71).  
- Main empirical claim: typed, higher-order dependency features via APTs substantially improve noun-compound compositionality detection.  
- Methodological contribution: formalization of alignment through offsetting and reduction, plus a hybrid composition mixing aligned and unaligned representations.  
- Experimental contribution: thorough parameter study (PPMI smoothing, composition operators, constituent weights, subsampling) and corpus preprocessing to prevent leakage.  
- Strength: strong empirical gains with clear ablations showing complementary information from aligned and unaligned features.  
- Strength: alignment via path offsetting is linguistically motivated and operationalized clearly.  
- Strength: transparent experimental setup noting pitfalls (e.g., avoiding compound-context leakage) and rationale for choices.  
- Strength: competitive baselines and tuning (CBOW/SG variants) strengthen attribution of gains to dependency structure.  
- Strength: insightful observations about NMOD prevalence, modifier informativeness, and optimal h/q ranges.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a single small dataset (90 items), risking optimistic estimates and requiring uncertainty measures and additional datasets.  
- Weakness: strong FREQ baseline (ρ=0.63) suggests frequency confound; need partial-correlation/regression analysis and per-frequency-bin results.  
- Weakness: preprocessing/parsing biases (merging compounds, single-relation choice, removed modifiers’ modifiers) lack sensitivity analysis or error breakdown.  
- Weakness: missing stronger count-based dependency baselines (typed first-order PPMI ± SVD) to isolate alignment/higher-order gains.  
- Weakness: scalability concerns—large APT dimensionality (80k–230k) with no runtime/memory analysis or approximation strategies.  
- Question: report bootstrap CIs/per-item analysis for key ρ values to show stability across compounds?  
- Question: sensitivity to choosing a single “most frequent” dependency relation vs keeping/marginalizing multiple relations?  
- Question: can you include a typed dependency count baseline without APT alignment to isolate alignment benefits?  
- Question: how do results change when controlling for frequency via partial correlation or regression with covariates?  
- Question: what is the computational cost (time/memory) of building and composing APTs versus CBOW; any feature pruning/hashing?  
- Question: do aligned APT gains generalize to other constructions (adj–noun, verb–object); any preliminary evidence?  
- Additional: clarify search ranges/grids for h and q and report best values for reproducibility.  
- Additional: provide full references for APTs/smoothing and ideally release code/data for replication.  
- Additional: consider additional baselines such as GloVe or dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014b).  
- Overall: well-motivated and carefully executed with clear gains and insights, but limited by small-scale evaluation and untested preprocessing choices; leans toward acceptance if analyses and stronger baselines are addressed.
