- Investigates compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by comparing typed dependency-based higher-order count models (APTs) against additive neural embedding composition, evaluated on Reddy et al.’s 90-compound dataset via correlation with human judgments.  
- Introduces a hybrid APT composition combining aligned (path-offset) and unaligned typed dependency features, achieving ρ up to 0.79 and surpassing prior count-based SOTA (0.714) and tuned CBOW embeddings (0.74).  
- Analyzes design choices: typed vs untyped contexts, aligned vs unaligned composition, dependency path order, and weighting schemes (PPMI variants, union vs intersection); finds small gains from context-distribution smoothing and that embedding subsampling can hurt low-frequency regimes.  
- Strength: Principled use of typed, higher-order dependency paths with offsetting/reduction to align spaces for composition, leveraging syntax and yielding empirical gains over untyped baselines.  
- Strength: Thoughtful preprocessing to collapse compounds into single tokens and prevent leakage, strengthening the validity of comparing composed and observed vectors.  
- Strength: Thorough ablation study within the APT family (aligned/unaligned/hybrid, union vs intersection, PPMI α, where to apply weighting), with insightful findings (unaligned typed features strong; hybrid helps).  
- Strength: Competitive and well-tuned CBOW baseline (ρ=0.74) that exceeds prior untyped count-based results, supporting the claim that typed dependency features still add value.  
- Strength: Practical insights that shifted PMI and aggressive subsampling can harm representations of low-frequency compounds.  
- Weakness: Evaluation limited to a single small dataset (90 compounds), with a strong frequency baseline (ρ=0.63) raising concerns about frequency confounds.  
- Weakness: Missing strong typed-context baselines (e.g., dependency-based SGNS, lexical function, tensor/matrix models) trained on the same corpus to isolate the effect of syntactic typing vs model family.  
- Weakness: Incomplete methodological details for the “CF” setting (PPMI after composition); unclear normalization of pseudo-counts from union/intersection and how global marginals are computed, affecting reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Heavy dependence on Malt parser outputs on web text without robustness checks (alternative parsers, gold subsets, or error impact quantification), risking fragility due to parsing errors.  
- Weakness: Presentation gaps and missing materials (e.g., referenced Table 4 not present, terse notation for reduction constraints and APT details), hindering clarity and reproducibility.  
- Question: Precisely how is PMI computed “after composition” in CF—what counts/denominators are used and is negative PMI clipped to PPMI?  
- Question: How many repetitions of 3-fold CV were run, and what statistical test supports the “differences > 0.005 are significant at 95%” claim (CV variability vs bootstrap)?  
- Question: Can the authors report partial correlations controlling for compound frequency or results stratified by frequency bins to quantify the frequency confound?  
- Question: Please add a dependency-based embedding baseline (typed contexts) and a lexical function model trained on the same corpus to test whether typing or model family drives improvements.  
- Question: Were constituent embeddings for neural baselines trained on the preprocessed corpus with compounds merged (removing constituent co-occurrences inside target compounds) to ensure fair comparison with APT preprocessing?  
- Question: Do APT compositions predict constituent-level literality (modifier vs head) better than embeddings—please report ρ for constituent-level scores as in Reddy et al.?  
- Question: What is the memory/time overhead of APTs at order 2 (80k–230k dims per noun) and alignment at inference time; what feature pruning thresholds were used?  
- Overall assessment: A well-motivated, carefully analyzed application of typed higher-order dependency features with an effective hybrid composition and strong improvements, but limited by narrow evaluation, missing typed baselines, and reproducibility/statistical reporting gaps that should be addressed.
