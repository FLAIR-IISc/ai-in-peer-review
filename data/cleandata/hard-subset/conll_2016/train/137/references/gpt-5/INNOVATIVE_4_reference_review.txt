Summary and contributions
This paper investigates detecting the compositionality of noun–noun compounds by comparing observed (holistic) phrase representations with composed representations built from the constituents. The key idea is to use typed, higher-order dependency features via Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), align dependent and head spaces through syntactic path offsetting, and compare aligned, unaligned, and hybrid (mixture) composition. Experiments on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds) with ukWaC show the hybrid APT approach achieving higher Spearman correlation with human judgments than prior baselines and neural embeddings.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) Empirical finding: Typed higher-order dependency features and a hybrid of aligned and unaligned APT composition improve compositionality detection, outperforming both untyped vector baselines and neural embeddings (best ρ ≈ 0.79 vs Reddy 0.71; CBOW ≈ 0.74).
2) Methodological refinement: A systematic comparison of aligned, unaligned, and hybrid composition in a typed dependency space, including path-offsetting and reduction, and using composed vs observed first-order features as a compositionality signal.
3) Analysis of weighting and smoothing: Evidence that modifiers contribute slightly more than heads (optimal h, q ≈ 0.3–0.5) and that smoothing choices (PPMI α=0.75 vs 1; subsampling) interact with low-frequency compounds.

Strengths
- Linguistically grounded, interpretable modeling: The APT framework retains full dependency paths and aligns spaces via explicit syntactic offsets. This is a principled and transparent alternative to black-box composition, and the hybrid variant is a simple, plausible idea that yields real gains.
- Clear empirical gains on a canonical benchmark: The method substantially outperforms the strong Reddy et al. baseline and also beats tuned CBOW/SG settings on the same corpus. The improvement over neural embeddings is notable given ongoing skepticism about count-based models.
- Careful experimental framing to reduce leakage: The preprocessing step to retokenize compounds and exclude compound-derived features from constituent APTs addresses a common pitfall in compositionality work. The authors also explore PPMI vs counts and report consistent trends across settings.
- Useful negative insights: The observation that shifted PMI and aggressive subsampling hurt here (due to low-frequency compounds) is practically valuable, and the reported optimal weights suggest interesting asymmetries in modifier/head informativeness.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior APT work: The core APT machinery (typed higher-order features, alignment via path offsetting, reduction) appears to be introduced “elsewhere.” The main new elements are application to compositionality detection and the hybrid composition variant. The paper should clarify what is genuinely new; as-is, the contribution feels more like a well-executed application and small methodological tweak than a fundamentally new model.
- Small, narrow evaluation: Results are only on 90 compounds from a single dataset and a single corpus/language, and only for noun–noun compounds. No tests on other phrase types (Adj–N, V–O) or larger MWE/compositionality datasets. This makes it hard to assess robustness and generality.
- Missing stronger baselines: The study compares to CBOW/SG on proximity contexts but omits dependency-based embeddings (e.g., word2vecf/contexts from Levy & Goldberg) and GloVe. Given the focus on typed dependencies, a dependency-embedding baseline is important and could narrow the gap.
- Statistical reporting and risk of overfitting: The claim that standard errors ≤ 0.0015 on a 90-item set seems optimistic; details of repetitions, fold splits, and exact testing protocol are needed. Hyperparameters h and q are tuned via repeated 3-fold CV on a tiny dataset; overfitting is a concern. Please report confidence intervals and formal significance tests for key model comparisons (e.g., hybrid APT vs CBOW).
- Reproducibility and clarity issues: Some definitions and implementation details are under-specified (feature pruning, path order limits in practice, reduction edge cases, cosine computed after composition with PPMI vs counts). References contain placeholders and inconsistencies, and some notational artifacts/footnotes hinder readability. No runtime/memory discussion despite large feature spaces, which matters for scalability and adoption.

Questions to authors
1) Novelty: Precisely delineate what is new here relative to prior APT work. Is the hybrid mixture of aligned and unaligned APTs the main innovation? Are there new alignment/reduction rules or training regimes?
2) Leakage control: Can you confirm that no contexts contributed by compound tokens leak into constituent APTs? How did you audit this, especially around parser errors and multiword retokenization?
3) Baselines: Can you add comparisons to dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg’s dependency word2vec/word2vecf) and GloVe? These are particularly relevant given your emphasis on typed dependencies.
4) Statistical robustness: Please provide confidence intervals and significance tests for the main deltas (e.g., hybrid vs unaligned, hybrid vs CBOW). How many CV repetitions, and were folds stratified? Are results stable across different random seeds/folds?
5) Generalization: How does the approach fare on other compositionality datasets (e.g., adjective–noun, verb–object, phrasal verbs) or larger MWE sets? Any preliminary results?
6) Ablations: What is the contribution of second-order vs first-order paths? What happens if you cap feature space (e.g., top-k contexts) or vary α more broadly? How sensitive are results to parse errors and to the choice of dependency scheme?
7) Practicality: Provide time/memory profiles for building APTs and composing representations versus embeddings. Is there a feasible path to scale beyond 90 compounds and to downstream tasks?

Overall, I find the direction promising and the empirical gains encouraging. The paper would benefit from clarifying novelty, strengthening baselines, and broadening evaluation. If addressed, I would lean toward acceptance as a meaningful step toward syntax-aware composition for compositionality detection.
