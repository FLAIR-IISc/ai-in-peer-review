- Paper studies detection of non-compositionality for noun compounds by comparing composed vs. holistic phrase representations using an APT framework; compares aligned APT, unaligned typed-dependency composition, a hybrid, and neural (word2vec) baselines.

- Empirical claim: typed dependency-based composition (hybrid APT) improves correlation with human compositionality judgments (best ρ≈0.79), outperforming Reddy et al. (0.71) and additive word2vec (≈0.74).

- Methodological finding: hybrid linear combination of aligned and unaligned compositions outperforms either alone; PMI smoothing (α=0.75) helps modestly while PMI shifting hurts for low-frequency compounds.

- Practical pipeline: preprocessing to collapse target compounds, avoid feature leakage, and restrict similarity to first-order dependency features to reduce higher-order noise.

- Strength: Clear empirical improvement on a standard benchmark with parameter sweeps substantiating main claims.

- Strength: Convincing motivation and analysis for typed, higher-order dependency contexts and why alignment via dependency paths matters.

- Strength: Thoughtful treatment of sparsity/frequency issues and interpretable findings about modifier informativeness (optimal h and q ≈0.3–0.5).

- Strength: Reasonably careful experimental controls—same corpus/parser as Reddy et al., preprocessing to prevent leakage.

- Strength: Clear task framing arguing compositionality prediction is a principled target separate from phrase similarity.

- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to a single small dataset (90 items), reducing statistical confidence and generality; significance/variance estimation under-specified.

- Weakness: Baselines are weak/unfair—missing dependency-based embeddings and typed count baselines without APT, so gains may conflate typed contexts vs. alignment benefits.

- Weakness: Method novelty is incremental and partly opaque; key APT details and "reference suppressed" citations hinder reproducibility.

- Weakness: Preprocessing choices (collapsing compounds, fixing relations, ignoring modifiers’ modifiers) and parser errors may bias results; sensitivity analysis is lacking.

- Weakness: Reporting issues—malformed references, missing experimental details (CV repetitions, hyperparameter ranges, pruning thresholds, significance testing).

- Request: Report number of 3-fold CV repetitions and how confidence intervals/standard errors for Spearman’s ρ were computed, with variance across repetitions.

- Request: Add dependency-based embedding baselines (e.g., Levy & Goldberg) and a typed count baseline without APT alignment to isolate alignment effects.

- Request: Report sensitivity to chosen dependency relation per compound and performance on high-confidence parses to assess parser-error impact.

- Request: Provide details on APT pruning/feature selection thresholds, order handling, and any smoothing beyond PMI α.

- Request: Release code and preprocessed compound-merged corpus indices for reproducibility.

- Additional note: The finding that unaligned typed features sometimes outperform aligned ones is interesting—include qualitative analysis of contexts captured by each, and fix bibliography/clarify relation to prior APT work.
