Summary
The paper evaluates an Anchored Packed Trees (APT) compositional framework for detecting the compositionality of noun–noun compounds. Using typed, higher-order dependency features and a composition procedure that can “align” modifier/head spaces via path offsets (or not), the authors compute the cosine similarity between composed and observed (holistic) compound vectors and correlate this with human ratings (Reddy et al., 2011). Experiments on ukWaC with 90 compounds report improved Spearman correlations over prior work (up to 0.79), with unaligned and hybrid APT variants outperforming cbow/skip-gram word2vec baselines and the Reddy (2011) count-based baseline.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical finding that typed, higher-order dependency-path features (via APTs) yield higher correlations with human compositionality judgments on Reddy et al. (2011) than untyped count-based vectors and standard word2vec baselines.
- Analysis of alignment choices in APT composition (aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid), with “unaligned” and hybrid variants performing best, suggesting different signals are captured by path-offset vs vanilla dependency features.
- A reasonably systematic (though modest) parameter exploration for PPMI smoothing, composition operators, and constituent weighting, and a clear formalization of APT alignment/offsetting.

Strengths
- Methodologically careful construction of holistic compound vectors: the corpus is preprocessed to treat each target compound as a token, and the authors attempt to avoid trivial leakage by excluding compound contexts from constituent vectors. This is the right direction for evaluating composition vs observation.
- The paper isolates syntactic signal by contrasting typed dependency features (with higher-order paths) against untyped co-occurrence embeddings and finds consistent gains. This supports the claim that syntactic typing is informative for compositionality detection.
- Clear operationalization of the alignment/offset mechanism in APTs and discussion of intersective vs additive composition, with empirical comparisons (⊔INT vs ⊔UNI, PPMI smoothing, normalised counts vs composed PPMI).
- Inclusion of frequency and random baselines, and a reasonably tuned word2vec baseline (cbow vs skip-gram, windows, subsampling), showing improvements over a commonly used neural baseline on this task.
- The reported improvements over the Reddy (2011) baseline are non-trivial on this dataset, and the hybrid APT variant consistently does best among the proposed methods.

Weaknesses
- Very limited evaluation: all results are on a single, very small dataset (90 compounds). Hyperparameters are tuned via repeated 3-fold CV on the same 90 items used for reporting, and gains as small as 0.01–0.03 are interpreted as significant. With such a small n and overlapping folds, significance claims based on repeated CV are questionable. No tests for comparing dependent correlations (e.g., Williams/Steiger) are provided.
- Missing stronger and more relevant baselines: (i) dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg, 2014) with simple additive composition; (ii) count-based PPMI/SVD or GloVe with typed dependency contexts; (iii) constituent-similarity baselines from Reddy et al. (2011) replicated on the same corpus/preprocessing; (iv) prior compositionality models such as Hermann et al. (2012). As presented, it is unclear whether the gains stem from typed dependencies per se, the higher-order paths, or the specific APT alignment machinery.
- Potential confounds due to frequency and preprocessing: frequency alone correlates highly with human judgments (ρ=0.63), yet the paper does not control for frequency (e.g., partial correlation or regression). The corpus is altered to retokenize compounds and to select a single majority dependency relation; how robust are the results to these choices? The decision to ignore dependency paths entering the modifier may bias the comparison toward head-driven features.
- The key conceptual motivation (alignment/offsetting to bring POS spaces together) is not supported by results: the unaligned APT composition outperforms aligned, and the hybrid merely blends signals. This undermines the core novelty and suggests the benefit may largely come from richer typed dependency features rather than the alignment mechanism.
- Incomplete analysis and ablations: there is no error analysis, no breakdown by compound type (e.g., degree of idiomaticity), no ablation on the order of dependency paths (1st vs 2nd), and no exploration of whether results generalize beyond noun–noun compounds (e.g., AN, V–N, V–O). The claim that shifted PMI is harmful is asserted but not demonstrated (results omitted).
- Reproducibility concerns: APT is “reference suppressed,” and several implementation details are missing (exact dependency label set, feature pruning, vocabulary thresholds, how PPMI is computed post-composition). Code/data are not promised. Some references are incomplete or malformed.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide proper statistical tests for comparing dependent correlations on the same 90 items (e.g., Williams/Steiger), and report confidence intervals? Are the differences between hybrid APT and unaligned APT, and between APT and cbow, statistically significant under these tests?
- How much of the gain remains after controlling for frequency (partial correlation), or when regressing out frequency and length? Please report partial correlations or a regression analysis.
- Please include baselines with dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) and/or typed-dependency count vectors with SVD/GloVe, using the same preprocessed corpus. Also include the constituent-similarity baselines from Reddy et al. (2011) under your preprocessing.
- Provide an ablation on dependency path order: 1st-order only vs including 2nd-order. How much do higher-order paths contribute independently of alignment?
- Clarify the retokenization pipeline: how often do compounds have conflicting dependency relations? How sensitive are results to choosing the majority relation? What fraction of compound occurrences are discarded or misparsed, and how might that bias results?
- Can you add an additional dataset (e.g., other noun compound compositionality sets, phrasal verb datasets) or at least a robust split (train/dev/test) that avoids tuning and testing on the same 90 items?
- Please release code and exact preprocessing details (including the reduction operator, path constraints, feature thresholds) to ensure reproducibility.

Overall, while the idea of leveraging typed, higher-order dependency features is sound and the empirical results are promising on this small benchmark, the evaluation is too limited, the strongest alternative baselines are missing, and the core novelty (alignment) is not convincingly supported. I recommend substantial strengthening of the experimental validation before acceptance.
