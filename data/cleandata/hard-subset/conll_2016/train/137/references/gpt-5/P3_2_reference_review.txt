Summary and contributions
This paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by leveraging typed, higher-order dependency contexts and a compositional framework based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs). The core idea is to align constituent spaces via dependency-path offsetting and reduction, then compose constituent representations and compare them to observed compound vectors; the similarity correlates with human compositionality judgments (Reddy et al., 2011). The paper also contrasts aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid APT composition and compares against neural word embeddings (word2vec) and prior untyped count-based baselines.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) Empirical finding: A hybrid APT model combining aligned and unaligned typed dependency features yields state-of-the-art correlation (ρ up to 0.79) on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, outperforming both untyped neural baselines (best cbow ρ=0.74) and earlier untyped count-based methods (ρ=0.71).
2) Methodological insight: Demonstrates that typed higher-order dependency features and path-based alignment matter for composition; unaligned typed features already help, and combining aligned and unaligned signals is best.
3) Experimental analysis: Shows the effect of PMI smoothing (α=0.75), composition operators (min vs add), and constituent/head weighting, with careful corpus preprocessing to avoid leakage from phrasal contexts into constituents.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains with careful controls: The reported improvements over strong baselines are consistent across parameter settings, with repeated 3-fold CV and small reported standard errors. The frequency baseline (ρ=0.63) is strong, yet APT models substantially exceed it.
- Well-motivated use of typed dependency contexts: The work reinforces that syntactic structure is crucial for compositional inference. The offsetting/reduction mechanism is principled, aligning feature spaces according to observed dependency paths.
- Thoughtful corpus preprocessing and leakage control: Recombining compounds into single tokens while preventing their contexts from contaminating constituent vectors is important for a fair evaluation of compositional inference.
- Insightful comparative analysis of composition operators and weighting: The finding that additive composition generally beats intersective, with optimal constituent/head and aligned/unaligned weights in [0.3–0.5], is useful guidance for practitioners.
- Transparent discussion of PMI choices: The paper explains why shifted PMI and aggressive subsampling hurt low-frequency compounds, a nuance often overlooked in embedding comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior APT/higher-order dependency work: The APT framework and high-order typed features have been proposed previously (references suppressed) and in Weeds et al. (2014). The primary advance here is an application to compositionality detection and the hybrid aligned/unaligned combination. This is valuable empirically but methodologically incremental.
- Evaluation scope is narrow: Results are only on the 90-item Reddy et al. dataset. While standard, it is small and known to correlate strongly with frequency. No external datasets (other compound compositionality sets, or other constructions like adj–noun, verb–object) are reported, limiting claims about generality.
- Fairness and strength of baselines: Neural baselines are untyped proximity embeddings (cbow/sg) with small parameter sweeps. Crucially, there is no comparison to dependency-based word embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014 dependency contexts), which would be a closer match to typed APT features. Also absent are strong count-based baselines such as SVD of PPMI with typed contexts.
- Potential overfitting/hyperparameter tuning on a tiny dataset: h and q are tuned via cross-validation on only 90 items; although repeated CV helps, selection of α and operator types may still favor best-case reporting. The method becomes weakly supervised w.r.t. human ratings; this should be acknowledged and mitigated (e.g., fixed hyperparameters, external dev set).
- Reliance on parse quality and choice of δ: The approach depends on accurate dependency parses and on selecting the dominant dependency relation δ per compound. The sensitivity to parsing errors (noted for three-noun compounds) and to δ selection is not quantified. Since 95% of usable relations are NMOD, the alignment advantage is partially undercut; deeper analysis when δ varies would strengthen the case.

Questions to authors
- Can you compare against dependency-based word embeddings (SGNS with dependency contexts) and/or typed PPMI+SVD baselines? This would isolate whether APT’s gains stem from typed contexts vs the APT alignment mechanism itself.
- How sensitive are results to the choice of δ? For compounds observed with multiple relations or POS tags (modifier as N vs J), what happens if δ is taken from a parser at test time vs corpus-majority? Please report an ablation.
- Can you provide partial correlations controlling for frequency to quantify how much of the signal is explained beyond frequency?
- How robust is the approach to parse errors and low-frequency compounds? Please report performance as a function of compound frequency and parsing confidence.
- Do results extend to other constructions (e.g., adjective–noun) and to larger compositionality datasets? Any preliminary findings?

Additional comments
- Reproducibility would benefit from releasing code, APT construction details (vocabulary cutoffs, pruning of rare paths, memory footprint), and compound lists with δ assignments.
- The decision to restrict similarity to first-order features after composition is reasonable but could bias against methods that exploit higher-order evidence; an ablation including second-order similarity (with appropriate smoothing) would be informative.
- Some references are “reference suppressed,” which impedes assessing novelty; please ensure proper anonymized citation of prior APT work.
