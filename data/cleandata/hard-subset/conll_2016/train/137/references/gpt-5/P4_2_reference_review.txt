Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies detection of (non-)compositionality in noun–noun compounds by comparing compositional representations with observed (holistic) phrase representations. It contrasts neural word-embedding baselines with a typed, dependency-based distributional framework based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), including alignment via dependency-path offsetting and higher-order features. Evaluation is on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds, reporting Spearman correlation between model scores and human judgments.

Main contributions, as assessed here:
1) Empirical finding that typed, higher-order dependency features in an APT framework yield state-of-the-art correlation on Reddy et al. (2011), outperforming neural baselines and an untyped-window baseline. The best hybrid APT model reaches ρ≈0.79 vs. 0.71 (Reddy) and 0.74 (CBOW).
2) A hybrid composition scheme that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APTs, with analysis of composition operators (min-based intersection vs. weighted addition), weighting of head vs. modifier (h), and contribution of aligned vs. unaligned modifier features (q).
3) Systematic analysis of parameter choices (PPMI vs. counts with post-PPMI; context distribution smoothing α; subsampling in word2vec; dimensionality) and observations such as modifiers being slightly more informative than heads (optimal h,q≈0.3–0.5).

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with thorough ablations: The APT models consistently outperform baselines, including CBOW addition (best CBOW ρ≈0.74) and the Reddy et al. (2011) untyped model (ρ≈0.71). Reporting across aligned, unaligned, and hybrid APTs; PPMI settings (α=0.75 vs 1); and composition operators provides convincing evidence that typed dependency paths and higher-order features matter for this task. The hybrid APT result (ρ≈0.79) is a meaningful gain on a well-known benchmark.

- Clear modeling of composition with typed dependencies and higher-order paths: The paper articulates a principled alignment procedure via path-offsetting and reduction (cancellation of inverse relations), explaining why spaces for different POS must be aligned and how higher-order features in one POS correspond to lower-order features in another. This makes the approach interpretable and linguistically grounded.

- Careful handling of potential leakage and data preparation: The corpus is preprocessed so compounds are single tokens; constituent representations do not inherit compound contexts, preventing trivial overlap that would favor intersective composition. This strengthens the validity of the compositionality evaluation.

- Broad baseline coverage and fair comparisons: Both CBOW and skip-gram are tuned across dimensionality and subsampling; simple baselines (random, frequency) and a published benchmark are included. The analysis that CBOW > skip-gram and that aggressive subsampling hurts rare compounds is informative and consistent with known behavior.

- Quantitative analysis of weighting parameters: The paper reports that optimal h and q fall in [0.3, 0.5], indicating that modifiers and unaligned typed features contribute slightly more, which aligns with intuition for noun–noun compounds and adds interpretability to the results.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope (single small dataset): Results are only on 90 compounds. This raises concerns about generalizability to other MWE types (e.g., verb–object, adjective–noun beyond N–N compounds) and to larger, more diverse datasets. The small size also limits robust significance testing across systems.

- Missing stronger baselines that use typed dependencies: The comparison omits dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014 dependency contexts) or other typed-composition baselines (e.g., lexical function models) trained on the same corpus. Since the main gain seems to come from typed dependency information, including such baselines would isolate the added value of APT-specific alignment/higher-order design versus simply using typed contexts.

- Ambiguity in statistical testing: The paper states that differences >0.005 are significant at 95% based on repeated 3-fold CV with “small errors,” but the testing methodology (e.g., how errors are computed, independence of folds, use of paired tests across the same items) is not specified. Given the small dataset, results could benefit from permutation tests or paired bootstrap on item-level correlations.

- Best gains arise from unaligned/hybrid models in a setting where alignment is central: For noun–noun compounds, unaligned or hybrid APTs outperform aligned ones, suggesting that the benefit may come primarily from typed dependency features rather than the alignment procedure that is central to the APT rationale. The paper acknowledges this but offers limited analysis of why alignment underperforms here (e.g., sparsity, path order truncation, reliance on only first-order features for evaluation).

- Preprocessing choices may introduce bias without sensitivity analysis: Compounds with multiple observed relations are assigned their most frequent relation; modifier-internal modifications are ignored; similarity uses only first-order dependencies on the observed side while composed representations may include second-order contributions. The impact of these choices on performance is not quantified (e.g., results by compound frequency, by relation type, or when considering different path orders at evaluation).

Questions to authors
- Can you include comparisons with dependency-based embeddings (e.g., word2vec trained on dependency contexts, or other typed context embeddings) to disentangle the contribution of typed contexts from APT-specific alignment and higher-order design?

- How are statistical significances computed precisely? Please detail the resampling/testing protocol, the number of repetitions, and whether tests are paired over the same items. Could you provide item-level variance or confidence intervals for the top systems?

- How sensitive are the results to the choice of using only first-order features on the observed side at evaluation? Does allowing higher-order observed features change the rankings?

- What is the effect of compound frequency on performance? A breakdown of correlation by frequency bins would clarify claims about subsampling and rare compounds.

- Can you provide analyses by dependency relation (e.g., NMOD vs. AMOD) and by cases where the modifier is tagged as noun vs. adjective? This may clarify when alignment helps or hurts.

- Could you test on additional compositionality datasets or phrase types to assess generalization (e.g., verb–object MWEs, larger compound lists), or at least provide a plan and expected limitations?

- Are code and preprocessing scripts (compound recombination, APT construction, parameter settings) planned for release to facilitate replication?
