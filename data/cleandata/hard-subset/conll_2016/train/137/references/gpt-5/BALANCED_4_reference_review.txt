Summary and contributions
This paper studies compositionality detection for English noun compounds by comparing neural word embeddings with a typed, higher-order dependency-based framework built on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs). The authors propose composing constituents either after aligning modifier features via dependency-path offsetting, without alignment, or with a hybrid of both. They evaluate on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds), correlating similarity between composed and observed (holistic) compound representations with human compositionality judgments.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical finding that typed, higher-order dependency features composed with a simple additive operation (especially a hybrid of aligned and unaligned APTs) outperform both prior results on the Reddy dataset and neural word embeddings, achieving Spearman ρ≈0.79 vs 0.71 in Reddy et al. (2011) and ≈0.74 for the best cbow baseline.
2) A systematic comparison of composition strategies (aligned, unaligned, hybrid; additive vs. intersective) and weighting schemes (normalised counts vs. PPMI with context smoothing), showing that unaligned and aligned signals are complementary and that mild PMI smoothing helps.
3) Careful corpus preprocessing to reduce leakage between constituent and compound representations, and analysis indicating that modifiers contribute slightly more than heads to compositionality prediction.

Strengths
- Clear empirical improvements over prior work on a standard benchmark: The hybrid APT model achieves ρ≈0.79, improving on Reddy et al.’s 0.71 and beating reasonably tuned cbow baselines. The study also shows cbow itself can reach 0.74 on this dataset, which helps fairly contextualize the gains from typed dependency features.
- Well-motivated and interpretable use of syntax: The APT alignment via dependency-path offsetting and reduction is linguistically principled. The demonstration that unaligned modifier features (reflecting typical NN contexts) and aligned features are complementary is insightful and supports the claim that typed higher-order contexts are informative for compositionality.
- Thoughtful experimental design decisions: The authors retokenize compounds, avoid reusing compound contexts in constituent representations, and restrict evaluation to first-order features when comparing composed vs. observed vectors. They also examine the effect of PMI smoothing (α=0.75) and composition operators, and tune mixture weights (h, q) via cross-validation.
- Transparent analysis of parameter effects: Reporting that optimal h and q lie in [0.3, 0.5] is informative and suggests the modifier’s slightly greater influence, aligning with intuition for many NN compounds.

Weaknesses
- Limited and small-scale evaluation: All results are on a single, small dataset (90 items). Even though improvements are statistically tested, the small size raises concerns about robustness and generality. No evaluation on other compositionality datasets, other phrase types (e.g., AN, VO), or other languages is provided, and there is little qualitative error analysis.
- Baselines are incomplete: While cbow/skip-gram are included, there is no comparison to dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014 dependency contexts), nor to competitive count-based dependency baselines (e.g., PPMI-SVD with dependency contexts). Given the core claim about the value of typed dependency information, comparisons to such baselines are important. Additionally, only additive composition is explored for neural baselines (albeit often strong), and no recent alternatives (e.g., GloVe; dependency-informed neural embeddings) are tested.
- Methodological coupling to holistic vectors and frequency: The method predicts compositionality by comparing composed vectors to holistic compound vectors learned from the same corpus. Although the authors took steps to reduce leakage, the approach still privileges compounds with good holistic representations and does not test zero-shot scenarios (unseen compounds). The frequency baseline is already strong (ρ≈0.63), and pre-tokenizing compounds may change constituent distributions in ways that benefit models tied to holistic representations.
- Clarity and completeness: Table 4 (neural embeddings results) is referenced but missing. Notation such as CF/CS is briefly introduced and could be clearer. The number of repetitions in “repeated 3-fold CV” is not specified; standard errors are not reported (only a threshold for significance). Several references have formatting issues (“reference suppressed”, “?” placeholders). No code/resources are released, and scalability/cost of APTs (80k–230k dims) is not discussed.
- Incremental novelty: The APT framework appears to be prior work by the authors; the novelty here is primarily the application to compositionality detection and a hybrid unaligned/aligned combination plus empirical analysis. Given CoNLL standards, stronger evidence across datasets and comparisons to more capable baselines would better justify publication.

Questions to authors
- How many repetitions were used in the repeated 3-fold CV? Please report standard deviations/intervals and perform pairwise significance tests between top systems.
- Can you include comparisons to dependency-based embeddings (e.g., dependency skip-gram) and a strong count-based dependency baseline (e.g., PPMI with SVD), to isolate the benefit of APT structure versus simply using typed contexts?
- How robust are the results to excluding all occurrences of a given compound from training (i.e., zero-shot compositionality prediction)? This would address reliance on holistic vectors.
- Can you evaluate on at least one additional dataset (e.g., larger compound compositionality sets or other MWE compositionality datasets) and provide an error analysis?
- Please clarify the preprocessing: quantify remaining overlap between constituent and compound features after retokenization, and discuss how retokenization affects constituent distributions.
- Provide compute and memory costs for APTs and ablate the effect of path order (1 vs 2 vs 3), to assess scalability.

Overall recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is well-motivated, technically sound, and shows promising gains; however, the evaluation scope and baselines are too limited for a CoNLL publication, and there are clarity/reproducibility gaps. Addressing the baseline and evaluation concerns, adding zero-shot tests and another dataset, and fixing presentation issues would strengthen the case substantially.
