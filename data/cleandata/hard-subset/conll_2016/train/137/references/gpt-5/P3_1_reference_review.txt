Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies compositionality detection for English noun–noun compounds using distributional representations that explicitly encode typed, higher-order dependency paths (Anchored Packed Trees, APTs). The main idea is to compare an observed compound’s vector to a composed vector built from its constituents, using several variants: aligned APT composition (path-offset alignment), unaligned composition, and a hybrid of both. The authors evaluate on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds) and compare against word2vec-based baselines trained on the same ukWaC corpus.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) Empirical finding: Typed, higher-order dependency features (APTs) produce substantially higher correlation with human compositionality judgments (ρ up to 0.79) than untyped window-based baselines and word2vec addition on the same corpus.
2) Methodological insight: For noun–noun compounds, unaligned APT composition can outperform aligned composition, and a simple hybrid (linear combination of aligned and unaligned) yields the best results.
3) Controlled comparison to neural baselines: A careful, same-corpus comparison showing cbow > skip-gram for this task, and that standard subsampling can hurt when compound tokens are low-frequency.

Strengths
- Clear empirical improvements on a standard benchmark: The best APT model (hybrid, additive composition, PPMI with smoothing) reaches ρ ≈ 0.79 vs. 0.71 in Reddy et al. and ≈0.74 for the best cbow setting. Improvements are consistent across APT variants and appear robust to reasonable weighting choices (PPMI α=0.75 vs. 1).
- Well-motivated use of syntax and higher-order contexts: Encoding full dependency paths and leveraging the path-offset alignment idea are linguistically and computationally grounded. The head-perspective composition and reduction operation concretely operationalize compositional alignment.
- Controlled baselines and fair training setting: All models (including phrase tokens) are trained on the same, dependency-parsed ukWaC. The authors investigate a range of hyperparameters for word2vec and APTs, report that weighted addition is the strongest naive composition, and provide frequency and random baselines to contextualize the task difficulty.
- Informative analyses: The observation that optimal weights h and q are in [0.3, 0.5] suggests the modifier contributes slightly more than the head to compositionality, which aligns with linguistic intuitions for noun compounds. The discussion of why subsampling and shifted PMI hurt on low-frequency compounds is thoughtful and useful.
- Relevance beyond the task: The paper argues convincingly that compositionality detection is a cleaner way to evaluate compositional models than phrase similarity tasks confounded by non-compositionality, and points to practical uses (e.g., deciding when to smooth holistic vs. constituent representations).

Weaknesses
- Limited scope and dataset size: All results are on 90 noun–noun compounds from one dataset. This raises concerns about generalizability to other MWE types (e.g., verb–object, light verb constructions, idioms) and to larger or more diverse compound inventories. A single small dataset also amplifies the risk of overfitting to idiosyncrasies (e.g., distribution of NMOD).
- Novelty is incremental: The APT machinery and higher-order typed contexts (and alignment) build directly on prior work (e.g., Weeds et al., 2014). The main novelty here is their application to compositionality detection and the hybrid aligned–unaligned combination. The methodological leap is modest relative to prior APT and lexical function work.
- Potential confounds in evaluation design: The corpus is preprocessed to retokenize compounds and select a single, most frequent dependency relation per compound. This choice may advantage dependency-typed models and underrepresent variation (e.g., AMOD vs. NMOD realizations, parsing errors). Moreover, similarity is computed over first-order features only, while composed representations are built from higher-order dependencies; more ablation is needed to show that improvements stem from composition rather than correlation with head-side contexts.
- Missing baselines and deeper comparisons: Stronger baselines such as dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg, 2014), lexical function models (Baroni & Zamparelli, 2010), and supervised generative/compositionality models (Hermann et al., 2012) on the same corpus are absent. Given the syntactic nature of the features, a fair comparison to dependency-based embeddings would be especially informative.
- Statistical reporting and reproducibility details: The paper claims differences >0.005 are significant with “sufficiently many repetitions,” but does not specify the number of repetitions, the test used, or confidence intervals. Computational costs (feature dimensionality is large: ~80k–230k) and scalability of APT composition/alignment are not discussed, nor is sensitivity to parse errors and dependency schemes.

Questions to authors
- Can you break down performance by dependency relation type (e.g., NMOD vs. AMOD vs. others) and by modifier POS? When does aligned composition help/hurt relative to unaligned?
- How many repetitions of 3-fold CV were used? Please report confidence intervals or standard deviations and the statistical test employed for comparing ρ.
- How sensitive are results to the maximum path order in APTs? Would allowing order >2 help the aligned models?
- Could you add a dependency-based embedding baseline (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) with simple addition and compare to APTs?
- What are the memory and runtime costs for building APTs and composing them at inference time? Do these methods scale to larger lexicons and longer phrases?
- How robust are results to parser choice/quality? Have you tried a more accurate dependency parser, or re-running without retokenizing compounds?

Additional comments
- The “reference suppressed” placeholders and several references have formatting errors; please fix for camera-ready.
- A qualitative analysis (e.g., top predicted vs. observed features for selected compounds) would strengthen the claims about interpretability and what alignment contributes.
- The proposed future use (using compositionality to decide when/what to smooth) is interesting; even a small pilot would enhance the paper’s impact.

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper with clear improvements on a standard benchmark and a linguistically principled approach. The main limitations are the narrow evaluation scope, incremental novelty, and missing syntactic embedding baselines. I view it as borderline accept; addressing the questions and adding a dependency-based embedding baseline would strengthen it.
