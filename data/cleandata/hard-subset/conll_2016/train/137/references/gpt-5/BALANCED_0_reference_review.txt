Summary and contributions
This paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by leveraging compositional distributional semantics with typed, higher-order dependency features (Anchored Packed Trees, APTs). The authors compare (i) aligned APT composition, (ii) unaligned typed dependency composition, (iii) a hybrid of aligned and unaligned APTs, against (iv) additive composition over neural embeddings (word2vec) and the prior Reddy et al. (2011) baseline on the Reddy dataset (90 compounds), using ukWaC.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding: Typed, higher-order dependency features (via APTs) substantially improve noun-compound compositionality detection over both untyped count-based baselines (Reddy et al.) and neural embeddings (best ρ≈0.79 with the hybrid APT vs 0.74 cbow and 0.71 Reddy).
2) Methodological insight: A hybrid composition that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APTs yields consistent gains, suggesting these variants capture complementary context information.
3) Careful experimental protocol to minimize leakage from phrasal contexts to constituents (recombining compounds into single tokens and excluding modifier-internal paths), plus a systematic exploration of weighting (PPMI vs counts, context smoothing α, additive vs intersective composition, and weighting parameters h, q).

Strengths
- Clear empirical improvements and careful comparisons: The APT models (aligned, unaligned, hybrid) consistently and significantly outperform the Reddy baseline and reasonably strong word2vec baselines (cbow/sgns with multiple dims and subsampling), with strong best result (ρ≈0.79). The frequency baseline is included and informative (ρ≈0.63), showing all models add value beyond frequency effects.

- Sound motivation for typed and higher-order dependency features: The paper convincingly argues why typed paths and path-based alignment matter for composition (and when alignment may be unnecessary for noun–noun compounds). The hybrid’s gains support the claim that different compositions capture complementary evidence.

- Thoughtful experimental controls to avoid leakage: Recombining compounds as single tokens and excluding modifier-internal paths help ensure that composed vectors must “predict” (rather than copy) phrasal contexts. The discussion about why PMI shifting and aggressive subsampling can harm low-frequency compounds shows good experimental care.

- Analysis of design choices: The paper examines PPMI vs counts, smoothing α, additive vs intersective operations, and reports consistent parameter ranges (h, q in ~[0.3, 0.5]). The observation that modifiers and unaligned typed features are slightly more informative is practically valuable.

- Relevance and clarity: The work addresses a real confound in compositionality vs similarity evaluation, and positions compositionality detection as a proxy to assess composition models without conflating idiomaticity.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and small dataset: Results are only on the 90-item Reddy dataset; this restricts generality and robustness conclusions. No evaluation on other compound datasets or broader MWEs, nor on different corpora or parser setups.

- Incomplete isolation of factors: While the paper attributes gains to typed dependency paths and higher-order features, there is no explicit ablation of “typed vs untyped” within the APT framework, nor of “first-order-only vs higher-order” to quantify each factor’s contribution. Similarly, alignment’s benefit is discussed but not isolated beyond noun–noun compounds.

- Comparability with neural baselines: The word2vec baselines are tuned over a few standard settings, but composition is restricted to weighted addition. Stronger or fairer neural baselines could include dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014 style), retrofitting to dependency contexts, or probing larger windows for compounds. The mismatch in context type (dependency vs proximity) remains a confound.

- Reproducibility details: The core APT mechanism is referenced to “reference suppressed,” and implementation/parameters (e.g., dependency scheme specifics, pruning thresholds, feature selection or dimensionality reduction, handling of rare paths) are not fully specified. Code/data release is not mentioned. The claim that errors ≤ 0.0015 and Δ>0.005 implies significance would benefit from explicit reporting of the number of CV repetitions and seeds.

- Preprocessing and parsing assumptions: Compounds are recombined based on the majority observed relation; parsing errors are acknowledged but their impact is not quantified. Ignoring modifier-internal paths is reasonable, but it may discard cues relevant to literality in some constructions.

Questions to authors
1) Can you report ablations that isolate: (a) typed vs untyped contexts within APTs; (b) first-order vs higher-order paths; and (c) alignment-only vs unaligned-only, to quantify each factor’s contribution?
2) Were word2vec models trained on the same recombined corpus with compounds as single tokens? If so, do results change when training baselines on the unrecombined corpus?
3) How many repetitions and random seeds were used in cross-validation to obtain the reported standard errors? Could you provide confidence intervals for key comparisons (e.g., hybrid APT vs cbow)?
4) What proportion of compounds had non-NMOD majority relations, and how sensitive are results to the choice of the majority relation?
5) Can you share more implementation details (dependency label set, pruning of rare paths, any smoothing or cutoff for contexts) and, ideally, code/artifacts?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Strengthen baselines by including dependency-based embeddings and/or contextualized composition baselines, to reduce the methodological gap (dependency vs proximity) in the comparison.
- Add an ablation showing only first-order typed features vs first+second order to directly support the “higher-order helps” claim.
- Evaluate on at least one additional dataset (e.g., larger compound sets or compositionality judgments for other constructions) to bolster generality.
- Improve reference formatting and fix placeholders (“reference suppressed” and citation inconsistencies).
- Consider reporting stability across parsers or dependency schemes to assess robustness of typed-path gains.

Recommendation: Weak Accept. The empirical gains are solid and the methodological insights about typed, higher-order dependency features and hybrid composition are meaningful for compositional semantics. The main limitations are the narrow evaluation and missing ablations/reproducibility details; addressing these would strengthen the contribution.
