Summary and main contributions
- The paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds using typed, higher-order dependency features encoded as Anchored Packed Trees (APTs). It evaluates three composition regimes: aligned (with path-offset alignment), unaligned, and a hybrid combination, and compares them to neural word embeddings with naïve additive composition.
- On the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds, the proposed APT-based methods achieve higher Spearman correlations with human compositionality judgments (up to 0.79) than prior work (0.71) and neural baselines, suggesting that typed dependency paths and higher-order features are useful for this task.
- The paper offers analysis on design choices: alignment vs. no alignment, intersection vs. union composition, PPMI smoothing, and relative weighting of modifier vs. head, yielding insights (e.g., modifiers and unaligned typed features are slightly more informative; shifted PMI and aggressive subsampling hurt due to low-frequency compounds).

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains on a standard benchmark with careful comparisons. The APT hybrid model achieves ρ=0.79, outperforming both the reported Reddy baseline (0.71) and well-tuned word2vec CBOW/SG baselines trained on the same corpus. The improvement is consistent across parameterizations and supported by multiple configurations, strengthening the claim that typed dependency information helps compositionality detection.
- Well-motivated methodology that ties syntactic structure to composition. The offset-and-reduction mechanism to align dependency-path spaces is linguistically principled; the hybrid finding (aligned + unaligned features both contribute) is informative and plausible: first-order head contexts overlap with modifier first-order paths under NMOD, while alignment brings in modifier second-order evidence.
- Thoughtful experimental design to mitigate leakage. The authors re-tokenize compounds and explicitly prevent compound-derived contexts from seeding constituent representations, ensuring that intersective composition cannot trivially reproduce observed phrasal vectors. This addresses a common pitfall in compositionality prediction.
- Useful analyses of weighting and smoothing. The paper reports that α=0.75 context smoothing slightly helps, shifted PMI and lower subsampling thresholds hurt (likely due to sparse compounds), and optimal weights h and q lie in 0.3–0.5, indicating that modifiers and unaligned typed features carry slightly more signal. These are actionable insights for practitioners.
- Baseline suite includes frequency and neural embeddings. The frequency baseline (ρ=0.63) reveals a strong frequency–compositionality correlation in this dataset; the fact that all methods surpass it suggests the models capture more than just frequency.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and potential overfitting to a small dataset. The main results hinge on 90 items with repeated 3-fold cross-validation. While the authors report small errors, the stability of model selection (h and q) and configuration choices on such a small set is uncertain. Evaluation on additional compositionality datasets (e.g., phrasal verbs, larger noun compound sets) would substantially strengthen the claims.
- Incomplete baseline coverage for count-based dependency models. The paper contrasts APTs with neural embeddings but does not include a strong count-based dependency baseline using first-order typed dependency vectors with weighted addition (i.e., without APT-specific alignment/higher-order paths) or a lexical-function-style model. This makes it hard to disentangle the gains due to typed dependencies per se from gains specific to APT’s higher-order offsetting and reduction.
- Statistical testing and reporting clarity. The claim that “errors ≤ 0.0015” and that differences >0.005 are significant lacks detail: what is the resampling protocol, what constitutes an “error” (standard error of ρ?), and are differences tested against baselines (e.g., CBOW 0.74 vs. best APT 0.79)? Reporting confidence intervals or permutation tests against key baselines would add rigor.
- Potential confounds from observed phrasal representations and frequency effects. The evaluation compares composed representations to observed phrase vectors constructed from the same corpus. Given the strong frequency–ρ correlation, it would be useful to report partial correlations controlling for log frequency and an analysis stratified by phrase frequency, to show robustness for rare vs. frequent compounds.
- Reproducibility and presentation gaps. Some crucial details are elided: the prior APT paper is “reference suppressed,” complicating reproduction; hyperparameters for APT pruning/feature selection and path-depth choices beyond “up to order 2” are not fully specified; tables/notation have minor inconsistencies. Code and preprocessing release, or an appendix with exact settings, would help.

Questions to authors
- Can you add a count-based typed dependency baseline that uses first-order dependency PPMI vectors with weighted addition (no APT offsetting), and a variant with second-order typed features but no alignment? This would help isolate the contribution of APT’s alignment and reduction from typed dependencies alone.
- How many occurrences per compound are observed in ukWaC after preprocessing? Please provide per-compound frequency statistics and report performance stratified by frequency bins, and a partial correlation with frequency controlled.
- Could you evaluate on an additional dataset (e.g., McCarthy et al. phrasal verbs, or a larger noun compound set), or provide a leave-one-domain-out analysis to reduce overfitting concerns?
- What is the exact resampling protocol for estimating errors and significance (number of repetitions, how folds are drawn, whether parameter tuning is nested within each training fold)? Please report confidence intervals for the main comparisons to CBOW and to Reddy.
- How sensitive are results to the maximum path order and to reduction? An ablation showing only first-order features, first+second without reduction, and with reduction, would clarify the role of higher-order paths and the alignment mechanism.
- For compounds where NMOD is not the dominant relation, how do aligned vs. unaligned vs. hybrid models perform? A breakdown by relation type would be insightful.

Overall, this is a solid and timely paper with convincing empirical gains and clear insights into the value of typed, higher-order dependency features for compositionality detection. Addressing the baseline and evaluation breadth concerns would further strengthen it.
