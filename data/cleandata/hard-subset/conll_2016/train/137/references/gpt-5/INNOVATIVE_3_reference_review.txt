Summary and main contributions
This paper investigates detecting the compositionality of noun–noun compounds by comparing composed representations against observed (holistic) phrase representations. It leverages anchored packed trees (APTs), which encode typed dependency paths (including higher-order paths) and enable alignment (via dependency-path offset and reduction) of constituent spaces prior to composition. The authors compare aligned, unaligned, and a hybrid (linear combination) of aligned and unaligned APT composition and contrast these to neural embeddings with naïve addition. On the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds) using ukWaC, the hybrid APT achieves ρ=0.79, outperforming Reddy’s 0.71 and CBOW (best 0.74).

Contributions as I see them:
1) A principled, structure-aware compositional framework for compositionality detection using typed, higher-order dependency features and APT alignment, with a hybrid aligned/unaligned variant that yields state-of-the-art correlation on a standard benchmark.
2) Empirical evidence that typed dependency paths encode information highly predictive of compositionality, and that aligned and unaligned APT features are complementary.
3) A careful comparison to neural embedding baselines and analysis of weighting schemes (PPMI smoothing, counts) and composition operators.

Strengths
- Conceptual boldness: The central idea—using full dependency-path-typed features and an explicit alignment (offset + reduction) to compose—is elegant and linguistically well-motivated. It brings syntactic structure into the core of the compositionality signal instead of treating composition as post-hoc vector arithmetic, which is a meaningful direction for distributional semantics.

- Clear empirical gains and complementary signals: The hybrid APT consistently outperforms both aligned-only and unaligned-only variants and improves over strong baselines (Reddy 0.71; CBOW 0.74) to 0.79 Spearman. The finding that aligned and unaligned spaces capture different aspects of the phenomenon is insightful and actionable for future models.

- Careful experimental design to avoid leakage: The preprocessing that collapses compounds into single tokens while excluding their contexts from constituent elementary representations is important and often overlooked. This supports a fair test of whether composition predicts the holistic vector.

- Thorough parameter exploration where it matters: The paper explores PPMI vs counts, smoothing α, intersective vs additive composition, and neural embedding choices (CBOW/SG, dims, subsampling). The observation that shifted PMI/subsampling degrades performance for low-frequency compounds is valuable and explains a common pitfall.

- Interpretability and linguistic plausibility: The method ties improvements to specific syntactic evidence (typed/higher-order paths), not opaque feature engineering. The offsetting notion generalizes across constructions and may encourage broader adoption of structure-aware composition.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior APT work is under-specified: The APT model is described as previously proposed (“reference suppressed”). If the alignment/offsetting machinery and higher-order typed features are largely prior work, the novelty here is primarily the application to compositionality detection and the hybrid mixing. The authors should clarify what is genuinely new (e.g., the hybrid formulation, specific alignment choices, evaluation framing).

- Limited evaluation scope and small dataset: Results hinge on a single, small benchmark (90 compounds) in one corpus and language. Claims of generality to other MWEs and constructions remain untested. No evaluation on other datasets (e.g., Farahmand et al., or phrase-level compositionality datasets) or across domains.

- Frequency confound not fully controlled: Frequency correlates strongly with human judgments (FREQ baseline 0.63). The study reports this but does not provide partial correlations or regression controlling for frequency. Some of the gains may still partly reflect frequency patterns encoded in typed paths.

- Mixed message on the value of alignment: For NN compounds, unaligned APT often outperforms aligned, with the hybrid winning overall. This raises questions about when alignment is actually necessary/beneficial and whether the gains mainly come from richer typed features rather than true compositional alignment. A deeper ablation (e.g., only first-order typed vs higher-order; only alignment without higher order) would clarify.

- Baseline breadth and fairness: Neural baselines are limited to word2vec with standard settings. Dependency-based embeddings, GloVe, or modern subword models might provide stronger baselines for comparison. Also, the restriction of similarity comparisons to first-order features could bias against some APT configurations; this choice deserves ablation.

Questions to authors
- What exactly is new relative to your prior APT work? Is the hybrid aligned/unaligned composition novel? Are the offset/reduction mechanics unchanged?

- Can you provide ablations isolating: (a) impact of typed vs untyped features; (b) adding higher-order paths (order 1 vs 2), both for constituents and compounds; (c) alignment without higher-order features?

- How robust are results when controlling for frequency (e.g., partial Spearman, or regression with frequency as covariate)? Does the hybrid APT still dominate?

- How were statistical significance tests performed for differences in ρ (details of repetition count, variance estimates)? You claim >0.005 is significant; please justify.

- Generalization: Have you tried your approach on other compositionality datasets (other compound lists, other languages, or VNP/AN constructions)? Any preliminary results?

- Parsing and preprocessing: How sensitive is performance to parsing errors and to your choice of dominant dependency relation for compounds with multiple parses? What proportion had δ ≠ NMOD, and how does performance differ across relation types?

- Baselines: Could you compare to dependency-based embeddings, GloVe, and/or fastText with subword information? These might narrow the gap and contextualize the advantage of typed dependencies.

Overall, I find the paper’s core idea and results promising and bold in spirit: integrating typed, aligned dependency paths into composition for compositionality detection is a worthwhile direction, and the hybrid finding is interesting. The main reservations are the limited novelty beyond prior APT work and the narrow evaluation scope. With clarifications and a bit more breadth/ablation, this would make a strong contribution.
