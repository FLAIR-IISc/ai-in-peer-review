Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds using distributional representations that encode typed dependency paths and higher-order contexts via Anchored Packed Trees (APTs). It compares (i) aligned APT composition (with path-offsetting/alignment), (ii) unaligned APT composition, and (iii) a hybrid linear combination, against neural baselines (CBOW/SG).
- Main contribution 1: An empirical demonstration that typed higher-order dependency features, combined via a hybrid aligned+unaligned composition, correlate better with human compositionality judgments on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset than prior untyped count-based and neural baselines (best ρ ≈ 0.79 vs 0.71–0.74).
- Main contribution 2: A detailed exploration of weighting schemes (PPMI with/without smoothing), composition operators (addition vs min-intersection), and constituent/head weights, showing stable gains and suggesting modifiers and unaligned features carry slightly more weight.
- Secondary contribution: A clear formalization of APT alignment via path-offsetting with reduction and its application to compound nouns.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results on a known benchmark. The reported Spearman correlations (up to 0.79) substantially exceed the Reddy et al. untyped baseline (0.71) and tuned CBOW (0.74), suggesting genuine benefits from typed dependencies and higher-order paths.
- Methodological clarity on APT alignment/offsetting. The paper explains how higher-order features of modifiers map to first-order features of compound heads via δ-offsetting and reduction, making the compositional mechanism interpretable and linguistically grounded.
- Systematic ablations and parameter sweeps. The study varies PPMI vs normalized counts, α-smoothing, operators (⊔UNI vs ⊔INT), and mixing weights (h, q), enabling a reasonably thorough view of design choices. The consistent optimal h and q in [0.3, 0.5] is informative about constituent contributions.
- Use of a large, consistently processed corpus (ukWaC) for both count-based and neural models, reducing confounds due to different training data that often plague such comparisons.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and potential overfitting to a tiny dataset. The Reddy dataset has only 90 compounds; results may be unstable and strongly confounded by frequency (the FREQ baseline scores 0.63). No additional datasets (e.g., other MWE compositionality sets, adjective–noun or verb–object) are used to test generality. A partial correlation analysis controlling for frequency is not reported.
- Baseline fairness concerns. The strongest baselines are untyped CBOW/SG with window contexts. Missing are competitive typed baselines: (a) dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014), (b) typed count vectors with naïve additive composition, and (c) path-based count baselines without APT alignment. Without these, it is unclear whether the gain is due to typed contexts per se or the specific APT machinery/hybrid mixing.
- Statistical testing and reporting are underspecified. The paper claims “errors ≤ 0.0015” and that differences >0.005 are significant at 95%, but does not specify the number of repetitions, the variance estimation method (bootstrap across items? across CV splits?), or whether hyperparameter searches were nested within CV. With only 90 items, the stated error bars seem implausibly small without careful justification.
- Preprocessing and possible leakage/confounds. The corpus is modified to merge target compounds into single tokens and to rewrite dependency paths. While the authors try to avoid leakage into constituent vectors, this global preprocessing uses information from all items, across CV folds. Selecting the “most frequent” dependency relation for a compound using the entire corpus may leak test information into training-time design choices. Additionally, ignoring dependency paths that include the modifier may remove relevant contexts for the observed compound representations, biasing evaluation.
- Incomplete/misaligned presentation and formatting issues. Table 4 is referenced but not present; some footnotes are misplaced; “Elsewhere (reference suppressed)” suggests this paper largely applies previously introduced APTs rather than introducing a novel model; several references are malformed/incomplete (e.g., “Schmid.,? \Q1994\E”, “Baroni and Lenci.,? \Q2010\E”), and there are inconsistent notations and minor typographic errors. These issues hinder reproducibility and polish.
- Reproducibility gaps. Critical details appear missing: feature pruning thresholds; vocabulary cutoffs; exact set of dependency relations and higher-order path limits; dimensionality after pruning; normalization schemes; training details for word2vec (epochs, negative samples); whether phrase segmentation (word2phrase) was used; and code/data release.

Questions to authors
- Significance and CV protocol: How many repetitions of 3-fold CV were run? How were the “errors” computed (across folds, across repetitions, or bootstrap across items)? Were hyperparameters (h, q, α, operator choice, hybrid mixing) tuned within the training folds only (nested CV), and were neural hyperparameters selected without peeking at test folds?
- Frequency control: What are the Spearman correlations after regressing out frequency (e.g., partial ρ controlling for log frequency)? How sensitive are results when restricting to compounds above a minimum frequency?
- Baselines: Can you add dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014) and a typed count baseline with naïve additive composition? These would directly test whether typed contexts alone explain the gains.
- Preprocessing/leakage: Was the selection of each compound’s “most frequent” dependency relation done using only training data for that compound in each fold? If not, please quantify the effect of selecting the relation per-fold. Also, what is the impact of keeping dependency paths that include the modifier (rather than discarding them)?
- Robustness to parsing noise and path order: How sensitive are APT results to parser errors (Malt on web text) and to the maximum path order (1 vs 2 vs 3)? Please provide an ablation on path order and on the reduction operation.
- Missing content/presentation: Table 4 is referenced but absent; please include it. Also provide exact training settings for word2vec (epochs, negative samples, min_count, phrase detection).

Additional comments
- The work is promising but currently feels like an application/extension of prior APTs rather than a substantially new model. Strengthening baselines, statistical rigor, and broader evaluation would substantially improve the paper.
- Please fix reference formatting, unify notation, and provide enough implementation details for reproduction (or code).
