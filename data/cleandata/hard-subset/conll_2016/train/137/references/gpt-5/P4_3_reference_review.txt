Summary and claimed contributions
The paper investigates detecting the compositionality of compound nouns using distributional models that explicitly encode typed, higher-order dependency paths via Anchored Packed Trees (APTs). It compares aligned APT composition (with dependency-path offsetting), unaligned composition, and a hybrid of both, against standard neural word embeddings with naïve additive composition, on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding: Typed dependency-based APT models (especially a hybrid of aligned and unaligned composition) substantially improve Spearman correlation with human compositionality judgments on the Reddy dataset (up to 0.79), outperforming strong baselines including word2vec addition and the original Reddy results.
2) Methodological: A hybrid composition strategy that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APTs, along with an analysis of weighting parameters (h for head/modifier and q for aligned/unaligned contributions) and PPMI smoothing (α), revealing the relative informativeness of modifier and unaligned typed features.
3) Experimental analysis: A controlled preprocessing pipeline that replaces compounds with single tokens to avoid leakage into constituent vectors, exploration of composition operations (intersection vs. union/addition), and an assessment of smoothing/subsampling decisions across count-based and neural models.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains with clear comparisons: The APT-based methods achieve ρ up to 0.79, improving over the Reddy baseline (0.71) and CBOW addition (0.74). The study also demonstrates that typed dependency features are more informative for this task than untyped proximity-based embeddings. The frequency baseline (0.63) confirms the task is nontrivial and that models capture more than frequency.
- Careful design to prevent leakage: The authors preprocess the corpus to treat compounds as single tokens and explicitly exclude compound co-occurrences from constituent representations. This makes the composition task faithful: composed vectors must infer contexts not trivially shared with observed compound vectors.
- Systematic exploration of parameters and operations: The paper evaluates PPMI smoothing (α = 1 vs 0.75), composition operators (intersection vs union/add), alignment vs unaligned vs hybrid composition, and weighting (h, q), with repeated 3-fold CV, and reports small standard errors and a practical significance criterion.
- Useful methodological insights: The observation that unaligned typed features already overlap meaningfully for NMOD compounds, and that hybrid composition further improves performance, is informative. The finding that lower subsampling thresholds (akin to shifted PMI) hurt performance for low-frequency compounds is also valuable.
- Reproducible choices for corpora and parsing: Using ukWaC with standard preprocessing (TreeTagger, Malt Parser) and reporting approximate feature dimensionalities gives reasonable transparency about the data pipeline.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior APT work: The APT framework and typed higher-order dependency features were introduced previously (with references suppressed here). The main new element appears to be applying APT composition to compositionality detection and proposing a hybrid aligned/unaligned combination. While the empirical findings are strong, the methodological novelty is incremental.
- Narrow evaluation: Results are only on the small Reddy dataset (90 compounds). No evaluation on other compositionality datasets (e.g., noun-noun or MWE datasets) or other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun), limiting claims about generalizability. No error analysis or robustness checks (e.g., sensitivity to parsing errors) are provided.
- Missing baselines using typed dependencies without APT: The paper does not compare against simpler typed dependency count-based vectors composed by weighted addition (without APT alignment or higher-order paths) or against dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014). Such baselines are crucial to isolate the contribution of alignment and higher-order paths beyond “typed vs untyped” features.
- Ambiguities in methodology and reporting: Details of cross-validation (exact number of repetitions, fold splits) and the selection of the majority dependency relation per compound are under-specified. The restriction of similarity computation to first-order features could bias against approaches relying more on higher-order information; an ablation isolating the benefit of higher-order paths is missing. The significance testing approach is described informally (differences >0.005), without reporting the number of repetitions or correction for multiple comparisons.
- Clarity and citation quality: The manuscript contains typographical issues (e.g., “siginificantly”), inconsistent notation (CF/CS defined implicitly), references with missing years or placeholders (“reference suppressed,” “? 2010”), and some equations/definitions are introduced tersely. These hinder readability and reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
- Cross-validation details: How many repetitions of 3-fold CV were run? Were folds stratified by compositionality scores? Were h and q tuned only on training folds and then applied once to test folds per repetition? Please provide the exact protocol and randomization seeds.
- Ablations: Can you report results for (a) typed 1st-order dependency vectors with weighted addition but without APT alignment or higher-order features, and (b) APTs limited to 1st-order paths only? This would help quantify the incremental gains from higher-order and alignment.
- Dependency-based embeddings: Did you try dependency-based word embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014) with addition as a baseline? If not, please justify and/or include results.
- Hybrid mechanism: How is q selected in CV, and how stable is it across folds? Do gains persist if q is fixed (e.g., 0.5)? Any insight into when aligned vs unaligned dominates (e.g., as a function of compound frequency or modifier/head POS ambiguity)?
- Preprocessing choices: You select the most frequent dependency relation per compound and ignore paths modifying the modifier. How sensitive are results to these choices? What happens if compounds with multiple relations are kept as separate types per relation, or if modifier-modifying contexts are included?
- Significance and repetitions: You state standard errors ≤ 0.0015. Please report the number of repetitions and provide confidence intervals per configuration. Did you apply any multiple-comparison correction?
- Generalization: Have you tested APT composition on other compositionality datasets or phrase types (e.g., adjective–noun, verb–object)? If not, can you discuss expected behavior and planned validation?
- Resources: Will you release code, trained APTs, and the preprocessed ukWaC compound annotations to facilitate replication?
