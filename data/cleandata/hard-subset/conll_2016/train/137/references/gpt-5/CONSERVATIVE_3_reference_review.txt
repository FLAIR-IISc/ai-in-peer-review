Summary and claimed contributions
The paper investigates detecting compositionality of noun–noun compounds by composing word representations built from typed, higher-order dependency features (Anchored Packed Trees, APTs). The authors compare (i) aligned APT composition, (ii) unaligned composition, and (iii) a hybrid interpolation, against naïve additive composition of word2vec embeddings. On the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds, they report improved Spearman correlations with human ratings, with a best ρ≈0.79 for the hybrid APT vs 0.71 in Reddy’s original setup and ≈0.74 for CBOW.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical evidence that typed, higher-order dependency features are highly informative for compound compositionality, with unaligned and hybrid APT variants outperforming window-based embeddings on the same corpus.
2) A practical interpolation (hybrid) of aligned and unaligned APT representations that yields small but consistent gains over either alone.
3) An evaluation protocol that avoids feature leakage from holistic phrase vectors into constituent vectors via careful retokenization and preprocessing.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant problem framing. The paper rightly highlights that phrase similarity evaluations can be confounded by non-compositionality and that compositionality should be modeled on a continuum. Evaluating composed vs. holistic representations against human compositionality judgments is a reasonable setup.

- Stronger than basic baselines on the same corpus. Using ukWaC throughout controls for corpus differences. The CBOW baseline (weighted addition) reaches ρ≈0.74, exceeding Reddy’s 0.71, and APT models further improve to ≈0.78–0.79. This suggests the added information from typed dependency paths contributes beyond windowed co-occurrences.

- Systematic comparison of alignment choices. The analysis contrasting aligned vs. unaligned APTs, and the finding that alignment helps less for noun–noun compounds (with NMOD relations prevalent), is informative. The small but consistent hybrid gains support the claim that these signals are complementary.

- Sensible engineering to prevent leakage. Retokenizing compounds and preventing their holistic contexts from contaminating constituent vectors is important and often overlooked. The discussion of why shifted PMI and aggressive subsampling harm low-frequency compounds is also thoughtful.

- Interpretable observations. The reported optimal weights h and q in [0.3, 0.5] (modifier slightly more informative; unaligned slightly more helpful) align with linguistic intuitions for many compounds.

Weaknesses
- Narrow and small-scale evaluation. Results are reported on a single dataset of only 90 items. With such a small test set, differences of 0.01–0.02 in Spearman ρ may not be reliable. The paper mentions repeated 3-fold CV and “differences > 0.005 are significant,” but does not specify the number of repetitions nor provide proper tests for dependent correlations or confidence intervals. There is substantial risk of overfitting/tuning to this small benchmark.

- Missing and potentially unfair baselines. The paper does not compare against dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014 dependency contexts) or strong count-based typed dependency baselines with simple additive composition, which are closer in spirit to APTs than window-based word2vec. Prior compositionality models (e.g., Hermann et al., other noun–noun-specific methods) are not reproduced. The improvement over Reddy may largely reflect the move from untyped windows to typed dependency features; this needs to be disentangled.

- Incremental novelty. APTs and alignment have been proposed elsewhere (references are “suppressed”). The hybrid model is a straightforward linear interpolation. The main contribution here is an application and empirical study; methodological novelty appears modest.

- Incomplete experimental details and ablations. Several aspects are under-specified: number of CV repetitions; search grid for h and q; pruning thresholds; coverage/occurrence counts for the 90 compounds; robustness across frequency bins; effect of higher-order paths vs first-order only; impact of the reduction/alignment step; results when not restricting evaluation to first-order features. Table 4 (embeddings) is referenced but not present. Without these, reproducibility and interpretability suffer.

- Presentation and citation issues. Numerous typos, formatting problems, and incomplete or malformed references (“reference suppressed,” missing years/pages) impair clarity and traceability. For a methods paper making empirical claims, clean presentation and complete citations are essential.

Questions to authors
- Can you add baselines closer to APTs: (a) dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) with weighted addition; (b) a count-based typed dependency vector space with simple additive/multiplicative composition but without APT alignment? This would help isolate whether typed contexts alone, rather than APT-specific machinery, drive the gains.

- How many repetitions of 3-fold CV did you run? Please report confidence intervals and/or use appropriate tests for comparing dependent correlations on the same 90 items (e.g., Steiger/Williams tests). Are the 0.01–0.02 differences between unaligned and hybrid significant?

- Provide ablations: (i) only first-order vs. up to second-order paths in APTs; (ii) effect of the reduction operator; (iii) evaluating similarity including higher-order features rather than restricting to first-order. How much does each component contribute?

- How sensitive are results to the choice of the compound’s dependency relation when multiple are observed in the corpus? If you vary this choice or use gold annotations, do conclusions hold?

- Please include coverage and frequency statistics for the compounds (distribution of counts in ukWaC) and stratified results by frequency. Do low-frequency compounds drive the observed effects (e.g., the harm from shifted PMI/subsampling)?

- Can you report constituent-level compositionality predictions (first vs. second head/modifier scores) and provide an error analysis indicating which compound types benefit most from aligned vs. unaligned APTs?

Overall, the direction is promising, but the empirical validation needs to be broadened and baselines strengthened to support strong claims.
