Summary
The paper studies detection of non-compositionality in noun–noun compounds by comparing composed vs. observed representations and correlating their similarity with human compositionality judgments (Reddy et al., 2011). It contrasts (a) an Anchored Packed Trees (APT) approach that uses typed dependency features, higher-order paths, and alignment via dependency-path offsets (including a hybrid combining aligned and unaligned features), with (b) naive additive composition over neural word embeddings (word2vec). On ukWaC, with a preprocessing step that collapses compound occurrences into single tokens, the best APT variant attains Spearman ρ up to 0.79 on the 90-compound dataset, outperforming the prior state-of-the-art (0.714) and CBOW (0.74). The paper provides an ablation over PMI smoothing, composition operators, alignment strategies, and a discussion of subsampling effects.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical evidence that typed, higher-order dependency features within a compositional framework (APT), especially a hybrid of aligned and unaligned modifiers, improve correlation with human compositionality judgments over prior count-based and neural (word2vec) baselines on Reddy et al. (2011).

2) Analysis of alignment vs. unaligned composition and a hybrid linear combination, showing complementary signals; and parameter studies on PMI smoothing (α=0.75), composition operators (add vs. min), and constituent weighting, with consistent gains over prior settings.

3) A preprocessing and evaluation protocol that collapses compounds and avoids direct leakage of compound contexts into constituent representations, enabling a stricter test of compositional generalization.

Strengths
- Strong empirical improvements with careful ablations. The paper reports consistent gains over the REDDY baseline and neural CBOW/SG, with the best APT hybrid achieving ρ=0.79 vs. 0.71–0.74 for baselines. The ablations (aligned vs. unaligned vs. hybrid, add vs. min, counts vs. PPMI, α values) make a clear case that typed dependency paths and higher-order features help.

- Methodologically well-motivated use of typed, higher-order dependency paths and alignment. The offset-and-reduction mechanism is principled, and the observation that second-order modifier features map to first-order head features is leveraged effectively. The hybrid indicates complementary evidence between aligned and unaligned spaces, which is empirically validated.

- Reasonable experimental controls. All models are trained on the same large corpus (ukWaC), compounds are collapsed to create holistic targets, and constituent vectors exclude compound-derived contexts to reduce leakage. Reporting that frequency correlates (ρ=0.63) and that all methods exceed this strengthens the claim that models capture more than frequency.

- Insightful analysis of training choices. The discussion that PMI shifting and aggressive subsampling hurt low-frequency compounds, and that CBOW benefits from higher subsampling thresholds here, is useful and consistent with the task’s sparsity properties. Reporting optimal constituent weights (h,q in [0.3,0.5]) is informative.

- Clarity of the core mechanism. Despite some notation density, the APT alignment/reduction process, feature types, and composition operators are described with sufficient detail to understand the approach.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope. Results are based on a single, small dataset (90 compounds), English-only, and limited to noun–noun compounds. Generalization to other MWE types (e.g., adjective–noun, verb–noun constructions) or larger, more diverse compositionality datasets is not demonstrated.

- Missing contemporary baselines most relevant to typed contexts. There is no comparison to dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg’s dependency SGNS) or to GloVe. Prior compositionality models (e.g., Hermann et al., 2012) are discussed but not re-evaluated on the same corpus/setting. These omissions make it harder to isolate the gain attributable specifically to typed dependency features vs. model class.

- Risk of overfitting/selection bias on a small dataset. Many hyperparameters are explored (PPMI settings, α, add/min, aligned/unaligned/hybrid, weights h,q), with model selection via repeated 3-fold CV. The number of repetitions, the exact selection protocol, and multiple-comparison considerations are not fully specified. Claims that differences >0.005 are significant rely on small standard errors but lack details on the statistical test and folds/seeds.

- Potential confounds in preprocessing and analysis. Collapsing compounds changes constituent distributions; while the design aims to avoid leakage, it may also reduce constituent evidence unevenly. Selecting the most frequent dependency label for ambiguous compounds can introduce noise. The evaluation acknowledges frequency correlates strongly with compositionality but does not provide partial correlations or regressions controlling for frequency, leaving some ambiguity about residual frequency effects.

- Reproducibility gaps and presentation issues. Some implementation details are missing (e.g., number of CV repetitions, exact values for h and q per model, word2vec training specifics like negative sampling, epochs, min-count). The decision to restrict similarity to first-order features is motivated but not quantified against alternatives. References contain placeholders/inconsistencies, and code/data availability is not stated.

Questions to authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- Were the word2vec models trained on the same preprocessed corpus with compounds collapsed? Please specify negative sampling rate, epochs, min-count, and the number of subsampling trials run.

- How many repetitions of 3-fold CV were performed? Please report variance across splits and conduct paired significance tests between the best APT hybrid and the best CBOW/SG baselines.

- Can you report partial Spearman correlations controlling for log-frequency of compounds, or a regression including frequency as a covariate, to quantify residual frequency effects?

- How sensitive are results to parsing noise and to the choice of dependency label for compounds seen with multiple relations? If you restrict to compounds consistently parsed as NMOD, does performance change?

- Why restrict similarity to first-order features? Have you evaluated including higher-order features on the observed side as well? Please report the effect.

- Can you compare against dependency-based embeddings (typed SGNS) and GloVe under your composition protocol? Also, can Hermann et al.’s model be retrained on ukWaC for a fairer comparison?

- Will code and processed resources (e.g., compound-collapsed ukWaC counts or APT extraction scripts) be released to facilitate replication?
