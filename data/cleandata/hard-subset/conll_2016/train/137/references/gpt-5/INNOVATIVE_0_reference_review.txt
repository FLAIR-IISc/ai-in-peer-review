Summary and Contributions
- Primary: Introduces and evaluates a syntax-aware compositional framework for detecting non-compositional compound nouns using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) with typed, higher-order dependency paths. A key idea is aligning modifier and head spaces via offsetting and path reduction, and a hybrid scheme that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APTs.
- Secondary: Provides a careful empirical comparison against strong neural baselines (word2vec CBOW/SG), showing that typed dependency contexts and APT composition substantially improve Spearman correlation with human compositionality judgments on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (hybrid APT ρ=0.79 vs CBOW up to 0.74 and Reddy 0.71).
- Tertiary: Offers diagnostic insights into composition operators (union vs intersection), PMI smoothing (α=0.75), constituent/head weighting, and practical cautions about shifted PMI and subsampling for low-frequency phrases.

Strengths
- Conceptual advance: The alignment of typed, higher-order dependency features via offsetting and reduction is a principled way to make composition structure-sensitive, and the hybrid combination with unaligned features is a clever, pragmatic twist. This is a bold, syntax-aware alternative to common proximity-based composition that directly addresses the confound of non-compositionality in phrase similarity tasks.
- Strong empirical gains on a standard benchmark: The hybrid APT achieves ρ=0.79 on Reddy et al. (2011), improving over both classic count-based weighted addition (0.71) and tuned neural CBOW (0.74). The gains are consistent across weighting schemes (PPMI vs counts) and composition operators, indicating robustness of the core idea that typed dependency paths help.
- Thorough ablations and analysis: The paper contrasts aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid APTs, union vs intersection operations, PPMI α choices, and constituent/head weights (h and q). Findings such as optimal h,q in [0.3,0.5] (modifier slightly more informative) and the negative effect of shifted PMI/subsampling on low-frequency compounds are useful takeaways for the community.
- Clear formalization and examples: The offsetting/reduction mechanism is well specified, with intuitive examples (e.g., adjective second-order features mapping onto noun first-order features). The discussion of feature spaces and order constraints makes the method reproducible in principle.
- Relevance and framing: Evaluating composition via compositionality detection (rather than direct phrase similarity) is well motivated and highlights an underappreciated angle: syntax-aware contexts matter for deciding when composition should or should not work.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and size: Results are only on 90 compounds from a single English dataset/corpus (ukWaC), which is small and risks overfitting in model selection (h, q). No validation on other compositionality datasets (e.g., phrasal verbs, other compound sets) or languages, and no tests on other phrase types (AN, VN) to demonstrate broader impact.
- Baseline comparability: The strongest APTs rely on typed dependency contexts, whereas the neural baselines are untyped proximity-based embeddings. This risks an apples-to-oranges comparison. Missing are dependency-based embedding baselines (e.g., dependency2vec, structured skip-gram) and stronger composition models (e.g., lexical function, tensor-based) applied to this task.
- Preprocessing and potential leakage: Collapsing compounds into single tokens and then computing observed phrasal vectors could inadvertently influence co-occurrence statistics of constituents via parsing and token recombination. While the paper tries to avoid feeding compound contexts into constituents, the description leaves ambiguity about leakage through overlapping contexts and parser errors (notably in three-noun compounds). The choice to select the most frequent dependency relation for ambiguous compounds may bias the evaluation.
- Reporting and significance: Although the paper claims tiny SEs (≤0.0015), detailed significance tests across models are not reported. Some differences are small (e.g., 0.78 unaligned vs 0.79 hybrid), and it would help to quantify whether improvements hold per split and across parameterizations. Also, the restriction to comparing composed vs observed vectors only on first-order features could favor APTs; an ablation on feature orders would clarify this.
- Novelty relative to prior APT work: The core APT machinery (typed higher-order paths, alignment via offsets/reduction) appears to be from prior work by the authors (“reference suppressed”). The novelty here is the application to compositionality detection and the hybrid combination. While useful, this is an incremental conceptual step; the paper’s impact rests primarily on the empirical finding that APTs outperform neural baselines for this task.

Questions to Authors
- Leakage control: After collapsing compounds into single tokens, how do you ensure that constituent APTs do not indirectly absorb compound-derived contexts (e.g., via shared sentences or dependency paths involving the compound token)? Can you quantify the extent of any such overlap?
- Frequency sensitivity: What is the distribution of compound frequencies, and how does performance vary with frequency? Does the frequency-compositionality correlation (FREQ baseline ρ=0.63) fully explain any subset of the gains?
- First-order restriction: You restrict similarity comparisons to first-order dependency features. What happens if you include second-order? Please provide an ablation showing the impact of each order in both observed and composed vectors.
- Fair baselines: Can you include dependency-based embedding baselines (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014) and compositional models beyond addition (e.g., lexical function), trained on the same corpus, to isolate the contribution of typed contexts vs the APT machinery?
- Robustness: Do results hold on other datasets (e.g., McCarthy et al.’s phrasal verbs, larger compound sets), and for other phrase types (AN, VN)? Any preliminary evidence?
- Reproducibility: Please detail the search ranges for h and q, the exact dependency label set, the order cutoff, and the number of dimensions retained. Will you release code and the preprocessing scripts for collapsing compounds and building APTs?

Overall, this is a bold and timely paper: it advances a syntax-aware view of distributional composition and shows clear benefits for compositionality detection. Despite the limited evaluation, the ideas are promising and likely to stimulate further work.
