- Studies noun–noun compositionality detection by predicting compound vectors from constituents using typed higher-order dependency APTs and comparing composed vs observed vectors on Reddy et al. (2011).
- Compares aligned APT composition, unaligned APT composition, and a hybrid linear combination to CBOW and skip-gram additive embeddings.
- Empirical claim: hybrid typed higher-order dependency features outperform untyped neural embeddings on Reddy (Spearman ρ=0.79 vs 0.71).
- Ablation analysis shows effects of alignment, composition operator (min vs addition), PMI smoothing (α), and embedding subsampling; modifiers are slightly more informative than heads.
- Methodological finding: unaligned typed dependency features provide complementary evidence to aligned composition despite theoretical appeal of alignment.
- Strength: strong empirical results with comprehensive ablations and reasonably tuned neural baselines (CBOW 0.74).
- Strength: clear task framing that separates compositionality detection from general phrase similarity.
- Strength: APT features capture full dependency paths, demonstrating the value of syntax/path structure for compounds.
- Strength: Insightful analyses on operator choice, PPMI smoothing (α=0.75), subsampling effects, and weighting hyperparameters favoring modifiers and unaligned features.
- Strength: Careful preprocessing—collapsing compounds and attempting to avoid direct feature leakage into constituent representations.
- Weakness: Very limited evaluation scope—only 90 compounds from Reddy; no other compositionality datasets or cross-domain tests.
- Weakness: Possible leakage/confounds because observed and constituent vectors come from the same collapsed parsed corpus; stricter leave-one-out or removal needed.
- Weakness: Reproducibility concerns—key APT technical details deferred to “reference suppressed,” incomplete references, and missing Table 4.
- Weakness: Statistical reporting is weak—unspecified number of CV repetitions, no confidence intervals or formal significance tests for N=90.
- Weakness: Design choices (similarity over first-order observed features only) may bias comparisons and favor unaligned models.
- Weakness: Practicality concerns—very high-dimensional APT spaces (80k–230k), no discussion of computational cost, sparsity handling, or parser error impact.
- Question: Report exact repeated 3-fold CV protocol, number of repetitions, h and q ranges/step sizes, and provide confidence intervals or p-values.
- Question: Provide concrete examples of the reduction function and clarify the path grammar constraint τ ∈ R*R* and any pruning rules.
- Question: Did you evaluate constituent-level compositionality annotations from Reddy, and what are those correlations?
- Question: How sensitive are results to removing all occurrences of each test compound when building constituent APTs to guard against leakage?
- Question: What is the effect of including second-order features on the observed side for similarity—does it change aligned vs unaligned rankings?
- Question: Provide runtime/memory estimates for APT construction/composition vs CBOW/SG and comment on scalability and parser-error sensitivity.
- Additional comment: Clean up the reference list and include full details or make the APT method self-contained rather than “reference suppressed.”
- Additional comment: Evaluate on additional datasets (e.g., phrasal verbs, adjective–noun sets) to support generality claims.
- Additional comment: Release code and preprocessed counts to aid reproducibility.
