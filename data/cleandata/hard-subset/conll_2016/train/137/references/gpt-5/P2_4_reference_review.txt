Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies compositionality detection for English noun–noun compounds using distributional models that encode typed dependency paths (Anchored Packed Trees, APTs). The authors compare several composition regimes—aligned (dependency-offset with path reduction), unaligned, and a hybrid of both—against standard neural baselines (word2vec CBOW/SG) and prior count-based methods on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds. They report sizable improvements in Spearman correlation with human judgements, with a best result of 0.79 (hybrid APT), exceeding CBOW (0.74) and the original Reddy baseline (0.71). They also analyze weighting, PPMI smoothing (α), and subsampling choices.

Contributions as I see them (ordered by strength)
1) Empirical finding: Typed, higher-order dependency features, and especially a hybrid of aligned and unaligned APT composition, substantially improve compositionality detection over strong neural and count-based baselines on the Reddy dataset.
2) Methodological refinement: An explicit composition procedure for compounds that leverages dependency-path alignment (offsetting and reduction) and shows that mixing aligned and unaligned signals (via parameter q) yields complementary evidence.
3) Analytical observations: Practical insights on representation choices—PPMI smoothing (α=0.75), additive vs intersective composition, and the relative informativeness of modifier vs head (h≈0.3–0.5)—that help explain performance differences.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains and careful baselines: The paper reimplements competitive baselines (CBOW/SG with reasonable parameter sweeps) and demonstrates statistically significant improvements of the APT variants, culminating in ρ=0.79. This is meaningful given the small dataset and closes the gap between typed and untyped approaches in a controlled setting (same ukWaC).
- Well-motivated use of syntax: The argument that typed, higher-order dependency paths capture structure crucial for composition is supported by the aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid comparisons. The hybrid’s incremental gains suggest the two views capture non-redundant evidence, which is a substantive insight for compositional models.
- Thorough ablations within the APT family: The paper explores additive vs intersective composition, path smoothing (α), composing counts vs composing PPMI and then reweighting, and the effect of alignment. This makes the conclusions robust within the proposed framework.
- Attention to data preprocessing and leakage: The authors pre-tokenize compounds and explicitly avoid feeding phrasal contexts into constituent vectors, which prevents a trivial overlap advantage for intersective methods. This strengthens the credibility of the composition results.
- Interpretable findings: The observation that modifiers contribute slightly more than heads (optimal h in [0.3, 0.5]) aligns with linguistic intuition for noun–noun compounds and improves understanding beyond raw scores.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope and dataset: All results are on a single, very small dataset (90 compounds). Even with repeated 3-fold cross-validation, overfitting or dataset-specific phenomena may inflate perceived gains. No evaluation on other compositionality datasets or other MWE/phrase types limits generalizability.
- Incomplete comparative coverage: The baselines do not include dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014) or more recent compositionality detectors. Given that the main claim hinges on the value of typed dependencies, comparing to strong dependency-based neural representations would sharpen the contribution.
- Potential frequency confound: The frequency baseline correlates strongly (ρ=0.63). The paper does not report partial correlations controlling for frequency, nor an analysis of performance stratified by frequency or compositionality bins. It remains unclear how much of the improvement is independent of frequency effects, especially since phrasal vectors for low-frequency compounds are fragile.
- Reproducibility details: While many settings are specified, key implementation aspects remain under-detailed: exact values chosen for h and q per condition, the number of CV repetitions, the precise dimensionality thresholds and pruning for APT features, and whether code/resources will be released. The “reference suppressed” for APT hinders tracing prior definitions and may obscure novelty.
- Practicality and scalability: APT spaces (80k–230k dimensions for nouns) and alignment operations may be computationally heavy compared to low-dimensional neural models. The paper does not quantify memory/runtime or discuss feasibility on larger vocabularies or longer paths, which matters for adoption.

Questions to authors
- Can you report partial correlations controlling for compound frequency, and/or results stratified by frequency buckets, to clarify independence from frequency confounds?
- How do APT models compare to dependency-based embeddings (e.g., SGNS on dependency contexts)? This would isolate the benefit of typed contexts vs the APT-specific alignment/offsetting.
- What are the exact optimal h and q per model variant, and how stable are they across CV folds? Any trends by compound relation type (e.g., NMOD vs others)?
- How many repetitions of 3-fold CV were run, and what are the resulting confidence intervals for each model? Could you include per-setting standard deviations?
- Can you provide computational cost (memory, training and inference time) for APT construction and composition, compared to CBOW/SG?
- Did you try higher than second-order paths or ablate the reduction operation? Do gains saturate by order?

Overall assessment
The paper makes a solid empirical case that typed dependency features and careful composition (with alignment and hybridization) improve detection of non-compositional compounds, and it provides informative analyses of weighting and smoothing choices. Novelty is incremental—the APT framework is prior work—but the application to compositionality detection and the hybrid finding are valuable. The main limitations are the narrow evaluation (single small dataset) and lack of comparisons to dependency-based neural baselines. With expanded evaluation and stronger baselines, this would be a compelling contribution. As is, it is a well-executed, informative empirical study with moderate novelty and clear insights.
