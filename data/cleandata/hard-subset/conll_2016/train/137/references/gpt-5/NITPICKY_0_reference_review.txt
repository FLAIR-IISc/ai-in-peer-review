Summary and contributions
- The paper evaluates compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), i.e., typed higher-order dependency features with path offsetting and reduction, and compares aligned vs unaligned composition and a hybrid of both. On the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 items), the hybrid APT approach achieves higher Spearman ρ than prior untyped models and neural baselines.
- A hybrid composition that linearly combines aligned (offset) and unaligned APT representations improves over either alone, suggesting complementary signals.
- The study also reports that simple CBOW embeddings plus weighted addition outperform skip-gram for this task, but are still below APTs.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains on the Reddy dataset. The reported ρ up to 0.79 with the hybrid APT model substantially exceeds the Reddy baseline (0.71) and the best neural setting (CBOW, 0.74). This supports the claim that dependency-typed contexts and higher-order paths are informative for compositionality detection.
- Clear intuition and worked example. The grad student example and Tables 1–2 concretely illustrate how higher-order dependency paths for different POS align after offsetting and reduction, which is the core idea behind APT composition.
- Hybrid composition insight. Showing that unaligned and aligned APTs contribute different predictive contexts is interesting and non-obvious; the hybrid’s consistent improvement suggests useful complementarity.
- Thoughtful preprocessing to limit leakage. Re-tokenizing compounds and ensuring compound contexts do not trivially feed back into constituents prevents overly optimistic intersective composition—a common pitfall.
- Comparison with neural baselines under multiple hyperparameters (model type, dimension, subsampling) is useful; the observation that CBOW benefits less from aggressive subsampling is practically relevant.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and unclear positioning. APTs are “proposed elsewhere” (reference suppressed), so the methodological novelty here is modest; the contribution is mainly empirical. The paper should more clearly articulate what is new beyond applying APTs to compositionality detection and adding the hybrid weighting.
- Small, single-dataset evaluation. Conclusions rest entirely on 90 compounds. No additional datasets (e.g., VNC/Test, other MWE compositionality sets, or constituent-level literality from Reddy) are used. Without external validation, it is hard to assess robustness or generality beyond N–N compounds in ukWaC.
- Statistical claims are under-specified and implausibly precise. The paper states that standard errors are ≤ 0.0015 and that Δρ > 0.005 is significant at 95%, but does not specify the number of repetitions, the test used, or whether significance accounts for multiple model comparisons. With only 90 items, such tiny errors seem unlikely unless repetition counts are very high and proper resampling is used. Please substantiate or revise.
- Potential selection/tuning bias and fairness issues. It is unclear whether choices like α (0.75 vs 1), composition operator (⊔INT vs ⊔UNI), and hybrid weight q were tuned strictly within training folds (nested CV) or chosen post hoc from test performance. For word2vec, the search space seems limited (no tuning of window beyond fixed 5/10, no negative sampling settings, epochs, min_count, or dependency-based embeddings). Dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg 2014), SVD on PPMI, and GloVe are natural baselines given the paper’s focus on syntactic context; their absence weakens the comparative claims.
- Methodological ambiguities that may favor APTs. (i) For APTs, similarity is computed only on first-order dependency features after composition; this choice may advantage unaligned APTs and should be justified with ablations. (ii) You ignore modifier-side dependencies during preprocessing, possibly discarding relevant signal. (iii) Compounds are inserted as single tokens; then observed compound vectors are compared to composed vectors. This conflates “inferring unseen phrases” with “matching seen phrases.” Please quantify how often each compound is observed; report correlations controlling for log frequency, and assess performance on low-frequency compounds separately.
- Reproducibility gaps. Key details are missing: pruning thresholds/min_count for features, the set and number of dependency relations R, reduction implementation, exact number of repetitions, seeds, training epochs, negative sampling parameters, and whether α and operator selection used nested CV. Code/data are not provided.
- Presentation/formatting problems. Multiple references are malformed or incomplete (e.g., “Baroni and Lenci.,? 2010”, “Schmid.,? \Q1994\E”), “reference suppressed” placeholders remain, Table 4 is referenced but not included, footnotes are mangled, and notation has glitches (e.g., inverse relation typography). These issues impede verification and must be fixed.

Questions to authors
- Novelty: Precisely delineate what is new relative to your prior APT work. Is the hybrid mixing the only methodological novelty? Any new alignment/reduction variants?
- Tuning/statistics: Was α, operator (⊔INT/⊔UNI), and hybrid q chosen within a nested CV on training folds, or selected after seeing test results? How many repetitions were used? What statistical test supports the “Δρ > 0.005” claim?
- Frequency control: What are the per-compound frequencies in ukWaC after re-tokenization? What is ρ after partialling out log frequency, or when restricting to low-frequency compounds?
- Baselines: Can you add dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg, 2014), SVD on PPMI contexts, and GloVe? Also report CBOW with dependency contexts to match APT’s syntactic signal.
- Ablations: (i) Report performance when computing similarity using all orders vs only first-order features. (ii) Effect of including modifier-side dependencies. (iii) Performance when compounds are not merged into single tokens (to assess reliance on observed compound vectors).
- Missing results: Table 4 is referenced—please include it. Also, provide the number of dependency relations used, pruning thresholds, and all word2vec training settings (epochs, negative sampling k, learning rate, min_count).

Overall recommendation
Despite promising empirical results and a clear intuition, the paper’s novelty is limited, the evaluation is narrow, statistical and methodological details are insufficient, and the presentation has many issues. I lean toward rejection unless the authors can substantially strengthen the evaluation, add stronger baselines, clarify tuning/statistics, and fix presentation/reproducibility concerns.
