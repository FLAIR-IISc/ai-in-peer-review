Summary and contributions
- This paper studies compositionality detection for English noun–noun compounds by contrasting “composed” representations with “observed” (holistic) ones, measured against human ratings (Reddy et al., 2011). The core technical vehicle is Anchored Packed Trees (APTs): typed dependency-path features with higher-order paths and an alignment mechanism via offsetting along the dependency path (with path reduction) to bring constituents into the head’s feature space before pointwise composition. They also consider unaligned and hybrid (aligned+unaligned) variants, and compare against neural word embeddings with additive composition.
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) Empirical finding: Typed, higher-order, dependency-based features—especially the proposed hybrid of aligned and unaligned APT composition—substantially outperform strong baselines on compound compositionality detection (ρ up to 0.79 vs 0.71 prior SOTA and 0.74 for tuned CBOW).
  2) Methodological framing: Composition as alignment in a typed dependency feature space via offsetting and reduction, and the insight that for noun–noun compounds, unaligned modifier features can be highly predictive (and complementary to aligned features), leading to the hybrid model.
  3) Evaluation protocol refinement: Careful corpus preprocessing to create holistic compound tokens while preventing leakage into constituent representations, enabling a fairer composed-vs-observed comparison.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains and a new best result on a standard benchmark. The hybrid APT model achieves ρ=0.79, outperforming the previous best (0.71) and well-tuned neural baselines (CBOW 0.74). The improvement is consistent across PPMI variants and composition choices and is non-trivial given how competitive simple addition often is in compositional settings.
- Bold, structure-aware representation design. The APT alignment via dependency-path offsetting and reduction is a principled way to make composition sensitive to syntax, moving beyond untyped proximity contexts or naïvely composed embeddings. The observation that unaligned NMOD features naturally overlap with head noun spaces—and the decision to explicitly combine aligned and unaligned evidence—is insightful and practically effective.
- Thoughtful experimental controls in a notoriously tricky task. The authors proactively avoid leakage by disallowing shared features between compound and constituents, and they surface a strong frequency baseline (ρ=0.63), demonstrating that their gains are not merely artifacts of frequency correlations.
- Useful negative/positive parameter insights. The paper explains why shifted PMI and heavy subsampling, which often help in other tasks, can hurt here due to the low frequency of many compounds. It also reports stable ranges for mixing weights (h, q), yielding interpretable takeaways about the modifier’s informativeness and the complementarity of aligned vs unaligned features.
- Directional impact for compositional semantics. The work strengthens the case that syntactic typing and higher-order paths are important for compositionality detection, and suggests a practical recipe (hybrid APTs) that is both conceptually fresh and empirically strong. The closing suggestion—using compositionality detection to guide smoothing choices—is promising.

Weaknesses
- Novelty relative to prior APT work is under-specified. APTs are cited as “Elsewhere (reference suppressed).” If the core APT machinery (typed higher-order paths + alignment by offsetting and reduction) was introduced previously, then the novelty here hinges on the hybrid formulation and the targeted application to compositionality detection. The paper should clarify what is new versus prior APT publications.
- Small benchmark and limited generality. The Reddy dataset has only 90 compounds and shows strong frequency effects. While this is the canonical testbed, claims would be stronger with additional datasets (e.g., larger noun compound sets, multiword expressions, adjective–noun or verb–object phrases) and languages. The current gains, though notable, could be sensitive to this dataset’s idiosyncrasies.
- Incomplete baseline coverage and analysis. The study compares to word2vec but not to GloVe or more recent dependency-based embeddings; it also omits stronger compositional baselines (e.g., lexical function models, supervised or generative compositionality models trained on larger corpora) on this task. An ablation of typed vs untyped APTs and of maximum path order would better isolate the sources of gain.
- Potential confounds in preprocessing. The strategy to recombine compounds as single tokens and select the most frequent dependency relation δ is reasonable, but the choice to ignore modifier-side dependencies (and to fix δ when multiple relations exist) may bias which contexts are learnable and comparable. A sensitivity analysis to these design decisions would help.
- Reproducibility and presentation issues. Several implementation details are elided (e.g., pruning thresholds, vocabulary caps, exact path inventories, handling of zero’th order features), and the references contain numerous formatting/placeholders. Code or a detailed appendix would substantially aid reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- What, precisely, is novel in this submission relative to your prior APT work? Is the hybrid composition (mixing aligned and unaligned paths) new? Were higher-order typed paths and the offset/reduction alignment previously introduced?
- How sensitive are results to the maximum dependency path order used in the elementary APTs? Do gains persist with only first-order paths? What about including up to third order?
- Can you report partial correlations controlling for compound frequency, or performance on a frequency-stratified split, to quantify how much improvement remains after factoring out frequency?
- How robust are results to alternative preprocessing choices: not fixing δ to the most frequent relation, or keeping modifier-side dependencies? Does the hybrid still win?
- Could you include a GloVe baseline and a dependency-based embedding baseline, and an untyped APT ablation, to better separate the role of typing from the compositional mechanism?
- Will you release code and scripts for corpus preprocessing, APT construction, and composition to aid reproducibility?

Overall, I find the direction compelling and the empirical gains convincing for this task. If the hybrid APT formulation and its application to compositionality detection are indeed new, I view this as a meaningful, structure-aware step beyond incremental composition-by-addition baselines.
