Summary and Contributions
The paper studies detection of non-compositionality in noun–noun compounds by leveraging compositional methods built over typed, higher-order dependency contexts (Anchored Packed Trees, APTs). Using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds with human compositionality judgments), it compares several composition strategies: aligned APT composition (offsetting dependency paths to align feature spaces), unaligned composition, and a hybrid that linearly combines aligned and unaligned representations. It contrasts these against neural word embeddings (word2vec, CBOW and SG) with additive composition and prior untyped baselines.

Contributions (as assessed):
1) Empirical demonstration that typed higher-order dependency features and APT-based composition, especially a simple hybrid of aligned and unaligned features, substantially improve correlation with human compositionality judgments (best ρ ≈ 0.79) over prior work (Reddy et al. 2011: ρ ≈ 0.71) and over neural CBOW/SG baselines (best ≈ 0.74).
2) A hybrid composition scheme that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APTs, showing that each predicts complementary contextual evidence for compound nouns and yields consistent gains.
3) Analysis of weighting and smoothing choices: showing modest benefits of context-distribution smoothing in PPMI (α = 0.75), that additive composition outperforms intersective/min, and that optimal constituent weights (h, q ∈ [0.3, 0.5]) suggest modifiers and unaligned typed features contribute slightly more to compositionality detection.

Strengths
- Substantive empirical gains with a clear ablation picture: The paper provides a thorough comparison across aligned, unaligned, and hybrid APT models under multiple weighting schemes (PPMI with α ∈ {1, 0.75}, composed counts vs composed PPMI) and composition operators (add vs min). The reported hybrid APT result (ρ = 0.79) exceeds prior state of the art (ρ = 0.71) and neural baselines (best CBOW ≈ 0.74), indicating the utility of typed, higher-order dependency paths for this task.
- Careful control of representations to avoid trivial overlap: The preprocessing ensures that features of compound tokens do not leak into constituent representations, so that composition must infer rather than memorize compound contexts. This strengthens the validity of comparing composed vs observed vectors.
- Fair baseline on neural embeddings trained on the same corpus: word2vec CBOW and SG are trained on the same ukWaC variant with compounds merged, with several dimensionalities and subsampling settings. The findings (CBOW > SG; higher subsampling hurts due to low compound frequency) are informative and relevant.
- Insightful analysis of parameter effects: The paper evaluates PPMI smoothing, intersective vs additive composition, and the relative contributions of aligned vs unaligned features (via q) and head vs modifier (via h), providing interpretable conclusions aligned with linguistic intuition for noun compounds (NMOD dominates).
- Task framing aligns evaluation with compositionality: By correlating similarity between composed and observed compound vectors with human compositionality scores, the paper avoids conflating similarity and compositionality, a known issue in phrase similarity evaluations.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior APT work: The core APT framework and the benefits of typed higher-order dependencies have been proposed previously (cited as Weeds et al., 2014 and “reference suppressed”). The primary novelty appears to be the hybrid alignment scheme and its application to compositionality detection of noun compounds. This is an incremental contribution rather than a fundamentally new model.
- Small evaluation set and statistical testing: The dataset has only 90 items. The claim that differences > 0.005 are statistically significant is based on repeated 3-fold CV with many repetitions, which may underestimate uncertainty due to reusing the same 90 labels. A more appropriate item-level paired test (e.g., permutation or bootstrap over compounds; Steiger’s test for dependent correlations) should be used to support significance claims, and confidence intervals over items should be reported.
- Incomplete comparison to strong dependency-based baselines: The study does not compare against dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014) or other compositional models adapted to compositionality detection (e.g., Hermann et al., 2012 generative model), which are closer in spirit to the proposed typed approaches than window-based word2vec. Including these would strengthen the case that typed higher-order paths, rather than model capacity or preprocessing, drive the gains.
- Reproducibility details for neural and APT settings are sparse: For word2vec, key settings such as negative sampling rate, number of iterations/epochs, min-count threshold, learning rate schedule, and downsampling exact values are not fully specified. For APTs, feature pruning thresholds, vocabulary cutoffs, and exact path order limits for composed vs observed vectors need clarification. Omitting shifted PPMI results “for compactness” reduces transparency.
- Potential confounds in corpus preprocessing: Compounds are merged into single tokens and used to build their observed vectors. While constituent vectors avoid leakage, the observed compound vectors’ quality varies with frequency. It would be useful to analyze performance stratified by compound frequency and by syntactic relation variability (since the “most frequent” relation is chosen when multiple relations are observed).

Questions to Authors
- Statistical testing: How are significance tests conducted? Please provide item-level confidence intervals and paired significance tests (e.g., permutation or bootstrap) for key comparisons (hybrid APT vs unaligned APT; hybrid APT vs CBOW).
- Dependency-based embeddings baseline: Did you try dependency-based word embeddings (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) or other typed embedding methods as baselines? If not, could you include them?
- Breakdown analyses: Can you report performance stratified by compound frequency bins and by compositionality level (low/medium/high), and provide per-compound scatterplots for top models?
- Word2vec configuration: Please specify negative sampling k, number of epochs, min-count threshold, learning rate, and whether HS was used. Were compounds with very low counts filtered out?
- APT details: What, if any, feature pruning thresholds and vocabulary cutoffs were applied? Are composed representations limited to order-2 paths for both constituents and observed compounds? Could you release code and the preprocessed lexicon?

Overall, the work is clearly written, technically plausible, and presents solid empirical improvements on a standard benchmark, with moderate novelty primarily in applying and slightly extending APTs to compositionality detection. Addressing the evaluation and baseline gaps would strengthen the contribution.
