- Paper studies detection of non-compositionality in noun–noun compounds by comparing composed phrase representations with holistic representations using APTs.  
- Introduces an APT-based compositional framework using typed, higher-order dependency-path features with composition via alignment (offsetting) and reduction.  
- Empirical result: APT-based composition (hybrid aligned+unaligned) achieves Spearman ρ up to 0.79 on Reddy et al. (2011), outperforming prior 0.71 and tuned cbow 0.74.  
- Main contribution: Strong empirical improvements on compound compositionality detection with careful exploration of composition operators, weighting, and PPMI smoothing.  
- Methodological insight: Typed, higher-order dependency-path features aid composition, and combining aligned and unaligned spaces provides complementary evidence.  
- Experimental analysis: Controlled comparison between dependency-typed models and untyped neural embeddings on the same ukWaC corpus, including parameter trends (modifier slightly more informative).  
- Strength: Clear empirical gains over strong baselines with repeated 3-fold CV and small standard errors supporting reliability.  
- Strength: Well-motivated use of dependency-typed, higher-order contexts; hybrid results reinforce the hypothesis.  
- Strength: Solid experimental setup and transparency in preprocessing, feature construction, and composition equations aiding reproducibility.  
- Strength: Thoughtful analysis of design choices (e.g., PMI shifting, subsampling) and informative parameter findings (h and q around 0.3–0.5).  
- Strength: Topic is relevant and significant to the community studying compositionality.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty—APTs and alignment are prior work and aligned composition is not the main driver of gains for NN compounds.  
- Weakness: Evaluation restricted to a single small dataset (90 items), raising robustness and generalization concerns and risk of overfitting during tuning.  
- Weakness: Missing stronger typed-context baselines (e.g., dependency-based embeddings or typed-count models without APT) to isolate APT-specific benefits.  
- Weakness: Statistical testing and ablation granularity are insufficient—paired significance tests and deeper ablations (first- vs second-order) are lacking.  
- Weakness: Potential frequency confound not fully addressed, since frequency correlates strongly with human ratings (ρ=0.63).  
- Request: Provide results with dependency-based embeddings and/or typed count baselines without APT alignment to isolate APT effects.  
- Request: Provide ablation showing contribution of higher-order paths vs only first-order typed features.  
- Request: Report partial correlations controlling for compound frequency or per-bin analyses to rule out frequency effects.  
- Request: Assess sensitivity to dependency parser/tagging accuracy and robustness to parsing noise.  
- Request: Report exact best h and q values (means and variance across folds) and whether they vary with constituent POS patterns.  
- Overall: Well-executed empirical study with solid results but limited methodological novelty and narrow evaluation; recommend acceptance conditional on adding stronger typed baselines and more detailed ablations/analyses.
