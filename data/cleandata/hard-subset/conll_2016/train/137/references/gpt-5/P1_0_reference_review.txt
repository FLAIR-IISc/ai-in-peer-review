Summary and claimed contributions
- This paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds using distributional semantics. It contrasts typed dependency-based representations with higher-order paths (Anchored Packed Trees, APTs) against standard untyped proximity-based word embeddings. The central idea is to predict a compound’s observed distributional vector from its constituents via composition and to use the similarity between composed and observed vectors as a proxy for human judgments of compositionality (Reddy et al., 2011).
- The paper evaluates: (i) aligned APT composition (offsetting modifiers to the head’s type space), (ii) unaligned APT composition, and (iii) a hybrid linear combination, and compares these with CBOW and skip-gram embeddings with additive composition, all on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) Empirical finding that typed, higher-order dependency features with a hybrid of aligned and unaligned composition outperform both untyped neural embeddings and prior baselines on the Reddy dataset (best ρ=0.79 vs 0.71).
  2) Careful ablation showing the impact of alignment, composition operator (min vs addition), PMI smoothing (α), and subsampling choices for embeddings; providing insights such as modifiers being slightly more informative than heads for this task.
  3) Methodological clarification that unaligned typed dependency features can contribute complementary evidence to aligned composition for compound nouns, despite the theoretical appeal of alignment.

Strengths
- Strong empirical results with thorough ablations:
  - The hybrid APT approach achieves a clear improvement over the Reddy et al. (2011) baseline (0.79 vs 0.71 Spearman), exceeding frequency and random baselines. The comparison against multiple APT variants (aligned/unaligned/hybrid; PPMI vs counts; α=1 vs 0.75; addition vs min) is comprehensive and informative. The neural baselines are tuned reasonably (CBOW vs SG, dims, subsampling), with CBOW at 0.74.
- Clear motivation and task framing:
  - The paper correctly separates the goals of compositionality detection from general phrase similarity, avoiding confounds by evaluating on the Reddy dataset and by explicitly composing constituents then comparing to observed compound vectors.
- Use of typed, higher-order dependency paths:
  - The APT features encode full dependency paths and show the value of syntax and path structure for this task. The discussion (Tables 1–2) clarifies how offsetting aligns feature spaces across POS and relations, and why higher-order paths are informative for compounds.
- Insightful analyses:
  - Findings that additive union operations generally outperform intersective minimum, that PPMI smoothing with α=0.75 helps, that CBOW benefits from less aggressive subsampling in this setting, and that optimal h and q values give slightly more weight to modifiers and to unaligned features are useful to the community.
- Attention to data preprocessing:
  - The corpus is preprocessed to collapse compounds into single tokens and to record dependency relations, and the authors attempt to avoid leakage by not letting compound context features directly populate constituent representations.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and external validity:
  - The sole evaluation is on 90 compounds from Reddy et al. (2011). No results on other compositionality datasets (e.g., phrasal verbs, adjective–noun datasets, larger or more recent resources), no cross-domain or cross-corpus testing, and no constituent-level compositionality prediction results. This limits claims about generality and robustness.
- Potential leakage/confounds from corpus manipulation:
  - Observed compound vectors and constituent vectors are derived from the same parsed corpus after compounds are collapsed into tokens. Although the paper attempts to avoid direct feature sharing, using the same corpus for both observed and composed representations risks subtle leakage and overlap. A stricter regime (e.g., removing all occurrences of each test compound when building constituent representations; leave-one-compound-out; or downweighting contexts derived from compound occurrences) would strengthen the causal interpretation.
- Reproducibility and clarity of the APT method:
  - Key technical details are deferred to “reference suppressed,” including the exact reduction and alignment procedures, the allowed path grammar (τ ∈ R*R*), and implementation specifics. Without code or a fully self-contained description, reproducing the APT variants is difficult. References are also incomplete/garbled, and Table 4 is mentioned but not shown.
- Statistical reporting and small-sample uncertainty:
  - While the paper states that repeated 3-fold CV yields small errors (≤0.0015) and that differences >0.005 are significant, it does not specify the number of repetitions, nor does it provide confidence intervals or formal tests. With N=90 items, even small methodological choices could have outsized effects; more transparent statistics would help.
- Design choices that may bias comparisons:
  - Similarity is computed only over first-order dependency features of the observed phrases, justified by noise in composed higher-order paths. This may inherently favor unaligned models and complicates the interpretation of the alignment benefit. It would be informative to show variants including higher-order features on the observed side, or to analyze how performance varies with allowed path orders.
- Practicality concerns:
  - The APT noun feature spaces are large (80k–230k dimensions). The paper does not discuss computational costs, sparsity handling, or scalability relative to dense embeddings, nor the impact of parser errors and POS ambiguity on real-world systems.

Questions to authors
- How many repetitions were used in the repeated 3-fold CV? Please report the exact protocol, ranges and step sizes for h and q, and provide confidence intervals or p-values for key comparisons.
- Can you detail the reduction function with concrete examples, and clarify the constraint τ ∈ R*R*? Are any path patterns pruned beyond order limits?
- Did you evaluate constituent-level compositionality (as annotated in Reddy et al.) with your models? If not, could you report those correlations?
- How sensitive are results to removing all occurrences of a test compound from the corpus when building constituent APTs? This would better guard against leakage.
- What is the effect of including second-order features on the observed side in the similarity computation? Does it change the relative ranking of aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid?
- Can you estimate runtime/memory costs of APT construction and composition versus CBOW/SG pipelines, and comment on scalability and parser error sensitivity?

Additional comments
- The reference list needs cleanup; several entries are malformed. Please include full details of the “reference suppressed” APT paper or make this submission self-contained.
- Consider evaluating on additional datasets (e.g., McCarthy et al. 2003 phrasal verbs; adjective–noun compositionality sets) to bolster generality claims.
- Making code and preprocessed counts available would greatly aid reproducibility.
