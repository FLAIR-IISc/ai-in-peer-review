This paper applies Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) with typed higher-order dependency paths to detect noun–noun compound compositionality, comparing aligned, unaligned, and hybrid composition on Reddy et al. (2011)'s 90-compound dataset.

Main empirical claim: APTs significantly outperform window-based baselines and word2vec addition (ρ up to ~0.79 vs. ~0.71 in prior work).

Methodological finding: Unaligned APT composition can beat aligned composition for noun–noun compounds, and a linear hybrid of both yields the best results.

Controlled comparison: Same-corpus experiments show cbow > skip-gram and that standard subsampling can harm low-frequency compound modeling.

Strength: Clear empirical improvements on a standard benchmark that are robust across APT variants and weighting choices.

Strength: Linguistically motivated use of full dependency paths and path-offset alignment with concrete head-perspective composition operations.

Strength: Fair, controlled training and baseline setup, with exploration of hyperparameters and contextual baselines (frequency, random).

Analytic insight: Optimal constituent weights suggest modifiers contribute slightly more than heads to compositionality; discussion explains why subsampling and shifted PMI hurt low-frequency items.

Relevance: Argues compositionality detection is a cleaner evaluation for compositional models and has practical uses (deciding when to smooth holistic vs. constituent representations).

Weakness: Narrow evaluation on a single small dataset (90 compounds) limits generalizability to other MWE types and risks overfitting.

Weakness: Incremental novelty — APT and alignment build on prior work; main novelty is application to compositionality and the hybrid combination.

Weakness: Preprocessing confound — retokenizing compounds and choosing a single dependency relation may advantage dependency-typed models and underrepresent variation.

Weakness: Potential methodological mismatch — similarity computed over first-order features while composition uses higher-order dependencies; more ablation needed to attribute gains to composition.

Weakness: Missing baselines — no dependency-based embeddings, lexical function models, or supervised compositionality models included for fair syntactic comparisons.

Weakness: Insufficient statistical reporting and reproducibility details (number of repetitions, tests, confidence intervals) plus missing discussion of computational costs and scalability.

Questions requested: Breakdown of performance by dependency relation and modifier POS; cases where aligned helps vs. hurts.

Questions requested: Number of 3-fold CV repetitions, confidence intervals/SDs, and statistical test used for comparing ρ.

Questions requested: Sensitivity to APT maximum path order and whether higher orders help aligned models.

Questions requested: Add a dependency-based embedding baseline (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) with simple addition for comparison.

Questions requested: Memory/runtime costs for building and composing APTs and scalability to larger lexicons/longer phrases.

Questions requested: Robustness to parser choice/quality and effect of not retokenizing compounds.

Additional comments: Fix reference formatting errors and placeholders for camera-ready.

Additional comments: Include qualitative analyses (top predicted vs. observed features) to illustrate interpretability and alignment contributions.

Additional comments: A small pilot applying compositionality for smoothing decisions would strengthen practical impact.

Overall verdict: Solid empirical paper with linguistically principled approach but limited scope and incremental novelty; borderline accept — adding dependency-based baselines and addressing the raised questions would strengthen it.
