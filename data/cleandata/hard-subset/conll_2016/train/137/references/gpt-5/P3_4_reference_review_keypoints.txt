- Paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds using typed dependency APTs, comparing neural and count-based representations and correlating predicted vectors with human judgments (Reddy et al., 2011).

- Key methods: encode higher-order typed dependency paths, align constituent spaces via dependency-path offsets, and mix aligned/unaligned representations to predict holistic compound vectors (best APT hybrid ρ≈0.79).

- Contribution: shows typed, higher-order dependency features improve NN compositionality detection and introduces a hybrid aligned/unaligned APT composition achieving state-of-the-art correlation on the benchmark.

- Contribution: careful experimental setup to avoid leakage by collapsing compounds for holistic vectors while excluding those contexts from constituent representations, and systematic comparison to tuned word2vec baselines.

- Contribution: analysis showing mixing aligned and unaligned APTs yields complementary information, improving predictions.

- Strength: principled, well-motivated modeling of typed contexts and alignment via offset-and-reduction, with clear examples illustrating why alignment is needed.

- Strength: strong empirical gains on a canonical benchmark, outperforming untyped count methods and tuned CBOW/SG baselines, with informative ablation studies.

- Strength: thoughtful experimental controls preventing trivial solutions by excluding holistic contexts from constituent vectors and limiting similarity scoring to first-order features.

- Strength: interpretable analyses (e.g., parameter optima near 0.3–0.5, CBOW subsampling effects) that provide insight into model behavior and sparsity issues.

- Weakness: very small, narrow evaluation set (90 NN compounds), limiting generality; additional datasets (other NN sets, VPC/VNC, AN phrases, MWE resources) and dependency-subtype analyses needed.

- Weakness: surprising finding that unaligned APT often outperforms aligned APT, with only small hybrid gains; lacks diagnostics to explain why alignment underperforms.

- Weakness: incomplete baselines—missing dependency-based SGNS, dependency-context GloVe, and standard PPMI+SVD typed dependency baselines to isolate APT-specific effects.

- Weakness: statistical testing concerns—CV repetition does not increase independent observations and reported tiny SEs likely underestimate across-item uncertainty; recommends paired permutation/bootstrap tests.

- Weakness: corpus preprocessing confound—collapsing compounds increases holistic token frequency and may change task difficulty; ablation without collapsing or reporting token counts is needed.

- Question: why do aligned APTs underperform unaligned ones—can you provide diagnostics (feature overlap, sparsity after reduction, per-relation breakdown) or evaluate including second-order features?

- Question: did you evaluate dependency-based SGNS (Levy & Goldberg) or GloVe on typed dependency contexts as baselines?

- Question: how sensitive are results to collapsing compounds—what happens if you train without collapsing for constituents and infer holistic compounds post hoc?

- Question: can you run paired permutation tests over the 90 items and report p-values for system comparisons?

- Question: what are the memory/time costs and scalability of building/composing high-dimensional APTs (~80k–230k dims), and do pruning strategies affect performance?

- Additional comment: add an error analysis showing examples where hybrid APT succeeds/fails versus CBOW, idiomatic vs compositional cases, and per-dependency relation impacts.

- Additional comment: consider releasing code and preprocessed resources to improve reproducibility.
