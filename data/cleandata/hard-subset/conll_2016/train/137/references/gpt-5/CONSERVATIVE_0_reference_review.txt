Summary and contributions
- The paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds using dependency-typed distributional features and an “Anchored Packed Trees” (APT) composition mechanism that aligns modifier/head spaces via dependency-path offsets. The central claim is that typed, higher-order dependency contexts, combined with simple additive composition, yield better correlation with human compositionality ratings (Reddy et al., 2011) than untyped count vectors and neural word embeddings.
- A hybrid approach that linearly combines aligned (offset) and unaligned APT representations of the modifier further improves performance, suggesting complementary information in both.
- Empirical observations about weighting indicate that modifiers and unaligned typed contexts contribute slightly more than heads/aligned contexts to predicting compositionality.

Strengths
- Well-motivated use of typed dependency contexts and higher-order paths: The rationale that compositionality judgments benefit from syntactic structure is sound and consistent with established literature. The alignment-by-offset idea is clearly explained and linguistically plausible.
- Solid gains on the standard benchmark: On the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, the best APT hybrid model achieves ρ≈0.79, improving over the previously reported 0.71 and a reasonably tuned CBOW baseline at 0.74. The improvements over the frequency baseline (0.63) indicate the model captures more than mere token frequency effects.
- Care to avoid trivial leakage: The preprocessing to merge compounds into single tokens while preventing shared features from directly inflating constituent vectors is a thoughtful design choice for this evaluation protocol.
- Analysis across reasonable parameter choices: The paper compares PPMI variants (with and without smoothing), additive vs intersective operations, aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid composition, and multiple word2vec settings. The finding that additive composition remains strong aligns with prior results and increases confidence that the gains come from the contextual typing rather than an idiosyncratic composition function.
- Informative empirical insights: The consistent optimal weights (h and q in [0.3, 0.5]) and the observation that typed unaligned contexts are often more predictive than purely aligned ones are interesting and potentially useful for practitioners.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior APT work and typed dependency models: Much of the technical core (APT representations, alignment via offsets, higher-order dependency features) appears to be based on the authors’ earlier work (reference suppressed) and established dependency-typed distributional approaches. The current paper is mostly an application to one compositionality dataset with modest methodological extensions (hybrid combination), rather than a substantive new model.
- Narrow and small-scale evaluation: Results are reported only on the 90-item Reddy dataset. This is a tiny testbed with high variance and known sensitivity to corpus/domain effects. No tests on other compositionality datasets (e.g., verb–object, adjective–noun beyond compounds, VPC/MWE sets) or similarity benchmarks are provided, limiting claims about generality.
- Missing strong baselines: The comparison omits dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg’s dependency contexts), SVD-PPMI or GloVe with typed contexts, and competitive composition functions (e.g., lexical function/tensor models applied to dependency-based spaces). Given the paper’s emphasis on typed contexts, a dependency-based embedding baseline is essential. Without these, it is hard to attribute gains specifically to the APT design rather than to the use of typed contexts per se.
- Methodological concerns and significance claims: The paper reports tiny standard errors (≤0.0015) and asserts significance for >0.005 differences, which seems overly optimistic given Spearman ρ on folds of ~60 items. No paired significance test is described. The evaluation protocol correlates composed-with-observed similarity against human scores using the same corpus both to build constituent and phrasal representations; while standard in this literature, it risks corpus-specific overfitting. Robustness to corpus choice/domain shift is not tested.
- Reproducibility and clarity gaps: Important implementation details are missing or under-specified: feature pruning thresholds, min counts, handling of rare contexts, exact dimensionalities per POS for each order, reduction rules beyond a brief definition, training settings for word2vec (epochs, min_count), and the exact cross-validation scheme. The procedure for selecting the “most frequent” dependency relation per compound is not clearly tied to folds. References are error-prone and incomplete, which hinders verification.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Can you include dependency-based word embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014 dependency contexts) and a strong SVD-PPMI or GloVe baseline with dependency-typed features? How do your APT models compare when all systems use typed contexts?
- Robustness: How do results change if (a) observed compound vectors are computed from a different corpus or domain, and/or (b) composition is evaluated on a held-out corpus to reduce corpus-specific coupling? Can you report results on additional datasets (e.g., adjective–noun, verb–object compositionality, VPCs)?
- Significance/testing: What statistical test supports the “>0.005 difference is significant” claim? Please provide paired tests over identical folds and report confidence intervals.
- Preprocessing details: Please clarify precisely how compounds and their dependency relations are selected and merged, and whether this selection is performed using only training folds. What is the impact of parsing errors you observed (e.g., three-noun compounds) on the final results?
- Ablations: Can you isolate the contributions of (i) typed vs untyped contexts, (ii) first-order vs higher-order paths, (iii) alignment vs no alignment, beyond the hybrid combination? A leave-one-factor-out analysis would make the source of improvements clearer.
- Efficiency/scalability: What are the memory and time costs for building and composing APTs at the reported dimensionalities (80k to 230k per POS)? How would this approach scale to larger vocabularies and sentence-level composition?

Overall, the paper presents a careful and linguistically motivated application of typed dependency contexts to compositionality detection with encouraging empirical gains. However, the limited novelty, small and narrow evaluation, and missing key baselines make it difficult to assess the contribution relative to established alternatives. Strengthening the experimental section along the lines above would substantially improve the paper.
