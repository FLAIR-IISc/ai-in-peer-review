Summary and main contributions
This paper studies compositionality detection for English compound nouns using typed, higher-order dependency features and compositional Anchored Packed Trees (APTs). Using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds, the authors compare several APT composition variants (aligned, unaligned, hybrid; intersective vs additive; PPMI vs normalized counts; α-smoothing) against neural word embedding baselines (CBOW, skip-gram) trained on the same corpus (ukWaC). They report new best Spearman correlations with human compositionality judgments (ρ up to 0.79), outperforming both the original Reddy baseline (0.71) and strong CBOW baselines (0.74).

Contributions (as I see them):
1) Empirical finding: Typed, higher-order dependency features composed via APTs yield state-of-the-art performance on noun-compound compositionality detection, substantially outperforming untyped proximity models and neural baselines trained fairly on the same data.
2) Methodological insight: A systematic comparison of aligned vs unaligned APT composition and their hybrid shows that unaligned typed features are highly informative and that combining aligned and unaligned views improves performance further.
3) Careful evaluation design: A reasonably thorough ablation over weighting schemes (PPMI vs counts), α-smoothing, composition operators (add vs intersect), and neural hyperparameters clarifies what matters for this task (e.g., subsampling harms low-frequency compounds).

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains with fair baselines: The best APT model achieves ρ=0.79 vs 0.71 (Reddy) and 0.74 (best CBOW), trained on the same corpus. This is a meaningful improvement on a widely used benchmark and suggests typed, structured context is beneficial for compositionality detection.
- Well-motivated experimental manipulations: The paper isolates the impact of typing and higher-order paths, alignment vs unaligned composition, and union vs intersection operations. The finding that unaligned typed modifiers overlap meaningfully with head noun features in noun–noun compounds is insightful and supported by results; the hybrid model’s consistent edge indicates complementary signals.
- Sensible treatment of data leakage: The preprocessing merges compounds into single tokens and ensures compound contexts do not leak into constituent representations, making the prediction-from-constituents problem nontrivial and more faithful to the goal of composition without observing the phrase.
- Analysis of weighting and smoothing: The comparison of composing counts vs composing PPMI and then computing similarity with cosine, plus the α=0.75 smoothing, is careful. The explanation for why shifted PMI hurts (sparsity of compounds) is plausible and useful to practitioners.
- Neural baselines calibrated on same data: While not exhaustive, the CBOW/SGNS baselines were tuned over dimensionality and subsampling, and the observation that subsampling can degrade rare compound representations provides practical guidance.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior APT work: The APT framework and alignment mechanism appear to be from prior work (reference suppressed). The new contribution here is primarily the application to compositionality detection of noun compounds and the comparative study; methodological innovation is incremental.
- Small and narrow evaluation: The main evaluation uses only 90 noun compounds from Reddy et al. This limits statistical power and external validity. The method is pitched as generally compositional, yet evidence is restricted to one phenomenon (NN compounds) in English. No evaluation on constituent-level literality (also annotated in Reddy) or on other phrase types (AN, VN) is presented.
- Heavy dependence on full dependency parses at web scale: The approach requires dependency-parsing 1.9B tokens and constructing very large typed feature spaces (up to ~230K dims for nouns). There is no discussion of computational cost, memory footprint, or runtime, nor comparison to lighter-weight alternatives (e.g., dependency-based embeddings or SVD of PPMI).
- Missing or incomplete comparisons: The study does not compare against dependency-based neural embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014) or other recent baselines such as GloVe trained on ukWaC, which could test whether “typed contexts” alone, independent of APT alignment, explain the gains. Prior non-neural models tailored to compounds (e.g., Hermann et al., 2012) are cited but not re-evaluated on the same corpus setup.
- Limited analysis and error characterization: The paper reports cross-validated averages and claims tiny standard errors but does not provide per-item error analysis, breakdown by frequency bins, or by syntactic relation (e.g., true NN vs adjectival uses). The strong correlation between frequency and compositionality (FREQ baseline ρ=0.63) suggests a confound; more diagnostics would improve interpretability.

Questions to authors
- Can you report constituent-level compositionality correlations (for head and modifier) in addition to phrase-level, given the dataset provides these? Do aligned vs unaligned APTs differ in predicting constituent literality?
- How large are the APT vocabularies and feature matrices in memory, and what are the typical training and inference times? Could you share implementation details and code to facilitate reproducibility?
- Did you evaluate dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg 2014) as a neural baseline that also exploits typed contexts? If not, can you comment on expected performance relative to your APT models?
- How robust are the results to parsing errors and alternative parsers? Given that 24% of co-occurring candidate lemmas were not linked as dependencies (often due to parsing issues), does performance change when restricting to high-confidence parses?
- The hybrid weighting q and h are tuned globally. Did you try conditioning them on relation type (e.g., NMOD vs AMOD) or frequency, or learning them per-compound? Any evidence that such conditioning helps?
- Can your approach generalize to AN or VN compositions where alignment seems more critical? Any preliminary results beyond NN compounds?

Additional comments
- Please clarify whether the cross-validation folds stratify by compound frequency, to control for the strong frequency–compositionality correlation.
- Consider releasing the preprocessed ukWaC with merged compound tokens and the list of dependency paths retained, to enable exact replication.
- Minor: A few references are malformed; please fix bibliographic details.
