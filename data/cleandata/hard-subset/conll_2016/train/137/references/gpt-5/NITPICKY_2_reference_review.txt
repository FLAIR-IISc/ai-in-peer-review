Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper studies compositionality detection for English noun–noun compounds using “anchored packed trees” (APTs), a typed, higher-order dependency feature space with an alignment operation (offset + path reduction) to enable composition. The authors compare aligned, unaligned, and hybrid (linear combination) APT composition with naïve addition of neural embeddings (word2vec) and prior count-based baselines.
- Main empirical claim: on the Reddy et al. (2011) 90-compound dataset, APT-based methods achieve higher Spearman correlation with human compositionality judgments (up to 0.79) than prior best (0.71) and than CBOW/SG embeddings (best ~0.74).
- Ancillary findings: (i) smoothed PPMI (α=0.75) helps slightly; (ii) unaligned typed dependency features often outperform aligned features; (iii) a hybrid of aligned and unaligned APTs performs best; (iv) modifiers contribute slightly more than heads in this dataset.

Strengths
- Clear, testable hypothesis about the utility of typed higher-order dependency features for compositionality detection. The comparison between aligned and unaligned APTs, and their hybrid, is informative and suggests complementary evidence captured by the two views.
- Empirical improvement over established baseline on a known benchmark. The reported hybrid APT result (ρ≈0.79) substantially exceeds the REDDY baseline (0.71) and the CBOW baseline (0.74), supporting the claim that structured dependency features matter for this task.
- Sensible analysis of weighting and operations. The paper systematically explores composition operators (min/add), constituent weighting (h), and mixture weight (q), and reports consistent optimal ranges (≈0.3–0.5), offering practical guidance.
- Careful corpus preprocessing to avoid trivial overlap between constituent and compound representations for APTs (merging compounds into single tokens and re-routing head contexts), which avoids an easy leakage that would artificially inflate intersective composition.
- The discussion linking PMI smoothing and subsampling to the low-frequency nature of many compounds is plausible and helps interpret why certain settings (e.g., high subsampling) hurt.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental and under-specified. APTs and higher-order typed dependency features with alignment are referenced as “elsewhere (reference suppressed)” and Weeds et al. (2014). The technical novelty here seems to be primarily an application to compositionality detection on noun compounds plus a hybrid combination. The alignment mechanism and reduction operator are not fully formalized or newly motivated; without the prior paper, this reads as a re-application rather than a substantial methodological contribution.
- Evaluation is limited and potentially fragile. Results are only on Reddy et al.’s 90-compound dataset, which is very small for correlation-based claims. No additional datasets (e.g., other noun compounds, MWEs, or controlled splits) are used. The stated standard errors (≤0.0015) and significance threshold (>0.005) are implausibly small for n=90; more robust uncertainty quantification (e.g., bootstrap CIs, permutation tests) is warranted. The strong frequency–compositionality correlation (ρ≈0.63) suggests confounding; no partial correlation controlling for log frequency (or a regression controlling for frequency, POS patterns, or length) is reported.
- Incomplete and inconsistent reporting. Key details are missing: feature pruning/thresholding, vocabulary size limits, negative sampling and epochs for word2vec, learning rates, random seeds, number of CV repetitions, exact cross-validation protocol on only 90 items (how are splits stratified?), and the exact search grids for h and q. Table 4 is referenced but not included; several tables/footnotes are malformed. It is unclear whether embeddings were trained on the preprocessed corpus with merged compound tokens (it appears so); if yes, this is fine for the evaluation protocol but should be explicitly stated and justified for comparability to prior work.
- Methodological choices risk leakage or bias. The dependency relation δ for each compound is chosen as the most frequent relation observed for that compound in the entire corpus, which blends training/test information in parameterization; while the task is to predict compositionality given the known compound, this choice should be justified and sensitivity-tested (e.g., if δ is forced to NMOD for all compounds). Additionally, the similarity is computed “restricted to 1st-order features,” which may advantage particular design choices; rationale is given (unreliable higher-order in composition), but ablations showing effect of including/excluding orders are needed.
- Presentation and referencing issues. Numerous typographical errors, inconsistent notation (e.g., zero’th), and many malformed or placeholder references (“reference suppressed,” question marks, missing years/venues). Footnotes intrude into the main text; equation/variable formatting is uneven; some claims lack citations (e.g., error magnitudes). This undermines clarity and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Novelty and prior art: What, precisely, is new relative to Weeds et al. (2014) and your prior APT work? Is the alignment/reduction operator identical to the prior, or modified here? What is the primary conceptual contribution beyond applying APTs to noun–noun compositionality?
- Statistical validation: Please provide exact CV protocol details (number of repetitions, split strategy), bootstrapped confidence intervals for ρ, and formal significance tests between models. The claimed SE ≤ 0.0015 for 90 items seems unrealistically low; how was it computed?
- Frequency control: Can you report partial correlations controlling for log compound frequency, and/or a regression that includes frequency (and possibly POS tag patterns) as covariates, to demonstrate gains beyond frequency effects?
- Ablations: (i) Use a fixed δ=NMOD for all compounds and compare. (ii) Compare similarity using 1st-order vs 1st+2nd-order features on the target side. (iii) Report results when training embeddings on the unmodified corpus (no merged compound tokens) to match Reddy et al.’s setting more closely.
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, preprocessing scripts, and the list of merged compound tokens with their selected δ? Please specify all hyperparameters for word2vec (negative samples, epochs, learning rate, min count) and APT feature pruning thresholds.

Overall, while the empirical gains are interesting and the study provides useful insights into typed dependency features for compositionality detection, the combination of limited novelty, small and potentially under-analyzed evaluation, methodological ambiguities, and significant presentation/reference issues prevents me from recommending acceptance at this stage.
