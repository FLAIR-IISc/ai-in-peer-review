Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper studies detection of compositionality in noun–noun compounds using compositional distributional semantics based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which encode typed, higher-order dependency paths. It contrasts “aligned” (path-offset) and “unaligned” compositions, and a hybrid of both, evaluated on the Reddy et al. (2011) 90-compound dataset.
- It reports improved Spearman correlations over prior work and over cbow/skip-gram baselines, arguing that typed dependency contexts and higher-order paths better support estimating compound compositionality by comparing composed vs observed (holistic) vectors.
- It explores parameter choices (PPMI vs counts, α smoothing, union vs intersection composition, weights h and q), and concludes the hybrid APT is best (ρ≈0.79), exceeding cbow (≈0.74) and the Reddy baseline (0.71).

Strengths
- Methodologically focused exploration of composition variants. The aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid comparison, and the study of intersective (min) vs additive (sum) composition, provide useful insight about how dependency-typed contexts contribute to compound meaning.
- Strong empirical results on a standard dataset. The reported correlations improve over the Reddy baseline and over reasonably tuned cbow/skip-gram settings. The observation that hybrid APT > unaligned APT > aligned APT is informative, and the consistent optimal h,q in 0.3–0.5 range is a replicable detail.
- Sound motivation for higher-order dependency features. The alignment intuition (adjective 2nd-order features mapping to noun 1st-order features) is clearly explained with concrete path examples, and the path-reduction operation is formalized.
- Attention to parameterization. The exploration of PPMI vs counts, α-smoothing, and post-composition PPMI (CF vs CS) shows awareness of recent findings (e.g., Levy et al.) and how they translate to this task; the discussion of why shifted PMI and aggressive subsampling hurt low-frequency compounds is plausible and practically useful.

Weaknesses
- Methodological confounds and baseline unfairness. You retokenize the 90 target compounds in ukWaC and train all representations on this altered corpus. This affects both observed phrasal vectors and the contexts of their constituents, and is not how the Reddy baseline was originally computed. Without a strong count-based, untyped window baseline trained on the same retokenized corpus (and a replication of the Reddy setup on your processed corpus), it is hard to isolate the gains due to typed dependencies vs corpus preprocessing. Likewise, the cbow/skip-gram baselines are trained on the retokenized corpus (giving them access to explicit phrase tokens), but you only test additive composition; stronger neural baselines (e.g., using dependency-based embeddings or phrase2vec variants, or at least expanded hyperparameter sweeps) are missing.
- Statistical rigor on a tiny dataset. With only 90 items, claiming that Δρ>0.005 is significant based on “repeated 3-fold CV” and very small “errors ≤ 0.0015” is unconvincing. Repetition across random splits does not substitute for item-level uncertainty. Please provide proper paired significance tests across items (e.g., randomization/permutation tests or Williams/Steiger tests for dependent correlations) between competing systems. Also report CIs over items. As-is, several reported differences (e.g., 0.78 vs 0.79) may not be reliable.
- Limited novelty relative to prior APT work. The paper references a prior “reference suppressed” source for the APT method. Here, the main advance appears to be applying APTs to the compositionality detection task and comparing alignment strategies. While valuable, the conceptual novelty is modest; more extensive analysis (e.g., constituent-level literality prediction, broader phrase types, or theoretical guarantees) would help.
- Missing or unclear experimental details. Important implementation choices are not specified: vocabulary thresholds and feature pruning; whether stopwords/functions were filtered; exact handling of 0th-order features; whether you cap path length; how frequently compounds occur after retokenization; whether δ (the dependency used for alignment) is estimated using the full corpus including test items (data leakage across CV); how you handle compounds with multiple observed relations; and how you restrict evaluation to 1st-order features for similarity while composing with higher-order features. Table 3 uses CF/CS without defining them in the caption; Table 4 is referenced but not shown; hyperparameter grids and optimal values for h and q per model are not reported.
- Presentation and reference quality. The paper contains numerous typos and formatting issues (“siginificantly,” “zero’th,” inconsistent hyphenation “cooccurrence/co-occurrence,” stray LaTeX artifacts, missing spaces), and many references are malformed/incomplete (e.g., “?, \Q2010\E,” missing years/pages, inconsistent venue details). This hinders reproducibility and polish expected at CoNLL.

Questions to authors
- Retokenization: Please clarify precisely how and when compounds are merged. Is δ (the relation used for alignment) chosen using the entire corpus (including test folds)? Do you remove all constituent-level contexts that originate from occurrences of the compound in the original text to avoid leakage?
- Baselines: Can you report an untyped, count-based window baseline (w2v-like but count/PPMI) trained on your retokenized corpus with weighted addition composition, and a replication of Reddy’s method on your processed corpus? Also try dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg 2014) as a stronger neural baseline.
- Significance: Please provide proper per-item paired significance tests between top systems (hybrid vs unaligned APT; hybrid APT vs cbow), with p-values and confidence intervals.
- Ablations: What is the effect of removing higher-order paths (1st-order only), using typed vs untyped dependencies within APTs, and excluding the unaligned component from the hybrid? How sensitive are results to feature pruning and PMI clipping?
- Scope: Can you report constituent-level literality correlations (the dataset includes these) and extend to other construction types (e.g., adj–noun) to support generality claims?

Additional comments
- Please fix table/caption inconsistencies (define CF/CS; include Table 4), unify notation, and clean the bibliography. A small qualitative error analysis (e.g., top gains/losses by frequency or POS patterns) would add insight.
