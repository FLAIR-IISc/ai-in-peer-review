Summary and main contributions
- The paper investigates compositionality detection for noun-noun compounds by comparing typed dependency-based, higher-order, compositional count models (Anchored Packed Trees; APTs) against naive additive composition over neural embeddings. It evaluates on Reddy et al.’s 90-compound dataset by correlating similarity between composed and observed (holistic) phrase vectors with human compositionality judgments.
- It introduces a hybrid APT composition that combines aligned (path-offset) and unaligned typed dependency features, and shows this hybrid yields the best performance (ρ up to 0.79), surpassing both prior count-based state of the art (0.714) and tuned CBOW embeddings (0.74).
- The paper provides an analysis of design choices: typed vs untyped contexts, aligned vs unaligned composition for compounds, order of dependency paths, and weighting schemes (PPMI with/without context smoothing, union vs intersection operations), and reports that context-distribution smoothing (α=0.75) offers small gains while subsampling in embeddings can hurt in this low-frequency regime.

Strengths
- Clear, principled use of typed, higher-order dependency paths: The APT framework encodes complete paths and uses offsetting/reduction to align spaces for composition. This leverages syntax in a way that is well-motivated for compositionality detection and addresses known limitations of untyped proximity models. The empirical gains over untyped baselines and embeddings support this.
- Thoughtful evaluation design to reduce leakage: The corpus is preprocessed to collapse compounds into single tokens and to prevent constituent elementary representations from inheriting phrasal contexts. This strengthens the validity of using similarity between composed and observed vectors as a proxy for compositionality.
- Thorough ablations within the APT family: The paper contrasts aligned, unaligned, and hybrid composition; union (addition) vs intersection (min); PPMI α choices; and where to apply PPMI (compose counts then weight vs composing PPMI). The result that unaligned typed features are surprisingly strong for NN compounds, and hybridization further helps, is insightful.
- Competitive and well-tuned embedding baseline: The CBOW baseline is tuned across dimensions and subsampling thresholds and achieves ρ=0.74, exceeding prior untyped count-based results, lending credibility to the conclusion that typed dependency features still confer an advantage.
- Practical insights on smoothing/subsampling: The analysis that shifted PMI and aggressive subsampling can harm low-frequency compound representations is useful for practitioners working on compositionality of sparse phrases.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset and potential frequency confound: All conclusions are drawn from a single, small dataset (90 compounds). The frequency baseline correlates strongly (ρ=0.63) with human judgments, and it is unclear how much of the gains are due to better modeling vs residual correlation with frequency and representation quality. Stratified analyses by frequency, or partial correlation controlling for frequency, are missing.
- Missing strong baselines with typed contexts: The paper does not compare against dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg), or other typed/distributional models (e.g., lexical function, tensor/matrix-based models) trained on the same corpus. This limits claims that syntactic typing per se (rather than count-vs-predict distinction) is the driver of improvements.
- Incomplete methodological details and potential ad hoc weighting: The “CF” setting computes PPMI after composition. It is unclear how pseudo-counts from union/intersection are normalized and how global marginals are obtained for PMI. Without a precise definition, this step may be ad hoc and difficult to reproduce. More generally, important hyperparameters (vocabulary cutoffs, feature pruning, number of repetitions in CV, significance test) are missing.
- Parsing quality and robustness: The approach depends heavily on dependency parses from Malt on web text; parsing errors are acknowledged (e.g., misparsed three-noun compounds), but there is no robustness check (alternative parsers, gold subsets, or quantifying error impact). Given the centrality of typed paths, this is a material risk.
- Presentation gaps: The paper refers to Table 4 for neural embedding results, but it is not included in the provided text. Some notation and definitions (e.g., reduction constraints τ ∈ R*R*, tree-interpretable types) are introduced tersely; the “reference suppressed” APT details are needed for a self-contained exposition. These hinder clarity and reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- CF setting: How exactly is PMI computed “after composition”? What are the counts for the composed phrase, and how are denominators (#⟨∗,τ,∗⟩, #⟨∗,τ,w′⟩) estimated? Are negative PMI values clipped to PPMI at that stage?
- How many repetitions of 3-fold CV were run, and what statistical test supports the “differences > 0.005 are significant at 95%” claim? Is the reported variability across CV repetitions or due to bootstrap over items?
- Can you report partial correlations controlling for compound frequency, or results stratified by frequency bins, to quantify the frequency confound?
- Could you add a dependency-based embedding baseline (typed contexts) trained on the same corpus (e.g., SGNS with dependency contexts) and a lexical function model on dependency vectors? This would directly test whether typing vs model family is the key factor.
- For neural baselines, were the constituents’ embeddings trained on the preprocessed corpus where compounds are merged into single tokens (thus removing co-occurrences between constituents within target compounds)? Please clarify to ensure fairness with APT preprocessing.
- Constituent-level literality: Do APT compositions predict constituent-specific literality (first vs second modifier) better than embeddings? Reporting ρ for constituent-level scores (as in Reddy et al.) would strengthen claims about modeling structure.
- Scalability: What is the memory/time overhead of APTs at order 2 (80k–230k dims per noun) and alignment at inference time? Any feature pruning thresholds used?

Overall assessment
This paper presents a well-motivated and carefully analyzed application of typed, higher-order dependency features for compositionality detection, with a novel and effective hybrid composition yielding strong improvements on a standard dataset. The main limitations are the narrow evaluation scope, missing strong typed baselines, and some reproducibility gaps around weighting and statistical testing. If the authors can address these concerns—especially by adding a dependency-based embedding baseline, clarifying CF PMI computation and significance testing, and providing analyses controlling for frequency—I would view this as a solid contribution to compositional distributional semantics and compositionality detection.
