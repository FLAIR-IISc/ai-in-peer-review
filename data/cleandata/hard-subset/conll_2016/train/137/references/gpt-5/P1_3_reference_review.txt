Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies compositionality detection for English noun–noun compounds using distributional methods. It frames the task as correlating the similarity between composed and observed (holistic) compound representations with human judgments (Reddy et al., 2011). The main technical vehicle is Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), a typed, dependency-path-based distributional representation with a composition mechanism that aligns feature spaces via path offsetting and reduction. The paper compares: (i) aligned APT composition; (ii) unaligned APT composition (typed features without alignment); (iii) a hybrid linear combination of aligned and unaligned; and (iv) word2vec baselines with additive composition. On ukWaC, the hybrid APT approach achieves ρ≈0.79 vs 0.71 in Reddy et al. (untyped window-based), and cbow baselines reach ρ≈0.74.

Contributions as I see them (ordered by strength)
1) Empirical result: Typed, dependency-path-based features (even without alignment) substantially improve compositionality detection over strong untyped window-based baselines, with a small further gain from hybridizing aligned and unaligned APTs. This is a clear, replicable takeaway for the task.

2) Methodological evaluation: A careful, controlled comparison of alignment vs no-alignment in typed spaces, composition operators (add vs min), and weighting schemes (PPMI with/without smoothing), revealing that unaligned typed features carry most of the signal for noun compounds (predominantly NMOD), while the aligned signal is complementary.

3) Practical insights: Subsampling/shifted PMI degrades performance on low-frequency compounds; modifiers and unaligned typed features are slightly more informative than heads and aligned features (optimal weights in [0.3, 0.5]).

Strengths
- Strong performance on a standard benchmark: The APT-based models deliver new state-of-the-art correlations on the Reddy et al. dataset, with consistent outperformance over both the original count-based baseline and well-tuned word2vec (trained on the same corpus). The improvements are not marginal (0.79 vs 0.71/0.74).

- Syntactically interpretable features: The use of typed, higher-order dependency paths provides a linguistically principled representation, aligning with the task goal (detecting non-compositionality) and offering interpretability that window-based models lack.

- Thoughtful experimental design: The authors pre-process the corpus to avoid trivial overlap between constituent and compound contexts, limit evaluation to first-order features for fair comparison, and explore both PPMI smoothing (α) and composition operators. The analysis of aligned vs unaligned spaces is insightful and grounded in the syntactic distribution of NMOD.

- Clear empirical findings beyond headline numbers: The paper reports that hybridizing aligned and unaligned improves over either alone, that additive composition outperforms min, and that subsampling/shifted PMI hurts—useful guidance for practitioners.

- Relevance: Compositionality detection is a meaningful way to evaluate compositional models without conflating non-compositionality with semantic similarity. The study addresses a recognized gap in evaluation.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of the method: APTs and the alignment mechanism were introduced elsewhere (reference suppressed), and here the main new element is applying them to noun compound compositionality and proposing a simple hybrid of aligned/unaligned. The best-performing model is largely an unaligned typed dependency model with additive composition; the incremental gain from the APT alignment is small.

- Small dataset and risk of overfitting: The Reddy dataset has only 90 compounds. Although repeated 3-fold CV is used, many design choices and hyperparameters were explored (PPMI α, composition operator, alignment mode, hybrid weight q, head/modifier weight h, inclusion of higher-order features). The claim that differences >0.005 are significant is based on repeated CV variance rather than per-item paired tests; selection bias is a concern on such a small dataset. No per-item analysis or learning curves are provided.

- Baseline coverage: The comparison omits competitive typed baselines, notably dependency-based skip-gram embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg, 2014) and strong count-based typed dependency spaces with naive composition beyond the specific APT instantiation. Given the main gains stem from typed features, a dependency-embedding baseline is essential to isolate the benefit of APT alignment.

- Incomplete reproducibility and clarity: Key implementation details are missing or referenced elsewhere (alignment/reduction formalism, feature pruning thresholds, exact higher-order path limits, vocabulary cutoffs). Word2vec settings omit min-count, epochs, negative samples, and whether the same preprocessed corpus with collapsed compounds was used (it is implied but should be explicit). The notation in Section 3 is dense, and the “reference suppressed” limits self-containedness.

- Corpus preprocessing choices may bias results: Selecting the most frequent dependency relation per compound and ignoring modifier-side contexts are reasonable but potentially impactful assumptions. The impact of parsing noise (noted 24% of contiguous occurrences lacking a head–modifier relation) is not quantified. It is unclear how results change if only clear NMOD cases are evaluated.

Questions to authors
- Please include a dependency-based embedding baseline (dependency skip-gram à la Levy & Goldberg, or similar typed embeddings) trained on the same preprocessed corpus, with additive composition. This would directly test whether APT-specific alignment yields gains beyond “typed-ness.”

- Can you report per-compound errors and stratify performance by compositionality level (e.g., highly non-compositional vs compositional) to show where APTs help most?

- How sensitive are results to restricting evaluation to compounds with clear NMOD parses? What happens if you exclude cases where the modifier is adjectival vs nominal?

- What are the selected values (and variability) of h and q across folds/models? Is a single global setting nearly optimal, suggesting a simpler recipe?

- Did you cap higher-order paths at order 2? Have you tried 3+ in elementary APTs, and if so, what was the effect?

- Will you release code and preprocessing recipes to ensure reproducibility?

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The empirical gains on a canonical dataset and the clear, linguistically grounded finding that typed dependency features are crucial for compositionality detection are valuable. However, novelty over prior APT work is limited, and stronger typed baselines and broader analysis would strengthen the case. If space permits, adding a dependency-embedding baseline and a brief error/sensitivity analysis would meaningfully improve the paper.
