Summary and contributions
This paper studies detection of non-compositionality in noun–noun compounds by leveraging typed, higher-order dependency features and a compositional framework based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs). The key idea is to build elementary typed dependency representations and to compose them either after aligning modifier and head spaces via dependency-path offsets or without alignment; a hybrid of both proves best. The approach is evaluated on the Reddy et al. (2011) 90-compound dataset by correlating cosine similarity between composed and observed compound vectors with human compositionality judgments. The paper also compares against neural word embeddings (CBOW/skip-gram) with tuned parameters.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding that typed, higher-order dependency features within an APT compositional framework yield state-of-the-art correlation on compound compositionality (ρ≈0.79), outperforming tuned neural baselines (CBOW ρ≈0.74) and prior work with untyped contexts (ρ≈0.71).
2) A hybrid composition strategy that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APTs outperforms either alone; analysis suggests aligned and unaligned features capture complementary information for compound meaning.
3) Systematic analysis of factors affecting performance: composition operator (add vs intersect), PPMI smoothing (α=0.75), and constituent weighting (h, q), with interpretable observations (e.g., modifiers slightly more informative than heads for this task).

Strengths
- Clear motivation around the confound between phrase similarity and compositionality. The paper targets compositionality directly, using an established dataset with human literality judgments, and demonstrates that typed syntactic information is informative for this task.
- Strong empirical improvements over credible baselines. The hybrid APT model achieves ρ≈0.79, a sizable and statistically significant gain over Reddy et al. (0.71) and over tuned CBOW (0.74). The frequency baseline is relatively strong (0.63), and all proposed methods surpass it by a wide margin, supporting the claim that the models learn beyond frequency effects.
- Careful comparison of modeling choices. The paper examines aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid composition, additive vs intersective operators, PPMI smoothing vs normalized counts, and the effect of constituent weighting. The finding that unaligned and aligned APTs add complementary value is interesting and actionable.
- Methodological care to reduce leakage. The preprocessing ensures that constituents’ elementary APTs do not inherit the compound’s contexts, making the composition task non-trivial. Restricting similarity computation to first-order features on the observed compound also avoids over-crediting high-order artifacts.
- Interpretable and linguistically grounded representations. By retaining dependency types and paths, the model provides a plausible account of why composition should respect syntactic structure; the offset/reduction mechanism is clearly explained and illustrated.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and dataset size. Results are reported only on the 90-item Reddy dataset; while standard, it is small and prone to variance. The claim that repeated 3-fold CV yields tiny errors (≤0.0015) and that >0.005 differences are significant needs more rigorous statistical backing (e.g., permutation tests, confidence intervals over splits), especially given the small N and correlated folds.
- Incomplete baselines. The neural baselines are window-based CBOW/SG, but dependency-based embeddings (e.g., dependency SGNS), GloVe, or retrofitted/syntactically informed embeddings could be stronger and more directly comparable to typed APTs. Prior compositional models tailored to compounds (e.g., lexical function models, Hermann et al. 2012 generative model with larger corpora) are discussed but not reproduced here.
- Novelty relative to prior APT/higher-order dependency work needs clarification. APTs and higher-order typed features are referenced as “reference suppressed” and to Weeds et al. (2014). It is unclear what is genuinely new beyond applying APTs to the Reddy task and proposing the hybrid combination. The fact that aligned APT composition underperforms unaligned in this setting raises questions about when alignment helps; deeper analysis would strengthen the methodological contribution.
- Potential sensitivity to preprocessing choices. Compounds are collapsed to single tokens using the most frequent dependency relation; many compounds have mixed POS tags for modifiers (N/ADJ). The impact of these choices on both observed vectors and the composition task is not quantified. Parser errors in multi-noun sequences are acknowledged but not analyzed for their effect.
- Reproducibility and detail gaps. Important implementation aspects are under-specified: the exact feature pruning, vocabulary thresholds, handling of zero/rare features, number of CV repetitions, and full parameter grids for h and q. No code or resources are provided.

Questions to authors
- Novelty: What is novel about the APT method or its use here beyond Weeds et al. (2014) and your prior “reference suppressed” work? Is the hybrid aligned/unaligned combination new? Please state explicitly.
- Statistical testing: How many randomized CV repetitions were performed? How were standard errors computed, given overlapping folds? Could you provide confidence intervals or nonparametric tests (e.g., bootstrap/permutation) to substantiate significance claims?
- Preprocessing: For compounds with multiple observed relations or modifier POS, how often did the majority relation/POS differ from the gold? Can you report performance stratified by relation (NMOD vs others) and by modifier POS?
- Baselines: Why not include dependency-based SGNS (Levy & Goldberg) or GloVe? Given your findings, do dependency-based embeddings narrow the gap to APTs?
- Alignment: Can you analyze why alignment hurts for noun–noun compounds? For example, what proportion of recovered features in each variant are head-verb arguments vs nominal modifiers, and how does that relate to compositionality scores?
- Robustness: How sensitive are results to limiting or removing occurrences of the target compound from the corpus (e.g., low-frequency or zero-shot settings)? Does APT retain its advantage when observed compound vectors are scarce or noisy?

Additional comments
- A qualitative error analysis (e.g., success/failure examples across idiomatic vs literal compounds) would add insight.
- Consider releasing code and preprocessed resources to facilitate reproducibility.
- Extending evaluation to other MWE/compositionality datasets (e.g., verb–object, phrasal verbs) would strengthen generality claims.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The empirical improvements and careful analysis of typed dependency composition are valuable, but broader evaluation and stronger baselines would solidify the contribution.
