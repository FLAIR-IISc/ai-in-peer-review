Summary and contributions
- The paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), a typed, higher-order dependency-based distributional representation with an alignment (“offsetting”) mechanism for composition. It evaluates aligned, unaligned, and hybrid (linear mix) composition and compares against count-based PPMI variants and neural word embeddings.
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) Empirical result: A hybrid APT composition method achieves state-of-the-art correlation on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (ρ≈0.79), outperforming prior weighted-addition baselines on untyped vectors (ρ≈0.71) and optimized CBOW embeddings (ρ≈0.74).
  2) Analysis of context typing and alignment: Evidence that typed dependency features and combining aligned and unaligned views provide complementary signals for compositionality detection.
  3) Methodological care for leakage: Corpus preprocessing that collapses compounds and prevents direct sharing of compound contexts with constituent vectors, aiming for a fairer test of composition.

Strengths
- Empirical improvements over strong baselines: The APT hybrid model consistently outperforms both the Reddy weighted-add baseline and well-tuned CBOW/SG embeddings. The comparison is careful (cross-validation, parameter exploration, significance discussion). This supports the claim that richer, typed contexts help for compositionality detection.
- Clear formalization and motivation: The explanation of typed higher-order features, path inversion/reduction, and alignment by dependency paths is precise, with concrete examples and equations. This makes the compositional mechanism understandable and justifies why aligned modifier features should correspond to head features.
- Insightful analysis of design choices: The paper examines PPMI smoothing (α), intersects vs union-like composition, and shows why subsampling (shifted PMI analog) can hurt on low-frequency compounds. The finding that unaligned and aligned features capture different useful contexts (hybrid best) is informative.
- Attention to leakage and evaluation correctness: Removing compound-derived contexts from constituent representations and collapsing compounds in dependency graphs increases the credibility of the evaluation; otherwise intersective methods would trivially match observed vectors.
- Practical relevance: Detecting non-compositionality is important for composition, MWE handling, and downstream tasks; showing that syntax-aware contexts help is useful for the community.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: APTs have been introduced previously by the authors; the primary novelty here is applying them to compositionality detection and contrasting aligned/unaligned/hybrid variants. While the empirical findings are valuable, the methodological advance is incremental relative to prior APT work.
- Narrow evaluation scope: Results are only on the small Reddy dataset (90 compounds), with high frequency–compositionality correlation. This raises concerns about robustness and overfitting, especially with multiple tuned hyperparameters (h and q) via CV. No tests on other MWE types, larger datasets, or languages; limited error analysis.
- Missing stronger baselines: The paper does not compare against dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg dep2vec) or strong count-based dependency spaces with simple additive/multiplicative composition, nor against lexical-function/tensor models specifically tuned for noun compounds on the same task. This weakens the claim that APT’s alignment (rather than typed contexts per se) is responsible for the gains.
- Methodological clarity gaps: Although the approach emphasizes higher-order features, similarity is computed on 1st-order features only; the justification (composed higher-order unreliability) is plausible but would benefit from empirical ablations. The decision to ignore modifier-internal dependency paths and to select the majority dependency label during collapsing could bias results; the impact is not quantified.
- Reproducibility details: Several implementation choices are under-specified (exact APT construction settings, pruning, dimensionalities per POS, training epochs/min-counts for word2vec, seeds). References are partially “suppressed,” and no code is provided. This reduces replicability.

Questions to authors
- Can you add comparisons to dependency-based embeddings (e.g., dep2vec) and to count-based dependency vectors with weighted addition? This would disentangle the benefit of typed contexts from the specific APT alignment mechanism.
- How sensitive are results to the compound-collapsing heuristics (choosing the most frequent dependency label; ignoring modifier-internal dependencies)? Please report performance under alternative choices or without collapsing to assess robustness.
- Please provide ablations on h and q without tuning (e.g., h=0.5, q in {0,1}) and report how much hybridization adds beyond the best single view. Do gains persist with coarser tuning?
- Could you include per-compound or category-level analysis (e.g., idiomatic vs semi-compositional; frequent vs rare) and where APT helps or hurts relative to CBOW?
- Can you evaluate on additional datasets (e.g., other compound noun compositionality sets, phrasal verbs, adjective–noun) to demonstrate generality?

Assessment against reviewing criteria
- Originality: Moderate. Main idea (APTs) exists; contribution is application and empirical insights on compositionality detection and context typing.
- Quality/soundness: Generally sound; significance testing and careful preprocessing; but broader baselines, ablations, and datasets would strengthen claims.
- Clarity: Good overall; formalism and examples are clear, though some implementation details and reference issues remain.
- Significance: Promising but limited by narrow evaluation; results suggest typed contexts and alignment matter for compositionality.
- Resources/reproducibility: Medium; more detail and code/data scripts would help.

Overall: I lean toward weak accept if space permits, due to solid empirical gains and clear methodological exposition, but encourage broader baselines and evaluations to solidify the contribution.
