Summary
The paper studies detection of non-compositionality in noun–noun compounds by comparing composed phrase representations with observed (holistic) representations. It introduces a compositional framework based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) that use typed, higher-order dependency-path features and composition via alignment (offsetting) and reduction of paths. On the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds, the authors show that APT-based composition (especially a hybrid of aligned and unaligned features) correlates more strongly with human compositionality judgments than both the classic weighted-addition baseline on untyped co-occurrence vectors and neural word embeddings trained with word2vec.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical result: Strong improvements on compound compositionality detection, achieving Spearman ρ up to 0.79 (hybrid APT), outperforming the prior 0.71 baseline and neural embeddings (best cbow 0.74). The paper carefully explores composition operators, weighting, and PPMI smoothing.
- Methodological insight: Demonstrates the utility of typed, higher-order dependency-path features for composition, and shows that combining aligned (offset) and unaligned spaces yields complementary evidence for noun–noun compounds.
- Experimental analysis: Provides a controlled comparison between dependency-typed models and untyped neural embeddings on the same corpus (ukWaC), analyzes effects of PMI smoothing and subsampling, and reports parameter trends (e.g., modifier slightly more informative than head).

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains over strong baselines. The best APT configuration substantially improves correlation (0.79) over the previously reported 0.71 and over tuned cbow (0.74). The authors use repeated 3-fold cross-validation and note very small standard errors, strengthening the claim that the differences are reliable.
- Well-motivated use of dependency-typed, higher-order contexts. The paper’s central hypothesis—that richer, typed dependency contexts better support composition and thus compositionality detection—is supported by consistent gains over untyped representations. The hybrid result suggests different evidence streams are complementary.
- Solid experimental setup and transparency. The paper carefully describes corpus preprocessing (merging compound tokens, preserving head contexts, avoiding leakage into constituent vectors), feature construction (PPMI with and without smoothing), and composition operators with explicit equations, aiding reproducibility.
- Thoughtful analysis of design choices. The observed degradation with shifted PMI and with aggressive subsampling (analogy to shifted PMI) is well reasoned, especially given low-frequency compounds. The finding that modifier weight h and hybrid weight q lie around 0.3–0.5 is informative for future work.
- Relevance and significance. Compositionality detection is a recognized challenge; showing that composition models can diagnose non-compositionality, and that syntactic typing matters, is of interest to the CoNLL community.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty of the core model for this paper’s scope. APTs and the alignment/offsetting mechanism are not introduced here (reference suppressed), and for noun–noun compounds specifically the aligned composition is not the main driver of gains (unaligned and hybrid outperform aligned). Thus, the conceptual contribution for this task is primarily the application and empirical study rather than a new method.
- Small and single-dataset evaluation. Results are reported only on the 90-item Reddy dataset. This raises concerns about robustness and generalizability (e.g., to other compound datasets, phrasal verbs, or larger, more diverse test suites). Hyperparameter tuning with repeated 3-fold CV on such a small set risks overfitting even if standard errors are small.
- Missing strong baselines with typed contexts. The neural baselines use window-based cbow/skip-gram. A more direct comparison would include dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014) or other typed-count models (e.g., dependency-based PPMI with first-/second-order paths but without APT alignment). This would help isolate the benefit of the APT machinery versus simply using typed contexts.
- Statistical testing and ablation granularity. While the paper claims small errors and significance for >0.005 differences, detailed significance testing across models (e.g., bootstrap over items or paired tests for correlated correlations) is not reported. Ablations for the impact of higher-order paths, alignment alone, and the hybrid mixture weight are summarized but not deeply dissected (e.g., performance with only first-order typed features vs including second-order).
- Potential frequency confound remains underexplored. Frequency correlates strongly with human ratings (ρ=0.63). It would strengthen claims to report partial correlations controlling for log frequency, or to analyze whether APT gains persist uniformly across frequency bins.

Questions to authors
- Can you report results with dependency-based embeddings (e.g., dep2vec) and/or typed count-based baselines without APT alignment to better isolate the effect of APT-specific alignment/reduction?
- How much do higher-order paths contribute beyond first-order typed features? Please provide an ablation with only first-order, and then adding second-order paths.
- Can you report partial correlations controlling for compound frequency (or provide per-bin results) to rule out frequency as the main driver of gains?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of dependency parser and tagging accuracy? Given the reliance on typed paths, have you assessed robustness to parsing noise?
- What are the exact best h and q values per model (means and variance across folds), and do they systematically vary with constituent POS (e.g., NN-NN vs JJ-NN compounds)?

Overall evaluation
This submission offers a well-executed empirical study showing that typed, higher-order dependency features and APT-based composition improve compound compositionality detection over strong baselines. While the methodological novelty is limited and the evaluation scope is narrow, the results are solid and relevant. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on adding stronger typed baselines and more detailed ablations/analyses as feasible.
