Summary
This paper studies detection of non-compositionality in noun–noun compounds by comparing composed phrase representations against observed holistic representations. The core modeling choice is to use typed, higher-order dependency features encoded as Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), with careful alignment of feature spaces before composition. The authors evaluate several APT variants (aligned, unaligned, hybrid; intersection vs union; PMI smoothing) and compare them to neural baselines (word2vec CBOW/SG). On the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds, the best APT configuration (hybrid, weighted-add) attains Spearman ρ≈0.79, exceeding both the Reddy baseline (0.71) and the best CBOW setting (0.74). They also report that unaligned typed dependency features and a hybrid of aligned/unaligned features are beneficial, and that PMI smoothing helps slightly.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: Typed higher-order dependency features (APT) substantially outperform untyped window-based neural embeddings for compound compositionality detection on the Reddy et al. benchmark, reaching ρ≈0.79 (hybrid APT) vs 0.74 (best CBOW) and 0.71 (previous best).
2) Methodological insight: A hybrid composition strategy that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APTs offers consistent gains over either alone, suggesting different, complementary signals about phrasal context are captured.
3) Careful experimental protocol: Preprocessing to collapse target compounds into single tokens and prevent leakage from compound contexts into constituent representations; exploration of composition operators, weighting, and PMI smoothing.

Strengths
- Clear, focused problem and evaluation: The paper evaluates compositionality directly, avoiding confounds present in phrase similarity datasets. The choice of Reddy et al.’s dataset aligns well with the question under study.
- Solid empirical improvements and thorough comparisons: Multiple APT configurations, careful parameter exploration (h and q), and reasonable neural baselines are provided. The reported gains over prior work and over frequency baselines are meaningful, and the authors discuss statistical significance via repeated cross-validation.
- Technical clarity of the APT framework: The presentation of typed, higher-order dependency paths, space alignment via path offsetting and reduction, and the rationale for intersective vs additive composition is generally well-motivated and informative.
- Attention to data leakage and preprocessing: Collapsing compounds into tokens and excluding modifier-internal modifications reduces trivial overlap, strengthening the validity of the comparison between composed and observed representations.
- Useful analysis: Observations that modifiers contribute slightly more than heads (optimal h, q in 0.3–0.5), and that PMI smoothing helps while shifted PMI hurts in this low-frequency setting, are practically valuable.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior APT work: Much of the technical machinery (typed higher-order dependency features and alignment) appears to derive from prior APT papers (citations “reference suppressed”; Weeds et al., 2014). The primary new contribution is an application and ablation in a small-scale compositionality task. Clarifying what is methodologically new here would strengthen the paper.
- Small, single-dataset evaluation: Results are reported only on 90 compounds. Even with repeated CV, small absolute differences (e.g., 0.01–0.03 ρ) risk over-interpretation, and generalization to other phrase types (e.g., verb–object, PP) remains untested despite claims about broader implications.
- Missing stronger baselines: Comparisons are limited to window-based CBOW/SG. Dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg, 2014), GloVe, or learned compositional models (e.g., lexical function) on this dataset would provide a fairer test of whether typed contexts or the APT alignment per se drive the gains.
- Potential confounds with frequency and representation quality: Frequent compounds yield better holistic vectors and also tend to be more compositional (as the strong FREQ baseline shows). While all methods beat FREQ, it would be useful to control for frequency (e.g., partial correlations or frequency-matched analyses) and report per-frequency-band performance.
- Reporting and clarity issues: Statistical significance is discussed informally (differences >0.005 deemed significant) without confidence intervals; multiple comparisons are not addressed. Some implementation details are missing (feature pruning thresholds, handling of low counts, robustness to parser errors). References are partially incomplete/garbled, and some notation (e.g., zero’th-order) and footnotes are hard to follow.

Questions to Authors
- What is the precise novelty over prior APT papers? Is the hybrid aligned/unaligned composition, the use on compound nouns, or the specific parameterization new?
- Can you report results with dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) as a stronger neural baseline? If not, please justify.
- How sensitive are the results to parser errors in ukWaC? Did you filter noisy parses or multi-noun compounds systematically?
- Can you provide partial correlations controlling for compound frequency, or stratify results by frequency bins?
- Could you include a qualitative error analysis (a few compounds where aligned helps vs hurts; where neural does better than APT)?
- Are code and preprocessed resources (compound-collapsed ukWaC indices; chosen δ per compound) planned for release?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider evaluating on additional datasets (e.g., phrasal verb compositionality; adjective–noun) to support claims of generality.
- Report confidence intervals/standard errors for ρ, and detail the number of CV repetitions; consider a paired test across folds for key comparisons.
- Clarify feature pruning, minimum counts, and whether PMI values were clipped; these choices affect stability on sparse compounds.
- Fix references and formatting issues; ensure double-blind compliance while making prior APT connections explicit after review.

Overall, the paper offers meaningful empirical evidence that typed dependency paths and APT-style composition help for compound compositionality detection, with a clear and well-motivated experimental design. Strengthening the baselines, clarifying novelty, and broadening evaluation would make it more compelling.
