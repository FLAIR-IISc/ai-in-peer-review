Summary and contributions
This paper studies detection of non-compositionality in noun compounds by comparing composed representations to observed (holistic) phrase representations. It builds on an Anchored Packed Trees (APT) framework that encodes complete, typed dependency paths and supports composition via alignment (offsetting) and reduction of paths. Using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds, it compares: (i) aligned APT composition, (ii) unaligned typed-dependency composition, (iii) a hybrid combination, and (iv) neural baselines (word2vec with additive composition). The main empirical finding is that typed, higher-order dependency features—especially the hybrid APT method—yield higher correlation with human compositionality judgments than prior work and than neural baselines.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical result: Typed dependency-based composition (APT) significantly improves correlation with human judgments on compound-noun compositionality (best ρ=0.79) over prior untyped baselines (Reddy et al. 2011: 0.71) and over additive word2vec baselines (best cbow ~0.74).
- Methodological analysis: Careful comparison of aligned vs unaligned composition in typed dependency spaces, showing a hybrid linear combination outperforms either alone, and that smoothing in PMI (α=0.75) helps modestly while PMI shifting hurts in this low-frequency setting.
- Practical pipeline for fair evaluation: Preprocessing to collapse target compounds, avoiding feature leakage, and restricting similarity to first-order dependency features to mitigate higher-order noise.

Strengths
- Clear empirical improvement on a standard benchmark. The hybrid APT method achieves ρ≈0.79, a sizable gain over the widely cited Reddy et al. baseline (0.71) and over tuned word2vec additive baselines (cbow up to 0.74). The paper provides parameter sweeps (PPMI α, composition operator, alignment vs unaligned vs hybrid) that substantiate the main claim.
- Convincing motivation for typed, higher-order contexts. The analysis shows why aligning via dependency paths is needed (spaces differ by POS/role), and why second-order features of modifiers can be predictive of first-order features of compounds. The hybrid result suggests complementary evidence from aligned and unaligned spaces, lending insight beyond a single “best” recipe.
- Thoughtful treatment of sparsity and frequency effects. The authors diagnose why shifted PMI and aggressive subsampling degrade performance for low-frequency compounds, and justify restricting similarity to first-order features. The discussion of modifiers being slightly more informative (optimal h and q ~0.3–0.5) adds interpretable insight.
- Reasonably careful experimental controls. Using the same corpus (ukWaC) and parser pipeline as Reddy et al. reduces confounds. The preprocessing to merge compounds while preventing leakage into constituent vectors demonstrates an awareness of pitfalls that often inflate compositionality scores.
- Relevance and clarity of task framing. The paper argues persuasively that evaluating composition against human phrase similarity is confounded by non-compositionality, and that compositionality prediction is a more direct, principled target for assessing compositional models.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and size. Results hinge on a single small dataset (90 items), which limits statistical confidence and generality. While the authors report tiny errors and claim that differences >0.005 are significant, the methodology for these estimates is under-specified for such small samples (number of repetitions, fold sizes, and the exact variance estimator for Spearman’s ρ). Additional datasets (e.g., adjective–noun, verb–object, other MWE benchmarks) are needed.
- Baselines could be stronger and fairer. The neural comparison uses untyped window-based word2vec with additive composition. Missing are dependency-based embeddings (e.g., dependency word2vec) or typed count-based models without APT alignment, and more recent or structure-aware composition baselines. Since the proposed advantage is typed dependency information, a typed non-APT baseline would clarify how much gain stems from alignment vs simply using typed contexts.
- Method novelty is incremental and somewhat opaque. The core APT mechanism (alignment via offsetting and reduction) is cited as “reference suppressed,” suggesting the composition framework itself is not new; the novelty is the application to compositionality detection and the hybrid variant. Some crucial details (e.g., reduction constraints τ ∈ R*R*, handling zero-th order features, pruning, and feature selection) are described succinctly but may be hard to reproduce without code or a full prior description.
- Preprocessing choices may bias results. The paper collapses compounds and fixes the dependency relation to the most frequent one, ignores modifiers’ own modifiers, and notes substantial parser errors in multi-noun compounds. These choices could affect both observed and composed vectors in ways that advantage certain models (e.g., typed unaligned features). A sensitivity analysis (e.g., varying how compound dependencies are resolved) would increase confidence.
- Reporting and references require cleanup. Several references are malformed (“?,” “reference suppressed”), and some experimental details are missing: exact number of repetitions in cross-validation, hyperparameter ranges for h and q, frequency distribution of compounds, vocabulary/pruning thresholds, and how significance was tested across folds/repetitions.

Questions to authors
- How many repetitions of 3-fold CV were run, and how were confidence intervals or standard errors for Spearman’s ρ computed? Please report variance across repetitions and per-fold sizes.
- Can you include a dependency-based embedding baseline (e.g., Levy & Goldberg dependency word2vec) and a typed count baseline without APT alignment to separate the benefit of typed contexts from APT’s alignment mechanism?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of the dependency relation for each compound (e.g., majority vs alternative relations), and to parser errors? Can you report performance when restricting to high-confidence parses?
- What pruning/feature selection thresholds were used for APT features (by order and type)? Any smoothing applied beyond PMI α?
- Will you release code and the preprocessed compound-merged corpus indices to facilitate reproduction?

Additional comments
- The finding that unaligned typed features outperform aligned ones for noun–noun compounds is interesting; a qualitative analysis of which contexts are captured by each would add insight.
- Please fix the bibliography and clarify the relationship to your anonymized prior APT work.
