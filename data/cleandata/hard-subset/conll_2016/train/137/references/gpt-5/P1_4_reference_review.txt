Summary and contributions
This paper investigates compositionality detection for English noun–noun compounds by comparing composed and observed phrase representations. The main technical focus is on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which encode typed higher-order dependency paths and support composition via feature-space alignment (offsetting and path reduction). The authors compare (i) aligned APT composition, (ii) unaligned typed APT composition, and (iii) a hybrid mixture of aligned and unaligned features, against neural word embeddings (word2vec CBOW/SG) composed by weighted addition. On the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds with human literality scores), the hybrid APT model achieves the best Spearman correlation (ρ≈0.79), improving over Reddy’s weighted-addition baseline (ρ≈0.71) and the best CBOW baseline (ρ≈0.74). They also analyze PMI smoothing, composition operators (add vs intersect/min), and weighting of modifier vs head.

As I see it, the key contributions are:
1) Methodological: A careful evaluation of typed dependency-based compositional representations (APTs), including alignment via higher-order paths and a hybrid aligned/unaligned mixture, for predicting compositionality of noun compounds.
2) Empirical: Strong results on the Reddy benchmark, with consistent gains over both a historical count-based baseline and modern neural CBOW/SG baselines, plus an informative analysis of PMI smoothing and weights.
3) Insight: Evidence that typed dependency signals and higher-order paths meaningfully help in compositionality detection, and that unaligned typed features and the modifier tend to carry slightly more weight.

Strengths
- Clear problem setup with a principled evaluation protocol. Using composed vs observed phrase similarity to predict human compositionality is appropriate for this benchmark, and the authors follow a cross-validated tuning procedure with a range of reasonable design choices.
- Strong empirical performance and careful comparisons. The hybrid APT achieves ρ≈0.79, surpassing prior reported results (Reddy ρ≈0.71) and a well-tuned CBOW baseline (best ρ≈0.74). Reporting frequency and random baselines (FREQ ρ≈0.63; RAND ρ≈0.02) is helpful for context.
- Technically motivated modeling and analysis. The APT alignment via dependency path offsetting and reduction is well motivated; the hybrid model’s gains suggest aligned and unaligned features contribute complementary evidence. The exploration of PMI smoothing (α=0.75) and the effect of subsampling in CBOW is nuanced and tied to data frequency.
- Sensible controls against leakage. By recombining compounds into single tokens and ensuring constituent elementary APTs do not inherit compound contexts, the evaluation focuses on composition rather than memorization from the compound’s own contexts.
- Interpretable findings. The consistent optimal weights h and q in [0.3, 0.5] offer an interpretable takeaway: the modifier and unaligned typed features are slightly more informative for this dataset.

Weaknesses
- Limited dataset and potential overfitting. The Reddy dataset contains only 90 items. Given many modeling and tuning choices (alignment vs unaligned vs hybrid, operator, PPMI vs counts, α values, CF vs CS, h and q), the risk of overfitting via model selection is nontrivial. Although 3-fold CV is used, nested CV or a held-out test would strengthen claims. No correction for multiple comparisons is discussed, and the significance statement (“>0.005 difference is significant”) lacks detail on the statistical test.
- Missing stronger baselines. The comparison excludes dependency-based neural embeddings (e.g., word2vec trained on dependency contexts) or GloVe with typed contexts, which would be more directly comparable to APTs. An ablation comparing typed first-order only vs higher-order paths, and typed vs untyped with matched context definitions, would isolate the sources of gains.
- Evaluation uses only first-order features downstream. Similarity is computed between composed and observed vectors restricted to first-order dependency features, while composed representations leverage higher-order paths. This supports the claim that higher-order features help predict first-order distributions, but it undercuts a direct assessment of whether higher-order features themselves match observed higher-order distributions. Reporting coverage/precision of recovered first-order features or evaluating against observed higher-order features would clarify the mechanism of improvement.
- Reproducibility and implementation details. Important details are missing: vocabulary thresholds, feature pruning, dependency relation inventory, maximum path length actually used per POS, counting and smoothing of low-frequency compounds, the exact number of CV repetitions, and how significance is computed. The computational and memory footprint of APTs (80k–230k dims for nouns) and composition runtime are not reported.
- Preprocessing choices may bias results. Compounds with multiple observed dependency relations are collapsed to the most frequent relation, and paths involving the modifier are dropped. These choices may discard legitimate variation and could favor some composition modes over others; an ablation or error analysis would help.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide nested cross-validation or a held-out test split to mitigate selection bias across the many configurations? Alternatively, report performance after fixing all hyperparameters on a development split and evaluating once on a held-out test.
- How many repetitions of 3-fold CV were run, and what exact statistical test supports the “>0.005 is significant” claim? Please include standard errors or confidence intervals in the tables.
- What is the effect of using dependency-based neural embeddings (e.g., SGNS over dependency contexts) as baselines? Do typed contexts alone (without APT alignment) close the gap?
- Can you report ablations: typed vs untyped contexts; first-order only vs inclusion of higher-order paths; aligned vs unaligned with matched feature spaces; and the effect of including/excluding zero-length (order-0) features?
- How sensitive are results to compound frequency? Please report correlations by frequency bins and partial correlations controlling for frequency, to assess whether gains reflect better handling of rare compounds.
- Please provide implementation details sufficient for reproduction: vocabulary cutoffs, feature pruning, dependency labels used, count thresholds, handling of parsing errors, and the sizes and sparsity of the APT matrices. Is code or data preprocessing available?

Overall evaluation
This is a well-motivated and empirically solid study showing that typed dependency-based composition, especially the hybrid APT approach, improves compositionality detection on a standard benchmark. However, the reliance on a small dataset, limited baselines, and missing ablations and statistical details weaken the strength of the claims. With additional controls (stronger baselines, ablations, clearer statistics, and a more robust evaluation split), this would be a strong accept; as is, it is a borderline accept with promising contributions.
