Summary
The paper investigates how the choice of contextual features affects vector composition for detecting the compositionality of noun–noun compounds. It contrasts typed, higher-order, dependency-path-based features (Anchored Packed Trees, APTs) with untyped proximity-based representations (including word2vec embeddings). The authors evaluate on Reddy et al.’s (2011) 90-compound dataset by correlating the cosine similarity between composed and observed compound vectors with human compositionality judgments. They find that typed dependency features, especially a hybrid that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APT representations, outperform both the original Reddy baseline and neural embeddings trained on the same corpus (ukWaC).

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: Typed, higher-order dependency features substantially improve compositionality detection over untyped co-occurrence and neural embeddings trained on the same corpus (best Spearman ρ = 0.79 vs. 0.71 Reddy and 0.74 word2vec-cbow).
2) Hybrid composition: A simple linear combination of aligned and unaligned APTs (“hybrid”) yields the strongest performance, suggesting the two capture complementary evidence.
3) Analysis of PMI smoothing and composition operators: The paper presents careful comparisons of PPMI vs. smoothed PPMI (α=0.75), intersective vs. additive composition, and weighting of modifier vs. head.

Strengths
- Clear empirical improvements on a standard benchmark. The results convincingly beat the prior best (0.71) and strong neural baselines (cbow up to 0.74) using the same corpus, indicating the gains are due to feature design rather than data scale.
- Careful experimental controls and baselines. The paper uses repeated 3-fold CV, reports frequency and random baselines (showing frequency is a strong confounder at ρ=0.63), compares multiple composition operators (INT vs. UNI), and explores α-smoothing, showing consistent trends (e.g., modest gains from α=0.75).
- Insightful finding about alignment vs. unaligned features. Despite APT’s core idea of space alignment, the unaligned and hybrid variants work best for NN compounds, a useful and honest result that informs when alignment helps. The analysis that NN compounds often share first-order contexts with modifiers is plausible and informative.
- Transparent discussion of design choices to avoid leakage. The authors explicitly prevent shared raw features between constituents and compounds, and argue for ignoring modifier-internal modifiers (e.g., adverbs), which aligns with the target task.
- Practical observations about neural models. The analysis that aggressive subsampling harms low-frequency compounds is useful guidance for practitioners, as is the parameter exploration (dimensions, subsampling, cbow vs. skip-gram).

Weaknesses
- Narrow evaluation scope and small dataset. All results are on a single, small English dataset (90 items), exclusively NN compounds. Without validation on other compositionality benchmarks (e.g., verb–particle verbs, adjective–noun datasets, or larger compound sets), it is hard to assess generality. The central claim (benefit of typed higher-order features and hybrid composition) needs broader evidence.
- Potential confounding by frequency and corpus coupling. While the FREQ baseline is reported, the methods’ gains are still evaluated as correlations against human scores using vectors trained on the same corpus where compound tokens were explicitly inserted. Partial correlation analyses controlling for compound frequency (or regression with frequency covariates) are missing and could alter conclusions. Out-of-domain validation (train on one corpus, test on another) would strengthen claims.
- Limited ablations to isolate what matters. The paper shows aligned vs. unaligned vs. hybrid APTs, but does not ablate: typed vs. untyped dependency features under the same APT pipeline; impact of path length (0/1/2+ orders); effect of including zero-order features; and the specific contribution of “offsetting + reduction” beyond simple path prefixing. Given that unaligned APTs do best for NN compounds, it would help to quantify exactly which typed paths drive the improvement (feature-level analysis or top-context inspection).
- Reporting and reproducibility gaps. The neural baseline table is referenced (Table 4) but not included; critical details for word2vec (negative samples, min_count, epochs, learning rate schedule) are not specified. APT construction details (complete set of relations, pruning/thresholding, vocabulary cutoffs) and code/data availability are not discussed. This limits reproducibility.
- Claims about statistical significance need sharpening. The paper states errors ≤ 0.0015 from repetitions and treats differences > 0.005 as significant at 95%, but does not describe the exact testing procedure (e.g., paired permutation/Bootstrap across items or CV splits). Given multiple comparisons and a 90-item dataset, a clearer protocol is needed.

Questions to authors
- Can you report partial correlations that control for compound frequency (and optionally for constituent frequencies)? Do the main conclusions hold?
- How sensitive are results to the maximum dependency path order? What happens if you restrict to first order only, or allow third order?
- Can you include an ablation comparing typed vs. untyped contexts within the same APT framework (same dimensions, same weighting), to isolate the benefit of typing?
- Why does unaligned outperform aligned for NN compounds? Can you provide qualitative examples showing which contexts are captured by each and how they complement in the hybrid?
- Please provide complete neural hyperparameters (negative sampling rate, min_count, epochs, alpha schedule) and include the missing Table 4.
- Do results replicate on another dataset (e.g., a larger set of English compounds or a verb–particle dataset)? If not yet, could you comment on expected behavior for other constructions where alignment should matter more?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider nested cross-validation for h and q to avoid optimistic bias on such a small dataset, and report variance across random splits.
- Provide an error breakdown by compositionality level (low/medium/high) and by syntactic subtype (true NN vs. cases tagged as adjectival modifiers).
- To mitigate corpus coupling, evaluate with train–test corpus separation or use partial correlation; alternatively, predict human scores via regression with multiple features (composed–observed similarity, frequency, constituent similarities) to quantify unique contribution.
- Release code, preprocessed dependencies, and compound annotations to facilitate replication.
- The exposition of APT offsetting and reduction is generally clear; a small illustrative figure aligning spaces for an NN compound would aid readers.

Overall recommendation: Weak reject. The empirical improvements are promising and the analysis is thoughtful, but the evaluation is too narrow (single small dataset, single language) and some ablations and reporting details are missing. With broader validation, stronger ablations isolating the source of gains, and improved reproducibility, this work would be a strong candidate.
