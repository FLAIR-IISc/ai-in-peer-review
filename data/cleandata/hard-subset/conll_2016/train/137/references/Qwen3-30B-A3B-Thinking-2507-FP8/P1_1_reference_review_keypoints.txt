- A novel APT method using typed dependency paths outperforms prior compositionality detectors (ρ = 0.79 vs 0.71).
- Thorough empirical investigation of composition strategies (aligned vs unaligned APTs, intersection vs union, hybrid).
- Evidence that syntactic structure via typed dependency paths is highly informative for compositionality detection.

- Addresses a gap by evaluating compositionality directly instead of conflating it with phrase similarity.
- APT is theoretically well-motivated and technically sound, with clear alignment, reduction, and composition mechanisms.
- Evaluation is thorough: uses Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark, multiple baselines, cross-validation, and shows robust improvements.
- Provides insights that aligned and unaligned APT features are complementary and that the modifier is slightly more informative than the head.
- Clearly explains limitations of untyped co-occurrence methods and why shifted PMI hurt performance for low-frequency compounds.

- Lacks a detailed ablation analysis to pinpoint which APT components drive the performance gains.
- Evaluation is limited to 90 noun compounds, so generalization to other phrase types is untested.
- Insufficient comparison to more recent tensor-based and neural compositionality methods post-2011.
- The link between compositionality detection and improving phrase similarity is not clearly articulated.
- Does not address scalability or computational costs of very high-dimensional APT feature spaces.

- Please provide examples where aligned features are more informative versus where unaligned features help.
- Which specific dependency paths (e.g., NMOD, AMOD, NSUBJ) are most predictive of compositionality?
- Could corpus-specific characteristics of ukWaC explain the performance gains; how do results change on other corpora?
- Can you clarify the relationship between frequency and compositionality: general linguistic phenomenon or dataset-specific?
- How would detecting non-compositional phrases and smoothing phrasal representations be integrated into pipelines for phrase similarity or other NLP tasks?
