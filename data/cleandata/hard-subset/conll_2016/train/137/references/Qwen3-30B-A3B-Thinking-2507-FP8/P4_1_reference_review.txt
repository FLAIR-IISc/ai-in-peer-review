

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper addresses the problem of compositionality detection in noun compounds (e.g., "red herring," "grad student") by proposing a distributional compositional approach using "Anchored Packed Trees" (APT). The authors argue that evaluating compositional methods against human similarity judgments confounds compositionality with similarity, and instead use Reddy et al.'s (2011) benchmark dataset with human judgments of compositionality. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. A novel compositional framework using typed higher-order dependency paths (APT) with vector space alignment before composition, which significantly improves compositionality detection.
2. Comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating that typed dependency features outperform untyped approaches, with a hybrid composition method (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) achieving state-of-the-art results.
3. Analysis showing that for compositionality detection, the modifier is slightly more informative than the head in determining compositionality, with optimal parameter values consistently in [0.3, 0.5] across models.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Contribution and Clarity**: The paper makes a clear, well-motivated technical contribution with the APT approach. The method is thoroughly explained with concrete examples (Table 1), mathematical formalization (Equation 1), and detailed descriptions of the alignment process. The authors successfully address a gap in the literature by incorporating syntactic structure into distributional composition, which is a significant methodological advance over most existing work that ignores syntactic structure.

2. **Rigorous Experimental Evaluation**: The paper uses a standard benchmark (Reddy et al., 2011) with human judgments of compositionality, and conducts thorough parameter exploration (PPMI vs. normalized counts, α values, composition operations, alignment vs. unaligned). The use of 3-fold cross-validation with statistical significance testing (error bounds ≤ 0.0015) provides strong evidence for the results. The comparison to multiple baselines (RAND, FREQ, REDDY) and different neural embedding configurations (cbow vs. skip-gram) adds to the robustness of the evaluation.

3. **Significant Performance Improvement**: The results (Table 5) demonstrate a clear and statistically significant improvement over previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71). This represents a 11% relative improvement, which is meaningful in the context of this task. The paper successfully shows that incorporating syntactic structure through typed dependency paths substantially enhances compositionality detection.

4. **Important Methodological Insight**: The paper makes a strong case that compositionality detection provides a better evaluation framework for compositional methods than direct phrase similarity evaluation. This insight is valuable for the field, as it addresses a fundamental issue in evaluating compositional distributional models that has been overlooked in previous work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope**: The paper focuses exclusively on noun-noun compounds, which represents a narrow subset of compositionality problems. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, the scope is sufficiently constrained that it's unclear how widely applicable their findings would be to other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun compounds or verb phrases). This limits the generalizability of their contribution.

2. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper compares different composition operations and parameter settings but doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component of the APT approach. It's not clear whether the alignment mechanism, the typed features, or the hybrid composition method is primarily responsible for the performance gain. A more detailed analysis of each component's contribution would strengthen the paper.

3. **Theoretical Justification for Hybrid Approach**: The paper states that the hybrid method (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) performs best, but provides only a vague explanation ("aligned and unaligned APT composition are predicting different contexts"). A more concrete theoretical justification or analysis of specific examples where the hybrid approach succeeds where others fail would significantly strengthen the argument.

4. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper doesn't compare to several recent approaches in compositional distributional semantics that also incorporate syntactic structure, such as Socher et al. (2012) or Grefenstette et al. (2013). While the comparison to Reddy et al. (2011) is appropriate, a more comprehensive comparison to the broader literature would better establish the novelty and significance of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the hybrid composition method (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) works best, but doesn't provide specific examples where this combination captures nuances missed by either approach alone. Could you provide 2-3 specific compound noun examples where the hybrid method clearly outperforms both aligned and unaligned approaches?

2. The paper notes that the cbow model with a higher subsampling threshold (10^-3) outperforms lower thresholds, which contradicts common recommendations. Given that the authors hypothesize this is due to the low frequency of compound phrases, could you elaborate on how this finding relates to the broader issue of sparse data in compositionality detection?

3. The optimal parameter values for h and q consistently fall in [0.3, 0.5], suggesting the modifier is slightly more informative than the head. Is there a linguistic explanation for this pattern, and how might it vary across different types of noun compounds?

4. How would the authors expect their APT approach to perform on non-noun compound phrases (e.g., adjective-noun compounds like "spelling bee" or verb phrases), given that the dependency structure differs significantly from noun-noun compounds?
