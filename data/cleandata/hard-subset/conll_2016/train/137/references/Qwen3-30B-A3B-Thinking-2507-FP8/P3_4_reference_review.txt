

# Peer Review: "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses a critical gap in distributional semantics: the evaluation of compositional models is confounded by non-compositional phrases (e.g., "red herring"). The authors propose using Anchored Packed Trees (APT) with typed higher-order dependency paths to better detect compositionality in compound nouns. They evaluate their method on Reddy et al.'s (2011) benchmark dataset of 90 compound nouns with human compositionality judgments.

The primary contributions are:
1. A theoretically grounded compositional approach (APT) that properly aligns vector spaces using dependency paths before composition
2. Demonstrated significant improvement in compositionality detection (Spearman's ρ=0.79 vs. previous state-of-the-art 0.714)
3. Insight that typed dependency features are highly informative for compositionality detection

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Significance**: The paper correctly identifies a fundamental problem in distributional semantics - that non-compositionality is inherent in language and must be accounted for in evaluation. The authors' distinction between "compositional" and "non-compositional" phrases is well-motivated by linguistic theory (Sag et al., 2002; McCarthy et al., 2003) and represents a significant conceptual contribution.

2. **Methodological Innovation**: The APT approach provides a principled way to handle different parts of speech within the same vector space by using dependency path alignment. The paper's detailed explanation of how this avoids the zero-vector problem with intersective composition (Table 1) demonstrates deep understanding of compositional semantics challenges.

3. **Strong Experimental Results**: The results are compelling, with the hybrid APT approach (combining aligned and unaligned features) achieving 0.79 Spearman's ρ, a statistically significant improvement over Reddy et al.'s 0.714. The paper thoroughly explores different composition operations and parameter settings, showing robustness across multiple configurations.

4. **Insightful Analysis**: The finding that optimal parameters h and q fall in [0.3,0.5] (suggesting the modifier is slightly more informative than the head) provides valuable linguistic insight about compound noun structure. The authors also correctly note that "the dependency paths encoded in the typed contextual features are highly informative," which is a significant contribution to understanding what aspects of syntax matter for compositionality.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Practical Application**: While the paper demonstrates improved compositionality detection, it fails to show how this would actually improve compositional models for phrase similarity tasks. The authors mention "one reason why intersective approaches to composition may work less well than additive approaches" but don't implement or test this proposed improvement. This weakens the practical significance of their findings.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper references Hashimoto et al. (2014) who integrated syntactic information into neural models, but doesn't compare their APT approach with this or other more recent syntactically-informed compositional models. Given the field's shift toward neural approaches, this comparison would strengthen the paper's contribution.

3. **Limited Discussion of Computational Costs**: The paper notes the feature space size (up to 230,000 dimensions), but doesn't address the computational cost of this approach compared to simpler methods. For large-scale applications, such high dimensionality could be prohibitive, making this a significant practical limitation.

4. **Overstated Claims About Compositionality Detection**: The paper claims their method "improves the detection of non-compositional phrases," but the evaluation is limited to the Reddy et al. dataset. The paper doesn't demonstrate whether this approach generalizes to other non-compositional phrases (e.g., phrasal verbs or idioms beyond compound nouns).

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you envision using your compositionality detection method to improve compositional phrase similarity models? Could you provide a concrete example of how this would work in practice?

2. The paper states that "the use of compositional methods to detect compositionality may also lead to improved compositional methods," but doesn't implement this. What specific improvements to compositional models would you expect from this approach?

3. The paper uses a very specific dataset (90 compound nouns) with human judgments of compositionality. How would you expect your method to perform on larger, more diverse datasets of non-compositional phrases (e.g., phrasal verbs or idioms)?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a theoretically sound contribution to understanding compositionality in distributional semantics. The APT approach is well-motivated and demonstrates clear improvements over previous work. However, the lack of connection between improved compositionality detection and better compositional models for practical NLP tasks weakens the paper's impact. The authors should address this gap in the revision, either by showing how their method could be integrated into a compositional model or by more clearly articulating the limitations of their approach for practical applications.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing with more recent syntactically-informed compositional models and by providing a more detailed analysis of why their approach works better than alternatives. Despite these limitations, this is a valuable contribution to the field that addresses an important conceptual gap in distributional semantics.
