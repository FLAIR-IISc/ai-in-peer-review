- Paper proposes an Anchored Packed Trees (APT) method using typed dependency paths to detect noun-compound compositionality and reports improved performance over prior work (0.79 vs. 0.71 Spearman).

- Contribution: shows typed dependency paths significantly improve compositionality detection compared to untyped contextual features.

- Contribution: systematically compares composition operations (aligned vs. unaligned, intersective vs. union) and identifies a hybrid approach as optimal.

- Contribution: analyzes head vs. modifier importance, finding modifiers slightly more informative and unaligned features slightly better than aligned ones.

- Strength: clear motivation that detecting non-compositional phrases is necessary for valid evaluation of compositional models.

- Strength: rigorous experimental setup with parameter tuning, 3-fold cross-validation, and statistical significance testing against multiple baselines.

- Strength: substantial empirical improvement over prior state-of-the-art, suggesting meaningful advancement for distributional semantics.

- Strength: clear methodological exposition of APT with illustrative examples and explanation of offsetting and composition operations.

- Strength: insightful analysis of parameter settings (h and q) and component contributions.

- Weakness: evaluation limited to 90 noun compounds from Reddy et al., so generalization to other phrase types is untested.

- Weakness: lacks comparison with more recent neural compositional models that could be stronger baselines.

- Weakness: insufficient theoretical or linguistic explanation for why APT and unaligned features outperform alternatives.

- Weakness: potential overfitting to the ukWaC corpus; no evaluation on other corpora to show generalizability.

- Weakness: missing ablation studies to isolate the effects of typed dependency paths vs. composition operations.

- Question: How would APT generalize to other phrase types (verb phrases, adj-noun, multiword expressions)?

- Question: Why were recent neural compositional models (e.g., Hashimoto 2014; Milajevs 2014) not compared, and how would APT fare against them?

- Question: Can the authors provide a linguistic or computational explanation for unaligned features being more informative?

- Question: Have the authors evaluated APT on other corpora to rule out corpus-specific advantages?

- Question: What exactly distinguishes the hybrid method from simply using unaligned features, and why is it superior?

- Overall recommendation: Weak Accept contingent on authors addressing generalization, comparisons to recent methods, theoretical justification, and ablation analyses.
