- Paper proposes Anchored Packed Trees (APT), encoding typed dependency paths to detect non-compositional phrases using compositional distributional semantics.
- Investigates how composition operations (aligned vs. unaligned, intersection vs. union) affect compositionality detection.
- Evaluated on Reddy et al. (2011) compound-noun dataset (90 examples), achieving Spearman ρ = 0.79 vs. prior best 0.714.
- Main contribution: integrated compositional distributional model (APT) that preserves syntactic structure in contextual features.
- Main contribution: empirical evidence that typed dependency paths with alignment are informative for compositionality detection.
- Main contribution: hybrid composition combining aligned and unaligned APTs yields best performance, implying complementary information.
- Strength: strong theoretical motivation emphasizing the importance of syntactic structure for detecting non-compositionality.
- Strength: significant empirical improvement (≈10.8% relative) over prior state-of-the-art with thorough parameter analyses.
- Strength: clear explanation of APT, typed higher-order dependency paths, and alignment process.
- Strength: comprehensive experimental evaluation (multiple composition ops, weighting schemes, cross-validation, statistical testing).
- Strength: insightful finding that modifiers (h and q in [0.3,0.5]) are slightly more informative than heads for this dataset.
- Weakness: lacks theoretical explanation for why the hybrid aligned+unaligned approach outperforms individual methods.
- Weakness: evaluation limited to compound nouns; unclear generalization to phrasal verbs, idioms, and other non-compositional phrases.
- Weakness: insufficient qualitative analysis of specific success/failure examples (e.g., "red herring").
- Weakness: limited comparison to more recent compositional/neural methods (e.g., Hashimoto et al., Milajevs et al.).
- Weakness: claim about necessity of syntactic structure is overstated given evaluation confined to one task.
- Question: request for examples showing how aligned and unaligned components capture complementary aspects of compositionality.
- Question: how the approach would generalize to other phrase types (phrasal verbs, idioms) and whether preliminary experiments exist.
- Question: how APT compares to other syntactic features (POS, semantic roles) versus dependency paths.
- Question: how compositionality detection could be integrated to improve larger compositional models in practice.
- Question: given the 10.8% improvement, what is the theoretical limit for this task and how close is APT to it?
- Overall recommendation: strong paper with meaningful contribution and compelling results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions.
