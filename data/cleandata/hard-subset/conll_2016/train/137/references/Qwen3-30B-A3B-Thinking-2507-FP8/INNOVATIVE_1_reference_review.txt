

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces Anchored Packed Trees (APT), a novel compositional method that explicitly incorporates syntactic structure through typed dependency paths to improve compositionality detection for noun compounds. The primary contribution is a new framework for composition that aligns vector spaces based on dependency paths before composition, rather than using standard naive composition operations. The paper demonstrates that this approach significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on the Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality dataset, achieving a Spearman's correlation of 0.79 with human judgments.

The secondary contribution is the insight that compositionality should be viewed as a continuum rather than a binary classification, providing a more nuanced evaluation framework. The tertiary contribution is the discovery that unaligned APT composition (without alignment) outperforms aligned composition, with a hybrid approach combining both yielding the best results.

## Strengths

1. **Genuinely novel compositional approach**: The APT method is a bold departure from standard composition techniques that ignore syntactic structure. By using typed dependency paths and alignment (or lack thereof) before composition, the authors address a fundamental limitation in current distributional semantic models. The method is theoretically grounded in syntactic structure, which is often overlooked in favor of purely statistical approaches. The 0.79 correlation score represents a significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.71), demonstrating the value of this novel approach.

2. **Nuanced perspective on compositionality**: The paper makes a strong theoretical argument that compositionality exists on a continuum rather than as a binary classification. This challenges the common simplification in the field and provides a more accurate framework for evaluating compositionality, which has broader implications for how we understand linguistic meaning.

3. **Insightful analysis of alignment**: The finding that unaligned composition outperforms aligned composition is counterintuitive and represents a significant theoretical contribution. The authors don't just report results but provide a thoughtful analysis of why this might be the case, suggesting that both aligned and unaligned features contribute to better compositionality detection. This challenges common assumptions in the field and opens new research directions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Narrow scope of application**: The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds, which limits the broader impact of the work. While this is a good starting point, the method's generalizability to other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-noun, or full sentences) remains unproven. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide evidence that the approach would work for other linguistic structures.

2. **Limited connection to broader compositionality challenges**: While the paper demonstrates improved compositionality detection, it doesn't clearly show how this translates to better phrase/sentence-level composition in other NLP tasks. The connection between compositionality detection and general compositionality improvement is underdeveloped, making it unclear how this work advances the field beyond this specific evaluation task.

3. **Methodological comparison issues**: The comparison with neural word embeddings is problematic because the authors don't optimize the embeddings for compositionality detection. The cbow model outperforms skip-gram, but this is a known result that doesn't necessarily validate the APT approach. A more meaningful comparison would use the same neural embeddings but apply the APT method to them.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you plan to extend this approach to other phrase types beyond noun compounds? Would the same principles apply to adjective-noun phrases or verb-noun constructions?

2. The paper suggests that the unaligned APT model outperforms the aligned model. Can you provide a more detailed linguistic explanation for why this might be the case, particularly given that the aligned approach was theoretically motivated by syntactic structure?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a genuinely novel and bold approach to compositionality detection that significantly outperforms previous work. The APT method challenges conventional wisdom about how composition should be handled in distributional semantic models and provides valuable insights about the relationship between syntactic structure and compositionality. While the scope is narrow, the novelty of the approach and the strength of the results justify acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to the understanding of compositionality in distributional semantics, which is a key challenge in the field.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's novel approach to compositionality detection, its strong empirical results, and its theoretical insights about the continuum of compositionality make it a valuable contribution to the field, even if the scope is limited to noun compounds. The authors have demonstrated that incorporating syntactic structure in a thoughtful way can lead to meaningful improvements in compositionality detection, which is an important step toward better compositional distributional models.
