

# Peer Review: "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates how compositional distributional models can improve detection of non-compositional phrases by incorporating syntactic structure. The authors introduce an Anchored Packed Trees (APT) approach that uses typed higher-order dependency paths as contextual features. The main contributions are: 1) A theoretically grounded compositional method that maintains syntactic structure within contextual features; 2) Demonstration that this approach significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art on compositionality detection; and 3) Evidence that a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned APT representations yields optimal results.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Grounding of Compositional Models**: The authors correctly identify a critical gap in the field—previous work on phrase similarity has conflated compositionality with similarity assessment, ignoring that non-compositional phrases like "red herring" cannot be meaningfully composed. Their approach to evaluate compositionality as a separate task is theoretically sound and addresses a fundamental limitation in the field. The APT method's use of typed dependency paths and alignment mechanism (with reduction) provides a principled solution to the problem of different parts of speech having incompatible contextual features.

2. **Strong Empirical Results**: The paper achieves a Spearman's correlation of 0.79 with human judgments, significantly outperforming the previous state-of-the-art (0.714). This represents a substantial 11% relative improvement. The systematic exploration of composition operations (intersective vs. union), weight parameters, and the innovative hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned representations) demonstrates thorough experimental design.

3. **Clear Explanation of Why the Approach Works**: The authors provide a compelling explanation for their results: "the dependency paths encoded in the typed contextual features are highly informative in the task of determining the compositionality of a noun compound." This insight is valuable because it moves beyond simply reporting better numbers to explaining *why* the method works, which is essential for theoretical advancement.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Generalizability**: The experiments are exclusively focused on noun-noun compounds (e.g., "grad student"), which represent a narrow subset of multiword expressions. The paper acknowledges this limitation but fails to provide evidence that the approach would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases). Given that compositionality exists on a continuum across different phrase types, this is a significant methodological limitation.

2. **Outdated Comparison to Neural Models**: The comparison to neural word embeddings uses only standard word2vec configurations (CBOW and skip-gram), ignoring more recent advances in contextualized embeddings (ELMo, BERT, etc.) that incorporate syntactic information more effectively. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't explore how their approach might integrate with these newer methods, which would strengthen the paper's relevance to current NLP research.

3. **Potential Evaluation Bias**: The evaluation relies on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset built from the same ukWaC corpus used for training. This creates a potential circularity where the model might be optimized for the specific characteristics of this corpus. The paper doesn't adequately address this concern or provide cross-corpus validation, which would be necessary to establish generalizability.

4. **Lack of Computational Analysis**: The paper mentions "approximately 230,000 dimensions" for the feature space but doesn't discuss the computational cost of this approach compared to simpler methods. For practical deployment, this dimensionality issue is significant, and the paper fails to address scalability concerns, which is a notable omission for a method with such high-dimensional representations.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you expect your APT approach to perform on non-noun compound phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases), given that the current experiments are limited to noun-noun compounds?

2. Have you considered how your approach might integrate with more recent contextualized embedding methods (e.g., ELMo, BERT) that already incorporate syntactic information in their architecture?

3. Could you provide evidence that the performance gains are not simply due to the specific characteristics of the ukWaC corpus used for both training and evaluation, perhaps by testing on a different corpus?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed study that makes a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating that syntactic structure is crucial for compositionality detection. The theoretical grounding and strong empirical results are compelling, particularly the 11% relative improvement over the previous state-of-the-art. However, the narrow scope of the experiments (limited to noun compounds) and the outdated comparison to neural models limit the paper's broader impact. Addressing these limitations would significantly strengthen the contribution of this work.

The paper makes a clear case for why compositional methods should be integrated with syntactic structure, and the APT approach provides a principled solution to the alignment problem. While the current results are impressive for the specific task of noun compound compositionality, the field would benefit from seeing how this approach generalizes to other phrase types and integrates with more recent neural architectures. With these limitations addressed, this work could become a significant contribution to the field of compositional distributional semantics.
