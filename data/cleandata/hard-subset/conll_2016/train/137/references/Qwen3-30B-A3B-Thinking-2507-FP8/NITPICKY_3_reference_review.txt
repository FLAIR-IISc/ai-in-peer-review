

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring"

## Summary
This paper investigates the use of compositional methods for detecting non-compositional phrases by introducing Anchored Packed Trees (APT), a distributional semantic model that uses typed higher-order dependency paths. The authors claim to improve compositionality detection for compound nouns by comparing their APT-based approach with neural embeddings and prior work (Reddy et al., 2011).

## Main Contributions
1. Introduction of APT as a compositional distributional modeling approach using typed dependency paths
2. Demonstration that APT-based composition outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods (0.79 vs 0.71)
3. Introduction of a hybrid composition method combining aligned and unaligned APT representations

## Strengths
The paper makes a clear contribution to the understanding of compositionality in distributional semantics, with a well-designed evaluation on a standard dataset. The focus on compositionality detection as an evaluation method for compositional models is particularly insightful. The authors demonstrate that syntactic structure encoded in typed dependency paths provides valuable information for compositionality detection.

## Weaknesses

**1. Contradiction between stated goal and methodology (Critical flaw)**

The paper claims: "we expect a good method of composition to be able to infer the representation of a phrase without ever having observed the phrase in the corpus." However, the authors explicitly state they "preprocessed the corpus by identifying occurrences of the 90 target noun phrases and recombining them into a single lexical item." This directly contradicts their stated goal. If phrases are recombined into single tokens, the model is trained on the phrase representations directly, not composed from their constituents. This undermines the entire premise of the evaluation, as the model is not truly "inferring" the phrase representation from its parts but rather using the phrase's own occurrences as its representation.

**2. Inconsistent parameterization of composition weights (Major flaw)**

The authors define composition as "(1 - h)Aδ₁ + hA₂" where Aδ₁ is the modifier's aligned representation and A₂ is the head's representation. However, they state "h is the parameter weighting the contributions of each constituent" but in the equation, h is actually the weight for the head, not the modifier. This is confusing and potentially incorrect parameterization. In their explanation, they claim "the modifier is slightly more informative than the head," which would require h to be the weight for the modifier, not the head. This inconsistency makes the results difficult to interpret.

**3. Undefined key terms in main text (Significant flaw)**

The paper uses acronyms CF (composition before PPMI) and CS (PPMI before composition) in Table 3 without defining them in the main text. While they are defined in the table caption, these are critical experimental distinctions that should be clearly explained when first introduced. This lack of definition makes it impossible for readers to understand the experimental design without constantly referring to the table.

**4. Formatting and technical inconsistencies (Minor but significant flaw)**

- The paper uses "naı̈ve" (with incorrect diacritics) instead of "naive" throughout the text.
- The references contain inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Baroni and Lenci.,? \Q2010\E" instead of "Baroni and Lenci, 2010").
- In Table 1, the feature types are inconsistently formatted (e.g., "NMOD" vs "NSUBJ.DOBJ").
- The paper states "α = 0.75" for PPMI smoothing but doesn't explicitly cite Levy et al. (2015) where this was established, making the claim less credible.

**5. Lack of statistical significance reporting for main result (Minor flaw)**

The paper claims the hybrid APT method achieves 0.79, which is better than the next best method (0.78), but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for this difference. While they state "any difference observed which is greater than 0.005 is statistically significant at the 95% level," they don't provide the actual standard errors or p-values for the main comparison, making it difficult to assess the significance of the claimed improvement.

## Conclusion

Despite the paper's interesting insights about compositionality in distributional semantics, the fundamental contradiction between the stated goal of "inferring phrase representations without observing the phrase" and the method of "recombining phrases into single tokens" is a critical flaw that undermines the entire evaluation framework. This methodological inconsistency is severe enough to invalidate the main claim of the paper. Additionally, the inconsistent parameterization of the composition weight and the undefined key terms in the main text further diminish the paper's credibility.

While the paper has a clear contribution to the field of compositionality detection, the methodological flaws are too substantial to recommend acceptance. The authors would need to completely rework their evaluation methodology to address the contradiction between their stated goal and their experimental approach before the paper could be considered for publication.

**Recommendation: Reject**
