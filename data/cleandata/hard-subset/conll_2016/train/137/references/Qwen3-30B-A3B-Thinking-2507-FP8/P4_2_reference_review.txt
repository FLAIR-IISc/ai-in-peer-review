

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates how different contextual features affect compositional distributional models' ability to detect compositionality in noun compounds. The authors use the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality. They introduce an "Anchored Packed Trees" (APT) approach that uses higher-order dependency paths to create linguistically motivated contextual features, and compare it with neural word embeddings and previous state-of-the-art methods.

The main contributions as I see them, in order of strength:

1. **A novel compositional framework (APT) that systematically incorporates syntactic structure through typed higher-order dependency paths**, which significantly outperforms previous methods in compositionality detection (0.79 vs. 0.714 Spearman correlation).

2. **Comprehensive empirical evaluation** of different composition operations (aligned vs. unaligned, intersective vs. union) and parameter settings, providing clear insights into what makes compositionality detection work better.

3. **Demonstration that syntactic structure is crucial for compositionality detection**, with the APT approach showing substantial improvement over previous untyped methods, suggesting that linguistic structure should be integrated into distributional models.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and clear problem definition:** The paper effectively identifies a critical gap in previous work - that compositionality detection has been conflated with phrase similarity evaluation. The authors correctly argue that non-compositional phrases like "red herring" are common in language and should be explicitly accounted for, not excluded from evaluation.

2. **Technical soundness of the APT approach:** The framework is well-motivated and carefully developed. The authors clearly explain the alignment process using dependency paths and the reduction operation (↓(τ)), which is well-defined and addresses the vector space misalignment problem noted in previous work. The implementation details for constructing APTs and performing composition are thorough and reproducible.

3. **Significant empirical results:** The APT approach achieves a 0.076 improvement in Spearman correlation (0.79 vs. 0.714) over the previous state-of-the-art, which is substantial for this task. The results are consistent across different parameter settings, and the authors provide thorough ablation studies to isolate the effects of different components.

4. **Methodical experimental design:** The authors carefully control for confounding factors (using the same corpus as Reddy et al., 2011), provide detailed preprocessing steps for handling compound nouns, and use appropriate evaluation metrics (Spearman correlation with human judgments).

5. **Insightful analysis of results:** The paper provides valuable insights into why certain approaches work better (e.g., unaligned models outperforming aligned ones, the modifier being slightly more informative than the head), which goes beyond simply reporting results and contributes to the field's understanding.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in the core compositional method:** While the APT framework is well-executed, the core idea of using dependency paths for compositionality is not entirely novel. Weeds et al. (2014) already explored higher-order dependency paths, and the authors acknowledge this. The main novelty appears to be the application to compositionality detection rather than the compositional method itself.

2. **Comparison with more recent compositional methods:** The paper compares with Reddy et al. (2011) and basic neural embeddings but does not compare with more recent compositional methods that have emerged since 2011 (e.g., tensor-based methods, more sophisticated neural composition models). This limits the context for evaluating the significance of their improvement.

3. **Lack of qualitative analysis:** The paper relies solely on quantitative results without providing any qualitative examples to illustrate how the APT approach works better than previous methods. This would have strengthened the argument for the approach and provided more intuitive understanding of the differences.

4. **Limited discussion of broader implications:** While the paper claims that "some knowledge of syntactic structure is crucial in the fine-grained understanding of language," it doesn't explore how these insights might impact other areas of NLP beyond compositionality detection.

5. **Statistical significance reporting:** The paper states "any difference observed which is greater than 0.005 is statistically significant at the 95% level," but this is not the standard way to report statistical significance. The authors should provide more conventional statistical reporting (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) to support their claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the unaligned typed dependency features are slightly more informative than the aligned typed dependency features." Could you provide specific examples of compound nouns where this pattern is particularly evident and explain why the unaligned approach works better for these cases?

2. The authors note that "the modifier is slightly more informative than the head in determining the compositionality of compound nouns." Is this consistent with linguistic intuition about noun-noun compounds, and how might this finding inform future compositional models?

3. Why was there no comparison with more recent compositional methods (e.g., tensor-based models or neural approaches published after 2011) that might have achieved better results on this task?

4. Given that many compound phrases are low-frequency tokens, how do you expect these findings to generalize to more common phrases or other types of multiword expressions?

5. The hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) achieved the best results. Could you elaborate on why this combination works better than either approach alone?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed study that makes a significant contribution to the field of compositional distributional semantics by demonstrating that incorporating syntactic structure through typed higher-order dependency paths substantially improves compositionality detection. The paper is technically sound, well-motivated, and provides valuable insights. While the core compositional method has some overlap with prior work, the application to compositionality detection and the substantial performance gains make this a meaningful contribution to the field. The paper would be strengthened by comparisons with more recent methods and qualitative examples, but as it stands, it represents a solid advance in understanding how syntactic structure can improve compositional distributional models.
