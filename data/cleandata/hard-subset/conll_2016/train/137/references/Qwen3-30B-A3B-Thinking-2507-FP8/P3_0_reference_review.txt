

# Peer Review: "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in distributional semantics: the difficulty of evaluating compositional methods when the very phrases being evaluated may be non-compositional. The authors propose a novel approach called Anchored Packed Trees (APT) that uses typed dependency paths to better model compositionality in noun compounds. They argue that traditional compositional methods fail because they assume compositionality where it doesn't exist (e.g., "red herring" is not compositional). The authors evaluate their approach using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality, comparing their APT-based methods against neural word embeddings and previous compositional methods.

## Main Contributions
1. **An integrated compositional distributional approach (APT) that maintains syntactic structure within contextual features** - This is the most significant contribution, as it demonstrates that syntactic information encoded in typed dependency paths is crucial for accurately modeling compositionality.
2. **Empirical demonstration that a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned features achieves optimal performance** - The finding that both aligned and unaligned features contribute to compositionality detection is a significant insight about the nature of compositionality.
3. **Demonstration that the APT approach outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods** - The authors achieve a Spearman correlation of 0.79 with human judgments, significantly outperforming the previous best result of 0.71.

## Strengths

### 1. Clear Problem Identification and Motivation
The paper effectively identifies a critical gap in the field: that traditional phrase similarity evaluation confounds compositionality with similarity judgments. The "red herring" example is perfect for illustrating this problem, and the authors properly contextualize it within the broader literature on compositionality (Sag et al., 2002; McCarthy et al., 2003). This is a strong foundation for the paper's contribution.

### 2. Novel Methodology with Strong Theoretical Basis
The APT approach is genuinely novel and well-motivated. The use of typed dependency paths (with syntactic relations) rather than untyped contexts is theoretically sound and addresses a key limitation of previous approaches. The explanation of how alignment works (with the reduction operation and offsetting) is clear and well-justified. The examples in Table 1 and Table 2 are particularly effective in demonstrating how the approach handles different parts of speech.

### 3. Thorough Evaluation with Appropriate Baselines
The evaluation is comprehensive, with multiple parameter settings tested (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, alignment strategies) and appropriate baselines (including the previous state-of-the-art and neural word embeddings). The use of 3-fold cross-validation and statistical significance testing (with p<0.05) is appropriate for this task. The authors also thoughtfully include the RAND and FREQ baselines, which provide important context for interpreting their results.

### 4. Important Empirical Findings
The finding that the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned features) outperforms both individual approaches is a significant insight about compositionality. The authors also correctly note that the modifier is slightly more informative than the head for compositionality detection (with optimal h and q in [0.3, 0.5]), which provides valuable linguistic insight. The results showing that PPMI (especially α=0.75) significantly outperforms untyped contexts is also important for the field.

### 5. Practical Implications for Compositionality Detection
The paper demonstrates that compositionality detection can be used to evaluate compositional methods without confounding compositionality with similarity judgments. This has important implications for the field, as it provides a cleaner evaluation metric for compositional methods. The authors also suggest a practical application: using compositionality detection to improve compositional methods by smoothing observed representations for non-compositional phrases.

## Weaknesses

### 1. Limited Scope and Generalization
The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds, despite compositionality being a broader linguistic phenomenon that occurs in many constructions (verb phrases, adjective phrases, etc.). The authors briefly mention this limitation in the conclusion, but without evidence that their approach generalizes to other constructions, the significance of their contribution is limited. This is a major weakness, as the field needs to understand compositionality across linguistic categories, not just noun compounds.

### 2. Lack of Theoretical Justification for the Hybrid Approach
The paper states that the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned features) achieves the best results, but provides no strong theoretical explanation for why this is the case. The authors note that "aligned APT composition and unaligned APT composition are predicting different contexts for compound nouns which all contribute to forming a better estimate," but this is merely an observation, not an explanation. Without a theoretical grounding, this finding remains somewhat empirical and unexplained.

### 3. Inadequate Comparison with Other Compositional Models
The paper compares against weighted addition (the previous state-of-the-art) and neural word embeddings, but does not compare against more linguistically motivated compositional models (e.g., lexical function method, full additive model, tensor-based methods) that have been shown to be effective in other work (Dinu et al., 2013; Baroni & Zamparelli, 2010). This omission weakens the claim that APT is "reasonably promising" and makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the authors' contribution.

### 4. Overstated Claims About Significance
The title and some sections of the paper overstate the significance of the improvement (0.71 to 0.79). While this is a meaningful improvement, it's not a dramatic breakthrough (the difference is only 0.08 in Spearman correlation), and the paper doesn't adequately discuss the practical implications of this improvement. The authors could have more carefully contextualized this improvement within the field's existing performance levels.

### 5. Ambiguity in Human Judgment Data
The paper doesn't provide sufficient detail about the human judgments used in the evaluation. Specifically, it doesn't mention inter-annotator agreement, how the judgments were aggregated, or whether the human judgments were consistent with linguistic intuition. This is a standard requirement for this type of evaluation, and the lack of detail makes it difficult to fully assess the validity of the results.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned features) works best, but provides no theoretical explanation for this. Can you provide a linguistic or computational justification for why both aligned and unaligned features are necessary for accurate compositionality detection?

2. The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds. How do you expect your approach to generalize to other types of phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases)? Do you have any preliminary evidence or plans to test this?

3. The authors note that using shifted PMI had a negative effect, which they attribute to low frequency of compound nouns. However, this is a common issue in distributional semantics. How would you expect your approach to perform on a larger corpus with more frequent compounds, or on a different type of phrase with higher frequency?

4. The paper mentions that "the modifier is slightly more informative than the head" for compositionality detection. Is this a general linguistic principle, or is it specific to the noun compounds in this dataset? How might this change for other constructions?

5. The FREQ baseline (using observed frequency) performs better than RAND, suggesting a correlation between frequency and compositionality. Is this a general linguistic phenomenon, or is it specific to the ukWaC corpus? How might this affect the interpretation of your results?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written, well-motivated paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of compositionality in distributional semantics. The APT approach is novel and well-justified, and the empirical results are significant. However, the limited scope to noun compounds and the lack of comparison with more linguistically motivated compositional models are substantial weaknesses that need to be addressed.

I recommend **acceptance with major revisions**. The authors should address the weaknesses identified above, particularly by expanding their evaluation to include more linguistic constructions and providing a stronger theoretical justification for their hybrid approach. If they can demonstrate that their approach generalizes beyond noun compounds and provide a more nuanced explanation for their key finding, this would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution to the field.
