- Paper proposes Anchored Packed Trees (APT), using typed higher-order dependency paths to detect non-compositional phrases.  
- Evaluated on Reddy et al. (2011) noun compound dataset and reports improved Spearman correlation (0.79 vs. 0.71).  

- Main contributions: novel APT method incorporating syntactic structure, comprehensive evaluation of composition operations, and significant empirical improvement.  

- Strength: Linguistically motivated approach—typed dependency paths capture syntactic relations important for compositionality.  
- Strength: Strong, statistically significant empirical results with careful cross-validation and parameter tuning.  
- Strength: Insightful analysis explaining why a hybrid of aligned and unaligned APTs performs best.  
- Strength: Clear link made between compositionality detection and evaluating compositional models.  

- Weakness: Evaluation limited to a single noun-compound dataset, limiting generalizability to other phrase types.  
- Weakness: Insufficient comparison with more recent compositional models (e.g., neural approaches).  
- Weakness: No analysis of failure cases or examples where the method fails.  
- Weakness: Unclear practical implications for downstream NLP tasks or concrete benefits beyond detection.  

- Question: Why does the unaligned model outperform the aligned model despite many noun-noun parses—does this generalize to other phrase types?  
- Question: Can the authors give examples where the hybrid model outperforms both aligned and unaligned models?  
- Question: How were parser misparses of three-noun compounds handled and how did they affect results?  
- Question: Provide an example where typed dependency paths were crucial for correct compositionality detection.  

- Overall recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address evaluation scope and comparisons to recent methods (Score: 7, Strong Accept).
