- Paper studies detection of non-compositional noun compounds using a novel APT method leveraging typed higher-order dependency paths and compares against embedding-based and compositional baselines.

- Contribution: Introduces Anchored Packed Trees (APT) using typed dependency paths to model compositionality and achieves 0.79 Spearman vs. prior 0.71.

- Contribution: Shows unaligned composition outperforms aligned composition, and hybrid approaches combining both work best.

- Contribution: Demonstrates syntactic structure (dependency paths) is crucial for modeling noun-compound compositionality versus purely distributional approaches.

- Strength: Argues compositionality is a continuum, separating compositionality evaluation from semantic similarity and addressing an important theoretical gap.

- Strength: Presents strong, statistically significant empirical results with thorough testing of composition operations and parameters.

- Strength: Uses a well-designed, reproducible experimental setup (Reddy et al. dataset, careful preprocessing, reported embedding parameters).

- Strength: Provides actionable insights on optimal composition operations and parameter ranges (e.g., ⊔UNI vs. ⊔INT, h and q in [0.3,0.5]).

- Strength: Clearly explains APT method with examples and motivates typed vs. untyped contextual features.

- Weakness: Lacks a compelling linguistic explanation for why APT and unaligned composition outperform aligned models.

- Weakness: Limited comparisons to more recent compositional models (e.g., tensor-based or neural compositional approaches).

- Weakness: Ambiguous definition of "typed" contextual features and insufficient differentiation from prior typed-feature work.

- Weakness: Insufficient discussion of limitations, such as POS ambiguity in modifiers, scalability, and applicability to other languages/datasets.

- Weakness: Title references "red herring" but the phrase is not analyzed experimentally, creating a misleading title-content alignment.

- Question: Please provide linguistic examples or evidence explaining why unaligned composition is better suited for compositionality detection.

- Question: How would APT perform on other multiword expression types (verb-noun, phrasal verbs, adjective-noun)?

- Question: How does the method handle modifier POS ambiguity (e.g., modifier tagged as noun vs. adjective) and its impact on scores?

- Question: Clarify why ⊔INT is chosen for aligned composition but ⊔UNI for unaligned composition.

- Question: Provide specific dependency-path examples that are especially informative for compositionality and explain their informativeness.

- Additional comment: Paper is technically strong and empirically convincing but would benefit from clearer limitations, broader baselines, and a title better aligned with its noun-compound focus.
