

# Peer Review: "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical challenge in distributional semantics: detecting whether phrases are compositional (meaning can be derived from their constituent words) or non-compositional (idiomatic, like "red herring"). The authors propose an innovative approach using "Anchored Packed Trees" (APT), which encodes typed dependency paths to model compositionality. They evaluate their method on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality, reporting significant improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Novel APT-based method for compositionality detection**: The paper introduces a method that uses typed higher-order dependency paths (rather than untyped features) to model compositionality, providing a more linguistically motivated approach to phrase composition.

2. **Key empirical finding about alignment**: The paper's most significant contribution is the counterintuitive finding that unaligned composition (not properly aligning vector spaces based on syntactic structure) outperforms aligned composition for compositionality detection, challenging a fundamental assumption in compositional distributional semantics.

3. **Systematic comparison of composition methods**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation of multiple composition strategies (additive, multiplicative, aligned, unaligned, hybrid) for compositionality detection, establishing a comprehensive benchmark for this specific task.

## Strengths

1. **Significant empirical improvement over previous work**: The authors report a Spearman's rho of 0.79, substantially exceeding the previous state-of-the-art (0.71) and neural embedding baselines (0.74). This is a meaningful improvement in the field, as compositionality detection is a challenging task with limited previous progress. The improvement is not marginal but represents a clear step forward in the field.

2. **Theoretical insight about compositionality detection vs. phrase similarity**: The paper makes a compelling argument that compositionality detection can serve as a valuable evaluation method for compositional models that avoids confounding compositionality with similarity judgments. This is a significant methodological contribution that could guide future research in distributional semantics.

3. **Clear and well-executed experimental design**: The paper demonstrates careful attention to experimental details, including consistent use of the ukWaC corpus, thorough parameter exploration (with 3-fold cross-validation), and appropriate evaluation metrics (Spearman's rho). The comparison with previous work is fair and directly comparable.

4. **Insight into the role of syntactic structure**: The paper provides valuable evidence that typed dependency features (with syntactic structure) are highly informative for compositionality detection, which supports the importance of incorporating linguistic structure in distributional models. This is a significant contribution to the ongoing debate about the necessity of syntax in distributional semantics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification for the unaligned approach**: The most surprising finding—that unaligned composition outperforms aligned composition for compositionality detection—is not adequately explained. The paper notes this counterintuitive result but fails to provide a theoretical basis or linguistic explanation for why this might be the case. This undermines the paper's claim that their approach is "integrated compositional" since the core method contradicts the theoretical foundation of the approach.

2. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The evaluation is restricted to noun compounds (specifically, noun-noun compounds), which represents only a small fraction of the compositionality phenomena in language. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't address how their method might generalize to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases, phrasal verbs). Without this, it's difficult to assess the broader applicability of their approach.

3. **Inadequate comparison with recent neural composition methods**: The paper compares their method to basic neural embeddings (cbow and skip-gram) but doesn't compare to more sophisticated neural composition methods developed in the last decade (e.g., tensor-based methods, LSTM-based composition, attention mechanisms). This is particularly problematic since the paper claims their method "outperforms the methods based on neural word embeddings," but their neural baseline is quite limited.

4. **Questionable interpretation of "non-compositionality"**: The paper's title ("When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring") is misleading. "Red herring" is a well-known idiom, so it's clearly non-compositional, not a "red herring" (i.e., not a false lead). This phrasing could confuse readers about the paper's actual contribution.

5. **Ambiguous justification for the hybrid method**: The hybrid method (combining aligned and unaligned composition) achieves the best results, but the paper doesn't provide a clear theoretical rationale for why this combination works better. The parameter q (which weights the aligned and unaligned components) is found to be in the range [0.3, 0.5], but the paper doesn't explain why this specific weighting is optimal or how it might generalize.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper's most significant finding is that unaligned composition outperforms aligned composition for compositionality detection. Given that alignment is a central theoretical assumption in compositional distributional semantics (as the authors themselves state), can you provide a theoretical explanation for why this counterintuitive result occurs? How does this align with the linguistic intuition that syntactic structure matters for composition?

2. The paper evaluates only noun compounds. How do you expect your method to perform on other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, phrasal verbs, adjective phrases)? Have you considered testing on a broader dataset like the one used in McCarthy et al. (2003), which examines compositionality across multiple phrase types?

3. The paper mentions that shifted PMI (analogous to negative sampling in word embeddings) had a negative effect on results. Given that negative sampling is a key component of word2vec's success, why might this be the case specifically for compositionality detection? Is it related to the low frequency of compound nouns in the corpus?

4. The paper claims that their APT-based method outperforms neural embeddings, but they only use basic cbow and skip-gram models. Have you considered comparing to more recent neural composition methods (e.g., those using transformers or attention mechanisms) that have achieved state-of-the-art results on phrase similarity tasks?

5. The hybrid method (using both aligned and unaligned composition) achieves the best results. Can you explain why combining these two approaches is beneficial, and is there a theoretical basis for the optimal weighting parameter q being in the range [0.3, 0.5]?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of distributional semantics, particularly in the area of compositionality detection. The empirical results are strong and represent a meaningful improvement over previous work. The most significant contribution is the finding that unaligned composition outperforms aligned composition for this specific task, which challenges a fundamental assumption in compositional distributional semantics and opens up new avenues for research.

However, the lack of theoretical justification for this key finding and the limited scope of evaluation are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. The paper would be much stronger if the authors could provide a more robust theoretical explanation for their results and demonstrate that their method generalizes to other phrase types.

I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the theoretical weaknesses and broaden the evaluation scope. The core method and empirical results are strong, but the paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the theoretical implications of their findings.
