

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to detecting compositionality in phrases using a compositional distributional semantics framework. The authors propose "Anchored Packed Trees" (APT), a method that encodes typed dependency paths to better capture compositional meaning. Their key insight is that non-compositional phrases (like "red herring") confound traditional similarity evaluations, and compositionality detection can serve as a more direct metric for evaluating compositional models. The authors evaluate their approach on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns, achieving a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, significantly outperforming the previous state-of-the-art (0.714).

**Main Contributions (in order of strength):**

1. **Conceptual Innovation**: The paper's most significant contribution is its fundamental shift in evaluation methodology—using compositionality detection as a metric separate from phrase similarity judgments. This is a bold new direction that addresses a critical blind spot in distributional semantics.

2. **Technical Advancement**: The APT model, which incorporates syntactic structure through typed dependency paths and alignment, provides a principled way to handle compositional meaning that goes beyond untyped co-occurrence vectors.

3. **Empirical Validation**: The clear, significant improvement over previous work (0.79 vs. 0.714) demonstrates that syntactic structure is crucial for compositionality detection, providing compelling evidence for their approach.

## Strengths

1. **Groundbreaking Evaluation Framework**: The paper's most compelling contribution is its conceptual rethinking of how we evaluate compositional models. By separating compositionality detection from phrase similarity, the authors provide a cleaner metric that avoids conflating two distinct linguistic phenomena. This is a bold, novel direction that could reshape how the field evaluates compositional methods.

2. **Significant Empirical Improvement**: The results are not incremental—they represent a clear, substantial jump in performance (0.79 vs. 0.714). The authors carefully compare multiple composition operations and parameter settings, demonstrating that their APT approach is consistently superior. This isn't just better performance—it's a meaningful step forward that validates their core insight.

3. **Linguistic Insight**: The paper makes a compelling case that syntactic structure is crucial for compositionality, aligning with linguistic theories but underexplored in distributional semantics. The observation that "the second-order features of adjectives correspond to the first-order features of nouns" reveals a deep structural insight that could inform future work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope**: The evaluation is restricted to noun compounds (90 examples), which is a narrow domain. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, it significantly reduces the generalizability of their findings. A more comprehensive evaluation across different phrase types would strengthen the paper's contribution.

2. **Comparison to Recent Methods**: The paper doesn't compare to more recent compositional models (e.g., transformer-based approaches) that might have achieved better results on this task. This limits the context for their improvement and makes it harder to assess the true novelty of their contribution.

3. **Technical Vagueness**: The paper is somewhat vague about the implementation details of the APT model, particularly regarding the "reduction" operation (Equation 1). This makes it difficult to assess the novelty of their method and to replicate their results.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you envision extending your APT approach to handle other types of phrases beyond noun compounds (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases)?

2. Could you provide more detail on how the "reduction" operation (Equation 1) handles complex dependency paths in practice, particularly in cases where multiple relations might interact?

3. Why did you choose to use only first-order dependency features for the observed compound nouns while using higher-order features for composition? How might this affect the results for different types of phrases?

## Recommendation

This paper introduces a bold new direction in distributional semantics by rethinking how we evaluate compositional models. The conceptual contribution—using compositionality detection as a separate metric—is significant and potentially transformative. While the scope is limited to noun compounds, the methodological insight is substantial, and the empirical results are compelling. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper to be a significant contribution that should be accepted. The minor limitations regarding scope and technical detail do not outweigh the paper's conceptual novelty and strong empirical results.
