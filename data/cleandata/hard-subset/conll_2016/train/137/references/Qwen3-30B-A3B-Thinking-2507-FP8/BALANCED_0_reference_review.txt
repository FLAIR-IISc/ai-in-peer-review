

# Review: "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper addresses the problem of detecting compositionality in noun compounds (e.g., "red herring")—whether the meaning of a phrase can be derived from its parts. The authors propose a new method called Anchored Packed Trees (APT) that encodes dependency paths from target words to context words, and then uses compositionality to predict features of compound nouns. The main contributions are:

1. A novel compositional method (APT) that incorporates syntactic structure into distributional semantics through typed dependency paths.
2. A new evaluation metric for compositional methods that focuses on compositionality detection rather than phrase similarity, avoiding confounding factors.
3. An insightful empirical analysis of different composition operations (aligned vs. unaligned, intersection vs. union) that demonstrates the hybrid approach (combining both aligned and unaligned features) yields optimal results.

## Strengths

1. **Novel evaluation metric**: The paper makes a significant contribution by using compositionality detection as an evaluation metric for compositional methods. This cleverly sidesteps the confounding issue of evaluating compositional methods on phrase similarity tasks, where compositionality itself affects results. This approach provides a cleaner way to assess compositional methods.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The authors achieve a Spearman correlation of 0.79 with human judgments of compositionality, a substantial improvement over the previous state-of-the-art of 0.714 (Reddy et al., 2011). This represents a meaningful advance in the field and demonstrates the value of their approach.

3. **Insightful analysis of composition operations**: The paper provides a thorough comparison of different composition strategies (aligned vs. unaligned, intersection vs. union), revealing that the hybrid approach (combining both) performs best. This offers valuable insights into how syntactic structure affects compositionality detection.

4. **Clear methodology**: The paper provides a detailed explanation of the APT approach with concrete examples and mathematical formalization, making it accessible and reproducible.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper exclusively evaluates on noun compounds, which represents a narrow domain. While the authors acknowledge in the conclusion that "further work is needed with other datasets and other types of phrase," they don't demonstrate the broader applicability of their method. This limits the perceived impact of their contribution.

2. **Lack of comparison to recent compositional methods**: The paper compares to Reddy et al. (2011) and neural word embeddings, but doesn't compare to more recent compositional approaches that have been proposed since 2011 (e.g., tensor-based methods, neural compositional models). This omission makes it difficult to assess how much their method advances the state-of-the-art.

3. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of the APT approach. For example, it doesn't discuss how well the method would work with more complex phrases (verb phrases, adjective phrases) or with languages having different syntactic structures.

4. **Potential overfitting concerns**: The hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned features) achieves the best results, but the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this isn't simply overfitting to the specific dataset. A more rigorous analysis of generalizability would strengthen their claims.

## Questions for Authors

1. How would the APT approach perform on non-noun compounds (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases)? Have you tried applying it to other phrase types?

2. The paper states that "the unaligned model outperforms the aligned model," but the hybrid model (which combines both) performs best. Can you provide more insight into why this is the case? Is it because aligned features are redundant or because they capture complementary information?

3. Given that the hybrid model combines aligned and unaligned features, how do you ensure this isn't simply a form of overfitting to the specific dataset? Have you tested the model on additional datasets?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper with a strong empirical contribution that advances the state-of-the-art in compositionality detection by a meaningful margin. The APT approach is well-motivated, and the evaluation metric is particularly clever. While the scope is limited to noun compounds, the methodological insights and strong results make this a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper would be strengthened by broader evaluation across phrase types, comparison to more recent compositional methods, and a more thorough discussion of limitations. However, these are not deal-breakers for a conference paper. The contribution is significant enough to warrant acceptance, especially given the clear value of the compositionality detection metric.

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions addressing the scope limitations and comparison to more recent methods).
