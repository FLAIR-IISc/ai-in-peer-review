

# Review for "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates the detection of compositionality in noun compounds (e.g., "red herring" vs. "grad student") using distributional semantic models. The authors propose applying their previously introduced Anchored Packed Trees (APT) approach to this specific task, comparing it against previous state-of-the-art methods on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. The main contributions as I see them are:

1. A significant empirical improvement over previous state-of-the-art results for compositionality detection (0.79 vs. 0.71 Spearman's ρ).
2. Demonstration that typed dependency paths with higher-order context features are highly informative for compositionality detection.
3. Insight that a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned composition methods works best for this task, with unaligned features being slightly more informative.

## Strengths

1. **Substantial empirical contribution**: The paper achieves a statistically significant improvement (0.79 vs. 0.71) over the previous state-of-the-art on a well-established benchmark. This is a meaningful advancement in the specific task of compositionality detection, which has important implications for evaluating compositional methods in distributional semantics.

2. **Strong theoretical grounding**: The paper provides a clear linguistic justification for using typed dependency paths in compositionality detection, connecting the approach to formal semantics and linguistic theory about the compositionality continuum. The authors effectively argue why syntactic structure matters for understanding compositionality.

3. **Rigorous experimental design**: The authors conducted thorough parameter exploration with cross-validation, carefully comparing different composition operations (aligned vs. unaligned, intersective vs. union), and different weighting schemes. The results are consistently robust across different parameter settings.

4. **Valuable insights into compositionality**: The paper provides important insights about the relative importance of different compositional methods for this specific task, which will inform future work on compositional distributional models. The finding that unaligned features are slightly more informative than aligned features is particularly noteworthy.

5. **Clear and well-structured presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-organized, with clear explanations of the technical approach, thorough experimental setup, and careful presentation of results with appropriate statistical analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of generalization**: The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds, yet the authors claim their approach is "reasonably promising" for broader application. Without demonstrating how the method would generalize to other phrase types (verb phrases, adjective phrases, etc.), this claim remains unsubstantiated. The paper should better articulate the limitations of their approach to noun compounds.

2. **Lack of computational cost analysis**: The paper mentions the high dimensionality of the feature space (80,000-230,000 dimensions), but fails to discuss the computational implications of this approach compared to simpler methods like neural embeddings. For a field increasingly concerned with practical applicability, this is a significant omission.

3. **Insufficient ablation study**: While the paper compares different composition operations, it doesn't isolate the contribution of the typed dependency paths themselves. A critical ablation study would compare the same compositional method using both typed and untyped features to determine the specific contribution of the typed paths.

4. **Limited novelty of the approach**: The APT method was previously proposed by the authors elsewhere (as indicated by "Elsewhere (reference suppressed)"). This paper is primarily an application of a known method rather than a novel methodological contribution, which is a limitation given the high bar for novelty at CoNLL.

5. **Dataset limitations not adequately addressed**: The Reddy et al. (2011) dataset contains only 90 compounds, which is small for modern NLP evaluation. The paper doesn't discuss how the results might scale to larger datasets or how the findings generalize beyond this specific, limited set of examples.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you expect your APT-based approach to scale to more complex phrase types beyond noun compounds (e.g., verb phrases or adjective phrases), and what modifications would be needed?

2. Could you provide more details about the computational cost (training and inference time) of your APT approach compared to simpler methods like neural embeddings, especially considering the high dimensionality of your feature space?

3. Could you conduct an ablation study to isolate the contribution of typed dependency paths from the compositional method itself, by comparing the same composition operation with both typed and untyped features?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed study that makes a meaningful empirical contribution to the specific task of compositionality detection for noun compounds. The results are robust, the methodology is sound, and the insights about the relative importance of different compositional approaches are valuable. However, the limited scope of the evaluation (only noun compounds), the lack of computational cost analysis, and the insufficient novelty of the core approach (APT was previously proposed by the authors) prevent it from being a strong candidate for acceptance at CoNLL. 

The paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the limitations noted above, particularly by demonstrating how the approach generalizes beyond noun compounds, providing computational cost analysis, and conducting the ablation study to isolate the contribution of typed dependency paths. With these improvements, the paper would make a stronger contribution to the field.
