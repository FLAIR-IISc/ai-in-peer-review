

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates compositionality detection for noun compounds using distributional semantic models. The authors propose an anchored packed tree (APT) approach that incorporates typed dependency paths to better model syntactic structure in compositional methods. The main contributions are:

1. Demonstrating that syntactic structure encoded through typed dependency paths significantly improves compositionality detection over previous methods (achieving ρ = 0.79 vs. previous state-of-the-art ρ = 0.71).
2. Introducing a hybrid compositional approach that combines aligned and unaligned APT representations, which outperforms both pure aligned and unaligned methods.
3. Providing a thorough empirical analysis of composition operations, weight types, and parameter settings for compositionality detection.

## Strengths

1. **Significant empirical improvement:** The paper demonstrates a clear and statistically significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71). This is a meaningful contribution to the field, as compositionality detection is an important problem with practical implications for NLP systems. The results are consistent across multiple parameter settings and composition operations.

2. **Theoretically grounded technical contribution:** The APT approach with typed dependency paths is well-motivated by linguistic theory (e.g., McCarthy et al., 2003), and the authors provide a clear explanation for why syntactic structure matters in compositionality detection. Their insight about the relationship between different order dependency features (e.g., how 2nd order features of adjectives correspond to 1st order features of nouns) is particularly compelling.

3. **Rigorous experimental design:** The paper carefully evaluates multiple aspects of the compositional model (weight types, composition operations, alignment methods) and uses proper cross-validation. The authors also provide meaningful baselines (RAND, FREQ, and previous state-of-the-art) that help contextualize their results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of evaluation:** The paper only evaluates on noun compounds, which represents a narrow subset of multiword expressions. The authors claim their approach could be extended to other phrase types, but they provide no evidence to support this. Without evaluation on other types of phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases), it's difficult to assess the broader applicability of their method.

2. **Lack of comparison to recent compositional models:** The paper doesn't compare to more recent compositional models (e.g., neural tensor networks, transformer-based approaches) that have been developed since Reddy et al. (2011). This makes it hard to determine whether their APT approach is still state-of-the-art or if it's been surpassed by newer methods.

3. **Unclear practical significance:** While the paper demonstrates improved compositionality detection, it doesn't explain how this would translate to better performance on downstream NLP tasks. The authors focus on compositionality detection as an end in itself, without showing how their approach could improve other NLP applications (e.g., machine translation, semantic parsing).

4. **Implementation details missing:** The paper lacks details about how they handled compounds with multiple dependency relations during preprocessing. Also, the paper states that the feature space for nouns is "approximately 80,000 dimensions" but doesn't explain what these dimensions represent or why this size is appropriate.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you plan to extend your approach to other types of multiword expressions beyond noun compounds? Have you considered evaluating on verb phrases or adverbial phrases?

2. The paper states that "the majority of occurrences where no dependency relationship was observed, this was due to the parser incorrectly parsing a three noun compound phrase." Could you provide more details about how you handled this specific parsing issue during preprocessing?

3. Have you considered comparing your approach to more recent compositional models (e.g., those using transformers or attention mechanisms) that have been shown to perform well on phrase-level tasks?

4. Could you provide a qualitative analysis of which types of phrases are being correctly or incorrectly classified by your model? This would help identify the limitations of your approach.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by demonstrating that syntactic structure encoded through typed dependency paths significantly improves compositionality detection. The empirical results are strong and well-supported, and the hybrid compositional approach is a valuable technical contribution.

However, the limited scope of evaluation (only noun compounds) and lack of comparison to recent compositional models are significant weaknesses that prevent the paper from making a broader impact. The authors should address these limitations in a revision by evaluating on additional phrase types and comparing to more recent methods.

Given the strong empirical results and theoretical contribution, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limitations noted above. The paper would be significantly strengthened by expanding the evaluation scope and providing more context for how this work fits within the current landscape of compositional models.
