

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates how the choice of contextual features (typed vs. untyped) and composition methods affects the detection of compositional phrases in noun compounds. The authors propose using anchored packed trees (APTs) with typed higher-order dependency paths to better capture syntactic structure for composition. They evaluate their approach using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality.

The main contributions of this work are:
1. A novel framework using typed higher-order dependency paths (APTs) for composition, which significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on compositionality detection.
2. A systematic comparison of compositional methods with different contextual feature types (typed vs. untyped), demonstrating that syntactic structure encoded through dependency paths is crucial for compositionality detection.
3. A hybrid compositional approach combining aligned and unaligned APT representations, which achieves the best performance on the evaluation task.

## Strengths

1. **Significant performance improvement over prior work**: The authors demonstrate a clear and statistically significant improvement (0.79 vs. 0.71) over the previous state-of-the-art method reported by Reddy et al. (2011). This 10% relative improvement is substantial in the field of distributional semantics, particularly for a well-established benchmark. The paper provides a convincing argument that syntactic structure encoded through typed dependency paths is critical for compositionality detection.

2. **Rigorous experimental evaluation**: The authors conduct a thorough parameter exploration across multiple variants of their approach (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, alignment methods), using 3-fold cross-validation with statistical significance testing. The results are well-organized and clearly presented in Tables 3-5, making it easy to understand the comparative performance of different methods. The authors also provide important insights about parameter optimization (e.g., optimal values of h and q in the range [0.3,0.5]).

3. **Insightful evaluation framework**: The paper establishes compositionality detection as a valuable proxy for evaluating compositional methods, avoiding the confounding factor of phrase similarity judgments. This provides a new perspective for assessing compositional models that is particularly relevant to the field's current challenges. The authors demonstrate that compositionality detection can serve as a more focused evaluation metric than phrase similarity tasks, which are confounded by non-compositional phenomena.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in compositional operations**: While the paper presents a novel framework for contextual features (typed dependency paths), the compositional operations themselves (pointwise addition and intersection) are not new. The authors acknowledge this by calling them "naive" and noting they've been used before. The main contribution is the feature representation rather than the composition method, which limits the paper's novelty claim.

2. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to noun compounds (90 examples from the Reddy et al. dataset). The authors do not demonstrate how their approach would perform on other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb phrases) or in other downstream tasks (e.g., phrase similarity, paraphrase detection). This significantly limits the generalizability of their findings and makes it difficult to assess the broader applicability of their method.

3. **Insufficient explanation of key findings**: The paper reports that the unaligned APT approach outperforms the aligned approach, but doesn't provide a thorough explanation for why this is the case. The authors note that "the unaligned typed dependency features are slightly more informative than the aligned typed dependency features" but don't explore this phenomenon in depth. A more detailed analysis of why unaligned features might be more informative would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach generalize to other phrase types beyond noun compounds, such as adjective-noun phrases or verb phrases? Would the same dependency path features be equally informative?

2. The paper shows that unaligned APT features outperform aligned features, but doesn't explain why. Can you provide more insight into the linguistic or statistical reasons for this counterintuitive finding?

3. The Reddy et al. dataset contains low-frequency compounds. How would your approach perform on higher-frequency phrases not included in this dataset, and how might the results change with larger datasets?
