

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes an "Anchored Packed Trees" (APT) approach for detecting compositionality in noun compounds, where compositionality refers to whether a phrase's meaning can be derived from its parts (e.g., "red herring" is non-compositional while "grad student" is compositional). The authors claim that using typed, higher-order dependency paths in contextual features significantly improves compositionality detection compared to previous methods. The main contributions are: (1) an APT-based compositional framework for predicting compositionality in compound nouns; (2) systematic evaluation of alignment versus unaligned composition; and (3) demonstration that typed dependency features outperform untyped approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Comprehensive Baseline Comparison**: The paper thoroughly compares against the previous state-of-the-art (Reddy et al., 2011) and provides strong statistical evidence that their approach (0.79 Spearman's ρ) significantly outperforms the baseline (0.714). The authors also include meaningful baselines (random, frequency-based) and validate their results through repeated 3-fold cross-validation.

2. **Detailed Parameter Analysis**: The authors conduct an exhaustive analysis of key parameters (PPMI smoothing α, composition operations, alignment vs. unaligned, weighting parameters) and provide clear evidence for their optimal settings. This level of parameter exploration is valuable for reproducibility and understanding the method's behavior.

3. **Methodological Rigor**: The paper adheres to established evaluation practices for compositionality detection, using the same corpus (ukWaC) and evaluation metrics (Spearman's ρ) as prior work. The preprocessing steps for compound phrases and the handling of dependency relations are carefully documented.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison to Established Compositional Methods**: The paper's main claim is about improving compositionality detection, yet it fails to compare against the most advanced compositional methods in the field (e.g., lexical function model, tensor-based methods). Dinu et al. (2013) demonstrated that these methods outperform naive composition (addition/multiplication) across multiple benchmarks. The authors' claim that "APT composition" outperforms previous work is questionable since they only compare to Reddy et al. (2011), which used naive composition. Without comparison to state-of-the-art compositional methods, the significance of their contribution is greatly diminished.

2. **Overstated Novelty**: The paper claims the APT approach is "novel," but Weeds et al. (2014) already investigated higher-order dependency paths for composition. The authors don't clearly explain how their method differs from or improves upon this prior work. The "alignment" concept is also similar to existing approaches like the lexical function method. This lack of clear differentiation from established work undermines the novelty claim.

3. **Limited Scope of Validation**: The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds (90 examples), but compositionality is a broader phenomenon across phrase types (adjective-noun, verb-noun, etc.). The authors acknowledge this limitation ("Further work is of course needed with other datasets and other types of phrase"), but this severely restricts the generalizability of their claims. Compositionality detection should be validated across multiple phrase types to be considered a significant contribution.

4. **Insufficient Explanation of Why APT Works**: The paper reports that the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) works best but provides no linguistic or computational explanation for this finding. Without understanding why certain approaches work better for specific phrases, the contribution remains largely empirical without theoretical grounding.

5. **Misalignment Between Title and Contribution**: The title suggests the paper is about "using compositional methods to improve detection," but the contribution is actually about improving compositionality detection. The paper doesn't demonstrate how this detection method improves actual compositional systems for downstream tasks (e.g., phrase similarity, paraphrasing), which would be a stronger contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to outperform "previous methods," but doesn't compare to the most advanced compositional methods (e.g., lexical function model). How does APT compare to these state-of-the-art compositional methods?

2. The paper uses the same evaluation metric (Spearman's ρ) as Reddy et al. (2011), but doesn't address whether the improved score is due to better compositionality detection or simply better parameter tuning of the same framework. How did the authors ensure that the improvement isn't merely from hyperparameter optimization rather than methodological innovation?

3. The paper focuses on noun compounds but compositionality applies to many phrase types. How would the APT approach need to be modified for other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun), and what evidence do you have that it would work similarly well?

## Final Assessment

While the paper presents a well-executed evaluation of an approach to compositionality detection, its claims of novelty and significance are overstated. The lack of comparison to established compositional methods and the limited scope of validation significantly undermine the contribution. The paper would be much stronger if it compared against the most advanced compositional methods in the field and validated its approach across multiple phrase types. As it stands, the contribution appears to be an incremental improvement rather than a significant advance in the field. The authors should temper their claims and provide clearer differentiation from existing work before the contribution can be considered meaningful.

The paper demonstrates methodological rigor and thorough evaluation, but falls short of providing convincing evidence that their approach represents a substantial advancement beyond established methods. For a CONLL paper, which values established methods and rigorous validation, this submission would benefit from a more conservative presentation of its contribution and a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art compositional approaches.
