

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary
This paper proposes an "Anchored Packed Trees" (APT) approach for compositional distributional semantics, specifically targeting the detection of non-compositional phrases. The authors claim that by using typed dependency paths (with higher-order paths) and proper alignment of vector spaces before composition, their method can better detect compositionality in noun compounds compared to previous approaches. They evaluate their method on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel method (APT) for compositional distributional semantics using typed dependency paths with alignment before composition
2. Empirical demonstration that their method outperforms the previous state-of-the-art on compositionality detection
3. Thorough analysis of different composition operations and parameter settings

## Strengths
1. **Use of established benchmark**: The authors correctly use the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, which is a well-established benchmark for compositionality detection, allowing for meaningful comparison with prior work.

2. **Methodological rigor in experiments**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of different composition operations (intersective vs. union), parameter settings, and the impact of alignment. The use of cross-validation and statistical significance testing adds credibility to their results.

3. **Clear performance improvement**: The authors demonstrate a statistically significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71 on Spearman's ρ), which is a concrete contribution that warrants attention.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of proper baseline comparison**: The paper compares against Reddy et al. (2011), but this is an untyped model, while their method uses typed features. A more appropriate baseline would be to use the same typed features as in their method but without the alignment component, to isolate the contribution of their key innovation.

2. **Insufficient justification for novelty**: The authors claim that "some knowledge of syntactic structure is crucial," but don't provide compelling evidence that their specific alignment mechanism is the key factor in their improvement. The improvement could stem from using higher-order dependency paths without the alignment component, which the paper doesn't properly isolate.

3. **Limited evidence for broader applicability**: While the paper claims that their method "appears that the APT approach to compositionality is reasonably promising," it doesn't provide evidence that this approach improves compositional methods for other NLP tasks beyond compositionality detection. The connection to broader improvements in compositional distributional semantics remains speculative.

4. **Overstatement of the problem**: The paper frames "non-compositional phrases" as a critical problem that needs addressing, but doesn't establish that this specific task is more important than other challenges in compositional semantics. The field has focused on phrase similarity tasks, and it's unclear why compositionality detection should be prioritized over these more directly applicable tasks.

5. **Lack of analysis of failure cases**: The paper doesn't examine cases where their method fails, which would help understand the limitations of their approach and provide guidance for future work.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide a comparison between your method and a variant that uses the same typed dependency features but without the alignment mechanism? This would help isolate whether the alignment is truly the key innovation.

2. The paper claims that "the compositionality detection task provides a way of evaluating compositional methods without confounding judgements of phrase similarity with judgements of compositionality." However, it's not clear why this is a more valuable evaluation than direct phrase similarity tasks. Can you provide evidence that this approach leads to better compositional methods for downstream applications?

3. The paper notes that "many of the compound phrases are comparatively low frequency tokens in the corpus." How does your method handle the sparsity issue that plagues low-frequency compounds, and why does your approach work better than standard smoothing techniques?

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents a method that achieves a statistically significant improvement on an established benchmark for compositionality detection. However, the paper's claims about the superiority of their approach are not fully supported by the evidence provided. The lack of a proper baseline comparison and insufficient justification for why their method works better than simpler alternatives weaken the contribution. While the empirical results are valid, the paper overstates the significance of the contribution by implying that this method represents a major advance in compositional distributional semantics, when it may simply be an effective parameterization of existing approaches.

The paper demonstrates that using typed dependency paths with proper alignment can improve compositionality detection, but the broader implications for compositional distributional semantics remain unclear. Without evidence that this approach leads to better phrase representations for other tasks, the contribution is limited to a specific evaluation task rather than a general advancement in the field.

Given the preference for established methods and skepticism toward unproven approaches, I find this paper's contribution to be incremental rather than transformative. While the results are valid and the methodology sound, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to justify the claim that this represents a significant advance in compositional distributional semantics. The paper would be strengthened by a more rigorous comparison against relevant baselines and evidence of broader applicability.
