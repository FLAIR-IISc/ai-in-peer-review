- Addresses confounding of compositionality with phrase similarity and proposes Anchored Packed Trees (APT) using typed dependency paths and alignment.  
- Contribution: introduces a compositional APT framework that preserves syntactic structure in contextual features.  
- Contribution: shows typed syntactic contextual features are highly informative for noun-compound compositionality detection.  
- Contribution: provides an evaluation framework that separates compositionality detection from phrase similarity to avoid confounding judgments.  
- Strength: significant methodological advance—APT is linguistically motivated and yields a statistically significant SOTA improvement (0.79 vs. 0.71).  
- Strength: thorough empirical evaluation with ablation studies, parameter sweeps, and comparisons to neural embeddings.  
- Strength: strong theoretical justification with examples illustrating the importance of typed dependency paths and alignment.  
- Weakness: limited evaluation scope—only 90 noun compounds, unclear generalization to verb phrases, idioms, or other MWEs.  
- Weakness: lacks comparison to more recent neural/transformer-based compositional methods.  
- Weakness: insufficient explanation for why the hybrid (aligned + unaligned) composition outperforms individual approaches.  
- Question: How would APT generalize to more complex multiword expressions (verb phrases, idioms) and what modifications are needed?  
- Question: Would applying APT to standard phrase similarity tasks improve benchmark performance?  
- Question: Do the optimal parameter settings (modifier slightly more informative) align with linguistic theory or indicate a model limitation?  
- Overall recommendation: Strong paper; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to broaden evaluation and clarify the hybrid approach.
