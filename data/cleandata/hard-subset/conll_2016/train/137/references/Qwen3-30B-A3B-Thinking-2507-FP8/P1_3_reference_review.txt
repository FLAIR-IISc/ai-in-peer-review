

# Peer Review: "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates the detection of compositionality in compound nouns using distributional semantics. The authors propose an Anchored Packed Trees (APT) approach that incorporates syntactic structure through typed dependency paths to improve compositional modeling. They evaluate their method on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality.

The main contributions of the paper, ordered by strength, are:

1. **Demonstration that syntactic structure significantly improves compositionality detection**: The authors show that using typed dependency paths (rather than untyped co-occurrence features) substantially improves performance on compositionality detection, achieving a Spearman correlation of 0.79 with human judgments (compared to 0.71 in Reddy et al., 2011).

2. **Systematic exploration of composition operations**: The paper provides a thorough comparison of different composition operations (intersection vs. union) and alignment strategies (aligned vs. unaligned APTs), revealing that a hybrid approach combining both works best.

3. **Insight into the role of modifier vs. head in compound nouns**: The authors discover that for this task, the modifier is slightly more informative than the head (optimal parameters h and q in [0.3,0.5]), which provides valuable linguistic insight.

## Strengths

1. **Significant empirical advancement in compositionality detection**: The paper makes a clear and substantial improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71), which is a meaningful gain in the field. The authors provide thorough experimental validation using repeated 3-fold cross-validation and statistically significant comparisons. This is a substantial contribution to the field, as compositionality detection is a critical problem for compositional distributional semantics.

2. **Clear demonstration of the importance of syntactic structure**: The paper provides compelling evidence that syntactic structure (in the form of typed dependency paths) is crucial for accurate compositionality detection. This is a significant insight that challenges the common assumption that simpler, untyped methods might suffice. The detailed analysis in Table 2 showing how features align across different parts of speech is particularly convincing.

3. **Thorough exploration of composition strategies**: The paper goes beyond simply claiming their method works better by systematically comparing multiple composition strategies (aligned vs. unaligned, intersection vs. union, with different weighting parameters). This level of detail is valuable for the community to understand what aspects of compositionality detection matter most.

4. **Clear methodological contribution with implementation details**: The paper provides clear implementation details of the APT approach, including the reduction operation (equation 1) and how to handle different dependency paths. This makes the method reproducible and provides a concrete example of how to integrate syntactic structure into distributional models.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on noun compounds, which is a narrow domain. Many non-compositional phrases are not noun compounds (e.g., verb phrases like "kick the bucket" or adjectival phrases like "green with envy"). It's unclear how well the APT approach would generalize to other phrase types. The authors should address this limitation and discuss potential challenges for other phrase types.

2. **Insufficient comparison with recent compositional methods**: The paper compares against Reddy et al. (2011) but doesn't compare against more recent compositional models that might have been developed since 2011. For example, the paper mentions the lexical function model as being state-of-the-art in Dinu et al. (2013), but doesn't compare against it directly. Given that the field has advanced significantly since 2011, this is a notable gap.

3. **Lack of computational complexity analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of their APT approach compared to simpler methods like neural embeddings. Given that APT uses higher-order dependency paths, it's likely significantly more computationally expensive. This is an important consideration for practical applications that the authors should address.

4. **Underdeveloped theoretical justification**: While the paper provides strong empirical results, the theoretical justification for why typed dependency paths improve compositionality detection is somewhat superficial. The paper notes that "some knowledge of syntactic structure is crucial" but doesn't provide deeper linguistic or theoretical justification for why this is the case. A more thorough discussion of the linguistic motivation would strengthen the contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "the compositionality of a phrase should not be judged categorically, rather it should be viewed as existing on a continuum." However, the evaluation metric (Spearman correlation) treats compositionality as a continuous scale, which aligns with this claim. Could you elaborate on how your method handles the continuum aspect of compositionality in comparison to categorical approaches?

2. Your results show that unaligned APT composition outperforms aligned composition. This is somewhat counterintuitive given the theoretical motivation for alignment. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, particularly for the specific structure of noun compounds?

3. Given that many compound nouns can be parsed with multiple dependency relations (e.g., "graduate student" can be parsed as either NMOD or AMOD), how does your method handle ambiguity in the dependency relations observed in the corpus?

4. The paper states that "the use of compositional methods to detect compositionality may also lead to improved compositional methods." Could you elaborate on this potential feedback loop and provide any preliminary evidence or examples of how compositionality detection might improve compositionality?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a significant empirical contribution to the field of compositional distributional semantics. The demonstration that syntactic structure significantly improves compositionality detection is compelling and well-supported by the data. The systematic exploration of different composition strategies is particularly valuable for the community.

While the scope is limited to noun compounds, the insights gained about the importance of syntactic structure are broadly applicable. The paper provides clear evidence that traditional untyped approaches are insufficient for capturing the nuances of compositionality, and that typed dependency paths are a promising direction for future work.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scope limitations and provide more detailed discussion of the theoretical underpinnings of the approach.
