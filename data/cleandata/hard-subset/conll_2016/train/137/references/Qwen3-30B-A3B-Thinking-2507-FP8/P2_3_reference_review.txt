

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a fundamental challenge in distributional semantics: the confounding relationship between compositionality and phrase similarity evaluation. The authors propose a novel approach to compositionality detection using "Anchored Packed Trees" (APT), which encodes contextual features based on typed dependency paths. Their main contributions are:

1. A principled approach to compositional modeling using typed dependency paths (APT) with proper alignment before composition, significantly outperforming previous methods in compositionality detection (Spearman's ρ = 0.79 vs. previous state-of-the-art 0.71).
2. Demonstrating that compositionality detection serves as a valuable evaluation task for compositional models, independent of the confounding factor of compositionality itself.
3. Providing evidence that syntactic structure encoded in dependency paths is highly informative for compositionality detection, with the APT approach outperforming neural embeddings.

## Strengths

1. **Significant empirical improvement over prior work**: The paper demonstrates a clear, statistically significant improvement (0.79 vs. 0.71) in compositionality detection using their APT approach. This is a meaningful advancement in the field, as compositionality detection is a well-established benchmark task. The authors carefully control for experimental variables (using the same corpus and dataset as Reddy et al., 2011), making their results directly comparable and credible.

2. **Strong theoretical motivation for the APT approach**: The paper provides a compelling linguistic rationale for using typed dependency paths. The authors correctly identify that traditional compositional models fail because they don't properly account for syntactic structure, leading to mismatched vector spaces. Their analysis of Table 1 (showing how different parts of speech have different feature types) is particularly insightful and justifies their approach.

3. **Clear connection between compositionality detection and compositional model improvement**: The paper makes a valuable conceptual contribution by demonstrating how compositionality detection can serve as an evaluation metric that avoids the confounding variable of compositionality itself. This is a significant methodological contribution to the field, as most previous work has evaluated compositional models directly against human similarity judgments, which are confounded by compositionality.

4. **Well-designed experimental framework**: The authors conduct thorough parameter exploration (including α values for PPMI smoothing, composition operations, and alignment strategies), providing a comprehensive evaluation of their approach. Their use of 3-fold cross-validation and statistical significance testing adds rigor to their results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient justification for compositionality detection as the primary evaluation metric**: While the authors correctly identify that phrase similarity evaluation confounds compositionality, they don't provide a strong theoretical argument for why compositionality detection should be the primary evaluation metric for compositional models. The paper claims that compositionality detection "provides a way of evaluating compositional methods without confounding judgements of phrase similarity with judgements of compositionality," but doesn't demonstrate how this evaluation directly translates to better phrase similarity models.

2. **Limited generalizability of the approach**: The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds (90 examples), but compositionality is a broader phenomenon in language. The authors don't provide evidence that their approach would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun phrases beyond the limited examples they discuss). This limits the scope of their contribution.

3. **Lack of analysis on why APT outperforms neural embeddings**: The paper reports that APT significantly outperforms neural embeddings (0.79 vs. 0.74), but provides little insight into *why* this is the case. The authors mention that "dependency paths encoded in the typed contextual features are highly informative," but don't provide a detailed analysis of which specific dependency features contribute most to the improvement or how they differ from what neural embeddings capture.

4. **Overstated claims about improving compositional models**: The paper claims that "the use of compositional methods to detect compositionality may also lead to improved compositional methods," but provides no empirical evidence for this. The paper only shows that their compositional method works well for the compositionality detection task, not that it leads to better phrase similarity models. This is a significant overstatement that weakens the paper's main contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more detailed analysis of which specific dependency features (e.g., NMOD, AMOD, NSUBJ) contribute most to the improved performance of the APT approach? This would help clarify why typed dependency paths are so informative.

2. How would you expect your APT approach to perform on non-noun compound phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adverbial phrases)? Have you considered extending your method to other phrase types?

3. The paper claims that compositionality detection "may also lead to improved compositional methods." Could you provide a concrete example of how the insights from your compositionality detection approach might be applied to improve a compositional model for phrase similarity?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of distributional semantics by demonstrating a significant improvement in compositionality detection using a principled syntactic approach. The APT method is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and represents a clear advancement over previous work. However, the paper's claims about the broader implications for compositional modeling are overstated, and the generalizability of the approach is limited to noun compounds. The paper would be stronger if it provided more analysis of why the APT approach works better and how it could be applied beyond the specific task of compositionality detection.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to clarify the scope of the contribution and temper the overstatements about the broader implications for compositional modeling. The empirical results are strong and the method is sound, making this a valuable contribution to the field.
