- Addresses evaluation confound caused by non-compositional phrases and proposes Anchored Packed Trees (APT) with typed dependency paths to detect compositionality in compound nouns (evaluated on Reddy et al.'s 90-item dataset).

- Contribution: Proposes a theoretically grounded APT compositional approach that aligns vector spaces using dependency paths before composition.

- Contribution: Reports improved compositionality detection (Spearman’s ρ = 0.79) over prior state-of-the-art (0.714).

- Contribution: Shows typed dependency features are highly informative for detecting compositionality.

- Strength: Correctly highlights the conceptual importance of accounting for non-compositionality in distributional semantics and grounds distinctions in linguistic theory.

- Strength: Methodological innovation—APT handles different parts of speech in one vector space and avoids zero-vector issues in intersective composition.

- Strength: Strong experimental results and robustness across composition operations and parameter settings.

- Strength: Provides linguistic insight that modifiers are slightly more informative than heads (optimal h and q in [0.3,0.5]) and that dependency paths matter.

- Weakness: Lacks demonstration of practical application—does not show how improved detection benefits compositional phrase similarity models.

- Weakness: Insufficient comparison with recent syntactically-informed neural compositional models (e.g., Hashimoto et al. 2014).

- Weakness: Limited discussion of computational costs and scalability given very high feature dimensionality (~230k).

- Weakness: Overstates generality of compositionality detection since evaluation is limited to one small compound-noun dataset.

- Question: How would this compositionality detection be used concretely to improve compositional phrase similarity models?

- Question: What specific improvements to compositional models do the authors expect from using their detection approach?

- Question: How would the method perform on larger, more diverse datasets of non-compositional phrases (e.g., phrasal verbs, idioms)?

- Overall: Strong, theoretically motivated contribution with clear empirical gains, but weakened by limited practical demonstrations, missing comparisons to recent work, and scalability/generalization concerns.
