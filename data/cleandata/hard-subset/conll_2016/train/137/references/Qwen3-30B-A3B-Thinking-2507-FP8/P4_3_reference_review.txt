

# Review: "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the role of contextual features in compositionality detection for noun compounds. The authors propose an Anchored Packed Trees (APT) approach that incorporates higher-order dependency paths with typed contextual features. They evaluate their method on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. The APT approach with higher-order dependency paths significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on compositionality detection (0.79 vs. 0.71 Spearman correlation with human judgments).
2. The authors demonstrate that combining aligned and unaligned APT representations through a hybrid model yields optimal performance, suggesting both syntactically aligned and unaligned contextual features contribute to better compositionality detection.
3. They provide empirical evidence that typed dependency features (with syntactic structure) are substantially more informative for compositionality detection than untyped co-occurrence features.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Rigor and Empirical Evidence**: The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation with careful parameter tuning and cross-validation. The authors systematically explore different composition operations (aligned vs. unaligned, intersective vs. union), weighting schemes, and similarity calculation methods. Their results consistently show significant improvements over previous work (0.79 vs. 0.71), with statistical significance established through repeated 3-fold cross-validation. This represents a substantial empirical contribution to the field.

2. **Novelty in Contextual Feature Design**: The paper makes a meaningful contribution by applying higher-order dependency paths with typed contextual features specifically to compositionality detection. While Weeds et al. (2014) explored higher-order dependency paths, this paper demonstrates their specific utility for compositionality detection in a way that previous work did not. The APT approach with path alignment represents a technically sound extension of prior distributional models.

3. **Clear Demonstration of Syntactic Importance**: The paper provides compelling evidence that syntactic structure encoded in typed dependency paths is crucial for compositionality detection. The significant performance gap between their typed APT approach (0.79) and the previous state-of-the-art using untyped features (0.71) clearly demonstrates the value of incorporating syntactic information in distributional models for this specific task.

4. **Practical Implications**: The findings have direct implications for improving compositional methods in NLP. By demonstrating that compositionality detection can be significantly improved through better contextual representation, the paper suggests a path toward developing more effective compositional models that can better handle non-compositional phrases.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds, which represents a narrow domain of multi-word expressions. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't sufficiently justify why this specific case is sufficient to demonstrate broader applicability. Compositionality is a general phenomenon across various phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases), and the paper doesn't explore whether their approach generalizes to these other types.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Recent Syntactic Approaches**: The paper compares with Reddy et al. (2011) but doesn't directly compare with more recent syntactically-informed approaches to compositionality, such as those by Hashimoto et al. (2014) or Milajevs et al. (2014). This weakens the claim that their approach represents a significant advancement, as it doesn't demonstrate superiority over the most recent methods in the field.

3. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific components (e.g., the effect of higher-order paths vs. the effect of typed features). Without this, it's difficult to determine which aspects of their approach are most responsible for the performance gains.

4. **Parameter Sensitivity Unclear**: While the paper reports optimal parameters (h and q in [0.3,0.5]), it doesn't discuss how sensitive their results are to these parameter choices. This is important for reproducibility and understanding the robustness of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper demonstrates significant improvements for noun compounds but doesn't address whether the APT approach would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases). Can you provide evidence or discussion about the potential applicability of your approach to other phrase types?

2. The authors claim that "the use of syntactic structure is crucial in the fine-grained understanding of language," yet don't provide a detailed comparison with other syntactic approaches. How does your method compare with recent neural approaches that incorporate syntactic information (e.g., Hashimoto et al., 2014)?

3. The paper reports that unaligned APT models generally outperform aligned models, which seems counterintuitive given the authors' argument for alignment. Could you elaborate on why unaligned features might be more informative for compositionality detection?

4. The authors note that "many of the compound phrases are comparatively low frequency tokens in the corpus." How does this low frequency impact the APT approach, and what techniques could be used to mitigate this issue for even rarer phrases?
