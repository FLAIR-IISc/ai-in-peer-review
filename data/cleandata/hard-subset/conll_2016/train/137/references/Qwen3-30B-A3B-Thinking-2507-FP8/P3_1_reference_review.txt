

# Peer Review: "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary

This paper addresses the problem of detecting compositionality in noun compounds (e.g., "red herring" vs. "grad student") using distributional semantic methods. The authors propose an "Anchored Packed Trees" (APT) approach where contextual features are based on typed dependency paths rather than untyped co-occurrence counts. They evaluate their method on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality. The authors claim that their APT-based approach significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods (0.79 vs. 0.71 Spearman's correlation).

## Main Contributions

1. **Demonstrating that typed dependency paths significantly improve compositionality detection**: The paper provides strong empirical evidence that incorporating syntactic structure through typed dependency paths substantially improves compositionality detection, addressing a key limitation in previous work that used untyped contextual features.

2. **Systematic evaluation of different composition operations**: The paper offers a thorough comparison of various composition operations (aligned vs. unaligned, intersective vs. union) and identifies a hybrid approach that combines both as optimal, providing valuable practical guidance for future work.

3. **Insight into the relative importance of head vs. modifier**: The paper reveals that the modifier is slightly more informative than the head for determining compositionality, and that unaligned features are slightly more informative than aligned ones, challenging some conventional assumptions about compositional structure.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and problem statement**: The paper clearly articulates the importance of compositionality detection for evaluating compositional models, noting that ignoring non-compositional phrases leads to confounded evaluations of compositional methods. This addresses a genuine limitation in current research practices that has not been sufficiently addressed in the literature.

2. **Rigorous experimental evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation with careful parameter selection, 3-fold cross-validation, and appropriate statistical significance testing. The comparison with multiple baselines (including the previous state-of-the-art) is well-executed and provides a clear benchmark for future work.

3. **Significant performance improvement**: The results are impressive, with the APT-based approach achieving 0.79 Spearman's correlation, a substantial improvement over the previous state-of-the-art of 0.71. This is a meaningful advancement that is likely to be impactful in the field of distributional semantics.

4. **Clear methodological explanation**: The APT approach is well-explained with concrete examples (Table 1), and the authors provide a detailed account of how the method works, including the offsetting process and composition operations. The paper is accessible to readers familiar with distributional semantics.

5. **Insightful analysis of results**: Beyond just reporting results, the authors provide valuable analysis of their findings, including the optimal parameter values (h and q in the range [0.3,0.5]) and the relative importance of different components of the model.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on noun compounds (90 compound nouns from the Reddy et al. dataset). This is a significant limitation because the broader claim is that syntactic structure is crucial for fine-grained semantic understanding, yet the authors provide no evidence that their approach generalizes to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun phrases, or multi-word expressions with different syntactic structures). Without this, the broader claim is unsubstantiated.

2. **Lack of comparison with recent neural compositional models**: The paper compares with Reddy et al. (2011) and some basic compositional models, but doesn't compare with more recent neural compositional models that have been proposed in the literature (e.g., Hashimoto et al., 2014; Milajevs et al., 2014). Given that the paper uses neural word embeddings as a baseline, it's surprising that it doesn't compare with more recent neural compositional approaches that have achieved state-of-the-art results.

3. **Insufficient explanation of why APT works better**: While the paper demonstrates that APT works better, it doesn't provide a clear linguistic or computational explanation for why it works better. The finding that unaligned features are slightly more informative than aligned ones is counterintuitive and merits more discussion. The authors should provide a stronger theoretical justification for their method.

4. **Potential overfitting to the specific dataset and corpus**: The paper uses the same ukWaC corpus as Reddy et al. (2011), so it's possible the APT method is simply better at leveraging the specific characteristics of this corpus rather than being a general improvement. The paper doesn't evaluate on other corpora or datasets to demonstrate the generalizability of their approach.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of different components of the APT method. For example, it's not clear how much of the improvement comes from using typed dependency paths vs. the specific composition operations used. This makes it difficult to understand exactly what makes the APT method successful.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds. How would you expect your APT approach to generalize to other types of phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun phrases, or multi-word expressions with different syntactic structures)? Have you considered testing on a more diverse set of phrases?

2. The paper doesn't compare with more recent neural compositional models (e.g., Hashimoto et al., 2014; Milajevs et al., 2014). Why was this comparison not included, and how do you expect your APT approach to compare with these more recent methods?

3. The paper finds that unaligned features are slightly more informative than aligned ones, which is somewhat counterintuitive. Can you provide a linguistic or computational explanation for this finding? Is it specific to the noun compound task, or would this hold for other composition tasks?

4. The paper uses the same corpus (ukWaC) as Reddy et al. (2011). Have you evaluated your method on other corpora to demonstrate its generalizability beyond this specific corpus?

5. The paper mentions that "the hybrid method" (using a combination of aligned and unaligned features) works best. However, it's not clear how this hybrid method is different from simply using unaligned features, which the paper shows outperforms aligned features. Could you clarify the rationale for the hybrid method and why it's better than just using unaligned features?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that incorporating syntactic structure through typed dependency paths substantially improves compositionality detection. The method is well-motivated, the evaluation is rigorous, and the results are impressive. However, the limited scope of evaluation and lack of comparison with more recent methods are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. If the authors can provide evidence that their approach generalizes beyond noun compounds and compare with more recent methods, this would be a strong paper for CoNLL. As it stands, the paper has substantial merit but requires addressing these limitations to fully realize its potential impact.

**Recommendation: Weak Accept** (with the understanding that the authors will address the weaknesses in their revision)
