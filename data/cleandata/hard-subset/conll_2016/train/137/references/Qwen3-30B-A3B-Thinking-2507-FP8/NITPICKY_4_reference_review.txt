

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary
This paper investigates the use of compositional methods to improve detection of non-compositional phrases, specifically focusing on noun compounds. The authors propose an Anchored Packed Trees (APT) approach that uses typed dependency paths to better capture compositional structure, and they evaluate their method on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for compositionality detection. The paper compares their APT-based models with neural word embeddings and finds that their hybrid APT approach (combining aligned and unaligned representations) achieves the best results (Spearman's ρ = 0.79), outperforming previous state-of-the-art (ρ = 0.71).

## Main Contributions
1. **An integrated compositional distributional approach** using typed dependency paths that maintains syntactic structure within contextual features, which proves more effective than untyped representations for compositionality detection.
2. **A novel hybrid composition method** that combines aligned and unaligned APT representations, demonstrating that both types of features contribute to better compositionality prediction.
3. **Empirical evidence** that dependency-based features are more informative for compositionality detection than untyped co-occurrence vectors, even when using state-of-the-art neural word embeddings.

## Strengths
1. **Significant methodological contribution**: The APT approach with typed dependency paths offers a more linguistically motivated way to handle compositionality than previous methods. The paper demonstrates that syntactic structure is crucial for fine-grained compositional understanding, which is a meaningful contribution to the field.
2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper consistently outperforms previous state-of-the-art by a statistically significant margin (0.79 vs. 0.71), and the results are robust across different parameter settings. The use of cross-validation and statistical significance testing adds credibility to the findings.
3. **Clear evaluation framework**: The paper uses the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset and evaluation method consistently, allowing for direct comparison with previous work. The inclusion of appropriate baselines (RAND, FREQ, REDDY) strengthens the evaluation.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical formatting errors in references**: The reference list contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that suggest poor attention to detail. Entries like "Baroni and Lenci.,? \Q2010\E" and "Grefenstette.,? 1994" indicate serious issues with reference management, which is unacceptable for a scholarly publication. This undermines the paper's credibility and suggests the authors may not have carefully proofread their submission.

2. **Inconsistent terminology and minor typos**: The paper uses "naı̈ve" (with an umlaut) instead of the standard "naive" multiple times (e.g., "naı̈ve compositional operations"), which is a distracting error. Additionally, there's a typo in "siginificantly" (should be "significantly") and inconsistent use of "compositionality" vs. "compositional" in some contexts. These minor errors, while seemingly small, reflect a lack of careful proofreading.

3. **Ambiguous methodological details**: The paper claims "weighted addition" works best but doesn't sufficiently explain how the weight parameter (h) was determined or why it consistently falls in the [0.3, 0.5] range. The description of "offsetting" in Section 3 (Table 2) is unclear without a full example of how δ is applied. The equation for reduction (Equation 1) contains a typo ("τ = τ1 r r τ2" should likely be "τ = τ1 r τ2" or "τ = τ1 r r τ2" if r r is a specific notation), which could confuse readers.

4. **Lack of sufficient explanation for key claims**: The paper states that "shifting PMI tended to have a strong negative effect on results" but provides no supporting evidence (e.g., no table or data showing this negative effect). This claim is presented as a fact without justification, which weakens the paper's credibility.

5. **Inconsistent definition of "normalised counts"**: In Equation 2, the authors define p(w', τ | w) as a conditional probability, but refer to it as "normalised counts" in the text, creating confusion. The paper doesn't clarify whether this is a standard normalisation or a specific approach they've developed.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you clarify the exact meaning of "normalised counts" used in Equation 2? Is this a standard normalisation technique or a novel approach?
2. How was the weight parameter h determined for the weighted addition composition? The paper states that optimal values consistently fall in [0.3, 0.5], but doesn't explain how this range was identified or why it's consistent across experiments.
3. Could you provide more detail on why shifting PMI had a negative effect? The paper states this as a fact but doesn't provide any supporting data or analysis.
4. Could you clarify the typo in Equation 1 ("τ = τ1 r r τ2" vs. "τ = τ1 r τ2" or "τ = τ1 r r τ2")? What does "r r" represent in the equation?

## Overall Assessment
While this paper presents a methodologically sound approach that significantly advances the state-of-the-art in compositionality detection, the numerous formatting errors, minor typos, and unclear methodological details are unacceptable for a scholarly publication. As a perfectionist reviewer, I believe these minor flaws overshadow the paper's contributions. The reference formatting errors alone are sufficient to warrant rejection, as they indicate a lack of attention to detail that is expected in top-tier NLP publications. The paper's contribution is significant, but the execution is marred by these avoidable errors that undermine the authors' credibility.

I recommend rejection due to the numerous minor but critical formatting and presentation issues that indicate a lack of careful proofreading and attention to detail. The paper would benefit from a thorough revision to address these issues before being considered for publication.
