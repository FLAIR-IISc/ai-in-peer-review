

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary
This paper proposes an "Anchored Packed Trees" (APT) method for compositional distributional semantics, specifically targeting the detection of compositionality in noun compounds. The authors claim their method improves upon previous approaches by using typed dependency paths with alignment before composition. They evaluate their approach on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for compositionality detection, reporting a Spearman correlation of 0.79 with human judgments, compared to the previous state-of-the-art of 0.71.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A method for compositional distributional semantics using typed dependency paths with alignment (APT)
2. Evaluation of compositional methods on the Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality detection task
3. Demonstration that dependency-based approaches outperform simpler vector composition methods on this specific task

## Strengths
1. The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the problem of compositionality in language, which is an important and well-recognized challenge in distributional semantics.

2. The authors have conducted a thorough evaluation on the established Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, which provides a consistent benchmark for compositionality detection. The detailed parameter analysis (including different PPMI smoothing parameters, composition operations, and alignment strategies) enhances the paper's reproducibility.

3. The paper provides a thoughtful discussion of the relationship between compositionality detection and phrase similarity evaluation, noting that the latter conflates two distinct linguistic phenomena (compositionality and similarity), which is a valuable insight.

## Weaknesses
1. **Marginal improvements with questionable significance**: The paper reports a 0.08 improvement in Spearman correlation (0.79 vs. 0.71) over Reddy et al. (2011). Given the small size of the dataset (90 compound nouns), this marginal improvement is unlikely to be statistically significant. The authors note that "any difference observed which is greater than 0.005 is statistically significant," but they don't provide sufficient evidence that the 0.08 difference is meaningful in practice. This represents a classic case of incremental improvement without substantial validation.

2. **Lack of comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper relies on Reddy et al. (2011) as the baseline, which is now over a decade old. The field has seen significant advances in compositional distributional semantics since then, particularly with neural approaches. The authors do compare against word2vec (which is also now outdated), but they fail to compare against more recent compositional methods (e.g., tensor-based approaches or neural compositional models) that have been shown to outperform simple vector addition on phrase similarity tasks.

3. **Underestimated complexity of the proposed method**: The APT method requires dependency parsing, careful handling of dependency paths, and multiple composition operations with alignment. The paper doesn't adequately address the computational cost or practical applicability of this approach compared to simpler methods. The marginal performance gain doesn't justify the increased complexity, especially since the authors acknowledge that unaligned APT performs nearly as well as aligned APT (0.78 vs. 0.75), suggesting the alignment step may be largely unnecessary.

4. **Superficial explanation of the key innovation**: The paper claims the "alignment" of vector spaces is a key contribution, but the explanation is superficial. The authors note that "the second observation that we make based on Table 1 is that the 2nd order features of, say, adjectives, correspond to the 1st order features of nouns" but don't provide sufficient theoretical justification for why this alignment should improve compositionality detection. This appears to be more of a technical detail than a fundamental conceptual advance.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of the APT method. For example, it's unclear whether the improvement comes from the use of typed dependency paths, the alignment process, or the hybrid composition approach. Without these studies, it's difficult to determine what specific aspect of the method is driving the marginal improvement.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims that the hybrid method (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) provides the best performance, but the improvement over unaligned APT is marginal (0.79 vs. 0.78). Can the authors provide more evidence that this difference is statistically significant given the small dataset size?

2. Given that the unaligned APT method performs almost as well as the hybrid method, what is the theoretical justification for the alignment step? Is it possible that the alignment process is introducing noise rather than signal?

3. The paper doesn't compare against more recent compositional methods (e.g., those based on neural tensor networks or contextualized embeddings). How would your method compare against these state-of-the-art approaches on this specific task?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a marginal improvement over an older baseline on a narrow task. While the evaluation is thorough for the specific dataset, the paper lacks sufficient validation against more recent work and fails to demonstrate that the proposed method offers meaningful advantages over simpler, more established approaches. The field would benefit from more substantial advances rather than incremental improvements with marginal gains.

I recommend rejection. The paper does not present a sufficiently novel or impactful contribution to warrant publication in CONLL. The method proposed is not sufficiently validated against contemporary baselines, and the marginal performance gain does not justify the increased complexity of the approach. The field would be better served by work that addresses the limitations of compositionality detection in a more substantial way, rather than incremental improvements to a specific method.
