- Introduces an APT-based compositional method that preserves syntactic structure via typed higher-order dependency paths.  
- Shows the APT approach significantly outperforms prior state-of-the-art on compositionality detection (Spearman 0.79 vs 0.714).  
- Finds a hybrid of aligned and unaligned APT representations yields the best results.  

- Correctly frames compositionality detection as distinct from phrase similarity and provides a principled alignment mechanism for incompatible contextual features.  
- Reports strong empirical gains and thoroughly explores composition operations, weighting, and hybridization.  
- Offers a clear explanation that dependency-typed contextual features drive improved compositionality detection.  

- Experiments are limited to noun-noun compounds, so generalizability to other phrase types is untested.  
- Comparisons to neural models are outdated, lacking evaluation against contextualized embeddings like ELMo or BERT.  
- Possible evaluation bias from using the ukWaC corpus both for training and the Reddy et al. (2011) test set; cross-corpus validation is missing.  
- High-dimensional feature space (~230k dimensions) is reported without analysis of computational cost or scalability.  

- How would APT perform on non-noun phrase types (e.g., verb or adjective phrases)?  
- Can APT integrate with modern contextualized embeddings (ELMo, BERT) that capture syntax?  
- Can you demonstrate gains are not corpus-specific by evaluating on a different corpus?  

- Overall: theoretically well-grounded with strong empirical improvements, but limited by narrow scope, outdated baselines, and missing scalability/validation analyses.
