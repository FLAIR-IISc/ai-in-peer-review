

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach to detecting compositionality in noun compounds by using "Anchored Packed Trees" (APT) with typed higher-order dependency paths. The authors argue that existing compositional methods fail because they don't properly account for syntactic structure, and that compositionality detection should be used as an evaluation metric rather than phrase similarity. They demonstrate that their APT-based approach significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art results on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.

## Main Contributions
1. A fundamentally new approach to compositionality detection using typed higher-order dependency paths (APT) that integrates syntactic structure directly into compositional modeling
2. A novel evaluation framework that decouples compositionality detection from phrase similarity evaluation
3. Demonstrating that syntactic structure is crucial for fine-grained understanding of language, challenging the trend toward purely distributional approaches

## Strengths

1. **Novel theoretical contribution**: The paper makes a bold claim about the necessity of syntactic structure for compositional modeling, which is a significant departure from the current trend of neural approaches that largely ignore syntax. The authors' argument that "some knowledge of syntactic structure is crucial in the fine-grained understanding of language" represents a principled challenge to the field's direction. This theoretical stance is compelling and potentially paradigm-shifting.

2. **Significant performance improvement**: The authors demonstrate a clear and statistically significant improvement over previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71), with the hybrid APT model achieving the best results. This isn't just a marginal improvement but a substantial jump that suggests their approach addresses a fundamental limitation in the field.

3. **Elegant evaluation framework**: The paper's core insight—that compositionality detection should be used as an evaluation metric rather than phrase similarity—represents a clever methodological innovation. By decoupling compositionality from similarity evaluation, they avoid the confounding factor that has plagued previous work. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could reshape how compositionality is evaluated.

4. **Theoretical justification for the approach**: The paper provides a clear explanation of why their method works (the alignment of vector spaces based on dependency paths), which goes beyond just empirical results. The demonstration that both aligned and unaligned representations contribute to better performance offers deeper theoretical insights into how compositionality works.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper evaluates exclusively on the Reddy et al. (2011) noun compound dataset (90 examples), which is a very narrow scope. While the authors acknowledge this as a limitation, the paper doesn't demonstrate how their approach might generalize to other phrase types or larger datasets. This significantly limits the perceived impact of the work.

2. **Insufficient comparison with recent methods**: The paper compares primarily to the Reddy et al. (2011) baseline and some traditional methods, but doesn't compare against more recent compositional approaches that have emerged since 2011. For example, they don't discuss how their method compares to more recent neural compositional models that incorporate syntactic information.

3. **Lack of theoretical discussion about why APT works**: While the paper explains how APT works, it doesn't sufficiently address why it works better than other approaches. The authors claim that "the dependency paths encoded in the typed contextual features are highly informative," but don't provide a deeper theoretical explanation for why this is the case. This limits the broader applicability of their insights.

4. **Computational complexity concerns**: The paper mentions high-dimensional vector spaces (80,000-230,000 dimensions) but doesn't address the computational costs or scalability of their approach. For a method that could potentially be applied to larger-scale NLP tasks, this is a significant oversight.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do you expect your APT approach to scale to larger datasets and more complex phrase structures beyond noun compounds?
2. Can you provide more insight into why both aligned and unaligned representations contribute to better performance? Is there a linguistic explanation for this phenomenon?
3. How would your approach handle cases where the dependency structure is ambiguous or where multiple dependency relations are possible for the same compound?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a bold and novel approach to a fundamental problem in compositional semantics. While the evaluation scope is limited, the theoretical contribution and significant performance improvement make this work highly valuable. The paper's core insight about the necessity of syntactic structure for compositionality detection represents a meaningful challenge to current trends in the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scope limitations and provide more thorough comparisons with recent methods. This work offers a promising new direction for compositional semantics that deserves to be highlighted at CoNLL.
