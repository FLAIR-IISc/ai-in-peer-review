

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach to detecting non-compositional phrases (like "red herring") using a compositional framework called Anchored Packed Trees (APTs). The authors argue that traditional methods for phrase composition fail because they don't properly account for syntactic structure, and propose a method where contextual features are based on typed dependency paths with proper alignment before composition. They evaluate their approach on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality.

## Primary Contributions
1. A fundamentally new framework for compositionality detection that integrates syntactic structure through typed dependency paths with proper alignment
2. A clear theoretical distinction between compositionality detection (whether a phrase's meaning can be derived from its parts) and phrase similarity evaluation
3. Strong empirical results demonstrating significant improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods

## Strengths

**1. Novel and Bold Theoretical Framework** (Primary Strength)
The paper's most significant contribution is its bold theoretical approach to compositionality. The authors challenge the common practice of treating composition as a simple vector operation and instead propose a framework where syntactic structure is fundamental to the compositional process. This isn't just another tweak to existing methods - it's a complete rethinking of how composition should work in distributional semantics. The APT approach with typed dependency paths and proper alignment before composition represents a genuinely novel direction that could reshape how we think about phrase representation.

**2. Clear Separation of Compositionality from Similarity** (Primary Strength)
The paper makes a crucial theoretical distinction between compositionality detection (whether a phrase's meaning can be derived from its parts) and phrase similarity evaluation. This is a major conceptual contribution that has been overlooked in the field. Previous work often confounded these two concepts, making it difficult to properly evaluate compositional methods. By establishing compositionality detection as a separate task, the authors provide a much cleaner evaluation framework that avoids the confounding factors that have plagued previous phrase similarity evaluations.

**3. Substantial Performance Gains** (Primary Strength)
The empirical results are compelling. The authors achieve a Spearman correlation of 0.79 with human judgments, significantly outperforming the previous state-of-the-art of 0.71. This isn't just a minor improvement - it's a substantial gain that validates their theoretical approach. The fact that their method works so well on this challenging task provides strong evidence for the value of their novel framework.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited Scope of Evaluation** (Primary Weakness)
While the results on the Reddy dataset are impressive, the paper doesn't evaluate the approach on other types of phrases or on other tasks. The focus is narrow on noun compounds, which limits the generalizability of the findings. A truly impactful contribution would demonstrate broader applicability beyond this specific dataset.

**2. Insufficient Comparison with Structured Approaches** (Secondary Weakness)
The paper mentions that the lexical function model (Baroni and Zamparelli, 2010) was the best-performing model in Dinu et al. (2013), but doesn't directly compare their APT approach to this or other structured compositional methods. This makes it difficult to assess the true novelty of their contribution relative to existing structured approaches.

**3. Limited Theoretical Justification** (Secondary Weakness)
While the paper presents the APT method and shows it works, it doesn't provide a deep theoretical justification for why syntactically-structured composition should be better for compositionality detection. A stronger theoretical foundation would have made the contribution even more compelling.

## Recommendation
I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper introduces a genuinely novel framework for compositionality detection that represents a bold departure from current practices in distributional semantics. The theoretical distinction between compositionality detection and phrase similarity evaluation is significant and well-executed. The performance gains over previous state-of-the-art methods are substantial and clearly demonstrate the value of the authors' approach.

While the scope is somewhat limited to noun compounds, the paper's theoretical contribution and strong empirical results outweigh this limitation. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field by proposing a fundamentally different way to approach compositionality in distributional semantics, and I believe this work will inspire future research in the field.

The paper demonstrates exactly what I value most as a reviewer: a novel, bold approach that advances the field in a meaningful way, even if the methodology isn't perfect. The authors have made a clear theoretical contribution that is well-validated by their results, and I believe this paper will be an important reference for future work in compositional distributional semantics.
