

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates the relationship between compositional methods and the detection of non-compositional phrases in distributional semantic models. The authors propose an improved method for evaluating compositionality using "anchored packed trees" (APTs) with typed dependency paths, which they argue better captures the syntactic structure of phrases than previous untyped approaches. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. A novel compositional method (APT) that integrates syntactic structure through typed dependency paths, significantly improving compositionality detection performance over previous methods.
2. A thorough empirical demonstration that the APT approach achieves state-of-the-art results (Spearman's ρ = 0.79) on Reddy et al.'s (2011) compositionality dataset.
3. The insight that a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned APT composition captures complementary aspects of phrase compositionality.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Performance Improvement**: The authors achieve a substantial improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71), which is a meaningful contribution to the field. This improvement is statistically significant across multiple parameter settings and composition operations, demonstrating the robustness of their approach. The paper clearly establishes that their method outperforms both traditional untyped models and neural word embeddings.

2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation**: The APT approach is well-motivated by linguistic considerations about the role of syntactic structure in composition. The paper provides a clear explanation of how typed dependency paths capture the relationship between phrase components, and the alignment process for composition is theoretically sound. This addresses a key limitation in previous work where untyped features led to poor compositionality.

3. **Important Problem Focus**: The paper correctly identifies a critical gap in the field: that existing evaluations of compositional models conflate compositionality with similarity judgment, making it difficult to assess compositional methods fairly. By using compositionality detection as a proxy for evaluating compositional methods, the authors provide a clearer pathway for methodological improvement.

4. **Thorough Experimental Analysis**: The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation across multiple parameter settings (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, alignment strategies) and provide detailed analysis of the results. The consistent finding that hybrid composition (combining aligned and unaligned features) works best is particularly insightful and adds depth to the contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Generalizability**: The paper's focus is exclusively on noun compounds, which limits the broader applicability of the findings. The authors do not adequately address whether their approach would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adverbial phrases), which is a significant limitation for a method claiming to improve compositional understanding.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Recent Methods**: The paper compares primarily with Reddy et al. (2011) and earlier work, but fails to engage with more recent compositional models (e.g., tensor-based approaches, neural composition methods) that might have achieved better results on this task. This weakens the claim of being state-of-the-art.

3. **Lack of Analysis on Computational Complexity**: The paper mentions the large feature space (230,000 dimensions), but doesn't analyze the computational costs of the APT approach compared to simpler methods. For a method that might be used in practical applications, this is a significant omission.

4. **Unclear Mechanism of Improvement**: While the paper demonstrates that APTs improve performance, it doesn't sufficiently explain *why* the typed dependency paths are so effective. A deeper analysis of which specific dependency paths contribute most to the improved performance would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

5. **Correlation vs. Causation**: The paper establishes correlation between their method and improved compositionality detection, but doesn't provide evidence that the improvement is directly due to the syntactic structure integration rather than other factors (e.g., corpus size, specific parameter tuning).

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you expect your APT approach to generalize to other phrase types beyond noun compounds? Have you considered applying your method to datasets with different phrase structures?

2. Can you provide more detailed analysis of the specific dependency paths that contribute most to the improved performance? Are certain types of relations (e.g., NMOD, AMOD) more informative than others?

3. How does the computational complexity of your approach compare to simpler compositional methods, particularly for larger corpora or real-time applications?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed study that makes a clear contribution to understanding compositionality in distributional semantic models. The authors successfully demonstrate that integrating syntactic structure through typed dependency paths significantly improves compositionality detection performance. The paper's main strengths are its strong empirical results, clear theoretical foundation, and important focus on a key limitation in current compositional evaluation methods.

However, the paper's narrow focus on noun compounds and lack of comparison with more recent compositional methods limit its broader impact. The authors should address these limitations in a revised version by expanding their analysis to other phrase types and engaging with more recent work in the field.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field, and with the suggested improvements, would be a strong candidate for publication. The results are robust and the method is well-justified, but the authors should strengthen the generalizability claims and provide more detailed analysis of why their approach works better.

Decision: **Weak Accept** (with significant revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above)
