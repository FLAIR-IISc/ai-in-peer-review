

# Review: "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary
This paper addresses a critical problem in distributional semantics: the failure of compositional methods to account for non-compositional phrases (e.g., "red herring"). The authors propose an improved approach using "Anchored Packed Trees" (APTs), which encode typed higher-order dependency paths to better model compositionality. They evaluate their method on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality, demonstrating that their approach significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel compositional method using typed higher-order dependency paths (APTs) that properly aligns vector spaces before composition.
2. A new evaluation framework for compositionality detection that directly measures how well compositional models predict observed phrase representations, avoiding the confound of phrase similarity judgments.
3. Empirical evidence showing a significant improvement over previous state-of-the-art (0.79 Spearman correlation vs. 0.71).

## Strengths

1. **Conceptual contribution to evaluation methodology**: The paper correctly identifies a fundamental flaw in how compositional methods have been evaluated in the past. Previous work has conflated compositionality with phrase similarity judgments, but the authors demonstrate that compositionality detection should be evaluated as a separate task. Their framework, where compositionality is measured by how well a compositional model predicts the observed phrase representation, is a significant methodological contribution that clarifies the research direction in this field.

2. **Strong empirical results with statistical significance**: The authors achieve a substantial improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71), with results that are consistently better across multiple configurations of their APT model. The paper reports statistically significant improvements (with differences greater than 0.005), and the authors provide sufficient details about their experimental setup (including 3-fold cross-validation) to support the reliability of their results.

3. **Theoretical grounding and linguistic motivation**: The paper builds on solid linguistic foundations, correctly noting that compositionality exists on a continuum rather than as a binary property. The APT approach is well-motivated by the observation that syntactic structure (dependency paths) is crucial for compositionality. The authors provide clear examples (Table 1) showing how different parts of speech have different dependency patterns, justifying the need for their alignment approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on noun compounds (90 phrases from Reddy et al., 2011), which limits the broader applicability of their approach. While noun compounds are a common test case, the field has seen significant progress on other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases). Without evidence that the APT approach generalizes beyond noun compounds, the impact of the work remains somewhat constrained.

2. **Lack of comparison to recent compositional methods**: The paper compares to Reddy et al. (2011) but fails to compare to more recent compositional methods that have emerged since then, such as those by Berant and Liang (2014) or Hashimoto et al. (2014). The authors mention these works in related work but don't evaluate against them, making it difficult to assess whether their improvement is truly significant or merely a result of parameter optimization on an outdated baseline.

3. **Insufficient implementation details for reproducibility**: While the paper provides some details about their APT construction, it lacks sufficient information to reproduce their results. For example, it's unclear how they handled ambiguous compound phrases (e.g., when a compound can be parsed in multiple ways) or exactly how they processed the ukWaC corpus to create their APT lexicon. This limits the practical impact of their work.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds. Could you elaborate on why you chose this specific phrase type as the focus of your evaluation, and whether you have plans to extend your approach to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases, or multi-word expressions beyond noun compounds)?

2. The paper mentions that "the use of compositional methods to detect compositionality may also lead to improved compositional methods" (Section 7). Could you provide more specific examples of how this might work in practice, or preliminary results showing that compositionality detection could improve general compositional methods?

3. Your results show that the unaligned APT model outperforms the aligned model. However, the paper's theoretical motivation suggests alignment should be beneficial. Could you provide more insight into why unaligned features might be more informative for this specific task, and whether this finding generalizes to other compositional tasks?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field by addressing a fundamental methodological issue in evaluating compositional methods. The APT approach is well-motivated, the evaluation framework is conceptually sound, and the empirical results are compelling. While the limited scope of evaluation and lack of comparison to recent methods are notable weaknesses, these could be addressed in future work. The paper provides a solid foundation for future research on compositionality detection and could significantly influence how the field evaluates compositional methods. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the reproducibility concerns and expand the discussion of the scope of their approach.
