

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of detecting non-compositional phrases (where the meaning of the phrase cannot be derived from the meanings of its parts) using distributional semantic methods. The authors build on Reddy et al.'s (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality, proposing a novel approach using "Anchored Packed Trees" (APTs) that encode typed higher-order dependency paths. Their key insight is that compositional methods need to properly align vector spaces before composition to account for syntactic structure.

The main contributions of this work, ordered by strength, are:

1. **A novel compositional framework (APT) that incorporates syntactic structure into distributional representations**: The authors propose a method where distributional features encode complete dependency paths from target words to context words, with a mechanism for aligning vector spaces before composition based on syntactic structure.

2. **Significant empirical improvement over previous state-of-the-art**: Their method achieves a Spearman correlation of 0.79 with human judgments, substantially exceeding the previous best result of 0.71 reported by Reddy et al. (2011).

3. **Insight into the role of syntactic structure in compositionality detection**: The paper demonstrates that typed dependency paths encoded in APT representations are highly informative for determining compositionality, and that both aligned and unaligned composition approaches contribute to the best results.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results with clear improvement**: The paper presents a significant and statistically significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71). The authors provide thorough experimental analysis across multiple configurations (aligned vs. unaligned composition, different weighting schemes, different similarity measures), and all variants of their APT approach consistently outperform previous methods. The use of 3-fold cross-validation and statistical significance testing (with error margins ≤ 0.0015) adds credibility to their claims.

2. **Strong theoretical foundation and linguistic motivation**: The paper provides a clear and compelling explanation for why syntactic structure matters in compositionality detection. The authors effectively demonstrate through Table 1 and Table 2 how different parts of speech have different contextual features that need to be aligned before composition. The insight that "the 2nd order features of, say, adjectives, correspond to the 1st order features of nouns" is particularly strong and well-justified.

3. **Careful experimental design and reproducibility**: The authors meticulously replicate the experimental setup from Reddy et al. (2011) for direct comparison, and they provide sufficient detail about their corpus preprocessing, parameter settings, and evaluation metrics. The paper clearly explains why they chose specific parameter values (e.g., α=0.75 for PPMI smoothing) and how they handled potential issues (e.g., compound noun parsing).

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on 90 compound nouns from Reddy et al.'s dataset. While this is a well-established benchmark, it's a very narrow scope that doesn't reflect the full diversity of non-compositional phrases in language (e.g., idioms, phrasal verbs, multiword expressions with different syntactic structures). The paper doesn't address whether their approach would generalize to other types of non-compositional phrases or larger-scale applications.

2. **Lack of comparison with more recent methods**: The paper references work up to 2014, but doesn't compare with more recent advances in compositional distributional semantics (e.g., transformer-based models, more sophisticated neural composition methods). For example, the authors mention "neural word embeddings" but don't compare with state-of-the-art neural approaches to compositionality that have emerged since 2014. This limits the significance of their claimed improvement over "previous state-of-the-art."

3. **Insufficient analysis of why their approach works better**: While the paper demonstrates that their approach works better, it doesn't provide a deep analysis of *why* it works better. For instance, it doesn't analyze which specific dependency paths or types are most informative, nor does it provide a detailed explanation of how the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned composition) specifically improves results. The paper states that "the unaligned model outperforms the aligned model" but doesn't explain why this is the case in linguistic terms.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper focuses exclusively on compound nouns (e.g., "grad student," "spelling bee"). How would your APT approach generalize to other types of non-compositional phrases, such as idiomatic expressions (e.g., "kick the bucket") or phrasal verbs (e.g., "give up")? Would you need to modify your approach for these different phrase types?

2. Your paper mentions that "the majority of occurrences where no dependency relationship was observed, this was due to the parser incorrectly parsing a three noun compound phrase." How would your approach handle cases where the parser makes errors in dependency parsing, and what impact would this have on your results?

3. The paper reports that optimal values of h and q were in the range [0.3,0.5], suggesting the modifier is slightly more informative than the head. Could you provide more detailed analysis of which specific dependency paths (e.g., NMOD, AMOD) contribute most to the compositionality scores, and why?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written, technically sound paper that makes a clear contribution to the field of compositional distributional semantics. The authors present a novel approach that significantly outperforms previous methods on a standard benchmark, with strong theoretical justification for their method. While the scope of evaluation is limited to compound nouns, the insights about the importance of syntactic structure in compositionality detection are valuable and could inform future work in the field.

The paper would be strengthened by a broader evaluation scope, comparison with more recent methods, and deeper analysis of why the approach works better. However, as it stands, the paper makes a solid contribution to the field that would be of interest to the CONLL community.

**Recommendation: Accept**
