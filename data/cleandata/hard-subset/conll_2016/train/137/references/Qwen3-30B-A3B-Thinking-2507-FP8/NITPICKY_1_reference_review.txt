

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper proposes an "Anchored Packed Tree" (APT) approach to compositional distributional semantics for detecting compositionality in noun compounds. The authors argue that maintaining syntactic structure in contextual features through typed dependency paths improves compositionality detection, addressing the confusion between phrase similarity and compositionality in evaluation. 

My assessment of the main contributions (in order of strength):
1. The APT approach to compositional distributional semantics, which maintains syntactic structure in contextual features
2. The demonstration that typed dependency paths significantly outperform untyped co-occurrence features for compositionality detection
3. The hybrid composition method (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) that achieves the best results

## Strengths
1. The paper identifies an important gap in the field: the conflation of phrase similarity and compositionality in evaluation, which has led to misleading conclusions about compositional models' effectiveness.

2. The theoretical motivation for the APT approach is sound, particularly the observation that different parts of speech require different alignment strategies, and the insight that higher-order dependency paths can be systematically aligned.

3. The experimental design is thorough, comparing multiple variants of the APT approach and neural embeddings with careful parameter tuning and cross-validation.

## Weaknesses
1. **Reference formatting inconsistencies**: The bibliography contains multiple formatting errors that undermine professionalism. For example, "Baroni and Lenci.,? \Q2010\E" and "Weeds et al.,? 2014" use non-standard citation formats with \Q and \E markers. The reference list is riddled with inconsistent punctuation, missing years, and strange character sequences that suggest poor manuscript preparation.

2. **Unverified claims of significant improvement**: The paper states "all of the results using standard PPMI (α = 1) and smoothed PPMI (α = 0.75) significantly outperform the result reported in Reddy et al. (2011)" but fails to provide Reddy et al.'s exact correlation coefficient for comparison. The Reddy et al. (2011) result is cited as "0.714" in the paper but listed as "REDdy 0.71" in Table 5, creating confusion. Without the exact number from Reddy et al., the claim of "significant outperformance" cannot be verified.

3. **Undefined terminology**: The paper states "We have also considered composition without alignment of the modifier's APT, i.e, using A1" but never defines what A1 is. This is critical for reproducibility, as A1 is used in equations (6) and (7) without explanation. The paper refers to "A1" as if it's defined earlier, but it's not properly introduced.

4. **Critical technical inconsistency**: The paper claims "the elementary representation for the constituent of a compound phrase will not contain any of the contextual features associated with the compound phrase token unless they occurred with the constituent in some other context." However, they also state that "we identified 1,236,264 occurrences of the candidate lemmas occurring contiguously (in the correct order)" and "modified to include the new compound token." This creates a contradiction—how can they exclude compound-specific features if they're modifying the corpus to include the compound as a single token?

5. **Minor but pervasive typographical errors**: The paper contains multiple instances of "naı̈ve" (with incorrect diacritics) instead of "naive." These are minor but unprofessional errors that suggest carelessness in preparation.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please provide the exact correlation coefficient from Reddy et al. (2011) that is being compared against, so the claim of "significant outperformance" can be verified.

2. What is the precise definition of A1 in the unaligned composition method? Is it the elementary APT without any alignment, or something else?

3. How did you ensure that the elementary representations for constituents did not contain features from the compound phrase when you modified the corpus to include compound tokens as single lexical items?

## Overall Recommendation
While the paper presents a theoretically interesting approach to compositionality detection, the cumulative effect of these minor but persistent flaws—particularly the inconsistent reference formatting, unverified claims, and undefined terminology—significantly undermines the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer who prioritizes meticulous attention to detail, these issues outweigh the paper's conceptual contributions. The paper's claims cannot be properly evaluated due to these inconsistencies, which is unacceptable for a conference submission. I recommend rejection.
