

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of detecting non-compositional phrases (phrases whose meaning cannot be derived from the literal meanings of their parts) in distributional semantics. The authors propose a novel method called Anchored Packed Trees (APT) that uses typed higher-order dependency paths to better model compositionality. They evaluate their approach on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality, comparing different composition operations (aligned vs. unaligned, intersection vs. union) and parameter settings. The authors report a significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71 Spearman correlation).

## Main Contributions
1. A novel compositional method (APT) that explicitly incorporates syntactic structure through typed higher-order dependency paths
2. Comprehensive empirical evaluation of different composition operations on a standard compositionality detection benchmark
3. Significant improvement over previous state-of-the-art methods for detecting compositionality in noun compounds

## Strengths

1. **Novel and linguistically motivated approach to compositionality**: The paper makes a compelling argument for the importance of syntactic structure in compositionality detection. By using typed dependency paths (rather than untyped contexts), the APT method better captures the linguistic relationships between words in phrases. This is particularly important for noun compounds where the modifier can be either a noun or an adjective, which the authors demonstrate is a key factor in their approach's success. The method aligns with linguistic intuition that compositionality depends on syntactic structure.

2. **Strong empirical results with statistical significance**: The paper reports a clear improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71), with statistically significant differences across multiple experiments. The use of 3-fold cross-validation, careful parameter tuning, and reporting of statistical significance (with differences >0.005 being significant at 95% level) adds credibility to their claims. The results are consistent across different parameter settings (PPMI smoothing, composition operations), suggesting robustness.

3. **Insightful analysis of composition operations**: The authors provide a thoughtful explanation for why their hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) outperforms both individual approaches. Their explanation that "the unaligned typed dependency features are slightly more informative than the aligned typed dependency features" for this specific dataset provides valuable insight into the nature of compositionality in noun compounds. This goes beyond simply reporting results to offering a theoretical understanding of why their method works.

4. **Clear connection between compositionality detection and compositional modeling**: The paper effectively demonstrates that compositionality detection can serve as a valuable evaluation metric for compositional methods. The authors correctly note that "evaluating measures of phrase-level similarity directly against human judgments of similarity ignores the problem that it is not always possible to determine meaning in a compositional manner." This insight positions their work as contributing to a broader understanding of compositionality in distributional semantics.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of evaluation**: While the paper makes a strong case for their method on noun compounds, it only evaluates on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, which consists exclusively of noun compounds. The authors acknowledge that "further work is of course needed with other datasets and other types of phrase," but this limitation restricts the generalizability of their findings. It's unclear whether their approach would work as well for verb phrases, adjective phrases, or other multiword expressions.

2. **Insufficient comparison with recent compositional methods**: The paper only compares with the Reddy et al. (2011) baseline, but doesn't compare with more recent compositional models that have been proposed since then (e.g., neural tensor networks, more advanced neural composition models). This makes it difficult to assess whether the improvements are due to their APT approach or simply better parameter tuning on existing methods.

3. **Lack of failure case analysis**: The paper doesn't provide any examples of phrases where their method fails or produces unexpected results. Without this, it's hard to understand the limitations of their approach. For instance, do they struggle with highly idiomatic phrases that have no compositional meaning at all, or with phrases where the modifier has multiple possible syntactic relationships?

4. **Unclear practical implications**: While the paper demonstrates improved performance on compositionality detection, it doesn't clearly articulate how this would translate to better performance on downstream NLP tasks. The authors mention that "the use of compositional methods to detect compositionality may also lead to improved compositional methods," but don't provide concrete examples or evidence of this benefit in practice.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper reports that "the unaligned model outperforms the aligned model" for noun compounds. Given that the majority of these compounds are parsed as noun-noun compounds (e.g., "grad student"), could you explain why alignment (which should be particularly relevant for these structures) doesn't seem to help, and whether this finding might generalize to other phrase types?

2. Could you provide specific examples of phrases where the hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) outperforms both the aligned and unaligned models? This would help clarify why the hybrid approach is beneficial.

3. The authors note that "the majority of occurrences where no dependency relationship was observed, this was due to the parser incorrectly parsing a three noun compound phrase." How did they handle these misparsed cases, and what impact did this have on the results?

4. The paper states that "the dependency paths encoded in the typed contextual features are highly informative in the task of determining the compositionality of a noun compound." Could you provide a specific example of a phrase where the typed dependency path was crucial for correct compositionality detection, and how it would have been missed without this information?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper makes a significant contribution to the understanding of compositionality in distributional semantics with a novel approach that incorporates syntactic structure through typed higher-order dependency paths. The results are strong and well-supported with thorough empirical evaluation. However, the limited scope of evaluation and lack of comparison with more recent compositional methods are notable weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly by expanding the discussion of limitations and potential future work to address the scope issue.

**Score: 7 (Strong Accept)**
