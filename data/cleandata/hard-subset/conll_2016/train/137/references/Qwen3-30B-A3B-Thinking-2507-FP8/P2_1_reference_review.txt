

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary
This paper addresses the problem of detecting non-compositional phrases (where the meaning cannot be derived from the literal meaning of the parts) using distributional semantics. The authors propose an approach called Anchored Packed Trees (APT) that encodes complete dependency paths from target words to context words, using typed contextual features. They investigate how different composition operations (aligned vs. unaligned, intersection vs. union) affect the ability to detect compositionality. The authors evaluate their approach on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality. Their results demonstrate that the APT approach significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods, achieving a Spearman's rank correlation coefficient of 0.79 compared to the previous best of 0.714.

## Main Contributions
1. An integrated compositional distributional approach (APT) that maintains syntactic structure within contextual features, demonstrating its effectiveness for compositionality detection
2. Empirical evidence showing that typed dependency paths (with proper alignment) are highly informative for detecting compositionality in compound nouns
3. A hybrid composition approach that combines aligned and unaligned APT composition, which yields the best results (0.79 correlation), suggesting both types of information contribute to predicting compositionality

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical motivation for the importance of syntactic structure**: The paper correctly identifies that non-compositionality is an integral part of language that should not be ignored, and the authors provide a compelling argument for why syntactic structure matters in compositionality detection. They correctly note that traditional approaches that ignore syntax (like untyped co-occurrence vectors) cannot capture the fine-grained semantic relationships needed for compositionality detection. This theoretical grounding is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Significant empirical improvement over prior work**: The paper demonstrates a meaningful improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.714), which is a 10.8% relative improvement. This is not just a marginal improvement but a substantial advancement in the field, especially considering the difficulty of the task. The authors provide thorough analysis of different composition operations and parameter settings, which strengthens the validity of their results.

3. **Clear explanation of the APT approach and its advantages**: The paper provides a well-structured explanation of the APT approach, including the use of typed higher-order dependency paths and the alignment process. The authors clearly demonstrate why their approach works better than previous methods by showing how the APT representation captures relevant syntactic structure that is missed by untyped approaches. The explanation in Table 2 and the accompanying text is particularly strong in this regard.

4. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The authors conducted thorough experiments with multiple composition operations (aligned/unaligned, intersection/union), different weighting parameters, and both normalised counts and PPMI. The use of 3-fold cross-validation and reporting of statistically significant differences (with p<0.05) adds rigor to the evaluation. The comparison with neural word embeddings (word2vec) also provides a relevant baseline.

5. **Insightful analysis of parameter settings**: The paper notes that optimal values of h and q were in the range [0.3,0.5] across all models, suggesting that for this dataset, the modifier is slightly more informative than the head in determining compositionality. This is an interesting finding that provides additional theoretical insight into the compositionality detection task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical explanation for the hybrid approach's success**: The paper identifies that the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned APT composition) yields the best results (0.79 vs. 0.78 for unaligned), but doesn't provide a compelling theoretical explanation for why this is the case. The authors note that "aligned APT composition and unaligned APT composition are predicting different contexts for compound nouns," but don't elaborate on what these different contexts are or why they're complementary. This is a significant gap in the paper's contribution.

2. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on compound nouns (90 examples from Reddy et al., 2011), but doesn't address whether their approach generalizes to other types of non-compositional phrases (e.g., phrasal verbs, idioms). The authors claim that "non-compositionality is an integral part of language," but their evaluation is limited to one specific type of phrase. Without evidence that their approach works for other phrase types, the broader significance of their contribution is questionable.

3. **Insufficient qualitative analysis**: The paper lacks a qualitative analysis of specific examples where the APT approach succeeds or fails. For instance, it would be valuable to see examples where the APT approach correctly identifies a non-compositional phrase like "red herring" and contrasts this with examples where it fails. This would help readers understand the strengths and limitations of the approach more concretely.

4. **Comparison with more recent methods is limited**: The paper compares primarily with Reddy et al. (2011), but doesn't adequately compare with more recent compositional methods like those proposed by Hashimoto et al. (2014) or Milajevs et al. (2014). For example, the authors mention that Milajevs et al. "consistently outperformed the traditional count-based vectors" but don't explain why their APT approach would be expected to outperform these more recent neural methods. This weakens the paper's claim of being a significant advancement.

5. **Overstated claims about the necessity of syntactic structure**: The paper claims that "some knowledge of syntactic structure is crucial in the fine-grained understanding of language," but the evidence provided is limited to the compositionality detection task. The authors don't provide evidence that syntactic structure is necessary for other tasks in distributional semantics, which makes this claim somewhat overreaching given the scope of their evaluation.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned APT) works best, but doesn't explain why. Could you provide specific examples where the aligned and unaligned components capture different aspects of compositionality that together lead to better performance?

2. The paper focuses exclusively on compound nouns. How do you expect your approach to generalize to other types of non-compositional phrases, such as phrasal verbs (e.g., "give up") or idioms (e.g., "kick the bucket")? Have you conducted any preliminary experiments on these phrase types?

3. The paper claims that "the dependency paths encoded in the typed contextual features are highly informative," but doesn't provide a direct comparison with other syntactic features (e.g., POS tags, semantic roles). How do you think your approach would perform if you used other syntactic features instead of dependency paths?

4. The paper states that "the use of compositionality detection may also lead to improved compositional methods." Could you elaborate on how this might work in practice, and provide an example of how compositionality detection could be integrated into a larger compositional model?

5. The paper reports a 10.8% relative improvement over the previous state-of-the-art. Given that the baseline (Reddy et al., 2011) was already using a sophisticated approach, what do you think is the theoretical limit for this task, and how close do you believe your approach is to reaching it?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of distributional semantics by demonstrating how syntactic structure can be effectively incorporated into compositional distributional models for the specific task of compositionality detection. The empirical results are compelling, and the paper is well-written with a clear explanation of the methodology. While the paper has some limitations in scope and theoretical explanation, these do not significantly detract from the core contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions above.
