

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates compositionality detection in noun compounds using distributional semantic models. The authors argue that current phrase-level similarity evaluation methods are confounded by compositionality, as non-compositional phrases (like "red herring") cannot be accurately represented by composing their constituent words. They introduce an Anchored Packed Trees (APT) approach that uses typed, higher-order dependency paths to model compositionality, with the key insight that syntactic structure encoded in these paths is crucial for accurately predicting compositionality.

The main contributions, ranked by strength, are:
1. A formalized compositional approach using typed dependency paths (APT) that properly aligns vector spaces before composition, addressing a key limitation in previous compositional methods.
2. Empirical demonstration that syntactic structure encoded in typed dependency paths significantly outperforms untyped representations in compositionality detection (achieving 0.79 Spearman's ρ vs. previous state-of-the-art of 0.71).
3. A hybrid compositional model that combines aligned and unaligned APT representations, which achieves the best performance by leveraging both types of contextual information.

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness of the APT Framework**: The paper provides a rigorous formalization of the APT approach, including the alignment operation and reduction function (Equation 1). The examples in Table 1 clearly demonstrate how the model handles different parts of speech and dependency paths. The authors effectively address a fundamental limitation in previous compositional methods by showing that unaligned composition often leads to zero vectors due to incompatible feature spaces.

2. **Significant Performance Improvement**: The results in Table 5 demonstrate a statistically significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71, p<0.05). The ablation studies (Tables 3-4) provide clear evidence that the typed dependency paths and the hybrid composition model are the key contributors to this improvement. The authors' argument that "syntactic structure encoded in the typed contextual features are highly informative" is strongly supported by the results.

3. **Novel Evaluation Framework**: The paper makes a compelling case for using compositionality detection as a cleaner evaluation framework than phrase similarity judgments, as it avoids confounding compositionality with similarity judgments. This provides a more precise way to evaluate compositional methods, which is a significant contribution to the field.

4. **Thorough Parameter Analysis**: The paper provides a comprehensive analysis of different composition operations (⊔INT vs. ⊔UNI), weight types (normalized counts vs. PPMI), and alignment strategies. The finding that optimal values of h and q consistently fall in [0.3,0.5] across different models provides valuable insight into the relative importance of modifier vs. head in noun compounds.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on noun compounds using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns. The authors acknowledge that "further work is needed with other datasets and other types of phrase," but the current evaluation lacks generalizability. Without testing on other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjectival phrases) or larger datasets, it's unclear whether the approach would generalize to broader compositional tasks.

2. **Comparison with Recent Methods**: The paper compares with Reddy et al. (2011) but doesn't benchmark against more recent compositional methods developed after 2011 (e.g., neural tensor networks, attention-based models). The authors reference Milajevs et al. (2014) who used neural embeddings with compositional models, but don't directly compare with their state-of-the-art results. This omission weakens the claim that the APT approach represents a significant advancement.

3. **Ambiguity in the Hybrid Model's Mechanism**: While the hybrid model (Equations 8-9) achieves the best results, the paper doesn't provide a clear linguistic or theoretical explanation for why unaligned representations are more informative than aligned ones (as noted in Table 5: "the unaligned typed dependency features are slightly more informative"). This lack of explanation makes it difficult to understand the core contribution of the hybrid approach.

4. **Limited Discussion of Practical Implications**: The paper focuses on the technical aspects of the model but doesn't discuss how this approach could be integrated into larger NLP systems. The authors mention that "the use of compositional methods to detect compositionality may also lead to improved compositional methods," but don't provide concrete examples of how this could be implemented in practice.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds. How would the APT approach perform on other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun compounds)? Do you have any preliminary results or plans to evaluate on these other phrase types?

2. The paper compares with Reddy et al. (2011) but doesn't compare with more recent compositional methods (e.g., those using neural tensor networks or attention mechanisms). Could you explain why these methods weren't included in your comparison, and how your approach might compare with them?

3. The hybrid model combines aligned and unaligned representations, with the unaligned approach performing slightly better. Is there a linguistic explanation for why the unaligned approach works better in this context, particularly regarding the dependency structure of noun compounds?
