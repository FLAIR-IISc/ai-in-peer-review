

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper proposes an "anchored packed trees" (APTs) approach to improve compositionality detection for noun compounds. The main contributions are: (1) an integrated compositional distributional approach that maintains syntactic structure within contextual features; (2) a detailed analysis of how different composition operations (aligned vs. unaligned, intersective vs. union) impact compositionality detection; and (3) demonstration that typed dependency features are highly informative for compositionality detection.

## Strengths
1. The paper presents a theoretically sound approach that properly integrates syntactic structure into distributional models, which is a significant contribution to the field of compositional distributional semantics. The APT framework addresses a key limitation in previous approaches by properly aligning vector spaces before composition.

2. The experimental design is thorough, with careful comparison against the Reddy et al. (2011) baseline, systematic parameter exploration, and appropriate statistical validation (3-fold cross-validation with significance testing). The paper clearly demonstrates that their approach outperforms the previous state-of-the-art.

3. The paper makes an important conceptual contribution by establishing compositionality detection as a useful evaluation metric for compositional methods, separate from phrase similarity judgments. This helps disentangle the effects of compositionality from other factors in phrase similarity evaluation.

## Weaknesses
1. **Citation and reference inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous minor formatting errors in citations and references. Examples include "Baroni and Lenci.,? 2010" vs. "Baroni and Lenci., 2010" and inconsistent use of "et al." in citations. The reference list contains odd formatting like "\Q2010\E" and missing punctuation. These are minor but numerous issues that undermine the paper's professionalism.

2. **Undefined technical terms**: The paper refers to "⊔INT" and "⊔UNI" composition operations without defining them. While the paper mentions these are "intersective" and "union" operations, it fails to provide clear mathematical definitions or explanations of how these operations work in practice. This is a significant technical gap for readers trying to reproduce the work.

3. **Insufficient explanation of key parameters**: The paper states "optimal values of both h and q were in the range [0.3,0.5]" but provides no linguistic or theoretical justification for these values. This is a minor but notable gap in the explanation of their results.

4. **Omission of relevant results**: The paper mentions "we found that shifting PMI tended to have a strong negative effect on results" but states "For compactness, we do not include the results with shifted PMI here." This omission is problematic as it prevents readers from evaluating the authors' claim about the negative effect of shifted PMI.

5. **Minor but pervasive technical inconsistencies**: The paper inconsistently uses "grad/N" vs. "grad/N" for part-of-speech tagging, has minor grammatical errors ("siginificantly"), and uses inconsistent terminology ("compositionality" vs. "compositional"). While these are minor individually, their cumulative effect creates a perception of sloppiness.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why did you choose α = 0.75 and α = 1.0 specifically for PPMI smoothing? What about other values like α = 0.5 or α = 0.9?

2. Can you provide a clear mathematical definition of the "⊔INT" and "⊔UNI" composition operations, as they appear to be novel operations not defined in the paper?

3. Given your claim that "shifting PMI tended to have a strong negative effect on results," why did you omit the results for shifted PMI? If the results were significantly worse, why not include them to strengthen your argument?

## Overall Recommendation
While the paper presents an interesting approach to compositionality detection and demonstrates a modest improvement over previous work, the numerous minor methodological inconsistencies, undefined technical terms, and formatting errors significantly undermine the paper's credibility. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these minor flaws too numerous to overlook, even though the core idea has merit. The paper would benefit from a thorough proofreading and clarification of key technical details before it could be considered for publication.

**Decision: Reject**
