

# Peer Review

## Summary

This paper investigates the detection of non-compositional phrases (such as idioms) using compositional distributional methods. The authors propose an integrated compositional approach called Anchored Packed Trees (APT) that maintains syntactic structure within contextual features to better model phrase compositionality. They evaluate their approach on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality, comparing their method against state-of-the-art approaches including neural word embeddings.

## Main Contributions

1. **Significant improvement in compositionality detection**: The authors demonstrate a substantial improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71 Spearman's correlation with human judgments), showing that their integrated compositional approach using typed higher-order dependency paths is highly effective for detecting non-compositional phrases.

2. **Insight into the role of syntactic structure**: The paper provides strong evidence that typed syntactic features (dependency paths) are highly informative for compositionality detection, suggesting that syntactic knowledge is crucial for fine-grained language understanding in distributional semantics.

3. **Hybrid composition approach**: The authors introduce a novel hybrid composition method that combines aligned and unaligned APT features, which outperforms all other approaches. This provides valuable insight into how different aspects of syntactic structure contribute to compositionality detection.

## Strengths

1. **Substantial performance improvement**: The paper demonstrates a clear, measurable improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71), which is a significant contribution to the field. The improvement is statistically significant across multiple parameter settings and composition operations.

2. **Novel evaluation framework**: The authors use compositionality detection as a proxy for evaluating compositional methods, which avoids the confounding factor of phrase similarity judgments. This is a clever and useful approach for evaluating compositional methods, as it directly measures how well a method can predict whether a phrase is compositional.

3. **Comprehensive experimental analysis**: The paper provides a thorough comparison of different composition operations (intersective vs. union), different weighting schemes (PPMI vs. normalized counts), and different alignment strategies (aligned vs. unaligned vs. hybrid). This detailed analysis helps to understand which aspects of the approach are most important.

4. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The APT approach is well-motivated by linguistic considerations about how words function in different syntactic roles. The paper clearly explains why typed dependency paths are important for compositionality detection and how their approach addresses the limitations of previous methods.

5. **Insightful parameter analysis**: The paper reports that optimal values for parameters h and q (which control the weighting of modifier vs. head and aligned vs. unaligned features) consistently fall in the range [0.3, 0.5], suggesting that the modifier is slightly more informative than the head for compositionality detection. This provides valuable theoretical insights.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The evaluation is restricted to noun compounds, which represent only one type of multiword expression. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't provide evidence that the approach would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases, idioms). This limits the broader applicability of the findings.

2. **Comparison to recent methods**: The paper compares against Reddy et al. (2011) and a few other older methods but doesn't compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods for compositionality detection or compositional modeling. For instance, there are more recent neural approaches that might outperform the authors' method, and the paper doesn't address this.

3. **Lack of concrete examples**: While the paper makes claims about why their approach works better (e.g., "the second-order dependency features of spelling, which would include evidence from other uses of spelling as a modifier..."), it doesn't provide concrete examples of these features or how they specifically improve performance. This makes it difficult to fully understand the contribution.

4. **Unclear practical implications**: The paper shows that their approach improves compositionality detection but doesn't clearly explain how this translates to improvements in other NLP tasks that rely on compositional modeling. It would be valuable to see how this could be applied to tasks like sentiment analysis, question answering, or machine translation.

5. **Potential overfitting to the dataset**: The evaluation uses a small dataset of 90 compound nouns. While cross-validation is used, it's possible that the approach is optimized for this specific dataset and might not generalize well to larger or different collections of phrases.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that "the majority of occurrences where no dependency relationship was observed, this was due to the parser incorrectly parsing a three noun compound phrase." How does your approach handle cases where the dependency parser makes errors, and how robust is your method to such parsing errors?

2. Your hybrid composition method (combining aligned and unaligned features) provides the best performance. Can you provide specific examples of compound nouns where aligned features are particularly helpful and where unaligned features are particularly helpful, to better understand when each approach is more appropriate?

3. The paper states that "the modifier is slightly more informative than the head in determining the compositionality of compound nouns." Can you provide some specific examples of compound nouns where this is particularly true, and explain why the modifier might be more informative in those cases?

4. The paper compares against methods from 2011 and older, but doesn't compare to more recent approaches. Are there any recent papers on compositionality detection or compositional modeling that you could compare against to demonstrate that your approach is still state-of-the-art?

5. The paper mentions that "the use of compositional methods to detect compositionality may also lead to improved compositional methods." Can you elaborate on how this feedback loop would work in practice, and provide examples of how this could improve compositional models for downstream tasks?

## Recommendation

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of distributional semantics by demonstrating a substantial improvement in compositionality detection using a syntactically informed approach. The method is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and provides valuable insights into the role of syntactic structure in compositional modeling. While the scope of evaluation is limited to noun compounds, the methodological contributions and insights are strong enough to warrant acceptance. The paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above in the author response, particularly regarding broader applicability and comparison to recent methods.
