

# Peer Review: "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary

This paper investigates the detection of non-compositional phrases (e.g., "red herring") using compositional distributional semantic methods. The authors argue that current approaches to phrase-level similarity evaluation fail to account for the continuum of compositionality, rather than treating it as a binary property. They introduce a novel approach called Anchored Packed Trees (APT) that uses typed higher-order dependency paths to better model compositionality in noun compounds. The authors evaluate their method against the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality, comparing APT-based approaches with neural word embeddings (word2vec) and other compositional models.

## Main Contributions

1. A novel compositional method (APT) that uses typed higher-order dependency paths to model compositionality, which significantly outperforms previous approaches (achieving 0.79 Spearman correlation with human judgments vs. the previous state-of-the-art of 0.71).

2. A thorough empirical analysis demonstrating that unaligned composition (where the modifier's vector is not aligned with the head) outperforms aligned composition for this task, with hybrid approaches combining both outperforming either alone.

3. A clear demonstration that syntactic structure (via dependency paths) is crucial for accurately modeling compositionality in noun compounds, providing evidence against purely distributional approaches that ignore syntactic structure.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical and Practical Significance**: The paper makes a strong argument that compositionality is a continuum rather than a binary property, and that current evaluation methods for phrase similarity conflate compositionality with semantic similarity. This is a significant theoretical contribution that addresses a real gap in the field. The authors provide a compelling rationale for why compositionality should be evaluated separately, which has implications for how we design and evaluate compositional semantic models.

2. **Strong Empirical Results**: The results are robust and well-presented. The APT approach achieves a significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71), with statistical significance confirmed through 3-fold cross-validation. The paper carefully tests multiple composition operations and parameter settings, providing a thorough analysis of what works best for this specific task.

3. **Well-Designed Experimental Setup**: The authors use a standard dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) and carefully preprocess the corpus to handle compound nouns correctly. They explicitly address potential confounding factors (e.g., by ensuring that compound phrase vectors don't leak into constituent representations). The paper also provides detailed parameter settings for neural word embeddings, making the results replicable.

4. **Insightful Analysis of Composition Operations**: The paper provides valuable insights about the optimal composition operations for this task (e.g., ⊔UNI generally outperforms ⊔INT, unaligned composition outperforms aligned composition, and optimal values for parameters h and q are in the range [0.3, 0.5]). These insights go beyond just reporting results and provide actionable guidance for future work.

5. **Clear Presentation of Methodological Innovations**: The APT approach is clearly explained with concrete examples (Table 1 and Table 2), and the paper effectively demonstrates why their method works better than previous approaches. The distinction between typed and untyped contextual features is well-motivated and clearly explained.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Explanation of Why APT Works Better**: The paper claims that APT composition improves detection of non-compositional phrases but doesn't provide a compelling linguistic explanation for why unaligned composition outperforms aligned composition. The authors note that "unlike the aligned model, unaligned composition does not assume the modifier and head share the same semantic space," but they don't provide linguistic evidence or examples to support this claim. This weakens the theoretical contribution of the paper.

2. **Limited Comparison with Recent State-of-the-Art**: While the paper compares against Reddy et al. (2011), it doesn't compare against more recent compositional models that might have surpassed their baseline. For example, there have been advances in tensor-based composition (e.g., Socher et al., 2012) and neural compositional models (e.g., Hashimoto et al., 2014) that could be relevant to this task. The paper would be stronger with a more comprehensive comparison to the current state-of-the-art.

3. **Ambiguous Definition of "Typed" Features**: The paper claims to use "typed" contextual features but doesn't clearly define what "typed" means in their specific implementation. They reference Baroni and Lenci (2010) but don't explicitly state how their approach differs from previous work on typed features. This ambiguity makes it difficult to assess the novelty of their approach.

4. **Limited Discussion of Method Limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address the limitations of their approach. For example, they note that "many compounds (e.g. graduate student and silver screen) are seen with the modifier tagged both as a noun and as an adjective" but don't discuss how their method handles this ambiguity. They also don't address how their method would scale to larger datasets or other language types.

5. **Title Misalignment with Content**: The paper's title refers to "red herring" as a non-compositional phrase, but the paper doesn't actually analyze "red herring" in their experiments. It's mentioned as an example in the introduction but not used as a case study. This disconnect between the title and the actual content is confusing and diminishes the paper's impact.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that unaligned composition outperforms aligned composition for compositionality detection, but provides limited theoretical justification. Could you provide specific linguistic examples or evidence that explains why unaligned composition might be better suited for this particular task?

2. The paper uses the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, which is focused on noun-noun compounds. How would your APT approach perform on other types of multiword expressions (e.g., verb-noun compounds, phrasal verbs, or adjective-noun compounds)?

3. The paper mentions that "many compounds (e.g., graduate student and silver screen) are seen with the modifier tagged both as a noun and as an adjective." How does your method handle compounds where the modifier can be interpreted in multiple ways (e.g., as a noun or adjective), and how does this affect the compositionality scores?

4. The paper states that "the use of an intersective pointwise composition operation such as multiply would largely lead to zero vectors" and therefore prefers ⊔INT for some cases. However, the paper doesn't clearly explain why they choose ⊔INT for aligned composition but ⊔UNI for unaligned composition. Could you clarify this distinction?

5. The paper claims that "the dependency paths encoded in the typed contextual features are highly informative in the task of determining the compositionality of a noun compound." Could you provide specific examples of dependency paths that are particularly informative for compositionality detection, and explain why these paths are informative?

## Additional Comments

The paper has a strong technical foundation and provides valuable empirical evidence for the importance of syntactic structure in modeling compositionality. The results are significant and well-supported. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the APT approach and a more comprehensive comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods. The title could be revised to better reflect the actual content of the paper, which is primarily about noun compounds rather than "red herring" specifically.
