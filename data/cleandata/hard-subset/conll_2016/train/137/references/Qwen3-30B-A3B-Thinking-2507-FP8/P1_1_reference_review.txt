

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates the detection of non-compositional phrases (phrases whose meaning cannot be determined from the meaning of their parts, such as "red herring") using compositional distributional methods. The authors propose an approach based on "Anchored Packed Trees" (APT), which encodes dependency paths from target words to context words with typed syntactic relations. They evaluate their method on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality.

The main contributions of the paper, in order of strength, are:

1. **A novel compositional method (APT) that incorporates syntactic structure through typed dependency paths**, which significantly outperforms previous approaches on compositionality detection (achieving ρ = 0.79 vs. previous best of 0.71).
2. **A thorough empirical investigation of different composition strategies**, including aligned vs. unaligned APTs, different composition operations (intersection vs. union), and the optimal combination of these approaches (the hybrid method).
3. **Clear evidence that syntactic structure (via typed dependency paths) is highly informative for compositionality detection**, providing an important insight for the field of distributional semantics.

## Strengths

1. **The paper addresses a significant gap in distributional semantics research**. Previous work on phrase similarity has largely ignored the compositionality continuum (Sag et al., 2002; McCarthy et al., 2003), focusing instead on similarity judgments that conflate compositionality with similarity. The authors correctly identify that non-compositional phrases cannot be reliably modeled by standard compositional methods, and their approach to evaluate compositionality directly (rather than as a byproduct of similarity) is both novel and valuable.

2. **The APT method is theoretically well-motivated and technically sound**. The authors provide a clear explanation of how the method works, including the alignment process, reduction operation, and different composition strategies. The distinction between typed and untyped contextual features (with the former being more informative) is a key insight that advances the field. The paper demonstrates a deep understanding of the theoretical challenges in compositional distributional semantics.

3. **The evaluation is thorough, well-designed, and provides clear comparative results**. The authors use the established Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark dataset with human judgments, compare to multiple baselines (including the previous state-of-the-art), and conduct careful parameter tuning using cross-validation. Their results are consistent across different parameter settings, with the hybrid APT method (combining aligned and unaligned features) achieving the best performance (ρ = 0.79), which is a statistically significant improvement over the previous best (ρ = 0.71).

4. **The paper provides valuable insights about the role of syntactic structure in compositional modeling**. The finding that both aligned and unaligned APT features contribute to better compositionality detection suggests that different aspects of syntactic structure provide complementary information. The paper also demonstrates that the optimal parameter values (h and q in [0.3,0.5]) indicate the modifier is slightly more informative than the head for determining compositionality.

5. **The paper clearly explains the limitations of previous approaches**. By showing that untyped co-occurrence methods (including neural embeddings) perform worse than their APT-based method, the authors convincingly argue that syntactic structure is crucial for fine-grained language understanding. Their analysis of why shifted PMI negatively affected results (due to low frequency of compound nouns) is particularly insightful.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of detailed analysis of why the APT method works better**. While the paper demonstrates that APT significantly outperforms previous methods, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of *which specific aspects* of the APT method contribute to the improvement. For example, is it the typed dependency paths, the alignment process, or the hybrid composition strategy that is most important? A more detailed ablation study would strengthen the claims.

2. **Limited scope of evaluation**. The paper evaluates on only 90 compound nouns from the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, which is a small and specific type of phrase (noun compounds). The authors acknowledge that further work is needed with "other datasets and other types of phrase," but the current evaluation doesn't provide evidence that their approach generalizes beyond noun compounds. It would be valuable to see if their method works for other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun combinations).

3. **Insufficient comparison to more recent work**. The paper compares to the Reddy et al. (2011) baseline but doesn't mention or compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods for compositionality detection. For example, there has been significant work on compositional distributional models since 2011, including tensor-based methods and neural approaches. A comparison to these would better establish the significance of their contribution.

4. **The connection between compositionality detection and the broader goal of phrase similarity is not clearly articulated**. The paper claims that compositionality detection "provides a way of evaluating compositional methods without confounding judgements of phrase similarity with judgements of compositionality," but it doesn't explain how this directly helps with the main goal of improving phrase similarity. This makes the contribution feel somewhat disconnected from the larger field of distributional semantics.

5. **The paper doesn't address potential limitations of the APT method**. For example, how does the APT method scale to larger corpora or more complex syntactic structures? The paper mentions that the feature space for nouns is approximately 80,000 dimensions (first-order) and 230,000 dimensions (second-order), which is quite large. The computational cost of working with such high-dimensional vectors isn't discussed, which could be a significant limitation for practical applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper demonstrates that the hybrid APT method (combining aligned and unaligned features) performs best. Could you provide specific examples of phrases where the aligned features were particularly informative versus where the unaligned features were more important? This would help clarify the complementary nature of these two approaches.

2. The authors state that "the dependency paths encoded in the typed contextual features are highly informative in the task of determining the compositionality of a noun compound." Could you identify specific dependency paths (e.g., NMOD, AMOD, NSUBJ) that are particularly predictive of compositionality? This would help understand the linguistic insights gained from the method.

3. The paper uses the same ukWaC corpus as Reddy et al. (2011). Given that the authors' method significantly outperforms Reddy's, is it possible that the improvement is partly due to the specific corpus characteristics rather than the method itself? How would the results change if the method were evaluated on a different corpus?

4. The authors note that "the more frequently occurring compounds tend to be compositional" (FREQ baseline performs better than RAND). Could you elaborate on this relationship between frequency and compositionality? Is this a general linguistic phenomenon, or specific to the ukWaC corpus and the 90 compounds in the dataset?

5. The paper mentions that "further improvements might be made by first detecting non-compositional phrases" and "if a phrase is judged to be non-compositional... the observed phrasal representation should be smoothed rather than its constituents' representations." How would this approach be integrated into a larger pipeline for phrase similarity or other NLP tasks?
