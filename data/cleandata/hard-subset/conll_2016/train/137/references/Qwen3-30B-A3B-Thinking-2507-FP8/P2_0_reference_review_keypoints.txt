- Proposes APT compositional framework that encodes typed dependency paths and aligns vector spaces by syntactic structure before composition.
- Reports a substantial empirical improvement (Spearman 0.79 vs. prior 0.71) on Reddy et al.'s 90 compound-noun benchmark.
- Shows syntactic structure (typed dependency paths) is highly informative for compositionality and that combining aligned and unaligned composition helps.

- Strong empirical results with thorough analyses, 3-fold cross-validation, and statistical significance testing.
- Solid theoretical and linguistic motivation explaining why alignment across parts-of-speech matters for composition.
- Careful experimental design and reproducibility: replication of prior setup, detailed preprocessing, and parameter choices.

- Evaluation is narrowly scoped to 90 compound nouns, limiting claims about generalization to other non-compositional phrase types.
- Lacks comparison to more recent neural/transformer-based compositionality methods, reducing the impact of the claimed SOTA.
- Does not deeply analyze why the method outperforms others or which specific dependency paths drive performance.

- How would APT generalize to idioms, phrasal verbs, and other multiword expressions — any required modifications?
- How sensitive is the approach to parser errors and how do parsing mistakes affect results?
- Can the authors provide analysis of which dependency relations (e.g., NMOD, AMOD) most influence compositionality scores?

- Overall: well-written, technically sound contribution with strong results; recommendation: Accept, but would benefit from broader evaluation, newer baselines, and deeper analysis.
