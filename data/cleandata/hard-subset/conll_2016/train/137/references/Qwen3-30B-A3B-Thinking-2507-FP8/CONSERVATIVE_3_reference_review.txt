

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary
This paper proposes an "Anchored Packed Trees" (APT) approach to compositional distributional semantics for detecting compositionality in noun compounds. The authors argue that traditional compositional methods fail because they don't properly handle syntactic structure, and they claim their APT method—using typed higher-order dependency paths with alignment before composition—significantly outperforms existing approaches on the Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality detection benchmark.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel compositional method (APT) that aligns vector spaces using dependency paths before composition
2. A demonstration that this method achieves improved results (ρ=0.79) over the previous state-of-the-art (ρ=0.71) on compositionality detection
3. An analysis of the relative importance of aligned versus unaligned features in compositionality detection

## Strengths
1. **Well-defined evaluation framework**: The paper uses the established Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark for compositionality detection, which provides a clear, standardized way to evaluate compositional methods. This is valuable for the field as it avoids the common pitfall of evaluating compositionality methods on phrase similarity tasks where compositionality confounds the results.

2. **Methodological clarity**: The paper provides a detailed description of the APT approach with concrete examples (Tables 1-2) that illustrate how their method handles syntactic structure differently from traditional approaches. This level of detail helps readers understand the proposed mechanism.

3. **Comparative analysis**: The paper thoroughly compares their method against several established baselines, including neural word embeddings (word2vec) and traditional compositional approaches. This comprehensive evaluation strengthens the validity of their claims.

## Weaknesses
1. **Marginal performance gains**: The paper claims significant improvements over the previous state-of-the-art (ρ=0.71 → ρ=0.79), but these gains are extremely marginal (0.08 correlation difference). Given the small size of the benchmark (90 compounds), this difference may not be statistically meaningful, and the paper doesn't provide sufficient statistical evidence to justify the claim of "significant" improvement. In NLP, such small gains are typically not considered meaningful for practical applications.

2. **Lack of relevance to core NLP tasks**: The paper focuses narrowly on compositionality detection, which is a niche task. It fails to demonstrate how this approach would improve performance on more important, general NLP tasks like sentence similarity or semantic textual similarity. Without this connection, the method remains a curiosity rather than a practical advancement.

3. **Inadequate baseline comparison**: The paper compares against Reddy et al. (2011), but fails to include more recent state-of-the-art compositional methods published after 2011 (e.g., tensor-based approaches, neural compositional models). This omission undermines the claim that their method "outperforms" the current state-of-the-art. For example, Milajevs et al. (2014) already showed that neural embeddings with simple addition outperformed traditional methods on similar tasks.

4. **Theoretical justification lacking**: The paper introduces the "hybrid" approach (combining aligned and unaligned features) as the best performer, but provides no clear theoretical justification for why this combination should work better than either approach alone. This appears to be a statistical hack rather than a principled improvement.

5. **High-dimensional feature space**: The paper describes using extremely high-dimensional feature spaces (80,000-230,000 dimensions), which is computationally expensive and likely to suffer from the curse of dimensionality. The paper fails to address how this would scale to larger datasets or practical applications.

## Questions to Authors
1. Given that the performance gains are marginal (0.08 in Spearman's ρ) and the benchmark is small (90 compounds), what statistical evidence do you have that these results are significantly better than the previous state-of-the-art?

2. How does your APT approach specifically address the "sparse data problem" that the paper claims is the motivation for compositional methods, given that your method requires even more context features (higher-order dependency paths)?

3. Why does the unaligned approach (which doesn't require the complex alignment process) outperform the aligned approach, contradicting your theoretical motivation for developing the alignment process?

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a methodologically detailed approach to compositionality detection, the marginal performance gains on a narrow benchmark, lack of relevance to core NLP tasks, and inadequate baseline comparisons make it difficult to justify as a meaningful contribution to the field. The paper claims a "significant" improvement over previous work, but the evidence provided falls short of establishing this. As a reviewer who values methodological rigor and practical relevance, I find the paper's claims overstated and its contribution insufficiently validated against established baselines and more important NLP tasks. The paper would be much stronger if it demonstrated how this method would improve performance on standard NLP tasks rather than focusing narrowly on compositionality detection.
