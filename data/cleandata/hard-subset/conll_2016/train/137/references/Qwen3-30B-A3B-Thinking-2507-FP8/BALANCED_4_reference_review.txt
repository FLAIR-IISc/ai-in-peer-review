

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates how compositional methods affect the detection of non-compositional phrases (like "red herring") using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. The authors propose an approach using Anchored Packed Trees (APT) to encode contextual features with typed higher-order dependency paths. They compare their APT-based compositional methods with neural word embeddings and other compositional approaches. The main contributions are:

1. A novel compositional method using Anchored Packed Trees with typed higher-order dependency paths for modeling compositionality in noun compounds.
2. Empirical demonstration that their approach significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71) on compositionality detection tasks.
3. Evidence that syntactic structure encoded through typed dependency paths is highly informative for compositionality detection, with implications for broader compositional distributional semantics.

## Strengths

1. **Well-motivated problem and clear contribution**: The paper addresses a significant challenge in distributional semantics - the need to distinguish compositional from non-compositional phrases for proper evaluation of compositional models. The authors clearly articulate why this matters (non-compositionality is an integral part of language, not a "red herring" to be ignored), and they provide a principled way to evaluate compositional models without confounding compositionality judgments with similarity judgments.

2. **Rigorous experimental evaluation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments with careful parameter tuning, multiple compositional operations, and appropriate baselines. Their comparison with the state-of-the-art (Reddy et al., 2011) is particularly convincing, with statistically significant improvements (0.79 vs. 0.71). The use of 3-fold cross-validation and attention to parameter settings (h, q, α) demonstrates methodological rigor.

3. **Theoretical grounding and insight**: The paper provides valuable theoretical insight into why syntactic structure matters for compositionality detection. The APT approach, building on Weeds et al. (2014), correctly identifies that typed dependency paths encode meaningful syntactic relationships that are crucial for compositional modeling. The finding that the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) achieves the best results is particularly insightful.

4. **Clear and compelling results**: The results are presented clearly, with tables that effectively summarize the performance across different model configurations. The consistent outperformance of APT-based methods over neural embeddings (0.79 vs. 0.74) is a strong contribution to the field.

5. **Practical implications**: The paper suggests that compositionality detection could be used to improve compositional methods themselves (e.g., by smoothing observed phrasal representations rather than constituent representations for non-compositional phrases), which is an important direction for future work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clarity on novelty**: The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from Weeds et al. (2014), who also investigated higher-order dependency paths. The connection to prior work needs to be more explicitly stated to clarify what is novel about the current approach.

2. **Terminology confusion**: The paper claims to "improve the detection of non-compositional phrases," but the evaluation is on compositionality detection (determining if a phrase is compositional), not directly on detecting non-compositional phrases. This subtle but important distinction needs clarification to avoid confusion.

3. **Unexplained counterintuitive result**: The unaligned APT model (which doesn't perform the alignment step) outperforms the aligned model, which contradicts the theoretical motivation for alignment. The authors don't adequately address this surprising finding, which weakens the theoretical foundation of their approach.

4. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper only evaluates on noun compounds (90 examples), which limits the generalizability of their findings. They don't discuss whether their approach would work for other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun phrases) or in other contexts.

5. **Underdeveloped discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't sufficiently address the limitations of their approach. For example, they don't discuss the computational cost of APT construction or whether their method would scale to larger corpora or more complex phrases.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the APT approach differ from the higher-order dependency path approach described in Weeds et al. (2014)? What specific novel contributions does this paper make to that line of work?

2. The paper claims to "improve the detection of non-compositional phrases," but the evaluation is on compositionality detection (determining if a phrase is compositional). Could you clarify the relationship between these two concepts and why this evaluation method is appropriate?

3. The unaligned APT model outperforms the aligned model, which contradicts the theoretical motivation for alignment. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, and how it affects your theoretical claims about the importance of syntactic structure?

## Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to distributional semantics by demonstrating the importance of syntactic structure for compositionality detection. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the insights have implications beyond the specific task evaluated. However, the paper needs to clarify its novelty relative to prior work, address the counterintuitive result about alignment, and provide a more thorough discussion of limitations and generalizability.

With revisions addressing these concerns, this paper would be a strong candidate for acceptance at CONLL. The core contribution - showing that typed dependency paths significantly improve compositionality detection - is well-supported by the evidence and represents an important step forward in the field.
