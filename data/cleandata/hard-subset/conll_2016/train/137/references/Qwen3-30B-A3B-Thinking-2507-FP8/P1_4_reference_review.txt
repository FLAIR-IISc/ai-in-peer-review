

# Peer Review: "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses a critical limitation in distributional semantic models: the confounding of compositionality with phrase similarity evaluation. The authors propose a novel compositional approach using Anchored Packed Trees (APT), which incorporates syntactic structure through typed dependency paths and alignment before composition. The main contributions are:

1. A novel compositional framework (APT) that maintains syntactic structure within contextual features, which is central to the compositional process.
2. A clear demonstration that syntactic structure encoded in typed contextual features is highly informative for determining the compositionality of noun compounds.
3. A comprehensive evaluation framework that separates compositionality detection from phrase similarity evaluation, avoiding confounding judgments.

## Strengths

1. **Significant methodological contribution to compositionality detection:** The paper makes a compelling case that compositionality should be evaluated separately from phrase similarity. This is a critical insight that addresses a fundamental limitation in the field. The APT approach with typed dependency paths and alignment is well-motivated by linguistic considerations and provides a principled way to incorporate syntactic structure into distributional models. The authors demonstrate a clear improvement over previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71), which is statistically significant and meaningful in the field.

2. **Thorough empirical evaluation:** The paper provides an exceptionally thorough experimental analysis, including ablation studies of different composition operations (intersective vs. union), parameter settings (α values, h, q), and comparisons with neural word embeddings. The authors systematically explore the impact of different design choices on performance, which strengthens their conclusions about the importance of typed dependency paths and the hybrid composition approach.

3. **Strong theoretical justification:** The paper provides excellent examples (Table 1) that clearly illustrate why typed dependency paths matter for compositionality detection. The explanation of how different parts of speech have different contextual patterns and how alignment before composition resolves vector space mismatches is particularly convincing. This theoretical grounding makes the method more than just an empirical improvement.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of evaluation:** While the paper makes a strong case for noun compounds, it doesn't provide evidence that the APT approach generalizes to other types of multiword expressions or more complex phrases. The dataset is limited to 90 compound nouns, and the paper doesn't address how the method would perform on verb phrases, idiomatic expressions, or other linguistic constructions. This narrow scope reduces the perceived impact of the work.

2. **Lack of comparison with recent compositional methods:** The paper compares with methods from 2011-2014 but doesn't evaluate against more recent compositional approaches (e.g., those using neural networks or transformers). The authors note that neural word embeddings outperform traditional count-based vectors, but they don't compare their APT approach with state-of-the-art compositional models built on top of these embeddings. This omission weakens the claim that their approach represents a significant advancement.

3. **Insufficient explanation for the hybrid approach's success:** While the paper demonstrates that the hybrid composition (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) achieves the best results, it doesn't provide a clear linguistic explanation for why this combination works better than either approach alone. The authors state that "aligned APT composition and unaligned APT composition are predicting different contexts for compound nouns which all contribute to forming a better estimate," but this remains somewhat abstract without specific examples or deeper analysis.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds. How would you expect your APT approach to generalize to more complex multiword expressions like verb phrases or idiomatic expressions? What modifications might be necessary?

2. Have you considered applying your APT approach to the more common task of phrase similarity, rather than just compositionality detection? Would it improve performance on standard phrase similarity benchmarks?

3. The paper reports optimal values of h and q in the range [0.3,0.5], suggesting the modifier is slightly more informative than the head. Is this consistent with linguistic theories of compositionality, or does it suggest a limitation in the model?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper with significant methodological contributions to the field of compositional distributional semantics. The APT approach is well-motivated, the evaluation is thorough, and the results represent a meaningful improvement over previous work. The primary weakness is the narrow scope of evaluation, but this is a common limitation in early-stage research that could be addressed in future work. The paper makes a compelling case that syntactic structure encoded in typed dependency paths is crucial for accurately modeling compositionality, which is an important insight for the field.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scope of evaluation and provide more concrete explanations for the hybrid approach's success. The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding compositionality in distributional models, which has implications for broader applications in NLP.
