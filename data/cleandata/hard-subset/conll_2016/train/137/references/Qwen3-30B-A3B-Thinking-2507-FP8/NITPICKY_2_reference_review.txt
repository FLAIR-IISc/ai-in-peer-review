

# Review of "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper proposes an "anchored packed trees" (APT) approach for compositional distributional semantics, specifically for noun compounds. The authors claim that maintaining syntactic structure in contextual features through APTs improves compositionality detection. They evaluate their approach on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset and report state-of-the-art results.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1. The APT method for compositional distributional semantics that maintains syntactic structure in contextual features.
2. A thorough experimental evaluation comparing various composition methods on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.
3. The finding that typed dependency paths are significantly more informative for compositionality detection than untyped co-occurrence vectors.

## Strengths

1. The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the APT method, including the alignment process and reduction operation. The authors effectively demonstrate how their approach handles different parts of speech in a way that avoids the zero-vector problem common in intersective composition.

2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with multiple parameter settings and comparisons to previous work. The results consistently show that APT-based models outperform previous state-of-the-art methods, with the hybrid method achieving the best performance (ρ = 0.79).

3. The paper makes an important methodological contribution by demonstrating that compositionality detection can serve as a useful evaluation metric for compositional methods, independent of phrase similarity judgments.

## Weaknesses

1. **Formatting inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous minor formatting errors that undermine its professional presentation. The term "naı̈ve" appears multiple times with an incorrect diacritic (should be "naive"). "Red herring" is inconsistently capitalized as "red herring" in the text. The reference list has inconsistent citation formatting (e.g., "Baroni and Lenci.,? 2010" should be "Baroni and Lenci, 2010"). These errors suggest a lack of attention to detail.

2. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper uses "order" to describe dependency paths without consistently defining what order 1, 2, etc. mean. For example, Table 1 shows features like "NMOD.NSUBJ" but doesn't explain the notation. The claim that "the 2nd order features of, say, adjectives, correspond to the 1st order features of nouns" is confusing without clear definition of what "correspond" means in this context.

3. **Methodological ambiguities**: The paper claims that "the use of an intersective pointwise composition operation such as multiply would largely lead to zero vectors" but provides no evidence or explanation for this claim. The meaning of "INT" and "UNI" operations in the composition equations is not clearly defined (INT probably means intersection, UNI probably means union).

4. **Insufficient methodological details**: The paper states that "we recombined them into a single lexical item" for compound phrases but doesn't explain why this was necessary or how it affects the evaluation. The preprocessing step for compound phrases is described in only a few sentences without sufficient detail for replication.

5. **Inconsistent experimental reporting**: The paper claims that "the differences are small" between different parameter settings but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these differences. The paper also states "we have used a sufficiently large number of repetitions that these are all small (≤ 0.0015)" but doesn't specify how many repetitions were used.

## Questions to Authors

1. What is the precise definition of "order" for dependency paths in your framework? How do you distinguish between different orders (e.g., what makes a feature "first-order" vs. "second-order")?

2. Can you clarify the relationship between the 2nd order features of adjectives and the 1st order features of nouns as mentioned in Section 3? How does this correspondence specifically help with compositionality detection?

3. Why did you choose to recombine compound phrases into single lexical items? How does this preprocessing step affect the evaluation of compositionality, and would the results differ if you had not performed this step?

4. The paper claims that "the majority of occurrences where no dependency relationship was observed, this was due to the parser incorrectly parsing a three noun compound phrase" but doesn't provide examples or evidence for this claim. Could you provide specific examples of these parsing errors?

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a methodologically sound approach to compositionality detection and achieves promising results, the numerous minor formatting inconsistencies, terminological ambiguities, and methodological omissions prevent it from meeting the standards expected for a CoNLL publication. The paper's strengths in methodology and experimental evaluation are overshadowed by these technical inconsistencies that undermine the paper's credibility and reproducibility.

I recommend rejection due to the substantial number of minor but persistent flaws that collectively detract from the paper's quality and impact. The authors should address these issues thoroughly before resubmission.
