- Summary: Addresses evaluating compositional methods when phrases may be non-compositional, proposing Anchored Packed Trees (APT) using typed dependency paths to model noun-compound compositionality and evaluating on Reddy et al. (2011).

- Contribution 1: Introduces APT, a compositional distributional approach that preserves syntactic structure via typed dependency paths.

- Contribution 2: Empirically shows a hybrid of aligned and unaligned features yields best compositionality detection performance.

- Contribution 3: Reports state-of-the-art Spearman correlation (0.79) with human judgments, improving over prior 0.71.

- Strength 1: Clearly motivates the problem and uses the “red herring” example to illustrate evaluation confounds.

- Strength 2: Proposes a novel, theoretically motivated method using typed dependency paths with clear explanation of alignment and offsetting.

- Strength 3: Provides thorough evaluation with multiple parameter settings, appropriate baselines, cross-validation, and significance testing.

- Strength 4: Reports important empirical findings (hybrid approach advantage, modifier informativeness, PPMI benefits).

- Strength 5: Offers practical implications for using compositionality detection to improve compositional methods and evaluation.

- Weakness 1: Limited scope — evaluation restricted to noun compounds, no evidence of generalization to other constructions.

- Weakness 2: No strong theoretical justification for why combining aligned and unaligned features works best.

- Weakness 3: Inadequate comparison to other linguistically motivated compositional models (e.g., lexical function, tensor methods).

- Weakness 4: Overstates significance of improvement (0.08 Spearman) without contextualizing practical impact.

- Weakness 5: Insufficient detail on human judgment data (inter-annotator agreement, aggregation, consistency).

- Question 1: Can you provide a linguistic or computational explanation for why both aligned and unaligned features are necessary?

- Question 2: How do you expect APT to generalize to other phrase types (verb/adjective phrases), and are there plans or evidence to test this?

- Question 3: How would APT perform on larger corpora with more frequent compounds or on higher-frequency phrase types given shifted PMI issues?

- Question 4: Is the modifier being more informative than the head a general principle or dataset-specific, and how might it vary across constructions?

- Question 5: Is the correlation between frequency and compositionality general or specific to ukWaC, and how does this affect result interpretation?

- Overall recommendation: Accept with major revisions — address generalization beyond noun compounds, theoretical justification for the hybrid method, and broader model comparisons.
