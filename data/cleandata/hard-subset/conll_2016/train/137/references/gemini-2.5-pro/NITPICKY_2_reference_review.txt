**Summary of the paper**
This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, using the benchmark dataset from Reddy et al. (2011). The authors propose that distributional models incorporating typed, higher-order dependency features are better suited for this task than models based on untyped, proximity-based contexts (such as standard word embeddings). They introduce a compositional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses dependency paths as features. The core idea is to compose vector representations of the constituent words and measure the similarity of the resulting vector to the observed (holistic) vector of the compound phrase. The authors systematically compare several variants of their APT model (aligned, unaligned, and a hybrid approach) against a strong word2vec baseline and the previous state-of-the-art. Their best-performing hybrid model achieves a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, a substantial improvement over the 0.71 reported by Reddy et al. (2011).

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the application and evaluation of a dependency-based compositional model (APT) for detecting noun-compound compositionality. The authors demonstrate that using typed, higher-order syntactic features significantly outperforms models based on untyped co-occurrence.
2.  A secondary contribution is a detailed experimental analysis that establishes a new state-of-the-art on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. This includes a thorough exploration of model variations (aligned vs. unaligned features, different composition functions) and a strong, well-tuned word2vec baseline that itself surpasses the previous state-of-the-art.

**Strengths**
1.  **Well-motivated approach:** The paper's central hypothesis—that explicit syntactic information in the form of typed dependency paths should be more informative for modeling composition than simple word proximity—is linguistically plausible and well-argued. The APT framework provides a concrete mechanism for testing this hypothesis.
2.  **Rigorous and comprehensive evaluation:** The experimental setup is commendable. The authors compare their method against multiple strong baselines, including the original work and a carefully tuned word2vec model. The use of repeated 3-fold cross-validation to tune hyperparameters (h, q) is methodologically sound, and the reporting of statistical significance lends credibility to the results. The inclusion of a frequency baseline (FREQ) provides important context for the task's difficulty.
3.  **Strong empirical results:** The proposed hybrid APT model achieves a Spearman’s correlation of 0.79, which is a very significant improvement over the prior state-of-the-art of 0.71. The clear progression of results from word2vec (0.74) to the various APT models (0.75-0.79) provides strong evidence for the paper's claims. The finding that unaligned and hybrid models outperform the strictly aligned model is an interesting and non-obvious result.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Lack of professional diligence in presentation:** The manuscript is marred by a startling number of formatting and typographical errors, which severely undermines its credibility. The entire reference section appears to be raw, unformatted BibTeX output, rendering it nearly unreadable (e.g., "Baroni and Lenci.,? \Q2010\E, Baroni and Lenci.,"). This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a conference submission. Further, there are numerous typos and grammatical mistakes throughout the text (e.g., "95% of were an NMOD relationship" in Section 5).
2.  **Inconsistent and unclear notation:** The notation in the core description of the APT model (Section 3) is inconsistent. The definition of reduction in Equation (1) uses `τ = τ1 r r τ2`, which appears to be a typo for `τ = τ1 r r̄ τ2`, as the text describes a relation cancelling with its inverse, which is denoted with an overbar. This notational sloppiness makes a key part of the method difficult to parse with confidence.
3.  **Insufficient methodological detail:** Several crucial experimental details are glossed over or omitted.
    *   In Section 5, the authors state that 76% of compound occurrences had a head-final dependency. The fate of the remaining 24% is not explained. How were these instances handled during preprocessing? This is a non-trivial portion of the data.
    *   In Section 5.2, the authors claim that for word embeddings, "add to be the only promising option" for composition. This is an empirical claim that is not substantiated. Standard alternatives like pointwise multiplication should have been reported, even if their performance was poor, to justify their exclusion.
    *   The distinction between the "CF" and "CS" settings in Table 3 is defined only opaquely within the results section itself, rather than in the experimental setup where it belongs.
4.  **Questionable evaluation restriction:** The evaluation is restricted to the similarity of *first-order* dependency features (Footnote 3). The APT composition model, however, relies crucially on second-order features for alignment. Discarding this information during evaluation is a curious choice. While a rationale is given (unreliability of higher-order composed features), this decision could potentially bias the comparison and its impact is not discussed. An analysis of what happens when higher-order features are included in the similarity calculation would strengthen the paper's conclusions.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you clarify your preprocessing methodology for the 90 noun compounds? Specifically, how did you handle the 24% of contiguous occurrences that lacked a clear head-final dependency parse?
2.  In your word embedding experiments (Section 5.2), could you provide the results for other compositional functions, such as pointwise multiplication, to substantiate the claim that weighted addition was the "only promising option"?
3.  Regarding the evaluation protocol in Footnote 3, have you analyzed the effect of restricting the similarity comparison to only first-order features? How do the results change if second-order features are included, and does this affect the relative performance of the different models (e.g., Aligned vs. Unaligned APT)?
4.  Please confirm and correct the notation for relation reduction in Equation (1).
