**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, arguing that this is a more direct way to evaluate compositional distributional models than using phrase similarity tasks. The authors propose a compositional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses typed, higher-order dependency paths as features. The core idea is that the vector for a modifier word can be "aligned" to the head word's vector space by prepending the connecting dependency path to its features before composition. The paper empirically evaluates this model on the standard Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. The compositionality of a phrase is measured as the cosine similarity between the composed vector and the observed holistic vector for the phrase.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The introduction of a novel, syntax-aware compositional model (APT) and a hybrid variant that combines both dependency-aligned and unaligned feature spaces. This model is shown to achieve a new state-of-the-art on the task of predicting noun compound compositionality, demonstrating the effectiveness of rich, typed dependency features.
2.  A thorough empirical investigation that compares the proposed APT model against strong baselines, including a well-tuned word2vec model. This analysis provides valuable insights into the relative merits of different vector representations (typed dependency vs. untyped embeddings) and compositional strategies (aligned vs. unaligned, additive vs. intersective) for this specific task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Methodologically Sound Evaluation:** The paper presents compelling results, achieving a new state-of-the-art Spearman correlation of 0.79, a substantial improvement over the previous best result of 0.71. The evaluation is rigorous, employing repeated 3-fold cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning and establishing the statistical significance of the improvements. Crucially, the authors include a strong and well-optimized baseline using modern word embeddings (CBOW), which itself outperforms the previous state-of-the-art. The fact that the proposed APT model surpasses this strong baseline significantly strengthens the paper's claims about the value of its syntax-aware approach.

2.  **Well-Motivated and Insightful Model:** The APT model is linguistically well-motivated. The concept of aligning vector spaces based on syntactic structure before composition is elegant and theoretically grounded. The detailed ablation of the model's components—comparing aligned, unaligned, and hybrid versions—is a major strength. This analysis leads to the interesting and non-obvious finding that while syntactic alignment is useful, a combination with unaligned first-order features is optimal for this task. This level of model analysis provides valuable insights for future work in compositional semantics.

3.  **Clarity of Presentation and Problem Formulation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It begins by clearly motivating the use of compositionality detection as a superior evaluation paradigm for compositional models compared to potentially confounded phrase similarity tasks. The experimental setup, including the data preprocessing and model parameters, is described in sufficient detail to facilitate reproducibility. The results are presented clearly in tables, and the conclusions are well-supported by the evidence provided.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope and Generalizability:** The experiments are conducted on a single, fairly small dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is the standard benchmark for this task, it raises questions about the generalizability of the findings. It is unclear how the APT model would perform on other syntactic constructions (e.g., verb-object phrases, adjective-noun phrases with adjectival modifiers) or in other languages. The conclusions about the superiority of typed dependency features might be specific to the idiosyncrasies of noun compound compositionality. The paper would be much stronger if it included even a small exploratory analysis on a different phrase type to demonstrate broader applicability.

2.  **Lack of Discussion on Model Complexity and Scalability:** The APT model relies on high-order dependency paths, resulting in very high-dimensional and sparse feature vectors (the paper mentions ~230,000 dimensions). This stands in contrast to the dense, low-dimensional, and computationally efficient word embedding models that are prevalent today. The paper does not discuss the practical trade-offs, such as the computational cost (time and memory) of building and using the APT lexicon compared to training word embeddings. A discussion of these practical considerations is essential for assessing the model's potential for real-world application.

3.  **Analysis Could Be Deeper:** While the quantitative results are excellent, the qualitative analysis is somewhat lacking. The paper concludes that the hybrid model is best because aligned and unaligned features provide complementary information, but it does not provide concrete examples to illustrate this. An error analysis or a case study would be highly beneficial. For instance, showing a specific non-compositional phrase (e.g., "spelling bee") where the APT model succeeds and the CBOW model fails, and explaining *which* specific dependency features enable this success, would provide much deeper insight into the model's mechanics and advantages.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The success of the unaligned (Eq. 7) and hybrid (Eq. 9) models is quite interesting, especially the fact that the purely unaligned model outperforms the purely aligned one. Could you elaborate on your interpretation of this? Does it suggest that for noun-noun compounds, the modifier (e.g., "grad") and the compound ("grad student") often appear in syntactically parallel contexts (e.g., as subjects of the same verbs), making their first-order features directly comparable without needing syntactic alignment?

2.  The framework is evaluated on noun compounds, which have a relatively simple head-modifier structure. How do you envision the APT alignment and composition mechanism generalizing to more complex phrases with multiple dependents, such as a verb with a subject and an object? Would composition be iterative, and how would the different alignment offsets (e.g., `NSUBJ` and `DOBJ`) be handled simultaneously?

3.  In your preprocessing, you mention that for a phrase like "(recently graduated) student", the dependency paths involving the modifier's modifier ("recently") are ignored. This seems to discard potentially important compositional information. Could you explain the rationale for this choice in more detail and discuss whether you considered alternative strategies for incorporating such nested modifications into the compositional process?
