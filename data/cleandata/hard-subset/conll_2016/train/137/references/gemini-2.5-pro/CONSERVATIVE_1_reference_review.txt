### Summary and Contributions

The paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, reformulating it as a test of compositional distributional models. The core idea is to measure the similarity between a vector for a compound phrase derived compositionally from its parts and a vector derived holistically from the phrase's occurrences in a corpus. A high similarity is taken to indicate high compositionality.

The primary contribution is a novel compositional method based on "Anchored Packed Trees" (APTs). This is a count-based model where features are typed, higher-order dependency paths. The model's key innovation is a process of "aligning" the vector space of a modifier with that of its head before composition, by prepending the dependency relation that connects them to all of the modifier's features.

A secondary contribution is a rigorous empirical evaluation on the Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark for noun compound compositionality. The authors show that their APT-based models, particularly a hybrid version, outperform not only the original result from Reddy et al. but also a strong baseline using modern word2vec embeddings with weighted addition, establishing a new state-of-the-art on this specific task.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodologically Sound Evaluation:** The experimental setup is careful and robust. The authors use the same large corpus (ukWaC) as the work they are directly comparing against (Reddy et al., 2011), ensuring comparability. They include strong and appropriate baselines, notably a well-tuned word2vec (cbow) model, which represents the current standard approach for many tasks. The use of repeated 3-fold cross-validation for parameter tuning is also good practice.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results on a Standard Benchmark:** The proposed method achieves a Spearman’s ρ of 0.79, a notable improvement over the previous state-of-the-art of 0.71 and the authors' own strong word2vec baseline of 0.74. This provides clear, quantitative evidence that for the specific task of predicting noun compound compositionality, the structural information captured by the APT model is highly effective.

3.  **Insightful Ablation and Analysis:** The paper does not simply present a single new model. It compares several variants: a purely "aligned" model (the main theoretical proposal), an "unaligned" typed dependency model, and a "hybrid" of the two. The finding that the unaligned and hybrid models outperform the purely aligned one is particularly interesting. It suggests that while the alignment mechanism contributes, the use of first-order typed dependency features from both constituents without alignment provides the most predictive signal, which is a valuable insight into the problem.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope and Unproven Generalizability:** The central weakness of this work is its narrow focus. The proposed APT model is evaluated on a single dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While it performs well here, this is very limited evidence for its utility as a general model of composition. The claims in the conclusion that the "APT approach to compositionality is reasonably promising" are not well-substantiated beyond this specific construction type. Established methods like adding word embeddings have shown remarkable robustness across a wide variety of compositional tasks (phrase similarity, paraphrase, sentence classification). It is unclear if the proposed complex, syntax-heavy machinery would provide any benefit, or even be applicable, to verb-object, adjective-noun, or full-sentence composition. The paper presents a solution that is highly tailored to one problem, without providing evidence of its broader applicability.

2.  **Complexity vs. Performance Gain:** The proposed APT framework introduces significant complexity, relying on higher-order dependency paths and a novel "alignment" operation. However, the results in Table 3 and 5 show that a simpler "unaligned" model using typed dependency vectors (ρ=0.78) performs nearly as well as the more complex "hybrid" model (ρ=0.79) and significantly better than the purely "aligned" model (ρ=0.75). This undermines the justification for the core novel mechanism of vector space alignment. The bulk of the performance gain over the word2vec baseline seems to come from using typed dependency features, rather than the more intricate alignment and reduction operations. The paper would be stronger if it better justified the necessity of its most complex components.

3.  **A Step Backwards in Representation:** The paper advocates for a return to high-dimensional, sparse, explicit vector representations (the authors note a feature space of ~230,000 dimensions). While such models have their place, the field has largely moved towards dense, low-dimensional embeddings for strong reasons, including computational efficiency, scalability, and better generalization due to the shared statistical strength captured in the dense dimensions. The paper makes no mention of the computational or memory costs of the APT approach, nor does it discuss how it might scale to tasks involving larger vocabularies or more complex compositional structures. This feels like a significant practical drawback compared to the highly scalable and widely-used neural embedding models it is compared against.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your results show that the "unaligned" model (ρ=0.78) is highly effective on its own, and the core "aligned" model (ρ=0.75) is less so, yet the "hybrid" model (ρ=0.79) is best. This suggests that the alignment mechanism provides only a marginal benefit on top of a strong, simpler model. Could you elaborate on the theoretical justification for the alignment mechanism, given that a simpler approach without it captures most of the performance? What specific types of compositional phenomena do you believe are captured by alignment that are missed by the unaligned model?

2.  The evaluation is confined to noun-noun compounds. How do you envision the APT framework, particularly the alignment and reduction steps, applying to other syntactic constructions, such as verb-object phrases or sentences with more complex dependency structures? Simple additive models of embeddings have proven to be a very difficult baseline to beat on these more general tasks. What is your argument for why a complex, dependency-path-based model would be superior in those settings?

3.  Could you comment on the practical scalability of the APT model? The feature space is noted to be ~230,000 dimensions. How do the memory requirements and computational time for vector construction and composition compare to a 100- or 300-dimension word2vec model on the same ukWaC corpus? Is this high-dimensional, sparse approach feasible for larger-scale, real-world applications?
