**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

This paper investigates the detection of non-compositionality in compound nouns. The core idea is to measure the similarity between a composed vector representation of a compound and its holistic vector representation derived directly from a corpus. The authors compare two main approaches for creating these vectors: standard, syntax-agnostic neural word embeddings (word2vec) combined with additive composition, and a syntax-aware model based on "Anchored Packed Trees" (APTs). The APT model uses typed, higher-order dependency paths as features and includes a structural alignment step before composition. Evaluating on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns, the authors show that their best-performing APT model achieves a new state-of-the-art Spearman correlation of 0.79 with human judgments, outperforming a strong word2vec baseline (0.74) and the previously reported best result (0.71).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  An empirical demonstration that syntax-aware compositional models, which leverage typed dependency features, can outperform strong syntax-agnostic baselines (like word2vec with addition) on the task of predicting the compositionality of noun compounds.
2.  A thorough ablation study of the proposed APT model, comparing aligned, unaligned, and hybrid feature combinations, as well as intersective and additive composition operations, which provides insight into the sources of the model's performance.
3.  The establishment of a new, stronger baseline for this task using tuned word2vec embeddings, which itself surpasses the previously published state-of-the-art.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of non-compositionality. Moving beyond a binary classification to predict a graded score is a well-motivated and nuanced approach to a fundamental limitation of many compositional models.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed hybrid APT model achieves a substantial and statistically significant improvement over both the original benchmark result and a carefully tuned word2vec baseline on a standard dataset. The performance gain from ρ=0.71 to ρ=0.79 is impressive.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a rigorous comparison, including multiple variants of their own model and several configurations for the word embedding baseline. The analysis of different parameters (e.g., the weighting parameter *h*, the hybrid parameter *q*, PPMI smoothing) is thorough and lends credibility to the results. The finding that a hybrid of aligned and unaligned features performs best is particularly interesting.

**Weaknesses:**

Despite the strong results, the paper suffers from a number of methodological and presentational flaws that, in aggregate, undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Methodological Opacity:** The central method of the paper, "Composition via Anchored Packed Trees," is not described in sufficient detail to be fully understood or reimplemented. The description relies heavily on a suppressed reference. Key concepts are introduced without adequate explanation. For example:
    *   The "reduction" operation in Equation 1 is defined recursively but the base case and the precise meaning of "a relation cancels with its inverse" are not fully explicated.
    *   The constraint that "all co-occurrence types are required to have a tree-based interpretation" is mentioned without defining what this interpretation is or how it leads to the "elimination of some incompatible co-occurrence types."
    *   This lack of self-containment is a major obstacle to evaluating the technical soundness and novelty of the proposed method.

2.  **Limited and Potentially Misleading Evaluation:**
    *   The evaluation is confined to a single, small dataset of 90 noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard benchmark, making broad claims about the general utility of APTs for composition based on this narrow scope is premature. The model's effectiveness on other phrasal types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object) remains entirely untested.
    *   A critical detail in Footnote 3 casts doubt on the evaluation protocol. The authors state that similarity is computed only on *1st-order* dependency features because higher-order features in the composed vectors are "not reliable." This is problematic. The core motivation for the APT model is its use of higher-order features for composition. If the very features that are meant to enable superior composition are then discarded at evaluation time, it is unclear what is actually being measured. This approach seems to discard the primary information the model is designed to capture, and the rationale is not sufficiently justified.

3.  **Lack of Attention to Detail and Formatting:** The paper is marred by a surprising number of minor errors and inconsistencies, which collectively suggest a lack of care in its preparation.
    *   **Reference Section:** The bibliography is exceptionally poorly formatted. Multiple entries contain placeholders (e.g., "?", "\Q2010\E"), inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Baroni and Lenci.,? ..., Baroni and Lenci.,"), and artifacts from bibliography software ("Coecke et al\."). The Schmid (1994) reference is incomplete. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   **In-text Clarity:** Justifications provided in footnotes are unscientific; for instance, Footnote 1 claims POS tags are omitted because they "can be easily inferred by the reader." The clarity of a scientific paper should not rely on reader inference.
    *   **Equation Definitions:** The parameters in the PMI formula (Equation 3) are not explicitly defined, forcing the reader to infer their meaning from context and Equation 2. Standard notation for composition operations is not used, and the custom operators are not clearly defined as pointwise operations.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you please provide a more self-contained and precise definition of the APT reduction mechanism and the "tree-based interpretation" constraint? What specific types of dependency paths are eliminated by this constraint, and what is the impact on performance if it is removed?
2.  Can you please elaborate on the decision to evaluate similarity using only 1st-order dependency features (Footnote 3)? Why are the composed higher-order features unreliable? Does this not invalidate the central premise that composing with higher-order features produces a better overall representation? What are the results if similarity is computed over the full feature space?
3.  The superiority of the `unaligned` and `hybrid` models over the `aligned`-only model is counter-intuitive given the initial motivation for alignment. Could you provide a deeper analysis of this result? Does this suggest that for English noun-noun compounds, the syntactic contexts of the modifier noun and the full compound are already so similar that explicit alignment offers little benefit or even introduces noise?
