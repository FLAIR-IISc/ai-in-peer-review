### **Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of multiword expressions, focusing on noun compounds. The core idea is to measure the similarity between a vector representation composed from a phrase's constituents and the holistic vector representation of the phrase observed in a corpus. The authors argue that the nature of the contextual features used to build these vectors is crucial. They compare standard methods using untyped, proximity-based contexts (implicit in word2vec) with their proposed approach, Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses explicit, typed, higher-order dependency paths as features. They introduce a novel composition mechanism for these typed spaces called "alignment" or "offsetting". The models are evaluated on the Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark, where the goal is to predict human compositionality ratings.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A strong empirical demonstration that syntactically-rich, typed dependency features are superior to untyped proximity-based features for modeling the compositionality of noun compounds. This is shown by the proposed APT models significantly outperforming strong word2vec baselines.
2.  The introduction and evaluation of a structured compositional operation ("alignment") for dependency-based vector spaces. The paper provides an interesting finding that a hybrid model combining this aligned representation with an unaligned one yields the best performance.
3.  A new state-of-the-art result on the widely used Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality dataset, improving the Spearman correlation from the previous best of 0.714 to 0.79.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a methodologically sound and rigorous evaluation. The authors compare their proposed APT models against appropriate and strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art and well-tuned neural word embedding models (word2vec). The performance gains are substantial and shown to be statistically significant, which convincingly supports the central claims of the paper. The use of repeated 3-fold cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning is appropriate and adds to the reliability of the results.

2.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Approach:** The APT framework is linguistically well-motivated. The central idea that syntactic structure should be encoded in the feature space for composition is compelling. Moving beyond first-order dependencies to higher-order paths is a logical step, and the "alignment" mechanism is an elegant solution to the problem of composing vectors from different syntactic categories (e.g., modifiers and heads) that naturally reside in different feature spaces. The discovery that a hybrid of aligned and unaligned features performs best is a subtle but important finding, suggesting that both direct semantic associations and structurally transformed ones contribute to modeling compositionality.

3.  **Clear Analysis and Insightful Discussion:** The paper provides a clear and insightful analysis of its results. The authors effectively contrast typed vs. untyped features and explain why the former are more powerful for this task. The discussion around the negative effects of heavy subsampling (in word2vec) or shifted PMI (in count models) on low-frequency compounds is sharp and provides a plausible explanation for some of the parameter-tuning results. Furthermore, the analysis of the optimal weights for the head/modifier and aligned/unaligned components provides valuable insights into the model's behavior and the nature of noun compound compositionality.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper's claims are based entirely on a single, rather small dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is the standard benchmark for this specific task, it limits the generality of the conclusions. It is unclear whether the superiority of the APT approach would hold for other types of phrases (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object), in other languages, or on other composition-related tasks like phrase similarity or paraphrase detection. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but it remains the most significant weakness of the study.

2.  **Clarity and Reproducibility of the APT Method:** The description of the APT model is dense, particularly the "reduction" operation in Equation 1 and the associated constraint of a "tree-based interpretation." For a reader not familiar with the authors' previous (and suppressed) work, these concepts are difficult to fully grasp. A more detailed, concrete example illustrating how a feature path is reduced (e.g., `NMOD.NMOD`) would greatly improve clarity and reproducibility. It is also unclear what constitutes an "incompatible co-occurrence type" and how these are filtered.

3.  **Scalability and Practicality:** The APT model is an explicit, count-based model operating in a very high-dimensional space (~230,000 dimensions for nouns). In an era dominated by dense, low-dimensional embeddings, the practical implications of this choice are not discussed. What are the computational and memory costs of building and operating with these APT representations compared to neural models? This is a critical consideration for the potential adoption of this method in larger-scale NLP applications. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of these practical trade-offs or potential ways to create dense versions of these syntactically-informed vectors.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The optimal weight for the head noun, `h`, was found to be in the range [0.3, 0.5], suggesting the modifier is often more informative than the head. This is somewhat counter-intuitive, as the head noun typically defines the semantic type of the compound (e.g., a *student* in *grad student*). Could you offer a linguistic intuition or provide examples from an error analysis that might explain this finding?

2.  The preprocessing step of creating holistic vectors for the 90 compounds is crucial. You mention that when modifying the dependency paths, paths including the *modifying* constituent are ignored. Could you please clarify this with an example? For instance, in "recently graduated student", how would the dependency path from "recently" to "graduated" be handled during the creation of the elementary vector for "graduated" and the holistic vector for "graduated student"? How sensitive is the overall method to parsing errors, which are known to be frequent around multiword expressions?
