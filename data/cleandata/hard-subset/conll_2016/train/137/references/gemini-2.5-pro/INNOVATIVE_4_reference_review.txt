### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel, structurally-aware compositional model based on "Anchored Packed Trees" (APTs) and applies it to the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds. The core idea is to represent words not with vectors of co-occurring words, but with vectors of typed, higher-order dependency paths. Composition is then performed not by simple vector addition, but through a syntax-guided "alignment" process that transforms a modifier's vector space to match its head's, followed by a pointwise operation.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel and bold compositional framework (APT):** The paper proposes a genuinely new way to think about composition in distributional semantics. Moving beyond the standard "pre-trained vectors + simple function" paradigm, the APT model integrates syntactic structure directly into the feature space and the compositional operation itself. The concept of "offsetting" feature spaces based on dependency paths is an elegant and powerful idea.
2.  **State-of-the-art performance on a challenging task:** The proposed method is shown to significantly outperform previous work and strong baselines, including well-tuned word2vec models, on the established Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark for noun compound compositionality. This demonstrates that the theoretical novelty translates into tangible empirical gains.
3.  **Insightful analysis of structured vs. unstructured features:** The comparison between aligned, unaligned, and hybrid models yields the interesting finding that both raw first-order dependency features and structurally transformed higher-order features contribute to the final prediction. This nuances the discussion around the role of syntax in composition.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Advance:** This work is a refreshing departure from the incremental improvements often seen in distributional semantics. The APT framework is a well-motivated, linguistically-grounded model of composition that directly tackles the limitations of commutative operations like vector addition. The idea of transforming vector spaces based on their syntactic relationship before combination is a significant conceptual contribution that could inspire a new line of research. It is precisely this kind of bold, foundational work that the field needs.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Validation:** The paper doesn't just propose an interesting idea; it demonstrates its effectiveness. Achieving a Spearman's rho of 0.79 is a substantial improvement over both the previous state-of-the-art (0.71) and a very strong, well-tuned neural baseline (0.74). The experimental setup is sound, using the same large corpus as prior work for a fair comparison and performing cross-validation for parameter tuning. This strong result provides compelling evidence that explicitly modeling syntactic structure within the compositional process is highly beneficial for this task.

3.  **Thoughtful Model Ablations and Analysis:** The authors do an excellent job of dissecting their model's performance. The comparison between aligned (purely compositional via structure), unaligned (more akin to a feature union), and hybrid models is particularly illuminating. The fact that the hybrid model performs best suggests that the reality of compositionality is complex, benefiting from both the direct contextual evidence of the constituents and the structurally inferred context. This is a subtle and important finding. The discussion of why smoothing PMI and using naive additive models works better than shifted PMI/subsampling for this specific task (due to low-frequency compounds) also shows a deep understanding of the models and the data.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are areas that could be strengthened. I view these less as reasons for rejection and more as important directions for the authors to consider in future work.

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The APT framework is presented as a general approach to composition, but it is only evaluated on the specific case of two-word noun compounds. Its applicability and performance on other syntactic constructions (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object, prepositional phrases, or full sentences) remain an open question. While this narrow focus is acceptable for a conference paper introducing a fundamentally new idea, the claims about the model's general promise would be stronger with even a preliminary experiment on a different phrasal type.

2.  **Scalability and Efficiency Concerns:** The feature space of the APT model, being based on dependency paths, is explicitly high-dimensional and sparse. The paper mentions dimensions in the hundreds of thousands. There is no discussion of the computational complexity, memory footprint, or scalability of this approach compared to dense, low-dimensional models like word2vec. While performance is paramount, practical applicability is also a concern, and some discussion of these trade-offs would be welcome.

3.  **Comparison to Other Structured Models:** The primary baseline for composition is the simple weighted addition of word embeddings. While beating this is a crucial benchmark, the related work mentions more sophisticated, structurally-aware models (e.g., tensor-based methods). The experimental comparison would have been more comprehensive if it included at least one such model as a baseline, to better situate the performance of APTs within the landscape of structured compositional approaches, rather than just against structure-agnostic ones.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The success of the hybrid model (Equation 9), which combines unaligned first-order features with aligned higher-order features, is fascinating. Could you provide more linguistic intuition on this? Does it suggest that for a compound like "grad student," the contexts of "student" (unaligned) are more informative than, say, the contexts of words that "grad" modifies in other sentences (which is what the aligned model primarily leverages)?

2.  Could you comment on the computational trade-offs of the APT model? Specifically, how does the time and memory required to build the lexicon and perform composition scale with the order of the dependency paths considered and the size of the corpus?
