**Summary of the paper**
This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds. The authors propose a compositional method based on what they term Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses typed, higher-order dependency paths as distributional features. The core idea of their model is to "align" the vector space of a modifier to that of its head before composition by transforming its features based on the syntactic relation between them. They evaluate this method on the noun compound dataset from Reddy et al. (2011), measuring the correlation between the similarity of a composed vector and an observed phrase vector with human judgments of compositionality. The authors compare their APT model (in several configurations) against the previous state-of-the-art, as well as a newly established baseline using word2vec embeddings, and report superior results.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is an empirical study showing that a compositional model using rich, typed dependency features (the "APT" model) can achieve a new state-of-the-art result on the specific task of predicting compositionality for the 90 noun compounds in the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.
2.  A secondary contribution is the establishment of a stronger baseline for this task using tuned word2vec (cbow) embeddings, which itself outperforms the previously published result.

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates a clear and significant improvement on the target task. The best proposed model achieves a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, which is a substantial improvement over the 0.71 reported by Reddy et al. (2011) and also higher than the authors' own well-tuned word2vec baseline of 0.74. This provides solid evidence for the effectiveness of the overall approach on this dataset.
2.  **Systematic Comparison:** The authors conduct a thorough set of experiments comparing different configurations of their proposed model. The ablation between aligned, unaligned, and hybrid models, as well as the use of different vector weighting schemes (PPMI, counts) and composition operators (intersective, additive), provides valuable insight into the behavior of the model. The inclusion and tuning of a word2vec baseline is also a commendable practice.
3.  **Well-motivated Features:** The use of typed, higher-order dependency paths as features is a principled choice for a task that is inherently syntactic and semantic. The results suggest that this rich, structured information is highly effective for modeling the compositionality of noun compounds, likely capturing more precise contextual information than traditional window-based co-occurrence counts.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Limited and Dated Evaluation:** The paper's claims rest entirely on a single, small dataset of 90 English noun compounds introduced over a decade ago (Reddy et al., 2011). While this allows for direct comparison with prior work, it severely limits the generality of the findings. It is unclear if the proposed complex machinery would be effective for other phrasal types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object) or on larger, more diverse datasets. The conclusion that the APT approach is "reasonably promising" as a general method for compositionality seems premature given this narrow empirical evidence.
2.  **Questionable Justification for Model Complexity:** The central novelty of the APT framework appears to be the syntax-guided "alignment" of vector spaces before composition. However, the authors' own results cast significant doubt on the utility of this mechanism. The "Unaligned" model, which forgoes this complex step and simply combines the typed feature vectors, achieves a score (ρ=0.78) that is nearly identical to the best-performing "Hybrid" model (ρ=0.79) and significantly better than the purely "Aligned" model (ρ=0.75). This strongly suggests that the performance gain comes not from the novel compositional machinery, but from the power of the underlying typed dependency features themselves. A simpler model based on adding these rich feature vectors appears to capture most of the benefit.
3.  **Conflation of Features and Composition Method:** The primary comparison is between the proposed method using typed dependency features and baselines (Reddy et al., word2vec) that use untyped, window-based co-occurrence features. This makes it difficult to isolate the contribution of the *composition method* from the contribution of the *feature representation*. A more compelling baseline would be to apply a standard composition function (e.g., weighted addition) directly to the authors' typed dependency feature vectors. The "Unaligned `⊔UNI`" model is effectively this baseline, yet it is presented as a variant of the proposed method rather than as a simpler, powerful alternative against which the more complex "Aligned" model should be justified. The results indicate this simpler baseline is extremely difficult to beat.
4.  **Novelty Obscured by Suppressed Reference:** The paper relies on a suppressed reference for the detailed proposal of the APT framework. This prevents a full assessment of the technical novelty of the work presented here versus what has been established in prior work by the authors.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The performance of the "Unaligned" model (ρ=0.78) is remarkably strong, nearly matching the "Hybrid" model and outperforming the "Aligned" model. Could you elaborate on the implications of this finding? Does it not suggest that the complex alignment and reduction machinery at the heart of the APT proposal provides minimal benefit for this task, and that the performance is primarily driven by using a simple additive model over rich, typed dependency features?
2.  To better disentangle the effects of feature choice from the composition model, it would be informative to see how an established, more complex compositional model performs on your feature set. For instance, have you considered applying a model like the lexical function model (Baroni and Zamparelli, 2010) to your typed dependency vectors as an additional baseline?
3.  Given the evaluation is confined to a single, small dataset of noun-noun compounds, can you comment on the potential scalability and applicability of the APT method to other compositional phenomena, such as verb-argument or adjective-noun constructions, which might involve different syntactic and semantic challenges?
