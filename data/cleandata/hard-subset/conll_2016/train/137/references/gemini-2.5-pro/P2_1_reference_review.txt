### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the problem of detecting non-compositional phrases, framing it as an evaluation task for compositional distributional semantic models. The core idea is that the similarity between a composed phrase vector and an observed (holistic) phrase vector should correlate with human judgments of compositionality.

The paper's primary contributions are:

1.  **A new state-of-the-art result on the Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality detection benchmark.** The proposed method, based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), achieves a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, a substantial improvement over the previous best of 0.71.
2.  **The application and thorough evaluation of a structured, dependency-based compositional model (APT) for this task.** The APT model uses higher-order, typed dependency paths as features. The paper introduces and evaluates a novel composition mechanism based on "aligning" the vector spaces of constituents by offsetting their feature paths, comparing this to simpler unaligned and hybrid approaches.
3.  **A rigorous empirical comparison against strong, modern baselines.** The authors show that a well-tuned word2vec (cbow) model with simple additive composition already surpasses the previous state-of-the-art, providing a much stronger baseline (ρ=0.74). The fact that the APT model significantly outperforms this strong baseline underscores the effectiveness of its structured, typed features.

### Strengths

1.  **Impressive Empirical Performance:** The paper's main strength is its excellent results. Achieving a Spearman's correlation of 0.79 on this task is a significant leap forward. The improvements are not marginal; they are demonstrated to be statistically significant over both the original work and a very strong, re-implemented baseline using modern word embeddings. This makes a compelling case for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

2.  **Well-Motivated and Intuitive Model:** The APT model is linguistically well-motivated. The use of typed, higher-order dependency paths directly encodes syntactic and semantic roles that are lost in standard proximity-based models (like word2vec). The core compositional idea of "aligning" vector spaces by prepending the dependency path from the head to the dependent (e.g., adding an 'AMOD' prefix to an adjective's features before composing with a noun) is an elegant way to handle the structural asymmetry of modification.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis:** The experimental setup is rigorous. The authors use the same corpus as the original paper for comparability and perform 3-fold cross-validation to tune parameters. More importantly, they test several variants of their model (aligned, unaligned, hybrid) and composition operators (intersective, additive). This provides valuable ablations that yield interesting insights. For example, the finding that a hybrid of aligned and unaligned features performs best suggests that both the raw first-order contexts of the modifier and the structurally transformed second-order contexts contribute valuable, complementary information for predicting the compound's meaning.

4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The technical details of the APT model, particularly the feature generation and alignment process, are explained clearly with a concrete example in Tables 1 and 2. The experimental setup and results are presented logically, making the paper's claims easy to verify.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper's claims are validated on a single, fairly small dataset consisting of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard and important benchmark for this specific phenomenon, it represents a very narrow slice of language. It is unclear whether the superiority of the APT model would generalize to other constructions (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object) or other tasks where compositionality is crucial (e.g., paraphrase detection, sentence similarity). The reliance on a single, small test set is the most significant weakness of the work.

2.  **Potential Novelty and Scalability Concerns:** The paper cites "(reference suppressed)" when introducing the APT model, suggesting the core method has been presented elsewhere. While its application and analysis on this task are novel, this may reduce the paper's contribution from a methodological standpoint. Furthermore, the feature space of the APT model is very high-dimensional and sparse (~230,000 dimensions for nouns). The paper does not discuss the computational costs, memory footprint, or scalability of this approach compared to dense, low-dimensional embeddings, which could be a significant practical disadvantage.

3.  **The Unaligned Model's Success is Under-analyzed:** One of the most interesting results is the strong performance of the *unaligned* typed model (ρ=0.78), which outperforms the more theoretically motivated *aligned* model (ρ=0.75). The hybrid model's success relies heavily on this unaligned component. The paper briefly states the intuition that first-order features of the modifier may be good indicators for the compound. However, this finding seems to partially undermine the central motivation for the complex alignment procedure. A more in-depth discussion of why simple concatenation of typed feature sets works so well for noun-noun compounds would strengthen the paper's analysis.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given that the evaluation is confined to 90 noun-noun compounds, could you comment on the expected performance of the APT model on other phrasal types? For example, in a verb-object phrase, the alignment via `DOBJ` path seems particularly crucial. Do you have preliminary results or strong intuitions about whether the performance gap between APT and word2vec+add would widen or narrow on other constructions?

2.  The strong performance of the unaligned model was surprising. Does this suggest that for noun-noun compounds in English, the modifier and head often appear in similar first-order dependency contexts (e.g., both can be subjects/objects of similar verbs), making the alignment less critical than for other phrasal types?

3.  Could you provide some information on the practical aspects of the APT model? Specifically, what are the relative training times and memory requirements for generating the APT lexicon versus training the 300-dim word2vec model on the ukWaC corpus?
