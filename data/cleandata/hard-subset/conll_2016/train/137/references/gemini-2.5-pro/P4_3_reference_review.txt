### Summary of the Paper
This paper investigates the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds. The central approach is to measure the similarity between a composed vector representation of a compound's constituents and a holistic vector representation learned from observing the compound in a corpus. A high similarity is hypothesized to correlate with high human-judged compositionality. The primary technical contribution is the application and evaluation of a compositional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses typed, higher-order dependency paths as distributional features. The authors systematically compare several composition strategies within this framework, including one that syntactically aligns the constituents' vector spaces ("aligned"), one that does not ("unaligned"), and a "hybrid" model that combines both. These APT-based models are benchmarked against strong baselines, including composed neural word embeddings (word2vec) and the previous state-of-the-art method, on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 noun compounds. The results demonstrate that the proposed hybrid APT model significantly outperforms all baselines, establishing a new state-of-the-art on this task.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel Analysis of Syntax-Based Composition for Compositionality Detection:** The paper demonstrates that a distributional model using typed, higher-order dependency features (APT) is highly effective for detecting noun compound compositionality. The key finding is that a hybrid composition strategy, which combines a syntactically-motivated alignment of feature spaces with a simpler unaligned combination, yields the best performance. This provides strong evidence that rich syntactic information in distributional features is crucial for this task.
2.  **Rigorous Empirical Evaluation and a New State-of-the-Art:** The study presents a thorough and methodologically sound comparison of different distributional models (typed count-based vs. untyped prediction-based) and composition operations. By outperforming strong baselines, including optimized word2vec models (ρ=0.74) and the previous state-of-the-art (ρ=0.71), the proposed method achieves a new state-of-the-art performance (ρ=0.79) on a standard benchmark dataset.

### Strengths
1.  **Technically Sound Methodology:** The experimental setup is rigorous and well-designed. The authors use the same corpus (ukWaC) as the prior work they compare against, ensuring a fair comparison. They employ cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., weighting parameters `h` and `q`) and report averaged results with significance testing. The inclusion of well-tuned neural embedding baselines (CBOW and Skip-gram) makes the claims about the superiority of the APT model robust and convincing.
2.  **Clear and Significant Results:** The paper reports substantial and statistically significant improvements over prior work and strong baselines. The final result of ρ=0.79 is a notable advance over the previous best of ρ=0.71. The presentation of results in Tables 3, 4, and 5 is clear and allows for a detailed comparison of the different model configurations. This ablation-style analysis effectively isolates the sources of performance gain, highlighting the contributions of smoothed PPMI, the hybrid composition strategy, and the use of typed dependency features.
3.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The use of dependency paths as features is linguistically well-motivated for modeling composition. The paper provides a clear intuition for why higher-order features are important and presents a thoughtful analysis of why simple feature space alignment might not be sufficient for noun-noun compounds, leading to the development of the successful hybrid model. This contrasts with many approaches that treat composition as a black-box vector operation.
4.  **High Clarity and Readability:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The core APT model and the composition process are explained effectively with concrete examples (Table 1 and 2), which demystifies the method. The motivation, experimental setup, and conclusions are all articulated clearly, making the paper's contributions accessible.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments are confined to a single, relatively small dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard benchmark for the task, the paper's strong conclusions about the general effectiveness of the APT model for compositionality detection are not fully substantiated. The model's performance on other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object) or in other languages remains an open question. For instance, the syntactic alignment procedure might be even more critical for constructions with clearer head-dependent roles than N-N compounds.
2.  **Unclear Novelty of the Core APT Method:** The paper states that the APT model was proposed "Elsewhere (reference suppressed)," which makes it difficult to assess the novelty of the underlying distributional framework. The primary contribution of this work appears to be the application of this framework to the compositionality detection task, along with the novel investigation of the hybrid aligned/unaligned composition strategy. If the core APT model is not new, the paper's contribution is more focused on analysis and application rather than the introduction of a fundamentally new compositional method.
3.  **Lack of Discussion on Scalability and Efficiency:** The paper reports a feature space size of up to 230,000 dimensions for the APT model. High-dimensional, sparse, count-based models can be significantly more computationally intensive and memory-demanding than low-dimensional dense models like word2vec. The paper does not discuss these practical trade-offs. A brief analysis of the computational costs (e.g., time to build the lexicon, memory usage, composition time) would provide a more complete picture of the proposed method's applicability.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you clarify the novelty of the Anchored Packed Trees (APT) framework itself? Is the main contribution of this paper the framework, or is it the specific application to compositionality detection and the novel analysis of combining aligned and unaligned composition strategies for this task?
2.  The results show that the unaligned model performs very strongly and that the optimal hybrid model gives more weight to the unaligned features (optimal `q` is in [0.3, 0.5], implying 1-`q` > 0.5). This is an interesting finding. Could you provide more linguistic intuition as to why unaligned typed features are so effective for noun-noun compounds? Does this imply that the constituents independently share more informative contexts with the compound than what is revealed through syntactic alignment via the NMOD relation?
3.  Given that the syntactic alignment in the APT model is designed to handle diverse dependency relations, have you considered evaluating your approach on other phrasal types where the syntactic relationship is less ambiguous than the NMOD relation common in N-N compounds (e.g., verb-direct object)? Would you expect the contribution of the aligned component of the model to be more pronounced in such cases?
