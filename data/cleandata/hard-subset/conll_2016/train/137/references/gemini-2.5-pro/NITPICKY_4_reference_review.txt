### Review

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the detection of non-compositional noun compounds using distributional semantic models. The primary contribution is the proposal and evaluation of a compositional method based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses typed, higher-order dependency paths as features. The authors demonstrate that this linguistically-informed approach, particularly a hybrid version that combines aligned and unaligned feature spaces, achieves a new state-of-the-art Spearman's ρ of 0.79 on the Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark. A secondary contribution is a thorough empirical comparison showing that this method outperforms not only the previous state-of-the-art but also a strong baseline using modern word embeddings (word2vec) with additive composition.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling results, achieving a new state-of-the-art on a standard and well-defined task. The reported correlation of 0.79 is a significant improvement over the previous result of 0.714 and the strong word2vec baseline of 0.74, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

2.  **Well-Motivated Model:** The APT model is linguistically motivated. By encoding syntactic dependency paths as features and explicitly aligning vector spaces based on the syntactic relation between constituents, the model moves beyond "naïve" compositional operations like simple addition. This provides a principled way to incorporate structure into the composition process, which is a key challenge in distributional semantics.

3.  **Solid Baseline Comparison:** The authors are diligent in comparing their model against a strong and relevant baseline (word2vec with weighted addition). They explore several parameter settings for the baseline, lending credibility to their claim of superiority. The inclusion of simpler baselines like frequency (FREQ) and random (RAND) also helps to contextualize the performance of all models.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper presents a promising direction and strong headline results, it is marred by a significant number of minor flaws, ambiguities in the methodology, and presentational issues that collectively undermine my confidence in the work.

1.  **Insufficient Clarity in Model Description:** The core contribution, the APT model, is not described in sufficient detail for the reader to fully grasp its mechanics.
    *   The model was proposed "elsewhere (reference suppressed)," which makes the current paper difficult to evaluate as a self-contained contribution.
    *   The formal definition of feature reduction in Equation (1) is opaque. The condition `τ = τ1 r r τ2` is not explained; the double `r` appears to be a typo, and the set `R` is not defined. The explanation that "a relation cancels with its inverse relation" is intuitive but insufficient as a formal definition.
    *   The constraint that co-occurrence types must have a "tree-based interpretation (τ ∈ R∗R∗)" is stated without defining the notation or explaining the theoretical implication.

2.  **Questionable Methodological Choices and Missing Details:** Several decisions in the experimental setup are either not well-justified or lack crucial details for reproducibility and interpretation.
    *   **Preprocessing:** The paper states that for corpus preprocessing, "dependency paths including the modifying constituent are ignored." The rationale provided is weak and anecdotal ("we do not apply adverbs to compound nouns"). This is a strong simplifying assumption that could discard valuable contextual information, and its impact is not analyzed.
    *   **Evaluation:** In Footnote 3, the evaluation is restricted to 1st-order dependency features because higher-order features in the composed vectors are deemed "not reliable." This seems to be an arbitrary limitation of the experimental design (using elementary APTs of order ≤ 2) rather than a fundamental property of the model. A more rigorous evaluation would have generated higher-order elementary APTs to properly test the model's compositional predictions.
    *   **Statistical Significance:** The paper claims that a difference greater than 0.005 is statistically significant at the 95% level. However, the statistical test used to determine this (e.g., paired t-test, bootstrap resampling) is not mentioned, nor is the number of repetitions for the cross-validation. This is a critical omission for an empirical paper.
    *   **Parameter Search:** For the word2vec baseline, the authors state it is "not possible or appropriate to carry out an exhaustive parameter search." While exhaustive searches are difficult, this justification is weak. The explored parameter space is quite limited, which could potentially handicap the baseline.

3.  **Superficial Analysis of Results:** The analysis of *why* certain models perform better is often superficial.
    *   The finding that shifted PMI (analogous to negative sampling) hurt performance is dismissed with a brief "suspicion" related to low frequency. This is an interesting negative result that warrants a more detailed analysis rather than a hand-wavy explanation.
    *   The discussion of the optimal weights `h` and `q` is cursory. The conclusion that the modifier is "slightly more informative" is interesting but not explored further. A deeper dive into these parameters could have provided more insight into the nature of noun-compound compositionality.

4.  **Extremely Poor Formatting of References:** The bibliography is riddled with severe formatting errors, suggesting a significant lack of attention to detail. This is not a minor cosmetic issue; it reflects poorly on the overall care with which the paper was prepared. Examples include:
    *   Inconsistent author-year formatting (e.g., `Baroni and Lenci.,? \Q2010\E, Baroni and Lenci.,`).
    *   Inconsistent use of diacritics (e.g., `Padó` in the text vs. `Pado` in the references).
    *   Extraneous characters and incorrect LaTeX commands (`Coecke et al\.,?`, `Schmid, Schmid.,? \Q1994\E, Schmid.,`).
    *   The title itself, "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring," is unnecessarily colloquial for a scientific publication.

**Recommendation: Reject**

Despite the strong performance reported, the accumulation of methodological ambiguities, missing experimental details, and egregious formatting issues is too substantial to ignore. The lack of a clear, self-contained description of the core model is a major weakness. While the core idea is promising, the paper in its current form does not meet the high standards of rigor and presentation expected at CoNLL. A thorough revision to address the clarity of the model, justify experimental choices, and meticulously correct the presentation would be required before this work is ready for publication.
