### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, using the benchmark dataset from Reddy et al. (2011). The authors propose a compositional distributional model called Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which utilizes typed, higher-order dependency paths as contextual features. The key idea is that the feature spaces of constituent words can be "aligned" based on the syntactic path connecting them before being composed. The paper evaluates this method by correlating the similarity between a composed vector and an observed (holistic) vector for a compound with human judgments of its compositionality. The authors conduct a thorough comparison of their APT model (in aligned, unaligned, and hybrid configurations) against strong baselines, including the original results from Reddy et al. and additive composition of word2vec embeddings. The results show that the proposed APT-based models significantly outperform all baselines, establishing a new state-of-the-art on this task.

### Main Contributions
1.  **Demonstration of the effectiveness of structured, syntax-based features for modeling compositionality.** The primary contribution is the empirical evidence that a compositional model using explicit, typed, higher-order dependency paths (the APT model) significantly outperforms strong, state-of-the-art models based on untyped, proximity-based contexts (like word2vec). This highlights the value of incorporating explicit syntactic structure for this specific, fine-grained semantic task.
2.  **A detailed ablation study of a novel compositional mechanism.** The paper presents a thorough analysis of its APT model, systematically evaluating the impact of different components: the alignment mechanism (aligned vs. unaligned vs. hybrid), the composition operator (intersective vs. additive), and feature weighting schemes (PPMI vs. raw counts). This provides valuable insights into what aspects of the model contribute most to its success.
3.  **Establishment of a new state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark.** The paper improves the Spearman correlation on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset from a previous best of 0.71 to 0.79, providing a strong new benchmark for future work on noun compound compositionality.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong and convincing empirical results.** The paper's central claim is well-supported by a clear and significant improvement over multiple strong baselines. The final APT model achieves a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, a substantial gain over the original SOTA of 0.71 and a well-tuned word2vec baseline (0.74). The experimental setup is rigorous, using the same large corpus as the original work and performing careful cross-validation to tune parameters.
2.  **Well-motivated and elegant model.** The APT model is linguistically well-motivated. The idea of using full dependency paths as features captures richer contextual information than simple word windows. Furthermore, the concept of aligning vector spaces by "offsetting" features based on the syntactic relation between a head and its dependent is an intuitive and principled approach to structured composition.
3.  **Thorough analysis and ablation studies.** The authors do an excellent job of dissecting their model's performance. The comparison between aligned, unaligned, and hybrid models is particularly insightful, as is the exploration of different composition functions (e.g., `⊔UNI` vs. `⊔INT`). This level of detail adds significant value and helps the reader understand the source of the performance gains.
4.  **Clarity of presentation.** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The core concepts of the APT model, including feature generation and the alignment mechanism, are explained clearly with helpful examples (Tables 1 and 2). The motivation, experimental setup, and conclusions are all laid out logically.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited scope of evaluation.** The experiments are confined to a single, relatively small dataset of 90 noun compounds. While this is the established benchmark for this specific problem, it leaves the generalizability of the APT model in question. The related work section notes that simple additive models perform very strongly on other compositional tasks (e.g., phrase similarity, paraphrase detection). It is unclear whether the benefits of the complex, structured APT model would transfer to these other tasks or to other types of phrases (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object).
2.  **Counter-intuitive results regarding the alignment mechanism.** A core motivation of the APT model is the syntactic alignment of feature spaces via offsetting. However, the results show that the `unaligned` model (ρ=0.78) performs almost as well as the best `hybrid` model (ρ=0.79) and significantly better than the purely `aligned` model (ρ=0.75). The paper acknowledges this finding but does not offer a deep analysis or a strong hypothesis for why this is the case. This weakens the argument for the proposed alignment mechanism being the key innovation, as the simpler, unaligned version provides most of the benefit.
3.  **Potential scalability and sparsity concerns.** The APT model relies on explicit, high-dimensional vector representations (e.g., ~230,000 dimensions for nouns). In contrast to dense, low-dimensional embeddings, such models can be more susceptible to data sparsity and computationally more expensive to store and manipulate. The paper does not discuss these practical trade-offs, which are important considerations for real-world applications.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The finding that the unaligned and hybrid models outperform the purely aligned model is surprising. Could you elaborate on your hypothesis for why this occurs? Does this suggest that, for noun-noun compounds, the first-order contexts of the modifier (from its independent usages) are more predictive of the compound's meaning than the second-order contexts derived via the head? Or could it be an artifact of the specific NMOD relation that dominates this dataset?
2.  Given the complexity of the APT model compared to simple addition of word embeddings, have you considered its performance on other, more standard compositional tasks like the Mitchell & Lapata phrase similarity datasets? A strong performance there would significantly bolster the claims about the model's general utility for composition. How would you expect the explicit syntactic information to help in a task where simple addition is already a very strong baseline?
