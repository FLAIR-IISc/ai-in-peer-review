### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of detecting non-compositionality in noun compounds. The central idea is to measure the compositionality of a phrase by computing the similarity between its observed (holistic) distributional representation and a representation compositionally derived from its constituents. The authors propose a novel compositional method based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses typed, higher-order dependency paths as distributional features. The key mechanism of the APT model is an "alignment" step, where the vector space of a modifier is transformed to match the space of the head word before composition.

The authors evaluate their method on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 noun compounds, correlating the model's compositionality scores with human judgments. They compare several variants of their APT model (including aligned, unaligned, and hybrid versions) against strong baselines, including a well-tuned word2vec model and the previous state-of-the-art. The proposed hybrid APT model achieves a new state-of-the-art Spearman's ρ of 0.79, significantly outperforming all baselines.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A powerful demonstration of the value of structured, syntax-aware features for compositionality modeling.** The paper convincingly shows that explicitly encoding higher-order dependency information allows for a more nuanced model of compositionality than what is achievable with standard untyped co-occurrence vectors, whether sparse (Reddy et al.) or dense (word2vec).
2.  **A novel and principled compositional model (APT) based on vector space alignment.** The proposed method for offsetting and reducing dependency paths to align vector spaces before composition is an elegant way to handle typed features for words in different syntactic roles, moving beyond the limitations of simple additive/multiplicative models.
3.  **A thorough empirical investigation that establishes a new state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark.** The paper provides a rigorous evaluation, exploring a wide range of model parameters and convincingly outperforming strong competitors.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Technical Contribution and Novelty:** The APT model is technically sound and well-motivated. It tackles a fundamental challenge in compositional distributional semantics: how to combine vectors that live in different "type spaces" (e.g., vectors for adjectives vs. nouns). The alignment-via-offsetting mechanism is a creative and linguistically grounded solution. This is a significant step beyond the naive, commutative operations (like addition) that are still prevalent.

2.  **Excellent Empirical Results and Analysis:** The paper achieves a substantial improvement over the previous state-of-the-art, raising the Spearman correlation from 0.71 to 0.79. The authors also establish a strong baseline with tuned word2vec models (ρ=0.74), showing that their gains are not just from using a better corpus or basic setup. The ablation-style comparison between aligned, unaligned, and hybrid models provides valuable insights into what aspects of the model are contributing to its success.

3.  **Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is very well-written. The introduction clearly frames the problem of non-compositionality as a confounding factor in evaluating compositional models. The motivation for using typed, higher-order dependencies is well-explained with a clear example in Table 1. The related work section is comprehensive and positions the current work effectively.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper's empirical claims rest entirely on a single, fairly small dataset (90 noun-noun compounds). While this is a standard benchmark, it limits the generalizability of the findings. It is unclear how the APT model would fare on other types of phrases (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object) or on other downstream tasks where composition is crucial (e.g., phrase similarity, paraphrase detection). For example, the success of the unaligned model seems particularly tied to the noun-noun construction; it likely would not work for adjective-noun phrases where the constituent words have different feature distributions.

2.  **The Success of the Unaligned Model Undermines the Central Claim:** A core novelty of the APT framework is the alignment mechanism. However, the results in Table 3 show that the unaligned model (ρ=0.78 with `⊔UNI`) performs almost as well as the best hybrid model (ρ=0.79) and significantly better than the purely aligned model (ρ=0.75 with `⊔UNI`). The authors' explanation—that nouns in a compound can share first-order contexts—is plausible. However, this finding significantly weakens the argument for the necessity and general utility of the alignment procedure, suggesting that the primary driver of performance on *this specific task* is simply the use of rich, higher-order typed features, rather than the sophisticated alignment. This point requires a more thorough discussion.

3.  **Practicality and Scalability Concerns are Not Addressed:** The APT model appears to be computationally demanding. It requires a dependency-parsed corpus and involves constructing very high-dimensional, sparse vectors with complex string features (the dependency paths). The paper provides no discussion of the computational costs, memory footprint, or scalability of this approach compared to the much more compact and efficient word2vec models. For instance, what is the vocabulary size of the dependency-path features, and how does it grow with the order of the paths? The practicality of deploying such a model in larger applications is a major open question.

4.  **Absence of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper relies solely on aggregate correlation scores to demonstrate the superiority of its model. A qualitative error analysis would have been highly beneficial. For instance, showing a few examples where the APT model correctly predicts compositionality while the word2vec model fails (and vice-versa) would provide deeper insight into the models' respective strengths and weaknesses. Does the APT model excel at capturing subtle, literal alterations of meaning (e.g., *student loan*) while word2vec is only good for detecting starkly idiomatic phrases (e.g., *red herring*)?

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you expand on the strong performance of the unaligned and hybrid models? Does the fact that the unaligned component is so effective for N-N compounds suggest that the APT framework should incorporate a mechanism to dynamically decide when to apply alignment based on the syntactic construction, rather than treating it as a mandatory step?

2.  Can you provide some details on the computational resources required for the APT model? Specifically, what was the feature space size for the noun vectors (including up to order 2 paths), and what were the approximate training/lexicon-creation times and memory requirements compared to the word2vec baseline on the ukWaC corpus?

3.  The optimal weighting parameter `h` being in the [0.3, 0.5] range suggests the modifier is weighted more heavily than the head. This is contrary to some linguistic theories where the head determines the primary semantics of a compound. Do you have a deeper intuition for this result? Could it be an artifact of the dataset or the specific compositionality task?
