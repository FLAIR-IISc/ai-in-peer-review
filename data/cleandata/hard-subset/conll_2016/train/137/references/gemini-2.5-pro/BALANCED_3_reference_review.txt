### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, i.e., determining whether the meaning of a compound (e.g., "climate change") can be derived from its constituent parts. The authors propose and evaluate a syntactically-informed compositional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which use higher-order, typed dependency paths as distributional features. The core idea is that the compositionality of a phrase can be measured by the similarity between a vector composed from its parts and an observed, holistic vector for the phrase. The APT model composes vectors by first aligning the feature spaces of the constituents based on the syntactic path between them. The authors conduct a rigorous evaluation on the standard Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, comparing their method to the previous state-of-the-art, as well as a strong baseline using word2vec embeddings with additive composition. The results demonstrate that the proposed APT-based models significantly outperform all baselines.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective syntactically-informed compositional model (APT) that achieves a new state-of-the-art on a standard compositionality detection task.** By incorporating explicit, higher-order dependency path information into the feature space and using a motivated alignment process, the model demonstrates superior performance over both traditional count-based and standard neural embedding models.
2.  **A thorough empirical investigation and ablation study of different compositional strategies.** The paper systematically explores a range of parameters and design choices for both the proposed APT model (aligned vs. unaligned, additive vs. intersective) and the neural baseline (cbow vs. skip-gram, etc.). This analysis provides valuable insights, such as the surprising effectiveness of unaligned features for noun compounds and the benefit of a hybrid approach.
3.  **A strong demonstration of the continued relevance of explicit, count-based distributional models.** In an era dominated by neural methods, this work provides compelling evidence that carefully engineered, linguistically-motivated features can outperform popular, general-purpose embeddings like word2vec on tasks that require a nuanced understanding of syntactic and semantic composition.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Improvement on a Standard Benchmark:** The paper reports a new state-of-the-art Spearman correlation of 0.79 on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. This is a substantial and statistically significant improvement over both the original work's result (0.71) and a well-tuned word2vec baseline that the authors establish (0.74). The performance gains are clearly demonstrated and provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.

2.  **Well-Motivated and Technically Sound Method:** The APT compositional model is grounded in linguistic intuition. The core idea of aligning vector spaces based on the syntactic path between constituents before composition is elegant and addresses a key limitation of naive compositional functions like addition, which ignore structure. The mechanism of offsetting and reduction is explained clearly and provides a principled way to handle composition within a structured, typed vector space.

3.  **Rigorous and Transparent Experimental Design:** The evaluation is conducted with great care. The authors use the same corpus as the original work for fair comparison, detail their preprocessing steps to avoid data leakage between constituent and compound representations, and use cross-validation for parameter tuning. The inclusion of multiple baselines (RAND, FREQ, Reddy et al., word2vec) and a detailed ablation of the APT model itself (e.g., aligned, unaligned, hybrid) allows for a clear understanding of where the performance gains come from.

4.  **Insightful Analysis and Discussion:** The authors go beyond simply reporting results. They provide thoughtful analysis of their findings, for instance, by hypothesizing why higher subsampling thresholds in word2vec and the avoidance of shifted PMI are beneficial for this task (due to the low frequency of many compounds). Their discovery that a hybrid model combining aligned and unaligned features performs best is an interesting finding, and their discussion of the relative informativeness of the modifier versus the head is insightful.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted exclusively on a single dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard benchmark, the paper's claims about the general promise of the APT approach are not fully substantiated. It remains unclear whether the model's effectiveness, and particularly the specific finding that unaligned features are so powerful, would generalize to other compositional phenomena (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object phrases) or other languages. Testing on additional datasets would significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.

2.  **Comparison to Neural Models Could Be Stronger:** The neural baseline consists of word2vec embeddings combined with weighted addition. While this is a common and reasonable baseline, the field has seen the development of more sophisticated neural models designed specifically for composition (e.g., those based on LSTMs, Tree-LSTMs, or Transformers). Comparing the APT model against a more contemporary, composition-aware neural architecture would provide a more stringent test and make the claims about the superiority of the explicit syntactic approach more compelling.

3.  **Lack of Discussion on Complexity and Scalability:** The paper does not address the computational trade-offs of the APT model. The feature space, containing higher-order dependency paths, is very high-dimensional (~230k for nouns) and sparse. Building this APT lexicon from a large, parsed corpus and performing the alignment and composition operations could be significantly more computationally expensive and memory-intensive than training and using dense word embeddings. A discussion of these practical aspects—including the reliance on a high-quality dependency parser—is missing and would be important for readers interested in applying this method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The success of the unaligned and hybrid APT models is a key finding, suggesting that for noun-noun compounds, the modifier's first-order features are highly predictive. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case for this specific construction? Does this imply that the syntactic alignment, which is a core motivation of the APT framework, is less critical for noun-noun compounds than it might be for other structures like verb-object phrases, where the semantic roles are more distinct?

2.  Your work convincingly demonstrates that the composed APT vectors are effective for the intrinsic task of compositionality detection. Have you considered evaluating these composed representations on downstream tasks, such as phrase similarity (e.g., the Mitchell and Lapata dataset) or paraphrase detection? Success on such tasks would provide external validation for the quality of the composed vectors.

3.  Could you comment on the computational resources (time, memory) required to extract the APT lexicon and perform composition, as compared to the word2vec baseline? Are there sparsity or scalability concerns that might limit the application of this method to even larger web-scale corpora or a wider range of linguistic phenomena?
