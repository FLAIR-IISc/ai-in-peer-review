**Summary**

This paper investigates the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, using the similarity between a composed vector and an observed (holistic) vector as a predictor for human compositionality ratings. The authors propose and evaluate a compositional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses typed, higher-order dependency paths as distributional features. This model includes a novel "alignment" step that structurally transforms a modifier's vector space to match the head's space before composition. The paper compares several variants of this APT model (aligned, unaligned, and a hybrid) against strong baselines, including the previous state-of-the-art and a well-tuned model using weighted addition of word2vec embeddings. The experiments, conducted on a standard benchmark dataset of 90 English compound nouns, show that the proposed hybrid APT model achieves a new state-of-the-art, suggesting that explicitly encoding syntactic structure in features is beneficial for this task.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is a rigorous empirical demonstration that a compositional model using structured, syntax-aware features (typed higher-order dependency paths) significantly outperforms models based on untyped, proximity-based features (such as word2vec) for detecting noun compound compositionality. The proposed hybrid APT model sets a new state-of-the-art on the Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark.
2.  The paper introduces and evaluates a hybrid composition method that interpolates between a structurally-aligned representation of the modifier (using higher-order features) and its unaligned representation (using first-order features). The success of this hybrid model provides evidence that both types of information are valuable and complementary for predicting compositionality.
3.  The work establishes a strong and well-optimized baseline using word2vec embeddings, which itself surpasses the previously reported state-of-the-art. This provides a more challenging and contemporary point of comparison for this benchmark task, strengthening the paper's primary claims.

**Strengths**

1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The paper reports impressive results, achieving a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, which is a substantial improvement over both the original benchmark result (0.71) and the strong word2vec baseline established in the paper (0.74). The use of repeated cross-validation and significance testing lends confidence to the claim that these improvements are not due to chance.
2.  **Methodological Rigor:** The experimental design is technically sound and carefully executed. The authors use the same corpus as the original benchmark work, control for data leakage by treating compounds as single lexical units during feature extraction for the holistic vectors, and perform systematic parameter tuning via cross-validation. The inclusion and thorough evaluation of multiple model variants (e.g., different composition functions, PPMI settings, word2vec parameters) make the comparison robust.
3.  **Linguistically Motivated Approach:** The core APT method is well-motivated by the principle that syntactic structure is crucial for composition. The idea of "aligning" vector spaces by offsetting feature paths based on the dependency relation between constituents is an explicit and interpretable way to incorporate syntax into the composition process, moving beyond the simple, commutative operations often applied to word embeddings.
4.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The proposed APT method, including the concepts of feature offsetting and reduction, is explained clearly with concrete examples (Tables 1 and 2). The experimental setup and results are presented in a clear and organized manner, facilitating understanding and reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Generalizability:** The empirical evaluation is confined to a single, relatively small dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard benchmark, the strong conclusions about the superiority of the APT model are not validated on other types of phrasal composition (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object) or in other languages. The method's effectiveness on this specific construction may not generalize, limiting the broader impact of the findings.
2.  **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** The APT model relies on dependency parsing the entire corpus and constructing very high-dimensional, sparse vectors from higher-order paths (the paper notes a feature space size of ~230,000). This appears significantly more complex and computationally intensive than the word2vec baseline. The paper does not discuss the practical trade-offs in terms of parsing time, memory usage for the sparse vectors, or composition speed. This is a crucial omission for assessing the model's viability for larger-scale applications.
3.  **Superficial Analysis of Hybrid Model's Success:** The hybrid model, which combines aligned and unaligned features, achieves the best results. However, the analysis of *why* this combination is so effective is brief. The paper suggests they predict "different contexts," but does not provide a deeper analysis or illustrative examples. It is unclear if the unaligned component is primarily capturing topical association while the aligned component captures more literal compositional semantics, or if another dynamic is at play. A qualitative analysis of which compounds benefit most from each component would have provided more valuable insight.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The optimal setting for the hybrid parameter `q` was in the range [0.3, 0.5], suggesting that the unaligned features (the modifier's conventional first-order dependency features) are at least as, if not more, informative than the structurally aligned, higher-order features. This seems somewhat counter-intuitive given the linguistic motivation for the alignment process. Do you have an interpretation for why the unaligned features carry so much weight in this specific task?
2.  How does the APT approach extend to other syntactic constructions, such as adjective-noun phrases (e.g., "black market") or verb-object phrases? The alignment process is dependent on the specific dependency relation (`δ`). Would you expect the same hybrid approach of combining aligned and unaligned features to be optimal for these other constructions as well?
3.  Could you provide some details on the computational and memory costs of the APT model compared to the word2vec baseline? Specifically, information on parsing time, the size of the final APT lexicon on disk/in memory, and the time required to compose vectors would be helpful for understanding the practical trade-offs involved.
