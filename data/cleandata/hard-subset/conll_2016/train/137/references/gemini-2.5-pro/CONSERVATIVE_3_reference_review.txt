### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds by comparing the similarity of a composed vector representation to an observed (holistic) vector representation. The primary contribution is the introduction and evaluation of a novel compositional method based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses typed, higher-order dependency paths as distributional features. This method is evaluated on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 noun compounds. A secondary contribution is the establishment of a new, strong baseline for this task using standard word2vec (cbow) embeddings with additive composition, which itself surpasses the previously reported state-of-the-art. The authors report that their proposed APT model achieves a new state-of-the-art result, outperforming both the previous work and the strong neural baseline.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental setup is well-designed and carefully executed. The authors use the established ukWaC corpus and the standard Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, ensuring comparability with prior work. The use of 3-fold cross-validation for parameter tuning is appropriate, and the comparison against multiple relevant baselines, including the original work by Reddy et al. and a modern, well-understood neural baseline (word2vec), is commendable. This rigor provides confidence in the reported empirical results.

2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper achieves a new state-of-the-art Spearman correlation of 0.79 on the noun compound compositionality task, a noticeable improvement over the previous best of 0.71 and the strong cbow baseline of 0.74. While the absolute gains are incremental, they are statistically significant according to the authors and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach on this specific benchmark.

3.  **Thorough Ablation and Analysis:** The authors conduct a thorough investigation of their proposed APT model, testing various configurations (aligned, unaligned, hybrid), composition functions (`⊔INT`, `⊔UNI`), and weighting schemes. This analysis yields interesting insights, particularly the finding that the unaligned model performs surprisingly well and that a hybrid approach is optimal. This level of detail is useful for understanding the different components of the model.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope and Questionable Generalizability:** The central claims of the paper rest on experiments conducted on a single, rather small dataset of 90 noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard benchmark for this specific task, the superiority of a complex, syntax-aware model like APT needs to be demonstrated more broadly before it can be considered a significant advance in compositional semantics. Established methods like the additive word2vec model have shown robust performance across a wide array of tasks (phrase similarity, paraphrase detection, etc.). The current work provides no evidence that the APT model's advantages extend beyond this narrow task. It is plausible that the model is particularly well-suited to noun compounds but may not generalize to other syntactic constructions (e.g., verb-object, adjective-noun) where the dependency structures are different.

2.  **Significant Model Complexity for Modest Gains:** The proposed APT model is substantially more complex than the baseline it is compared against. It requires dependency-parsed data, the construction of very high-dimensional sparse vectors from higher-order dependency paths, and a bespoke alignment mechanism. In contrast, the cbow baseline is conceptually simple, computationally efficient, and relies only on token co-occurrence in a linear window. The performance improvement from ρ=0.74 to ρ=0.79, while statistically significant, may not be practically significant enough to justify the considerable overhead in representational and computational complexity. For a new, complex method to be compelling, it should offer a more substantial performance leap or provide clear qualitative advantages that simpler models lack.

3.  **Empirical Results Undermine Theoretical Motivation:** The core motivation for the APT model is the alignment of vector spaces via dependency path offsetting to enable principled composition. However, the results in Table 3 show that the `unaligned` model (ρ=0.78) performs significantly better than the `aligned`-only model (ρ=0.75). The best performance is achieved by a `hybrid` model that relies heavily on the unaligned features (optimal q is in [0.3, 0.5], meaning 50-70% of the modifier's contribution comes from the unaligned vector). This finding seems to contradict the central premise of the paper—that proper alignment is the key to effective composition. It suggests that the performance gains may stem simply from using rich, typed dependency features rather than from the novel alignment mechanism itself. The paper does not offer a compelling explanation for this counter-intuitive result.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The paper's claims are based entirely on the noun-compound compositionality task. Could you comment on the generalizability of the APT approach? Have you considered evaluating it on other established compositional tasks, such as the Mitchell and Lapata phrase similarity datasets or sentence-level paraphrase tasks, to show that its benefits are not specific to this single evaluation?

2.  The strong performance of the `unaligned` model relative to the `aligned` model is a key, if puzzling, result. Could you provide a more detailed analysis or hypothesis for why this might be the case? Does this finding not weaken the core argument that dependency-based alignment is the crucial element for improving compositional models?

3.  Given the significant increase in model complexity (requiring parsers, storing high-dimensional sparse vectors), how do you justify the trade-off for the performance gain over the simple and highly effective word2vec+addition baseline? Can you provide some qualitative examples of compound phrases where the APT model succeeds and the baseline fails, which would better illustrate the unique strengths of incorporating explicit syntactic structure in this manner?
