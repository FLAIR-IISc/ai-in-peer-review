**Summary of the paper**
This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun-noun compounds. The authors propose a compositional method based on "Anchored Packed Trees" (APTs), which represent words using typed, higher-order dependency paths as features. The core idea is that a phrase's vector can be composed from its constituents' vectors by first "aligning" their feature spaces based on the dependency relation between them. The authors evaluate this approach on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns, comparing their method against previous results and a baseline using word2vec embeddings. Their best-performing "hybrid" APT model achieves a Spearman's correlation of 0.79 with human judgments, outperforming both the original Reddy et al. result (0.71) and a well-tuned word2vec baseline (0.74).

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is an empirical demonstration that using structured, typed dependency features can improve performance on the specific task of detecting noun-noun compound compositionality. The paper achieves a new state-of-the-art result on the widely used Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark.
2.  The paper provides a strong baseline using modern word embeddings (word2vec) with a simple additive composition, showing that this established method is highly competitive and significantly outperforms the older count-based models originally used on this task.
3.  The authors introduce a novel, if complex, compositional framework (APTs) that explicitly models syntactic structure in the feature space, including a mechanism for aligning vectors before composition.

**Strengths**
1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a clear state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark task. The improvement over the word2vec baseline, while not massive, is statistically significant and demonstrates the value of the information captured by the proposed features.
2.  **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a careful exploration of their model's parameter space, including different feature weighting schemes (PPMI with and without smoothing), composition operations (intersective vs. additive), and model variants (aligned, unaligned, and hybrid). This provides valuable insight into which components of their method are most effective.
3.  **Well-Chosen Problem:** The problem of compositionality is fundamental to distributional semantics, and this paper correctly identifies the confound between judging phrase similarity and phrase compositionality. Using a compositionality detection task is a sound way to evaluate a compositional model directly.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Extremely Limited Evaluation:** The paper's primary weakness is that its claims are supported by experiments on only a single, small dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard benchmark, it is insufficient to justify the general utility of the complex APT framework. The conclusion that "the APT approach to compositionality is reasonably promising" is overstated. It is entirely unclear if these results would generalize to other phenomena (e.g., adjective-noun phrases, verb-object constructions), other tasks, or other languages. A convincing argument for a new compositional framework requires much broader empirical validation.
2.  **Motivation for "Alignment" Undermined by Results:** The central theoretical novelty proposed in Section 3 is the "alignment" of vector spaces via offsetting. However, the results in Table 3 show that the "Unaligned" model (ρ=0.78) performs substantially better than the "Aligned" model (ρ=0.75). The best performance is achieved by a "Hybrid" model that combines the two. This strongly suggests that the alignment mechanism, which is the most novel part of the proposal, is not the key to success and may in fact be less effective than a simpler approach that does not perform alignment. The paper does not provide a satisfying explanation for this counter-intuitive result, which significantly weakens its core theoretical claims.
3.  **Complexity vs. Performance Gain:** The proposed APT model is significantly more complex than the baseline of adding word embeddings. It requires a dependency-parsed corpus, extraction of higher-order paths, and a bespoke composition mechanism. In contrast, the word2vec baseline is simple, fast, and relies on off-the-shelf tools. The performance gain of the best APT model over the strong CBOW baseline is 0.05 Spearman's rho. The paper does not discuss whether this modest improvement justifies the substantial increase in representational and computational complexity. For many practical applications, the simpler, established method might be preferable.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  The central motivation for the APT model appears to be the alignment of vector spaces before composition. Yet, your results demonstrate that the unaligned model outperforms the aligned one. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of why this might be the case? Does this finding not challenge the fundamental premise of your approach?
2.  The evaluation is confined to a single, small dataset of noun-noun compounds. Given the model's reliance on specific dependency relations (e.g., NMOD), how do you foresee its applicability to other compositional structures, such as verb-object or adjective-noun phrases, where the syntactic and semantic interactions are different?
3.  Could you comment on the practical trade-offs between your method and the much simpler word2vec+addition baseline? Given the significant added complexity of parsing, extracting higher-order features, and implementing the APT framework, under what circumstances would the observed 0.05 gain in correlation be worth the cost?
