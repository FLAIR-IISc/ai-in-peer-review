### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds. The authors propose a novel, highly structured distributional model called Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses typed, higher-order dependency paths as features. The core idea is that composition should be a syntactically-aware process. To compose vectors for a head and a modifier (e.g., *student* and *grad* in *grad student*), the model explicitly transforms the modifier’s vector space to align with the head's space, guided by the syntactic dependency path connecting them. The authors evaluate this approach on the standard Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, comparing their method against previous work and strong neural embedding baselines. Their best model, a hybrid that combines aligned and unaligned features, achieves a new state-of-the-art result, demonstrating the value of incorporating explicit syntactic structure into compositional models.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel, Structurally-Aware Compositional Framework (APTs):** The primary contribution is the introduction of the Anchored Packed Trees framework. The concept of aligning vector spaces by "offsetting" features along dependency paths is a bold and highly original idea. It represents a refreshing departure from the dominant paradigm of applying simple, commutative operations (like addition) to opaque, dense vectors. This work re-introduces explicit linguistic structure into composition in a principled and innovative way.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance in Compositionality Detection:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that this sophisticated model is not just a theoretical exercise. The APT-based models significantly outperform both the previous state-of-the-art and well-tuned neural embedding baselines on a standard benchmark task. This result lends substantial credibility to the claim that explicit syntactic information is crucial for modeling compositionality.

### Strengths
1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Depth:** The paper's main strength is its bold conceptual leap. The APT framework, particularly the mechanism of aligning vector spaces via dependency path transformations and reduction, is a fundamentally new way of thinking about composition in vector space. In a field often characterized by incremental improvements to neural architectures, this work presents a compelling, linguistically-motivated alternative that is both elegant and powerful. It successfully bridges ideas from formal semantics (function application) with the practicalities of distributional models.
2.  **Strong and Well-Analyzed Empirical Results:** The authors not only propose a new model but also demonstrate its effectiveness convincingly. Achieving a new state-of-the-art (ρ=0.79) is a significant result. More importantly, the analysis is insightful. The comparison between aligned, unaligned, and hybrid models reveals a nuanced picture: both the modifier's original syntactic contexts and its transformed contexts relative to the head are informative. This is a subtle but important finding that simpler models would miss entirely.
3.  **Thorough Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The authors do not compare their model only to older, weaker systems. They implement and tune a strong baseline using word2vec with weighted addition, which itself surpasses the previous published SOTA. The fact that their APT models then significantly outperform this strong contemporary baseline makes their primary claim—that structure matters—much more convincing.

### Weaknesses
While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are some limitations that should be acknowledged, though in my view they do not warrant rejection given the novelty of the contribution.
1.  **Potential Scalability and Sparsity Concerns:** The model relies on explicit, high-dimensional count vectors derived from specific, typed dependency paths. This approach is inherently vulnerable to data sparsity, especially for higher-order paths. While the use of a large corpus like ukWaC mitigates this, it is unclear how well the model would perform with less data or how it would scale to even larger, web-scale corpora where the feature space could become computationally prohibitive. The complexity of constructing and manipulating these representations may be a barrier to wider adoption compared to the efficiency of dense embedding models.
2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The proposed compositional mechanism is evaluated on a single task (compositionality of noun compounds) using a relatively small dataset of 90 phrases. Although this is the standard benchmark for this specific problem, the power of the APT framework lies in its generality. Its true potential will only be realized if it can be shown to work on other compositional phenomena, such as verb-argument structures, adjective-noun modification in different contexts, or full sentence-level tasks. The current evaluation, while strong for the chosen task, leaves the broader applicability of the method as an open question.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The vector space alignment via path offsetting and reduction is the most fascinating part of the model. The reduction rule (where a relation cancels with its inverse) is explained briefly. Could you elaborate on how this works with more complex, multi-step paths? For instance, does `A → B ← C` reduce in any way, and how does the model handle cases where paths create cycles or other complex graph structures?
2.  Given the high dimensionality and explicit nature of the APT vectors, what is the computational overhead of your method (both for lexicon creation and for composition at test time) compared to the word2vec baseline? Is the performance gain worth a potentially significant increase in computational cost?
