### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds. The core idea is that the degree of compositionality can be measured by the similarity between a phrase's observed (holistic) distributional representation and a representation composed from its constituent words. The authors propose a novel compositional method based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses typed, higher-order dependency paths as features. The key mechanism of this method is to "align" the vector space of a modifier to that of its head based on the syntactic relation between them before performing a pointwise composition operation. The authors evaluate this method on the Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark dataset, comparing it against the previous state-of-the-art, a strong baseline using modern word embeddings (word2vec), and several variants of their own model.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel, Syntactically-Informed Compositional Model (APT):** The primary contribution is the APT model itself. Unlike naive additive/multiplicative models that treat words as a bag, this approach explicitly encodes syntactic structure (dependency paths) into the features and uses this structure to align vector spaces before composition. This is a well-motivated, linguistically grounded approach to composition.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Results and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper demonstrates that the proposed APT model achieves a new state-of-the-art on a standard compositionality detection task, significantly outperforming both the original baseline and a strong neural embedding baseline. The experimental setup is sound, using the same large corpus as prior work and carefully exploring model parameters. The finding that a hybrid of aligned and unaligned features performs best is a particularly interesting and valuable result.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper's strongest argument is its results. The best-performing hybrid APT model achieves a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, a substantial improvement over the previous SOTA of 0.71 reported by Reddy et al. (2011) and the authors' own strong word2vec baseline (ρ=0.74). This provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of incorporating structured syntactic information into distributional models for this task.

2.  **Linguistically Motivated Model:** The APT approach is well-motivated from a linguistic and formal semantics perspective. The idea that composition should be guided by syntactic structure (function application) is not new, but this paper offers a practical and effective implementation within a distributional framework. The concept of aligning feature spaces based on the dependency path between constituents is intuitive and elegant. This contrasts favorably with the purely heuristic nature of simple vector addition/multiplication.

3.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis:** The authors conduct a careful evaluation, comparing multiple variants of their model. They test aligned-only, unaligned-only, and hybrid models, as well as intersective (multiply) and union (add) composition operations. This analysis reveals a nuanced picture: while the theoretically motivated "alignment" helps, the unaligned first-order features of the constituents are also highly informative. The fact that their hybrid model works best is an important finding, suggesting that both aligned higher-order features and unaligned first-order features capture complementary aspects of meaning.

4.  **Reproducibility and Fair Comparison:** The experimental setup is solid. The authors use the same large-scale corpus (ukWaC) and benchmark dataset as the prior work they compare against, ensuring a fair comparison. They also implement and tune a strong, modern baseline using word2vec, demonstrating that their gains are not simply due to using a better vector space model but are attributable to their compositional method.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper's claims are evaluated on a single task (noun compound compositionality) and a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011). While this is a standard and appropriate benchmark, the generality of the APT approach remains an open question. It is unclear how this method would perform on other compositional phenomena, such as adjective-noun modification, verb-argument composition, or sentence-level tasks. The focus on noun-noun compounds, where constituents are of the same POS, may be a particularly favorable case for this type of model.

2.  **Clarity and Self-Containment of the Method:** The description of the APT model, particularly the "reduction" operation (Equation 1) and the elimination of "incompatible co-occurrence types," is very brief and relies on a suppressed reference. This makes the mechanics of the alignment process difficult to fully grasp from this paper alone. A more detailed explanation or a clearer, more illustrative example would significantly improve the paper's self-containment and help the reader understand precisely how feature spaces are transformed and why certain paths are eliminated.

3.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis of the Hybrid Model's Success:** The finding that the hybrid model outperforms both the pure aligned and pure unaligned models is one of the most interesting results. However, the paper offers limited insight into *why* this is the case. It suggests they capture different, complementary contexts but does not provide a deeper analysis. Is it because the alignment process is imperfect and loses some useful information? Or do the unaligned first-order features capture a different kind of semantic association that is crucial for compositionality judgments? An error analysis showing examples where the aligned model fails but the hybrid succeeds (and vice-versa) would greatly strengthen this part of the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The success of the hybrid model is intriguing. Could you provide more intuition on what types of information the aligned second-order features and the unaligned first-order features are respectively capturing? For instance, for "spelling bee", what key features might be contributed by the aligned vs. unaligned component of the modifier "spelling"?

2.  The feature space for the APT model appears to be very high-dimensional and sparse (e.g., ~230k dimensions for nouns). Could you comment on the scalability and computational efficiency of this approach compared to methods based on low-dimensional dense embeddings, both in terms of training the initial representations and performing the composition?

3.  How do you envision the APT alignment process extending to other syntactic constructions, such as verb-object or adjective-noun phrases, where the constituents have different POS tags and the syntactic relationship is different from NMOD? Would the same principles of offsetting and reduction apply directly?
