### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the task of predicting the compositionality of noun compounds, using the similarity between a composed phrase representation and an observed (holistic) one. The authors propose and evaluate a compositional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses typed, higher-order dependency paths as features. The core idea of the APT model is to syntactically "align" the feature space of a modifier with that of its head before composition. The authors compare this structured approach against standard compositional baselines using word2vec embeddings and the previous state-of-the-art. They demonstrate that their dependency-based models significantly outperform proximity-based models on the Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark dataset.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A new state-of-the-art on noun compound compositionality detection.** The proposed hybrid APT model achieves a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, a substantial improvement over the previous state-of-the-art of 0.714 and a strong word2vec baseline (0.74) implemented by the authors. This provides strong evidence that syntactically-informed, dependency-based features are more effective for this task than standard, untyped proximity-based features.
2.  **A thorough empirical analysis of different compositional strategies.** The paper systematically explores a range of models and parameters, including: typed vs. untyped features (APT vs. word2vec), aligned vs. unaligned composition within the typed space, additive vs. intersective operations, and different feature weighting schemes (PPMI vs. counts). This detailed investigation provides valuable insights into what aspects of a compositional model contribute most to its success on this task.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Analysis:** The paper presents a clear and significant improvement on a well-established benchmark. The performance gain over both the original baseline and a well-tuned word2vec baseline is convincing. The analysis is thorough, exploring various hyperparameters and model configurations (e.g., Tables 3 and 4), which strengthens the authors' conclusions. The finding that a hybrid of aligned and unaligned features performs best is particularly interesting and well-supported by the data.

2.  **Well-Motivated Method:** The core idea of using richer, typed dependency features for composition is linguistically well-motivated. The problem with simple vector addition/multiplication is its insensitivity to syntax, and the APT framework is a principled attempt to address this. The concept of aligning vector spaces based on the syntactic relation between constituents is an elegant solution to the problem that words with different POS tags or syntactic roles live in different distributional spaces.

3.  **Careful and Replicable Experimental Setup:** The authors are careful to use the same corpus (ukWaC) and evaluation dataset as the work they are comparing against (Reddy et al., 2011), making for a fair comparison. The description of the data preprocessing, especially the handling of the target compound nouns to create holistic representations, is clear and seems methodologically sound. This attention to detail increases confidence in the reported results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of the APT Model Description:** The description of the core APT composition mechanism in Section 3 is overly dense and difficult to fully grasp without access to the suppressed reference. The process of "offsetting" and "reduction" is explained abstractly with a formal definition (Eq. 1) but lacks a concrete, step-by-step walkthrough. For example, showing how the feature vectors for `grad` and `student` are specifically transformed and then combined to produce a vector for `grad student` would make the method much more accessible and intuitive. The current examples in Table 1 and 2 illustrate the features and the effect of offsetting, but not the full compositional process.

2.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The experiments are conducted on a single dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this provides a focused and deep analysis, the paper's broader claims about the promise of the APT approach for compositionality would be significantly strengthened by evaluation on other phenomena. For instance, testing on adjective-noun or verb-object phrases, or on different tasks like phrase similarity (e.g., Mitchell & Lapata, 2010), would demonstrate the generality of the approach. As it stands, it is unclear if the model's success is specific to the particularities of noun-noun compounds.

3.  **A Counter-intuitive Key Finding Lacks Deeper Analysis:** One of the most interesting results is that the fully `Aligned` model—the primary motivation of the APT framework—is outperformed by the `Unaligned` and `Hybrid` models. The authors correctly conclude that both aligned higher-order features and unaligned first-order features are valuable. However, the paper would benefit from a deeper discussion of *why* this is the case for noun-noun compounds. A possible hypothesis is that for N-N compounds, the modifier and head are of the same word class and their first-order dependency contexts (e.g., what verbs they are subjects/objects of) are already highly compatible and informative without alignment. An error analysis or qualitative inspection of examples where alignment helps vs. hurts would strengthen this part of the discussion.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The `Unaligned` model (using typed, higher-order dependency paths but without the alignment step) performs surprisingly well (ρ=0.78), significantly outperforming the word2vec baseline (ρ=0.74). This suggests that simply using typed dependency features is a major source of the performance gain, separate from the alignment mechanism. Could you elaborate on why you think these unaligned typed features are so much more effective than the untyped proximity features of word2vec for this specific task?

2.  In your data preprocessing, you mention that for a phrase like `(recently graduated) student`, dependency paths involving the modifier (`graduated`) are ignored when creating the corpus. This seems to discard potentially useful contextual information about how the modifier is used. Could you clarify the rationale for this decision? Did you experiment with including these contexts, and if so, what was the impact?

3.  Given that the `Hybrid` model works best, it implies that both aligned and unaligned features capture complementary information. Could you provide a qualitative example of a compound where the aligned features might correctly predict a co-occurrence that the unaligned features miss, and vice-versa? This would help clarify the distinct contributions of the two feature types.
