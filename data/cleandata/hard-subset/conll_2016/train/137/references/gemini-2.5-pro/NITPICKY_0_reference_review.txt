### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the task of detecting the compositionality of compound nouns. The authors propose using a compositional distributional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which leverages typed, higher-order dependency paths as contextual features. The core idea is that the composition of constituent words should involve an "alignment" of their syntactic feature spaces. The authors compare several variants of their APT-based model (aligned, unaligned, and a hybrid) against strong baselines, including word2vec embeddings and the previous state-of-the-art method by Reddy et al. (2011). Experiments on the Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark dataset of 90 compound nouns show that the proposed hybrid APT model achieves a new state-of-the-art Spearman's rank correlation of 0.79 with human judgments.

### Contributions

1.  An empirical demonstration that syntax-aware, count-based models using typed dependency features (the APT framework) can significantly outperform syntax-agnostic, prediction-based models (word2vec) on the task of modeling noun compound compositionality.
2.  The introduction of a hybrid composition model that interpolates between structurally aligned and unaligned dependency features, which achieves a new state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackles an Important Problem:** The paper addresses the crucial and often-overlooked problem of non-compositionality in distributional semantics. Evaluating compositionality directly, rather than through downstream similarity tasks, provides a more focused and interpretable evaluation of compositional models.
2.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The core hypothesis that syntactic information, specifically typed dependency paths, is critical for modeling composition is linguistically well-founded. The APT framework, which explicitly encodes and manipulates this structural information during composition, is a principled way to explore this hypothesis.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed models achieve substantial and statistically significant improvements over several strong baselines on the established benchmark dataset. Outperforming not only the original work on the dataset but also a well-tuned word2vec baseline is a notable result that underscores the value of the syntactic features employed.

### Weaknesses

Despite the strong results, the paper is beset by a considerable number of minor flaws in methodology, presentation, and experimental design that collectively undermine its contribution and call into question its readiness for publication.

1.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper exhibits a lack of meticulousness that is unacceptable for a top-tier conference. The **References** section is in a state of complete disarray. It is not alphabetized, formatting is wildly inconsistent (e.g., compare "Baroni and Lenci.,? \Q2010\E" with "Curran.,? 2004"), and it contains raw processing artifacts. This is not a minor oversight; it suggests a rushed submission and a disregard for scholarly convention. Further, Table 3 uses unexplained acronyms (CF, CS) in the header; their definition is buried deep within the results section, hindering readability. The informal phrasing in places, such as "Reduction essentially means..." (Sec 3) and the justification in Footnote 1 ("omit the POS tags since they can be easily inferred by the reader"), lacks the precision expected in a scientific paper.
2.  **Insufficient Methodological Justification:** Several key methodological choices lack clear motivation.
    *   The hybrid model (Eq. 8 & 9), which delivers the final state-of-the-art result, is presented as a simple linear interpolation between aligned and unaligned representations. There is no theoretical justification provided for this specific formulation; it appears to be an ad-hoc engineering trick to combine two feature sets that both work well, rather than a principled modeling contribution.
    *   The evaluation is restricted to the similarity of 1st-order dependency features (Footnote 3). The justification given—that higher-order paths in the composed representation are "not reliable"—is unconvincing and feels post-hoc. A robust compositional model should produce a complete vector, and the evaluation should ideally consider this entire output space, not just a pre-selected subset of features that aligns with the target.
3.  **Limited and Potentially Biased Experimental Setup:**
    *   The reliance on a single, small dataset (N=90 compounds) makes it difficult to assess the generality of the findings. Conclusions drawn, such as the modifier being more informative than the head, may be artifacts of this specific dataset.
    *   The comparison to the word2vec baseline feels incomplete. The authors state they tried "a number of commonly used and recommended parameter settings," but this is not a substitute for a systematic hyperparameter search. Critical parameters like the number of negative samples are not mentioned, and window sizes are fixed based on "recommendations." The conclusion that APTs outperform word2vec could be weakened if the baseline was not optimally tuned for this specific task.
    *   The authors dismiss shifted PMI for their count-based model with a brief, speculative rationale. A more rigorous approach would be to present these negative results and analyze them, rather than omitting them for "compactness."

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given that the core APT framework appears to be from prior, suppressed work, could you clarify the primary novel contribution of this paper? Is it the application of APTs to this task, the introduction of the hybrid model, or the analysis itself?
2.  Can you provide a more principled motivation for the linear interpolation used in the hybrid model (Eq. 8-9)? Is there a theoretical basis for this combination, or are there alternative, more motivated ways to combine aligned and unaligned syntactic information?
3.  The dismissal of higher-order features during evaluation (Footnote 3) seems problematic. Have you attempted an evaluation against the full composed vector? If so, how do the results change? If not, why is it appropriate to evaluate a compositional model on only a fraction of its output?
4.  The state of the reference section is a significant concern. Can you provide assurance that this, along with other formatting and presentation issues, will be thoroughly addressed in any subsequent version of the manuscript?
