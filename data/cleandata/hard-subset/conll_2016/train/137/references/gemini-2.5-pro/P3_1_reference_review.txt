### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, framing it as a problem of measuring the similarity between a composed representation of the constituents and a holistic representation of the compound observed in a corpus.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel, syntax-aware compositional model (APT):** The paper proposes and evaluates the Anchored Packed Trees (APT) model for this task. Unlike standard additive/multiplicative models that operate on word embeddings, APT leverages typed, higher-order dependency paths as features. Its key innovation is the ability to "offset" or align the vector space of a modifier based on the syntactic relation to its head before composition, allowing for a more structurally-informed combination of meaning.
2.  **A rigorous empirical evaluation demonstrating the value of structured representations:** The authors conduct a thorough comparison on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. Crucially, they not only surpass the previous state-of-the-art but also establish a new, strong baseline using modern word2vec (CBOW) embeddings. Their finding that the syntax-infused APT model, particularly a hybrid version, outperforms this strong neural baseline provides compelling evidence for the utility of explicit syntactic information in modeling compositionality.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and elegant methodology:** The APT model is linguistically well-motivated. The paper clearly articulates the problem with naive composition (e.g., vector spaces for words of different POS are not directly comparable) and proposes a principled solution through the "offsetting" mechanism, which aligns vector spaces based on syntactic paths. This provides a transparent and interpretable alternative to more black-box neural composition functions.

2.  **Strong and convincing experimental results:** The evaluation is robust. The authors don't just compare against an old baseline; they create a powerful one with tuned word2vec embeddings that itself achieves a new state-of-the-art (ρ=0.74). The fact that their proposed APT models consistently outperform this strong baseline (up to ρ=0.79) significantly strengthens their claims. The detailed analysis across different model variations (aligned, unaligned, hybrid, intersective vs. additive) provides valuable insights into what aspects of the model contribute to its success.

3.  **Insightful analysis of results:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting numbers. The authors provide thoughtful discussion on their findings. For instance, the analysis of why the hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned features) works best is insightful, suggesting that both first-order features of the constituents and higher-order relational features are complementary for this task. Similarly, their hypothesis connecting the effect of subsampling in word2vec to shifted PPMI and the low frequency of target compounds is plausible and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying models.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited scope of evaluation:** The paper's empirical claims rest entirely on a single dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is the standard benchmark for this specific task, the broader claims about the promise of the APT approach for compositionality in general are not yet substantiated. It remains unclear how this method would perform on other phrasal types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object) or other languages, where syntactic relations might manifest differently. The reliance on high-quality dependency parses could also be a bottleneck for other domains or languages.

2.  **Potential complexity and scalability concerns:** The APT model, which relies on extracting higher-order dependency paths from a large, parsed corpus, appears significantly more complex and computationally expensive than the word2vec baseline. The feature space is noted to be very high-dimensional (~230,000) and sparse. A discussion of the trade-offs in terms of computational resources (parsing time, memory for feature storage, composition time) versus performance gains would make the paper more complete and help practitioners assess the model's practical utility.

3.  **Minor lack of clarity in preprocessing details:** The description of the corpus preprocessing for building the elementary representations could be clearer. The paper states that "dependency paths including the modifying constituent are ignored" and that elementary representations for constituents do not contain contextual features from the compound token. This is a critical step for ensuring a fair evaluation, but the exact procedure is slightly ambiguous. For example, when building the vector for `spelling`, are all corpus instances of `spelling bee` simply ignored, or are the co-occurrence counts from `spelling` *within* that phrase specifically excluded? This detail is important for reproducibility.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The success of the "unaligned" and "hybrid" models is fascinating, as it suggests that the modifier's first-order contexts are highly predictive, somewhat contrary to the initial motivation for alignment. Could you elaborate on this finding? Does it suggest that for noun-noun compounds, the modifier and the compound often share similar semantic roles (e.g., both function as nouns that can be subjects/objects of similar verbs), making their first-order feature spaces more directly comparable than for, say, an adjective-noun pair?

2.  Could you provide more detail on the preprocessing step for constructing the elementary vectors? Specifically, to ensure the representations of `w1` and `w2` are not "contaminated" by observing the compound `w1_w2`, were all instances of the 90 target compounds masked or removed from the corpus before extracting co-occurrence statistics for the individual constituents?

3.  Given the high dimensionality and sparsity of the APT vectors, have you considered applying dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., SVD) to the explicit count matrices before composition? This might offer a middle ground between your explicit, structured model and the dense, predictive embeddings, potentially retaining the benefits of typed features while improving robustness and efficiency.
