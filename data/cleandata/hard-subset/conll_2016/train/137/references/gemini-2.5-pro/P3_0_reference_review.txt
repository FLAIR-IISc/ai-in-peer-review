### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates the task of detecting the compositionality of noun-noun compounds. The central hypothesis is that distributional models using typed, dependency-based contextual features are better suited for this task than models based on untyped, proximity-based co-occurrences. The authors test this by applying a compositional framework based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which use higher-order dependency paths as features, to the benchmark dataset from Reddy et al. (2011). The core idea is to predict a compound's compositionality by measuring the similarity between a vector composed from its constituents and the "holistic" vector observed for the compound in a corpus.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **Strong empirical evidence for the superiority of typed features for modeling compositionality.** The paper provides a compelling comparison showing that the dependency-based APT models significantly outperform both the previous state-of-the-art and a well-tuned word2vec baseline, which both rely on untyped contexts.
2.  **An insightful analysis of different compositional strategies within a typed vector space.** The authors explore theoretically-motivated "aligned" composition (where the modifier's vector space is transformed to match the head's) versus a simpler "unaligned" approach. The finding that a hybrid of these two performs best is novel and suggests that both general and role-specific contexts of the modifier are informative for noun compounds.
3.  **A new state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark.** The paper conducts a rigorous and well-controlled set of experiments, establishing a new and substantially higher performance bar (ρ = 0.79) on the Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and sound methodology.** The paper's core hypothesis is clear and linguistically plausible: the syntactic roles words play, captured by dependency relations, are critical for understanding composition. The APT framework is a principled method for representing and composing this information. The experimental design is a strength in itself, using a direct comparison between a composed vector and an observed vector as a proxy for compositionality.
2.  **Rigorous and thorough evaluation.** The experiments are very well-executed. The authors make the effort to reproduce prior work on the same large corpus (ukWaC) and create strong, updated baselines using modern word embeddings (word2vec). Their careful preprocessing to avoid data leakage between constituent and compound representations is commendable and crucial for the validity of the results. The exploration of multiple parameters for both their own models and the baselines lends strong credibility to their final conclusions.
3.  **Insightful analysis and discussion.** The paper goes beyond simply reporting scores. The analysis of the aligned vs. unaligned models is particularly interesting and provides a nuanced understanding of composition. The authors correctly observe that for noun-noun compounds, the first-order features of the modifier (as a noun itself) are highly predictive, which explains the strong performance of the unaligned and hybrid models. This is a non-obvious finding that contributes to our understanding of distributional composition. The discussion of hyperparameter effects, such as PPMI smoothing and word2vec's subsampling threshold, adds further value.
4.  **Clarity and readability.** The paper is well-written, with the concepts explained clearly using concrete examples (e.g., Tables 1 and 2). The structure is logical, guiding the reader from the background and motivation through the methodology, experiments, and conclusions in a coherent manner.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited scope and generalizability of claims.** The paper's strong conclusions about the value of typed features for compositionality are based entirely on a single dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard benchmark, it is a narrow evaluation ground. It remains an open question whether these findings will generalize to other compositional phenomena, such as adjective-noun phrases, verb-object constructions, or full sentences, where the syntactic interactions are different. The title and conclusion make broad claims that are not fully supported by the limited experimental scope.
2.  **Incremental novelty of the core model.** The APT framework itself is not a novel contribution of this paper, as it appears to have been introduced in prior work by the same authors ("reference suppressed", Weeds et al., 2014). The contribution is therefore the application and in-depth analysis of this existing framework on a new task. While this is a valid and useful contribution, the paper could be more explicit about which specific aspects of the modeling (e.g., the hybrid composition) are novel to this work.
3.  **Lack of discussion on computational trade-offs.** The paper focuses solely on model performance without addressing practical considerations like computational cost. Explicit, high-dimensional count-based models built on complex features like higher-order dependency paths are typically far more demanding in terms of memory and processing time than low-dimensional embedding models like word2vec. Some discussion of the scalability and resource requirements of the APT approach would be necessary to assess its practicality for real-world applications.
4.  **The key finding on unaligned features needs more analysis.** The strong performance of the unaligned and hybrid models is a key result. The intuition provided—that the modifier's general contexts are predictive—is plausible. However, this could be strengthened with qualitative analysis. For example, showing which specific features from the unaligned modifier vector contribute most to the similarity for a compositional phrase (e.g., *climate change*) versus a non-compositional one (*gravy train*) would provide deeper insight and more concrete evidence for the authors' interpretation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your finding that the unaligned and hybrid models outperform the purely aligned model is one of the most interesting results. Could you provide a more detailed analysis, perhaps with a few contrasting examples from the dataset? For example, which features from the unaligned vector for "spelling" are most helpful in predicting the contexts of "spelling bee", and how does this differ from the unaligned vector for "gravy" in predicting "gravy train"?
2.  How do you anticipate the APT framework, and specifically the trade-off between aligned and unaligned features, would perform on other phrasal types like adjective-noun or verb-object pairs? For an adjective-noun pair like "red car", would you still expect the unaligned features of "red" (as an adjective) to be as informative as the unaligned features of "grad" (as a noun) are for "grad student"?
3.  Could you comment on the computational resources (e.g., training time, model size/memory) required to build and apply your APT models on the ukWaC corpus, particularly in comparison to the word2vec baselines? This would help clarify the practical trade-offs of your approach.
