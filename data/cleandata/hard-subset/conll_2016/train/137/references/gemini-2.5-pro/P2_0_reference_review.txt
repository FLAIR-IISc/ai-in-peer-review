### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, framing it as a way to evaluate compositional distributional models. The core idea is that for a compositional phrase (e.g., "climate change"), a vector composed from its parts should be highly similar to the vector observed for the phrase as a whole, while for a non-compositional phrase (e.g., "red herring"), this similarity should be low.

The authors propose a compositional model based on what they term Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses rich, typed, higher-order dependency paths as features. A key aspect of their model is the "alignment" of vector spaces before composition, where a modifier's feature space is transformed based on its syntactic relation to the head. They conduct a thorough evaluation on the standard Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 noun compounds, comparing their APT model against a strong, optimized word2vec baseline and the previously reported state-of-the-art.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A Novel, Syntactically-Rich Compositional Model (APT):** The primary contribution is the APT model itself. It's a well-motivated approach that directly incorporates higher-order dependency structures into the distributional representation and composition process. The mechanism for aligning vector spaces based on dependency paths is an elegant way to make composition sensitive to syntactic structure.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Results on a Standard Benchmark:** The paper demonstrates a significant improvement on the task of compositionality detection, increasing the Spearman's correlation from the previous best of 0.71 to 0.79. Crucially, they achieve this by not only proposing a new model but also by first establishing a much stronger baseline (0.74 using tuned word2vec), showing that their gains are not just due to improved base representations.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Compositional Factors:** The authors provide a detailed analysis of their model's components. The comparison between aligned, unaligned, and a hybrid composition strategy is particularly interesting, revealing that both syntactically aligned features and general first-order features contribute complementary information to the task.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper's strongest point is its results. Achieving a new state-of-the-art on a benchmark task is a significant achievement. The evaluation is conducted carefully, using the same large corpus (ukWaC) as the original work for fair comparison and employing repeated 3-fold cross-validation to ensure stable parameter estimation and reporting. The inclusion of multiple strong baselines (FREQ, REDDY, and their own tuned word2vec) makes the reported gains for the APT model highly convincing.

2.  **Well-Motivated and Interpretable Model:** The APT model is linguistically well-grounded. The intuition that a word's meaning is partially defined by its syntactic roles, and that composition should respect these roles, is compelling. Using explicit dependency paths (e.g., `NSUBJ.DOBJ`) as features makes the representations more interpretable than opaque dense embeddings. The example in Table 1 clearly illustrates how higher-order features can bridge the gap between words in different syntactic positions (e.g., connecting an adjective to the features of a verb that its modified noun is the object of).

3.  **Thorough Ablation and Parameter Analysis:** The authors do an excellent job of dissecting their model and the baselines. They experiment with multiple variants of their APT model (aligned vs. unaligned vs. hybrid; intersection vs. addition; different PPMI smoothing), which provides valuable insights into what makes it work. For instance, the finding that the hybrid model outperforms both the fully aligned and unaligned versions suggests a nuanced reality where both general and syntactically-situated contexts are important for composition. The analysis of word2vec parameters, leading to the insight that less aggressive subsampling is better for this task due to the low frequency of many compounds, is also a valuable contribution.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper's claims are based entirely on a single dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is the standard benchmark for this specific problem, it is a very narrow evaluation. It remains unclear how the APT model would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object phrases) or other downstream tasks where compositional models are used (e.g., sentence similarity, paraphrase detection). The model's heavy reliance on noun-modifier (NMOD) relations might not transfer seamlessly to other syntactic constructions.

2.  **Potential Scalability and Sparsity Issues:** The APT model operates in a very high-dimensional, sparse vector space (~230,000 dimensions for nouns). In contrast, the trend in the field has been towards dense, low-dimensional vectors. The paper does not address the computational or memory costs of this approach or how it would scale to even larger corpora or vocabularies. Furthermore, higher-order dependency paths are inherently sparse. The authors note that they had to abandon shifted PMI (analogous to negative sampling) because it hurt performance on low-frequency compounds, which suggests the model may be sensitive to data sparsity.

3.  **Clarity on Novelty vs. Prior Work:** The paper frequently cites "(reference suppressed)" when describing the APT model. This makes it difficult to assess how much of the model is novel to this paper versus being introduced in previous work by the authors. A clearer delineation of the specific contributions of *this* submission would be beneficial. (This is a minor point contingent on what the suppressed reference contains).

4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The reference list is poorly formatted, containing artifacts like `?` and `\Q2010\E`, which detracts from the overall polish of the paper. This is easily fixable but should be addressed.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The evaluation is focused solely on the Reddy et al. (2011) noun compound dataset. Could you comment on the expected performance and necessary adaptations of the APT model for other compositional tasks, such as adjective-noun or verb-object composition, or on broader phrase similarity benchmarks? How does the model's reliance on specific dependency relations affect its generalizability?

2.  The hybrid model, which combines aligned and unaligned modifier representations, delivers the best performance. This is an interesting empirical result. Could you provide more linguistic or theoretical intuition on why this combination is so effective? Does it suggest that both the "general" context of the modifier (from the unaligned vector) and its context *relative to a head* (from the aligned vector) capture complementary and equally important aspects of meaning for composition?

3.  Given the high dimensionality and sparsity of the APT feature space, could you discuss the potential scalability challenges of this model? How does its performance and computational cost compare to dense models when moving to even larger corpora or when extending the model to include dependency paths of order 3 or 4?
