### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates methods for detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, i.e., determining if a phrase's meaning can be derived from its constituents. The authors compare the performance of standard compositional models built on neural word embeddings (word2vec) against a syntactically-informed, count-based model using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs). The APT approach represents words using vectors of typed, higher-order dependency paths. A key aspect of the proposed composition method is the alignment of the modifier's vector space to the head's space based on the syntactic relation between them. The evaluation is conducted on the benchmark dataset from Reddy et al. (2011), which contains 90 noun compounds with human compositionality judgments. The primary metric is the Spearman correlation between a model's predicted compositionality score (cosine similarity between the composed and observed phrase vectors) and the human scores. The results show that the APT-based models, particularly a hybrid version combining aligned and unaligned features, outperform both the previous state-of-the-art and the strong word2vec baselines.

### Main Contributions

1.  **Empirical Demonstration of Syntactically-Rich Features for Compositionality:** The work provides strong evidence that count-based models incorporating explicit, typed higher-order dependency features (the APT model) are more effective for predicting noun compound compositionality than popular, dense, prediction-based models like word2vec, which rely on untyped linear context. This challenges the prevailing trend of favoring neural embeddings for all tasks and highlights the value of structured syntactic information for this specific problem.
2.  **Introduction and Evaluation of a Hybrid Composition Model:** The paper introduces a hybrid APT model that combines a syntactically-motivated feature alignment process with direct, unaligned composition. The finding that this hybrid model achieves the best performance is a significant contribution, suggesting that both aligned higher-order features and unaligned first-order features provide complementary information for judging compositionality.
3.  **Establishment of a New State-of-the-Art:** The paper presents a comprehensive and rigorous evaluation on a standard benchmark task, establishing a new state-of-the-art result (ρ=0.79) that significantly surpasses the previous best result (ρ=0.714) reported by Reddy et al. (2011) as well as a strong, well-tuned word2vec baseline (ρ=0.74).

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Rigor and Soundness:** The experimental setup is technically sound and carefully designed. The authors use the same large-scale corpus (ukWaC) as the work they compare against, ensuring a fair comparison. The use of repeated 3-fold cross-validation for parameter estimation is appropriate. The preprocessing step of treating compound nouns as single lexical items to obtain their observed vectors, while ensuring those instances do not contribute to the constituent vectors, is crucial for the validity of the evaluation and is handled correctly.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results and Thorough Analysis:** The paper reports a significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art. The results are presented clearly and supported by a detailed analysis of various model configurations and parameters (e.g., cbow vs. skip-gram, PPMI smoothing, composition functions, weighting parameters). This thoroughness strengthens the credibility of the findings and provides valuable insights, such as the observation that cbow outperforms skip-gram and that a higher subsampling threshold is beneficial for this specific task.
3.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow. The motivation for using syntactically-informed features is clearly articulated. The APT model, including the concepts of higher-order features and feature space alignment, is explained effectively with concrete examples in Tables 1 and 2. This clarity makes the work accessible and its contributions understandable.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is conducted on a single dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard benchmark, it limits the generalizability of the claims. The conclusion that APTs are superior for compositionality detection may not hold for other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object phrases), which exhibit different syntactic and semantic composition phenomena, or for languages other than English. The strong performance could be specific to the particularities of this dataset and phrase type.
2.  **Counterintuitive Findings Regarding Feature Alignment:** A central theoretical motivation for the APT approach is the syntactic alignment of feature spaces prior to composition. However, the experimental results show that the unaligned composition model (ρ=0.78) substantially outperforms the aligned model (ρ=0.75). The paper proposes a hybrid model as the solution, but it does not provide a deep analysis of why the core theoretical mechanism is less effective on its own. This finding partially undermines the primary justification for the APT composition framework and warrants a more detailed explanation. For instance, is the alignment process itself flawed, or is it simply less important for noun-noun compounds where the constituent nouns might already exist in similar semantic spaces?
3.  **Lack of Discussion on Practicality and Scalability:** The proposed APT model relies on explicit, high-dimensional, sparse count vectors (e.g., ~230,000 dimensions for nouns). In contrast, neural embeddings are low-dimensional and dense. The paper does not address the practical implications of this difference, such as the computational complexity, training time, and memory footprint of constructing and utilizing APT representations compared to word2vec. These are important considerations for the potential adoption of the model in real-world applications and represent a significant trade-off that is not discussed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The unaligned APT model's superior performance over the aligned version is a surprising and important result. Could you provide a more in-depth hypothesis for this? Does this suggest that for noun-noun compounds, shared first-order contexts (e.g., verbs that take either the modifier, the head, or the compound as an argument) are more indicative of compositionality than the higher-order contexts captured by alignment?
2.  The paper focuses exclusively on the task of *detecting* compositionality. Have you considered evaluating the composed APT vectors on downstream tasks where composition is a means to an end, such as phrase similarity or sentence-level paraphrase detection? This would help to assess whether the APT representations, which are effective for predicting compositionality, are also generally superior compositional representations.
3.  Could you provide more details on the computational trade-offs of the APT model? Specifically, what are the relative costs (in terms of time and memory) for building the APT lexicon from the ukWaC corpus and performing composition compared to training the word2vec models and performing vector addition?
