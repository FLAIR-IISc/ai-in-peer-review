### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, i.e., determining whether a phrase's meaning can be derived from its constituents. The authors propose using a compositional distributional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which employs typed, higher-order dependency paths as features. The core methodology correlates the similarity between a composed vector (derived from the constituents) and an observed holistic vector of the compound with human judgments of compositionality. Through experiments on the Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark, the authors demonstrate that their syntax-aware APT model significantly outperforms previous methods, including strong baselines using word2vec embeddings.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong empirical demonstration that typed, dependency-based features are more effective for modeling noun-compound compositionality than untyped, proximity-based features.** The paper shows a clear and significant performance gap between its best syntax-aware APT model (ρ=0.79) and strong, contemporary baselines that rely on linear word windows, such as word2vec (ρ=0.74) and the previous state-of-the-art (ρ=0.71).
2.  **A thorough and insightful analysis of different compositional strategies within the APT framework.** The authors systematically evaluate several architectural choices, including the composition operator (additive vs. intersective), feature weighting (PPMI vs. counts), and a novel comparison between syntactically-aligned, unaligned, and hybrid composition. The finding that a hybrid approach is optimal provides a valuable insight into the nature of the task.
3.  **A new state-of-the-art result on the widely used Reddy et al. (2011) noun compound compositionality dataset.** The work convincingly surpasses prior results through a combination of a more expressive feature representation and rigorous experimentation.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation and Strong Results:** The paper addresses the important and challenging problem of non-compositionality in language. The proposed methods achieve a new state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark with a convincing margin over well-chosen baselines. The primary claim—that incorporating syntactic structure into distributional features is beneficial for this task—is well-supported by the results presented in Table 5, which show a clear hierarchy of performance from untyped models to the proposed hybrid APT model.
2.  **Rigorous and Systematic Evaluation:** The experimental design is sound and convincing. The authors use the same large corpus (ukWaC) as the prior work they compare against, ensuring a fair comparison. The use of repeated 3-fold cross-validation for parameter tuning lends robustness to the reported scores. The inclusion of carefully tuned word2vec baselines and detailed ablations of the proposed APT model (e.g., aligned vs. unaligned, `⊔INT` vs. `⊔UNI`) strengthens the conclusions and allows the reader to understand the source of the performance gains.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Model Components:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting a final SOTA number. The analysis of different model variants provides valuable insights into the problem. For instance, the finding that the hybrid model—which combines syntactically motivated aligned features with less constrained unaligned features—performs best is particularly interesting, suggesting that both types of contextual information are complementary. The brief analysis of the optimal weight parameter `h`, suggesting the modifier is slightly more informative than the head for this task, is another nice detail that adds depth to the results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper's empirical claims are based entirely on a single, relatively small dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard benchmark, the strong conclusion about the general superiority of the APT approach for modeling compositionality would be more compelling if validated on other phrasal types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object) or on datasets in other languages. The evaluation is also restricted to the intrinsic task of predicting compositionality ratings, without showing whether these improved compositional vectors are useful for any downstream applications.
2.  **Clarity and Reproducibility of the APT Method:** The description of the core APT methodology in Section 3 is quite abstract and may be difficult for readers to fully grasp without access to the suppressed external reference. The "offsetting" and "reduction" mechanisms are central to the method, but the formal definition of reduction (`↓(τ)`) and the condition that "all co-occurrence types are required to have a tree-based interpretation" are not explained with sufficient detail or concrete examples. A worked example showing how the feature vectors for a sample modifier and head are transformed and composed would significantly improve the clarity and self-containedness of the paper.
3.  **Comparison to More Recent Contextual Models:** The baselines, while appropriate for comparing against the original Reddy et al. (2011) work, are somewhat dated from a 2023 perspective. The field has largely shifted towards contextualized word representations (e.g., BERT). While a full experimental comparison may be out of scope, the paper would be strengthened by a discussion of how these modern architectures might approach compositionality (e.g., by producing a single contextualized vector for a multi-word phrase) and how the proposed method compares conceptually. The current work exists firmly in the paradigm of composing static word vectors, and its relevance would be clearer if it were positioned relative to the dominant contextual paradigm.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The finding that the hybrid model outperforms both the fully aligned and fully unaligned models is one of the most interesting results. Your interpretation is that the two feature types are complementary. Could you elaborate on your hypothesis for *why* this is the case? For example, do you suspect the unaligned component captures broader semantic associations while the aligned component is better at modeling literal, systematic compositional patterns?
2.  In Section 5, you state that during preprocessing, "dependency paths including the modifying constituent are ignored." This seems like a strong choice, as contexts of the modifier might provide useful information (e.g., contexts of "spelling" might inform the meaning of "spelling bee"). Could you clarify the rationale for this? Was this decision based on theoretical grounds, or was it an empirical choice found to work best in preliminary experiments?
