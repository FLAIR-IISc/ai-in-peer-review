**Summary of the paper**

This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds. The authors propose and evaluate a compositional distributional model based on what they term Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses typed, higher-order dependency paths as contextual features. The core idea is that the feature space of a modifier word's vector can be "aligned" with the head word's vector space by prepending the dependency path that connects them. The paper presents an empirical study on the Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark, comparing this APT-based approach (in aligned, unaligned, and hybrid configurations) against strong baselines, including word2vec embeddings combined with weighted addition. The proposed hybrid APT model, which combines aligned and unaligned features, achieves a new state-of-the-art result, improving the Spearman's ρ correlation with human judgments from 0.71 to 0.79.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is a strong new state-of-the-art result on the widely used Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality detection task. The authors demonstrate that a model using explicit, structured dependency features (the hybrid APT model) can outperform popular neural embedding models.
2.  The paper provides a detailed ablation study on the utility of different feature types for this task. Specifically, it analyzes the contribution of standard first-order dependency features (unaligned model) versus structurally transformed higher-order features (aligned model), showing that a combination of both is optimal.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a clear and significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art, as well as over strong, contemporary baselines (word2vec). The reported ρ of 0.79 is a substantial gain over the 0.71 of Reddy et al. (2011) and the 0.74 achieved by the authors' tuned word2vec baseline. The use of repeated cross-validation and significance testing lends credibility to the results.
2.  **Thorough Exploration of Model Space:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments. They investigate multiple composition functions (intersective `⊔INT` and additive `⊔UNI`), different feature weighting schemes (PPMI, smoothed PPMI, raw counts), and a range of parameters for both their proposed model and the word2vec baselines. This thoroughness provides a convincing case for their final model configuration.
3.  **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper is grounded in a clear linguistic intuition: that syntactic structure, as captured by dependency paths, is crucial for modeling composition. The idea of aligning vector spaces based on the syntactic relation between constituents is logical and builds effectively on prior work in dependency-based distributional semantics.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Methodological Opacity:** The central method, Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), is not adequately described. The paper relies on a suppressed reference ("elsewhere (reference suppressed)") for crucial details. This makes the work difficult to assess and reproduce. Key concepts like the "reduction" operation (Eq. 1) and the elimination of "incompatible co-occurrence types" are mentioned but not sufficiently explained. For a paper whose main contribution hinges on this methodology, this omission is a serious flaw.
2.  **Questionable Experimental Design Choices:**
    *   The evaluation is restricted to first-order dependency features (Footnote 3). The rationale given is that higher-order paths in the composed representations are "not reliable". This seems to be a self-imposed limitation. Why not construct the elementary APTs with paths up to order 3 or 4, so that the composed vectors would have reliable second-order features for a fair comparison? Restricting the similarity calculation to first-order features seems to discard the very information that the higher-order model is designed to capture and could artificially favour certain models.
    *   The corpus preprocessing step of ignoring all dependency paths involving the *modifying* constituent of a compound is poorly justified. The argument that a modification like "(recently graduated) student" is internal is understood, but this decision discards a potentially large and valuable set of contexts for the modifier. This warrants a more detailed explanation and possibly an ablation study.
    *   The dismissal of negative results for shifted PMI "for compactness" is not ideal. Given the prominence of negative sampling (which shifted PMI approximates) in the literature, a brief report on why it failed here would be more informative than its complete omission.
3.  **Inconsistent and Unprofessional Formatting:** The paper suffers from numerous formatting and presentation issues that detract from its quality.
    *   The reference list is in a state of disarray. It is plagued by inconsistent formatting, strange character sequences (e.g., `? \Q2010\E`), incomplete entries, and repeated author names (`Schmid.,? \Q1994\E, Schmid.,`). This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a submission to a major conference.
    *   The tables are inconsistently titled. Table 5 has a caption, while Tables 3 and 4 do not.
    *   The tone is occasionally too informal. Footnote 1, for example, states that POS tags "can be easily inferred by the reader," which is not the standard for formal academic writing.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact formula used for smoothed PMI in Equation (3)? The exponent α is applied to the marginal probability of the feature type (`#⟨∗, τ, ∗⟩`), which is unconventional. Standard practice (e.g., Levy et al., 2015) applies the smoothing exponent to the context distribution. Is this a novel formulation or a typo?
2.  Given that the full APT methodology is in a suppressed reference, could you please provide a more self-contained explanation of the "reduction" process? Specifically, what are the conditions that make a co-occurrence type "incompatible" and lead to its elimination?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the evaluation being restricted to 1st-order features (Footnote 3)? What is the performance correlation when similarity is computed over all features up to order 2, even if the composed ones are considered "unreliable"? This would help clarify whether the model is truly leveraging higher-order information effectively.
