### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, i.e., determining whether a phrase's meaning can be derived from its constituent parts. The authors propose a compositional distributional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which use typed, higher-order dependency paths as features. A key idea of the APT model is to syntactically "align" the feature space of a modifier to that of its head before composition. The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation on the standard Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark, comparing several variants of their APT model (using different weighting schemes, composition functions, and alignment strategies) against the previous state-of-the-art and a strong baseline using modern word embeddings (word2vec). The results show that the proposed APT-based methods significantly outperform all baselines, establishing a new state-of-the-art on this task.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong, state-of-the-art result on a standard benchmark, supported by a rigorous and comprehensive empirical investigation.** The paper not only improves over previous work but also implements and surpasses a strong word2vec-based baseline, demonstrating the effectiveness of using structured, dependency-based features for this task. The thorough ablation of different composition functions and model variants lends significant weight to the conclusions.
2.  **An insightful analysis into the role of different types of syntactic features in composition.** By comparing an "aligned" model (the core motivation of the APT framework) with an "unaligned" and a "hybrid" model, the paper reveals the surprising effectiveness of unaligned first-order dependency features of the modifier, providing a nuanced and non-obvious finding.
3.  **The introduction and evaluation of the APT framework for composition.** While the core mechanism of alignment did not prove superior in isolation, the framework itself—using higher-order, typed dependency paths—is shown to be a powerful way to represent words for compositional tasks, outperforming the more common untyped, window-based embeddings.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Empirical Results and Analysis:** The paper sets a new state-of-the-art on the Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality detection task. The improvement from the previously reported ρ=0.71 to ρ=0.79 is substantial. Critically, the authors also show their method outperforms a strong, well-tuned word2vec baseline (ρ=0.74) which they implement themselves, demonstrating that the gains are not simply due to using a modern setup but are attributable to their proposed model. The analysis is detailed, for instance, in exploring how different composition operators (`⊔UNI` vs `⊔INT`) and PPMI weighting schemes interact with the models.
2.  **Methodologically Sound and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental design is a model of good practice. The authors use the same corpus as the original work for fair comparison, employ repeated 3-fold cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning, and report statistical significance, making the results highly credible. The inclusion of multiple baselines (RAND, FREQ, REDDY, word2vec) provides clear context for the performance of the proposed models.
3.  **Well-Motivated Approach with Interesting Findings:** The APT model is linguistically well-motivated. Using dependency paths to capture syntactic context and aligning vector spaces based on the grammatical relation between constituents is an elegant idea. The most interesting result is perhaps that the theoretically motivated "aligned" model is outperformed by the simpler "unaligned" model, and that a hybrid of the two works best. This is a valuable negative result that complicates a simple theoretical story and deepens our understanding of what types of information are most useful for this specific task.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper's strong conclusions are based entirely on a single, rather small dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard benchmark, it is difficult to assess the generality of the APT model's success. It is unclear if these findings would transfer to other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object), other languages, or other compositional tasks like paraphrase detection or phrase similarity. The claim that "knowledge of syntactic structure is crucial in the fine-grained understanding of language" is well-supported for this specific task, but its broader validation requires more diverse experiments.
2.  **Clarity of the APT Model Description:** The technical description of the APT model in Section 3 is dense and could be improved for clarity. The "offsetting" and "reduction" mechanisms are central to the paper's contribution but are explained abstractly. A concrete, step-by-step example illustrating the composition of a phrase like "grad student"—showing the initial feature vectors for "grad" and "student," the effect of offsetting the "grad" vector, and the final composed vector—would greatly aid reader comprehension. The definition of reduction (Eq. 1) is also somewhat opaque without a worked example.
3.  **The Central Technical Contribution is Empirically Undermined:** The primary novelty presented for the APT framework is the alignment of vector spaces via offsetting. However, the results in Table 3 clearly show that the unaligned model (ρ=0.78 for `⊔UNI`) performs substantially better than the aligned model (ρ=0.72). The hybrid model's success (ρ=0.79) is driven more by the strong unaligned component than the aligned one (optimal q is in [0.3, 0.5], favoring the unaligned part). This raises questions about the value of the alignment mechanism itself, at least for this N-N compound task. The paper would be strengthened by a deeper discussion of why this well-motivated mechanism appears to be less effective than simply using the constituents' original typed dependency features.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The superior performance of the unaligned model over the aligned one is a surprising and interesting result. Could you elaborate on your interpretation of this finding? Does this suggest that for noun-noun compounds, the modifier's first-order dependency contexts (e.g., verbs that take "spelling" as an object) are more predictive of the compound's contexts than the second-order contexts (e.g., contexts of other nouns modified by "spelling," like "test")?
2.  The preprocessing step mentions that when creating the observed vector for a compound, "dependency paths including the modifying constituent are ignored." Could you clarify the rationale for this? It seems that modifications of the modifier (e.g., contexts from "difficult spelling bee") could potentially be relevant for the semantics of the compound itself. What was the impact of this design choice?
