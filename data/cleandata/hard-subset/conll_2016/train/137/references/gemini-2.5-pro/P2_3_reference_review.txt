**Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the problem of detecting non-compositionality in noun compounds. The authors propose that distributional models using syntactically-rich, typed dependency features are better suited for this task than models based on untyped, proximity-based contexts (like standard word embeddings). They introduce a compositional method based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses higher-order dependency paths as features. The core idea is to "align" the vector space of a modifier with that of its head before composition by prepending the dependency relation that links them to the modifier's features. The authors evaluate their method on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns, comparing the similarity of composed representations to holistically-observed representations and correlating this with human compositionality judgments.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A new state-of-the-art result on a standard compositionality detection benchmark.** The proposed hybrid APT model achieves a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, a substantial improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.71) and a strong, well-tuned word2vec baseline (0.74).
2.  **A compelling demonstration that syntactically-informed, typed features are highly effective for modeling compositionality.** By outperforming strong word embedding baselines that use untyped window-based co-occurrence, the paper makes a strong case for integrating deeper syntactic knowledge directly into distributional representations for this type of task.
3.  **A thorough analysis of different compositional strategies within their proposed framework.** The paper insightfully compares aligned vs. unaligned composition of dependency vectors, showing that both contribute useful information and their combination (the hybrid model) works best.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper's primary strength is its performance. Achieving a Spearman's ρ of 0.79 on this task is a significant improvement. The evaluation is conducted rigorously: the authors compare against the original paper's result, implement and tune a strong word2vec (cbow) baseline, and include other useful baselines like random and frequency-based predictors. This careful comparison provides convincing evidence for the effectiveness of their approach.

2.  **Linguistically Motivated and Technically Interesting Model:** The APT model is well-motivated. The problem of composing vectors for words that play different syntactic roles (e.g., nouns vs. adjectives) is well-known, and the proposed "offsetting" or "alignment" mechanism is an elegant, interpretable solution. It explicitly models how the syntactic relation between a modifier and a head transforms the modifier's contextual features. This contrasts favorably with the more naive, commutative operations (like addition) typically applied to word embeddings.

3.  **Excellent Analysis and Ablation Study:** The paper does not simply present its best-performing model. The comparison between the `aligned`, `unaligned`, and `hybrid` versions of the APT model is particularly insightful. The finding that the unaligned model outperforms the aligned one, and that a hybrid of the two is best, suggests that both the literal first-order contexts of the modifier and its transformed second-order contexts are valuable for predicting the compound's meaning. This level of analysis adds significant value to the paper.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The APT method is explained with a clear, concrete example in Tables 1 and 2. The authors use the same standard corpus (ukWaC) and evaluation dataset as the work they are building on, which facilitates comparison and aids reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper's claims about a general compositional method are supported by experiments on only one specific task and linguistic phenomenon: compositionality detection for 90 English noun-noun compounds. It is unclear how well the findings would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object) or other tasks where compositionality is crucial (e.g., phrase similarity, paraphrase detection). While excelling at one task is valuable, the limited scope tempers the broader claims about the APT model's general utility.

2.  **Potential Lack of Novelty of the Core Method:** The paper cites "(reference suppressed)" when introducing the APT approach. This implies the core method may have been published previously (e.g., in a workshop paper). If so, the novelty of this submission would lie in its application to compositionality detection and the specific hybrid model, rather than the APT framework itself. While still a valid contribution, it would be less of a fundamental methodological advance than if the method were entirely new. This needs clarification.

3.  **Practicality and Scalability Concerns:** The APT model relies on a high-dimensional, sparse feature space based on dependency paths (the paper mentions ~230,000 dimensions). This is in stark contrast to the low-dimensional, dense vectors used by neural embedding models. The paper provides no discussion of the computational trade-offs, such as training time, memory footprint, or inference speed. For the method to be widely adopted, these practical considerations are important, and their omission is a weakness.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The evaluation is focused solely on noun-noun compounds. How do you expect the APT model, particularly the alignment mechanism, to perform on other phrasal types like adjective-noun phrases or verb-direct object phrases, where the syntactic relationships are different? Does the model's strong performance hinge on the specific nature of noun-noun compounds?

2.  Could you please clarify the novelty of the APT framework in this paper relative to the work cited as "(reference suppressed)"? Is the core contribution here the application of APTs to compositionality detection, the development of the hybrid model, or the APT framework itself?

3.  Could you comment on the computational costs (training time, memory usage) of building and using the APT models compared to the word2vec baseline? Given the high dimensionality of the feature space, are there scalability challenges that might limit its application to even larger corpora or vocabularies?
