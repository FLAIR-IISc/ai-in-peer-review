### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, a crucial sub-problem for evaluating and building compositional distributional semantic models. The core idea is that for a compositional phrase, a vector composed from its parts should be highly similar to a holistic vector for the phrase observed in a corpus. The paper introduces a compositional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which use typed, higher-order dependency paths as features. This model includes a mechanism for aligning the vector spaces of constituents before composition. The APT approach is systematically evaluated on the standard Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 noun compounds and compared against strong baselines using modern word embeddings (word2vec) with simple additive composition. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A new state-of-the-art on compositionality detection.** The proposed hybrid APT model achieves a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, a substantial improvement over the previous best result of 0.71 on this widely used benchmark. The paper also establishes a strong new baseline of 0.74 using a tuned CBOW model with additive composition.
2.  **A detailed investigation into the role of syntactic features in composition.** The work provides an insightful comparison between models using untyped proximity features (word2vec), typed first-order dependency features, and typed higher-order dependency features. A key finding is that for noun-noun compounds, a hybrid model using both aligned higher-order features and unaligned first-order features is most effective.
3.  **A methodologically rigorous evaluation.** The experiments are well-designed, using the same large corpus as prior work, carefully controlling for data leakage between constituent and compound representations, and performing systematic parameter tuning via cross-validation.

### Strengths

1.  **Significant Performance Improvement:** The paper delivers a clear and significant improvement on a standard, established task. The increase in Spearman's ρ from 0.71 to 0.79 is a major advance and convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of incorporating structured syntactic information into the representations.
2.  **Strong Empirical Analysis:** The authors do not simply present a single winning model. They conduct a thorough analysis by comparing several variants of their proposed APT model (aligned, unaligned, hybrid; intersective vs. additive composition) against well-tuned word embedding baselines. This systematic approach leads to interesting and non-obvious conclusions, such as the surprising effectiveness of the *unaligned* typed model and the further gains from a hybrid approach. This provides valuable insights for future work in this area.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written and the proposed method is explained clearly with concrete examples. The experimental setup is described in sufficient detail, including preprocessing steps, parameter choices, and model variations, which lends confidence to the results and facilitates reproducibility. The careful handling of the corpus to create holistic phrase vectors without contaminating the constituent vectors is a prime example of methodological rigor.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper's strong conclusions about the superiority of the APT model for composition are based entirely on a single dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is an important benchmark, it is a narrow evaluation setting. It remains unclear whether the findings, particularly the utility of higher-order paths and the specific alignment mechanism, would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object phrases) or to other languages with different syntactic structures.
2.  **Lack of Discussion on Complexity and Scalability:** The APT model, which relies on explicit, high-dimensional count vectors derived from a dependency-parsed corpus, is inherently more complex and computationally intensive than the word2vec+addition baseline. The feature space is noted to be ~230,000 dimensions. The paper does not discuss the practical trade-offs in terms of computational cost (for both parsing and feature extraction), memory requirements, and scalability. This is an important consideration, as the simpler baseline is already quite strong and much easier to deploy.
3.  **Contribution of Higher-Order Features is Not Isolated:** The paper motivates the APT model by emphasizing the ability of higher-order features to align vector spaces. However, the analysis does not clearly disentangle the contribution of using *typed first-order dependencies* from the contribution of using *higher-order paths*. The unaligned model, which performs very well, seems to rely primarily on first-order features. An ablation study with a model using only typed first-order dependency features would have been very valuable to quantify how much of the performance gain over word2vec comes from using typed relations, and how much is added by the more complex higher-order features.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The superior performance of the unaligned and hybrid models over the purely aligned model for noun-noun compounds is a key finding. Could you elaborate on your intuition for why this is the case? Is it because the `NMOD` relation is semantically underspecified, making the modifier's general first-order contexts (e.g., verbs it typically acts as a subject/object for) more predictive than contexts projected through the `NMOD` path?
2.  In the final evaluation, similarity is computed between the composed and observed vectors, restricted to first-order dependency features (footnote 3, page 5). This seems to discard information generated during composition, particularly the higher-order features in the composed vector. Could you clarify the rationale for this decision and whether you experimented with including higher-order features in the similarity calculation?
3.  Could you comment on the generalizability of the APT approach? Do you foresee any specific challenges in applying it to other compositional phenomena, such as verb-object or adjective-noun composition, where the syntactic relations are more varied and perhaps more semantically constrained than the N-N `NMOD` relation?
