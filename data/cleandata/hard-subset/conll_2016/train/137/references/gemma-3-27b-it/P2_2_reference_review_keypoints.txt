- Paper shows typed dependency features, especially higher-order paths, improve detection of non-compositional noun compounds.  
- Compares APT-based methods and naive compositional operations against neural embeddings on Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.  
- Empirically demonstrates the value of syntactic (typed) dependency information for compositionality detection.  
- Provides a thorough benchmark comparing multiple compositional methods, alignment strategies, and feature types.  
- Reframes Reddy et al. (2011) dataset as an evaluation of compositional models rather than just a similarity task.  
- Strong empirical results achieving state-of-the-art performance on the compositionality detection task.  
- Comprehensive experimental setup including PPMI smoothing, composition ops, alignment strategies, and corpus preprocessing.  
- Clear motivation linking linguistic theory and prior work to the compositionality detection problem.  
- Clear, detailed explanation of the APT model and its alignment/composition process, with helpful illustrative tables.  
- Reliance on a single, small dataset (90 noun compounds) limits generalizability to other phrase types and domains.  
- APT model complexity and feature engineering requirements raise scalability and adaptation concerns.  
- Paper lacks a detailed error analysis identifying which compounds fail and why.  
- Some hyperparameter choices (e.g., CBOW subsampling) need stronger justification or further exploration.  
- Question: Will typed dependency gains generalize to verb/adjective phrases or other phrase types?  
- Question: Please provide error analysis highlighting specific failure cases and reasons.  
- Question: What are the main bottlenecks to scaling APT, and how can they be mitigated?  
- Question: Can you quantify the contribution of different dependency path orders and identify diminishing returns?  
- Paper is well-written and organized.  
- Suppressing the APT paper reference harms reproducibility.  
- Recommend including discussion/comparison of computational costs versus neural embeddings.  
- Overall assessment: Strong contribution with compelling empirical evidence; recommend acceptance.
