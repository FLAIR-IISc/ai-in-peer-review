This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths within vector space models on compositional semantics, specifically focusing on compound noun compositionality. The authors evaluate their approach using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, comparing it to both traditional compositional methods and neural word embeddings.

The primary contribution is a detailed empirical investigation into the effect of incorporating syntactic information, specifically dependency paths, into distributional representations for improving compositionality detection. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that, while seemingly counterintuitive given recent trends, carefully constructed count-based models with typed dependency features can achieve competitive performance with state-of-the-art neural embeddings on this specific task. Finally, the thorough experimentation with different composition operations and parameters provides a valuable resource for future research in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors demonstrate a commitment to thorough experimentation, systematically varying parameters such as PPMI smoothing, composition operations (INT, UNI), alignment strategies (aligned, unaligned, hybrid), and weighting factors. The use of 3-fold cross-validation strengthens the reliability of the results.
2. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of simple compositional methods when dealing with non-compositional phrases, and the importance of considering syntactic structure. The connection to prior work is well established.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The presented results, particularly with the hybrid APT model, surpass the previously reported state-of-the-art on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. This demonstrates the efficacy of the proposed approach.
4. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The description of anchored packed trees (APTs) and the handling of dependency paths are well-explained. The discussion of order-2 features and the alignment process is clear and demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying linguistic principles.
5. **Insightful Analysis of Results:** The observation that unaligned features often outperform aligned features, and the interpretation related to shared contextual information, show a nuanced understanding of the data and methodology.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is confined to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) focusing solely on compound noun compositionality. The extent to which these findings generalize to other types of phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun phrases) or other semantic tasks remains unclear. The authors acknowledge this but don’t sufficiently address the potential for task-specific optimization.
2. **Excessive Detail and Redundancy:** The paper contains a substantial amount of detail regarding experimental setup and parameter tuning. While thoroughness is commendable, certain sections feel overly verbose and could be streamlined. The repetition of equations and explanations diminishes readability. For example, the detailed descriptions of each composition operation feel repetitive given the established groundwork.
3. **Formatting Inconsistencies & Typos:** There are numerous minor formatting issues throughout the paper. Equations are not consistently formatted, citation styles vary slightly (e.g., sometimes “et al.” is used, sometimes full author lists), and I identified several minor typos (e.g., “significantly” misspelled, inconsistent use of boldface). While individually minor, these errors accumulate and detract from the professionalism of the submission.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper presents correlation scores but lacks any detailed error analysis. Understanding *which* compounds the model struggles with and *why* would provide valuable insights and guide future research. Are there specific types of non-compositional phrases that consistently pose challenges?
5. **Supressed Reference:** The reference to “elsewhere” regarding the APT approach is unacceptable. A full citation is necessary for reproducibility and proper attribution. Hiding key details behind a “suppressed reference” is a major oversight.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of your approach when applied to phrase types beyond compound nouns? What modifications would be necessary to address these limitations?
2.  What specific types of errors does the model make when predicting compositionality? Can you provide examples of compounds where the model performs poorly and explain the likely reasons for the failure?
3.  Please provide the full citation for the work referenced as “elsewhere” regarding the APT approach.
4.  Why are the higher-order dependency paths limited to order 2? Would increasing the path length improve performance, and if not, why?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of shifted PMI feels somewhat incomplete. The authors mention it didn’t improve results, but don’t fully explore *why* this might be the case.
*   The motivation for choosing a window size of 100 in the initial corpus processing is not clearly explained.
*   Consider providing a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used for each model to improve readability.



Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of compositional semantics. However, the limitations regarding generalizability, the presence of numerous minor flaws, and the unacceptable suppressed reference prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance. I recommend **rejection** pending major revisions addressing these concerns. The authors need to demonstrate a greater commitment to clarity, conciseness, and attention to detail.
