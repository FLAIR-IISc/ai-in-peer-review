## Peer Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual features (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependency paths) on the ability of distributional semantic models to handle non-compositional phrases, specifically focusing on noun-noun compounds. The authors leverage the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of compound nouns and their compositionality judgements to compare models employing compositional operations on pre-trained word representations with models where composition is integrated into the distributional learning process (APT – Anchored Packed Trees). They demonstrate that incorporating typed dependency features, particularly through a hybrid alignment/unaligned approach, improves compositionality detection compared to previous state-of-the-art methods.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Investigation of Typed Dependency Features:** The most significant contribution is the systematic exploration of the benefit of incorporating typed dependency information in distributional semantic models for compositionality. The comparison between typed and untyped features, and the investigation of higher-order paths, provides valuable insights into how syntactic information can improve semantic representation.
2. **Strong Performance on Compositionality Detection:** The paper achieves improved correlation with human judgements of compositionality using the proposed APT-based models, establishing a new baseline on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the approach.
3. **Hybrid Alignment Strategy:**  The introduction of a hybrid alignment strategy—combining aligned and unaligned dependency features—proves beneficial, suggesting that both aspects of contextual information contribute to better representation of compound nouns.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of non-compositionality and its impact on phrase-level semantic modeling. The rationale for investigating this issue is well-grounded in the limitations of purely compositional approaches.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The use of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset provides a well-defined benchmark for evaluating compositionality detection. The comparison with a strong baseline (Reddy et al.'s original results) and the thorough parameter search add credibility to the findings.
3. **Detailed APT Model Description:** The explanation of the Anchored Packed Trees (APT) approach is comprehensive and easy to follow. The inclusion of Table 1, illustrating feature construction, is particularly helpful. The discussion of feature reduction and offsetting is also well-explained.
4. **Significant Improvement over Baseline:**  The reported improvements in Spearman's rank correlation coefficient compared to prior work are meaningful, and demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach. The superior performance of the hybrid model over fully aligned or unaligned models is a noteworthy result.
5. **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The exploration of various parameters (PPMI smoothing, different composition operations) and models (cbow vs. skip-gram) reveals important insights into the factors influencing performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) consisting of noun-noun compounds. While this allows for a focused analysis, it limits the generalizability of the findings.  It is unclear how well these methods would transfer to other types of phrases or compositional phenomena.
2. **Complexity of APT Model:** The APT model, while well-explained, is relatively complex. This complexity may hinder its adoption by other researchers or its scalability to larger datasets.  A more streamlined or readily implementable version might be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on quantitative results. Providing some qualitative examples of where the model succeeds and fails would enhance understanding of its strengths and weaknesses.  Illustrating what features drive the compositionality judgements would be valuable.
4. **Insufficient Discussion of Error Analysis:**  While the paper reports results, it lacks a detailed analysis of *why* certain errors occur. Identifying patterns in misclassified compounds could provide clues for further model refinement.
5. **Neural Embedding Parameter Search Limited:** The statement that the neural embedding parameter search wasn't exhaustive feels like an understatement. More comprehensive exploration, potentially with automated hyperparameter optimization, could have yielded even better results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide some examples of compound nouns where your model correctly predicts compositionality, and some where it fails? What are the key differences in the linguistic properties of these examples?
2.  The hybrid model performs best.  Can you explain, intuitively, *why* combining aligned and unaligned features is more effective than using either approach alone? What specific information does each contribute?
3.  The paper mentions that the corpus preprocessing focused on modifying dependency paths related to the compound noun itself.  Did you consider any modifications to the dependency paths of words *outside* the compound that might interact with its meaning?
4.  Given the complexity of the APT model, what are the computational costs associated with building and using it compared to simpler methods like word2vec with additive composition?
5.  Do you anticipate the proposed approach generalizing well to other types of multiword expressions (e.g., verb-particle constructions, idioms) or is it heavily reliant on the specific properties of noun-noun compounds?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise, although some sections (e.g., the mathematical definitions) could benefit from further clarification.
*   The extensive list of references is a strength, demonstrating familiarity with the relevant literature.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between the different compositional methods used in the experiments.



Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics.  The empirical investigation of typed dependency features is particularly noteworthy, and the improved performance on the compositionality detection task is encouraging. Addressing the identified weaknesses through further analysis and experimentation would strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
