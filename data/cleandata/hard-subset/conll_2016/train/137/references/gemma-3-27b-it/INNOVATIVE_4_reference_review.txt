This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths within vector space models on compositional semantics, specifically addressing the challenge of non-compositional phrases. The authors propose an ‘anchored packed trees’ (APT) approach and evaluate it on a compound noun compositionality detection task. 

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a nuanced exploration of how syntactic information – specifically, dependency relations – can be incorporated into distributional models to *better* handle phrases that deviate from strict compositionality. The work doesn't present a radically new compositional function, but rather a thoughtful investigation into how the *representation* of words, informed by their syntactic roles, impacts downstream composition.  A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the detailed experimental setup and comparison of various configurations of APT models (aligned, unaligned, hybrid) with standard neural word embeddings, offering concrete insights into the effectiveness of different approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Core Problem:** The paper directly tackles a significant issue in distributional semantics: the failure of compositional models to account for non-compositionality.  Ignoring this issue leads to inaccurate similarity measurements and limits the applicability of these models.
2. **Novelty in Representation:**  The APT approach, with its focus on encoding complete dependency paths and aligning vector spaces based on syntactic structure, is a genuinely interesting and potentially impactful approach. It moves beyond simply composing pre-existing word vectors and aims to build representations that inherently consider compositional structure.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation, systematically varying parameters like PPMI smoothing, composition operations (INT, UNI), and alignment strategies.  The use of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is appropriate, and the comparison to neural word embeddings provides a valuable benchmark.
4. **Insightful Results:**  The finding that unaligned APTs and a hybrid approach perform best suggests that while syntactic alignment is helpful, capturing broader contextual information (even without strict alignment) is crucial. The observation regarding weighting parameters (h and q) being consistently between 0.3 and 0.5 offers a useful guideline for future work.
5. **Clear Presentation:** Despite the complexity of the concepts, the paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed methods and experimental setup.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) and a single task (compound noun compositionality detection). While this allows for a focused investigation, it limits the generalizability of the findings.  Testing on other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun phrases) and different tasks (e.g., phrase similarity) would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Dependency on Pre-existing Parser:** The APT approach relies heavily on the accuracy of the dependency parser. Errors in parsing will propagate through the system and potentially impact the results.  The paper doesn’t sufficiently address the potential impact of parser errors or explore alternative parsing strategies.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study on Path Order:** While the paper investigates the use of higher-order dependency paths, it doesn’t present a rigorous ablation study to quantify the benefit of including paths beyond first order.  It would be helpful to see the performance impact of limiting the path length to 1 or 2.
4. **Missing Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the model’s predictions. Identifying specific types of non-compositional phrases where the model struggles would provide valuable insights for future improvement.
5. **Somewhat Incremental Advance:** While the work is solid and presents valuable insights, it is not a paradigm shift. The improvements over existing methods are incremental, and the overall approach builds upon established techniques rather than introducing a fundamentally new framework.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the performance of the dependency parser? Have you considered evaluating the model with different parsers or incorporating parser confidence scores into the representation?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying specific types of compound nouns where the model performs poorly?
3.  What is the computational cost of building and composing APT representations compared to standard word embeddings? Is this approach scalable to larger datasets and more complex phrases?
4.  You mention that shifting PMI did not improve results. Given the potential sparsity issues with compound nouns, could you elaborate on why you believe this is the case? Did you explore different smoothing parameters or alternative techniques for handling sparsity?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The suppressed reference in Section 3 needs to be clarified.
*   Consider discussing the limitations of using correlation as the primary evaluation metric. Other metrics, such as precision and recall, might provide a more nuanced assessment of the model’s performance.

Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful study that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. While the work is not revolutionary, the thoughtful exploration of syntactic information and the detailed experimental evaluation are commendable. Despite the weaknesses, I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and comments above. The novelty lies in the focused exploration and nuanced understanding of how syntactic features can improve compositional representations, rather than a completely new method itself.
