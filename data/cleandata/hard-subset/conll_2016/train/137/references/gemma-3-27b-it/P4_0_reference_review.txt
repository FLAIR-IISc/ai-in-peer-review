This paper investigates the impact of contextual features – specifically typed vs. untyped, and higher-order dependency paths – on compositional distributional semantics, focusing on detecting non-compositional phrases (like “red herring”). The authors evaluate various models, including those integrating composition into the distributional model versus applying compositional operations to pre-trained representations, using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of compound nouns and human judgments of compositionality.

The primary contributions of the work are: (1) a thorough empirical evaluation of different compositional methods, specifically anchored packed trees (APTs) with various alignment and composition strategies; (2) demonstration that incorporating dependency path information – particularly higher-order paths – improves compositionality detection; and (3) a finding that unaligned, typed dependency features can perform competitively with, and sometimes outperform, aligned features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The paper presents a highly systematic and detailed experimental setup. The authors explore a wide range of parameters for both the APT models (weighting schemes, alignment strategies, composition operations) and the neural word embedding baseline. This thoroughness strengthens the validity of the findings and allows for nuanced comparisons.
2. **Novel Application of APTs:** The application of Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) to the compositionality detection task is a strong point.  The explanation of how APTs capture dependency paths and align vector spaces is clear and well-motivated.  The investigation of higher-order dependency paths is particularly valuable, demonstrating a method for encoding more structural information.
3. **Clear Outperformance of Baselines:** The results consistently show that models incorporating typed dependency features (APTs) outperform the baseline methods from Reddy et al. (2011) and the neural word embedding models, indicating that capturing syntactic relationships is crucial for assessing compositionality.  This is a significant finding.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Alignment:** The observation that unaligned dependency features can perform well, and even better than aligned ones in some configurations, is a surprising and insightful result. It challenges the assumption that strict alignment is always necessary for effective composition.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide substantial detail regarding their experimental setup, including corpus details, parameter settings, and evaluation metrics, enhancing the reproducibility of their work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Overall Approach:** While the application of APTs to this specific task and the analysis of alignment are valuable, the core idea of using dependency information to improve compositional semantics isn't entirely novel. Previous work (cited in the paper) has also explored similar avenues. The paper's innovation lies more in the detailed experimentation and specific findings than in a fundamentally new theoretical approach.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, while useful, is relatively small (90 compound nouns). The generalizability of the findings to other types of phrases and larger datasets remains an open question.  The paper acknowledges this somewhat, but further evaluation on more diverse data would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Complexity of APTs:** The complexity of the APT model and the numerous parameters involved can make it difficult to understand and implement. The paper could benefit from a more concise explanation of the core concepts and a clearer justification for the choices made in the experimental design. The numerous equations, while precise, increase cognitive load.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on reporting overall performance metrics.  A deeper analysis of the types of errors made by different models (e.g., specifically identifying non-compositional phrases that are misclassified) would provide valuable insights and suggest directions for future research.
5. **Uneven Discussion of Results:**  The discussion sometimes feels uneven, with certain experimental results receiving more attention than others.  For example, the impact of different PPMI smoothing parameters is mentioned but not analyzed in depth.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) performs best. Could you elaborate on *why* you think this is the case? What specific aspects of compositionality are captured better by each component?
2.  The paper focuses on noun-noun compounds. Do you anticipate that the findings would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun phrases, verb-object phrases)? If not, what modifications to the approach might be necessary?
3.  The cbow model significantly outperforms the skip-gram model.  Can you offer any hypotheses about why this is the case, given the specific task of compositionality detection?
4. The paper touches upon the idea that the modifier is slightly more informative than the head for compositionality. Is there a way to further explore this dynamic, maybe using attention mechanisms or different weighting schemes based on part of speech?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation used for dependency paths could be clarified.
*   A brief table summarizing the key differences between the various APT models (aligned vs. unaligned, different composition operations) would be helpful for readers.
*   Consider adding a section discussing potential limitations and future directions in more detail.
