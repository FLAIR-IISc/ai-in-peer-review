This paper investigates the effect of contextual features (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependency paths) on compositional distributional semantics, specifically focusing on the detection of non-compositional phrases, using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of compound nouns. The central claim is that incorporating richer contextual information, particularly through typed dependency paths, improves the ability of distributional models to discern compositional from non-compositional phrases. The primary contribution is an empirical exploration of different compositional methods—including variations of APT (Anchored Packed Trees) composition—and their effectiveness on this task, showing that a hybrid approach leveraging both aligned and unaligned dependency features yields the best results. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that even relatively simple techniques, like the cbow model with optimized parameters, can achieve strong performance on compositionality detection. Finally, the paper reinforces the importance of syntactic structure in compositional semantics, showing typed dependency features outperform untyped features.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup & Thoroughness:** The authors perform a comprehensive set of experiments, systematically varying parameters of both the APT model (weighting schemes, alignment, composition functions) and the neural word embedding model (model type, dimensions, subsampling). The use of 3-fold cross-validation and clear reporting of results facilitate a proper evaluation of the proposed methods. This level of detail is highly commendable.
2. **Relevance of Task & Dataset:** The use of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is a strong point. This dataset provides a specific and focused benchmark for evaluating compositional models beyond simple phrase similarity, explicitly tackling the problem of non-compositionality. The task of compositionality *detection* is a valuable one, as it provides a way to assess whether a model is actually learning compositional representations.
3. **Novel Hybrid Approach:** The introduction of a hybrid model that combines aligned and unaligned dependency features demonstrates insightful thinking. The finding that this combination performs best suggests a nuanced relationship between syntactic structure and compositional semantics, where both explicit alignment and broader contextual information are valuable.
4. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The inclusion of several baselines (RAND, FREQ, and the Reddy et al. result) provides a clear benchmark for assessing the improvements offered by the proposed methods.  Demonstrating that the proposed methods outperform established baselines strengthens the validity of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of APT Exploration:** While the paper thoroughly explores variations *within* the APT framework, the exploration feels somewhat isolated. The paper doesn’t deeply compare the *computational cost* or *scalability* of the APT approach to the neural embedding approach. This omission limits a practical assessment of the method’s viability for large-scale applications.
2. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper focuses heavily on quantitative results (Spearman's rho). However, there is a lack of qualitative analysis. Providing concrete examples of phrases where the different models succeed or fail would give a more nuanced understanding of their strengths and weaknesses. Illustrating *why* certain phrases are correctly/incorrectly classified would enhance the insights.
3. **Dependence on Pre-processing:** The significant pre-processing step of recombining compound phrases into single lexical items and modifying dependency paths is a crucial aspect of the methodology. However, the paper doesn't fully discuss the potential impact of this step on the generalizability of the findings to other contexts where such pre-processing is not feasible or desirable. Specifically, the effect of potentially losing information during this process is not addressed.
4. **Sparse Feature Space Concern:** The paper acknowledges the potentially sparse feature space for APTs. The experiments show some improvements with PPMI smoothing, but a deeper exploration of techniques to address sparsity (e.g., dimensionality reduction, feature selection) might further enhance performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you discuss the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of the APT models compared to the neural word embedding models?
2. Can you provide a few examples of compound nouns where the hybrid APT model correctly predicts compositionality, but simpler models (e.g., unaligned APT or cbow) fail, and explain *why* the hybrid model succeeds in these cases?
3. How sensitive are the results to the specific parameters used in the pre-processing step (e.g., the threshold for identifying dependency relationships)? Did you explore alternative pre-processing strategies?
4. Did you investigate the performance of your model on other datasets related to compositional semantics, beyond the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, to assess its generalizability?



**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and well-structured.
* The references are comprehensive and relevant.
* The suppressed reference in section 3 should be included to improve reproducibility.
* Consider providing a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used for each model to improve clarity.



Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful study that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. While some areas could benefit from further exploration, the strengths of the paper—particularly the rigorous experimental setup, relevant task, and novel hybrid approach—outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and comments raised above.
