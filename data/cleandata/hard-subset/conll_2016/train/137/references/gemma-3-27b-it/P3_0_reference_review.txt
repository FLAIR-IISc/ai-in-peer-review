## Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

**Summary:** This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependency paths) on the ability of distributional semantic models to account for compositionality in phrases, specifically noun-noun compounds. The authors compare models leveraging these different contextual features with naive compositional operations applied to state-of-the-art distributional representations, evaluating performance on Reddy et al.'s (2011) compositionality detection dataset.  They find that incorporating typed dependency information, particularly using a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned features, yields the best performance.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Investigation of Contextual Feature Types for Compositionality:** The paper provides a thorough empirical comparison of different types of contextual features (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependencies) within a compositional distributional semantic framework. This is valuable as it directly addresses a known limitation in these models – their struggle with non-compositional language.
2. **Demonstration of Improved Compositionality Detection with Hybrid Approach:**  The identification of a hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned dependency paths) as superior is a significant finding. It suggests that both structural alignment *and* broader contextual information are crucial for accurately modeling phrase meaning.
3. **Solid Baseline and Replication:** Replicating the Reddy et al. (2011) experiments provides a useful baseline and allows for a fair comparison of the proposed methods. The improved performance over the original baseline, even with optimization, showcases the benefits of the presented approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of compositional semantics and the challenges posed by non-compositional phrases, grounding the work in a well-established area of NLP.  The introduction effectively frames the issue and highlights the limitations of existing approaches.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors adhere to the experimental setup of a previous key work (Reddy et al., 2011) for comparability, while also systematically exploring variations in feature types and compositional operations. The use of 3-fold cross-validation is appropriate.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Results:** The results are presented clearly and analyzed in a nuanced manner. The authors discuss the relative performance of different models, parameter settings, and compositional operations, offering insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. The discussion around the optimal weighting parameters (h and q) is particularly insightful.
4. **Strong Performance Gains:**  The consistently reported gains over the baseline, particularly with the hybrid approach and PPMI smoothing, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
5. **Well-Connected to Existing Literature:** The paper demonstrates a strong understanding of the relevant literature, referencing key works in distributional semantics, compositional semantics, and dependency parsing.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset:** The reliance on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, while providing a useful benchmark, is a limitation.  The dataset consists of only 90 noun-noun compounds.  Generalizability to other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-noun) or broader semantic phenomena is unclear.  More diverse and larger-scale evaluation is needed.
2. **Computational Complexity and Scalability:**  The method relies on dependency parsing and the construction of higher-order dependency paths. This can be computationally expensive, particularly for large-scale applications.  The paper doesn’t adequately address the scalability of the approach.
3. **Limited Exploration of Compositional Functions:** The paper focuses primarily on additive and intersective compositional functions. While justified by previous work, a more comprehensive exploration of other compositional functions (e.g., tensor-based methods) could reveal further improvements. The rapid exploration of compositional functions seems somewhat limited given the large parameter space.
4. **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** While quantitative results are presented, a qualitative analysis of errors would be valuable.  Understanding *why* the model fails on certain phrases could provide insights for future improvements. Providing examples of phrases the model correctly identifies as compositional vs. non-compositional would also strengthen the analysis.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalizability:** How do you anticipate the performance of your method on different types of phrases (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-noun) and in different languages? Do you foresee needing significant adjustments to the approach for these scenarios?
2. **Scalability:** What are the computational costs associated with constructing the APT lexicon and performing composition with higher-order dependency paths, particularly for large-scale applications? Have you explored any techniques to improve the scalability of the approach?
3. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide some examples of phrases where your model struggles, and discuss potential reasons for these failures?  What specific linguistic features contribute to these errors?
4. **Alignment Strategy:** The hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned features seems promising. Can you elaborate on the intuition behind this combination and the factors that determine the optimal weighting between aligned and unaligned features?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The suppression of the reference to the work describing the APT approach is unfortunate. It hinders reproducibility and understanding.
*   Consider providing a clearer explanation of the reduction process in Equation (1).
*   A more thorough discussion of the limitations of PPMI would be helpful.



Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The empirical investigation of contextual feature types and the demonstration of improved compositionality detection with a hybrid approach are significant findings.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
