This paper investigates the impact of contextual features—specifically, typed versus untyped and higher-order dependency paths—on compositional distributional semantics, with a focus on improving the detection of non-compositional phrases. The authors leverage a dataset of compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality to compare different vector space models, contrasting models where composition is intrinsic (built into the distributional model) with those employing naive compositional operations on pre-trained representations.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the systematic exploration and empirical evaluation of different compositional approaches using typed dependency information within distributional semantic models, particularly anchored packed trees (APTs). While the idea of incorporating dependency information is not novel, the thoroughness of the investigation, covering alignment strategies, composition functions, and weighting parameters in the context of APTs, is significant. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that combining aligned and unaligned APT representations via a hybrid approach yields improved performance in predicting compositionality, highlighting the benefits of capturing both structural and distributional information. Finally, the comprehensive empirical comparison against neural word embeddings and a baseline established by Reddy et al. (2011) provides a valuable benchmark for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a rigorous set of experiments, varying key parameters like PPMI smoothing, composition operations (INT, UNI), weighting factors, and alignment strategies. This systematic approach allows for a nuanced understanding of the impact of each component on performance. The inclusion of well-established baselines (Reddy et al., 2011) and a comparison with neural word embeddings provides a strong foundation for assessing the proposed methods.
2. **Novel Exploration of Hybrid Approach:** The introduction of a hybrid model that combines aligned and unaligned APT representations is a noteworthy contribution. The findings suggest that both types of information are valuable for compositionality detection, and combining them leads to superior results. This adds complexity but demonstrably improves performance.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Dependency Paths:**  The paper meticulously explores the use of higher-order dependency paths as contextual features. The explanation of how these paths are constructed and integrated into the vector space model is clear and well-justified. The discussion regarding the role of different dependency relations and their impact on composition is insightful.
4. **Strong Performance Gains:** The reported performance improvements over the previous state-of-the-art (Reddy et al., 2011) are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methods. Achieving a Spearman's rank correlation coefficient of 0.79 with the hybrid APT model indicates a strong ability to predict human judgments of compositionality.
5. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation behind the research. The background information on distributional semantics and compositionality is comprehensive, and the experimental setup is explained in detail.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Corpus Diversity:** The experiments are conducted solely on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of compound nouns. While this allows for a direct comparison with previous work, the generalizability of the findings to other types of phrases and domains is uncertain. Testing the models on a more diverse set of linguistic phenomena would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Computational Cost & Scalability:** The APT approach, particularly with higher-order dependency paths, is likely to be computationally expensive and may not scale well to larger corpora or more complex sentences. The paper doesn't explicitly address these scalability concerns.
3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** While the authors explore several parameters, the search space is still limited. The optimal parameter settings may vary depending on the specific dataset and task. A more systematic parameter optimization strategy, perhaps using techniques like grid search or Bayesian optimization, could further improve performance.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on quantitative results. A qualitative error analysis, examining specific examples where the models fail to accurately predict compositionality, could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approaches and suggest avenues for future research.
5. **Reiteration of Established Concepts:** The introduction and related work sections cover established concepts in distributional semantics. While necessary for context, these sections are somewhat lengthy and could be streamlined to focus more on the specific contributions of the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does the performance of the hybrid APT model vary across different types of compound nouns (e.g., those with strong semantic head-modifier relationships versus those with more idiomatic meanings)?
2. What is the computational complexity of constructing and composing APTs with higher-order dependency paths, and how does this affect scalability to larger corpora?
3. Have you investigated alternative methods for smoothing PPMI values beyond the α parameter, such as those commonly used in language modeling?
4. Could you elaborate on the specific reasons why the skip-gram model consistently underperforms the cbow model in your experiments?
5.  What kind of error analysis have you performed, and what are the most common types of errors made by your models?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The use of a suppressed reference in Section 3 (“Elsewhere, we proposed…”) is unusual and should be replaced with a proper citation.
*   Consider providing a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset and how these limitations might affect the generalizability of the findings.
*  The table layout could be improved for readability, using more whitespace or alternative formatting.
