## Review for "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependency paths) on compositional distributional semantics, specifically focusing on noun-noun compounds. The authors explore different compositional methods – naively composing pre-trained representations versus building composition *into* the distributional model using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) – and evaluate them on Reddy et al.'s (2011) compositionality detection dataset.  The core idea is that richer, typed contextual features derived from dependency parsing can better capture the compositional nature of phrases.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Systematic Exploration of Typed Contextual Features:** The paper provides a thorough empirical investigation into the benefits of incorporating typed dependency relations (and higher-order paths) into distributional models for compositionality.  This is a valuable contribution, moving beyond simple co-occurrence counts.
2. **Empirical Validation of APTs for Compositionality Detection:** The study demonstrates that APTs, especially when combining aligned and unaligned dependency features, achieve state-of-the-art results on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, outperforming baseline neural word embedding approaches. 
3. **Re-evaluation of Compositionality Detection Task:** The work reinforces the importance of focusing on *detecting* compositionality, rather than directly modeling phrase similarity, as a way to properly evaluate compositional methods.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistently better performance of the APT models, across different configurations, provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The improvement over the Reddy et al. (2011) baseline and the neural embedding baseline is significant.
2. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors carefully replicate the experimental setup of Reddy et al. (2011), ensuring a fair comparison.  The exploration of different parameters (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, alignment strategies) is commendable.
3. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The central argument – that syntactic structure is crucial for compositional semantics – is well-grounded in linguistic theory and aligns with recent trends in NLP. The use of dependency parsing is a logical choice to capture this structure.
4. **Clear Explanation of APTs:**  The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), including the feature alignment and composition process. The use of Table 1 and Table 2 to illustrate the features is particularly helpful.
5. **Insightful Analysis:** The observation that unaligned and aligned APTs capture different aspects of compositionality and that a hybrid approach yields the best results is a key insight.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) focusing on noun-noun compounds. While the results are strong on this dataset, it’s unclear how well the approach will generalize to other types of phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun phrases) or different languages.
2. **Computational Complexity:**  The APT approach, particularly with higher-order dependency paths, is likely to be computationally expensive, both in terms of model building and composition. The paper doesn’t adequately address the scalability of the method.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The experiments involve a substantial number of hyperparameters. While the authors perform a reasonable search, it's possible that the optimal configuration is highly sensitive to the specific dataset and parameter ranges explored.  A more systematic hyperparameter optimization strategy could strengthen the results.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:**  The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis. Identifying the types of compounds where the model performs poorly could provide valuable insights for future improvements.  For example, are there specific semantic relationships or syntactic structures that pose challenges?
5. **Incremental Benefit of Hybrid Model:** While the hybrid model performs best, the improvement over the unaligned model is relatively modest. The justification for combining the approaches could be strengthened.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of building and using APTs, particularly with higher-order dependency paths? How does this compare to the cost of training and using neural word embeddings?
2.  Do you have any insights into *why* the hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) performs better than either approach alone?  What specific types of compounds benefit most from the inclusion of aligned features?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of dependency parser?  Could the performance vary significantly with different parsers or parsing settings?
4.  Do you have any plans to evaluate the approach on other datasets or types of phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun phrases)?
5.  Can you elaborate on the role of the smoothing parameter (α) in the PPMI calculation?  What is the theoretical justification for the observed optimal value of α = 0.75?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
*   The related work section is comprehensive and provides a good overview of the field.
*   The suppression of a reference in section 3 feels odd; it’s best to include all relevant citations.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and well-motivated approach to compositional semantics. The thorough experimental evaluation and compelling results make a significant contribution to the field. While the limited generalizability and computational complexity are potential concerns, the benefits of the APT approach are clear. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
