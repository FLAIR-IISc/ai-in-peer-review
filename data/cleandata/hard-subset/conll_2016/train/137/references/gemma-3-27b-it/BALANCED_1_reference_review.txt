This paper investigates the impact of contextual features – specifically typed vs. untyped and higher-order dependency paths – on compositional distributional semantics, aiming to improve the detection of non-compositional phrases. The authors leverage the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for compositionality detection of compound nouns, comparing different models based on anchored packed trees (APTs) with standard neural word embeddings. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating that incorporating typed dependency features, and particularly a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned APTs, enhances performance in predicting compositionality beyond previously reported results. A secondary, though valuable, contribution is a thorough experimental comparison of different compositional methods within the APT framework, identifying optimal parameter settings and demonstrating the benefits of higher-order dependencies. Finally, the work highlights the importance of syntactic structure in compositional semantics, advocating for its explicit encoding in distributional models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, consistently outperforming the baseline established in that work and exceeding previously reported performance levels. This suggests a genuine improvement in the ability to model compositionality. The consistent gains across different parameter settings and model variations strengthens this argument.
2. **Detailed Exploration of APTs:** The paper provides a comprehensive and well-explained exploration of APTs as a compositional framework. The clear description of aligned vs. unaligned features, different composition operations (INT, UNI), and the rationale behind the hybrid approach demonstrates a deep understanding of the method and facilitates reproducibility. The analysis of how different orders of dependency paths contribute to performance is also insightful.
3. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup is carefully designed, mirroring the previous work by Reddy et al. (2011) for fair comparison while also including a comprehensive search over relevant parameters. The use of 3-fold cross-validation adds robustness to the results. The detailed description of the corpus preprocessing and parameter choices contributes to the transparency and reproducibility of the work.
4. **Insightful Analysis & Discussion:** The discussion of results is nuanced and insightful, offering plausible explanations for the observed performance gains. The authors correctly point out that the frequency of compounds correlates with compositionality and address potential reasons for the success of their approach.  The acknowledgement of the limitations regarding the sparsity of the corpus, and the potential impact of this on parameter choice, demonstrates a critical understanding of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is solely focused on compound noun compositionality. While the findings are valuable within this specific domain, it remains unclear how well these results generalize to other types of phrases or compositional phenomena (e.g., verb phrases, sentences).  This limitation needs to be explicitly acknowledged and potentially addressed in future work.
2. **Complexity of APTs:** While the detailed explanation of APTs is a strength, the complexity of the framework itself could be a barrier to adoption. Compared to simpler compositional methods (e.g., adding word embeddings), implementing and tuning APTs requires significant effort and expertise. A clearer articulation of the trade-offs between complexity and performance would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study on Alignment:** The paper demonstrates the benefit of the hybrid approach which combines aligned and unaligned features. However, a more systematic ablation study specifically focused on the contribution of the *alignment* step itself would strengthen the conclusions. How much does alignment contribute *independent* of simply adding more features?
4. **Limited Exploration of Neural Architectures:** The comparison with neural word embeddings is somewhat limited, using only the standard `word2vec` tool with a relatively constrained parameter search. Exploring more advanced neural compositional models, or different pre-trained embedding types, could provide a more comprehensive benchmark.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of constructing and composing APTs compared to simpler compositional methods?  A brief discussion of scalability would be helpful.
2.  Regarding the hybrid approach, how was the weighting parameter 'q' optimized?  Was there a significant sensitivity to this parameter, or does the performance remain relatively stable across a wide range of values?
3.  You mention that the results show the importance of syntactic structure. Are there specific types of dependency relations which appear particularly crucial for compositionality prediction? A brief analysis of the most informative relation types would be insightful.
4. The study focuses on noun-noun compounds. Are there any insights from this work that could be transferred to other phrase types, such as adjective-noun or verb-object combinations, and how might the APT framework be adapted for these cases?

**Additional Comments:**

* The suppressed reference in section 3 should be clarified.
* The notation used throughout the paper is generally clear, but some definitions (e.g., δ) could benefit from further explanation.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between the different APT models explored (aligned, unaligned, hybrid, composition operations).



**Overall:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The strong empirical results, detailed exploration of APTs, and insightful analysis justify acceptance. While the limited generalizability and complexity of APTs are weaknesses, they do not outweigh the strengths of the work. Addressing the questions above during the author response period would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
