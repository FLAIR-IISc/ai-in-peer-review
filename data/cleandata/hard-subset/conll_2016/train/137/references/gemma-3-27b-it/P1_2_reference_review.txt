## Peer Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on compositional distributional semantic models, with a specific focus on improving the detection of non-compositional phrases, particularly noun compounds. The authors compare models leveraging different types of contextual features within a vector space model to more traditional "naive" compositional operations applied to pre-trained word embeddings. They evaluate their approaches on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, correlating model predictions with human judgements of compositionality.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Investigation of Typed vs. Untyped Contextual Features:** The core contribution is a thorough empirical exploration of how different definitions of context – specifically, whether to include dependency relation types – impact a model’s ability to discern compositional versus non-compositional phrases.  This provides valuable insight into the role of syntactic information in compositional semantics.
2. **Demonstration of the Effectiveness of Higher-Order Dependency Paths:** The paper shows that utilizing higher-order dependency paths within a typed vector space model improves performance on compositionality detection, suggesting the importance of considering broader syntactic relationships.
3. **Replication and Extension of Existing Work:** The work builds on and extends prior research by Reddy et al. (2011), providing a more comprehensive analysis and demonstrating performance improvements through a systematic exploration of different model configurations.



**Strengths:**

1. **Well-Defined Problem & Motivation:** The paper tackles a crucial issue in distributional semantics – the challenge of non-compositionality – and clearly articulates the problem’s importance. Acknowledging that language isn't entirely compositional is a significant strength, and framing the evaluation around *detecting* compositionality, rather than just phrase similarity, is a smart move.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors employ a solid experimental methodology, replicating previous work and systematically varying key model parameters (PPMI weighting, composition operation, order of dependency paths).  The use of the Reddy et al. dataset provides a good benchmark.
3. **Clear Presentation of Results:** The results are presented in a clear and organized manner, using tables to effectively communicate performance across different model configurations. The discussion of optimal parameter settings and the correlation with human judgements is insightful.
4. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The explanation of anchored packed trees (APTs) and the rationale behind incorporating dependency paths (including the alignment and reduction steps) is thorough and well-explained. The examples in Table 1 are particularly helpful.
5. **Outperformance of Previous Baselines:**  The presented results demonstrably outperform the baseline established by Reddy et al. (2011), establishing the value of the proposed methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:**  While the Reddy et al. dataset is a good starting point, it’s relatively small (90 compound nouns).  The findings may not generalize to other types of phrases or a broader range of compositional phenomena.  Evaluation on more diverse datasets would strengthen the claims.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study on APT Components:**  The paper explores many parameters, but a more focused ablation study specifically isolating the impact of different aspects of the APT framework (e.g., the effect of alignment *vs.* higher-order paths alone) would provide a deeper understanding of what drives performance gains.  It is somewhat unclear which components of the APT approach are most crucial.
3. **Missing Discussion of Error Analysis:**  The paper would benefit from a qualitative error analysis.  What types of non-compositional phrases does the model struggle with?  What types of compositional phrases are misclassified? Understanding these failure cases could provide valuable insights for future work.
4. **Complexity of APTs:** The APT framework, while well-explained, appears quite complex.  This complexity may make it challenging to implement and scale to larger datasets or more complex linguistic structures.  The paper doesn’t fully address the computational cost of building and composing these representations.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) performed best. Can you elaborate on *why* you believe this is the case? What specific information does the unaligned component contribute that the aligned component misses?
2.  The results suggest that higher subsampling thresholds (10^-3) were beneficial for the cbow model.  This seems counterintuitive given the low frequency of some compound nouns. Could you elaborate on why you believe this parameter setting performed well, and if it's specific to this dataset or a more general phenomenon?
3.  Could you provide examples of compound nouns that your model correctly identifies as non-compositional, and also examples where it fails? A brief error analysis would greatly enhance our understanding of the model's strengths and weaknesses.
4.  How computationally expensive is the construction of the APT lexicon compared to training the word2vec embeddings? What is the scaling behavior of the APT approach as the corpus size and vocabulary increase?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a clearer connection to related work on idiom detection. While the focus is on compositionality, there is overlap with the challenges of identifying idiomatic expressions.
*   The reference to “reference suppressed” in Section 3 is confusing. This should be clarified.




Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. While the scope of the evaluation is limited, the thorough empirical analysis and clear presentation of results are commendable. I recommend acceptance, pending satisfactory responses to the questions raised above and minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined.
