## Review for "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on compositional distributional semantics, specifically focusing on the detection of non-compositional phrases (like “red herring”). The authors explore different compositional methods – aligning vectors based on dependency structure versus naive composition – and evaluate them on Reddy et al.’s (2011) dataset, aiming to correlate model predictions with human judgments of compositionality.  They demonstrate that incorporating dependency information, particularly through their Anchored Packed Trees (APT) approach, yields improvements over standard word embedding methods and untyped models.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Validation of Dependency-Based Composition:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation demonstrating the benefits of incorporating syntactic dependency information (specifically, dependency paths) into compositional distributional models for the task of compositionality detection. This strengthens the argument for moving beyond simple word embedding addition/multiplication.
2. **Detailed Exploration of APT Composition:** The APT approach, with its nuanced consideration of alignment and feature reduction, is explored extensively. The systematic experimentation with different weighting schemes and alignment strategies provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of this method.
3. **Replication and Extension of Existing Work:** The work effectively builds upon the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset and experiments, providing a solid baseline and demonstrating improvements upon prior state-of-the-art.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results consistently show that models leveraging typed dependency information (especially the hybrid APT approach) outperform both neural word embeddings and untyped distributional models on the compositionality detection task.  The gains are statistically significant and demonstrate a clear advantage for the proposed methods.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors perform a thorough investigation of different parameters (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, alignment strategies) within the APT framework. This level of detail strengthens the credibility of their findings and allows for a nuanced understanding of the method’s behavior.
3. **Clear Motivation and Connection to Linguistic Theory:** The paper is well-motivated, grounding the work in the linguistic challenges of non-compositionality. The authors clearly articulate why traditional compositional methods might struggle and how their approach addresses these issues.  The connection to formal semantics and dependency grammar is well established.
4. **Reproducibility:** The authors are transparent about the corpus, tools, and parameters used, enhancing the potential for reproducibility. The referencing of specific parameter settings from word2vec, for example, is particularly helpful.
5. **Well-Written and Structured:** The paper is generally well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is confined to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) focusing on noun-noun compounds. While a valuable benchmark, it's unclear how well these findings generalize to other types of phrases (e.g., verb-object, adjective-noun) or to other languages.
2. **Complexity of the APT Model:** The APT model, while effective, is relatively complex. The numerous parameters and alignment strategies can make it difficult to understand and implement.  A more streamlined or simplified version of the approach might improve accessibility.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study on Feature Order:** The paper mentions incorporating dependency paths up to order 2. While the rationale for this is provided, a more systematic ablation study examining the contribution of different path orders to performance would be beneficial.  Is the gain substantial beyond order 1?
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:**  While the results are positive, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of *where* the model still fails. Identifying specific types of non-compositional phrases that are challenging for the model could inform future research.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) consistently performs best. Can you elaborate on *why* you believe this is the case?  What specific scenarios or phrase types benefit most from the unaligned features?
2.  You mention that the optimum values for ‘h’ and ‘q’ consistently fell within the range [0.3, 0.5].  Is this range consistent across different experimental setups (e.g., different PPMI smoothing parameters)?
3.  You briefly discuss the limitations of shifting PMI.  Could you elaborate on why it negatively impacted performance, especially considering its success in word embedding training? Is this related to the low frequency of compounds in the corpus?
4.  Could you comment on the computational cost of the APT approach compared to simpler methods like adding word embeddings?  Scalability might be a concern for larger-scale applications.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The sentence "In general, we would expect there to be little overlap between APTs which have not been properly aligned" seems counterintuitive given the description of how dependencies are structured. Perhaps rephrase to highlight the expected difference in feature *weighting* rather than a complete lack of overlap.
*   The paper would benefit from a visual example illustrating the APT construction and alignment process.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The thorough empirical evaluation, clear motivation, and insightful analysis of the APT approach are commendable. While the limited generalizability and complexity of the model are weaknesses, they are outweighed by the strengths of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and comments above.
