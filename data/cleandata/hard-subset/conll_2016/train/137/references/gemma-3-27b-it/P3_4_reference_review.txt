## Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on the ability of distributional semantic models to capture compositionality, specifically within noun-noun compounds. The authors leverage the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, evaluating both models *integrating* composition (APT-based) and those *applying* composition to pre-trained representations (word embeddings).  The core idea is that richer, dependency-informed contexts can better represent phrases, even non-compositional ones, allowing for better discrimination.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation of Contextual Features for Compositionality:** The systematic comparison of typed vs. untyped dependency features, combined with higher-order paths, provides valuable insights into the role of syntactic information in distributional semantics. This goes beyond simply applying existing techniques and digs into *why* they succeed or fail.
2. **Demonstration of APTs' Effectiveness:** The results clearly show that APT-based compositional models, particularly those incorporating both aligned and unaligned dependency paths, outperform standard word embedding approaches on the compositionality detection task. This strengthens the case for integrating compositional principles *within* the distributional model rather than as a post-hoc step.
3. **Replication & Extension of Existing Work:**  The paper builds upon Reddy et al. (2011) and provides a more thorough exploration of parameters and methodologies, establishing a strong baseline and demonstrating significant improvements.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors consistently achieve higher Spearman’s rank correlation scores compared to the original Reddy et al. (2011) results, demonstrating a genuine advancement in compositionality detection. The systematic parameter tuning and cross-validation provide confidence in the robustness of these findings.
2. **Well-Motivated Methodology:**  The rationale for exploring typed dependency features and higher-order paths is clearly articulated and grounded in linguistic theory. The argument that syntactic structure is crucial for understanding phrase meaning is convincing. The explanation of why intersective and additive methods might have different impacts is well done.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The use of a dedicated dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) focused on compositionality, combined with detailed explanations of preprocessing and model configurations, allows for meaningful comparisons and reproducibility. The comparison against multiple baselines (including a frequency baseline) strengthens the validity of the results.
4. **Clear and Comprehensive Presentation:**  The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and results. The inclusion of Table 1, illustrating the typed dependency features, is particularly helpful for understanding the core concepts. The discussion of the limitations and potential future directions is also valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Compositional Functions:** The paper primarily focuses on additive and intersective composition functions.  While the authors justify this choice based on preliminary experiments, exploring other more sophisticated functions (e.g., tensor-based methods) could potentially reveal further improvements or nuanced insights.
2. **Corpus Dependency:** The performance gains are demonstrated on the ukWaC corpus. While this is consistent with previous work, it raises questions about the generalizability of the results to other corpora with different characteristics (e.g., domain, size, parsing quality). A brief discussion of this limitation would be beneficial.
3. **Alignment Rationale Not Fully Developed:** While the hybrid approach (aligned + unaligned) performs best, the justification for combining these two approaches could be more detailed. Why is it beneficial to retain some unaligned information, and what specific types of compounds benefit most from this? A deeper analysis of error cases might shed light on this.
4. **Missing Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis.  Identifying the types of compounds that are consistently misclassified by the different models could provide valuable insights for future research.  For example, are there specific semantic categories or syntactic structures that pose particular challenges?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalizability:**  Have you explored the performance of your models on other corpora, or do you anticipate significant performance variations based on corpus characteristics (e.g., domain, size, parsing accuracy)?
2. **Alignment Benefits:** Could you elaborate on the specific scenarios where the unaligned features contribute most to the performance gain in the hybrid approach? Do certain types of non-compositional phrases particularly benefit from the unaligned information?
3. **Alternative Compositional Functions:**  Have you experimented with other compositional functions beyond additive and intersective operations? If so, what were the results? If not, could you briefly discuss the potential benefits or challenges of exploring alternatives?
4. **Error Analysis:**  Could you provide some examples of compounds that are consistently misclassified by your models, and discuss the potential reasons for these errors?  What types of compounds are the most challenging?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the relationship between compositionality detection and other related tasks, such as idiom detection and metaphor recognition.
* The suppressed reference in Section 3 should be included for completeness.




**Overall:**

This is a strong and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributional semantics. The systematic exploration of contextual features and the demonstration of the effectiveness of APT-based compositional models are particularly noteworthy. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper far outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions and suggestions outlined above.
