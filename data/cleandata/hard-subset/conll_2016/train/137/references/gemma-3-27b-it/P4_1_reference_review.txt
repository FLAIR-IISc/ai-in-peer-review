This paper investigates the impact of contextual features – specifically typed versus untyped and higher-order dependency paths – on compositional distributional semantics, using a dataset of compound nouns and human judgments of compositionality. The central idea is that the way context is defined in distributional models affects their ability to accurately represent phrases, particularly non-compositional ones. The authors compare models employing these different contextual features with naively composed state-of-the-art distributional representations.

The primary contributions of this work are: 1) A thorough empirical evaluation of different contextual feature definitions (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependencies) within distributional models for assessing phrase compositionality; 2) Demonstration that incorporating dependency structure – specifically through higher-order dependency paths – improves compositionality detection, outperforming simpler methods; and 3) Evidence that a hybrid approach, combining aligned and unaligned dependency features, yields the best performance, suggesting both syntactic structure and general co-occurrence information are valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors meticulously adapt a well-established experimental setup (Reddy et al., 2011) and build upon it with a systematic exploration of different contextual feature definitions. They clearly articulate the rationale behind each experimental choice and parameter setting.
2. **Comprehensive Comparison:** The paper provides a comprehensive comparison between different compositional models, including APT models with varying alignment strategies and neural word embeddings. This allows for a nuanced understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
3. **Novel Hybrid Approach:** The introduction of the hybrid model, combining aligned and unaligned dependency features, is a valuable contribution. This approach demonstrates that considering both syntactic structure and broader co-occurrence patterns leads to improved performance. The reasoning behind this is well articulated.
4. **Detailed Analysis of Results:** The authors present a detailed analysis of the results, including the influence of different parameters (e.g., weighting factors *h* and *q*) and the effect of PPMI smoothing. This level of detail strengthens the credibility of the findings.
5. **Clear Connection to Linguistic Theory:** The paper is grounded in linguistic theory, particularly the discussion of compositionality and the role of syntactic structure. This provides a theoretical justification for the proposed methods and helps to interpret the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is limited to a single dataset of compound nouns. While this dataset is well-established, the findings may not generalize to other types of phrases or linguistic phenomena. Evaluating on a broader range of phrase types would strengthen the generalizability of the conclusions.
2. **Complexity of APT Model:** The APT model, while theoretically sound, is complex and may be difficult to implement and scale. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost and practical limitations of the model.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study within APT:** The paper explores different configurations *of* the APT model (aligned, unaligned, hybrid) but lacks a detailed ablation study *within* the core APT mechanism. For example, the contribution of higher-order dependencies beyond first-order could be more clearly isolated. Is the gain from second-order substantial, or marginal?
4. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The performance of the models appears to be sensitive to parameter settings (e.g., subsampling threshold for word2vec). A more robust analysis of parameter sensitivity would be beneficial, perhaps using techniques like grid search or Bayesian optimization.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on overall performance metrics. A more in-depth error analysis, identifying specific types of compounds where the models struggle, would provide valuable insights and guide future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do the performance differences between the aligned, unaligned, and hybrid APT models vary across different types of compound nouns (e.g., those with strong semantic headship vs. those with more coordinate semantics)?
2. Could you elaborate on the computational cost and scalability of the APT model, particularly for larger corpora and more complex dependency parses?
3.  You mention that shifted PMI had a negative effect on results. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why this might be the case, given the low frequency of many compound nouns?
4. Would the conclusions of your work hold if you were to apply them to a dataset containing multi-word expressions that aren't exclusively noun compounds? What modifications, if any, would be needed?
