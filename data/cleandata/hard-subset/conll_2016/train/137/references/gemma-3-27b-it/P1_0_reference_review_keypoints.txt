- Empirical finding: typed dependency features improve detection of non-compositional noun-noun compounds.  
- APT-based models with a hybrid aligned/unaligned strategy outperform previous state-of-the-art on Reddy et al. (2011).  
- Systematic comparison of typed vs. untyped features and higher-order dependency paths yields insights on syntactic information utility.  
- Clear motivation and problem definition around non-compositionality in phrase-level semantics.  
- Rigorous experimental setup using a standard benchmark, strong baselines, and thorough parameter search.  
- Detailed and accessible description of the Anchored Packed Trees (APT) model and feature construction.  
- Significant Spearman correlation improvements; hybrid model outperforms fully aligned or unaligned variants.  
- Comprehensive ablation studies exploring PPMI smoothing, composition operations, and embedding types.  
- Evaluation restricted to a single noun-noun compound dataset, limiting generalizability to other phrase types.  
- APT model complexity may hinder adoption and scalability; a simpler implementation would help.  
- Paper lacks qualitative examples illustrating specific successes and failures.  
- Insufficient error analysis to explain patterns behind misclassifications.  
- Neural embedding hyperparameter search was limited and could be more exhaustive.  
- Requests for examples of predicted-success and predicted-failure compounds to illuminate linguistic differences.  
- Asks for an intuitive explanation of why combining aligned and unaligned features is beneficial.  
- Queries whether dependency-path modifications for words outside the compound were considered.  
- Requests comparison of computational costs between APT and simpler methods (e.g., word2vec with additive composition).  
- Asks whether the approach generalizes to other multiword expressions (verb-particle, idioms).  
- Writing is generally clear but some mathematical definitions need clarification.  
- Overall recommendation: accept with minor revisions, with suggested additional analyses and experiments.
