- Paper studies how typed vs. untyped contextual features and higher-order dependency paths affect compositional distributional models for detecting non-compositional noun compounds.
- Compares typed vector-space models and APT-based methods against naive compositional operations on pre-trained embeddings, evaluated on Reddy et al. (2011).
- Contribution: thorough empirical comparison of typed vs. untyped contextual features for compositionality detection.
- Contribution: shows higher-order dependency paths in a typed model improve non-compositionality detection.
- Contribution: replicates and extends Reddy et al. (2011), demonstrating systematic improvements across model configurations.
- Strength: well-motivated problem framing focused on detecting compositionality rather than just phrase similarity.
- Strength: rigorous experimental setup with replication, systematic parameter variation, and use of a standard benchmark.
- Strength: clear presentation of results and discussion of optimal parameters and human-judgement correlations.
- Strength: detailed feature engineering and clear explanation of APTs, alignment, and reduction steps with helpful examples.
- Strength: outperforms previous baselines from Reddy et al. (2011).
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a small dataset (90 compounds), raising concerns about generalizability to other phrase types.
- Weakness: lacks an ablation study isolating APT components (e.g., alignment vs. higher-order paths) to pinpoint drivers of improvement.
- Weakness: missing qualitative error analysis to characterize failure modes on compositional and non-compositional phrases.
- Weakness: APT framework appears complex and potentially costly; paper doesn’t fully address computational/scalability costs.
- Question: why does the hybrid (aligned + unaligned APT) model outperform—what unique information does the unaligned component add?
- Question: why did a high subsampling threshold (10^-3) help cbow despite low-frequency compounds—dataset-specific or general effect?
- Question: please provide examples of compounds correctly/incorrectly classified to enable error analysis.
- Question: how computationally expensive is building the APT lexicon relative to training word2vec, and how does it scale with corpus/vocabulary size?
- Additional: connect more clearly to idiom-detection literature due to overlap with compositionality challenges.
- Additional: clarify the “reference suppressed” note in Section 3.
- Overall: solid, well-written contribution recommended for acceptance pending responses to questions and minor revisions.
