This paper investigates the impact of contextual features—specifically, typed versus untyped, and higher-order dependency paths—on compositional distributional semantics, focusing on the challenging problem of non-compositional phrases. The authors evaluate their approach on a dataset of compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality, comparing different compositional models built on both traditional distributional methods and neural word embeddings.

The primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical investigation into the role of dependency-based contextual features for improving compositionality detection, particularly in the context of noun-noun compounds. The authors systematically explore different feature types (typed vs. untyped, higher-order) and compositional operations (addition, intersection) within an APT (Anchored Packed Trees) framework.  A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration that integrating aligned and unaligned dependency features offers a notable performance boost. The paper also provides a comprehensive and well-structured experimental setup, carefully adapting existing methods and datasets for a rigorous evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a very thorough set of experiments, systematically varying key parameters like PPMI weighting, composition operation, and alignment strategies. This level of detail allows for nuanced conclusions and a clear understanding of the factors influencing performance. The comparison against a strong baseline (Reddy et al., 2011) and inclusion of neural word embeddings provide valuable context.
2. **Insightful Analysis of Dependency Features:** The work highlights the importance of incorporating syntactic information, specifically dependency paths, into compositional models. The distinction between typed and untyped features, and the exploration of higher-order paths, provide valuable insights into how linguistic structure can enhance semantic representation. The finding that a combination of aligned and unaligned features performs best is particularly interesting and suggests a nuanced relationship between syntactic structure and compositional meaning.
3. **Rigorous Methodology and Dataset:** The use of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is appropriate, as it directly addresses the challenge of compositionality. The detailed description of the corpus preprocessing steps, including handling of compound nouns and dependency parsing, enhances the reproducibility of the results.  The controlled experimental design and use of cross-validation further strengthen the validity of the findings.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The introduction effectively motivates the research problem, and the related work section provides a good overview of relevant literature. The experimental setup is described in detail, and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Method:** While the experimental investigation is thorough, the core methodology—using dependency trees and compositional operations—is not fundamentally novel. The primary novelty lies in the specific application and systematic comparison of different feature types within this existing framework.  The paper would benefit from a stronger articulation of *why* these specific choices (APT, dependency features) are particularly well-suited to the problem of non-compositionality.
2. **Complexity of APT Framework:** The description of the APT framework, particularly the offset and reduction steps, is somewhat dense and could be made more accessible. A visual illustration of the process might be helpful for readers unfamiliar with the approach. This complexity makes it harder to assess the true contribution of the dependency structure *independent* of the intricacies of the APT implementation.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are presented clearly, there's a lack of qualitative error analysis. Examining specific examples where the models fail could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and suggest avenues for future improvement. Understanding *what kinds* of non-compositional phrases are most challenging would be particularly useful.
4. **Potential for Corpus Bias:** The reliance on the ukWaC corpus, while common in this field, introduces potential biases. The web-derived nature of the corpus might not accurately reflect the distribution of compositional and non-compositional phrases in other domains. A discussion of this limitation would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the theoretical motivations for choosing the APT framework specifically? What advantages does it offer over other distributional compositional models (e.g., tensor-based methods) in handling non-compositionality?
2.  The hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned features) performs best. Can you provide examples of compound nouns where the aligned features are particularly important versus those where the unaligned features are more informative?
3.  You mention that the optimal values for *h* and *q* are consistently between 0.3 and 0.5. Does this suggest that the dependency information is providing relatively limited signal compared to the constituent word meanings, or is this a more nuanced interaction?
4.  Have you considered incorporating semantic information beyond dependency structure, such as WordNet or other lexical resources, to further improve compositionality detection?
