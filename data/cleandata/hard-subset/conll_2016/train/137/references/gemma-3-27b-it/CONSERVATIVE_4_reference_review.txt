This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition – specifically, typed vs. untyped dependency relations and higher-order dependency paths – on compositional distributional semantics, focusing on noun compound meaning. The authors aim to improve the detection of non-compositional phrases, using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.

The primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical exploration of different configurations within a dependency-based distributional model for assessing phrase compositionality. While the core ideas of leveraging dependency parsing for semantic representation aren’t novel, the systematic investigation of feature typing (order of dependency paths), alignment strategies, and composition functions (addition, intersection) is valuable. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that dependency-based models, with careful parameter tuning, can achieve performance exceeding previous state-of-the-art results on the Reddy et al. dataset.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors adopt a solid experimental framework, replicating the setup of Reddy et al. (2011) for fair comparison and utilizing established evaluation metrics (Spearman’s rank correlation). The cross-validation procedure is appropriate, and the parameter search, while not exhaustive, is a reasonable effort.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration of Model Configurations:** The paper demonstrates a commendable breadth in investigating different model components – feature order, alignment strategies, and composition functions – providing a nuanced understanding of their interplay. The hybrid approach, combining aligned and unaligned features, appears particularly promising.
3. **Outperformance of Baseline:**  The reported results consistently outperform the baseline from Reddy et al. (2011), showcasing a tangible improvement over the prior state-of-the-art. This suggests the explored techniques are genuinely effective for the task.
4. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the problem of compositional semantics and the motivation behind the research. The introduction provides adequate background and context.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core concept of incorporating dependency parsing into distributional semantics is not new. Existing work (cited within the paper) has already explored this avenue. The paper’s novelty lies primarily in the *systematicity* of the exploration, not a fundamentally new approach. This diminishes the impact of the work.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The reliance solely on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is a significant concern. This dataset, while useful, is relatively small and focused on noun-noun compounds. Generalizability to other phrase types or domains remains unproven. Evaluating on more diverse datasets is critical.
3. **Insufficient Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to Reddy et al. (2011) is appropriate given the experimental setup, the paper would benefit from comparing against more recent and powerful pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) even if adapted for compositionality detection.  The reported performance of cbow models, while good, is unlikely to compete with the capabilities of these transformer-based models. Ignoring these more modern approaches weakens the claim of achieving "state-of-the-art" results.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks any qualitative error analysis. Understanding *where* the model fails would provide valuable insights for future improvements. Identifying specific types of non-compositional phrases that pose challenges could inform the development of more sophisticated techniques.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the size of the corpus?  Would performance significantly degrade with a smaller corpus, and if so, what strategies could be employed to mitigate this issue?
2.  Have you explored the use of pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec, GloVe) as initialization for the APT representations? Would this improve performance, and if not, why do you think that is the case?
3.  Can you provide examples of noun compounds where the hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned features) significantly outperforms the other models? What characteristics do these compounds share?
4.  The paper mentions the importance of dependency path order. How would the results change if you limited the maximum path order considered? Is there a diminishing return for including higher-order paths?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The suppression of the reference to the "elsewhere" work (regarding APTs) is a minor concern. Full transparency is expected in publications.
*   The discussion of shifted PMI feels somewhat incomplete. A more thorough justification for its exclusion would be helpful.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between the models explored (e.g., feature typing, alignment, composition function).
