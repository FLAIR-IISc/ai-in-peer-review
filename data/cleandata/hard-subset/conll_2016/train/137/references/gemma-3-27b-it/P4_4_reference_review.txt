This paper investigates the impact of contextual features – specifically typed vs. untyped, and higher-order dependency paths – on compositional distributional semantics, using a dataset designed to evaluate a model’s ability to handle non-compositional phrases. The core idea is that the way context is defined affects how well models capture phrase meaning, particularly when compositionality isn't straightforward. The authors experiment with anchored packed trees (APTs) and compare them to naive compositional methods applied to state-of-the-art word embeddings.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the systematic investigation of different contextual features within a compositional distributional model, specifically leveraging dependency parsing to inform the creation of these features. The exploration of higher-order dependency paths and the comparison with simpler untyped approaches provide a nuanced understanding of how syntactic information can be incorporated to improve compositionality detection. A second, supporting contribution is the thorough empirical evaluation using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, allowing for a direct comparison to prior work and a detailed analysis of the effectiveness of different model configurations. Finally, the demonstration that APT models, particularly those employing a hybrid aligned/unaligned approach, outperform standard neural word embedding composition provides valuable insights for the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors meticulously follow the experimental setup from Reddy et al. (2011), ensuring a fair comparison and facilitating reproducibility. The use of the ukWaC corpus and the Reddy dataset provides a solid foundation for evaluation. The careful consideration of parameter tuning via cross-validation further strengthens the reliability of the results.
2. **Detailed Exploration of Contextual Features:** The paper goes beyond simply comparing different models and delves into the specifics of *how* contextual features are defined and used. The distinction between typed and untyped features, combined with the investigation of higher-order dependency paths, provides a granular analysis of their impact on compositionality.
3. **Novelty in Hybrid Approach:** The proposed hybrid approach, combining aligned and unaligned APTs, is a significant contribution. The results demonstrate that leveraging both perspectives – considering both the specific dependency relation and the broader contextual information – leads to improved performance. This suggests a more nuanced approach to compositional semantics is required.
4. **Outperformance of State-of-the-Art:** Consistently outperforming previous state-of-the-art results, including those utilizing neural word embeddings, lends credibility to the proposed APT-based methods and highlights their potential for advancing the field.
5. **Clear Explanation of APTs:** The explanation of Anchored Packed Trees is clear and well-motivated, demonstrating a strong understanding of the underlying linguistic principles. The example in Table 1 effectively illustrates how dependency paths are represented and utilized.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Composition Operations:** The paper primarily focuses on additive and intersective composition operations. While the authors acknowledge the rationale for this choice based on previous work, exploring a wider range of compositional functions (e.g., tensor-based methods) could provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed models.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, while valuable, is relatively small (90 compound nouns). While the authors adequately address the limitations of the dataset, results might not generalize to more complex phrases or different domains. Expanding the evaluation to include other datasets could strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The optimal parameters (h and q) appear to consistently fall within a narrow range ([0.3, 0.5]). While this suggests some stability, a more in-depth analysis of the parameter space and the sensitivity of the results to these parameters would be beneficial. The rationale behind this narrow range could be further explored.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the results are presented clearly, a more detailed error analysis could provide valuable insights into the types of phrases where the models struggle and guide future research. Understanding *why* the models fail in certain cases is crucial for improving their performance.
5. **Supressed Reference:** Mentioning a "suppressed reference" without providing details hinders reproducibility and transparency. Details on this prior work are necessary.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for suppressing the reference to your earlier work on APTs? Providing access to that work is crucial for understanding the full context of this research.
2.  You mention that higher subsampling thresholds yielded better results for the cbow model than typically recommended. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why you believe this is the case, given the potential drawbacks of aggressive subsampling?
3.  Given the improved performance of the hybrid APT model, did you investigate whether combining other model configurations (e.g., aligning neural embeddings) could lead to further gains?
4.  What types of errors does the model make on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset? Specifically, can you provide examples of compound nouns where the model incorrectly predicts compositionality?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The figures and tables are informative and effectively support the presented results. The references are comprehensive. Consider a broader discussion of the limitations of distributional semantics in capturing non-compositional meaning, and potential avenues for addressing these limitations beyond the scope of this work.
