This paper investigates the impact of contextual features – specifically, typed vs. untyped and higher-order dependency paths – on compositional distributional semantics, focusing on the detection of non-compositional phrases. The authors leverage the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of compound nouns and their compositionality judgements to compare models employing different feature definitions and compositional operations.

The primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical exploration of different compositional distributional semantic models using dependency-based features, and a demonstration that incorporating typed dependency information and higher-order paths can improve compositionality detection. A second contribution is the detailed comparison of various compositional operations (addition, intersection) within these models, providing insights into their effectiveness. Finally, the paper provides a strong baseline and detailed experimental setup for future work in this area, including a replication of previous results and a careful consideration of parameter settings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup and Baseline:** The authors meticulously replicate and extend the experimental setup from Reddy et al. (2011), creating a strong baseline for comparison. The use of a well-established dataset allows for direct comparison with existing work and facilitates evaluation of the proposed methods. The attention to detail in preprocessing and parameter tuning is commendable.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration of Feature Engineering:** The systematic investigation of typed vs. untyped features and higher-order dependency paths is a significant strength.  The analysis clearly shows how different feature representations impact model performance, offering valuable insights into the relationship between syntactic structure and semantic composition. The explanations around why higher-order paths are beneficial and how they relate to the role of the head noun are insightful.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Compositional Operations:** The exploration of different compositional operations (INT, UNI) and weighting parameters (h, q) is well-executed. Identifying the optimal settings for each model, and analyzing their effects, adds depth to the study.  The finding that a hybrid approach, combining aligned and unaligned APTs, yields the best results is particularly noteworthy.
4. **Strong Performance Improvements:** The proposed models consistently outperform the baseline established by Reddy et al. (2011), demonstrating the effectiveness of the incorporated features and compositional strategies.  The reported Spearman’s rank correlation coefficients show statistically significant improvements, highlighting the value of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is valuable, it is relatively small (90 compound nouns).  Evaluating the models on a wider range of phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun phrases) and datasets would strengthen the generalizability of the findings. The reliance on a single dataset limits the conclusions that can be drawn about the broader applicability of the proposed methods.
2. **Complexity of APT Models:** The anchored packed tree (APT) models, while promising, are conceptually complex and require significant implementation effort. The detailed description in Section 3 is dense, and a more intuitive explanation of the core ideas might improve accessibility. It is not immediately clear why these models are superior to simpler alternatives without a significant effort to understand the details.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on quantitative results.  A qualitative error analysis, examining specific examples where the models succeed or fail, could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and guide future development.  Understanding *why* certain phrases are difficult to handle could lead to more targeted improvements.
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The computational complexity of constructing and composing the APT models is not discussed.  Given the large dimensionality of the feature spaces (80,000-230,000), scalability could be a concern for real-world applications. A discussion of the computational trade-offs would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of dependency parser used? Have you explored the impact of using different parsers or parsing accuracy on the performance of the models?
2.  You mention that the hybrid model performs best, combining aligned and unaligned APTs. Can you provide more intuition as to *why* this is the case?  Specifically, what types of phrases benefit most from the aligned features, and which benefit from the unaligned features?
3.  Given the computational complexity of the APT models, have you considered any dimensionality reduction techniques or approximation methods to improve scalability?
4.  You state that the choice of subsampling threshold was surprising, given literature. Could you further investigate *why* a higher threshold seems to be beneficial for your specific task and dataset?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation used for the compositional operations (e.g., ⊔INT, ⊔UNI) could be made more consistent and easier to understand.
*   The suppressed reference in Section 3 should be included in the final version of the paper.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between the various models and their computational costs.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional distributional semantics. While the scope of evaluation is somewhat limited, the rigorous experimental setup, comprehensive analysis of feature engineering, and promising results justify acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
