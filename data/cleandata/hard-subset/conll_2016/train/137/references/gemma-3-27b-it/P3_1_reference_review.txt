## Review for “When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases”

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on compositional distributional semantics, specifically aiming to improve the detection of non-compositional phrases. The authors leverage the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset and compare different models – including variations of anchored packed trees (APTs) and neural word embeddings – in their ability to correlate with human judgments of compositionality.  The core argument is that richer contextual information, encoded via typed dependency relations and higher-order paths, allows for better compositional modeling and thus more accurate identification of non-compositional phrases.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Systematic Exploration of Typed Dependency Features:** The paper provides a thorough empirical analysis of the impact of typed versus untyped dependency features within a compositional distributional semantic framework. This is a valuable contribution, as the role of syntactic information in compositionality is often assumed but rarely systematically investigated with such detail.
2. **Effective Hybrid APT Model:** The introduction and successful demonstration of a hybrid APT model, combining aligned and unaligned dependency features, represents a significant improvement over individual approaches.  This suggests a nuanced understanding of how different types of contextual information contribute to compositional understanding.
3. **Strong Baseline & Reproducibility:** The careful replication of the Reddy et al. (2011) experiment provides a solid baseline and ensures reproducibility, which is crucial for advancing the field.



**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:**  The authors adhere closely to the established Reddy et al. (2011) experimental framework, facilitating direct comparison and showcasing genuine improvements. The comprehensive parameter search for both APTs and word embeddings demonstrates a commitment to robust evaluation.
2. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper effectively articulates the limitations of traditional compositional methods, particularly their failure to account for non-compositionality. The motivation for exploring richer contextual features is well-justified.
3. **Detailed APT Model Explanation:** The explanation of the Anchored Packed Tree (APT) approach is exceptionally clear and well-structured, making the methodology accessible.  The explanation of feature alignment and reduction is particularly helpful.
4. **Demonstrated Performance Gains:** The results consistently show improvements over the baseline, particularly with the hybrid APT model, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The observed correlation scores are noteworthy.
5. **Insightful Analysis of Parameter Choices:** The observation that optimal weighting parameters in the compositional models fall within a specific range (0.3-0.5) provides valuable insights into the relative importance of modifier and head information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is a good starting point, its limited size (90 compounds) raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings. Expanding the evaluation to more diverse datasets and types of non-compositional phrases (beyond noun-noun compounds) would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on quantitative results. A qualitative analysis of the errors made by the models – identifying specific types of non-compositional phrases that pose challenges – would provide valuable insights for future work. What kinds of "red herrings" *still* fool the system?
3. **Dependency on Specific Parser:** The performance of the APT models is intrinsically linked to the quality of the dependency parsing. The paper doesn’t thoroughly discuss the potential impact of parsing errors on the results. How robust are the findings to variations in parser performance or different parsing schemes?
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The APT models, particularly with higher-order dependency paths, are likely computationally more expensive than simpler methods like adding word embeddings. The paper doesn't address the trade-off between performance and computational efficiency.  This is important for practical applicability.
5. **Missing Comparison to More Recent Compositional Models:** While the related work section is comprehensive, it doesn’t engage with very recent advances in compositional semantics, particularly those utilizing transformer-based models or more complex neural architectures.  A comparison to these more state-of-the-art approaches would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the quality of the dependency parsing? Have you investigated the impact of parsing errors on the performance of the APT models?
2.  Can you provide a qualitative analysis of the types of non-compositional phrases that the models struggle with? What specific linguistic features characterize these challenging cases?
3.  What is the computational cost associated with the different APT configurations (e.g., order of dependency paths)? How does this compare to the cost of using simpler compositional methods like adding word embeddings?
4.  Have you considered evaluating the models on datasets beyond the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, such as datasets of verb-particle constructions or idiomatic expressions?
5.  How do the results compare to those obtained using more recent, transformer-based compositional models?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly presented.
*   The suppression of the reference in Section 3 is unusual and should be addressed.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key parameters used for each model configuration for easier comparison.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The systematic exploration of typed dependency features and the development of the hybrid APT model are particularly noteworthy. While the limited scope of evaluation and lack of qualitative error analysis are weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and concerns raised above.
