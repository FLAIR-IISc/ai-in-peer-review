This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on compositional distributional semantics, specifically in the context of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds. The authors compare models leveraging these features with those applying naive compositional operations to pre-trained word embeddings, using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.

The primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical evaluation of different compositional approaches using dependency-based contextual features, demonstrating that incorporating syntactic structure through typed dependency paths, and specifically using a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned features, improves compositionality detection performance compared to previous state-of-the-art methods. A secondary contribution lies in the systematic experimentation with various compositional operations (addition, intersection) and weighting schemes within the APT framework. Finally, the paper provides a detailed comparison between dependency-based compositional methods and those based on neural word embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive and well-executed empirical evaluation, systematically exploring a range of model configurations and parameter settings. The use of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, a standard benchmark for compositionality detection, allows for direct comparison with existing methods. The careful ablation studies examining the impact of different features (typed vs. untyped, higher-order paths) and composition operations are commendable.
2. **Novelty in Feature Engineering:** The exploration of typed higher-order dependency paths as contextual features is a novel and potentially valuable contribution. This approach effectively integrates syntactic information into the distributional representations, addressing a limitation of many existing methods that rely on simpler co-occurrence statistics. The idea of combining aligned and unaligned features is particularly insightful.
3. **Clear Presentation and Detailed Methodology:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation, methodology, and results. The detailed description of the APT framework, including the equations for feature alignment and composition, enables reproducibility. The explanation of the preprocessing steps and parameter tuning procedures adds to the rigor of the study.
4. **Outperformance of Baselines:** The reported results consistently outperform the baseline methods, including the previously published state-of-the-art, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The hybrid model's performance suggests a complementary benefit from incorporating both aligned and unaligned dependency information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation Dataset:** While the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is a valuable resource, its limited size (90 compound nouns) may restrict the generalizability of the findings. Evaluating the proposed methods on larger and more diverse datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Corpus Dependency:** The performance gains observed are largely tied to the specific characteristics of the ukWaC corpus. While the authors acknowledge this, it would be beneficial to investigate the robustness of the approach across different corpora with varying linguistic properties. The detailed discussion of corpus pre-processing (handling of compound noun parsing inconsistencies) suggests that the results might be sensitive to parsing quality.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the misclassified compound nouns. Identifying the types of compounds that the model struggles with could provide valuable insights for future improvements. For example, are there specific syntactic structures or semantic relationships that pose challenges?
4. **Complexity and Scalability:** The APT framework appears computationally complex, particularly for constructing higher-order dependency features. The paper does not address the scalability of this approach to larger-scale applications or more complex linguistic structures. The computational cost of feature engineering and composition could be a practical limitation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the choice of dependency parser and the quality of parsing accuracy? Have you considered using different parsers or incorporating parsing confidence scores into the model?
2. Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying the types of compound nouns that are frequently misclassified by the proposed models?
3. What is the computational cost of constructing the APT features and performing the composition operations, particularly for higher-order dependency paths? Is the approach scalable to larger datasets and more complex linguistic structures?
4. How do the performance gains translate to downstream NLP tasks that rely on compositional semantics, such as phrase similarity or sentence understanding?
