This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on compositional distributional semantics, specifically focusing on non-compositional phrases and the detection of compositionality in compound nouns. The core contribution is a detailed exploration of how incorporating syntactic information – particularly dependency paths – into distributional models affects their ability to represent and compose phrases, using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. A secondary, though valuable, contribution lies in the thorough experimental comparison of different compositional methods (addition, intersection) and weighting schemes within the proposed framework, alongside a solid baseline using word2vec embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The paper’s central idea – explicitly linking compositional ability to the *definition of context* within distributional models – is genuinely interesting and represents a worthwhile departure from simply applying compositional operations to pre-trained embeddings. The focus on typed dependency features and higher-order paths offers a nuanced perspective on how syntactic structure can be leveraged for better composition. This isn’t just another application of a standard technique; it’s a fundamental question about *how* distributional semantics can better capture meaning.
2. **Detailed Exploration & Thoroughness:** The authors don't just propose a single method; they systematically explore a range of parameters – PPMI vs. normalized counts, different composition operations (INT, UNI), weighting schemes, and the integration of aligned/unaligned dependency features. This thoroughness demonstrates a deep engagement with the problem and provides valuable insights into the relative importance of different design choices.
3. **Clear Connection to Linguistic Theory:** The paper consistently grounds its methodology in linguistic principles, referencing concepts like compositionality, dependency parsing, and the importance of syntactic structure. This grounding elevates the work beyond purely empirical experimentation. The discussion of aligning vector spaces based on dependency paths is particularly well-motivated.
4. **Competitive Results & Baselines:**  Achieving performance improvements over the established Reddy et al. (2011) baseline, and demonstrating competitive results with word2vec, is a positive indicator. While the gains might not be massive, the fact that the proposed method performs well alongside standard techniques validates the approach.
5. **Strong Focus on Non-Compositionality:** The explicit acknowledgement and consideration of non-compositional phrases is commendable. Many distributional semantics approaches implicitly assume compositionality, so directly addressing this issue is a significant strength.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scalability & Corpus Dependency:** The method's reliance on dependency parsing introduces potential issues with scalability and robustness. Dependency parsing is not perfect, and errors in parsing can propagate through the system, affecting the quality of the contextual features. The results also appear heavily tied to the specific characteristics of the ukWaC corpus. More evaluation on diverse datasets would strengthen the findings.
2. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** While the quantitative results are presented clearly, the paper lacks sufficient qualitative analysis. Providing examples of phrases where the proposed method excels or fails, and explaining *why*, would significantly enhance understanding and provide valuable insights. What specific dependency paths are proving most informative? Why does alignment (or lack thereof) matter in certain cases?
3. **Redundancy in Experimental Setup:** The sheer number of experiments, while demonstrating thoroughness, also leads to some redundancy. Several parameter combinations yield similar results, making it difficult to draw clear conclusions about the optimal configuration. A more targeted experimental design could have streamlined the analysis.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** There's minimal discussion of the types of errors the system makes. Understanding *what* the model gets wrong is crucial for identifying areas for improvement. Are there specific types of non-compositional phrases that consistently pose challenges?
5. **Suppressed Reference:** The reference to "elsewhere" regarding the APT proposal is problematic. A complete citation is essential for reproducibility and proper attribution.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the accuracy of the dependency parser used? Have you explored the impact of using different parsers or pre-processing steps to improve parsing accuracy?
2. Can you provide specific examples of compound nouns where your method significantly outperforms the word2vec baseline, and explain the linguistic reasons behind this performance difference?
3. The paper mentions potential issues with sparse data. Have you considered techniques like regularization or dimensionality reduction to mitigate this issue?
4. Could you elaborate on why the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) consistently outperforms both aligned and unaligned approaches alone? What does this suggest about the interplay between syntactic structure and distributional information?
5. Please provide the full citation for the "elsewhere" reference regarding the APT proposal.



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear and concise. The paper would benefit from more visual illustrations (e.g., diagrams showing dependency paths and feature alignment). The section on Related Work is quite extensive, but could be slightly more focused on the most relevant prior research.
