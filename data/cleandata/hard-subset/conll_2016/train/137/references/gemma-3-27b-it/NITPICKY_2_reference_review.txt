This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on compositional distributional semantics, specifically in relation to non-compositional phrases. The authors evaluate their approach on Reddy et al.’s (2011) dataset of compound nouns, aiming to improve compositionality detection. 

The primary contribution is a thorough empirical exploration of different compositional methods utilizing dependency-based distributional features. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating that incorporating higher-order dependency paths, even without explicit alignment, can yield competitive results. A tertiary, though less substantial, contribution is the reproduction and extension of Reddy et al.’s experimental setup, providing a more comprehensive analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Extensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a remarkably thorough experimental evaluation, systematically varying parameters of the APT model (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, alignment) and comparing it with a well-established neural embedding baseline. This level of detail allows for nuanced insights into the effectiveness of different approaches.
2. **Focus on Dependency Structure:** The emphasis on incorporating syntactic dependency information into distributional models is valuable. The use of higher-order dependency paths, representing relationships beyond direct neighbors, demonstrates an attempt to capture richer semantic context.
3. **Clear Presentation of APT Model:** The explanation of the Anchored Packed Trees (APT) approach is relatively clear and well-structured, particularly the description of feature alignment and reduction. Table 1 effectively illustrates the concept of typed features.
4. **Competitive Performance:** The results show that the proposed APT models achieve performance comparable to, and in some cases exceeding, the neural word embedding baseline, and significantly improve on previously reported results, showcasing a genuine contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Method:** While the *combination* of techniques is well-explored, the underlying compositional methods (addition, multiplication) and the use of dependency-based features are not novel. The paper primarily represents a meticulous investigation and optimization of existing techniques rather than a groundbreaking methodological innovation.
2. **Overly Granular Parameter Tuning:** The sheer number of parameters explored (various PPMI settings, composition functions, alignment strategies) risks overfitting to the specific dataset and obscuring generalizable findings. The paper would benefit from a more focused exploration of the most promising configurations. The justification for many of these parameter choices remains weak.
3. **Insufficient Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of *where* the models struggle. Identifying specific types of non-compositional phrases that consistently pose challenges would provide valuable insights and guide future research. Simply reporting correlation scores is insufficient.
4. **Formatting and Presentation Issues:** Several minor formatting inconsistencies detract from the paper's polish. For example, equation numbering is inconsistent, and some references appear incomplete (e.g., "reference suppressed"). Additionally, Tables 3 and 5 are poorly labelled, making it difficult to quickly grasp their purpose. The use of boldface is also inconsistent.
5. **Unclear Motivation for Certain Choices:** The rationale for including the "hybrid" model (combining aligned and unaligned features) feels somewhat ad hoc. A stronger theoretical justification for this approach would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting specific examples of compound nouns where the models perform poorly and discussing potential reasons for these failures?
2.  What is the computational cost associated with constructing and composing APTs compared to training and using word2vec embeddings? This information is crucial for assessing the practical applicability of the proposed approach.
3.  The paper mentions "distributional smoothing" but refers to a suppressed reference. Please provide details of the smoothing technique used.
4.  Why was shifted PMI found to be detrimental to performance? Further investigation into this observation could be insightful.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "state-of-the-art" is used liberally throughout the paper. While the results are competitive, a more cautious and nuanced use of this term is recommended.
*   Please ensure consistent formatting of references and equation numbering. A thorough proofread is essential.
*   Consider including a table summarizing the key parameters and their corresponding performance results for easier comparison.



Despite the thoroughness of the experimentation and the competitive results, the lack of significant methodological novelty and the numerous minor flaws lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the work demonstrates a strong understanding of the field and a commendable attention to detail, it does not present a sufficiently impactful contribution to warrant acceptance at a highly selective conference like CoNLL. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly improving the clarity of motivation, providing a detailed error analysis, and meticulously polishing the presentation, would substantially strengthen the paper.
