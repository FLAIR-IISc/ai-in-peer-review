This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition – specifically typed versus untyped dependency relations and higher-order paths – on compositional distributional semantics, focusing on the detection of non-compositional phrases. The authors explore these features within both a model where composition is integral to the distributional model (APT) and a more conventional approach applying compositional operations to pre-trained representations. The core contribution lies in a comprehensive empirical evaluation on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, demonstrating performance gains using typed dependency features and a hybrid alignment strategy within the APT framework. A secondary contribution is the detailed experimentation with parameters for both APT models and neural word embeddings, providing valuable insights into their relative strengths. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The extensive experimentation on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is a significant strength. The authors systematically explore various configurations of APT models (PPMI weighting, alignment strategies, composition operations) and neural word embeddings, offering a thorough comparative analysis. This allows for concrete conclusions regarding the effectiveness of different approaches.
2. **Focus on a Challenging Problem:** Addressing non-compositionality in distributional semantics is a crucial and often overlooked issue.  The paper correctly identifies the limitations of directly applying compositional methods to phrases where meaning isn't readily derived from constituent parts and provides a method of addressing this. The use of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, specifically designed to evaluate compositionality detection, is appropriate.
3. **Clear Presentation of the APT Model:** The description of the Anchored Packed Trees (APT) approach is relatively clear and well-motivated. The rationale behind encoding full dependency paths and the alignment process are explained adequately, making the method understandable to readers familiar with distributional semantics.
4. **Improved Performance:**  The reported performance gains, particularly with the hybrid APT model combining aligned and unaligned features, represent a genuine advancement over the baseline results from Reddy et al. (2011) and outperform neural embedding-based approaches. This demonstrates the value of incorporating syntactic information directly into the distributional model. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the experimental setup and results are valuable, the core ideas presented (using dependency information, exploring different compositional functions) aren't entirely novel. Previous work (referenced in the paper) has already explored similar avenues. The primary innovation lies in the *combination* of these techniques within the APT framework and the detailed analysis on the compositionality detection task, but this feels somewhat iterative rather than groundbreaking.
2. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is restricted to the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, which focuses solely on compound nouns. The generalizability of the findings to other types of phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun phrases) remains unclear. This limitation should be explicitly acknowledged and discussed. Further evaluation on other compositional datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity & Lack of Statistical Significance Reporting:** The paper details numerous parameter settings explored but lacks a comprehensive report of statistical significance for the performance differences. While the authors mention errors being small, providing p-values or confidence intervals would significantly enhance the robustness of the results. The sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., the weighting parameter *h* in the composition operations) also raises concerns about the stability of the findings.
4. **Suppressed Reference:** The reference to a previous work detailing the APT model is "suppressed." This is unacceptable for a rigorous scientific publication.  The methodology is central to the paper, and complete access to the previous description is necessary for proper evaluation and reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide the full reference for the “suppressed” work describing the APT model.  Full access to this information is essential for assessing the novelty and reproducibility of your approach.
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of dependency parser? Different parsers may produce different dependency trees, which could impact the features extracted and the performance of the APT model.
3.  Can you elaborate on the rationale for the observed optimal range of *h* (0.3-0.5) in the compositional operations?  What linguistic or semantic factors might explain this preference for a balanced weighting of the constituents?
4.  What steps were taken to ensure the fairness of comparison between the APT models and the neural word embedding models, particularly regarding corpus size and training data? Are the embeddings pre-trained on the same corpus used to build the APT lexicon, or on a different one?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more concise introduction. The lengthy background on distributional semantics feels somewhat excessive.
*   Consider including an ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component of the APT model (e.g., typed features, higher-order paths, alignment). This would provide a clearer understanding of the individual impact of each feature.
*   The discussion of related work could be more focused and critical.  It often feels like a list of papers rather than a synthesis of relevant research.
