## Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependency paths) on the ability of distributional semantic models to handle non-compositional phrases. The authors evaluate their approach using Reddy et al.’s (2011) dataset for compositionality detection, comparing models based on anchored packed trees (APTs) with standard compositional operations applied to pre-trained word embeddings. They find that untyped dependency features with a hybrid alignment strategy, combined with pointwise addition, achieve the best performance, surpassing previous state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Investigation of Contextual Features for Compositionality:** The systematic exploration of different contextual features (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependencies) within distributional models specifically targeting non-compositionality is a valuable contribution. This provides concrete insights into which features are more effective for modeling phrase meaning beyond simple word combination.
2. **Improved Performance on Compositionality Detection:** The paper demonstrates a clear improvement over existing state-of-the-art results on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset using a specific configuration of APTs and composition operations. This validates the proposed approach and provides a strong benchmark for future work.
3. **Highlighting the Value of Untyped Dependency Features:** The finding that untyped dependency features outperform fully typed ones (and even hybrid approaches) for this task is somewhat counterintuitive and offers a nuanced understanding of how syntactic information interacts with distributional semantics.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors carefully replicate the experimental setup of Reddy et al. (2011), allowing for a fair comparison and ensuring reproducibility.  The use of 3-fold cross-validation and detailed parameter tuning demonstrate a commitment to robust evaluation.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration of Model Configurations:** The paper thoroughly explores a wide range of model configurations, including variations in PPMI smoothing, composition operations (INT vs. UNI), alignment strategies, and feature orders. This detailed analysis provides a nuanced understanding of the factors influencing performance.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and the related work provides a solid background. The descriptions of the APT model and experimental setup are clear and concise.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Results:** The authors don’t just present numbers; they offer insightful interpretations of the results, explaining why certain configurations perform better than others. The discussion on the surprising effectiveness of untyped features is particularly valuable.
5. **Addresses a Core Issue in Compositional Semantics:** The paper directly tackles the problem of non-compositionality, a known limitation of many compositional semantic models.  By focusing on this issue, the authors contribute to a more realistic and nuanced understanding of how meaning is combined at the phrase level.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) focused on compound nouns. While the results are significant within this context, it's unclear how well these findings generalize to other types of phrases (e.g., verb-particle constructions, adjective-noun phrases) or other languages.
2. **Overly Detailed Model Description:** While thoroughness is appreciated, the description of the APT model (Section 3) is dense and potentially difficult to follow for readers unfamiliar with dependency parsing and distributional semantics.  A more intuitive explanation or visual illustration could improve accessibility.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth error analysis. Identifying specific types of non-compositional phrases where the model struggles could reveal limitations and guide future research.  What kinds of "red herrings" are still fooling the system?
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The authors do not discuss the computational cost of constructing and using the APT representations. Given the complexity of dependency parsing and the high dimensionality of the feature space, this is a relevant consideration.
5. **Incremental Improvement:** While statistically significant, the performance improvement over Reddy et al. (2011) is not dramatic. The authors should more explicitly address whether this improvement is practically meaningful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How do the performance gains translate to different phrase types beyond compound nouns?  Are you planning to evaluate the approach on other datasets that focus on different forms of non-compositionality?
2.  Could you provide a simplified example illustrating how the alignment process (offsetting) works in practice, perhaps with a smaller dependency tree?
3.  What is the computational cost (in terms of time and memory) of creating and using the APT representations compared to the neural word embeddings?
4.  Have you investigated any techniques for identifying and explicitly handling highly idiomatic phrases within the APT framework, rather than simply attempting to model them compositionally?
5. Could you elaborate on why you believe untyped features outperformed the fully typed ones, given the theoretical advantages of incorporating dependency relation information?




**Additional Comments:**

* The reference suppression in Section 3 is a minor annoyance.  Please provide the full citation.
* The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of related work on idiom detection and handling.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The rigorous experimental setup, comprehensive analysis, and insightful results justify acceptance. While the limited generalizability and density of the model description are minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and suggestions outlined above.
