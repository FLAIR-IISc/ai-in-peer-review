This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths within distributional semantic models on compositional phrase representation, specifically focusing on noun compounds and their compositionality. The authors propose an “Anchored Packed Trees” (APT) approach and evaluate it on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.

The primary contribution is a thorough empirical exploration of different configurations of distributional semantic models leveraging dependency parse information for compositional semantics. The systematic investigation of typed vs. untyped features and higher-order dependencies, alongside established baselines, provides a valuable comparative analysis. A secondary, but useful, contribution is the demonstration that even relatively simple modifications to existing compositional methods—incorporating dependency information—can yield improvements over prior work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The paper adheres to established experimental protocols (using the Reddy et al. dataset and comparing against their baseline) which allows for a fair and interpretable evaluation. This reliance on existing benchmarks is commendable, enhancing the trustworthiness of the results.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration:** The authors explore a wide range of parameters within their APT model (PPMI variations, composition operations, alignment strategies), demonstrating a thoroughness that strengthens the validity of their conclusions.
3. **Clear Presentation of Dependency Information:** The use of anchored packed trees and the detailed explanation of dependency paths (Table 1) provide a clear and understandable representation of how syntactic information is integrated into the model.
4. **Outperformance of Previous Work:** The APT models consistently outperform the Reddy et al. (2011) baseline, suggesting a genuine improvement in compositionality detection. This provides concrete evidence for the benefit of incorporating syntactic information.
5. **Insightful Analysis of Composition Operations:** The finding that unaligned models often perform well, and the benefit of a hybrid approach, provide valuable insights into the interplay between alignment and inherent compositional structure.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Approach:** While the exploration is thorough, the core idea of incorporating dependency information into distributional semantics isn't entirely novel. Previous work (cited within the paper) has already investigated similar concepts. The paper would be stronger if it clearly articulated a unique theoretical contribution beyond systematically testing different configurations of established techniques.
2. **Overly Complex Methodology:** The APT approach, while clearly explained, feels unnecessarily complex. The benefits of higher-order dependencies and the alignment process aren't overwhelmingly demonstrated – simpler configurations achieve competitive results.  A more focused approach on the most impactful components would strengthen the paper.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper presents performance metrics but lacks a detailed analysis of *where* the models succeed and fail. Investigating specific examples of misclassified phrases could reveal crucial insights into the limitations of the approach and guide future research.
4. **Modest Performance Gains:** While statistically significant, the improvements over the baseline are relatively modest. The correlation scores, while better, remain below what might be considered a strong demonstration of compositional understanding. This raises questions about the practical applicability of the method.
5. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is confined to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) focusing on noun-noun compounds.  The extent to which the findings generalize to other types of phrases or compositional phenomena is unclear.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, including specific examples of phrases where the model fails and a discussion of the potential reasons for these failures?
2.  What is the computational cost of the APT approach compared to simpler compositional methods like adding pre-trained word embeddings? Is the increase in performance worth the added complexity?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the quality of the dependency parse? Could errors in the parse significantly degrade performance?
4.  Do you have any plans to evaluate the model on other datasets or with different types of phrases (e.g., verb-object combinations, adjective-noun phrases)?
5.  Given that the performance gains are modest, can you articulate a clear practical application where this improved compositionality detection would be valuable?

**Additional Comments:**

The discussion of related work is comprehensive. However, it would be helpful to briefly compare the computational complexity of the approaches discussed.  The suppression of the reference to the APT method in section 3 is unusual and should be addressed.
