This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on compositional distributional semantics, specifically for detecting non-compositional phrases. The authors adapt an existing dataset of compound nouns with compositionality judgments to evaluate different vector space models.

The primary contribution is the exploration of higher-order dependency paths *within* distributional models, rather than solely as a pre-processing step for composition. This is a relatively bold approach, attempting to build compositional understanding directly into the vector space itself. A secondary contribution lies in the thorough empirical evaluation across different compositional operations (addition, intersection) and weighting schemes, providing a reasonably comprehensive comparison. The re-purposing of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is also valuable, offering a focused benchmark for compositionality detection.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of encoding dependency path information *directly* into the distributional features, and then leveraging that structure during composition, is genuinely interesting. Most approaches treat composition as a separate step applied to pre-existing word embeddings.  This paper proposes a more integrated perspective, which is commendable.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The authors meticulously explore several variations of the APT model – different weighting schemes, PPMI variations, alignment strategies – demonstrating a willingness to thoroughly investigate the design space. This level of detail is rare and appreciated.
3. **Positive Results:** The APT models consistently outperform the baseline neural word embedding models, particularly the skip-gram model. This provides concrete evidence supporting the benefits of incorporating typed dependency information into distributional representations. Achieving improvements over a well-established baseline like word2vec is a significant result.
4. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of standard compositional methods when dealing with non-compositional phrases and frames the work as an attempt to address this crucial issue. The introduction provides a strong rationale for the study.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope & Generalizability:** The evaluation is restricted to compound nouns, a relatively narrow domain. It’s unclear whether the observed benefits would extend to other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun phrases, verb-object phrases).  The success within this specific domain doesn't guarantee broader applicability.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study Detail:** While different components are explored, the paper lacks a detailed ablation study pinpointing the precise contribution of each element (e.g., higher-order paths *vs.* typed features). More granular analysis would strengthen the claims. For instance, how much does performance *degrade* when using only first-order features versus second-order?
3. **Preprocessing Sensitivity:** The complex preprocessing step of incorporating compound nouns into the corpus, and dealing with parsing errors, introduces potential artifacts. While acknowledged, the impact of this preprocessing on the results isn't fully explored.  It's possible the observed performance gains are partially due to this preprocessing.
4. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** While parameter optimisation is discussed, it’s not entirely clear how exhaustive this tuning was, especially for the neural word embedding models. A more systematic parameter search might yield different results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study quantifying the impact of each component of the APT model (e.g., higher-order paths, typed features, alignment)?
2.  How sensitive are the results to variations in the preprocessing step?  Specifically, what impact does the handling of parsing errors have on performance?
3.   Do you foresee challenges in applying this approach to languages with freer word order and less explicit syntactic marking?
4. Is there a discussion regarding the computational cost of incorporating higher-order dependency paths? What practical implications are there for scaling this model to larger datasets?

**Additional Comments:**

The references are well-organized and cover the relevant literature. The writing is generally clear and concise. While a more rigorous ablation study and broader evaluation would strengthen the paper, the novelty of the approach and the positive empirical results warrant acceptance, even with the acknowledged limitations. The exploration of integrating syntactic structure directly into distributional semantics is a promising direction for future research.
