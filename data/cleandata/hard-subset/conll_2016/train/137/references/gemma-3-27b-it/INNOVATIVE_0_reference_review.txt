This paper investigates the impact of contextual features—specifically, typed vs. untyped dependency relations, and higher-order paths—on compositional distributional semantics, aiming to improve the detection of non-compositional phrases. The core idea is that how context is defined influences a model's ability to accurately represent phrases, particularly those where meaning isn't simply the sum of its parts (like “red herring”). They evaluate this using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of compound nouns and their assessed compositionality.

The primary contribution is a detailed exploration of how incorporating richer dependency information (typed features, higher-order paths) into distributional models affects their ability to discern compositional vs. non-compositional phrases.  A secondary contribution is a strong empirical baseline, achieving state-of-the-art results on the Reddy dataset, suggesting the benefits of their approach, and demonstrating that thoughtfully designed, linguistically informed features can still outperform simpler neural methods. A tertiary, though less significant, contribution is the observation that combining aligned *and* unaligned dependency information yields the best performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Focus:** The paper tackles a critical but often overlooked issue in compositional semantics: the impact of contextual definition. Most work assumes context is implicitly “good enough,” but this paper directly challenges that, exploring *how* context affects compositionality detection. This is a bold and insightful question.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors significantly improve upon the previously reported state-of-the-art on the Reddy dataset. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of their approach and isn’t merely theoretical.
3. **Linguistic Motivation:** Grounding the approach in dependency parsing and linguistic theory is a major strength. The justification for using typed features and higher-order paths is well-articulated and logically sound.  The idea of alignment (or lack thereof) being important is intriguing.
4. **Comprehensive Exploration:** The systematic experimentation with different feature types, composition operations, and weighting schemes is commendable.  This thoroughness builds confidence in the robustness of the findings.
5. **Clear Writing & Structure:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly sets the stage for the research question, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) focusing solely on noun-noun compounds. While achieving SOTA on this dataset is valuable, it’s unclear how well these findings generalize to other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object) or different languages. This is a common limitation in this field, but it’s still a significant concern.
2. **Complexity & Scalability:**  The anchored packed tree approach, while theoretically sound, appears computationally complex. The paper doesn’t fully address the scalability of this method to larger corpora or more complex sentence structures. The overhead of dependency parsing and constructing these trees might be prohibitive for some applications.
3. **Insufficient Comparison to Stronger Neural Baselines:** While the authors compare to word2vec, more recent and sophisticated neural compositional models (e.g., those incorporating attention mechanisms or transformers) are not included in the comparison. A stronger baseline would help to further validate the advantages of the proposed approach. Specifically, the current neural baselines seem somewhat underexplored in parameter tuning.
4. **Over-Reliance on Existing Tooling:** The heavy reliance on pre-trained parsers (MaltParser, TreeTagger) introduces potential error propagation. Parsing errors could significantly impact the quality of the dependency features and, consequently, the performance of the model. A brief analysis of parser accuracy on the corpus would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of constructing and composing the anchored packed trees? How does this scale with sentence length and corpus size?
2.  Have you explored incorporating more recent and powerful neural compositional models (e.g., models based on transformers) into your comparison? If not, what were the reasons for omitting them?
3.  What is the parser accuracy on the ukWaC corpus? Did you consider any error analysis related to parsing quality and its impact on the results?
4.  The hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned features shows the best performance. Can you provide more intuition as to *why* this combination is so effective? What specific types of compounds benefit most from the inclusion of unaligned features?

**Additional Comments:**

The suppression of the reference in section 3 is a stylistic choice I find slightly frustrating. While understandable, it hinders complete reproducibility. It would be preferable to include it, or at least a brief description of the omitted work. The section on experimental set-up is rather verbose; streamlining this would improve readability.
