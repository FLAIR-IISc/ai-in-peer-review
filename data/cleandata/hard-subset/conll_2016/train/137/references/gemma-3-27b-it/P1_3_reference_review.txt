## Peer Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on compositional distributional semantics, specifically focusing on improving the detection of non-compositional phrases. The authors leverage a dataset of compound nouns with human compositionality judgments (Reddy et al., 2011) to compare different approaches – compositional models built *into* the distributional model (using Anchored Packed Trees – APTs) versus applying naive compositional operations to pre-trained distributional representations. They explore various parameters within the APT framework (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, alignment of vector spaces) and benchmark against neural word embeddings.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Investigation of Contextual Feature Typing in Composition:** The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation of how different types of contextual features (typed vs. untyped dependency relations) influence the performance of compositional models in detecting non-compositionality. This is a valuable contribution, clarifying the importance of syntactic information for this task.
2. **Demonstration of APT effectiveness:** The experiments show that APT models, particularly a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned features, achieve state-of-the-art performance on the compound noun compositionality detection task, outperforming neural word embeddings.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Compositional Operations & Parameters:** The systematic exploration of different compositional operations (addition, intersection) and parameter settings within the APT framework provides valuable insights into effective model configurations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough and well-controlled set of experiments using a relevant dataset and established baselines (neural word embeddings, Reddy et al., 2011). The use of cross-validation to estimate parameters is commendable.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of compositionality in distributional semantics and the challenge of evaluating compositional methods when dealing with non-compositional phrases. The introduction effectively establishes the context and significance of the research.
3. **Novel Hybrid Approach:** The proposed hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned APTs demonstrates insightful reasoning and delivers improved performance, suggesting a nuanced understanding of how syntactic information contributes to compositionality.
4. **Detailed APT Explanation:** The explanation of the APT method is detailed and clear, making it easier to understand the underlying mechanics and rationale for its design. The example in Table 1 is particularly helpful.
5. **Reproducibility:** The detailed descriptions of the experimental setup, parameters, and corpus preprocessing steps enhance the reproducibility of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to compound nouns. While this allows for a focused investigation, it raises questions about the generalizability of the findings to other types of phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases). This limits the broader impact of the work.
2. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Parameter Choices:** While the authors explore various parameters (α, h, q), the rationale behind choosing specific values or ranges isn't fully explained. A deeper connection to linguistic theory or cognitive principles could strengthen the argument.
3. **Potential for Data Bias:** The use of the ukWaC corpus, while common, might introduce biases related to its web-derived nature. Further investigation of corpus-specific effects would be valuable.
4. **Comparison to SOTA is Limited:** While the paper outperforms previous work on *this specific dataset*, it doesn't directly compare against the latest and most advanced neural compositional models (e.g., transformer-based approaches) that have emerged since the Reddy et al. (2011) baseline.
5. **Redundancy in Tables and Results Discussion:** Some results are presented in multiple tables with minimal added insight, making the paper slightly verbose. A more concise presentation of findings would improve readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalizability:** How do you expect the findings to generalize to other types of phrases beyond compound nouns? Are there specific phrase types where you anticipate the APT approach to be particularly effective or ineffective?
2. **Parameter Rationale:** Could you provide a more detailed explanation for the choice of parameter ranges and the optimal values identified in your experiments? Are there linguistic or cognitive principles that motivated these choices?
3. **Modern Baselines:** Have you considered comparing the APT approach to more recent, state-of-the-art neural compositional models (e.g., those based on transformers)? If not, could you comment on the potential challenges and expected performance differences?
4. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a qualitative error analysis of the model's predictions? What types of non-compositional phrases does the model struggle with, and what insights can be gleaned from these errors?
5. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of training and deploying the APT model compared to simpler approaches like neural word embeddings? This information is important for assessing the practical applicability of the method.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference suppression in section 3 should be resolved.
*   Consider including a more visually appealing illustration of the APT concept.
*   Minor editing for clarity and conciseness would improve readability.



Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of distributional semantics. The strong empirical evaluation and novel hybrid approach are particularly commendable. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
