- Systematically investigates typed vs. untyped dependency features and higher-order paths for detecting non-compositional phrases.
- Introduces a hybrid APT model that combines aligned and unaligned dependency features and improves performance.
- Reproduces Reddy et al. (2011) experiments to provide a strong, reproducible baseline.
- Rigorous experimental setup with comprehensive parameter search for APTs and embeddings.
- Clear motivation and problem definition highlighting limits of traditional compositional methods.
- Exceptionally clear and detailed explanation of the Anchored Packed Tree (APT) model and feature alignment.
- Demonstrated consistent performance gains, particularly from the hybrid APT, with notable correlation scores.
- Insightful analysis showing optimal compositional weighting parameters fall in the 0.3–0.5 range.
- Evaluation limited to a small dataset (90 compounds), raising generalizability concerns.
- Lacks qualitative error analysis to identify which non-compositional phrases remain challenging.
- Results depend on dependency parser quality; robustness to parsing errors or different parsers is not analyzed.
- No discussion of computational cost and trade-offs for higher-order APT configurations versus simpler methods.
- Missing comparisons to recent transformer-based or advanced neural compositional models.
- Questions: sensitivity to parsing quality and parsing errors?
- Questions: request for qualitative error analysis and characterization of difficult cases.
- Questions: what are computational costs of different APT orders vs. simple compositional methods?
- Questions: have models been evaluated on datasets beyond Reddy et al. (2011), e.g., verb-particle or idioms?
- Questions: how do results compare to transformer-based compositional models?
- Paper is well-written; suppressed reference in Section 3 should be fixed and add a parameter summary table.
- Overall assessment: strong contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
