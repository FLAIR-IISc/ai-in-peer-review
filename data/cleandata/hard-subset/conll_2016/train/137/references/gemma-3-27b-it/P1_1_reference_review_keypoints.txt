- Paper examines typed vs. untyped contextual features and higher-order dependency paths for compositionality detection in noun compounds using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs).
- APTs, especially a hybrid of aligned and unaligned dependency features, outperform naive composition on embeddings and prior state-of-the-art on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.
- Systematic comparison of typed vs. untyped contextual features clarifies how linguistic structure affects compositional semantics.
- Demonstrates efficacy of APTs for predicting human compositionality judgments, surpassing neural embedding baselines.
- Analysis shows alignment of dependency features matters, with a hybrid aligned/unaligned approach giving the best results.
- Strong empirical results: higher correlation with human judgments across APT configurations.
- Well-motivated study using an appropriate dataset and clear problem framing around non-compositionality.
- Detailed, reproducible experimental setup with insightful ablation studies (weighting schemes, alignment vs. no alignment).
- Theoretical grounding in dependency parsing and clear explanation of feature alignment and reduction.
- Clear writing and organization, making arguments and findings easy to follow.
- Evaluation limited to one dataset and noun-noun compounds, raising questions about generalizability to other phrase types.
- Lacks qualitative error analysis to identify which compounds fail and why.
- APT implementation is complex and depends on dependency-parsed corpora; computational cost and scalability are unclear.
- Comparisons omit more recent neural compositional models (e.g., attention/transformer-based baselines).
- Request for intuition on why the hybrid aligned/unaligned model works and what each captures.
- Ask why cbow outperformed skip-gram for this task and what cbow properties helped.
- Query sensitivity to choice of dependency parser and whether different parsers were tested.
- Request for estimates of computational cost/time to build the lexicon and perform composition for given corpus sizes.
- Suggest expanding related work coverage on recent neural compositional models and adding a table summarizing key parameters and results.
- Recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the raised points.
