## Peer Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on the ability of distributional semantic models to capture compositionality in noun compounds. The authors experiment with anchored packed trees (APTs) and compare them to standard compositional operations applied to state-of-the-art word embeddings, evaluating performance on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for compositionality detection. They find that APTs, particularly those combining aligned and unaligned features, outperform neural word embeddings and that incorporating dependency information is beneficial.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation of Typed vs. Untyped Contextual Features for Compositionality:** The paper provides a thorough empirical comparison of how different levels of contextual typing impact performance on a dedicated compositionality detection task. This is a valuable contribution to understanding the role of syntactic information in compositional semantics.
2. **Demonstration of APTs for Compositionality Detection:** The work demonstrates the effectiveness of the APT approach, particularly the hybrid aligned/unaligned model, for predicting compositionality, exceeding the performance of standard neural word embedding-based methods.
3. **Replication and Extension of Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark:** By re-examining and extending the existing benchmark, the authors provide a clear baseline and contribute to a more nuanced understanding of compositional semantics evaluation.



**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors carefully replicate and extend the experimental setup of Reddy et al. (2011), using the same corpus and dataset, which facilitates fair comparison and builds upon existing work.  The exploration of different PPMI smoothing parameters and compositional operations (add, multiply) within both APTs and neural embeddings demonstrates a systematic approach.
2. **Clear and Detailed Methodology:** The description of the APT model, including the concept of anchored packed trees, feature offsetting, and reduction, is clear and well-explained. Table 1 is especially helpful in illustrating the typed features.
3. **Strong Performance Results:** The APT models consistently outperform the neural word embedding baselines, particularly the hybrid approach, indicating the value of incorporating dependency information into compositional models.  The achieved correlation scores represent a significant improvement over the previous state-of-the-art reported in Reddy et al. (2011).
4. **Insightful Analysis of Model Parameters:**  The observation that the modifier is slightly more informative than the head in determining compositionality (optimal *h* values between 0.3-0.5) provides valuable insight into the nuances of noun compound composition.
5. **Focus on a Challenging Problem:** The paper addresses the critical issue of non-compositionality, which is often overlooked in compositional semantic models, and proposes a method that explicitly attempts to tackle this challenge.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is restricted to noun-noun compounds and a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011). While a thorough analysis is provided *within* this context, the generalizability of the findings to other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun phrases, verb phrases) and different corpora remains unclear.
2. **Complexity of APT Model:** The APT model, while theoretically sound, is complex and potentially computationally expensive. The paper doesn’t discuss the computational cost of building and composing APTs, which could be a barrier to wider adoption. A clearer discussion of scalability would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the results demonstrate improvements in correlation with human judgements, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *which* compounds the models struggle with and *why* would provide deeper insights into the limitations of the approaches and guide future research.  For example, are there specific types of non-compositional phrases that consistently pose a challenge?
4. **Motivation for Suppression of Reference:** The reference to the prior work on APTs is suppressed. This is unusual and weakens the reproducibility and assessability of the work.  Why was this reference suppressed, and what is the impact of this on understanding the complete methodology?
5. **Somewhat Redundant Discussion of Related Work:**  The Related Work section is lengthy and somewhat repetitive. While providing context is important, a more focused and concise discussion would improve readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Generalizability:** How do you anticipate the performance of the APT model and the findings regarding typed features to generalize to other types of phrases (e.g., adjective-noun, verb phrases) and different corpora? Do you foresee any specific challenges in adapting the method to other linguistic structures?
2.  **Computational Cost:** Can you provide an estimate of the computational cost (time and memory) of building and composing APTs compared to standard word embedding-based approaches?  What strategies could be employed to improve the scalability of the APT model for larger corpora?
3. **Suppressed Reference:** Why was the reference to the prior work on APTs suppressed? Is it unpublished work? If so, can you provide more details on its availability or a summary of its key contributions?
4. **Error Analysis:** Can you share some examples of compounds where the model performed poorly and provide a possible explanation for the failures? What types of compounds consistently challenge the model?
5.  **Impact of Feature Order:** You mention features up to order 2. Have you investigated the impact of using higher-order dependency paths?  At what point does adding higher-order features provide diminishing returns?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the APT model.
* The formatting is clear and consistent.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the key parameters used in each experiment for easier comparison.




Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The rigorous experimental evaluation, insightful analysis, and promising results make it a worthwhile addition to the literature. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding generalizability, computational cost, and error analysis, would further strengthen the work.
