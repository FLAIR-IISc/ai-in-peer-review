This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on compositional distributional semantics, specifically for non-compositional phrases. The authors explore this using a dataset of compound nouns and compare models integrating composition into the distributional model with those applying compositional operations to pre-trained representations.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a relatively thorough empirical investigation into the impact of dependency path order and typing on compositional semantics, demonstrating modest performance gains with more sophisticated contextual features. A secondary contribution is the confirmation that untyped dependency features, when combined effectively, can achieve competitive results, reinforcing the practical applicability of simpler methods. Finally, the adaptation of the Reddy et al. (2011) evaluation framework for comparing these approaches is a useful addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct a fairly extensive set of experiments, varying parameters within both APT models (PPMI smoothing, composition operations) and neural word embeddings (model type, dimensions, subsampling). This allows for a nuanced understanding of the factors influencing performance.
2. **Detailed Feature Engineering:** The use of anchored packed trees (APTs) with typed dependency paths is a well-defined and theoretically motivated approach to incorporating syntactic structure into distributional semantics. The explanation of the feature construction process in Table 1 is particularly clear.
3. **Clear Comparison to Baseline:** The comparison to the Reddy et al. (2011) baseline and the inclusion of additional baselines (RAND, FREQ) provides valuable context for evaluating the proposed methods.  The outperformance of these baselines, even if modest, is a positive sign.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Composition Operations:** The finding that different composition operations (INT vs. UNI) perform better under different conditions (aligned vs. unaligned features) is a useful observation, suggesting a complex interplay between syntactic structure and compositional mechanisms.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Modest Performance Gains:** While the results are statistically significant, the magnitude of the performance improvements over the baselines (especially the neural word embedding approach) is relatively small. The reported Spearman’s rho values hover around 0.78-0.79, which, while respectable, doesn’t represent a breakthrough. This raises questions about the practical impact of the proposed methods.
2. **Lack of Novelty in Core Methodology:** The core idea of using dependency information in distributional semantics is not new.  The APT framework itself, while well-defined, builds upon existing work and doesn't introduce a fundamentally novel algorithmic or theoretical advancement. The paper’s contribution primarily lies in the detailed experimentation and analysis of this existing approach.
3. **Overly Detailed Experimental Section:** The experimental setup section is excessively lengthy and granular. While thoroughness is appreciated, the level of detail regarding parameter tuning for each model (e.g., specific subsampling thresholds) detracts from the overall readability and makes it difficult to grasp the key findings. Many of these parameter explorations feel like implementation details rather than central research contributions.
4. **Inconsistent Reporting & Suppression of Results:** The statement "For compactness, we do not include the results with shifted PMI" is problematic. If results are not presented, there should be a strong justification, and the authors should acknowledge the potential impact on the conclusions. Suppressing negative results without explanation weakens the credibility of the research. Additionally, there's a 'reference suppressed' statement without explanation - this is unacceptable in a scientific paper.
5. **Ambiguous Interpretation of Hybrid Model Success:** The claim that the hybrid model performs best isn’t fully explained. Why does combining aligned and unaligned features yield the best results? What specific benefits does each component contribute? This requires a more in-depth discussion.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you elaborate on why the performance gains achieved by the APT models are relatively modest compared to the neural word embedding baseline? What are the limitations of the APT approach that prevent it from achieving higher accuracy?
2.  Please provide the results of the experiments with shifted PMI.  Explain why these results were suppressed.
3.  Can you clarify the theoretical rationale behind the success of the hybrid model? What specific linguistic or computational properties explain why combining aligned and unaligned features yields the best results?
4.  Please clarify the “reference suppressed” statement. What paper is being referred to?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more concise and focused presentation of the experimental results. Consider using summary tables and visualizations to highlight the key findings.
*   The introduction could be more focused on the specific challenges of non-compositional phrases and the motivations for investigating compositional methods.
*   Proofread carefully for minor grammatical errors and inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of capitalization in table captions).



Overall, this paper presents a solid empirical investigation of compositional semantics using dependency-based features. However, the modest performance gains, lack of significant methodological novelty, and overly detailed experimental section raise concerns. While the work is technically sound, it doesn’t demonstrate a substantial advancement in the field. I lean towards **rejection**, but would consider a minor revision if the authors address the weaknesses outlined above and provide a more compelling justification for the significance of their findings.
