This paper investigates the impact of contextual features (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependency paths) on compositional distributional semantics, specifically focusing on noun-noun compounds and their compositionality. The authors compare models integrating composition into the distributional model with naive compositional approaches applied to pre-trained embeddings. The primary claimed contribution is a demonstration of the value of typed dependency features in predicting compositionality, with a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned features performing best. A secondary contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation across various configurations of APT models and neural embeddings on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The paper presents a very detailed and extensive experimental setup, systematically exploring numerous parameter settings for both the APT models and neural embeddings. This level of thoroughness is commendable and provides a strong basis for the reported findings. The detailed breakdown of parameter exploration, while lengthy, offers valuable insights into the sensitivity of the models.
2. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The introduction clearly articulates the challenges of compositional semantics, specifically addressing the issue of non-compositional phrases and how they confound traditional evaluation metrics. The justification for using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is well-presented, and the connection to existing work is established effectively.
3. **Novelty in Hybrid Approach:** The proposed hybrid approach, combining aligned and unaligned dependency features, demonstrates a degree of innovation. The rationale for this approach – capturing different aspects of compositional meaning – is sound, and the performance improvement suggests its potential effectiveness.
4. **Detailed Explanation of APT Model:** The description of the Anchored Packed Trees (APT) model is exceptionally detailed, explaining the features, alignment process, and composition operations with sufficient clarity. This allows for a thorough understanding of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvement, Not Transformative:** While the experimental results are thorough, the performance gains achieved, particularly compared to the baseline neural embedding models, are relatively modest. The improvement, while statistically significant, doesn't appear to represent a paradigm shift in compositional semantics. The conclusion that syntactic structure *crucially* impacts compositionality seems overstated given the narrow performance difference.
2. **Overly Detailed, Redundant Presentation:** The paper suffers from excessive detail. Many experimental configurations are presented, but the discussion of their significance is often cursory. The inclusion of every single parameter sweep clutters the presentation and obscures the key findings. The constant referencing of table numbers instead of integrating the results into the prose makes the paper difficult to read.
3. **Justification for Parameter Choices:**  The rationale behind certain parameter choices (e.g., the unusually high subsampling threshold for cbow) isn’t fully explained. Simply stating a parameter "worked best" without a deeper analysis of *why* it performs better weakens the scientific rigor. The author’s suspicion regarding the sparse nature of the data is acknowledged but not thoroughly investigated.
4. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) focusing on compound nouns. The results may not generalize to other types of phrases or different domains. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not propose clear directions for future work to address it.
5. **Formatting and Style Issues:** Numerous minor formatting inconsistencies exist throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface, uneven spacing). The writing style, while clear, can be verbose and repetitive. The suppression of a reference ("reference suppressed") is unprofessional.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of why the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned features) outperforms the individual approaches? What specific linguistic phenomena or types of compounds benefit most from this combined representation?
2.  You mention that the unusually high subsampling threshold for cbow worked best. Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind this observation? Did you explore alternative techniques for handling sparse data in the context of neural embeddings?
3.  Given the limited performance gains over strong baseline models, how do you envision this approach being applied to more complex compositional tasks or real-world NLP applications?
4.  The reference that is suppressed, please provide.

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider streamlining the presentation by focusing on the most significant experimental results and summarizing the less impactful configurations.
*   Improve the overall readability by integrating the results more seamlessly into the text and reducing the reliance on table references.
*   Proofread the paper carefully to address the formatting and stylistic inconsistencies.



**Recommendation:** Reject. While the work is technically sound and demonstrates a rigorous experimental methodology, the incremental nature of the improvements, the excessive detail, and the limited generalizability prevent a positive recommendation. The paper feels more like a comprehensive exploration of parameter space than a groundbreaking contribution to the field. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly streamlining the presentation and providing a more compelling justification for the observed results, would significantly strengthen the paper.
