This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths within vector space models on compositional semantics, specifically focusing on compound noun compositionality. The authors compare models leveraging these features with those applying naive compositional operations to pre-trained word embeddings. The core contribution lies in exploring how different representations of context influence a model’s ability to accurately represent and compose phrases, measured against human judgments of compositionality using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. A secondary, though potentially valuable, contribution is the detailed experimental setup and comparison across various configurations of APT models and word2vec embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation framework, systematically exploring various parameter settings for both APT models and word2vec. The use of cross-validation and clear reporting of Spearman's rank correlation coefficients are commendable. This allows for a nuanced understanding of the impact of different modeling choices.
2. **Detailed Analysis of Dependency Features:** The exploration of typed versus untyped dependency features and higher-order paths is a valuable contribution to the field. The insightful discussion of how different dependency paths align (or don't align) vector spaces, and the resulting impact on compositionality, demonstrates a strong grasp of the underlying linguistic principles. The observation regarding the potential benefit of a hybrid approach, combining aligned and unaligned features, is particularly insightful.
3. **Replication and Extension of Existing Work:** The study successfully replicates and extends the work of Reddy et al. (2011), achieving improved performance and providing further evidence supporting the importance of contextual information in compositional semantics. The comparison to both baselines and prior state-of-the-art methods establishes a clear benchmark for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Contribution:** While the experimental work is thorough, the core ideas – leveraging dependency information for compositional semantics, comparing different compositional operations, and utilizing word embeddings – are not novel. The paper primarily presents an empirical investigation of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally new approach. The improvements over Reddy et al. (2011) are incremental and primarily stem from parameter tuning and a more detailed exploration of existing concepts.
2. **Overly Detailed and Repetitive Presentation:** The paper suffers from excessive detail in certain sections, particularly the descriptions of the APT model and experimental setup. While thoroughness is appreciated, the level of detail often obscures the key insights. Redundancy is also prevalent; concepts are repeatedly explained with little added value. For instance, the discussion of composition operations (addition, multiplication) is repeated multiple times without significant new information. This impacts readability and makes it difficult to discern the most important findings.
3. **Limited Scope & Generalizability:** The evaluation is confined to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) focusing on compound noun compositionality. While this allows for in-depth analysis, it raises questions about the generalizability of the findings to other types of phrasal and sentential composition.  The focus on noun-noun compounds, where a dependency relationship is often present, may not translate well to more complex syntactic structures.
4. **Minor Formatting and Presentation Issues:** There are inconsistencies in the referencing style (e.g., the parenthetical notes like "Baroni and Lenci.,?" are distracting).  The presentation of tables could be improved for clarity. The suppression of a reference (“reference suppressed”) is unprofessional and should be remedied.
5. **Unjustified Parameter Choices:** While a parameter search was performed, the rationale behind some of the parameter choices (e.g., the higher subsampling threshold for cbow) is not clearly justified and feels somewhat arbitrary. The authors state it's a counterintuitive finding but provide a weak explanation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind suppressing the reference in Section 3?
2.  How do you believe the findings of this study would generalize to other types of compositional phrases (e.g., adjective-noun phrases, verb phrases)?
3.  Given the incremental nature of the contribution, what is the single most important takeaway message for the reader?
4.   Can you provide a more detailed explanation for why a higher subsampling threshold yielded better results with the cbow model, beyond simply stating it’s related to low frequency compounds?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Please standardize the referencing style throughout the paper.
*   Consider streamlining the descriptions of the APT model and experimental setup to focus on the most essential details.
*   The paper would benefit from a more concise and focused discussion section.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the thoroughness of the experimental work, I am inclined towards **rejection**. While the paper presents a valuable empirical investigation, the incremental nature of the contributions and the issues with presentation and scope outweigh the strengths. The paper reads more like an extended experiment log than a compelling research contribution.  Addressing the weaknesses regarding conciseness, justification of choices, and generalization would be necessary for reconsideration.
