## Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and the use of higher-order dependency paths on the ability of distributional semantic models to capture phrase compositionality, specifically focusing on noun-noun compounds. The authors evaluate various models, including those integrating composition directly into the distributional model (APT models) and those applying compositional operations to pre-trained word embeddings, using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. The core argument is that incorporating richer syntactic information via dependency parsing and thoughtfully defining contextual features can improve compositionality detection.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Evaluation of Typed vs. Untyped Context in Composition:** The systematic comparison of models utilizing typed and untyped dependency features provides valuable insights into the importance of syntactic information for compositional semantics. This is a solid experimental contribution.
2. **Demonstration of APT Model Effectiveness:** The paper provides evidence that their Anchored Packed Tree (APT) approach, particularly when combining aligned and unaligned features, achieves competitive or superior performance compared to baseline methods, including those utilizing neural word embeddings.
3. **Reaffirmation of Compositionality as a Useful Evaluation Tool:** The work reinforces the utility of datasets like Reddy et al. (2011) for evaluating compositional models *independent* of direct similarity judgements, offering a more targeted assessment of a model’s ability to capture non-literal meaning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors carefully replicate the experimental setup from Reddy et al. (2011), allowing for direct comparison and providing a strong foundation for their results.  The use of 3-fold cross-validation is also commendable.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration of Model Variations:** The authors explore a wide range of parameter settings within the APT framework, including different weighting schemes, alignment strategies, and PPMI smoothing factors, showcasing a thorough understanding of the model's nuances.
3. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The motivation for focusing on dependency relations and higher-order features is clearly articulated, linking back to theoretical arguments about the role of syntax in semantic composition.  The discussion of the continuum of compositionality is also well-integrated.
4. **Competitive Results:** The achieved performance, surpassing the previous state-of-the-art on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, is a significant finding and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed methods.
5. **Detailed Analysis and Discussion:** The analysis of the results, particularly the observation regarding the optimal weighting parameters (h and q), provides valuable insights into the relative importance of the modifier and head in determining compositionality.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is confined to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) focused on noun-noun compounds. While this allows for a focused investigation, it limits the generalizability of the findings. Testing on other types of phrases (e.g., adjective-noun phrases, verb-object constructions) would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Complexity of APT Models:** The APT models, while effective, are complex and require significant preprocessing and parameter tuning. This complexity may hinder their adoption by researchers lacking expertise in dependency parsing and distributional semantics.  The presentation of the APT models is also quite dense and could be simplified.
3. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the authors discuss optimal parameters, a more in-depth error analysis, identifying specific types of compounds that the models struggle with, would provide valuable insights for future research.  Understanding *why* the models fail on certain examples is crucial.
4. **Missing Comparison to Recent Compositional Methods:** The related work section, while thorough, doesn't engage with very recent advances in compositional semantics using transformers or other large language models.  A comparison to these methods, even if briefly, would contextualize the contribution of this work.
5. **The Suppression of a Reference:**  The phrase “Elsewhere (reference suppressed)” is jarring. Suppressing references is generally discouraged; if the work is relevant, it should be cited.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with building and using the APT models compared to simpler compositional approaches (e.g., adding word embeddings)?
2.  What specific challenges did you encounter when applying the APT models to the ukWaC corpus, and how did you address them? (e.g., parser errors, sparse data)
3.  Given your observation that a hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned features) performs best, can you hypothesize *why* the unaligned features contribute to improved performance, even when syntactic structure is explicitly modeled?
4.  You mention that the use of shifted PMI negatively impacted results. Do you believe this is specifically due to the low frequency of compound nouns, or might it be a general limitation of shifted PMI in this compositional context?
5.  How would you envision extending the APT approach to handle more complex phrasal structures and compositional phenomena beyond noun-noun compounds?




**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between the various APT model configurations (alignment, composition function, weighting).
*   The writing is generally clear, but some sections (e.g., the explanation of the reduction operation) could benefit from further clarification.
*   Consider including a brief discussion of the limitations of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset itself.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The empirical evaluation is rigorous, the approach is well-motivated, and the results are promising. While the limitations regarding the scope of evaluation and the complexity of the models should be addressed, the paper is of high quality and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
