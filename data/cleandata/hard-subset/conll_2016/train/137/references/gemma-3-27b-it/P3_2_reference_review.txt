## Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

**Summary:** This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependency paths) on the ability of distributional semantic models to capture compositionality, particularly in noun-noun compounds. The authors experiment with both building compositional representations *into* the distributional model (APT-based approach) and applying compositional operations *to* pre-trained embeddings. They evaluate these methods using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, correlating model-derived similarity scores with human judgments of compositionality.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation of Contextual Features for Compositionality:** The paper provides a thorough and well-executed empirical comparison of different contextual feature types (typed/untyped, higher-order dependencies) within both intrinsic compositional modeling (APTs) and extrinsic compositional operations on pre-trained embeddings. This is a valuable contribution to understanding what kinds of linguistic information are most helpful for compositional semantics.
2. **Demonstration of the Effectiveness of Dependency-Based Features:**  The results consistently show that incorporating dependency information – particularly via higher-order dependency paths – significantly improves compositionality detection compared to simpler, untyped methods.  This strengthens the argument for linguistically motivated distributional semantic models.
3. **Re-establishment of a Strong Baseline:** The authors demonstrate that carefully tuned, simpler models (cbow embeddings) can achieve competitive performance, providing a solid baseline for future research and highlighting the importance of parameter optimization.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The use of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is appropriate for the task, and the authors meticulously detail their experimental setup, including corpus preprocessing, model parameters, and evaluation metrics. This allows for reproducibility and facilitates comparison with prior work.
2. **Well-Justified Methodology:**  The motivation for exploring typed dependency features and higher-order paths is clearly articulated, grounded in linguistic theory and previous research.  The explanation of the APT approach is thorough and demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying principles.
3. **Clear and Detailed Results:** The results are presented clearly in tables, and the discussion highlights significant findings and trends. The ablation studies (e.g., comparing aligned vs. unaligned APTs, different composition operations) provide valuable insights.
4. **Strong Performance Improvements:** The demonstrated improvements over the Reddy et al. (2011) baseline are significant, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed methods. The hybrid APT model provides the best overall performance.
5. **Insightful Analysis:** The authors’ discussion of parameter settings (e.g. subsampling thresholds) and their potential explanations is commendable and showcases a deep understanding of the models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is limited to the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, which focuses solely on compound nouns.  While this allows for focused analysis, it raises questions about the generalizability of the findings to other types of phrases or compositional phenomena (e.g., verb phrases, adjectival phrases).  Testing on additional datasets would strengthen the claims.
2. **Complexity of APT Implementation:** The APT approach, while theoretically sound, is complex to implement and potentially computationally expensive.  The authors don’t adequately address the practical challenges associated with scaling this approach to larger corpora or more complex linguistic structures.  A discussion of computational costs would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  The paper lacks a detailed error analysis.  Identifying specific types of non-compositional phrases where the models struggle could provide valuable insights for future improvement.  For example, are there particular semantic domains or linguistic constructions that pose a greater challenge?
4. **Incremental Improvements:** While the improvements achieved are statistically significant, they are not *massive*.  The performance gains, particularly with the hybrid APT model, are incremental. The authors could better emphasize the practical implications of these gains in real-world applications.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Generalizability:**  Do you anticipate the observed benefits of typed dependency features and higher-order paths to generalize to other types of phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adjectival phrases)? If so, what modifications, if any, would be necessary?
2. **Computational Cost:** Can you provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with the APT approach, particularly in terms of memory usage and processing time? How does this compare to the cost of training and using the neural word embeddings?
3. **Error Analysis:**  Have you conducted any error analysis to identify specific types of non-compositional phrases where the models struggle? If so, could you share some examples and discuss potential reasons for the errors?
4. **Future Directions:** Considering the incremental gains achieved, what are the most promising directions for future research in this area?  Are there other linguistic features or modeling techniques that you believe could further improve compositionality detection?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The introduction provides a good overview of the relevant literature, although it could be slightly more concise.
*   The notation and terminology are generally clear, although some definitions could be made more explicit.
*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical complexity of the topic.



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics.  The rigorous experimental evaluation and insightful analysis of contextual features provide important insights for researchers working on this challenging problem. While the scope of the evaluation is limited, the results are compelling and suggest that linguistically motivated distributional semantic models have the potential to achieve significant improvements in compositionality detection.
