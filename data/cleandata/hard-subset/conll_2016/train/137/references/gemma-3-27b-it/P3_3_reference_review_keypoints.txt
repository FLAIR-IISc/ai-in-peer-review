- Main contribution: Systematic comparison of typed vs. untyped dependency context shows the importance of syntactic feature definition for compositionality detection.
- Main contribution: Demonstrates Anchored Packed Tree (APT) models, especially combined aligned+unaligned features, achieve competitive or superior performance to baselines including neural embeddings.
- Main contribution: Reinforces the utility of compositionality-focused evaluation (Reddy et al. 2011) as a targeted test of non-literal meaning capture.

- Strength: Rigorous experimental setup with replication of prior work and 3-fold cross-validation enabling direct comparison.
- Strength: Comprehensive exploration of APT parameter settings (weighting, alignment, PPMI smoothing) showing deep model analysis.
- Strength: Clear, well-motivated focus on dependency relations and higher-order features, and discussion of compositionality as a continuum.
- Strength: Achieves competitive results, surpassing prior state-of-the-art on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.
- Strength: Detailed analysis of results, including insights on optimal weighting parameters for modifier vs. head importance.

- Weakness: Evaluation limited to a single dataset (noun-noun compounds), reducing generalizability across phrase types.
- Weakness: APT models are complex, require heavy preprocessing and tuning, and their presentation is dense which may hinder adoption.
- Weakness: Limited error analysis — lacks detailed examination of which compound types the models fail on and why.
- Weakness: Missing comparison to recent transformer/large-language-model compositional methods, leaving contextualization incomplete.
- Weakness: Unnecessarily suppressed reference (“Elsewhere (reference suppressed)”) which should be cited if relevant.

- Q: Please quantify the computational cost of building/using APT models versus simpler compositional methods (e.g., vector addition).
- Q: What specific challenges arose when applying APTs to ukWaC (parser errors, sparsity), and how were they mitigated?
- Q: Why do unaligned features improve performance in the hybrid model even when syntactic structure is modeled — what is the hypothesized mechanism?
- Q: Is the negative impact of shifted PMI due to low compound noun frequency specifically, or a broader limitation in this compositional setting?
- Q: How could the APT approach be extended to handle more complex phrasal structures beyond noun-noun compounds?

- Additional: Add a summary table comparing APT configurations (alignment, composition function, weighting) for clarity.
- Additional: Clarify some dense explanations (e.g., the reduction operation) to improve readability.
- Additional: Briefly discuss limitations of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset itself.

- Overall assessment: Strong, well-motivated empirical paper with rigorous experiments and promising results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
