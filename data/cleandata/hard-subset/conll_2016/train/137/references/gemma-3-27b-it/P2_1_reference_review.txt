## Review for "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on compositional distributional semantics, specifically focusing on the detection of non-compositional phrases (like "red herring"). The authors use a dataset of compound nouns with human compositionality judgments (Reddy et al., 2011) to evaluate their approach, comparing models based on anchored packed trees (APTs) with those applying naive compositional operations to pre-trained word embeddings. The core finding is that incorporating dependency-based features – particularly through the hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned APTs – improves compositionality detection compared to previous work.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Investigation of Dependency-Based Context in Composition:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of how different levels of dependency information (typed vs. untyped, higher-order paths) affect a model’s ability to predict compositionality, a problem often glossed over in compositional semantics.
2. **Demonstration of Improved Compositionality Detection:** The results show that the proposed APT-based models, especially the hybrid approach, achieve better correlation with human judgments of compositionality than previously reported state-of-the-art results, suggesting a tangible advancement in understanding and modeling non-compositional phrases.
3. **Detailed Experimental Setup and Baseline Comparison:** The authors rigorously reproduce and extend the experimental setup of Reddy et al. (2011), providing a strong baseline for comparison and a clear methodology for future research.



**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Crucial Issue:** The paper tackles a fundamental problem in compositional semantics – the fact that not all phrases are compositional – and directly confronts its impact on evaluation. This is a significant strength, as it acknowledges the limitations of simply evaluating against human similarity judgements.
2. **Methodological Rigor:**  The experimental design is well-defined and thorough. The reproduction of the Reddy et al. (2011) setup, along with the systematic exploration of different APT configurations (PPMI variations, alignment strategies, composition functions), provides a strong foundation for the conclusions drawn.  The careful consideration of parameter settings and cross-validation demonstrates a commitment to robust results.
3. **Clear and Detailed Explanations:** The paper is well-written and provides clear explanations of the APT approach, dependency features, and compositional operations. Table 1, illustrating typed dependency features, is particularly helpful for understanding the core concepts.  The discussion of why certain compositional functions perform better than others (e.g., weighted addition) is insightful.
4. **Strong Performance Gains:** The observed improvements over the existing state-of-the-art in compositionality detection are substantial, lending credibility to the proposed approach.  The fact that a hybrid approach consistently outperforms purely aligned or unaligned methods suggests a beneficial interplay between different types of dependency information.
5. **Insightful Analysis:** The authors offer good interpretations of the results. For example, the observation that the modifier might be more informative than the head in certain contexts adds nuance to the understanding of noun compound compositionality.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope - Focus on Noun Compounds:** The study is confined to noun-noun compounds. While this provides a focused investigation, it raises questions about the generalizability of the findings to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adjective-noun phrases). The paper acknowledges this but could benefit from a more explicit discussion of potential differences and limitations.
2. **Data Set Size & Bias:** The Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, while valuable, is relatively small (90 compounds). This limits the statistical power of the results and raises concerns about potential biases inherent in the dataset. The dependence on a single dataset reduces the confidence in the broader applicability of the observed improvements.
3. **Computational Complexity:** The APT approach, especially with higher-order dependency paths, is computationally expensive. The paper does not discuss the computational cost of building and using these models, which could be a barrier to adoption.  A brief discussion of scalability and potential optimization strategies would be beneficial.
4. **Theoretical Justification:** While the authors provide intuition for why dependency-based features are helpful, the theoretical justification for the specific choices made (e.g., the reduction operation in APTs) could be strengthened. A more in-depth discussion of the linguistic motivations behind these design decisions would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How would the proposed method generalize to other types of phrases besides noun-noun compounds? Do you anticipate similar benefits from incorporating dependency-based features?
2.  Given the limited size of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, have you considered evaluating your method on other datasets or through additional qualitative analysis to assess the robustness of your findings?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed estimate of the computational cost of building and using the APT models, particularly with higher-order dependency paths? Are there any optimization strategies that could improve scalability?
4. Could you elaborate on the linguistic motivations behind the reduction operation used in APTs?  Is there a specific theoretical framework (e.g., formal semantics) that informs this choice?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to a suppressed paper ("Elsewhere...") should be replaced with a proper citation if possible.
*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used for each model and the resulting performance metrics for easier comparison.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The authors have identified and addressed a crucial issue – the impact of non-compositionality on evaluation – and demonstrated a promising approach for improving compositionality detection. While there are some weaknesses, they are outweighed by the methodological rigor, clear explanations, and significant performance gains. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and comments above.
