## Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependency paths) on the ability of distributional semantic models to capture compositionality, specifically in noun compounds. The authors compare models leveraging these different feature types with naive compositional operations applied to state-of-the-art distributional representations, evaluating performance on Reddy et al.’s (2011) compositionality detection dataset. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that incorporating typed dependency information, especially via higher-order paths, improves the prediction of human judgements of compositionality compared to simpler, untyped approaches and even neural embeddings.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of the Value of Typed Dependency Information:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that leveraging typed dependency relations, and specifically higher-order paths, in distributional models significantly improves performance on compositionality detection. This is a valuable finding demonstrating the importance of syntactic structure for semantic understanding.
2. **Comprehensive Comparison of Compositional Approaches:** The thorough comparison of various compositional methods (APT with different alignment strategies, neural embeddings) and feature types provides a useful benchmark and insightful analysis for researchers working in compositional semantics.
3. **Re-evaluation of Compositionality Detection Task:**  The paper effectively utilizes the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, framing it not just as a similarity task but as a means to *evaluate* compositional models based on their ability to predict compositionality, offering a more targeted and interpretable assessment.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate consistently significant improvements using the proposed typed dependency features, achieving state-of-the-art results on the compositionality detection task. The gains over Reddy et al.'s baseline and even strong neural embedding baselines are convincing.
2. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The paper includes a comprehensive exploration of parameters (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, alignment strategies) within the APT model, alongside a careful comparison with neural embeddings, ensuring a robust evaluation. The careful consideration of preprocessing (handling compound nouns in the corpus) is also commendable.
3. **Clear Motivation and Framing:** The paper effectively motivates the need for considering non-compositionality in semantic models, arguing against solely relying on similarity-based evaluation.  The connection to linguistic theory (Sag et al., 2002) and previous work (Reddy et al., 2011) is well-established.
4. **Detailed Explanation of APT Model:** The description of the Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) and the alignment/composition process is clear and well-explained, making the methodology accessible to readers. Table 1 is particularly helpful in illustrating the features.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Dataset:**  The study relies on a single, relatively small dataset (90 noun compounds). While the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is a valuable resource, the generalizability of the findings to other types of phrases (verb phrases, adjective phrases) and domains remains unclear.
2. **Complexity of APT Model:** The APT model is complex and requires significant feature engineering. This complexity might make it difficult to scale to larger datasets or adapt to different languages without substantial effort. The paper acknowledges this, but it's a significant practical consideration.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the paper presents overall performance results, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *which* compounds the model struggles with and *why* would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for improvement. What types of non-compositionality are particularly challenging?
4. **Motivation for Hyperparameter Choices:** Some of the hyperparameter choices (e.g., the higher subsampling threshold for cbow) are justified but could benefit from more detailed explanation or further experimentation. The reasoning is provided, but more exploration would strengthen the argument.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Generalizability:** Do you anticipate similar performance gains with typed dependency features on other types of phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adjective phrases)? Are there any theoretical reasons why this approach might be less effective for certain phrase types?
2. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a brief error analysis, highlighting specific examples of compounds where the model performs poorly and discussing potential reasons for these errors?
3. **Scalability:** What are the primary bottlenecks in scaling the APT model to larger datasets? What strategies could be employed to address these bottlenecks?
4. **Feature Importance:**  Can you quantify the relative contribution of different order dependency paths (1st order, 2nd order) to the overall performance?  Is there a point of diminishing returns with higher-order paths?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The suppression of the reference to the APT paper is unfortunate and hinders reproducibility.
* The authors should include more discussion of the computational cost associated with the APT model and compare it with the cost of training and using neural embeddings.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The empirical results are compelling, and the thorough experimental setup strengthens the validity of the findings. While the limitations related to dataset size and model complexity are acknowledged, they do not detract significantly from the overall value of the work. I recommend acceptance.
