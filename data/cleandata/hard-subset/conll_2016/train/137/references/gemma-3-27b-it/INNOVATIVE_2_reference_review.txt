This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature typing (using dependency relations) and higher-order dependency paths on compositional distributional semantics, specifically targeting the detection of non-compositional phrases like “red herring.” The authors propose and evaluate methods for aligning vector spaces before composition, aiming to improve correlation with human judgments of compositionality on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. 

The primary contribution is a thorough exploration of how different levels of syntactic information – untyped vs. typed dependency features, and first- vs. second-order paths – affect a model's ability to discern compositional vs. non-compositional phrases. A secondary, valuable contribution lies in the empirical demonstration that, even with relatively simple compositional operations, incorporating dependency information substantially improves performance compared to baseline methods. Finally, the paper offers a compelling argument for evaluating compositional methods *specifically* on their ability to identify non-compositionality, rather than solely focusing on phrase similarity, providing a more nuanced evaluation framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Focus:** The paper directly addresses a long-standing challenge in compositional semantics: how to handle non-compositional language. While compositionality has been studied extensively, focusing on *detecting* its absence is a refreshingly different angle. This framing highlights the limitations of purely compositional approaches and pushes for more sophisticated models.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results consistently demonstrate that incorporating dependency information, particularly through the use of higher-order paths, leads to significant improvements over existing methods, including the state-of-the-art results reported by Reddy et al. The improvement is not dramatic, but consistent and statistically significant, suggesting a real benefit from the proposed approach.
3. **Well-Defined Evaluation:** The use of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset provides a focused and appropriate benchmark for evaluating compositionality detection. The authors acknowledge and address the dataset's limitations and provide a clear rationale for their experimental setup.
4. **Comprehensive Exploration:** The paper systematically explores a range of parameters – weighting schemes, PPMI smoothing, alignment strategies – offering a detailed analysis of their impact on performance. This thoroughness demonstrates a strong understanding of the problem and a commitment to rigorous evaluation.
5. **Practical Implications:** The insights gained from this work can inform the design of more robust and accurate compositional models, potentially benefiting a wide range of NLP applications, including machine translation, question answering, and text summarization.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is confined to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) focusing on compound nouns. While this allows for a focused analysis, it raises questions about the generalizability of the findings to other types of phrases (e.g., verb phrases, prepositional phrases) and other languages.
2. **Methodological Complexity:** The APT (Anchored Packed Trees) approach, while conceptually sound, is complex and potentially difficult to implement and scale. The paper doesn't fully address the computational cost of generating and composing these representations, which could be a barrier to adoption.
3. **Insufficient Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to Reddy et al. (2011) is important, the paper could benefit from a more thorough comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art neural compositional models (e.g., those utilizing transformers) that haven't been directly evaluated on this specific compositionality detection task.
4. **Interpretability of Results:** While the paper demonstrates *that* dependency information improves performance, it doesn’t fully explain *why*. A deeper analysis of the learned representations and the specific features that contribute most to compositionality detection could provide valuable insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does the performance of your best model degrade when evaluated on a dataset of non-noun compound phrases? 
2. What is the computational cost of generating and composing APT representations compared to simpler methods like adding pre-trained word embeddings?
3. Could you provide an ablation study specifically focusing on the contribution of higher-order dependency paths versus typed vs untyped features?
4. Do the optimal values for parameters *h* and *q* vary significantly across different subtypes of compound nouns (e.g., adjective-noun vs. noun-noun)?
