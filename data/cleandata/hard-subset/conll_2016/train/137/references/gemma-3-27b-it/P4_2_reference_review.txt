This paper investigates the impact of contextual features—specifically typed vs. untyped, and higher-order dependency paths—on the ability of distributional semantic models to capture compositionality, particularly in the context of non-compositional phrases. The authors aim to evaluate whether incorporating more structured contextual information can improve the detection of compositionality in compound nouns, using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the systematic exploration and comparison of different compositional approaches leveraging dependency-based contextual features. The authors demonstrate that incorporating typed dependency paths within distributional models—specifically using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs)—can improve compositionality detection compared to previously reported state-of-the-art results. A secondary, but valuable contribution, is the thorough empirical evaluation of various composition operations (addition, intersection) and parameter settings within the APT framework. The authors also provide a nuanced analysis showing that a hybrid approach—combining aligned and unaligned APTs—yields the best performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently achieves improvements over the baseline (Reddy et al., 2011) across multiple configurations of the APT model. The reported Spearman’s rank correlation coefficients demonstrate a statistically significant performance gain, indicating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. This result is supported by a thorough parameter search and cross-validation methodology.
2. **Detailed Exploration of Contextual Features:** The paper provides a comprehensive investigation of different types of contextual features – typed vs. untyped, first-order vs. higher-order dependency paths. This detailed analysis provides valuable insights into the importance of syntactic structure for compositional semantics. The explanation of how APTs encode complete dependency paths and align representations is clear and well-motivated.
3. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup closely follows that of Reddy et al. (2011) for fair comparison, using the same dataset and corpus (ukWaC). This allows for a direct assessment of the proposed improvements. The controlled experiments with different composition operations and parameter settings further strengthen the validity of the findings.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Composition Operations:** The paper goes beyond simply proposing a model and delves into the performance of different composition operations (e.g., ⊔INT, ⊔UNI). The observation that the optimal operation depends on the alignment of APTs is a significant finding, suggesting a more nuanced interaction between syntactic structure and composition.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of APTs:** While the application of APTs to compositionality detection is valuable, the core concept of APTs is not entirely novel (acknowledged by mentioning a suppressed reference). The novelty lies in the specific implementation and the systematic evaluation within the context of compositionality detection, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic contribution.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is relatively small (90 compound nouns). While it serves as a valuable benchmark, the generalizability of the findings to other types of phrases and domains is unclear. A more extensive evaluation on diverse datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on quantitative results (correlation scores). A qualitative analysis of the errors made by the model—identifying specific types of phrases where it struggles—would provide deeper insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.
4. **Complexity of Implementation:** The APT approach involves several parameters and preprocessing steps (e.g., dependency parsing, feature alignment). This complexity could hinder its adoption by researchers without significant NLP expertise. A discussion of implementation challenges and potential simplifications would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions a “suppressed reference” regarding the original proposal of APTs. Could you provide the citation for this reference?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the quality of the dependency parser used? Were any experiments conducted to assess the impact of parsing errors on performance?
3.  The paper observes that the optimal parameters (h and q) are within a narrow range. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the parameter space and explain why these values perform best?
4.  Given the limitations of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, do you have plans to evaluate the proposed approach on other datasets that contain a wider range of compositional and non-compositional phrases?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The explanation of the compositional operations (⊔INT, ⊔UNI) could be clarified with more concrete examples.
*   The motivation for using shifted PMI was somewhat unclear; a more detailed explanation of why it was not beneficial in this context would be helpful.
*   Consider providing ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component of the APT model (e.g., typed vs. untyped features, higher-order paths).
