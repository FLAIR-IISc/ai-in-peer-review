This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on compositional distributional semantics, specifically focusing on detecting non-compositional phrases. The authors propose an “Anchored Packed Trees” (APT) approach, leveraging dependency parsing to build contextual features and explore different composition operations. The primary claimed contribution is demonstrating that incorporating syntactic structure—specifically, dependency paths—into distributional models improves compositionality detection, as measured against a benchmark dataset of compound nouns. A secondary contribution lies in the comparative evaluation of different compositional functions (addition, intersection) and parameter settings within the APT framework.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors meticulously adapt a well-established experimental protocol (Reddy et al., 2011) using the same dataset and corpus (ukWaC), allowing for direct comparison and demonstrating incremental improvement over previous state-of-the-art. This careful adherence to established methods provides a solid foundation for their findings.
2. **Systematic Exploration of Feature Engineering:** The systematic investigation of typed vs. untyped features and higher-order dependency paths is commendable. The authors explore several configurations of the APT model, revealing nuanced insights into the importance of syntactic information for compositional semantics.
3. **Clear Presentation & Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly articulates the motivation behind the research – the limitations of purely distributional approaches when dealing with non-compositional language. The explanation of the APT approach is sufficiently detailed and easy to follow.
4. **Outperformance of Baselines:** The APT models demonstrably outperform both the original Reddy et al. (2011) results and neural word embedding baselines, indicating a genuine advantage conferred by the incorporation of dependency information. The hybrid model, combining aligned and unaligned features, shows particularly promising results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Advancement, Not a Paradigm Shift:** While the improvements achieved are statistically significant, they are incremental. The performance gains over strong baselines, while welcome, do not represent a dramatic leap forward in the field. The paper’s impact hinges on demonstrating substantial performance improvements, and this feels somewhat lacking. The results are better, but not *transformatively* better.
2. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is solely focused on compound noun compositionality. While this is a valuable task, it's unclear how well these findings generalize to other types of compositional phenomena (e.g., verb-object combinations, adjectival modification). The conclusions should be tempered by this limited scope. The authors acknowledge this but do not sufficiently address it.
3. **Overly Complex System:** The APT model, with its various parameters (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, alignment strategies), becomes somewhat complex. This complexity raises concerns about overfitting and the interpretability of the results. The optimal parameter settings might be highly specific to this dataset and task and not generalize well to other scenarios.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit significantly from an error analysis. Identifying the types of non-compositional phrases where the APT models still struggle would provide valuable insights and guide future research directions. Understanding *why* the model fails in certain cases is as important as knowing that it succeeds in others.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of dependency paths up to order 2? Were higher-order paths explored, and if so, why were they not included in the final models?
2.  Given the complexity of the APT model, could you perform an ablation study to assess the individual contribution of each component (e.g., typed features, higher-order paths, alignment)?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the specific dependency parser used? Have you considered evaluating the model’s performance with different parsers?
4.  What is the computational cost associated with building and composing the APT representations, compared to simpler methods like adding pre-trained word embeddings?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The suppression of the reference to the earlier work on APTs is unfortunate. Providing a pointer to that publication is crucial for reproducibility and allows the community to understand the full context of this work.
*   The explanation of the ‘reduction’ process in the APT composition (Section 3) could be clearer. A more intuitive explanation would be beneficial.
*   The claim that subsampling benefits the cbow model due to low frequency of compounds is interesting but requires more thorough justification – it goes against standard recommendations.
