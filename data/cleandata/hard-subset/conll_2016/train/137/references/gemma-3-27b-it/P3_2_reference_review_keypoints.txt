- Investigates how contextual feature definitions (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependency paths) affect distributional models’ ability to detect compositionality in noun-noun compounds, comparing APT-built compositional representations and compositional operations on pre-trained embeddings using Reddy et al. (2011).

- Contribution: Thorough empirical comparison of contextual feature types across intrinsic (APT) and extrinsic (pre-trained embeddings) compositional methods.

- Contribution: Shows dependency-based features, especially higher-order dependency paths, substantially improve compositionality detection.

- Contribution: Re-establishes tuned cbow embeddings as a strong, competitive baseline.

- Strength: Rigorous, reproducible experimental setup with detailed preprocessing, parameters, and evaluation.

- Strength: Well-motivated methodology grounded in linguistic theory and clear exposition of the APT approach.

- Strength: Clear, detailed results and informative ablation studies (aligned vs. unaligned APTs, composition operations).

- Strength: Significant performance improvements over the Reddy et al. (2011) baseline, with a hybrid APT model performing best.

- Strength: Insightful analysis of parameter effects (e.g., subsampling thresholds) showing deep model understanding.

- Weakness: Evaluation limited to Reddy et al. (2011) compound-noun dataset; generalizability to other phrase types is uncertain.

- Weakness: APT implementation is complex and potentially computationally expensive; paper lacks detailed discussion of scalability and costs.

- Weakness: Missing detailed error analysis identifying specific non-compositional phrase types where models fail.

- Weakness: Improvements, while statistically significant, are incremental and practical impact could be better contextualized.

- Question: Do the benefits of typed dependency features and higher-order paths generalize to other phrase types (verbs, adjectives), and what modifications would be needed?

- Question: Can the authors provide detailed computational cost metrics for APT (memory, runtime) and compare them to neural embedding training/usage?

- Question: Have the authors performed error analysis to identify challenging non-compositional phrase types and can they provide examples?

- Question: Given incremental gains, what are the most promising future directions or additional linguistic features/modeling techniques to pursue?

- Additional: Introduction is comprehensive but could be slightly more concise.

- Additional: Notation and terminology are mostly clear; a few definitions could be made more explicit.

- Additional: Paper is well-written and accessible despite technical complexity.

- Overall: Strong, valuable contribution demonstrating linguistically motivated distributional models improve compositionality detection, though scope, scalability, and error analysis need further work.
