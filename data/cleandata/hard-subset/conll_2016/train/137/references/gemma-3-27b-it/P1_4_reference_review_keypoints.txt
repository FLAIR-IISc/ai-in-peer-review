- Paper studies typed vs. untyped contextual features and higher-order dependency paths for detecting compositionality in noun compounds using APTs vs. neural embeddings.
- Uses anchored packed trees (APTs) and standard compositional ops on word embeddings, evaluated on Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality dataset.
- Finds APTs—especially hybrid aligned/unaligned models—outperform neural embeddings and that dependency information helps.

Main contributions:
- Empirically compares typed vs. untyped contextual features for compositionality detection.
- Demonstrates APTs (hybrid aligned/unaligned) are effective for compositionality and beat embedding baselines.
- Replicates and extends Reddy et al. (2011), providing clearer baselines and insights.

Strengths:
- Rigorous experimental setup replicating prior work and systematically exploring parameters and compositional ops.
- Clear, detailed methodology and explanation of APTs, with helpful illustrations.
- Strong empirical results with APTs improving correlation with human judgments.
- Analysis showing modifier tends to be slightly more informative than head for compositionality.
- Focuses on the challenging and important problem of non-compositionality.

Weaknesses:
- Evaluation limited to noun-noun compounds and a single dataset, limiting generalizability.
- APT model is complex and potentially computationally expensive; scalability/costs are not discussed.
- Lacks detailed error analysis identifying which compounds fail and why.
- A reference to prior APT work is suppressed, weakening reproducibility and clarity.
- Related Work section is somewhat long and repetitive.

Questions to authors:
- How well will APTs and typed features generalize to other phrase types and corpora?
- What are the time/memory costs of building/composing APTs versus embedding methods, and how can scalability be improved?
- Why was the prior APT reference suppressed and is the prior work available?
- Can you provide examples and explanations of compounds the model handles poorly?
- What is the effect of using higher-order dependency paths beyond order 2, and where do returns diminish?

Additional comments:
- Paper is well-written and clearly formatted.
- Recommend adding a table summarizing key experimental parameters for clarity.

Overall:
- Strong paper with rigorous evaluation and promising results; addressing generalizability, computational cost, and error analysis would strengthen it further.
