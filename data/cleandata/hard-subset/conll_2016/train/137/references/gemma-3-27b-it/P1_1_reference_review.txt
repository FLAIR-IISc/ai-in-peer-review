## Peer Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the impact of contextual feature definition (typed vs. untyped) and higher-order dependency paths on the ability of distributional semantic models to account for compositionality in noun compounds. The authors experiment with anchored packed trees (APTs) – a compositional distributional approach – and compare it to applying naive composition operations to pre-trained word embeddings, using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for evaluating compositionality detection. They find that APTs, particularly a hybrid approach leveraging both aligned and unaligned dependency features, outperform both neural embeddings and previously reported results.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Systematic Evaluation of Typed vs. Untyped Contextual Features for Compositionality:** The paper provides a detailed empirical comparison of using typed dependency relations versus untyped ones within a distributional compositional framework. This is a valuable contribution to understanding how linguistic structure impacts compositional semantics.
2. **Demonstration of APTs for Compositionality Detection:** The authors successfully demonstrate the efficacy of their APT approach, showing significant improvement over baseline methods (including state-of-the-art neural embeddings) in predicting human judgments of compositionality for noun compounds.
3. **Insightful Analysis of Alignment in Composition:**  The exploration of aligning dependency features before composition, and the eventual finding that a *hybrid* approach of aligned and unaligned features performs best, provides valuable nuance to the compositional modelling landscape.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently shows improvements in correlation with human judgments across various configurations of the APT model. Achieving a higher correlation than the previous state-of-the-art (Reddy et al., 2011) is a significant result.
2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of evaluating compositional semantics, acknowledging the prevalence of non-compositionality in language and the shortcomings of solely relying on similarity-based evaluation. The use of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is appropriate and well-justified.
3. **Detailed Experimental Setup:** The authors provide a thorough description of their experimental setup, including parameters, preprocessing steps, and evaluation metrics. This makes the work highly reproducible. The ablation studies (e.g., different weighting schemes, alignment vs. no alignment) are particularly insightful.
4. **Sound Theoretical Grounding:** The APT approach is grounded in linguistic theory, particularly dependency parsing, and demonstrates a thoughtful approach to incorporating syntactic structure into distributional semantics. The explanation of feature alignment and reduction is clear and logically presented.
5. **Clear Writing and Organization:** The paper is well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the authors’ arguments and understand their experimental findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is restricted to a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) focusing solely on noun-noun compounds. While this allows for a focused investigation, the generalizability of the findings to other types of phrases or semantic phenomena remains unclear. Testing on a broader range of phrases and/or semantic relations is needed.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  While the paper presents quantitative results, it lacks qualitative error analysis. Understanding *which* compounds the model struggles with and *why* could offer valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and directions for future improvement. For example, are there specific types of non-compositional phrases that consistently pose a challenge?
3. **Complexity of APT Implementation:** The APT approach appears complex to implement and requires a dependency-parsed corpus. This could be a barrier to adoption for researchers without access to such resources or expertise in dependency parsing. A discussion of the computational costs and scalability of the approach would be helpful.
4. **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While the comparison to Reddy et al. (2011) is relevant, more recent and sophisticated neural compositional models (e.g., those incorporating attention mechanisms or transformers) should be included as baselines for a more comprehensive evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  You mention that the hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned features) performs best. Can you provide a more intuitive explanation for *why* this is the case? What specific types of information are captured by the aligned versus unaligned features that contribute to this improved performance?
2.  You mention that the cbow model outperformed the skip-gram model. What specific characteristics of the cbow model do you believe contributed to this superior performance in the context of compositionality detection?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of dependency parser? Did you experiment with different parsers, and if so, did this affect the performance of the APT model?
4.  Regarding the computational cost of the APT model, can you provide an estimate of the time required to build the lexicon and perform composition for a corpus of a given size?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work, particularly recent advances in neural compositional models.
*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the key parameters and results for each experimental configuration.
*   The reference to a "suppressed" reference should be clarified.



Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful study that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The paper’s strengths outweigh its weaknesses, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
