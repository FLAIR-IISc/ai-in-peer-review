This paper investigates the impact of contextual features – specifically, typed versus untyped dependency relations, and higher-order dependency paths – on compositional distributional semantics, with a focus on improving the detection of non-compositional phrases. The core idea is that a model's ability to represent compositionality is linked to how it defines context. The authors explore this using a dataset of compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality, comparing their approach, based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), to applying naive compositional operations to state-of-the-art distributional representations. 

The primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical exploration of different contextual feature configurations within a distributional semantic framework for assessing phrase compositionality. A secondary, though less substantial, contribution lies in demonstrating that APT-based approaches, leveraging dependency information, can achieve competitive, and in some cases superior, performance compared to simpler methods utilizing pre-trained word embeddings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup & Baseline Comparison:** The authors diligently replicate the experimental setup of Reddy et al. (2011), ensuring a fair comparison and establishing a clear baseline. They explore a wide range of parameters for both APT-based models and neural word embeddings, demonstrating a commitment to thoroughness. This carefulness allows for a meaningful assessment of the proposed method’s effectiveness.
2. **Focus on a Challenging Problem:** The paper directly addresses the well-known problem of non-compositionality in language. Recognizing that standard evaluation metrics based on human similarity judgments can be misleading when dealing with idioms or non-literal phrases, the authors use a dataset specifically designed to evaluate compositionality, which is a valuable contribution.
3. **Detailed Exploration of Dependency Features:** The investigation of typed versus untyped dependency features, and the incorporation of higher-order dependency paths, provides a nuanced understanding of how syntactic information can impact compositional semantics. The tables illustrating the feature spaces are particularly helpful in visualizing this impact.
4. **Solid Performance Gains:** The APT-based models, particularly the hybrid approach, consistently outperform the baseline and achieve competitive results compared to neural word embeddings. While not a massive leap in performance, the consistent improvement demonstrates the value of incorporating richer dependency information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Contribution of APTs:** While the experiments are well-executed, the novelty of the APT approach itself is somewhat limited. The core idea of incorporating dependency information into distributional models is not new (Padó and Lapata, 2007). The paper primarily adds to existing work through a careful investigation and optimization of this approach, rather than introducing a fundamentally new concept. The suppression of the reference describing the APT approach hinders assessment of its novelty.
2. **Limited Generalizability:** The experiments are focused exclusively on compound nouns. While this provides a controlled setting for evaluating compositionality, it's unclear how well the findings would generalize to other types of phrases or sentences. Compositionality is a complex phenomenon, and the specific challenges associated with compound nouns may not be representative of language as a whole.
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The optimal performance is heavily reliant on parameter tuning (e.g., the weighting parameters *h* and *q*). The paper does not adequately discuss the robustness of the results to changes in these parameters. It's possible that the observed improvements are sensitive to specific parameter settings that may not generalize to other datasets or tasks.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper presents quantitative results but lacks qualitative error analysis. Understanding *which* phrases the model struggles with and *why* would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and guide future research.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide the reference that describes the APT approach which is suppressed in the paper? It is difficult to assess the novelty of your approach without knowing the prior work.
2. How sensitive are your results to variations in the weighting parameters *h* and *q*? Could you provide a sensitivity analysis showing the impact of these parameters on performance?
3. Can you provide examples of phrases where the model performs poorly and discuss the possible reasons for these errors?
4. What are the computational costs associated with the APT approach compared to simpler methods like adding word embeddings?
