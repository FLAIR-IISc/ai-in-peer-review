- Investigates typed vs. untyped dependency features and higher-order dependency paths for detecting non-compositional phrases using APTs and Reddy et al. (2011) data.  
- Empirically evaluates how dependency-based context affects compositional distributional semantics.  
- Shows APT-based (especially hybrid aligned/unaligned) models improve correlation with human compositionality judgments.  
- Reproduces and extends Reddy et al. (2011) experimental setup, providing strong baselines.  
- Tackles the important problem that not all phrases are compositional and its impact on evaluation.  
- Demonstrates methodological rigor with thorough experiments, parameter tuning, and cross-validation.  
- Provides clear, detailed explanations of APTs, dependency features, and composition functions.  
- Reports substantial performance gains over prior state-of-the-art in compositionality detection.  
- Offers insightful analysis (e.g., modifier sometimes more informative than head) to interpret results.  
- Study is limited to noun-noun compounds, raising generalizability concerns to other phrase types.  
- Relies on a small dataset (90 compounds), limiting statistical power and risking dataset bias.  
- APT approach with higher-order paths is computationally expensive; scalability and costs are not discussed.  
- Theoretical justification for specific APT design choices (e.g., reduction operation) is underdeveloped.  
- How would the method generalize to phrase types beyond noun-noun compounds?  
- Have you evaluated on other datasets or performed additional qualitative analyses to test robustness?  
- Please provide detailed estimates of computational cost and possible optimization strategies for APTs.  
- Elaborate on the linguistic/theoretical motivations behind the APT reduction operation.  
- Replace the suppressed citation ("Elsewhere...") with a proper reference where possible.  
- Include a table summarizing key hyperparameters and resulting performance metrics for easier comparison.  
- Overall: strong, methodologically rigorous paper with significant gains; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
