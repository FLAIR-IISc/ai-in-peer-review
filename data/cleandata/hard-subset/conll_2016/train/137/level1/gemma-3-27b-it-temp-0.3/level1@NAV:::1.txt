## Review for "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

This paper investigates compositional distributional semantics, specifically focusing on the challenge posed by non-compositional phrases. The authors compare an integrated compositional approach using sparse, high-dimensional representations (based on anchored packed trees) with applying compositional operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings. The core idea is to leverage typed dependency information to better model phrase meaning, particularly for non-compositional cases.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the thorough empirical evaluation of different compositional methods *within* a framework designed to specifically address non-compositionality. The use of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, which provides human judgments of compositionality, is a significant strength. The authors systematically explore different parameter settings for both their APT model and neural embeddings, providing a comprehensive comparison. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that incorporating typed dependency information, even without full alignment, improves performance, suggesting that syntactic structure is valuable for compositional semantics.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevant Problem & Dataset:** The focus on non-compositionality is crucial, as standard compositional models often struggle with these phrases. Utilizing the Reddy et al. dataset allows for a direct evaluation of a model’s ability to discern compositional vs. non-compositional meaning.
2. **Comprehensive Experimentation:** The authors explore a wide range of parameters for both the APT model (PPMI smoothing, composition operations) and neural embeddings (model type, dimensions, subsampling), demonstrating a commitment to rigorous evaluation.
3. **Clear Performance Gains:** The results consistently show that the proposed APT-based methods outperform the baseline neural embedding approaches, and even improve upon the previous state-of-the-art reported in Reddy et al. (2011).
4. **Insightful Analysis:** The finding that a hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) yields the best results suggests a nuanced interplay between syntactic structure and distributional information.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of APT Framework:** While the application to compositionality detection is valuable, the underlying APT framework itself isn’t presented as particularly novel. The paper relies heavily on previously published work regarding APTs.
2. **Corpus Dependency:** The experiments are conducted solely on the ukWaC corpus. The results might not generalize to other corpora with different characteristics (e.g., domain, size).
3. **Complexity & Scalability:** The high dimensionality of the APT representations (80,000-230,000 dimensions) raises concerns about computational cost and scalability to larger datasets or more complex phrases.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with constructing and composing the high-dimensional APT representations? How does this compare to the computational cost of working with the lower-dimensional neural embeddings?
2.  Have you explored the impact of different dependency parsers on the performance of the APT model? Dependency parsing accuracy can vary significantly, and this could influence the quality of the typed features.
3.  The paper mentions smoothing distributional representations. Could you provide more details on how this smoothing is implemented and why it is particularly beneficial in this context?
