**Summary**

This paper proposes a compositional distributional approach to improve the detection of non-compositional phrases, specifically compound nouns. The authors use a dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality and compare their approach to state-of-the-art neural word embeddings. The proposed method, called Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), uses typed contextual features and aligns vector spaces before composition. The results show that the APT approach outperforms the neural word embeddings and achieves state-of-the-art performance on the task.

**Contributions**

1. **Improved compositionality detection**: The paper proposes a new approach to compositionality detection that outperforms existing methods, including state-of-the-art neural word embeddings.
2. **Typed contextual features**: The authors introduce typed contextual features, which are shown to be highly informative in the task of determining the compositionality of compound nouns.
3. **Alignment of vector spaces**: The paper demonstrates the importance of aligning vector spaces before composition, which is a novel contribution to the field of compositional distributional semantics.

**Strengths**

1. **Robust evaluation**: The authors use a robust evaluation framework, including 3-fold cross-validation and repeated experiments, to ensure the reliability of their results.
2. **Comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper compares the proposed approach to state-of-the-art neural word embeddings, providing a clear understanding of the performance gain.
3. **Insightful analysis**: The authors provide an insightful analysis of the results, including the importance of typed contextual features and the alignment of vector spaces.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited generalizability**: The paper focuses on compound nouns, and it is unclear whether the proposed approach generalizes to other types of phrases or linguistic phenomena.
2. **Computational complexity**: The APT approach may be computationally expensive, which could limit its applicability to large-scale datasets.
3. **Parameter tuning**: The authors perform extensive parameter tuning, which may not be feasible in all scenarios.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of the APT approach and how it compares to other compositional methods?
2. How do you plan to extend the APT approach to other types of phrases or linguistic phenomena?
3. Can you discuss the potential applications of the APT approach in natural language processing tasks, such as machine translation or text summarization?
