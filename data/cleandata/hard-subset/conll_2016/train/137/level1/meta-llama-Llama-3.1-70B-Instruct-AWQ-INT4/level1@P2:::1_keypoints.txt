- Summary: Proposes Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), a compositional distributional method that detects non-compositional compound nouns and outperforms neural embeddings on a 90-item human-annotated dataset.

- Contribution: Improved compositionality detection — APT outperforms existing methods including state-of-the-art neural word embeddings.

- Contribution: Typed contextual features — introduces typed contextual features shown to be highly informative for compositionality judgment.

- Contribution: Vector-space alignment — demonstrates that aligning vector spaces before composition is important and novel.

- Strength: Robust evaluation — uses 3-fold cross-validation and repeated experiments for reliable results.

- Strength: Comparison to state-of-the-art — directly compares APT to neural embeddings, showing clear performance gains.

- Strength: Insightful analysis — provides analysis explaining the impacts of typed features and alignment.

- Weakness: Limited generalizability — study restricted to compound nouns, unclear applicability to other phrase types or phenomena.

- Weakness: Computational complexity — APT may be computationally expensive, limiting scalability.

- Weakness: Extensive parameter tuning — relies on heavy tuning which may not be feasible in all settings.

- Question: Requests details on the computational complexity of APT and comparisons to other compositional methods.

- Question: Asks how the authors plan to extend APT to other phrase types or linguistic phenomena.

- Question: Requests discussion of potential NLP applications for APT (e.g., machine translation, text summarization).
