- Investigates the impact of contextual features (typed dependency paths) on compositional distributional models for detecting non-compositional phrases.
- Compares an integrated sparse high-dimensional compositional approach (APT) to applying compositional operations to neural embeddings.
- Primary contribution: thorough empirical evaluation using a dependency-parsed ukWaC corpus and the Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality dataset.
- Shows that incorporating higher-order dependency paths into representations improves compositionality detection.
- Demonstrates that unaligned but typed dependency features can outperform aligned features for this task.
- Strength: strong empirical evaluation with extensive experiments over PPMI weighting, composition operations, and embedding dimensionality.
- Strength: clear motivation and problem framing around non-compositionality and justified use of the Reddy et al. dataset.
- Strength: novel feature engineering—exploration of higher-order dependency paths and alignment strategies within the APT framework.
- Strength: detailed experimental setup and parameter reporting that enhances reproducibility.
- Weakness: limited scope of neural baselines; insufficient comparison to recent neural compositional models (e.g., attention-based methods).
- Weakness: experiments use a single corpus (ukWaC), so generalizability to other corpora or domains is unclear.
- Weakness: APT framework is complex and may be difficult for others to implement; needs a more streamlined presentation.
- Question: What is the computational cost of the APT framework compared to simpler neural embedding approaches?
- Question: How sensitive are the results to the choice of dependency parser; have other parsers been evaluated?
- Question: How could compositionality detection be integrated into training to improve compositional models?
