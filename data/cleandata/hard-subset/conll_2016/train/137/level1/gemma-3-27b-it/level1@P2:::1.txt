This paper investigates the detection of non-compositional phrases using both an integrated compositional distributional approach (using sparse high-dimensional representations based on anchored packed trees – APTs) and a more ad-hoc approach of applying compositional operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings. The core idea is to leverage compositionality detection as a testbed for evaluating different compositional methods.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Empirical evaluation of dependency path order in compositional distributional semantics:** The authors systematically explore the impact of using first and second-order dependency paths within their APT model, demonstrating that higher-order paths, while seemingly beneficial conceptually, don’t consistently improve performance. This highlights the challenges in effectively incorporating richer syntactic information into distributional models.
2. **Comparison of APT-based composition with direct composition of neural embeddings:** The paper provides a head-to-head comparison of a more theoretically grounded compositional method (APTs) with the simpler, but widely used, approach of composing pre-trained word embeddings. This comparison demonstrates that, on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, the APT-based approach can outperform the neural embedding approach, suggesting that explicitly modeling dependency structure is valuable.
3. **Demonstration of the importance of alignment (or lack thereof) in compositional spaces:** The authors show that a hybrid approach – combining aligned and unaligned APT representations – yields the best results, indicating that while aligning vector spaces according to dependency structure is important, leveraging unaligned representations also captures relevant compositional information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong experimental setup and baseline comparison:** The authors carefully follow the experimental setup of Reddy et al. (2011) allowing for a direct comparison and clear demonstration of improvements. They also compare against strong baselines, including neural embeddings, increasing confidence in their findings.
2. **Clear motivation and problem definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of non-compositionality in language and frames compositionality detection as a useful evaluation task for compositional semantic models.
3. **Thorough exploration of parameters:** The authors perform a comprehensive parameter search within the APT model, exploring different weighting schemes, smoothing techniques (PPMI vs. normalized counts), and composition operations (addition vs. intersection). This rigorous exploration strengthens the validity of their conclusions.
4. **Insightful analysis of results:** The findings regarding the effectiveness of the hybrid alignment approach and the superior performance of the cbow model are well-supported by the experimental results and offer valuable insights into the factors influencing compositional semantics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited dataset size and domain:** The evaluation is conducted solely on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, which consists of only 90 compound nouns. While this dataset is a standard benchmark, its limited size and narrow domain raise concerns about the generalizability of the findings. A more diverse and larger dataset would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Complexity of the APT model:** The APT model is relatively complex and involves numerous parameters. While the authors perform a thorough parameter search, the complexity of the model may make it difficult to interpret the results and understand the specific factors driving performance improvements.
3. **Lack of error analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis. Identifying the types of non-compositional phrases where the APT model performs poorly could provide valuable insights for future research.
4. **Limited discussion of computational cost:** The authors do not discuss the computational cost of the APT model compared to the neural embedding approach. This information would be useful for assessing the practicality of the method.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the preprocessing step of recombining compound noun phrases into single lexical items? Could different parsing or tokenization strategies impact performance?
2.  You mention that the unaligned APT model often captures relevant information. Could you elaborate on what types of compositional relationships are better represented in the unaligned space?
3.  Given the success of the hybrid approach, did you investigate any strategies for dynamically weighting the contribution of the aligned and unaligned components based on the specific phrase being analyzed?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to a suppressed paper in section 3 should be clarified.
*   The table captions could be more descriptive, explicitly stating what “CF” and “CS” refer to.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of related work on techniques for handling non-compositional phrases in other NLP tasks (e.g., machine translation, question answering).



Overall, this paper presents a solid contribution to the field of compositional semantics. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of a novel compositional method and demonstrate its potential advantages over more traditional approaches. While the limited dataset size and complexity of the model are weaknesses, the strong experimental setup and insightful analysis make this a valuable and informative study. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
