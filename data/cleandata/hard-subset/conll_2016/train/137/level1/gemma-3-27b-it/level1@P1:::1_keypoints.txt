- Paper compares an anchored packed tree (APT) compositional model to composing neural word embeddings, focusing on how context definition (typed vs untyped, higher-order dependencies) affects handling of compositional vs non-compositional phrases.
- Experiments use and extend the Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality detection dataset (ukWaC corpus, Spearman’s correlation).
- Primary contribution: systematic empirical investigation of contextual features (dependency path order and typing) within a distributional compositional framework.
- Secondary contribution: showing higher-order dependency paths improve performance even without explicit alignment.
- Provides a robust re-implementation and extension of Reddy et al. (2011) experiments, offering a strong baseline.
- Strength: rigorous experimental setup and parameter exploration for both APT and neural embeddings, enabling credible comparisons.
- Strength: detailed analysis of typed vs untyped contexts and higher-order dependencies, with an interesting hybrid aligned/unaligned feature finding.
- Strength: clear empirical improvement—hybrid APT outperforms Reddy et al., with useful findings on PPMI smoothing and optimal weighting parameters.
- Strength: focuses on the challenging and relevant problem of compositionality detection (distinguishing literal vs idiomatic meanings).
- Weakness: limited evaluation scope—small dataset (90 compounds) that may not generalize across phrase types or domains.
- Weakness: APT framework is complex with many parameters and dense description, hindering accessibility and reproducibility.
- Weakness: lacks qualitative error analysis to explain model successes and failures on specific compounds.
- Weakness: limited discussion of linguistic motivations tying specific dependency relations to observed performance gains.
- Question: please provide the suppressed references critical to understanding the APT framework.
- Question: please explain why higher subsampling thresholds in neural embedding experiments performed better than typical recommendations.
- Question: which kinds of non-compositional phrases (e.g., metaphorical) is the APT model likely to struggle with, and why?
- Question: are there plans to release code and data to facilitate reproducibility?
- Additional: writing is generally good but could be more concise; tables are well formatted; consider adding related work on idiomatic expressions and metaphor detection.
