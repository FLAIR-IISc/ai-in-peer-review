This paper investigates compositional distributional semantics, focusing on the challenge posed by non-compositional phrases. The authors compare an integrated compositional approach based on sparse, high-dimensional representations using anchored packed trees (APTs) with a simpler approach of applying compositional operations to state-of-the-art neural word embeddings. The core idea is to explore how the definition of context (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependencies) impacts a model’s ability to handle composition, particularly in differentiating compositional from non-compositional phrases. The experiments utilize the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for compositionality detection.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the thorough empirical investigation of the impact of different contextual features, specifically dependency path order and typing, within a distributional compositional framework. While the APT framework itself isn’t entirely novel, the systematic exploration of its components and comparison to neural embeddings on a targeted task (compositionality detection) provides valuable insights. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating that utilizing higher-order dependency paths, even without explicit alignment, improves performance. Finally, the paper provides a robust re-implementation and extension of the Reddy et al. (2011) experiments, serving as a strong baseline for future work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Experimental Setup & Baseline:** The authors carefully replicate and extend the Reddy et al. (2011) experiments, providing a strong and well-defined baseline. This includes thorough parameter exploration for both the APT model and neural embeddings, lending credibility to the findings. The consistent use of the ukWaC corpus and the evaluation metric (Spearman’s rank correlation) allows for direct comparison with prior work.
2. **Detailed Analysis of Contextual Features:** The paper provides a detailed analysis of how different types of contextual features (typed vs. untyped, higher-order dependencies) affect compositionality detection. The use of anchored packed trees allows for a nuanced representation of dependency relationships, and the systematic experimentation with different configurations reveals valuable insights into the importance of these features. The hybrid model combining aligned and unaligned features is a particularly interesting finding.
3. **Clear Demonstration of Improvement:** The authors demonstrate that their proposed approach (specifically the hybrid APT model) consistently outperforms the baseline results reported in Reddy et al. (2011), indicating a genuine improvement in compositionality detection performance. The findings related to PPMI smoothing and the optimal weighting parameters (h and q) further contribute to a deeper understanding of the model’s behavior.
4. **Focus on a Challenging Problem:** The study tackles a crucial problem in compositional semantics: dealing with non-compositional phrases. By focusing on compositionality *detection* rather than just phrase similarity, the authors highlight the importance of distinguishing between literal and idiomatic meanings, and provide a valuable benchmark for evaluating compositional models.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset is a valuable resource, it's relatively small (90 compound nouns) and may not generalize well to other types of phrases or domains. Expanding the evaluation to include a wider variety of compositional and non-compositional phrases would strengthen the conclusions.
2. **Complexity of the APT Framework:** The APT framework, while powerful, is also complex. The numerous parameters and configurations can make it difficult to understand and reproduce the results. A more streamlined or simplified version of the framework might be more accessible and facilitate further research. The paper’s description of APTs, while detailed, is dense and could benefit from clearer illustrations.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on quantitative results (Spearman’s rank correlation). A qualitative error analysis, examining specific examples where the model performs well or poorly, would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and potential areas for improvement. Why does the model succeed on some compounds and fail on others?
4. **Limited Discussion of Linguistic Motivation:** While the paper acknowledges the linguistic motivation behind using dependency paths, the connection between specific linguistic phenomena and the observed performance improvements could be further elaborated. For example, how do different types of dependency relations (e.g., NMOD vs. AMOD) contribute to the model’s ability to detect compositionality?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper mentions suppressed references. Could you provide these references? They appear critical to understanding the full context of the APT framework.
2. Could you elaborate on the reasons for the unexpected results regarding subsampling thresholds in the neural embedding experiments? Why did higher thresholds perform better than typically recommended?
3. What specific types of non-compositional phrases would you expect the APT model to struggle with, and why? Would phrases with metaphorical meanings pose a particular challenge?
4. Are there plans to release the code and data used in the experiments to facilitate reproducibility and further research?

**Additional Comments:**

* The writing quality is generally good, but the paper could benefit from more concise explanations of technical concepts.
* The tables are well-formatted and informative.
* Consider adding a brief discussion of related work on idiomatic expressions and metaphor detection.
