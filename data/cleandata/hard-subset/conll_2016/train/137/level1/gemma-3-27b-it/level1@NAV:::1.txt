## Review for "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases"

This paper investigates the impact of contextual features, particularly typed dependency paths, on compositional distributional models for detecting non-compositional phrases. The authors compare an integrated compositional approach using sparse high-dimensional representations with applying compositional operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings. The work is motivated by the observation that existing compositional methods struggle with non-compositional language and that evaluating them requires datasets that specifically target this phenomenon.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the thorough empirical evaluation of compositional methods using a dependency-parsed corpus and the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for compositionality detection. The investigation into the impact of incorporating typed dependency information (higher-order paths) into distributional representations, and comparing this to simpler compositional operations on neural embeddings, provides valuable insights. A secondary contribution is the demonstration that unaligned, but typed, dependency features can outperform aligned ones in this specific task, highlighting the importance of considering broader contextual information.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation using a well-established dataset and a variety of model configurations. The extensive experimentation with different parameters (PPMI weighting, composition operations, dimensionality of embeddings) strengthens the findings.
2. **Clear Motivation & Problem Framing:** The authors clearly articulate the challenge of non-compositionality in language and its impact on compositional distributional semantics. The justification for using the Reddy et al. dataset as a testbed is well-reasoned.
3. **Novelty in Feature Engineering:** The exploration of higher-order dependency paths as contextual features, particularly within the APT framework, is a valuable contribution. The investigation of alignment strategies within this framework adds further nuance.
4. **Reproducibility:** The detailed description of the experimental setup and parameter settings enhances reproducibility, which is crucial for advancing the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Neural Baselines:** While the comparison to neural embeddings is useful, the exploration of different neural compositional methods (e.g., incorporating attention mechanisms) feels somewhat limited. A more comprehensive comparison with recent advances in neural compositional models would be beneficial.
2. **Corpus Specificity:** The results are demonstrated on a single dataset (ukWaC), and it is unclear how well these findings generalize to other corpora or domains.
3. **Complexity of APT Framework:** The APT framework, while promising, is complex and might be difficult for other researchers to implement and build upon. A more streamlined presentation of the framework's core concepts would improve accessibility.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the APT framework compared to the simpler neural embedding approach?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the specific dependency parser used? Have you considered experimenting with different parsers?
3.   You mention the possibility of using compositionality detection to *improve* compositional methods. Could you provide more detail on this idea – specifically, how would you integrate compositionality detection into the training process of a compositional model?
