This paper investigates the detection of non-compositional phrases using compositional distributional models, comparing a novel integrated approach based on sparse high-dimensional representations (APT – Anchored Packed Trees) with a simpler approach of applying compositional operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings. The core idea is to leverage typed dependency information to improve compositional representations, particularly for phrases that deviate from strict compositional semantics.

The primary contribution is the thorough exploration and empirical evaluation of the impact of incorporating typed dependency information (via APTs) into compositional distributional semantics, specifically in the context of identifying non-compositional phrases. While the use of dependency information isn’t entirely novel, the method of representing it via anchored packed trees, and the detailed comparison against a strong neural baseline, offer valuable insights. A secondary contribution is the detailed experimental setup, utilizing the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for compositionality detection, which allows for a controlled comparison of different approaches. Finally, the results demonstrating that a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned APT features offers the best performance is a noteworthy, if incremental, advancement.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The use of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset provides a well-defined benchmark for evaluating compositionality detection. Rigorous experimentation with various parameters (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, weighting schemes) and comparison with both neural baselines and previous state-of-the-art results strengthen the validity of the findings. The 3-fold cross-validation is appropriate.
2. **Novel APT Representation:** The Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) provide a principled way to incorporate dependency information into distributional representations. The concept of aligning vector spaces based on dependency paths, and the subsequent reduction step to address feature space dimensionality, demonstrates a thoughtful approach to compositional modeling.  The clear explanation and illustration with Table 1 are particularly helpful.
3. **Insightful Comparison with Neural Baselines:**  Directly comparing the APT-based approach to compositional operations applied to word2vec embeddings is valuable.  The finding that the cbow model outperforms skip-gram in this setting, and the observation about the optimal subsampling threshold, highlight the importance of parameter tuning for specific tasks.
4. **Hybrid Approach Effectiveness:** The identification of the hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) as the best-performing configuration suggests that both dependency structure and general co-occurrence information are crucial for compositionality detection. This nuanced result strengthens the paper’s overall contribution.
5. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper is generally well-written and provides a clear explanation of the methods and experimental setup. The inclusion of relevant background information and related work helps contextualize the contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Generalizability:** The evaluation is entirely focused on compound nouns and a single dataset. While the chosen dataset is a standard benchmark, it's unclear how well the proposed approach would generalize to other types of non-compositional phrases (e.g., idioms, phrasal verbs) or different languages.
2. **Computational Complexity:** The APT approach, with its high-dimensional feature spaces and dependency path encoding, likely has a higher computational cost compared to simpler methods like applying compositional operations to pre-trained embeddings. This aspect is not discussed in detail, and could be a practical limitation.
3. **Dependence on Parser Accuracy:** The accuracy of the dependency parser used to generate the APTs is crucial. Errors in parsing could introduce noise into the representations and negatively impact performance.  The paper doesn't adequately address the potential impact of parsing errors. There is mention of the parser making errors on three-noun compounds, but no discussion of error rates elsewhere.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  While the paper reports overall performance metrics, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Identifying specific types of non-compositional phrases where the method struggles could provide valuable insights and guide future research.  For example, are some dependency relations more informative than others?
5. **Reference Suppression:** The paper mentions a suppressed reference regarding the APT method. This hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to fully assess the novelty of the approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the computational cost of the APT approach compared to the neural baseline, in terms of both training time and memory usage?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the APT approach to the accuracy of the dependency parser? Have you investigated the impact of parser errors on the results?
3.  What types of non-compositional phrases does the method struggle with most? Is there a specific subset of the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset where performance is significantly lower?
4.  Can you elaborate on the reasoning behind the observed optimal range of h and q values (0.3-0.5) in the compositional operations? What linguistic intuition might explain this preference for the modifier being slightly more informative than the head?
5.  Given the suppressed reference, can you at least provide a high-level description of the key differences between the current implementation of APTs and the previous work?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of potential future directions, such as exploring different types of compositional operations or incorporating other sources of linguistic information (e.g., semantic roles).
*   A visualization of the high-dimensional APT feature space would be helpful for understanding the representations and identifying potential patterns.
*  It would be beneficial to include a table comparing the performance to other recent methods on the same dataset, even if they don’t directly address compositionality detection. This would better position the work within the broader field of distributional semantics.
