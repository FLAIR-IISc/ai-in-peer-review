This paper investigates the detection of non-compositional phrases using compositional distributional methods, specifically comparing an integrated approach using sparse high-dimensional representations with applying composition operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings. The primary contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation of different compositional approaches, particularly focusing on the role of dependency paths (typed vs. untyped, higher-order) in improving compositionality detection, using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. A second contribution lies in demonstrating that an integrated compositional distributional approach, leveraging dependency structures, achieves better performance than simply applying compositional operations to pre-trained embeddings. Finally, the paper provides a detailed exploration of different composition operations (INT, UNI) and parameter settings for both APT models and neural embeddings, offering insights into their relative strengths and weaknesses.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper presents a very thorough experimental evaluation, exploring a wide range of parameters and model configurations (different PPMI smoothing values, composition operations, dependency order, and neural embedding settings). This level of detail allows for a nuanced understanding of the factors influencing performance. The use of 3-fold cross-validation and reporting of Spearman’s rank correlation provides a robust assessment of the models.
2. **Focus on Dependency Structure:** The investigation of typed and higher-order dependency paths is a key strength. The work convincingly argues for the importance of incorporating syntactic structure into compositional models, going beyond simple word vector combination. The APT model, with its focus on aligned dependency features, offers a principled way to represent and compose phrases based on their syntactic structure.
3. **Strong Baseline Comparison:**  The authors rigorously compare their methods against a relevant baseline (Reddy et al., 2011) and establish a clear improvement, demonstrating the value of their approach. The inclusion of the FREQ baseline provides a useful point of comparison, illustrating the contribution of compositional methods beyond simply relying on phrase frequency.
4. **Detailed Analysis & Insights:** The analysis of results goes beyond simply reporting performance numbers. The paper offers insightful discussions of why certain configurations perform better than others (e.g., the role of PPMI smoothing, the effect of higher-order dependencies, the observation that modifiers are slightly more informative than heads). This analytical depth strengthens the credibility of the findings.
5. **Reproducibility:** The paper provides sufficient details regarding the experimental setup (corpus, preprocessing steps, parameter settings) to facilitate reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Method:** While the experimental analysis is extensive, the core APT model itself, and the basic idea of incorporating dependency structures, are not entirely novel. Previous work (cited in the paper) has already explored similar concepts. The contribution lies more in the detailed evaluation and optimization of these techniques within the specific context of compositionality detection.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, while a valuable resource, is relatively small (90 compound nouns).  This limits the generalizability of the findings.  A larger and more diverse dataset would be beneficial to confirm the robustness of the observed performance gains.
3. **Computational Complexity:** The use of high-dimensional sparse representations (APT models) can be computationally expensive, both in terms of training and inference. The paper does not explicitly address this issue or compare the computational cost of the different methods.
4. **Parameter Sensitivity:** The optimal performance of the models appears to be sensitive to parameter settings (e.g., the weighting parameters *h* and *q*).  The paper does not offer guidance on how to choose these parameters in a practical setting, beyond cross-validation.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper reports overall performance metrics, a more detailed error analysis (e.g., examining specific examples where the models fail) would provide further insights into the limitations of the approaches and suggest directions for future work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost (memory usage, training time, inference time) of the APT models compared to the neural embedding-based approaches?
2.  How do you think the results would generalize to other types of non-compositional phrases beyond noun-noun compounds (e.g., verb-particle constructions, idioms)?
3.  Were any attempts made to incorporate external knowledge sources (e.g., lexical resources, knowledge graphs) into the compositional models? If not, why?
4.  Regarding the observation that modifiers are slightly more informative than heads, do you have any linguistic intuition as to *why* this is the case?
