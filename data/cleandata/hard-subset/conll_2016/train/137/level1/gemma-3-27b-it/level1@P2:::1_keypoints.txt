- Empirical evaluation: Examines first- and second-order dependency path order in APTs and finds higher-order paths do not consistently improve performance.  
- Comparison: Head-to-head comparison shows APT-based composition can outperform direct composition of neural embeddings on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset.  
- Alignment insight: A hybrid of aligned and unaligned APT representations yields best results, indicating both alignment and unaligned information matter.  
- Strong experimental setup: Follows Reddy et al. (2011) setup and compares against strong baselines, lending credibility to results.  
- Clear motivation: Paper clearly frames non-compositionality detection as a useful evaluation for compositional semantic models.  
- Thorough parameter search: Comprehensive exploration of weighting, smoothing, and composition operations within the APT model.  
- Insightful analysis: Findings about hybrid alignment and cbow superiority are well-supported and informative.  
- Limited dataset: Evaluation only on the small, 90-item Reddy et al. (2011) dataset limits generalizability.  
- Model complexity: APT model is complex with many parameters, making interpretation and attribution of gains difficult.  
- Missing error analysis: Lacks detailed error analysis identifying phrase types where APT fails.  
- Computational cost unclear: No discussion of runtime or resource costs compared to neural methods.  
- Question on preprocessing sensitivity: How sensitive are results to recombining compound nouns and different parsing/tokenization?  
- Question on unaligned representations: Which compositional relationships are better captured by unaligned APTs?  
- Question on hybrid weighting: Did the authors try dynamically weighting aligned vs. unaligned contributions per phrase?  
- Minor clarifications: Clarify suppressed paper reference, expand table captions (define “CF” and “CS”), and add related-work discussion on non-compositional phrase handling.  
- Recommendation: Solid contribution with insightful results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
