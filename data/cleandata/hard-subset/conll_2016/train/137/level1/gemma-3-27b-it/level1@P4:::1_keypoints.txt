- Thorough empirical comparison of integrated sparse high-dimensional APT models vs. applying composition to neural embeddings for detecting non-compositional phrases.  
- Primary focus on the role of dependency paths (typed vs. untyped, higher-order) in improving compositionality detection.  
- Demonstrates that an integrated APT approach leveraging dependency structures outperforms simple composition of pre-trained neural embeddings.  
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation exploring many parameters (PPMI smoothing, composition ops, dependency order, neural settings) with 3-fold CV and Spearman correlation.  
- Strong focus on dependency structure; APT’s aligned dependency features provide a principled syntactic compositional representation.  
- Solid baseline comparisons (Reddy et al., 2011 and FREQ) showing clear improvements from the proposed approach.  
- Detailed analysis offering insights on why certain configurations work better (PPMI smoothing, higher-order deps, modifiers vs. heads).  
- Experimental setup is described sufficiently for reproducibility (corpus, preprocessing, parameters).  
- Core method lacks strong novelty: dependency-based composition and APT-like ideas have prior work; main contribution is empirical depth.  
- Dataset is small (90 compounds in Reddy et al., 2011), limiting generalizability of results.  
- APT’s high-dimensional sparse representations can be computationally expensive; paper does not quantify computational cost.  
- Model performance is sensitive to parameters (e.g., h and q) with little practical guidance beyond cross-validation.  
- Limited error analysis; more example-level failure analysis would clarify limitations and future directions.  
- Question: please report computational costs (memory, training/inference time) comparing APT and neural methods.  
- Question: how would results generalize to other non-compositional types (verb-particle constructions, idioms)?  
- Question: were external knowledge sources (lexical resources, knowledge graphs) considered or tried?  
- Question: any linguistic explanation for why modifiers are slightly more informative than heads?  
- Minor formatting issue: “reference suppressed” in Section 3.  
- Related work section could be more concise; some cited papers are over-detailed.  
- Suggest briefly discussing potential applications beyond compositionality detection (e.g., paraphrase detection, semantic relatedness).
