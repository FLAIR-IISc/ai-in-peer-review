- Paper compares an integrated typed-dependency compositional model (APTs) with simple neural addition (word2vec) for noun–noun compound compositionality, reporting top Spearman ρ=0.79 (hybrid APT) vs 0.74 (CBOW) and 0.71 (count baseline).

- Main contribution 1: Empirical evidence that typed, higher-order dependency features in APTs improve compound compositionality detection over untyped count models and neural baselines.

- Main contribution 2: A hybrid composition strategy combining aligned and unaligned APT features yields complementary benefits and best performance.

- Main contribution 3: Careful comparison of composition operators, weighting, and PPMI smoothing, with insights about when shifted PMI/subsampling harms low-frequency compounds.

- Strength: Clear, consistent empirical gains over strong baselines using the same corpus and reasonable parameter sweeps, lending credibility.

- Strength: Well-motivated modeling of typed, higher-order contexts with alignment (offset + reduction) to reconcile constituent feature spaces.

- Strength: Thorough ablations and informative analysis (union vs intersection, PPMI choices, weighting), with sensible parameter observations (h, q in [0.3,0.5]).

- Strength: Fair treatment of neural baselines within the constrained setting (CBOW/skip-gram trained on ukWaC with parameter sweeps).

- Strength: Careful corpus preprocessing to avoid leakage by collapsing compounds and preventing constituent reuse of compound contexts.

- Weakness: Very limited evaluation set (90-item Reddy dataset); statistical significance claims and low reported errors are unconvincing without clearer testing and larger/more varied datasets.

- Weakness: Incomplete baseline coverage for neural/embedding approaches—missing dependency-based embeddings, phrase-aware models, and stronger composition methods—potentially overstating APT superiority.

- Weakness: Novelty is mainly application and analysis; core APT machinery appears previously introduced, so contributions are incremental.

- Weakness: Missing methodological details (feature pruning, counts/min_counts, word2vec hyperparameters, parsing error handling, composition order limits, statistical testing) hinder reproducibility.

- Weakness: Potential frequency confound not controlled—frequency correlates strongly with compositionality (FREQ ρ=0.63) and may explain some gains.

- Question: Clarify how “errors ≤ 0.0015” and the 0.005 significance threshold were computed; provide variance estimates (CIs, bootstrap, permutation tests).

- Question: Did you try dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg) or other typed-context embeddings, and how would they compare to APTs?

- Question: Provide neural training details (min_count, negative sampling k, epochs, learning rate) and whether phrase-aware training or compound vectors were tried.

- Question: Specify APT configuration details (frequency cutoffs/pruning, max path order, smoothing/backoff) and consider releasing code/parameters.

- Question: Report results controlling for frequency (partial correlation or stratified evaluation) and robustness on low-frequency compounds.

- Question: Can the approach predict constituent-level literality judgments in the dataset, and how does it perform?
