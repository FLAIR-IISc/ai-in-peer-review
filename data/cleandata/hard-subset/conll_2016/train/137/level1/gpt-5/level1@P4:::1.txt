Summary and contributions:
The paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds, comparing an integrated, dependency-typed, higher-order distributional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) against naïve additive composition applied to state-of-the-art neural word embeddings. Using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds) and ukWaC, the authors report improved Spearman correlations with human compositionality judgments, with a hybrid APT model (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) achieving ρ up to 0.79.

Main contributions (as assessed):
1) Empirical demonstration that typed, higher-order dependency features within an integrated compositional framework (APTs) substantially outperform untyped window-based representations (including word2vec) for compositionality detection on noun compounds, achieving new state-of-the-art correlation on the Reddy dataset.
2) Introduction and empirical validation of a hybrid APT composition that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APTs, showing complementary benefits and yielding the best performance.
3) A controlled comparison on the same corpus with analyses of composition operators (add vs min), PMI smoothing, and constituent weighting, suggesting robustness and offering insights into when alignment helps.

Strengths:
- Clear empirical gains over strong prior baselines on the canonical dataset. The reported correlations (hybrid APT 0.79; unaligned APT 0.78; aligned APT 0.75) exceed Reddy et al. (0.71) and the best neural baseline (cbow 0.74). The comparison uses the same corpus (ukWaC) across models, improving fairness. The authors discuss statistical significance and provide consistent trends across parameter settings.
- Well-motivated use of syntactic structure. The paper makes a strong case that typed, higher-order dependency paths are informative for compositionality detection, with concrete examples and rationale (e.g., offsetting to align type spaces). This provides interpretability and linguistic plausibility that simple window-based embeddings lack.
- Thoughtful experimental design to mitigate leakage. The corpus is preprocessed to merge compounds into single lexical items; elementary constituent representations do not inherit phrasal features unless observed elsewhere; similarity is computed against observed phrasal vectors using only first-order features, limiting trivial overlap. This design supports the claim that composition predicts phrasal behavior rather than memorizing co-occurrences.
- Analysis of modeling choices. The paper explores PMI smoothing (α), composition operators (⊔UNI vs ⊔INT), computing PPMI before vs after composition, and constituent weighting (h) and mixing (q). The findings (e.g., additive composition generally strongest; α = 0.75 mildly helpful; modifiers slightly more informative than heads; unaligned APTs competitive for NMOD) are useful for practitioners.

Weaknesses:
- Limited evaluation scope. Results are only on one small dataset (90 items) and exclusively on noun–noun compounds. Generalizability to other MWE types (e.g., verb–object, adjective–noun beyond compounds) and larger datasets is not shown. No qualitative error analysis is provided to understand where the method helps or fails (e.g., idioms vs semi-compositional).
- Baseline coverage could be stronger. The neural comparison is limited to word2vec (cbow/skip-gram) with additive composition. Missing baselines include dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg dependency contexts), GloVe, lexical function/tensor models trained on the same corpus, or phrase-level neural models. This weakens the strength of claims that “neural” methods are outperformed, as only a subset is tested.
- Frequency and statistical considerations. Frequency strongly correlates with compositionality in this dataset (FREQ baseline 0.63), but the study does not report partial correlations controlling for frequency or per-frequency-bin performance. The assertion that differences >0.005 are significant lacks details of the testing protocol (e.g., number of repetitions, test used) and confidence intervals; given the small N, this should be more explicit.
- Methodological details and clarity gaps. Some notation and equations are dense; references are incomplete/suppressed in places; Table 4 is referenced but not shown in the provided text; the exact number of cross-validation repetitions is not specified. The computational footprint (dimensions ~80k–230k for APTs) and runtime/space trade-offs relative to embeddings are not discussed, which matters for practicality.
- Sensitivity to parsing and preprocessing. The approach relies on dependency parses (Malt on ukWaC) and specialized preprocessing (merging compounds, ignoring certain modifier-internal dependencies). The impact of parsing errors and of these preprocessing choices on results is not quantified. Alignment underperforms unaligned APTs for NMOD compounds; the explanation and ablation (e.g., path orders, dependency distribution) could be expanded.

Questions to authors:
- How does performance vary with compound frequency? Can you report partial correlations controlling for frequency, or breakdowns by frequency bins, for all models?
- Did you evaluate dependency-based embeddings (e.g., embeddings trained on typed dependency contexts) with additive composition? This seems the closest neural counterpart to APT features.
- What is the computational cost (time/memory) of building and composing APTs at the reported dimensionalities versus training/applying embeddings? Any pruning or sparsity control used?
- How sensitive are results to parsing quality and to the choice of parser? Have you tried alternative parsers or robustness checks?
- Can you provide an error analysis highlighting classes of compounds where APTs help most/least, and examples illustrating the complementary contributions of aligned vs unaligned features?
- How many repetitions were used in cross-validation, and what statistical test supports the “>0.005” significance threshold? Please include confidence intervals or variance estimates.

Overall, the paper presents a technically sound, empirically supported case for typed, higher-order dependency features in compositionality detection and provides a useful hybrid approach. Broadening evaluation and strengthening baselines and statistical reporting would further solidify the contribution.
