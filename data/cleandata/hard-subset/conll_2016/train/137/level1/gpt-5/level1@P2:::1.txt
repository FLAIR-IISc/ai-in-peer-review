Summary
The paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by comparing an integrated compositional distributional approach based on typed higher-order dependency features (anchored packed trees, APTs) against naïve composition (weighted addition) of neural word embeddings. Using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds and ukWaC, the authors construct observed (holistic) phrase representations and predict compositionality as the similarity between composed constituent vectors and holistic phrase vectors. They explore aligned APT composition (with syntactic-path alignment), unaligned APT composition, and a hybrid of the two, and contrast them with CBOW/Skip-gram baselines. The best APT hybrid achieves ρ=0.79, beating the previous state of the art (ρ=0.71) and the best neural baseline (CBOW, ρ=0.74). They also analyze effects of PPMI smoothing, composition operators, and parameter weighting of head/modifier.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that typed higher-order dependency features in a compositional framework (APTs) substantially improve noun–noun compositionality detection over strong untyped embedding baselines and the prior state of the art.
2) Introduction and validation of a hybrid composition strategy that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APT compositions, yielding consistent gains and suggesting complementary signals.
3) Careful experimental analysis of parameter choices (PPMI smoothing, composition operator, head/modifier weights, subsampling for embeddings) that clarifies when and why methods succeed or fail on this task.

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains and solid baselines: The hybrid APT approach reaches ρ=0.79, a sizable and statistically meaningful improvement over Reddy et al. (0.71) and over tuned CBOW (0.74). The inclusion of simple RAND and frequency baselines helps calibrate task difficulty and shows the learning-based methods add value beyond frequency effects.

- Well-motivated use of syntax and higher-order features: The paper convincingly argues that typed, higher-order dependency paths encode the structural cues necessary for composition, especially for modifiers; the success of unaligned and hybrid APTs supports this intuition. The analysis of why intersective composition can fail without alignment (sparse and mismatched feature spaces) is cogent.

- Methodological care to avoid leakage: Recombining compounds into single tokens while ensuring that constituent representations do not inherit compound contexts prevents trivial overlap that would artificially inflate intersective composition performance. This strengthens the validity of the evaluation.

- Transparent ablation and parameter analysis: The exploration of aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid, INT vs UNI composition, PPMI α=1 vs α=0.75, and head/modifier weights (h, q) provides insight. The finding that modifiers contribute slightly more (optimal h, q≈0.3–0.5) and that subsampling harms rare compounds is well reasoned.

- Clarity and replicability of the neural baselines: The word2vec settings (architectures, dimensions, window sizes, subsampling thresholds) are specified, and the observation that CBOW consistently beats Skip-gram on this task is informative.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope and potential overfitting to a small dataset: Results hinge on 90 compounds from a single dataset and corpus (ukWaC). This raises concerns about generality (e.g., other MWEs, other languages, other syntactic constructions). The repeated 3-fold CV provides some robustness but cannot substitute for evaluation on additional datasets.

- Missing comparisons to strong typed/dependency baselines: APT is compared to untyped CBOW/SG; however, dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg, 2014) or other typed/compositional baselines (lexical function models, tensor-based models) could narrow the gap and would better isolate the contribution of the APT design from the benefit of typed contexts.

- Reproducibility gaps: Key APT construction details are deferred to “reference suppressed.” Important implementation choices (path-length limits, vocabulary/feature pruning, smoothing of elementary APTs, thresholding, memory considerations) are not fully specified. No code/resources are released, which may hinder reproduction of the 0.79 result.

- Evaluation design choices may bias in favor of APTs: Similarity is computed against observed phrase vectors restricted to first-order dependency features, while composition uses typed higher-order features—this asymmetry may privilege models that explicitly exploit such typed paths. The choice to select a single “most frequent” dependency relation for compounds that occur under multiple relations could introduce a confound.

- Statistical testing/reporting: The claim that differences >0.005 are significant is based on small standard errors across repeated CV, but the exact test, number of repetitions, and multiple-comparison handling are not detailed. Given the small dataset, careful reporting of variance per model would strengthen the conclusions.

Questions to Authors
- Can you provide fuller details (or an appendix) on APT construction: maximum path order retained, vocabulary/context cutoffs, feature selection/pruning, any smoothing of elementary APTs, and the exact reduction algorithm? Will you release code and the preprocessed corpus indices?

- How sensitive are results to the parser/tagger and to parsing errors in NMOD detection? Did you try alternative dependency parsers or robustness checks?

- Why restrict evaluation similarity to first-order features? Do the conclusions hold if you include higher-order features on the observed side, or if you vary the truncation order?

- How were h and q estimated in CV (grid vs continuous optimization), and are the learned values stable across folds? Could you report per-fold variability?

- Have you compared against dependency-based word embeddings (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) or GloVe trained on dependencies, and/or simple typed additive composition baselines? This would help disentangle the benefit of typed contexts from APT-specific alignment/hybridization.

- For compounds that appear under multiple dependency relations, how would using a mixture over relations (rather than selecting the most frequent) affect performance?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important question with a well-motivated syntactic approach and delivers strong empirical gains with thoughtful analysis. The main limitations are the narrow evaluation, missing comparisons to typed embedding baselines, and reproducibility details. With clarifications and, ideally, an additional dataset or stronger baselines, this would be a solid contribution.
