- Paper compares APT-based typed, higher-order dependency composition (with alignment and a hybrid mix) to naive additive word2vec for noun–noun compositionality detection and reports best APT ρ ≈ 0.79 vs Reddy baseline 0.71 and cbow 0.74 on the 90-item dataset.

Contributions:
- Empirical: Typed, higher-order dependency features via APTs, especially a hybrid aligned/unaligned mix, substantially improve correlation with human judgments for noun–noun compounds.
- Methodological: Clear formulation of alignment in typed dependency spaces using path offsetting/reduction and a tunable hybrid parameter q for mixing aligned and unaligned features.
- Analysis: Systematic exploration of weighting (h), PPMI smoothing (α), and composition operators (min vs add) with interpretable findings (modifier informativeness, unaligned features useful for NMOD, cbow > skip-gram, subsampling harms rare compounds).

Strengths:
- Strong empirical gains with thorough ablation studies and explicit significance testing showing meaningful improvements over baselines.
- Effective integration of syntax via APT alignment to address space-mismatch for intersective composition, well-motivated for NMOD compounds.
- Careful corpus preprocessing (collapsing compound tokens) to reduce leakage and keep the task faithful to composing unseen phrases.
- Insightful parameter analyses providing generalizable guidance (optimal α ≈ 0.75, sensible h and q ranges, cautions on subsampling).
- Clear task framing linking compositionality detection to broader compositional model evaluation and downstream use cases.

Weaknesses:
- Limited baselines using dependency/typed embeddings or typed count vectors, so gains may conflate typed-context benefits with APT-specific alignment/higher-order features.
- Small 90-item dataset risks overfitting/optimism despite CV and global tuning; significance claims may be inflated.
- Evaluation protocol fairness concerns: both composed and observed vectors come from same corpus with different representation pipelines, complicating attribution of gains.
- Narrow scope focused on noun–noun (mostly NMOD) compounds; lack of other constructions reduces generalizability of the “integrated models are better” claim.
- Reproducibility issues: several hyperparameters and preprocessing details are underspecified and a referenced prior is suppressed, limiting replication.

Questions to authors:
- Can you add a dependency-based embedding baseline (e.g., Levy & Goldberg) and/or a typed PPMI additive baseline to isolate APT alignment/higher-order effects?
- Exactly how was cross-validation conducted: were APTs/embeddings trained once on the full corpus and only h, q tuned, or were any corpus-derived choices fold-specific?
- What are the post-collapsing frequency distributions of the 90 compounds, how many are very rare, and how do results change if rare compounds are excluded?
- Can you report per-relation and per-compound analyses (e.g., NMOD vs others, idiomatic vs compositional) to show where alignment helps or hurts?
- How sensitive are results to parser errors; did you try filtering low-confidence parses or alternative parsers?
- Did you try using second-order features at evaluation time or back-off strategies to better leverage higher-order signals?
- Please provide missing reproducibility details (vocab thresholds, feature pruning, negative sampling k, learning rates, epochs, seeds, exact CV protocol).
