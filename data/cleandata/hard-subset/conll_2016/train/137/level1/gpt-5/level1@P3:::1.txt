Summary and claimed contributions
This paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds via distributional composition. It contrasts an “integrated” compositional framework based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) that encode typed, higher-order dependency paths and perform alignment (via path prefixing and reduction) before composition, against naïve additive composition on neural embeddings (word2vec). Using the Reddy et al. (2011) 90-compound dataset, the authors report improved correlations with human compositionality judgments, with the best APT “hybrid” model achieving ρ ≈ 0.79, outperforming both the Reddy baseline (0.71) and their best cbow additive baseline (0.74).

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first)
1) Empirical finding: Typed, higher-order dependency features (via APTs), particularly in a hybrid mixing aligned and unaligned composition, substantially improve correlation with human compositionality judgments for noun–noun compounds relative to strong count-based and word2vec additive baselines.

2) Methodological contribution: A clear formulation of alignment for composition in typed dependency spaces using path offsetting with reduction, and a hybrid mixture (parameter q) of aligned and unaligned features tailored to NMOD compounds.

3) Analysis/insights: Careful exploration of weighting (h), PPMI smoothing (α), and composition operators (min vs add), yielding interpretable conclusions (e.g., modifiers slightly more informative than heads; unaligned typed features informative for NMOD; cbow > skip-gram; subsampling harms rare compounds).

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains and ablations: The paper systematically compares aligned, unaligned, and hybrid APT composition, different weighting schemes (counts vs PPMI α ∈ {1, 0.75}), and two composition operators, then contrasts to word2vec cbow/skip-gram with multiple dimensions and subsampling thresholds. The reported best APT result (ρ ≈ 0.79) is a meaningful improvement over the Reddy baseline (0.71) and the best cbow configuration (0.74), with an explicit significance criterion.

- Integration of syntax into distributional composition: The APT alignment via dependency-path prefixing and reduction addresses the space-mismatch problem across POS and relational roles, a core barrier for intersective operations. The hybrid approach is well motivated for NMOD compounds where unaligned first-order overlaps are expected.

- Careful corpus preprocessing to reduce leakage: Collapsing compound occurrences into single tokens for observed phrase vectors while keeping constituents’ elementary APTs free of those compound contexts mitigates trivial intersective wins and keeps the task faithful to “composing unseen phrases.”

- Insightful analysis of parameter effects: The observed benefits of α = 0.75 PPMI smoothing, the harms of shifted PMI and aggressive subsampling in the low-frequency regime of compounds, and the balanced optimal h, q ∈ [0.3, 0.5] provide useful, generalizable guidance for future work on compositionality detection.

- Clear task framing and linkage to broader goals: The paper rightly argues for compositionality detection as a testbed for compositional models and suggests downstream uses (e.g., choosing when to smooth observed phrasal vectors vs constituent vectors).

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines for typed/dependency representations: The main comparison for neural models uses untyped cbow/skip-gram. A more direct test of “syntax helps” would include dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg dependency word2vec) or typed count vectors without APT alignment/higher-order paths. As-is, gains may conflate the benefit of typed contexts with the benefit of APT-specific alignment and higher-order features.

- Small dataset and potential for overfitting/optimism: The Reddy dataset has only 90 items. Though the authors use repeated 3-fold CV and keep h and q global, even small tuning on such a small set can inflate significance estimates (differences ≥ 0.005 claimed significant). A second dataset (e.g., larger noun compound compositionality sets, or phrasal verbs) would strengthen claims.

- Evaluation protocol comparability and fairness: The authors preprocess ukWaC to insert compound tokens, then evaluate similarity between composed representations and these observed compound vectors. While they attempt to prevent leakage into constituent APTs, both composed and observed vectors are learned from the same corpus, and typed APTs benefit from dependency parsing while word2vec uses proximity windows. This makes it hard to disentangle modeling from input representation advantages. Reporting a strong GloVe baseline, a window-based PPMI baseline with weighted add, and a dependency-typed count baseline would clarify incremental gains.

- Scope and generalization: The study focuses exclusively on noun–noun (mostly NMOD) compounds. The paper discusses broader potential (e.g., adjectives, verb–object), but no experiments are presented for other constructions, where alignment should matter more. The claim that “integrated compositional models are better” would be more convincing with at least one additional construction.

- Reproducibility details: Some critical hyperparameters and preprocessing choices are underspecified (e.g., vocabulary thresholds, feature pruning for APTs, negative sampling k for word2vec, learning rates, number of epochs, random seeds, exact CV protocol). Reference to “reference suppressed” for prior APT work further limits replicability within a double-blind context.

Questions to authors
- Can you include a dependency-based embedding baseline (e.g., Levy & Goldberg’s dependency word2vec) and/or a typed PPMI baseline with simple weighted addition but without APT alignment? This would help isolate the contribution of APT alignment and higher-order features from typed context alone.

- How exactly was cross-validation conducted? Were APTs/embeddings trained once on the full corpus and only h, q tuned per fold, or were there any fold-specific corpus-derived choices? Please detail how you prevented any tuning on test items beyond h, q selection.

- What are the frequency distributions of the 90 compounds post-collapsing? How many compounds have very low counts, and how sensitive are results to excluding rare compounds?

- Can you report per-relation (e.g., NMOD vs other relations) and per-compound analyses (e.g., highly idiomatic vs highly compositional) to illustrate where alignment helps/hurts?

- How sensitive are results to parse errors? You note parser mistakes in multi-noun sequences; did you try filtering sentences with low parser confidence or re-parsing with a more accurate parser?

- Did you explore using second-order features at evaluation time (with smoothing) or back-off strategies to leverage higher-order signals more fully?

Overall, the paper offers a solid and well-argued case that typed dependency-based composition, particularly with the APT alignment/hybrid strategy, improves compositionality detection for noun compounds. Strengthening the baseline suite (typed embeddings) and broadening the evaluation would make the contribution more definitive.
