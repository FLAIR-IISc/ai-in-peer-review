Summary and contributions
This paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by comparing: (i) an integrated, typed-dependency compositional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) that explicitly encodes higher-order dependency paths and supports alignment (offset + reduction) before composition; and (ii) naïve pointwise addition over neural embeddings (word2vec). Using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds, the authors report substantial gains over prior work and neural baselines, with a best Spearman ρ of 0.79 for a hybrid APT composition versus 0.74 for CBOW and 0.71 for the original (untyped) count-based baseline.

As I see them, the main contributions (strongest first):
1) Empirical evidence that typed, higher-order dependency features in an integrated compositional model (APTs) substantially improve compound compositionality detection over both (a) untyped count-based models and (b) strong neural baselines trained on the same corpus.
2) A hybrid composition strategy combining aligned and unaligned APT features, showing complementary benefits and delivering the best performance.
3) A careful comparison of composition operators (union vs intersection), weighting (h, q), and PPMI smoothing (α), with insights about why subsampling/shifted PMI can hurt low-frequency compound representations.

Strengths
- Clear, consistent empirical gains over strong baselines: The hybrid APT achieves ρ=0.79, outperforming both the best neural baseline (CBOW ρ=0.74) and the Reddy et al. (2011) weighted-addition baseline (ρ=0.71). The study uses the same ukWaC corpus to reduce confounds and includes reasonable parameter exploration for both APT and word2vec, lending credibility to the comparison.

- Well-motivated modeling of typed, higher-order contexts: The APT framework encodes full dependency paths and uses alignment (offset + reduction) to put constituents in the head’s type space before composition, addressing the classic mismatch in feature spaces across POS categories. The hybrid result suggests aligned and unaligned features are complementary, which is theoretically plausible for NMOD compounds and supported empirically by small but consistent gains.

- Thorough ablations and informative analysis: The paper explores union vs intersection composition, PPMI vs counts-with-PPMI-after-composition, and α-smoothing. It reports that union generally outperforms intersection, hybrid > unaligned > aligned, and α=0.75 helps slightly. The observation that modifier weight h and unaligned weight q sit in [0.3,0.5] is insightful: the modifier and unaligned (typed) features are slightly more informative for this task. The discussion on why shifted PMI and aggressive subsampling can hurt low-frequency compounds is useful and consistent with prior findings.

- Fair treatment of neural baselines within a constrained setting: The authors train CBOW and skip-gram on ukWaC, sweep dimensionality and subsampling thresholds, and find CBOW clearly superior. This reduces the risk that APT’s gains are due to corpus mismatch.

- Careful corpus preprocessing to avoid leakage: Compound occurrences are collapsed into single tokens and constituent representations are prevented from reusing those compound contexts, making the composition test more faithful (i.e., predicting the compound from its parts without direct overlap).

Weaknesses
- Very limited evaluation set: All results are on the 90-item Reddy dataset. This is small, and the reported “errors ≤ 0.0015” and the claim that differences >0.005 are significant at 95% are not convincing without a clear statistical testing protocol. With n=90 (and testing folds of ~30 items), variance in ρ is typically nontrivial. Stronger validation (e.g., permutation tests, confidence intervals, bootstrap over items, partial correlations) is needed, and evaluation on additional datasets (e.g., larger compound collections, MWEs) would improve generality.

- Incomplete baseline coverage for neural/embedding approaches: The comparison omits dependency-based embeddings (e.g., embeddings trained on typed dependencies), phrase-aware embeddings, or GloVe. Given the central claim that typed contexts matter, a dependency-based embedding baseline is essential. Limiting composition to simple addition for embeddings may also understate their potential (e.g., Hadamard, learned composition, holographic/tensor methods). As a result, the superiority of APTs over “neural” approaches may be overstated.

- Novelty is primarily in application and analysis: The APT machinery (alignment via offset and reduction; higher-order typed features) appears to have been introduced previously (“reference suppressed”). The paper’s main novelty is applying it to compositionality detection with a hybrid alignment strategy. This is valuable but incremental methodologically.

- Missing methodological details hinder reproducibility and interpretation: Several implementation choices are unspecified or only sketched: feature pruning thresholds, min counts for contexts and compounds, negative sampling/epochs/min_count for word2vec, handling of parsing errors beyond brief remarks, exact order limits used during composition vs evaluation, and statistical testing procedures. The references list is also incomplete. The paper would benefit from releasing code or a more precise recipe.

- Potential frequency confound is not controlled: Frequency correlates strongly with compositionality in this dataset (FREQ baseline ρ=0.63). While cosine reduces norm effects, models might still indirectly exploit frequency. A partial correlation controlling for frequency, or stratified evaluation by frequency bands, would strengthen the claim that gains come from better modeling of compositionality rather than frequency proxies.

Questions to authors
- Significance and variance: How exactly are the “errors ≤ 0.0015” computed, and what test justifies the 0.005 threshold for significance at 95%? Please report per-fold/per-item variance (e.g., CIs via bootstrap) and, if possible, permutation tests over rankings.

- Dependency-based embedding baselines: Did you try dependency-based word embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014) or other typed-context embeddings? If not, can you comment on expected performance vs APTs?

- Neural training details: What min_count, negative sampling k, number of epochs, and learning rate schedule were used for word2vec? Did you try phrase-aware training or retrofitting compounds to have dedicated vectors?

- APT configuration: What frequency cutoffs/pruning were applied to typed paths and context vocabulary? Up to which order were paths included in elementary vs composed APTs? Any smoothing/backoff besides PMI? Can you release code or a parameter file?

- Frequency control: How do results change when controlling for frequency (partial correlation) or when evaluating on frequency-stratified subsets? How many compounds are low-frequency, and how robust are per-item errors there?

- Constituent-level literality: The dataset includes judgments for each constituent. Can your approach predict constituent literality as well, and if so, how does it perform?

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper with a well-motivated integrated model and convincing gains on the target task. Addressing the evaluation scope, statistical testing, and baseline coverage would considerably strengthen the submission.
