Summary: The paper investigates compositionality detection for compound nouns by contrasting an integrated compositional distributional approach based on typed, higher-order dependency features (Anchored Packed Trees, APTs) with naive additive composition of neural embeddings. Using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds with human compositionality judgments, the authors show that APT-based composition—particularly a hybrid of aligned and unaligned representations—yields higher Spearman correlations with human scores than CBOW/SG word2vec baselines, achieving up to ρ = 0.79 vs. 0.74 for CBOW and 0.71 for the previous state of the art.

Contributions (as I see them):
- Primary: Empirical evidence that typed, higher-order dependency features support compositionality detection substantially better than untyped embedding-based composition on the Reddy benchmark, with a new SOTA (ρ = 0.79).
- Secondary: A hybrid composition scheme that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APTs, revealing complementary signals between offset (structure-aware) and direct (structure-agnostic) modifier features.
- Tertiary: Careful experimental design to avoid leakage (compounds re-tokenized; constituent contexts separated), plus diagnostics on PPMI smoothing, subsampling, and constituent weighting that illuminate when and why intersective vs. additive operations succeed.

Strengths:
- Strong quantitative gains with clear evidence: The hybrid APT method improves ρ from 0.71 (Reddy) and 0.74 (best CBOW) to 0.79, with small error bars and a clear significance threshold (>0.005).
- Methodological rigor: The preprocessing to treat compounds as single tokens while preventing contextual overlap with constituents is thoughtful and crucial for a fair test of composition.
- Insightful analysis: Systematic ablations (aligned vs. unaligned vs. hybrid, ⊔INT vs. ⊔UNI, PPMI α) and the observation that modifiers weigh slightly more than heads (optimal h, q ∈ [0.3, 0.5]) offer interpretable takeaways.
- Clear positioning against strong baselines: Includes word2vec with parameter sweeps and frequency baselines; argues convincingly that typed paths add information beyond untyped co-occurrences.

Weaknesses:
- Limited scope: Evaluation is confined to 90 noun compounds from a single corpus; generalization to other constructions (V–N, Adj–N, phrasal verbs) and datasets is untested.
- Baseline coverage: No comparison to dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg) or GloVe; learned composition functions for embeddings are not explored.
- APT novelty vs. prior work: The core APT idea appears in earlier work (“reference suppressed” / Weeds et al., 2014); the novelty here is primarily the task/application and hybridization.
- Parser/noise sensitivity: Results rest on automatic dependency parses; robustness to parsing errors and to low-frequency compounds (where shifted PMI/subsampling fail) is only qualitatively discussed.

Questions to Authors:
- How does APT compare to dependency-based word2vec or GloVe? Could these close the gap to the hybrid APT?
- Do results hold on other compositionality datasets or phrase types (e.g., Reddy-style compounds in different domains, V–O idioms)?
- How sensitive are gains to parse quality and to the maximum path order? Any ablation on order-1 vs. order-2 only?
- Can you release code/preprocessing to ensure reproducibility of the leakage-avoidance steps?
