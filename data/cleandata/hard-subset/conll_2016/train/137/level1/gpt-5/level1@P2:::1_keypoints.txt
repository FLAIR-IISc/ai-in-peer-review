- Paper studies noun–noun compositionality detection using typed higher-order dependency features (APTs) vs naïve neural embedding composition, evaluating on Reddy et al. (2011) (90 compounds) with ukWaC and reporting best hybrid APT ρ=0.79.  
- Contribution: shows typed higher-order dependency features in a compositional APT framework substantially improve compositionality detection over untyped embedding baselines and prior SOTA.  
- Contribution: introduces and validates a hybrid composition strategy combining aligned and unaligned APTs, yielding consistent gains and suggesting complementary signals.  
- Contribution: provides careful experimental analysis of parameter choices (PPMI smoothing, composition operators, head/modifier weights, subsampling) clarifying method behavior.  
- Strength: clear empirical gains with solid baselines and simple RAND/frequency controls, demonstrating meaningful improvements (ρ from 0.71/0.74 to 0.79).  
- Strength: well-motivated use of syntax and higher-order features, with analysis supporting why alignment and typed paths help composition.  
- Strength: methodological care to avoid leakage by recombining compounds while preventing constituent contamination of contexts.  
- Strength: transparent ablation and parameter sweeps (aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid, INT vs UNI, PPMI α, head/modifier weights) offering insight into what works.  
- Strength: neural baseline settings are clearly reported and CBOW vs Skip-gram comparison is informative.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to 90 compounds from one dataset/corpus (ukWaC), raising generality and overfitting concerns despite 3-fold CV.  
- Weakness: missing comparisons to strong typed/dependency baselines (e.g., dependency-based embeddings, lexical function or tensor models) that could isolate APT-specific gains.  
- Weakness: reproducibility gaps—key APT implementation details deferred and no code/resources released, hindering exact reproduction of results.  
- Weakness: evaluation asymmetry—observed phrase vectors use first-order features while composition uses higher-order features, potentially biasing results; single most-frequent relation choice may confound.  
- Weakness: statistical testing/reporting insufficiently detailed (exact tests, repetitions, multiple-comparison handling) given small dataset and small reported differences.  
- Question: please provide full APT construction details (max path order, pruning, smoothing, reduction algorithm) and release code/preprocessed indices.  
- Question: how sensitive are results to parser/tagger choice and parsing errors for NMOD detection; were alternative parsers or robustness checks tried?  
- Question: why restrict observed similarity to first-order features, and do results hold when including higher-order features or varying truncation order?  
- Question: how were head/modifier weights (h, q) estimated in CV (grid vs continuous) and are learned values stable across folds—please report per-fold variability.  
- Question: have you compared to dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg) or simple typed additive baselines to disentangle typed-context benefits from APT-specific effects?  
- Question: for compounds with multiple dependency relations, would using a mixture over relations instead of the most frequent relation affect performance?  
- Overall: well-motivated syntactic approach with strong empirical gains and thoughtful analysis, but limited evaluation scope, missing stronger typed baselines, and reproducibility details need addressing for a solid contribution.
