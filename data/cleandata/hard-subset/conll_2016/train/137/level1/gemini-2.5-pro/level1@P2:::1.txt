### Summary of the paper

This paper investigates the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, framing it as a benchmark for evaluating compositional distributional semantic models. The authors propose a novel approach using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which are sparse, high-dimensional vector representations whose features consist of typed, higher-order dependency paths. A key aspect of their compositional model is the "alignment" of constituent vectors by systematically transforming their features based on the syntactic dependency path connecting them. This integrated, structure-aware approach is compared against the common ad-hoc method of applying simple weighted addition to state-of-the-art dense neural word embeddings (word2vec). Experiments are conducted on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. The results show that the APT-based models significantly outperform both the previous state-of-the-art and a strong, well-tuned neural embedding baseline, establishing a new benchmark for the task.

### Main contributions

1.  **A Novel, Structurally-Aware Compositional Model (APT):** The primary contribution is the application of the APT framework to compositionality detection. This model moves beyond simple vector arithmetic by incorporating explicit syntactic structure into the composition process itself. The mechanism of "aligning" vector spaces by offsetting features based on dependency paths is a principled and novel way to handle composition for typed, dependency-based representations.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The paper presents a new state-of-the-art result on the widely used Reddy et al. (2011) noun compound compositionality dataset. The proposed hybrid APT model achieves a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, a substantial improvement over the previous SOTA (0.71). Crucially, this result also surpasses a strong, optimized neural embedding baseline (0.74) that the authors implemented, providing compelling evidence for their model's effectiveness.
3.  **Insightful Comparison of Compositional Approaches:** The work provides a valuable direct comparison between an integrated compositional model that leverages rich, typed syntactic features (APT) and an ad-hoc model using generic, untyped dense embeddings (word2vec). The analysis highlights the benefits of explicit syntactic information for this fine-grained semantic task.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Methodological Foundation:** The proposed APT model is linguistically and formally well-motivated. Unlike many popular methods that treat composition as a post-hoc operation on pre-trained vectors, the APT framework integrates composition as a core part of the model. The idea of aligning feature spaces based on syntactic structure is an elegant solution to the problem that constituents in a phrase exist in different distributional spaces (e.g., an adjective's features differ from a noun's). This provides a principled alternative to simple commutative operations like vector addition.
2.  **Rigorous and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental design is excellent. The authors ensure a fair comparison to prior work by using the same corpus (ukWaC). They establish a very strong baseline by carefully tuning a word2vec model, which itself beats the previous state-of-the-art. This makes the subsequent gains from their own model more significant and convincing. The use of repeated cross-validation for parameter tuning and the systematic comparison of different model variants (aligned, unaligned, hybrid; additive vs. intersective) demonstrate experimental rigor and provide valuable insights into what components contribute to the model's success.
3.  **Clear and Significant Improvements:** The results are unambiguous and demonstrate a large performance gain on a standard benchmark. The improvement from the previous SOTA of 0.71 to 0.79 is substantial. The fact that the APT model also comfortably outperforms a strong, optimized neural baseline (0.74 vs. 0.79) makes a compelling case for the value of structure-aware, explicit vector space models for certain semantic tasks.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The introduction clearly situates the work in the context of previous research. The APT model, though complex, is explained well with concrete examples in Section 3 and Table 1. The experiments and results are presented transparently, allowing the reader to easily grasp the key takeaways.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper's claims are based on experiments on a single, fairly small dataset of 90 noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard benchmark for this specific task, it is unclear how the APT model's superiority would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object) or other compositional tasks (e.g., phrase similarity, paraphrase detection). The paper itself notes that simple addition of neural embeddings is a very strong baseline on other tasks, so demonstrating the APT model's effectiveness more broadly would significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.
2.  **Scalability and Sparsity Concerns are Not Addressed:** The APT model uses high-dimensional, sparse vectors (up to 230,000 dimensions for nouns). This approach can suffer from data sparsity and may have high computational and memory costs compared to dense models. The paper does not discuss these practical aspects. For instance, how does the model's performance degrade with less frequent words? The authors note that shifted PPMI hurts performance due to low-frequency compounds, which hints at sparsity issues, but a more thorough discussion of the model's robustness and efficiency would be welcome.
3.  **Insufficient Justification for the Hybrid Model's Success:** The best-performing model is a hybrid that linearly interpolates between the aligned and unaligned representations of the modifier. Surprisingly, the purely unaligned model (ρ=0.78) performs much better than the purely aligned model (ρ=0.75), which seems to partially undermine the central motivation for alignment. The paper offers only a brief explanation that the two models capture different predictive contexts. A deeper analysis is needed to explain why the unaligned features (i.e., the modifier's standard first-order dependency contexts) are so effective for this specific task, and what complementary information the alignment process provides.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The strong performance of the unaligned model component was surprising. Could you provide more intuition or an error analysis on why the modifier's unaligned, first-order features are so predictive for noun-noun compositionality? Does this suggest that for this particular construction, simply knowing the modifier's typical distributional neighbors is a stronger signal than the more complex context prediction offered by alignment?
2.  Regarding preprocessing (Section 5), you state that when creating the representation for the compound token, "dependency paths including the modifying constituent are ignored." For a phrase like "(recently graduated) student", this would mean information about "recently" is discarded. What was the rationale for this design choice? It seems plausible that the contexts of the modifier could also be relevant to the composed meaning.
3.  Could you comment on the computational trade-offs of the APT model versus the word2vec baseline? Specifically, how do they compare in terms of time and memory required for training the representations and for computing the composed vectors?
