- Addresses detection of compositionality for noun-noun compounds using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) with syntactic alignment, achieving ρ=0.79 and outperforming prior best (0.71) and a tuned word2vec baseline.  
- Contribution: new state-of-the-art result on the Reddy et al. (2011) noun-compound benchmark (Spearman’s ρ = 0.79).  
- Contribution: systematic analysis of typed syntactic features showing that a hybrid of aligned and unaligned representations works best.  
- Strength: strong empirical performance with rigorous, fair evaluation and a strong tuned baseline.  
- Strength: principled, linguistically motivated model that encodes rich typed dependency information and syntactic alignment.  
- Strength: insightful ablation comparing aligned, unaligned, and hybrid variants, revealing complementary information sources.  
- Weakness: limited scope — evaluation is only on a small 90-item English N-N dataset, raising generalizability concerns.  
- Weakness: core method (offsetting/reduction) is described densely and needs a clear, worked example to aid understanding.  
- Weakness: counter-intuitive result that unaligned > aligned lacks deeper analysis or explanation of why this occurs.  
- Weakness: parameter tuning (h and q) via 3-fold CV on 90 items may be unstable and prone to overfitting; sensitivity analysis is missing.  
- Question: request interpretation of why unaligned APT outperforms aligned APT — does this mean modifier first-order contexts are more predictive?  
- Question: ask for statistical significance tests comparing hybrid vs. unaligned and unaligned vs. CBOW on the 90 items.  
- Question: ask how the APT alignment mechanism would apply to other constructions (e.g., V–O) and whether the balance of aligned vs. unaligned features would change.
