- Summary: Proposes Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), a high-dimensional sparse, dependency-typed compositional model with an "offsetting" alignment operation, and achieves new SOTA on noun-compound compositionality (Spearman 0.79) versus word2vec baselines.

- Contribution 1: Introduces the APT framework that integrates typed, higher-order dependency paths into vector representations and composition via path offsetting.

- Contribution 2: Demonstrates strong empirical gains on the Reddy et al. benchmark, improving correlation from 0.71 to 0.79 and providing a well-tuned word2vec baseline.

- Contribution 3: Offers an empirical analysis showing unaligned typed features are surprisingly effective and that a hybrid of aligned+unaligned features performs best.

- Strength 1: Methodologically novel and rigorous—extends dependency-based models with higher-order paths and a principled, structure-sensitive composition operator.

- Strength 2: Thorough experimental setup—uses the same ukWaC corpus as prior work, sensible preprocessing, cross-validated parameter search, and strong baselines.

- Strength 3: Clear, significant empirical improvement that evidences the benefit of encoding typed dependency information for this task.

- Weakness 1: Limited evaluation scope—results are based on a single small dataset (90 compounds), leaving generalization to other constructions, tasks, languages, and noisy parses uncertain.

- Weakness 2: Insufficient interpretation of the hybrid result—paper lacks deeper qualitative analysis explaining why unaligned contexts sometimes outperform aligned ones.

- Weakness 3: Scalability and practicality concerns—very high-dimensional sparse vectors (~230k dims) with no discussion of memory/runtime trade-offs or potential dense-factorization approaches.

- Question 1: Request for qualitative analysis comparing salient features from unaligned vs. aligned modifier vectors on compositional and non-compositional examples.

- Question 2: How would the offsetting mechanism generalize to more complex structures (e.g., prepositional phrases, coordination)?

- Question 3: The modifier-weighting finding (h > head weight): does this align with linguistic theory and what is the authors' intuition for why modifiers dominate in this model?
