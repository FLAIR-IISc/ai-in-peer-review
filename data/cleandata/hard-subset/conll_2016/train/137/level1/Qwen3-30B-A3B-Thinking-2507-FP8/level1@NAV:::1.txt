

This paper investigates compositionality detection in noun compounds using an integrated compositional distributional approach with sparse high-dimensional representations (APT) versus the common practice of applying naive composition operations to neural embeddings. The main contributions, in order of strength, are: (1) demonstration that typed dependency paths with higher-order features significantly improve compositionality detection, achieving 0.79 Spearman correlation (vs. 0.71 previously); (2) discovery that both aligned and unaligned APT compositions contribute to better performance, suggesting different contextual information types are valuable; and (3) showing that optimized neural embeddings (cbow with 100 dimensions) outperform previous baselines but remain inferior to the APT approach.

Strengths: The paper presents compelling empirical evidence of significant improvement over previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71) with statistically significant results across multiple configurations. The careful experimental design comparing aligned vs. unaligned APT, different composition operations, and parameter settings provides valuable insights into what makes compositionality detection work. The paper's focus on compositionality detection rather than phrase similarity evaluation avoids confounding issues with compositionality, offering a more principled testbed for compositional methods.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks sufficient explanation for why APT outperforms neural embeddings - the claim that "syntactic structure is crucial" needs stronger theoretical justification beyond empirical results. The work is limited to noun compounds without exploring generalizability to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases), which weakens the broader contribution claim. The computational complexity of the APT approach (230k dimensions for 2nd-order paths) is not discussed, making it unclear how practical this approach would be for real-world applications.

Questions: How would your approach perform on non-noun compound phrases like verb phrases (e.g., "to eat")? What are the computational constraints of your APT approach compared to neural embeddings, and how would you address them for larger-scale applications?
