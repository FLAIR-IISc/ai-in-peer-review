

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary
This paper investigates compositionality detection for noun compounds using compositional distributional models. The authors propose an integrated compositional approach using anchored packed trees (APTs) with typed higher-order dependency paths, and compare it with the standard approach of applying naive composition operations to neural word embeddings. The main contributions, as I see them, are: (1) demonstrating that typed dependency features with higher-order paths significantly improve compositionality detection over previous methods, (2) showing that a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned APT representations yields the best results, and (3) establishing a new state-of-the-art performance on the Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality detection dataset.

## Strengths
1. **Significant Performance Improvement**: The paper demonstrates a clear and meaningful improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.79 vs. 0.71 Spearman's correlation). This represents one of the most substantial improvements in the field for this specific task, and the results are statistically significant across multiple experimental configurations.

2. **Rigorous Experimental Design**: The authors follow the experimental setup of Reddy et al. (2011) closely, making their comparison fair and meaningful. They provide thorough analysis of different composition operations (aligned vs. unaligned, PPMI vs. normalized counts) and parameter settings (h and q), which adds substantial value to the field by clarifying which design choices matter most for compositionality detection.

3. **Strong Motivation and Problem Statement**: The paper provides a compelling rationale for why compositionality detection is important (non-compositional phrases are integral to natural language, and compositionality exists on a continuum), making the evaluation task both theoretically sound and practically relevant. This contrasts with many studies that evaluate compositional methods against human similarity judgments without accounting for compositionality.

4. **Insight into Syntactic Structure**: The paper provides valuable evidence that syntactic structure (via typed dependency paths) is highly informative for compositionality detection. This has important implications for the field, suggesting that incorporating syntactic knowledge into distributional models may be crucial for fine-grained semantic understanding.

5. **Practical Implications**: The authors thoughtfully discuss how compositionality detection could lead to improved compositional methods (e.g., by smoothing observed phrasal representations for non-compositional phrases rather than their constituents), suggesting a promising direction for future work.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of Explanation for Why Typed Features Work**: The paper states that "the dependency paths encoded in the typed contextual features are highly informative" but doesn't provide a clear linguistic or computational explanation for this phenomenon. Without this explanation, it's difficult to understand whether the improvement is due to the specific structure of the APT model or simply the use of more features.

2. **Limited Comparison with Recent Methods**: The paper doesn't compare with more recent compositional methods beyond those mentioned in the related work. For example, they don't compare with Hashimoto et al. (2014) or Hermann et al. (2012), which incorporated syntactic structure into neural models and achieved strong results. This weakens the claim that their approach is state-of-the-art.

3. **No Discussion of Computational Cost**: The APT model uses high-dimensional sparse vectors (80,000-230,000 dimensions), while neural embeddings use only 50-300 dimensions. The paper doesn't address the computational cost of their approach, which is a critical practical consideration for real-world applications.

4. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds, but compositionality is a general phenomenon. The authors don't discuss how their approach would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adverbial phrases), which limits the broader applicability of their findings.

5. **Insufficient Implementation Details**: The paper lacks sufficient detail on the implementation of the APT model, making it difficult to replicate their results or understand the exact nature of their contribution. For instance, the exact method for constructing the APT lexicon and handling the dependency paths isn't fully explained.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims that "the dependency paths encoded in the typed contextual features are highly informative" for compositionality detection but doesn't explain why. Could you provide a linguistic or computational explanation for this phenomenon? What specific linguistic properties of the dependency paths contribute to this improved performance?

2. The paper doesn't compare with more recent compositional methods that incorporate syntactic structure (e.g., Hashimoto et al., 2014; Hermann et al., 2012). How do your results compare with these approaches? Did you consider including them in your evaluation?

3. The APT model uses high-dimensional sparse vectors (80,000-230,000 dimensions) compared to the neural embedding approach (50-300 dimensions). How does the computational cost of your approach compare to the neural embedding approach? Would your method be practical for large-scale applications?

4. The paper focuses exclusively on noun compounds. How do you expect your approach to generalize to other types of phrases (e.g., verb phrases, adverbial phrases)? Have you considered testing on other phrase types, and if so, what were the results?
