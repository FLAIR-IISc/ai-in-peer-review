- Proposes an APT-based compositional model using typed higher-order dependency paths for noun-compound compositionality detection.
- Demonstrates typed dependency features with higher-order paths materially outperform naive neural embedding composition.
- Finds a hybrid (aligned + unaligned) APT representation gives best results and reports new SOTA on Reddy et al. (2011) (Spearman 0.79 vs 0.71).

- Significant performance improvement over prior work, with statistically robust gains.
- Rigorous experimental design following Reddy et al. (2011) and extensive analysis of composition ops and parameters.
- Clear motivation: compositionality is a continuum and important to evaluate separately from similarity judgments.
- Provides evidence that syntactic structure (typed dependency paths) is highly informative for compositionality detection.
- Discusses practical implications and directions for improving compositional methods for non-compositional phrases.

- Fails to explain why typed dependency features work linguistically or computationally versus merely adding features.
- Limited comparison to recent syntax-aware compositional methods (e.g., Hashimoto et al. 2014; Hermann et al. 2012), weakening SOTA claim.
- Omits discussion of computational cost despite using very high-dimensional sparse APT vectors versus low-dim neural embeddings.
- Evaluation limited to noun compounds; no assessment or discussion of generalization to other phrase types.
- Insufficient implementation detail on APT construction and dependency-path handling, hindering reproducibility.

- What linguistic or computational properties of dependency paths make them so informative for compositionality detection?
- How do your results compare to more recent syntax-aware compositional models (e.g., Hashimoto et al., Hermann et al.) and were they considered for evaluation?
- How does the computational cost and scalability of the high-dimensional APT model compare to low-dimensional neural embeddings for large-scale use?
- Can the approach generalize to other phrase types (verb/adverbial phrases), and have you tested or plan to test this?
