- Key contribution: Typed dependency paths with higher-order features substantially improve noun-compound compositionality detection, achieving 0.79 Spearman (vs. 0.71 prior).
- Key contribution: Both aligned and unaligned APT compositions provide complementary contextual information and boost performance.
- Key contribution: Optimized neural embeddings (cbow, 100d) beat older baselines but still fall short of the APT approach.
- Strength: Strong empirical improvement over prior state-of-the-art with statistically significant results across configurations.
- Strength: Careful experimental design comparing aligned vs. unaligned APT, multiple composition ops, and parameter settings yields useful insights.
- Strength: Focus on compositionality detection (not phrase similarity) provides a more principled evaluation for compositional methods.
- Weakness: Paper lacks theoretical explanation for why APT outperforms neural embeddings; the "syntax is crucial" claim needs stronger justification.
- Weakness: Evaluation is limited to noun compounds, leaving generalizability to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases) untested.
- Weakness: Computational complexity of APT (e.g., ~230k dimensions for 2nd-order paths) is not addressed, raising practical scalability concerns.
- Question: How does the approach perform on non-noun phrase types such as verb phrases (e.g., "to eat")?
- Question: What are the computational constraints of APT versus neural embeddings, and how would the method scale to larger applications?
