

# Peer Review

**Summary and Contributions:**  
The paper investigates compositionality detection for noun compounds, comparing an integrated compositional distributional approach (using anchored packed trees with typed higher-order dependency paths) against the standard "adhoc" approach of applying simple composition operations to neural embeddings. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that an integrated compositional model with typed dependency paths significantly outperforms previous methods (Spearman's ρ = 0.79 vs. 0.71), (2) showing that syntactic structure encoded in dependency paths is crucial for accurate compositionality detection, and (3) introducing a hybrid composition method that combines aligned and unaligned representations for optimal performance.

**Strengths:**  
1. **Compelling evidence for syntactic structure's importance:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that incorporating syntactic structure via typed dependency paths is essential for compositionality detection. The APT approach (0.79 ρ) significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art (0.71 ρ), with the largest gains coming from using typed, higher-order dependency paths rather than untyped co-occurrence vectors. This is a significant contribution as it directly challenges the assumption that neural embeddings alone suffice for compositionality tasks.

2. **Novel hybrid composition approach:** The authors' hybrid method (combining aligned and unaligned APT representations) achieves the best performance (0.79 ρ), demonstrating that both composition mechanisms capture different aspects of compositionality. This is a methodological advance that could inform future work on compositional models, particularly for cases where syntactic structure varies across phrases.

3. **Rigorous experimental design:** The authors conduct thorough parameter analysis across multiple composition operations, PPMI smoothing parameters, and alignment strategies. Their finding that optimal values for h and q consistently fall in [0.3,0.5] (suggesting modifiers are slightly more informative than heads) provides valuable insights about the relative importance of phrase components in compositionality judgments.

**Weaknesses:**  
1. **Limited scope of evaluation:** The work is restricted to noun compounds (90 examples from Reddy et al.'s dataset), while compositionality is a broader phenomenon applicable to various phrase types. The authors acknowledge this limitation but fail to demonstrate how their approach would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases or adverbial phrases), which significantly limits the perceived impact of their contribution.

2. **Insufficient explanation for performance gains:** While the paper shows APT outperforms neural embeddings (0.79 vs. 0.74), it doesn't clearly explain *why*. The authors note neural embeddings use untyped co-occurrences while APTs use typed dependency paths, but provide no detailed analysis of which specific features or dependency types contribute most to the performance gain. Without this, it's unclear whether the improvement stems from syntactic structure or other factors.

3. **Weak connection to broader compositional modeling:** The paper frames compositionality detection as a testbed for compositional methods, but fails to demonstrate how their findings directly improve compositional modeling itself. While they mention "the use of compositional methods to detect compositionality may also lead to improved compositional methods," this claim is not substantiated in the paper. The connection between their detection task and actual compositional modeling improvements remains tenuous.

**Questions to Authors:**  
1. How do you plan to extend your approach to other phrase types beyond noun compounds, and what specific challenges do you anticipate in doing so?

2. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of which specific dependency paths (e.g., NMOD, AMOD) contribute most to the performance gain of your APT approach? This would help clarify the nature of the syntactic information that matters most.

3. The paper notes that 76% of compound occurrences had dependency relationships, with 95% being NMOD. How might the performance of your approach change if the dataset included more diverse dependency relationships (e.g., for verb compounds)?

**Overall Assessment:**  
This is a well-executed study that makes a clear, incremental contribution to compositionality detection by demonstrating the importance of syntactic structure. The results are compelling for the specific task of noun compound compositionality, but the limited scope and lack of clear connection to broader compositional modeling applications prevent it from being a major contribution to the field. The work would be strengthened by demonstrating generalizability to other phrase types and providing clearer explanations for why the APT approach outperforms neural embeddings. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the scope limitations and provide more detailed analysis of feature contributions.
