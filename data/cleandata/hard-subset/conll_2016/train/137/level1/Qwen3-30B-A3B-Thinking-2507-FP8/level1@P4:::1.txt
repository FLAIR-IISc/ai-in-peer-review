

Summary of the Paper:
This paper investigates compositionality detection for noun compounds, focusing on distinguishing between compositional phrases (e.g., "spelling bee") and non-compositional phrases (e.g., "red herring"). The authors propose an integrated compositional distributional approach using anchored packed trees (APTs) with typed higher-order dependency paths, comparing it against the common approach of applying naive composition operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings.

Main Contributions:
1. A novel integrated compositional approach using APTs with typed dependency paths that significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods in compositionality detection (0.79 vs. 0.71 Spearman correlation).
2. Systematic exploration of composition operations (aligned vs. unaligned, intersective vs. union) and parameter settings for compositionality detection.
3. Demonstration that typed dependency features encoded in APTs are highly informative for compositionality detection, suggesting syntactic structure is crucial for fine-grained language understanding.

Strengths:
1. The paper provides a clear and well-motivated evaluation framework that properly addresses the limitation of using phrase similarity tasks for evaluating compositional methods. The authors correctly identify that compositionality detection is a better testbed as it avoids confounding compositionality with similarity judgments, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. The technical approach is sound and well-explained. The paper clearly demonstrates the necessity of vector space alignment for handling different parts of speech (e.g., adjectives vs. nouns) and provides a convincing explanation for why simple composition operations like addition work better than intersection. The hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned features) achieves the best results, showing careful consideration of different compositional strategies.

3. The results are compelling and represent a substantial improvement over previous work. The hybrid APT approach achieves 0.79 Spearman correlation, a statistically significant 0.08 improvement over the previous state-of-the-art (0.71). The paper provides thorough analysis of parameter settings (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, alignment strategies) that supports the validity of these results.

4. The paper offers a valuable insight about the relationship between frequency and compositionality (the FREQ baseline outperforms RAND), which is important for future work in this area and helps contextualize the results.

5. The comparison between integrated compositional approaches and naive composition of neural embeddings is well-executed and provides a clear benchmark for future work in this space.

Weaknesses:
1. The evaluation is limited to noun compounds, which represent only a small subset of multiword expressions. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide evidence that their approach would generalize to other phrase types (e.g., verb phrases, adverbial phrases), which significantly limits the scope and impact of the contribution.

2. The paper doesn't adequately explain why the unaligned approach outperforms the aligned approach, despite being counterintuitive. The authors note that "the unaligned typed dependency features are slightly more informative," but provide no detailed analysis or empirical evidence to support this claim.

3. The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of the APT approach compared to neural embeddings. The APT approach uses high-dimensional sparse representations (80k-230k dimensions) while neural embeddings are dense (50-300 dimensions), which is a significant practical consideration for real-world applications that the authors don't discuss.

4. The parameter search for neural embeddings appears limited to "commonly used and recommended parameter settings" without evidence of thorough exploration. The paper states the cbow model with 100 dimensions and t=10^-3 performed best, but doesn't demonstrate that this was the optimal configuration across a broader parameter space.

5. The paper doesn't provide sufficient explanation for why the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned features) works best. The authors state that "aligned APT composition and unaligned APT composition are predicting different contexts for compound nouns," but don't elaborate on what these contexts are or why they complement each other.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper states that unaligned features outperform aligned features, despite the theoretical expectation that alignment would improve composition. Could you provide more detailed analysis of what specific contexts these unaligned features capture that the aligned features miss?

2. The optimal values of h and q were in the range [0.3,0.5], suggesting the modifier is slightly more informative than the head. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case specifically for noun compounds?

3. How would you expect your APT approach to scale to larger corpora or to other phrase types beyond noun compounds?
