- Proposes a compositional distributional approach integrating syntactic structure (APT with typed higher-order dependency paths) rather than composing after the fact.  
- Systematically evaluates composition methods (aligned vs. unaligned, intersective vs. union) and finds combining aligned and unaligned APTs performs best.  
- Shows typed dependency paths yield much more informative features for compositionality detection, achieving 0.79 Spearman vs. prior 0.71.

- Clarifies the distinction between compositionality detection and phrase similarity evaluation, providing a cleaner evaluation framework.  
- Delivers strong empirical gains over prior work, demonstrating the benefit of syntactic structure for the task.  
- Provides a careful, nuanced analysis of composition operations and alignment strategies, highlighting complementary strengths of hybrid representations.  
- Explains the APT approach clearly with illustrative examples and a well-motivated experimental setup comparable to prior work.

- Evaluation is limited to noun compounds, so generality to other multiword expressions is untested.  
- Lacks comparison to more recent neural compositional models (e.g., attention/transformer-based approaches).  
- Omits analysis of computational cost and scalability despite very high feature dimensionality (≈230k dims).  
- Does not deeply analyze why alignment improves performance or what linguistic aspects aligned vs. unaligned representations capture.

- How would the APT approach perform on other phrase types (verb phrases, adverbial phrases); any preliminary results or theoretical justification?  
- Have you compared to modern neural compositional models (attention/transformer-based) that postdate Reddy et al. (2011)?  
- What are the computational costs and scaling properties of APT versus simpler methods like weighted addition of neural embeddings?

- Recommendation: accept — significant theoretical and empirical contribution despite scoped limitations.
