- Introduces an APT-based composition method that aligns vector spaces via dependency paths for compound-noun compositionality detection.  
- Proposes a hybrid model combining aligned and unaligned APTs, achieving state-of-the-art results on Reddy et al. (2011).  
- Extensive experiments show APTs encoding syntactic structure outperform neural embeddings and untyped co-occurrence methods (hybrid model Spearman 0.79).  
- Novelty: using APTs to encode syntactic structure addresses a key gap in compositional distributional semantics.  
- Comprehensive evaluation with rigorous experiments across models and parameter settings on the Reddy dataset.  
- Theoretical rigor: detailed explanation of the APT framework, feature alignment, and reduction enabling reproducibility.  
- Practical implication: results underscore the importance of incorporating syntactic structure in compositional methods.  
- Dataset limitation: experiments limited to the small Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds), raising generalizability concerns.  
- Sparse representations: sparse elementary features may limit intersective composition performance; smoothing techniques are not integrated or explored in depth.  
- Comparison with neural models is limited by parameter choices and omission of newer contextualized embeddings (e.g., BERT).  
- Computational complexity: building and aligning high-dimensional dependency-based APTs may be expensive, but costs are not discussed.  
- Open questions: performance on larger/diverse datasets, extending the hybrid APT to contextualized embeddings, and computational trade-offs versus neural methods.  
- Overall assessment: novel and well-executed with clear improvements but narrow evaluation; recommendation—accept with minor revisions.
