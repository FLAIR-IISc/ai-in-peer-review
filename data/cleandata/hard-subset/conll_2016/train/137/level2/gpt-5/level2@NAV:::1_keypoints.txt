- Core result: Typed dependency-based APTs, especially a hybrid of aligned and unaligned features, yield higher Spearman correlation with human compositionality judgments than cbow/skip-gram and prior untyped baselines on the Reddy et al. dataset.

- Contribution 1: Empirical finding that typed higher-order dependency features composed via APTs outperform naïve composition of untyped neural embeddings (best ρ=0.79 vs 0.74 for cbow, 0.71 prior SOTA).

- Contribution 2: Modeling insight that a linear hybrid of aligned and unaligned APTs outperforms either alone, indicating complementary signals from different path alignments.

- Contribution 3: Evaluation analysis showing effects of PMI smoothing (α=0.75), composition operators, and constituent weighting, with consistent trends and statistical testing.

- Strength: Claims are clear and supported by statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, including a re-tuned cbow on the same corpus.

- Strength: Careful leakage control—compounds re-tokenized and contextual features separated so composed vectors cannot trivially match observed ones.

- Strength: Informative ablation studies (aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid, PPMI vs counts, composition operators, parameter sweeps) with interpretable parameter behavior.

- Strength: Solid motivation for using typed, higher-order dependency features with a principled syntactic alignment mechanism.

- Weakness: Limited evaluation on a single small dataset (90 compounds), risking overfitting from repeated 3-fold CV and limiting generality.

- Weakness: Reproducibility gaps—missing implementation details (pruning thresholds, word2vec hyperparams, full APT construction choices) and a “reference suppressed” APT provenance.

- Weakness: Potential confounds from heavy preprocessing and parser-dependent retokenization; robustness to parsing errors is not quantified.

- Weakness: Missing comparisons to modern contextual encoders or phrase-level models; simple baselines like averaged contextualized embeddings are not evaluated.

- Question: Can you report robustness to parsing errors (e.g., performance on high-confidence parses or with different parsers)?

- Question: How sensitive are results to compound and constituent frequency, and can frequency effects be more directly controlled?

- Question: Will you release code and preprocessed resources to ensure reproducibility?

- Question: Do the gains generalize to other MWE/compositionality datasets (e.g., verb–noun) or other languages?
