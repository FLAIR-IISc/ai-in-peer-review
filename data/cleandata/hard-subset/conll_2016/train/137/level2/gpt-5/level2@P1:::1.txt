Summary and contributions
The paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by contrasting (i) an integrated compositional distributional approach based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) that use typed, higher-order dependency features and alignment-by-path, against (ii) “adhoc” composition over neural word embeddings (word2vec) via weighted addition. Evaluation is conducted on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset (90 compounds) using ukWaC, measuring the Spearman correlation between human compositionality ratings and the similarity of composed vs. observed (holistic) compound representations.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical finding: Typed, higher-order dependency features with APT-based composition substantially outperform neural embedding baselines and prior untyped count-based approaches on the Reddy benchmark (best ρ≈0.79 vs. 0.74 for cbow and 0.71 for prior state of the art).
2) Hybrid composition: A simple linear combination of aligned and unaligned APTs yields the best results, suggesting complementary information from path-aligned and direct features.
3) Parameter insights: A careful analysis of weighting (h, q), PPMI smoothing (α), and subsampling shows robust trends (e.g., modifier slightly more informative; shifted/strong subsampling harms low-frequency compounds).

Strengths
- Clear empirical gains over strong baselines. The APT models, particularly the hybrid variant, consistently and significantly improve correlation with human judgments (ρ up to 0.79) over both the best neural baseline (cbow ρ≈0.74) and the prior best (0.71). Comparisons are controlled on the same corpus, strengthening the claim that typed dependency structure adds value beyond untyped windows.

- Thoughtful experimental design to avoid leakage. The corpus is preprocessed so compounds become single tokens; constituent representations do not trivially inherit compound contexts. This strengthens the test: composed representations must recover holistic behavior without seeing the compound’s contexts via its parts.

- Informative analysis of compositional variants. The paper systematically compares aligned, unaligned, and hybrid APT composition; INT vs UNI operations; PPMI vs count composition; and α-smoothing. The observation that unaligned APTs often outperform aligned ones for NMOD compounds, yet hybrid improves further, is insightful and actionable.

- Solid neural baselines and parameter exploration. Both cbow and skip-gram are tuned across dimensions and subsampling thresholds. The finding that lower subsampling harms performance (analogous to shifted PMI) is plausible given low compound frequency and helps interpret divergent results across paradigms.

- Technical clarity of the APT mechanism. The description of dependency-path typing, reduction, and offsetting is sufficiently detailed to understand how alignment works and why higher-order paths support composition (e.g., 2nd-order adjective features mapping to 1st-order noun features).

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation scope. The dataset is small (90 items) and limited to English noun–noun compounds. Results may not generalize to other constructions (e.g., verb–object, adjective–noun with non-NMOD relations), languages, or larger-scale compositionality datasets. Including at least one additional dataset or a larger held-out set would strengthen claims.

- Dependence on parsing quality and path design. The approach relies on accurate typed dependencies and higher-order paths. The paper notes parser errors (e.g., three-noun compounds) but does not quantify their effect on performance. An ablation comparing gold vs. automatic parses (or a noise analysis) would clarify robustness.

- The alignment contribution is under-realized for this task. While alignment-by-path is a conceptual contribution of APTs, the best results come from unaligned or hybrid models for NMOD compounds. This weakens the case that alignment itself is crucial in noun–noun compound compositionality (though it may matter more in other syntactic settings).

- Frequency confound not fully controlled. Frequency correlates strongly with compositionality on this dataset (FREQ baseline ρ=0.63). The paper does not report partial correlations controlling for frequency, or stratified results by frequency bands. This makes it hard to assess how much gains persist beyond frequency effects.

- Reproducibility details could be richer. While many parameters are reported, some important settings are not fully specified: number of CV repetitions, vocabulary/feature cutoffs, feature selection thresholds, and computational footprint (APT dimensionality is large: ~80k–230k dims). Public code or more procedural detail would aid replication.

Questions to authors
- Cross-validation protocol: How many repetitions of 3-fold CV were run? How were folds constructed to avoid any leakage between training-tuned parameters and test (especially given corpus preprocessing that introduces compound tokens)?

- Frequency control: Can you report partial Spearman correlations controlling for log compound frequency, or results stratified by frequency, to quantify gains beyond frequency?

- Parser robustness: What is the estimated error rate for dependency types relevant to compounds (e.g., NMOD vs alternatives)? Do results change if you filter instances with low parser confidence or ambiguous dependency types?

- Higher-order paths: You compose and then restrict comparison to 1st-order features. What is the contribution of including 2nd-order features on the observed side, or removing 2nd-order features from the composed side? An ablation isolating higher-order benefits would be helpful.

- Efficiency and scalability: What are memory/time requirements to build APTs (80k–230k dims per noun) on ukWaC, and how would this scale to larger corpora or to sentence-level composition?

Additional comments
- It would strengthen the narrative to include brief error analyses (e.g., examples where APTs help vs hurt, especially borderline or idiomatic compounds like spelling bee vs climate change).

- Given that cbow is a strong baseline here, adding a dependency-based neural embedding baseline (e.g., dependency-context SGNS) could help separate the effect of typed contexts from APT-specific modeling.

- The paper would benefit from releasing preprocessing scripts and evaluation code to facilitate adoption.

Overall assessment
A well-executed and thoughtful study that advances compositionality detection via typed, higher-order dependency features and provides solid empirical evidence against strong baselines. While the evaluation scope is narrow and some methodological aspects (alignment contribution, frequency confounds) merit deeper analysis, the results are compelling and the insights are valuable to the community. I lean toward acceptance, contingent on clarifying analyses and additional controls.
