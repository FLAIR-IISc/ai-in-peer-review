- Paper compares APT-based typed higher-order dependency composition with naïve additive neural embeddings for noun–noun compositionality, using ukWaC and the Reddy et al. (2011) 90-item dataset.

- Main contribution: empirical demonstration that typed higher-order dependency features and APT-based composition (hybrid aligned/unaligned) substantially improve compositionality detection (ρ up to 0.79 vs 0.71).

- Main contribution: careful analysis of composition design choices (alignment vs unaligned APTs, additive vs intersective ops, PPMI smoothing α, constituent/head and hybrid weighting) with insights on when alignment helps.

- Main contribution: controlled corpus preprocessing to avoid leakage (merging compound tokens) and analysis of subsampling and shifted PMI effects on low-frequency compounds.

- Strength: clear, well-motivated use of typed dependency contexts showing syntactic structure in features helps compositionality detection compared to untyped windows.

- Strength: solid empirical improvements and thorough within-family comparisons (aligned/unaligned/hybrid; INT vs UNI; α values; CF vs CS), finding hybrid APT with additive composition best.

- Strength: attention to leakage and evaluation protocol by restricting similarity to first-order features and preprocessing corpus to avoid trivial inflation.

- Strength: useful analysis of weighting and smoothing choices, with actionable findings (moderate PPMI smoothing helpful; shifted PMI hurts low-frequency settings; modifier slightly outweighs head).

- Strength: stronger baselines than Reddy et al., retraining word2vec on the same corpus and achieving ρ=0.74 with cbow, making APT improvements more meaningful.

- Weakness: missing competitive baselines—no dependency-based neural embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014) or typed-space composition baselines to fairly close the gap.

- Weakness: limited external validity—evaluation on only one small dataset (90 items) and only noun–noun compounds limits generality to other phrase types and larger datasets.

- Weakness: ambiguity about comparability of training conditions—unclear if word2vec and APT models were trained on the same preprocessed corpus with merged compounds and how that affects fairness.

- Weakness: some methodological choices may bias results—restricting similarity to first-order features could disadvantage aligned APTs; frequency is a strong predictor (ρ=0.63) and partial correlations controlling for frequency are not reported.

- Weakness: reproducibility and presentation issues—missing implementation details (“reference suppressed”), no code/hyperparameter release, unclear number of CV repetitions, and formatting/notation errors.

- Question: did you train dependency-based neural embeddings (à la Levy & Goldberg 2014) on ukWaC and evaluate additive composition, and how would they compare to APTs?

- Question: were word2vec models trained on the same preprocessed corpus with merged compounds, and did low-frequency compound vectors show instability or require mitigation?

- Question: how many repetitions of 3-fold CV were run, what is the variance across runs, and can exact confidence intervals for top systems be provided?

- Question: how sensitive are APT results to maximum path order in elementary representations and to feature pruning thresholds?

- Question: can you report partial correlations controlling for compound frequency to show improvements beyond frequency effects?

- Suggestion: include ablations on typed vs untyped contexts within APTs, order-1 only vs up to order-2, and alignment effects per dependency type (e.g., NMOD vs others).

- Suggestion: add error analysis contrasting idiomatic vs literal compounds and identify dependency paths driving correct predictions.

- Suggestion: improve references/notation, release code/models, and clarify omitted implementation/hyperparameter details for reproducibility.

- Overall: well-motivated, carefully executed study with meaningful improvements and insights, but would be substantially strengthened by missing baselines, broader evaluation, and clearer reproducibility.
