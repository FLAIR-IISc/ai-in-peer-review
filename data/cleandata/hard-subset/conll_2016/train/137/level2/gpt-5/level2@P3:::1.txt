Summary and claimed contributions
The paper investigates compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by contrasting an integrated, typed, dependency-based compositional model—Anchored Packed Trees (APTs)—with naive addition of neural word embeddings. The study operates on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 English compounds and the ukWaC corpus, measuring Spearman correlation between predicted compositionality and human judgments.

As seen by this reviewer, the primary contributions are:
1) An empirical demonstration that typed higher-order dependency features substantially improve compositionality detection, with APT-based models (aligned, unaligned, and hybrid) outperforming both the Reddy et al. (2011) count-based untyped baseline and word2vec-based additive composition.
2) A hybrid APT composition that linearly combines aligned and unaligned spaces, yielding the best performance (ρ = 0.79), suggesting complementary information from both views of context.
3) Analysis of weighting choices (PPMI smoothing, constituent weights) and insights into why subsampling/shifted-PMI harms low-frequency compound representations.

Strengths
1) Strong empirical gains on a standard benchmark. The best APT model (hybrid) reaches ρ = 0.79, exceeding the Reddy et al. (2011) baseline (0.71), the frequency baseline (0.63), and word2vec-based addition with cbow (0.74). Results are reported with repeated 3-fold CV and clear significance criteria (>0.005 difference at 95% confidence), indicating robustness.
2) Careful experimental control of leakage. The corpus preprocessing avoids contaminating constituent vectors with compound contexts, ensuring that intersective composition cannot trivially recreate observed compound features. This strengthens the validity of the evaluation of the compositional mechanism rather than memorization.
3) Clear articulation of the APT alignment mechanism. The offset-and-reduction procedure that aligns modifier and head spaces via complete dependency paths is well motivated, and the contrast with unaligned and hybrid variants is informative, revealing that unaligned typed features already capture much of the signal, while alignment adds complementary predictive context.
4) Insightful analysis of distributional weighting. The study shows that PPMI with context distribution smoothing (α = 0.75; Levy et al., 2015) slightly improves results; shifted PMI and aggressive subsampling harm performance, plausibly due to low-frequency compounds losing informative high-frequency contexts—an observation relevant to future work on MWEs.
5) Transparent reporting of parameter effects. The observation that optimal constituent weights h and hybrid weights q fall in [0.3, 0.5] is useful, indicating the modifier’s slightly greater informativeness for these compounds and the value of unaligned typed features.

Weaknesses
1) Limited evaluation scope and potential overfitting risk. The dataset contains only 90 noun compounds, all English, with a single task and metric. While repeated CV mitigates variance, conclusions on generality are limited. Extending to larger and more diverse MWE datasets and to other constructions (e.g., verb–object, adjective–noun, phrasal verbs; cf. McCarthy et al., 2003) would strengthen claims.
2) Incomplete baselines. The comparison omits dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) and GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014). A strong typed count baseline without higher-order paths (e.g., first-order dependency features in the style of Padó & Lapata, 2007) would help attribute gains among typing, higher-order context, and APT alignment. Current results suggest most of the gains come from typed features (the unaligned APT scores 0.78), so teasing apart contributions is important.
3) Novelty is incremental relative to prior APT/higher-order path work. Much of the modeling draws on earlier APT/higher-order dependency ideas (e.g., Weeds et al., 2014; “reference suppressed”). The main novel aspects here are applying APTs to compositionality detection and introducing the hybrid mixing of aligned and unaligned contexts. This is valuable but should be framed as a focused empirical contribution.
4) Reproducibility and detail gaps. Important training details are missing or terse: word2vec settings (negative samples, iterations, min-count), APT feature pruning/thresholds, vocabulary sizes after preprocessing, number of CV repetitions, and the exact statistical test for significance. The treatment of low-frequency compounds (e.g., smoothing, backoff) could be elaborated.
5) Potential preprocessing bias. Compounds are merged and annotated with the most frequent dependency relation, and modifier-side contexts are dropped. While justified for the task, these choices may bias observed compound vectors in ways that interact with typed features. An ablation showing the impact of these preprocessing decisions would increase confidence.

Questions to the authors
- How many repetitions were used in repeated 3-fold CV, and what exact statistical test supports the significance claims?
- What were the word2vec training details (negative samples, epochs, min-count), and how sensitive were results to these choices?
- How were rare compounds handled (e.g., min frequency thresholds for compound tokens, smoothing)? What is the distribution of token counts across the 90 compounds?
- What is the impact of limiting APT features to order ≤2? Does order-1 only reduce performance materially? Conversely, do higher orders (>2) help?
- Did dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) or GloVe (Pennington et al., 2014) perform differently from cbow/skip-gram window-based embeddings on this task?
- Can a qualitative analysis be provided showing exemplar contexts uniquely recovered by aligned vs unaligned APTs?

Suggestions for improvement
Essential (feasible within rebuttal/camera-ready):
- Add a baseline with dependency-based word embeddings (Levy & Goldberg, 2014) and a typed first-order count model (Padó & Lapata, 2007-style) with additive composition, to quantify the contributions of typing, higher-order paths, and APT alignment.
- Provide full training details for reproducibility (APT feature thresholds, vocabulary sizes, word2vec hyperparameters, number of CV repetitions, significance test).
- Include an ablation on APT order (1 vs 2) and an analysis of the effect of the compound-merge preprocessing (e.g., keep modifier-side contexts vs remove).

Optional but valuable:
- Evaluate on additional datasets or constructions (e.g., adjective–noun compositionality, phrasal verbs) to probe generality.
- Release code and scripts for preprocessing and APT construction.
- Provide qualitative error analysis contrasting cases where cbow succeeds vs APT fails and vice versa.

Overall evaluation
The paper presents a careful and well-motivated empirical study showing that typed higher-order dependency features are highly informative for compositionality detection, with a simple hybrid of aligned and unaligned APTs achieving state-of-the-art results on a standard dataset. While the novelty is primarily empirical and the evaluation scope is narrow, the findings are solid and relevant. With strengthened baselines and added reproducibility details, the work would make a meaningful contribution to CoNLL.
