Summary
The paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by contrasting an integrated, typed dependency-based compositional model (Anchored Packed Trees, APTs) with naïve additive composition of neural word embeddings. Using ukWaC and the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, the authors show that APT variants—particularly a hybrid of aligned and unaligned typed features—achieve higher Spearman correlation with human compositionality judgments than cbow/skip-gram embeddings and prior untyped baselines.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: Typed higher-order dependency features, composed via APTs, outperform naïve composition over untyped neural embeddings for compositionality detection on noun compounds (best ρ=0.79 vs 0.74 for cbow, 0.71 prior SOTA).
2) Modeling insight: A hybrid that linearly combines aligned and unaligned APTs improves over either alone, suggesting complementary signals from typed paths at different alignments.
3) Evaluation analysis: Demonstrates the effect of PMI smoothing (α=0.75), composition operators (min vs weighted add), and constituent weighting, with consistent trends and statistical testing.

Strengths
- Clear, testable claims backed by statistically significant improvements over strong baselines, including a re-tuned cbow model on the same corpus.
- Careful treatment of leakage: compounds are re-tokenized and contextual features are separated so composed vectors cannot trivially match observed ones.
- Informative ablations: aligned vs unaligned vs hybrid APTs; PPMI vs counts; composition operators; parameter ranges (h, q) with consistent interpretation (modifier slightly more informative).
- Solid motivation for typed, higher-order dependency features and a principled alignment mechanism grounded in syntax.

Weaknesses
- Limited scope: evaluation on a single, small dataset (90 compounds) risks overfitting in model selection (repeated 3-fold CV on the same items) and limits claims of generality.
- Reproducibility gaps: several implementation details are missing (e.g., pruning thresholds, negative sampling/epochs for word2vec, full APT construction choices); “reference suppressed” further obscures the APT method’s provenance.
- Potential confounds: The heavy preprocessing (compound retokenization and parser-dependent paths) may introduce bias; robustness to parsing errors is not quantified.
- Comparators: No evaluation against modern contextual encoders or phrase-level models; even simple baselines like averaging contextualized token embeddings could be informative.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report robustness to parsing errors (e.g., performance when restricting to high-confidence parses or varying parsers)?
- How sensitive are results to compound frequency and to constituent frequency? Can you control for frequency effects more directly?
- Will you release code and preprocessed resources to ensure reproducibility?
- Do the gains hold for other MWE/compositionality datasets (e.g., verb–noun combinations) or other languages?
