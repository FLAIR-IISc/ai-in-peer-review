Summary and claimed contributions
The paper studies compositionality detection for noun–noun compounds by contrasting an “integrated” compositional distributional model based on anchored packed trees (APTs) with standard neural embeddings combined via naïve addition. Using ukWaC and the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compounds, the authors build typed, higher-order dependency feature spaces, explore alignment (offset) for composition, and evaluate by correlating cosine similarity between composed and observed compound representations with human compositionality judgments.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical demonstration that typed higher-order dependency features and APT-based composition (especially a hybrid of aligned and unaligned variants) substantially improve compositionality detection over prior untyped count-based and neural baselines on the Reddy dataset (ρ up to 0.79 vs 0.71).
2) A careful analysis of design choices that affect composition: aligned vs unaligned APTs, additive vs intersective operations, PPMI smoothing (α), and constituent/head weighting (h) and hybrid weighting (q), with insights about when alignment helps for noun–noun compounds.
3) A controlled corpus preprocessing to avoid leakage (merging compound tokens), and an analysis of subsampling and shifted PMI effects for low-frequency compounds.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated use of typed dependency contexts. The work convincingly argues and shows empirically that preserving syntactic structure in features is helpful for compositionality detection, a setting where untyped windows are often too blunt. The consistent gains of APT models over cbow/skip-gram support the central thesis.

- Solid empirical improvements and thorough comparisons within the APT family. The paper reports multiple variants (aligned, unaligned, hybrid; INT vs UNI; α values; CF vs CS) and finds the hybrid APT with additive composition best (ρ=0.79). The observation that unaligned typed features remain predictive for NMOD compounds is informative and nuanced.

- Attention to leakage and evaluation protocol. The corpus is preprocessed so that compound contexts do not trivially inflate constituent representations; evaluation measures similarity to observed compound representations restricted to first-order features, avoiding over-crediting higher-order paths that were not reliably learned.

- Useful analysis of weighting and smoothing choices. The findings that (i) moderate PPMI smoothing is beneficial, (ii) shifted PMI hurts in low-frequency settings, and (iii) the modifier slightly outweighs the head, are concrete, actionable insights for practitioners.

- Stronger baselines than in Reddy et al. The paper re-trains word2vec on the same corpus with varied dimensions and subsampling, achieving ρ=0.74 with cbow, making the APT improvements more meaningful.

Weaknesses
- Missing competitive baselines that narrow the gap to APTs. The neural baselines use untyped proximity contexts and naïve addition only. A fairer comparison would include dependency-based embeddings (e.g., Levy & Goldberg 2014) and composition in typed spaces, or at least show whether cbow/SG trained on dependency-based contexts shrinks the gap. Likewise, an updated count-based untyped baseline (PPMI with smoothing and SVD) would contextualize the gains beyond Reddy (2011).

- Limited external validity: one small dataset and one phrase type. The Reddy dataset has only 90 items, all noun–noun compounds. The paper concludes broadly about compositional methods, but evaluations on additional datasets (e.g., other compound sets, adjective–noun, verb–object, VPC/VNC compositionality) would strengthen claims.

- Ambiguity about comparability of training conditions. The corpus is preprocessed to merge target compounds. It is implied that both APT and word2vec models were trained on this preprocessed corpus so both have observed compound tokens. Please clarify this, as differences could affect the learned representations and fairness of the comparison.

- Some methodological choices warrant further analysis. Restricting similarity to first-order features at evaluation may bias against aligned APTs whose strength lies in stitching higher-order evidence. An ablation showing the effect of including higher-order features on both composed and observed sides would help. Also, frequency is a strong predictor (ρ=0.63); reporting partial correlations controlling for frequency would show whether improvements go beyond frequency effects.

- Reproducibility and presentation issues. Key implementation details are elided (“reference suppressed”); no code or hyperparameter grids are released; exact number of CV repetitions is not given. References contain numerous formatting errors and placeholders, and there are minor notational inconsistencies, which detract from clarity.

Questions to authors
- Did you also train dependency-based neural embeddings (à la Levy & Goldberg 2014) on ukWaC and evaluate them with additive composition? If not, can you comment on expected performance relative to APTs?

- Were word2vec models trained on the same preprocessed corpus with merged compound tokens? If so, did you observe any instability for low-frequency compound vectors, and how did you mitigate it?

- How many repetitions of 3-fold CV were run, and what is the variance across runs? Can you provide exact confidence intervals for the top systems?

- How sensitive are the APT results to the maximum path order included in elementary representations and to feature pruning thresholds?

- Could you report partial correlations controlling for compound frequency to discount that confound?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Include an ablation on: (i) typed vs untyped contexts within the APT framework; (ii) order-1 only vs up to order-2; (iii) effect of alignment per dependency type (NMOD vs others).

- Add error analysis contrasting idiomatic vs literal compounds, showing which dependency paths drive correct predictions.

- Improve references and notation; ensure all equations and footnotes are clear. Consider releasing code and models for reproducibility.

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated, carefully executed study with meaningful improvements and insights. Addressing the missing baselines and expanding evaluation scope would substantially strengthen the contribution.
