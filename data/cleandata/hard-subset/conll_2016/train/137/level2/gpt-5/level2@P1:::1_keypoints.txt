- Empirical finding: APT-based composition with typed, higher-order dependency features substantially outperforms neural embedding baselines and prior untyped count approaches on the Reddy et al. dataset (best ρ≈0.79).

- Hybrid composition: A linear combination of aligned and unaligned APTs yields the best performance, indicating complementary information from path-aligned and direct features.

- Parameter insights: Analysis of weighting (h, q), PPMI smoothing (α), and subsampling shows robust trends (modifier slightly more informative; strong/subsampled settings harm low-frequency compounds).

- Controlled comparison: Models are compared on the same corpus with preprocessing that prevents leakage by making compounds single tokens, strengthening the evaluation.

- Systematic variant analysis: The paper compares aligned/unaligned/hybrid APTs, INT vs UNI operations, PPMI vs count, and α-smoothing, revealing nuanced patterns (e.g., unaligned often better for NMOD).

- Strong neural baselines: CBOW and skip-gram are well-tuned, revealing sensible effects (lower subsampling harms performance for low-frequency compounds).

- Clear APT technical exposition: Dependency-path typing, reduction, and offsetting are explained sufficiently to understand alignment and higher-order path utility.

- Limited dataset: Evaluation is small (90 items) and confined to English noun–noun compounds, limiting generalizability to other constructions, languages, or larger datasets.

- Parser/path dependence: Method relies on accurate typed dependencies and higher-order paths; parser errors are noted but their quantitative impact is not measured.

- Alignment contribution ambiguous: Best results often come from unaligned or hybrid APTs, weakening the claim that alignment-by-path is crucial for noun–noun compositionality.

- Frequency confound: Frequency correlates strongly with compositionality (FREQ ρ=0.63) and the paper lacks partial correlations or stratified analyses controlling for frequency.

- Reproducibility gaps: Some experimental details (CV repetitions, vocabulary/feature cutoffs, thresholds, computational footprint) are underspecified and APT dimensionality is large (~80k–230k).

- CV protocol question: Clarify number of 3-fold CV repetitions and fold construction to avoid tuning/test leakage given preprocessing.

- Frequency-control question: Provide partial Spearman correlations controlling for log compound frequency or stratified results by frequency bands.

- Parser robustness question: Report parser error rates for compound-relevant dependency types and whether filtering low-confidence parses affects results.

- Higher-order path ablation question: Quantify the contribution of 2nd-order features on both composed and observed sides via targeted ablations.

- Efficiency/scalability question: Provide memory/time requirements for building APTs on ukWaC and discuss scaling to larger corpora or sentence-level composition.

- Error analysis suggestion: Include examples where APTs help vs. hurt (e.g., borderline or idiomatic compounds) to strengthen qualitative insights.

- Additional baseline suggestion: Add a dependency-context neural embedding baseline to disentangle benefits of typed contexts from APT-specific modeling.

- Code release suggestion: Release preprocessing scripts and evaluation code to facilitate replication and adoption.

- Overall assessment: Solid, well-executed study with compelling empirical gains from typed higher-order dependency features, but needs broader evaluation and deeper analyses on alignment, frequency confounds, and parser robustness.
