- Empirical finding: typed higher-order dependency features (APTs) substantially improve noun–noun compositionality detection over untyped counts and additive word2vec.
- Hybrid APT (linear mix of aligned and unaligned spaces) gives best performance (ρ = 0.79), indicating complementary information in both views.
- Analysis of weighting (PPMI smoothing, constituent weights) shows why shifted-PMI/subsampling harm low-frequency compound representations.

- Strong empirical gains: hybrid APT reaches ρ = 0.79 vs baselines (Reddy 0.71, freq 0.63, cbow-add 0.74) with repeated 3-fold CV and clear significance criteria.
- Careful leakage control in preprocessing prevents compound contexts contaminating constituent vectors, strengthening evaluation validity.
- Clear explanation of APT alignment (offset-and-reduction) and informative contrast between aligned, unaligned, and hybrid variants.
- Insightful distributional-weighting analysis: PPMI with α=0.75 helps slightly; shifted PMI/subsampling hurt due to low-frequency compound context loss.
- Transparent parameter reporting: optimal constituent and hybrid weights lie in [0.3, 0.5], indicating modifier informativeness and value of unaligned typed features.

- Limited evaluation scope and potential overfitting: only 90 English noun compounds, single task/metric limits generality.
- Incomplete baselines: missing dependency-based embeddings (Levy & Goldberg), GloVe, and a typed first-order dependency baseline to isolate contributions.
- Incremental novelty: builds on prior APT/higher-order path work; main novelties are applying APTs to compositionality and hybrid mixing.
- Reproducibility/detail gaps: missing word2vec hyperparameters, APT feature thresholds, vocabulary sizes, CV repetitions, exact statistical test, and low-frequency treatment.
- Potential preprocessing bias: compound merging/relation annotation and dropping modifier-side contexts may interact with typed features; ablation needed.
