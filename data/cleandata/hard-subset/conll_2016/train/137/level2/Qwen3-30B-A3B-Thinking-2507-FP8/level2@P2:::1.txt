

**Summary of Contributions**  
The paper's primary contribution is demonstrating that typed dependency path representations (APT) significantly improve compositionality detection for noun compounds compared to prior untyped methods. The secondary contribution is showing that a hybrid composition approach (combining aligned and unaligned APT features) achieves optimal performance, revealing that both syntactically aligned and raw contextual features contribute to predicting phrase compositionality. The tertiary contribution is clarifying that neural word embeddings (even state-of-the-art cbow) underperform when used with naive composition, partly due to their untyped nature.

**Strengths**  
1. **Strong empirical results with clear superiority**: The APT-based hybrid method (ρ=0.79) significantly outperforms Reddy et al.'s (2011) state-of-the-art (ρ=0.71) and neural embeddings (ρ=0.74), with statistical significance (p<0.05). This is a substantial and well-validated improvement, as confirmed by multiple cross-validation runs and consistent parameter optimization (h/q≈0.3–0.5).  
2. **Insightful analysis of feature types**: The paper effectively demonstrates why typed dependencies matter via Table 2 (e.g., unaligned features capture relevant context like "spelling bee" from "spelling test"). The hybrid approach’s success proves that both aligned (syntactically precise) and unaligned (broad contextual) features are necessary, resolving ambiguity in prior work.  
3. **Thorough baseline comparison**: The inclusion of meaningful baselines (RAND, FREQ, REDDY) contextualizes results. The FREQ baseline’s strong performance (ρ=0.63) reveals a frequency-compositionality correlation in the dataset, which the authors appropriately address by showing their method surpasses this baseline.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Under-explained neural embedding underperformance**: The paper reports cbow (ρ=0.74) outperforms skip-gram but lags behind APT (ρ=0.79). However, it fails to critically analyze *why* neural embeddings underperform despite being "state-of-the-art." Is it solely due to untyped features, or do low-frequency compounds (e.g., "gravy train") hurt their representation? This gap weakens the claim that APT’s superiority stems from "integrated compositional methods" rather than just syntactic features.  
2. **Overstated novelty of "integrated" approach**: The paper frames APT as an "integrated compositional approach," but it still relies on simple addition (⊔UNI) after alignment, not a novel compositional mechanism like tensor-based methods (e.g., Socher et al., 2012). This misrepresents the contribution; APT’s innovation is in *feature engineering* (typed paths), not composition.  
3. **Superficial handling of human judgments**: The paper uses Spearman correlation but does not analyze *why* human judgments disagree (e.g., "spelling bee" is rated medium compositionality). This omission risks conflating method performance with dataset limitations, as human ratings are noisy (scale 0–5).  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Why do neural embeddings (even with optimal cbow parameters) fail to match APT performance? Is the issue with untyped features, low-frequency compounds, or a fundamental limitation of neural models for compositionality detection?  
2. How would the results change if the dataset included non-noun compounds (e.g., verb phrases), which the paper notes as future work?  
3. The hybrid method’s success (Table 3) suggests unaligned features are slightly more informative (q≈0.3–0.5). Could this be due to noise in dependency parsing (e.g., 76% of compounds had NMOD relations, but parsing errors occurred)?
