- Novel APT approach shows typed higher-order dependency paths with proper alignment significantly improve noun-compound compositionality detection.  
- Hybrid (aligned + unaligned APT) representation yields the best performance.  
- Syntactic structure encoded in dependency paths is highly informative for compositionality detection.  
- Integrates syntactic structure directly into composition, providing a linguistically grounded method.  
- APT with typed dependency paths outperforms prior work (Spearman’s rho 0.79 vs. 0.71).  
- Experimental design is rigorous (Reddy et al. benchmark, cross-validation, aligned vs. unaligned distinction).  
- Thorough parameter analysis shows optimal h and q in [0.3, 0.5], implying modifiers slightly more informative than heads.  
- Comparison with neural embeddings shows APT’s syntactic features outperform standard neural embeddings for this task.  
- Paper lacks explanation why the hybrid approach outperforms single approaches.  
- Neural embedding experiments use a narrow CBOW/skip-gram parameter range and need broader hyperparameter tuning.  
- No analysis of computational cost or scalability of the APT approach provided.  
- Limited discussion of generalizability to phrase types beyond noun compounds.  
- Question: How do aligned vs. unaligned findings generalize to other phrase types?  
- Question: What is the computational cost of APT compared to simpler composition methods?  
- Question: Can the hybrid approach be theoretically justified via linguistic properties rather than only empirically?
