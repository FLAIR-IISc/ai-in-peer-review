

Summary: This paper investigates the use of compositional methods for detecting non-compositional phrases in natural language. The authors propose an integrated compositional distributional approach using anchored packed trees (APTs) with typed dependency paths, comparing it to the common approach of applying naive composition operations to neural embeddings. They evaluate their method on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality.

Main contributions:
1. A novel APT-based compositional approach that maintains syntactic structure within contextual features, which is central to the compositional process.
2. Empirical demonstration that using typed dependency paths and proper alignment of vector spaces significantly improves compositionality detection performance.
3. A comparative analysis showing that their integrated approach outperforms the state-of-the-art method by Reddy et al. (2011) and other baseline methods.

Strengths:
1. The paper addresses an important gap in compositional distributional semantics by focusing on compositionality detection rather than phrase similarity, which avoids confounding compositionality with similarity judgments. This is a thoughtful approach to evaluating compositional methods that has been underexplored in the literature.

2. The authors provide a thorough comparison of different composition methods (aligned vs. unaligned, intersective vs. union operations) and demonstrate that combining both approaches (hybrid method) yields the best results. Their analysis of parameter settings (h and q) provides valuable insights into the relative importance of modifier and head in compound nouns.

3. The results are compelling - the authors achieve a Spearman correlation of 0.79 with human judgments, significantly outperforming the previous state-of-the-art (0.71). The paper demonstrates that using higher-order dependency paths encoded in typed contextual features is highly informative for determining compositionality.

4. The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the APT approach, including the mathematical formalization of the composition process, which will be valuable for future work in the field. The authors effectively address potential issues with sparsity through their methodological choices.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper doesn't sufficiently explain why the unaligned models performed better than the aligned models, which contradicts the authors' theoretical motivation for alignment. This is a significant finding that deserves more thorough discussion, as it challenges the core premise of the APT approach.

2. The authors don't provide a detailed analysis of which specific compound nouns were most challenging for their model, which could help identify limitations and guide future research directions. The paper would benefit from a qualitative analysis of failure cases.

3. The comparison with other compositional methods is somewhat limited - they primarily compare to Reddy et al. (2011) and basic neural embeddings, but don't compare to more recent compositional methods in the field, such as tensor-based approaches or neural network compositions that have been proposed since 2011.

4. The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of their approach compared to simpler methods, which could be important for practical applications. The authors mention that "the size of the feature space for nouns is approximately 80,000 dimensions," but don't address how this affects scalability or runtime.

Questions to authors:
1. The paper states that "the unaligned model outperforms the aligned model" but doesn't provide a clear explanation for this counterintuitive finding. Could the authors elaborate on why alignment might not be beneficial in this specific task, and how this relates to the linguistic properties of the compound nouns in their dataset?

2. The authors note that "optimal values of both h and q were in the range [0.3,0.5]". What does this suggest about the relative importance of the modifier versus the head in compound nouns, and how does this align with linguistic theories of compounding?

3. The paper mentions that "many of the compound phrases are comparatively low frequency tokens." How does this affect the generalizability of their approach to other datasets with more frequent compounds, and would their method be affected by the distribution of compound frequencies?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of compositional distributional semantics. The authors have conducted a thorough evaluation of their approach and demonstrated significant improvements over previous work. With some additional clarification on the unaligned model's performance and a more comprehensive comparison to recent compositional methods, this work would be even stronger. The paper's focus on compositionality detection as an evaluation method for compositional models is particularly insightful and well-executed.
