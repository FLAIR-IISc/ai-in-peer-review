

This paper investigates compositionality detection for noun compounds using a novel integrated compositional distributional approach (APT) compared to standard ad-hoc composition of neural embeddings. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that APT-based composition significantly outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods (0.79 vs. 0.71 Spearman correlation), (2) establishing that both aligned and unaligned APT representations contribute to better compositionality prediction, and (3) showing that syntactic structure encoded in typed dependency paths is crucial for fine-grained language understanding.

Strengths: The paper provides strong empirical support for its claims through rigorous experiments on the standard Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark. The improvements over previous methods are statistically significant (p<0.005) and substantial. The methodological details are sufficiently thorough for reproducibility, including comprehensive parameter exploration and clear experimental setup. The paper also makes a theoretically sound distinction between integrated compositional models and ad-hoc composition approaches.

Weaknesses: The scope is limited to noun compounds, and the paper doesn't sufficiently address how the approach might generalize to other phrase types or sentence structures. The comparison with neural embeddings is restricted to basic cbow/skip-gram models without exploring more advanced neural composition methods. The paper also doesn't fully explain why syntactic structure provides such significant gains beyond noting its importance.

The paper would benefit from: (1) discussing limitations regarding phrase type generalizability, (2) comparing with more recent neural composition techniques, and (3) providing deeper analysis of why syntactic structure improves compositionality detection. While the work is compelling for its specific task, the authors should acknowledge the narrower scope more explicitly.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to compositionality detection and demonstrates that integrated compositional approaches with syntactic awareness outperform simpler methods. With minor revisions addressing the scope limitations and comparison with more advanced neural methods, this work would make a strong contribution to the conference.
