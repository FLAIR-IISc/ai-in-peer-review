- Primary contribution: Typed dependency path representations (APT) significantly improve noun-compound compositionality detection over untyped methods.  
- Secondary contribution: A hybrid of aligned and unaligned APT features yields optimal performance, showing both syntactic and raw contextual features matter.  
- Tertiary contribution: Neural word embeddings (e.g., cbow) underperform with naive composition, partly attributed to their untyped nature.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results — APT-based hybrid (ρ=0.79) significantly outperforms prior SOTA and neural baselines with robust cross-validation.  
- Strength: Clear analysis of feature types demonstrating why typed dependencies and combining aligned/unaligned features are necessary.  
- Strength: Thorough baseline comparison (RAND, FREQ, REDDY) revealing and addressing dataset frequency-compositionality effects.  
- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of why neural embeddings underperform — untyped features vs low-frequency compounds or other causes not explored.  
- Weakness: Overstates novelty of “integrated” composition since composition after alignment uses simple addition; main innovation is feature engineering (typed paths).  
- Weakness: Superficial treatment of human judgment noise and disagreement; lack of analysis on rating variability and dataset limitations.  
- Question: Why do neural embeddings (even tuned cbow) fail to match APT — untyped features, low-frequency data, or fundamental model limits?  
- Question: How would results generalize to non-noun compounds (e.g., verb phrases) noted as future work?  
- Question: Could the hybrid method’s reliance on unaligned features be caused by dependency parsing noise (e.g., parsing errors in NMOD relations)?
