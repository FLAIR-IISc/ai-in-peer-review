

Summary: The paper investigates compositionality detection for noun compounds using a novel distributional compositional approach called Anchored Packed Trees (APT). The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that typed higher-order dependency paths with proper alignment significantly improve compositionality detection over previous state-of-the-art, (2) showing that a hybrid approach combining aligned and unaligned APT representations yields optimal results, and (3) providing evidence that syntactic structure encoded in dependency paths is highly informative for compositionality detection.

Strengths: The paper makes a significant contribution to compositionality detection through a well-motivated approach that integrates syntactic structure directly into the compositional model. The APT approach with typed dependency paths provides a linguistically grounded method for composition that outperforms previous work (0.79 vs. 0.71 Spearman's rho), representing a substantial improvement. The experimental design is rigorous, using the Reddy et al. benchmark with appropriate cross-validation and careful attention to the distinction between aligned and unaligned composition. The paper provides a thorough analysis of parameter effects, particularly noting that optimal values for h and q fall within [0.3, 0.5], suggesting the modifier is slightly more informative than the head in determining compositionality. The comparison between APT representations and neural embeddings is particularly valuable, showing that the syntactic structure encoded in APT features provides more useful information than standard neural embeddings for this specific task.

Weaknesses: The paper does not sufficiently explain why the hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned representations) outperforms either approach alone, which is a key finding. A more detailed analysis of the complementary information provided by aligned and unaligned features would strengthen the paper. The neural embedding experiments are limited in scope, using only a narrow parameter range for CBOW and skip-gram models without exploring more comprehensive hyperparameter tuning. The paper does not address computational costs or scalability of the APT approach, which is important for practical applications. The generalizability of the approach to other phrase types beyond noun compounds is not adequately discussed, limiting the broader impact of the findings.

Questions to Authors: How might the findings about the relative informativeness of aligned versus unaligned representations generalize to other types of phrases beyond noun compounds? What is the computational cost of the APT approach compared to simpler composition methods, and how does this impact potential real-world applications? Could the hybrid approach be theoretically justified based on linguistic properties of compositionality, rather than solely empirical results?
