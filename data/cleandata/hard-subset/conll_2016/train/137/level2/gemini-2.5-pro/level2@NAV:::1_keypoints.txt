- Proposes APT: sparse, high-dimensional, syntax-aware representations encoding higher-order dependency paths for noun-compound compositionality detection.  
- Introduces a novel feature-space vector alignment step and compares integrated compositional APTs to weighted-addition of dense word2vec embeddings.  
- Evaluated on Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark, achieving a new state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments.  

- Contribution: Shows sparse, syntax-aware APTs can significantly outperform robust syntax-agnostic dense embeddings on noun-compound compositionality.  
- Contribution: Provides a detailed ablation study analyzing composition operations (additive vs. intersective) and feature-space alignment.  
- Contribution: Establishes a new strong baseline (SOTA) on the Reddy et al. dataset.  

- Strength: Strong empirical performance (Spearman ρ=0.79), outperforming prior SOTA (0.71) and tuned neural baseline (0.74) with sound experimental methodology.  
- Strength: Linguistically motivated, interpretable method leveraging explicit dependency-path syntactic information.  
- Strength: Thorough analysis exploring aligned, unaligned, and hybrid variants and revealing key component effects (e.g., effectiveness of unaligned typed features).  

- Weakness: Evaluation limited to a single small English dataset (90 compounds), leaving generalizability to other phrase types and languages unclear.  
- Weakness: Alignment motivation weakened because unaligned model (ρ=0.78) outperforms aligned-only (ρ=0.75); hybrid wins, so alignment’s centrality is questionable.  
- Weakness: Practicality and scalability concerns due to very high-dimensional sparse vectors (~230k) with no discussion of memory or runtime trade-offs versus compact neural models.
