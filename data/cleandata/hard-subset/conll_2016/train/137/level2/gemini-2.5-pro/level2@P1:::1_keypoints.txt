- APT model with sparse, structured syntactic features significantly outperforms dense embedding composition; hybrid APT achieves new SOTA Spearman’s ρ = 0.79.  
- Comparison of APT composition variants shows the unaligned model surprisingly outperforms the theoretically-motivated aligned version, with a hybrid of both performing best.  
- A strong word2vec (CBOW) baseline is established (ρ = 0.74), exceeding previous published SOTA and raising the bar for future work.  
- Significant empirical improvement over prior work, demonstrating effectiveness of dependency-based composition on the benchmark.  
- Experimental design is thorough and principled: same corpus as prior work, cross-validated hyperparameter tuning, averaged results, and multiple baselines.  
- Ablation study (aligned vs. unaligned vs. hybrid) provides valuable insight that different alignment strategies are complementary.  
- Paper is clearly written, with well-explained motivation, model description, and results presentation.  
- Evaluation is limited to a single small dataset (90 noun-noun compounds), so generalizability to other constructions, languages, or downstream tasks is unclear.  
- The paper lacks a detailed explanation or error analysis for why the unaligned model outperforms the aligned model.  
- Practical trade-offs are not discussed: APT uses very high-dimensional (≈230k) sparse vectors, raising scalability, memory, and data-sparsity concerns.  
- Request for deeper analysis: provide intuition or error examples explaining the strong performance of the unaligned model versus the aligned one.  
- Question about scalability of alignment: how would APT alignment behave on more syntactically complex phrases (e.g., multi-argument verb phrases)?  
- Clarify preprocessing choice: why dependency paths involving the modifier were ignored, and whether modifier-modifying contexts (e.g., “very hot” in “very hot water heater”) might help composition.
