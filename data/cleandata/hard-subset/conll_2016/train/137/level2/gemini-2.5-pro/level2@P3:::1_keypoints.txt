- Task & approach: Detects noun-compound compositionality using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) — sparse dependency-path vectors with structural alignment of modifier to head — compared to additive word2vec baselines.  
- Main result: Hybrid APT sets a new state-of-the-art on Reddy et al. (2011), achieving Spearman’s ρ = 0.79 (previous 0.714).  
- Contribution 1: Presents state-of-the-art empirical results on the standard noun-compound compositionality benchmark.  
- Contribution 2: Evaluates a structure-aware compositional model that integrates syntactic alignment into the feature space.  
- Contribution 3: Provides a thorough comparative analysis of sparse dependency-based vs. dense neural representations and different composition strategies.  
- Strength: Strong experimental methodology — same corpus (ukWaC), strong neural baselines, and robust cross-validation for tuning.  
- Strength: Excellent empirical performance with clear gains over neural baselines (cbow ρ=0.74 → hybrid APT ρ=0.79).  
- Strength: Insightful analysis of aligned, unaligned, and hybrid models, showing complementary information captured by hybrid modeling.  
- Strength: Clear writing and presentation; APT mechanics and alignment explained with helpful examples.  
- Weakness: Limited evaluation on a single, small dataset (90 compounds), constraining claims about generality across tasks and phrase types.  
- Weakness: Unclear why the unaligned APT (ρ=0.78) outperforms the aligned-only model (ρ=0.75); needs deeper analysis of alignment’s effectiveness.  
- Weakness: Practical concerns about very high-dimensional sparse vectors (up to ~230k dims) vs. low-dim dense embeddings—memory, compute, and scalability trade-offs not discussed.  
- Question 1: Can authors explain whether the unaligned model’s superiority is specific to the NMOD relation or indicates a broader limitation of the alignment method?  
- Question 2: Has error analysis been done to see if modifier vs. head importance (weight h) varies by compound type (literal vs. idiomatic)?
