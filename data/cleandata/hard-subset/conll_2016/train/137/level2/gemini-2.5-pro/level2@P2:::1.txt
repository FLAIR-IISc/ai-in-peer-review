**Summary of the paper**

This paper investigates the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds. The authors compare two distinct approaches. The primary approach is an integrated compositional distributional model using sparse, high-dimensional vectors derived from typed, higher-order dependency paths, which the authors call Anchored Packed Trees (APTs). This model is designed to explicitly handle syntactic structure by "aligning" the vector spaces of constituents before composition. The second approach represents the current common practice: applying a simple composition function (weighted addition) to dense, low-dimensional neural word embeddings (word2vec). Both methods are evaluated on the Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark dataset by measuring the correlation between a model's predicted compositionality score (similarity between a composed vector and an observed phrase vector) and human judgments. The experimental results show that the APT-based models, particularly a hybrid model that combines aligned and unaligned features, achieve a new state-of-the-art on this task, significantly outperforming both the original reported baseline and a strong, updated baseline using word2vec.

**Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is the demonstration that an integrated compositional model built on explicit, high-dimensional syntactic features (the APT model) can substantially outperform standard compositional methods that use dense neural embeddings for the task of compositionality detection. This provides strong evidence for the value of retaining structured syntactic information for this specific semantic challenge.
2.  The paper presents a thorough and rigorous empirical study, establishing a new state-of-the-art on a standard benchmark. This includes a valuable analysis of multiple variants of the proposed model (e.g., aligned vs. unaligned composition) and an updated, strong baseline using word2vec, which itself surpasses the previously published best result.
3.  The paper introduces a novel "hybrid" composition method within the APT framework that combines aligned (structurally-aware) and unaligned features. The success of this hybrid model suggests that both types of information are complementary and important for accurately modeling noun compound compositionality.

**Strengths**

1.  **Impressive Empirical Results:** The paper achieves a new state-of-the-art on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, improving the Spearman’s rho from a previous best of 0.714 to 0.79. This is a very significant improvement. The authors also responsibly establish a much stronger baseline using word2vec (0.74), which makes their own model's superior performance even more convincing.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Interpretable Model:** The APT model is linguistically well-motivated. The core idea of using typed dependency paths as features and explicitly aligning vector spaces based on the syntactic relationship between constituents is intuitive and provides a clear contrast to the more black-box nature of composing dense embeddings. The distinction between aligned, unaligned, and hybrid models allows for a clear analysis of the contribution of syntactic structure.
3.  **Rigorous and Transparent Evaluation:** The experimental setup is sound. The authors use the same large corpus (ukWaC) as the original work for fair comparison, employ cross-validation for hyperparameter tuning, and report significance. They explore a reasonable range of parameters for both their own model and the word2vec baseline, lending credibility to their findings. The inclusion of multiple baselines (RAND, FREQ, REDDY) effectively situates the contributions of the work.
4.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The APT approach is explained with a helpful concrete example (Table 1), and the experimental methodology is described in sufficient detail for replication.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The claims about the general promise of the APT approach are based on experiments on a single task (noun compound compositionality) using a single dataset. While the paper excels on this task, it is unclear how well the findings generalize. For instance, would the model's advantage hold for other compositional phenomena like adjective-noun or verb-object phrases, or on other tasks like phrase similarity? Showing strong performance on at least one other compositional task would make the claims much more robust.
2.  **Lack of Discussion on Practicality and Scalability:** The APT model relies on very high-dimensional sparse vectors (e.g., ~230,000 dimensions for nouns). The paper does not discuss the computational trade-offs (memory footprint, processing time) compared to the compact neural embedding models. While performance is key, a brief discussion of the practical costs of building and using these large representations would be valuable for researchers looking to apply this method.
3.  **Insufficient Intuition for Hybrid Model's Success:** The hybrid model, which linearly combines aligned and unaligned vectors for the modifier, yields the best results. The paper notes that this suggests both are useful but does not delve deeper into *why* this might be the case. Is it because the NMOD relation is semantically vague, making the modifier's original context (unaligned) just as informative as its context projected through the head (aligned)? A more detailed analysis or error analysis could provide valuable insights into the nature of noun-noun composition.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please comment on the practical trade-offs of the APT model? Specifically, how do the memory requirements and computation time for creating the representations and performing composition compare to the word2vec baseline?
2.  Your best-performing model is a hybrid of aligned and unaligned composition. Could you elaborate on your intuition for why the unaligned features provide such a strong complementary signal? Does an error analysis reveal specific types of compounds where the unaligned component is particularly helpful?
3.  You make an interesting observation that less aggressive subsampling (a higher threshold) in word2vec helps on this task, hypothesizing that this is due to the low frequency of many target compounds. Did you analyze if this effect is primarily driven by the low-frequency items in the dataset, or does it improve performance across the board, even for more frequent compounds?
