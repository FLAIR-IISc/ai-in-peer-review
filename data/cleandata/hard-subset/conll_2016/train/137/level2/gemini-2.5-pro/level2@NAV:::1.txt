**Summary of the paper:**
This paper addresses the problem of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds, proposing a compositional model based on sparse, high-dimensional, syntax-aware representations called Anchored Packed Trees (APTs). These representations encode higher-order dependency paths as features. The authors compare this integrated compositional approach, which includes a novel vector alignment step, against the common ad-hoc method of applying weighted addition to dense neural embeddings (word2vec). The evaluation is performed on the standard Reddy et al. (2011) benchmark, correlating model predictions with human judgments of compositionality and demonstrating that the proposed syntax-aware model achieves a new state-of-the-art.

**Main Contributions:**
1.  A strong empirical demonstration that a sparse, syntax-aware distributional model (APT) can significantly outperform a robust, syntax-agnostic dense neural embedding model on the task of compositionality detection for noun compounds.
2.  A detailed ablation study of the proposed APT model, systematically analyzing the impact of different composition operations (additive vs. intersective) and the novel concept of feature-space alignment, providing valuable insights into the model's behavior.
3.  A new state-of-the-art result on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, establishing a strong new baseline for future research in this area.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper presents convincing results, achieving a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, which is a notable improvement over both the previous state-of-the-art (0.71) and a well-tuned neural embedding baseline (0.74). The experimental methodology, including repeated cross-validation, is sound.
2.  **Linguistically Motivated Method:** The APT approach is well-motivated, leveraging explicit syntactic information from dependency paths. This provides a compelling, interpretable alternative to the black-box nature of many neural models and directly tests the value of structured linguistic features for this specific task.
3.  **Thorough Analysis:** The authors perform an excellent analysis of their model, exploring multiple variants (aligned, unaligned, hybrid) and parameter settings. This sheds light on which components are most critical for success and reveals interesting findings, such as the surprising effectiveness of unaligned typed features.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Evaluation Scope:** The study's conclusions are based entirely on a single, relatively small dataset of 90 English noun compounds. The generalizability of these findings to other phrasal types (e.g., adjective-noun, verb-object) or other languages remains an open question.
2.  **Motivation for Alignment Undermined by Results:** A key theoretical novelty is the feature alignment process. However, the results show that the unaligned model (ρ=0.78) substantially outperforms the aligned-only model (ρ=0.75). While the hybrid model is best, this finding weakens the argument that alignment itself is the crucial mechanism and warrants a deeper analysis in the paper.
3.  **Practicality and Scalability Concerns:** The paper does not address the scalability of the APT model. Given the use of very high-dimensional sparse vectors (~230k), a discussion of the computational trade-offs (memory, processing time) compared to the compact neural embedding models is essential for assessing the model’s practical viability.
