**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the task of detecting the compositionality of noun compounds. It introduces a compositional distributional model based on Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which use sparse, high-dimensional vectors derived from typed dependency paths. The key mechanism of this model is the structural alignment of a modifier's vector space to the head's space before composition. This integrated approach is compared against a common ad-hoc method: applying weighted addition to state-of-the-art dense word embeddings (word2vec). Both methods are evaluated by measuring the similarity between a composed vector and an observed vector for a given compound, and correlating this similarity score with human judgments of compositionality from the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. The work demonstrates that the dependency-based APT models, particularly a hybrid version, outperform strong neural embedding baselines and set a new state-of-the-art on this benchmark.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **State-of-the-art Empirical Results:** The paper presents a new state-of-the-art on the widely used Reddy et al. (2011) noun compound compositionality dataset. The proposed hybrid APT model achieves a Spearman's ρ of 0.79, a notable improvement over the previous benchmark of 0.714.
2.  **Evaluation of a Structure-Aware Compositional Model:** The work provides a detailed evaluation of the APT model, which integrates composition directly into the feature space through a syntactically motivated alignment of dependency-path vectors. This provides a compelling alternative to more common ad-hoc composition functions applied to pre-trained embeddings.
3.  **A Thorough Comparative Analysis:** The paper offers a valuable and detailed comparison between different representation types (sparse dependency-based vs. dense neural) and composition strategies (aligned vs. unaligned, additive vs. intersective) for the compositionality detection task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Experimental Methodology:** The experimental setup is rigorous and well-designed. The work directly compares against the established benchmark from Reddy et al. (2011), uses the same large corpus (ukWaC) to ensure a fair comparison, and implements a very strong baseline using modern neural embeddings (word2vec). The results are robustly validated using repeated 3-fold cross-validation for parameter tuning.
2.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The proposed APT models achieve impressive results, significantly outperforming not only the previous state-of-the-art but also the strong neural baseline established within the paper itself. The performance gain from ρ=0.74 (cbow) to ρ=0.79 (hybrid APT) clearly demonstrates the practical effectiveness of using typed dependency features for this task.
3.  **Insightful Analysis and Discussion:** The paper provides more than just performance numbers; it offers a nuanced analysis of the results. The investigation into aligned, unaligned, and hybrid models is particularly insightful. The discovery that a hybrid model performs best suggests that both aligned (structurally transformed) and unaligned (direct) dependency features capture complementary information about compositionality, which is a valuable finding.
4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The problem is well-contextualized, and the mechanics of the APT model, including the core concept of vector space alignment via path offsetting, are explained effectively with clear examples (Tables 1 and 2).

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is confined to a single, relatively small dataset of 90 English noun-noun compounds. While this is a standard benchmark, the conclusions about the superiority of the APT model are based solely on this specific linguistic construction and task. To strengthen the claims about the model's general utility, it would be beneficial to evaluate it on other compositional tasks (e.g., phrase similarity) or on different phrase types (e.g., adjective-noun or verb-object constructions). While this may be beyond the scope of a short paper, the current limited evaluation should be acknowledged as a potential constraint on the generality of the findings.
2.  **Unclear Implications of the Unaligned Model's Performance:** A central motivation for the APT model is the syntactic alignment of vector spaces. It is therefore surprising that the unaligned model (ρ=0.78) significantly outperforms the aligned-only model (ρ=0.75). The paper hypothesizes this may be due to feature overlap in NMOD relations but does not explore this critical finding in depth. A more detailed analysis of why alignment is less effective than expected on its own would greatly improve the paper. For example, is this an artifact of noun-noun compounds, or does it point to a more fundamental aspect of how distributional information should be combined?
3.  **Practical Considerations of the Model:** The APT model uses high-dimensional, sparse vectors (up to 230,000 dimensions). While effective, this contrasts sharply with the low-dimensional dense vectors used in the neural baseline. The paper would be more complete with a brief discussion of the practical trade-offs, such as memory usage, computational cost, and scalability of the APT approach compared to the word2vec models. This would provide readers with a fuller picture of the costs and benefits associated with the performance gains.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could the paper elaborate further on the finding that the unaligned APT model outperforms the aligned version? Is this phenomenon hypothesized to be specific to the NMOD relation, which is predominant in the dataset, or could it suggest a broader limitation of the proposed alignment method?
2.  The analysis of the optimal weight parameter `h` suggests the modifier is slightly more informative than the head. This is an interesting result. Has an error analysis been performed to see if the relative importance of the modifier versus the head changes depending on the type of compound (e.g., highly literal vs. highly idiomatic)?
