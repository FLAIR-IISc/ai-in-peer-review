- Core claim: incorporating typed dependency information, especially higher-order paths, improves detection of non-compositional phrases over simple compositional ops on neural embeddings.  
- Main contribution: experimental comparison on Reddy et al. (2011) dataset enabling direct evaluation of compositional approaches.  
- Secondary contribution: exploration of aligned vs unaligned dependency features within the APT model, showing benefits of syntactic structure.  
- Strength: addresses an important problem—handling non-compositionality in distributional semantics using compositionality detection as a sensible testbed.  
- Strength: comprehensive experiments across parameters (PPMI smoothing, composition ops, embedding dimensionality) and multiple baselines.  
- Strength: clear, well-structured presentation of the Anchored Packed Trees (APT) model with helpful illustrative tables.  
- Strength: empirically strong results—APT (especially hybrid) outperforms neural embeddings and prior state-of-the-art.  
- Strength: insightful analysis of parameter effects (e.g., roles of h and q) on model behavior.  
- Weakness: limited error analysis—insufficient exploration of why specific models fail on certain phrases.  
- Weakness: evaluation limited to a single dataset (Reddy et al. 2011), leaving generalizability to other non-compositional phenomena unclear.  
- Weakness: likely higher computational complexity of APT (high-dimensional features, dependency path calculations) with no detailed trade-off discussion.  
- Weakness: refers to a “suppressed reference” about prior APT work, hindering reproducibility and context.  
- Weakness: missing experimental details in corpus preprocessing, especially how multiple dependency relations for compound nouns were handled.  
- Question: please provide a more detailed error analysis highlighting challenging types of non-compositional phrases.  
- Question: what are the memory and processing-time costs of APT compared to simple neural embedding addition?  
- Question: can the authors reveal the “suppressed reference” or provide a citation (e.g., technical report)?  
- Question: how was the most frequent dependency relation chosen for compound nouns, and how were ties resolved?  
- Additional comment: paper is well-written with informative figures/tables and comprehensive references; minor improvements to error analysis and experimental details would strengthen it.
