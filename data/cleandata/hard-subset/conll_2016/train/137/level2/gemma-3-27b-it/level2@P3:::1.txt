This paper investigates compositional distributional semantics, specifically focusing on the detection of non-compositional phrases. The core claim is that incorporating typed dependency information, particularly higher-order paths, into distributional models can improve the detection of non-compositionality compared to applying simple compositional operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings. A significant contribution of the work lies in the experimental setup, utilizing the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for compositionality detection, allowing for a direct comparison of different compositional approaches. A further contribution is the exploration of both aligned and unaligned dependency features within the APT model, demonstrating the benefits of incorporating syntactic structure into compositional semantics.

Strengths:

1. **Relevant and Well-Defined Problem:** The paper addresses a critical issue in distributional semantics – the handling of non-compositionality. Recognizing that standard compositional methods struggle with idioms and non-literal phrases is crucial for building more robust semantic models. The use of compositionality detection as a testbed is a sensible approach.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The extensive experimentation with different parameters (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, dimensionality of embeddings) and models (APT, neural embeddings) is a major strength. The inclusion of multiple baselines (RAND, FREQ, Reddy et al.) provides a strong context for evaluating the proposed methods.
3. **Clear Presentation of the APT Model:** The explanation of the Anchored Packed Trees (APT) approach is clear and well-structured. The use of Table 1 to illustrate the typed dependency features is particularly helpful in understanding the core idea of the model. The explanation of the alignment and composition processes is also concise and informative.
4. **Empirically Strong Results:** The results demonstrate that the proposed APT models, particularly the hybrid approach, outperform both neural embeddings and the previous state-of-the-art reported in Reddy et al. (2011). This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of incorporating typed dependency information into compositional models.
5. **Insightful Analysis of Results:** The discussion of the optimal parameter settings (e.g., the role of h and q in the composition operations) provides valuable insights into the behavior of the models and the nature of compositional semantics.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper presents strong overall results, there is limited discussion of *why* certain models fail or make specific errors. A more detailed error analysis, perhaps focusing on specific types of non-compositional phrases, could provide further insights into the limitations of the proposed approaches.
2. **Dataset Specificity:** The evaluation is exclusively based on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset. While this allows for direct comparison, the generalizability of the results to other types of non-compositional phenomena (e.g., verb-particle constructions, multi-word idioms) is unclear. Testing on additional datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Computational Complexity:** The APT model, with its higher-dimensional feature spaces and dependency path calculations, is likely to be computationally more expensive than simpler approaches like adding neural word embeddings. A discussion of the computational trade-offs would be beneficial, particularly for large-scale applications.
4. **Suppressed Reference:** The paper refers to a "suppressed reference" regarding previous work on APTs. While understandable for space constraints, this hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult for readers to fully understand the context of the current work. Providing a citation, even if it's to a technical report, would be preferable.
5. **Missing Details in Experimental Setup:** The description of the corpus preprocessing steps, particularly the handling of multiple dependency relationships for compound nouns, could be more detailed. Clarifying how the most frequent dependency relation was selected and how this impacted the data would be helpful.

Questions to Authors:

1. Could the authors provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting specific types of non-compositional phrases that are particularly challenging for the proposed models?
2. What is the computational cost associated with the APT model, particularly in terms of memory usage and processing time, compared to simpler approaches like adding neural word embeddings?
3. Could the authors elaborate on the "suppressed reference" and provide a citation, even if it's to a technical report or workshop proceeding?
4. In the corpus preprocessing stage, how was the most frequent dependency relation selected when multiple relations were observed for a compound noun? Were any criteria used to resolve ties?

Additional Comments:

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The figures and tables are informative and contribute to the overall understanding of the work. The references are comprehensive and relevant. Minor improvements to the error analysis and experimental details would further strengthen the paper.
