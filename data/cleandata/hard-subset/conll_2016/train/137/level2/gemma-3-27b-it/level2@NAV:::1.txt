## Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

This paper investigates the impact of contextual features, particularly typed dependency paths, on the ability of compositional distributional models to handle non-compositional phrases. The authors compare an integrated compositional approach leveraging sparse, high-dimensional representations with applying composition operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings. The central motivation is that evaluating compositionality detection provides a strong testbed for compositional semantic models. 

The primary contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation of different compositional methods – including variations of APT-based composition – on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for compositionality detection. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating that incorporating typed dependency information, especially higher-order paths, improves performance compared to purely untyped approaches. A third, albeit less prominent, contribution is showing that cbow embeddings outperform skip-gram embeddings in this specific task, offering potentially useful parameter insights.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive experimental setup, systematically varying parameters within both the APT model and neural embeddings. The use of the established Reddy et al. dataset provides a solid benchmark.
*   **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of non-compositionality in language and its importance for evaluating semantic models.
*   **Detailed Model Description:** The APT model, with its use of anchored packed trees and dependency paths, is described with sufficient detail for understanding and potential replication.
*   **Significant Results:** The authors consistently outperform the state-of-the-art reported in Reddy et al. (2011), indicating a genuine advancement. The finding regarding the relative performance of cbow vs skip-gram is also valuable. 

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Novelty in Core Idea:** While the execution is solid, the core idea of leveraging dependency information for compositional semantics isn't entirely new. The paper doesn’t sufficiently position itself against the broader landscape of related work beyond directly citing relevant papers.
*   **Dataset Limitations:** The Reddy et al. dataset is relatively small (90 compound nouns) and may not generalize well to other types of non-compositional phrases or more complex compositional scenarios.
*   **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the results are reported, there’s limited discussion of *where* the models fail and *why*.  A closer examination of misclassified examples could reveal crucial insights.
*   **Presentation:** The paper is quite dense and could benefit from more visual aids (e.g., diagrams illustrating the APT process) and clearer sectioning.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the APT approach, particularly concerning the dimensionality of the feature space, and how this scales with larger corpora?
2.  Have you explored methods to address the issue of sparse data in the higher-order dependency paths? 
3.  What specific types of errors does the model make frequently? Is there a discernible pattern in the misclassified examples?
