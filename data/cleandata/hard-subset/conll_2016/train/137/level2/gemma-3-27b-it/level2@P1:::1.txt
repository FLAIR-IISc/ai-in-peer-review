This paper investigates the detection of non-compositional phrases, using this as a testbed for compositional distributional semantics. The authors compare an integrated compositional distributional approach, leveraging sparse high-dimensional representations based on dependency trees, with a simpler approach of applying compositional operations to pre-trained neural embeddings.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the comprehensive empirical evaluation of compositional methods using the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for compositionality detection. The authors systematically explore different parameter settings for both their integrated approach (APT models) and the neural embedding baseline, providing a thorough comparative analysis. A secondary, related contribution is the demonstration that incorporating typed dependency information—specifically, higher-order dependency paths—into distributional representations improves performance on this task, outperforming standard neural embedding baselines. Finally, the detailed ablation study investigating alignment vs. unaligned APTs, and different composition operations (INT vs UNI) provides useful insights into the behavior of these models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a well-designed and extensive empirical evaluation. The systematic exploration of different parameters (PPMI smoothing, composition operations, alignment) for both the APT models and the neural embedding baseline is a significant strength. This level of detail allows for a nuanced understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
2. **Strong Baseline & Reproducibility:** The use of established methods like word2vec and the clear description of experimental setup facilitates reproducibility.  The comparison to the Reddy et al. (2011) state-of-the-art result provides a strong benchmark for the proposed methods. The authors surpass the previous state-of-the-art, indicating a genuine improvement.
3. **Insightful Ablation Study:** The ablation study concerning aligned vs. unaligned APTs, and different composition operations, provides valuable insights into how these components affect performance.  The finding that a hybrid approach (combining aligned and unaligned features) performs best suggests a complex interplay between dependency structure and distributional similarity.
4. **Focus on Compositionality as a Testbed:** The framing of non-compositionality detection as a testbed for compositional semantics is a sensible approach. It highlights the importance of evaluating models not just on similarity tasks, but also on their ability to handle phrases that defy simple composition.
5. **Clear Presentation of the APT Model:** The explanation of the Anchored Packed Trees (APT) model is relatively clear, with the illustrative example in Table 1 helping to understand the concept of typed features.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper provides quantitative results, it lacks a detailed qualitative error analysis. Understanding *which* types of non-compositional phrases the models struggle with could provide valuable insights for future work. For example, are certain types of idioms consistently misclassified?
2. **Corpus Specificity:** The results are based on a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011). While this allows for a direct comparison to existing work, it raises questions about the generalizability of the findings. Testing on other compositionality detection datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Complexity of the APT Model:** The APT model, with its various parameters and stages (feature construction, alignment, composition), is relatively complex. While the explanation is clear, the added complexity may not be fully justified by the performance gains, particularly given the strong performance of the simpler neural embedding baseline after optimization.
4. **Motivation for Specific Parameter Choices:** The justification for some parameter choices, such as the higher subsampling threshold for the cbow model, is somewhat limited. A more detailed explanation of the rationale behind these choices would be helpful.
5. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Embedding Models:** The experiments primarily use the word2vec tool. Comparing against more recent and potentially more powerful embedding models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) would provide a more contemporary benchmark.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide examples of phrases that your model consistently misclassifies, and what characteristics do these phrases share?
2. Have you experimented with other compositionality detection datasets to assess the generalizability of your findings?
3. Is there a clear trade-off between the computational cost of the APT model and its performance gains compared to the simpler neural embedding approach?
4. You mention that shifted PMI didn't perform well; did you experiment with different parameter settings for the shift? Was this consistent across different parameter settings?
5. Regarding the hybrid model, could you elaborate on why combining aligned and unaligned features leads to better performance? What specific information does each component contribute?
