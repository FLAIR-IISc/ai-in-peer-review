This paper investigates the detection of non-compositional phrases using compositional distributional methods, comparing an integrated approach with applying composition operations to neural embeddings. The core idea is to explore how the definition of context (typed vs. untyped) and the use of higher-order dependency paths impact a model’s ability to support composition. The authors leverage the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset for evaluating compositionality detection.

The primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical evaluation of different compositional methods – specifically, APT-based models with varying degrees of alignment and composition functions – on the challenging task of compositionality detection. While the general idea of using compositional models for this task isn’t novel (as acknowledged in the related work), the detailed exploration of typed contextual features and higher-order dependency paths, coupled with the comparative analysis against strong neural embedding baselines, provides valuable insights. A second, supporting contribution lies in the demonstration that models incorporating dependency information (especially the hybrid approach) consistently outperform those relying solely on untyped co-occurrence statistics. Finally, the paper contributes a comprehensive experimental setup and replication of Reddy et al.’s (2011) work, allowing for direct comparison and extension of previous findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The paper offers a well-structured and thorough empirical evaluation. The authors systematically explore various parameters within the APT model (PPMI weighting, alignment strategies, composition functions) and compare them against strong baselines (neural word embeddings). This meticulousness provides a solid foundation for drawing conclusions.
2. **Focus on Dependency Information:** The emphasis on incorporating dependency information – both through typed features and higher-order paths – is a key strength. The results clearly demonstrate the benefits of leveraging syntactic structure for improving compositionality detection. The hybrid approach, intelligently combining aligned and unaligned features, appears particularly promising.
3. **Replication & Extension of Existing Work:** By replicating and extending the experiments from Reddy et al. (2011), the authors ground their work in established literature and provide a clear benchmark for comparison. Achieving better performance than the previous state-of-the-art underscores the value of their approach.
4. **Clear Presentation of APT Model:** The explanation of the Anchored Packed Trees (APT) model is clear and well-illustrated with the example in Table 1. This helps readers understand the core concepts and appreciate the nuances of the proposed method.
5. **Insightful Analysis of Results:** The authors provide thoughtful analysis of the results, offering explanations for observed trends (e.g., the benefit of smoothing PPMI, the preference for unaligned features in some cases). This demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying mechanisms.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Neural Baselines:** While the paper compares against neural word embeddings, the exploration of different neural architectures and training procedures feels somewhat limited. More recent and sophisticated neural compositional models (e.g., those based on transformers or graph neural networks) could potentially offer stronger baselines and provide a more challenging test for the APT approach.
2. **Dataset Limitations:** The Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, while valuable, is relatively small (90 compound nouns). The generalizability of the findings to other types of phrases or domains is uncertain. Exploring additional datasets or creating a larger, more diverse dataset would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Hyperparameter Optimization:** The paper mentions parameter optimization but lacks detail regarding the specific search strategies employed. A more detailed description of the optimization process would increase confidence in the reported results. The rationale provided for the optimal cbow parameter settings relating to subsampling could also be expanded.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A deeper error analysis would be beneficial. Identifying specific types of non-compositional phrases that the models struggle with could provide valuable insights for future research. Why does the model fail for certain cases?
5. **Theoretical Justification:** The motivation for the hybrid approach is intuitively reasonable, but a more rigorous theoretical justification would strengthen the argument. Why is combining aligned and unaligned features particularly effective?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details regarding the hyperparameter optimization process, including the search space and the optimization algorithm used?
2.  Have you experimented with more recent neural compositional models (e.g., transformer-based models) as baselines? If not, could you comment on the potential impact of including such models in the evaluation?
3.  Can you provide some examples of phrases where the model performs poorly, and discuss the potential reasons for the errors?
4.  What is the computational cost associated with the APT model, compared to the neural embedding baselines? Is the increased complexity justified by the performance gains?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation used for the composition operations (e.g., ⊔INT, ⊔UNI) could be made more consistent.
*   The reference suppression ("reference suppressed") in Section 3 should be resolved.
*   A table summarizing the key characteristics of the datasets used in related work might be helpful for readers.
