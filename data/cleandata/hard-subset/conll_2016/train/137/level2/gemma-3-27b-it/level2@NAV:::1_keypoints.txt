- Paper evaluates how contextual features (typed dependency paths) affect compositional distributional models for detecting non-compositional phrases.  
- Main contribution: thorough empirical comparison of compositional methods (including APT variants) on the Reddy et al. (2011) compositionality dataset.  
- Secondary contribution: showing typed dependency information, especially higher-order paths, improves detection over untyped approaches.  
- Tertiary contribution: cbow embeddings outperform skip-gram for this task, offering useful parameter insight.  
- Strong empirical evaluation with systematic parameter variations and use of a standard benchmark.  
- Clear motivation and problem definition emphasizing non-compositionality as a testbed for semantic models.  
- Detailed description of the APT model and dependency-path-based composition enabling reproducibility.  
- Significant results that outperform prior state-of-the-art reported in Reddy et al. (2011).  
- Limited novelty: leveraging dependency information for composition is not entirely new and broader related work positioning is weak.  
- Dataset limitation: Reddy et al. dataset is small (90 compounds) and may not generalize to other phrase types or complex compositions.  
- Lack of error analysis: paper gives results but insufficient examination of failure cases and misclassification patterns.  
- Presentation could be improved—paper is dense and would benefit from more visuals and clearer sectioning.  
- Questions for authors: clarify APT computational cost and scaling with feature dimensionality and corpus size.  
- Questions for authors: have they addressed sparsity in higher-order dependency paths?  
- Questions for authors: what error types occur frequently and is there a pattern among misclassified examples?
