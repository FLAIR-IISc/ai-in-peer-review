## Review: When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases

This paper investigates compositional distributional semantics, specifically focusing on improving the detection of non-compositional phrases. The authors compare an integrated compositional approach using sparse, high-dimensional representations (based on anchored packed trees - APTs) with a simpler approach of applying compositional operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings. The core motivation is that accurately identifying non-compositionality is crucial for evaluating compositional models and understanding language.

The primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical evaluation of different compositional methods, particularly highlighting the benefits of incorporating dependency structure (via APTs) into the compositional process.  A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating that even relatively simple modifications – like using higher-order dependency paths – can significantly improve performance. Finally, the paper provides a strong baseline using optimized neural embeddings, offering a valuable comparison point for future research.

**Strengths:**

*   **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments using a well-established dataset (Reddy et al., 2011) and rigorous cross-validation. The results clearly demonstrate the advantage of incorporating dependency information.
*   **Novel Use of APTs:** The application of APTs to compositionality detection is a novel and insightful approach. The detailed explanation of how dependency paths are used to align vector spaces is particularly valuable.
*   **Clear Comparison to Baselines:** The comparison to both neural embeddings and previous work (Reddy et al., 2011) provides a strong context for the reported improvements.
*   **Detailed Analysis:** The authors provide a nuanced discussion of the results, exploring the impact of different parameters (PPMI smoothing, composition operations) and offering insightful interpretations.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Scope of Neural Embedding Exploration:** While the neural embedding baseline is strong, the parameter search seems somewhat limited. Exploring a wider range of embedding dimensions and training parameters could further refine the comparison.
*   **Corpus Dependency:** The results are heavily tied to the ukWaC corpus.  It would be beneficial to test the proposed methods on other corpora to assess their generalizability.
*   **Complexity of APTs:** The explanation of APTs, while detailed, is also quite complex. This could make it difficult for readers unfamiliar with dependency parsing to fully grasp the method. A more intuitive explanation or visual representation might be helpful.
*   **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the results are presented clearly, a deeper error analysis (e.g., identifying specific types of phrases where the model struggles) would provide further insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of constructing and composing APTs compared to the neural embedding approach?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the quality of the dependency parsing? Did you explore the impact of using different parsers?
3.  Do you have any hypotheses about why the hybrid model (combining aligned and unaligned APTs) performs best?
