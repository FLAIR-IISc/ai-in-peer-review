**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes an integrated compositional distributional approach to improve the detection of non-compositional phrases, specifically focusing on noun compounds. The authors introduce a new method called Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), which uses typed higher-order dependency paths to construct vector representations for words. They compare their approach with a traditional compositional method using neural word embeddings. The authors evaluate their method on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) approach**: The authors propose a new method for constructing vector representations for words using typed higher-order dependency paths. This approach is shown to be effective in detecting non-compositional phrases.
2. **Improved results on compositionality detection**: The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, outperforming previous methods.
3. **Comparison with neural word embeddings**: The authors compare their approach with a traditional compositional method using neural word embeddings, showing that their method is more effective in detecting non-compositional phrases.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of syntactic structure**: The authors' approach effectively incorporates syntactic structure into the compositional process, leading to improved results.
2. **State-of-the-art results**: The authors achieve state-of-the-art results on the Reddy et al. (2011) dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach.
3. **Comparative evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough comparative evaluation of their approach with a traditional compositional method using neural word embeddings.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation dataset**: The authors only evaluate their approach on a single dataset (Reddy et al., 2011), which may not be representative of all compositionality detection tasks.
2. **Parameter optimization**: The authors perform an extensive parameter search, which may not be feasible in all scenarios.
3. **Lack of theoretical analysis**: The authors do not provide a thorough theoretical analysis of their approach, which may limit its generalizability.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the construction of the APT lexicon and how it is used in the compositional process?
2. How do you plan to extend your approach to other types of phrases and compositionality detection tasks?
3. Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the differences between the aligned and unaligned APTs and how they contribute to the composition process?
