**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes an integrated compositional distributional approach to improve the detection of non-compositional phrases, specifically compound nouns. The authors use a dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality and compare their approach to state-of-the-art neural word embeddings. The proposed approach, called Anchored Packed Trees (APTs), uses typed higher-order dependency paths to represent words and phrases. The authors experiment with different composition operations and parameter settings, including the use of normalised counts and PPMI values, and evaluate their approach using Spearman's rank correlation coefficient.

**Main Contributions**

1. The proposal of an integrated compositional distributional approach, APTs, which uses typed higher-order dependency paths to represent words and phrases.
2. The evaluation of APTs on a dataset of 90 compound nouns with human judgments of compositionality, achieving state-of-the-art results.
3. The comparison of APTs to state-of-the-art neural word embeddings, showing that APTs outperform neural word embeddings in detecting non-compositional phrases.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to compositional semantics, which is well-motivated and grounded in linguistic theory.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, using a range of composition operations and parameter settings.
3. The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on a challenging dataset, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper assumes a high degree of linguistic annotation, which may not be available for all languages or datasets.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the linguistic implications of their results, which could be an interesting area for future research.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison to other compositional semantics approaches, to better situate the proposed approach in the broader field.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the linguistic annotation used in the dataset, and how this annotation was obtained?
2. How do you plan to extend the proposed approach to other types of phrases, such as verb phrases or sentence-level constructions?
3. Can you provide more analysis on the linguistic implications of your results, and how they relate to existing theories of compositional semantics?
