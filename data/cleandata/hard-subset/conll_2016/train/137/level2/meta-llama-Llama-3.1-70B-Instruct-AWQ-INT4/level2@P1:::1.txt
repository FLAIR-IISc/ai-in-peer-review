**Summary of the Paper**

The paper "When a Red Herring is Not a Red Herring: Using Compositional Methods to Improve the Detection of Non-Compositional Phrases" proposes a new approach to compositionality detection for compound nouns using compositional methods. The authors compare an integrated compositional distributional approach using sparse high-dimensional representations with the ad-hoc compositional approach of applying simple composition operations to state-of-the-art neural embeddings.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose a new compositional approach using Anchored Packed Trees (APTs) that incorporates syntactic structure into the contextual features of words, which is then used in the compositional process.
2. The authors demonstrate that the APT approach outperforms the state-of-the-art neural word embeddings and other compositional methods in detecting compositionality for compound nouns.
3. The authors show that the use of typed dependency features and higher-order dependency paths in the APT approach is highly informative in the task of determining the compositionality of a noun compound.

**Strengths**

1. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed APT approach using a standard dataset and compares it with other state-of-the-art methods.
2. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the results, including the effect of different parameters and composition operations on the performance of the APT approach.
3. The paper highlights the importance of incorporating syntactic structure into the compositional process, which is a crucial aspect of language understanding.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper focuses primarily on compound nouns, and it is unclear how well the APT approach will generalize to other types of phrases.
2. The authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the APT approach, which may be a concern for large-scale applications.
3. The paper assumes that the dependency parses of the corpus are accurate, which may not always be the case.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend the APT approach to other types of phrases, such as adjective-noun phrases or verb phrases?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the APT approach and how it compares to other compositional methods?
3. How do you plan to handle errors in the dependency parses of the corpus, and what impact do you think this will have on the performance of the APT approach?
