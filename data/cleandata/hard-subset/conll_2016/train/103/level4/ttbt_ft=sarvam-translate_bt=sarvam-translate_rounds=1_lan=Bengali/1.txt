This paper proposes a method for evaluating the quality of content using word embedding. This method calculates similarity (either directly or indirectly through matrix factorization), which yields impressive results compared to standard datasets.

The proposed method is a natural but important next step in the evolutionary path of content evaluation research. The issue that concerns me the most in the results is that, while achieving state-of-the-art results for all three datasets, there are significant inconsistencies between which method performs better and which performs less well (which is below the state-of-the-art standard). In reality, none of the proposed methods consistently outperform the state-of-the-art standard, and SVD-based methods perform significantly worse on genomics datasets. This is a concern for anyone who wants to use your method and apply it to any dataset. I suspect that the low SVD results compared to genomics are related to the OOV term ratio (see comments below), and that it may be possible to automatically predict which method will perform best based on vocabulary matching with GloVe, etc., but there is no discussion of this in the paper.

Other problems:
- The proposed method has strong similarities with methods proposed in the lexical chaining literature, which I would encourage authors to read and incorporate into any future versions.
- You emphasize that your method has no parameters, but word embedding methods have plenty of parameters, which are inherent in your method. This is not a very big deal, but it is something to acknowledge.
- How does your method deal with OOV terms, such as terms that are not present in the pre-trained GloVe embedding (i.e., genomics datasets)? Are they simply ignored? What impact does this have on the method?

Low-level problems:
- In the description of word embedding in section 2.1, you implicitly assume that vector length is not important (cosine similarity can be used to measure similarity between two vectors). If the vectors are of unit length, this is not a problem, but word2vec does not actually return unit-length vectors (the pre-trained vectors are normalized through post-hacks, and if you run word2vec yourself, the vector lengths are certainly not uniform). This is a minor detail, but important.
- The graphs in Figure 1 are so small that they cannot be read.
