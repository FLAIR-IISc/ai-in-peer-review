This paper proposes a method for evaluating the quality of a topic using word embedding, which is for calculating similarity (either directly or indirectly through matrix factorization), achieving impressive results on standard datasets. The proposed method is a natural but important next step in the evolving path of research on topic evaluation. What bothers me the most is that, while you achieve state-of-the-art results for all three datasets, there are significant inconsistencies between the methods that perform and those that perform less well (below state-of-the-art). In practice, none of the proposed methods consistently beat state-of-the-art, and SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on genomics datasets. This is a significant concern for anyone who wants to shelf your method and use it on any arbitrary dataset. I suspect that the low results for SVD on genomics are related to the ratio of OOV words (see comments below), and it may be possible to automatically predict which method will perform best based on vocabulary matching with glove, etc., but there is no such discussion in the paper.

Other issues:
- The proposed method has strong similarities with methods proposed in the literature of lexical chaining, which I would encourage the authors to read and incorporate into any future version of the paper.
- You emphasize that your method has no parameters, but word embedding methods have a large number of parameters, which are inherent in your method. This is not a big deal, but acceptable.
- How does your method deal with OOV words, for example in genomics datasets (i.e., not present in the pre-trained glove embedding)? Are they simply ignored? What impact does this have on the method?

Low-level issues:
- In your description of word embedding in Section 2.1, you implicitly assume that vector length is insignificant (in saying that cosine similarity can be used to measure similarity between two vectors). If the vectors are unit lengths, this is not problematic, but word2vec does not actually return unit-length vectors (pre-trained vectors are post-hoc generalized, and if you run word2vec yourself, the vector length is certainly not the same). This is a small thing, but important.
- The graphs in Figure 1 are so small that they cannot be read.
