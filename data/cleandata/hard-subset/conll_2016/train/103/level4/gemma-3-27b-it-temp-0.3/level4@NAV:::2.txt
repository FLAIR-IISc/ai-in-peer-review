This paper introduces a novel approach to topic model evaluation, grouping the top *n* words of each topic into clusters – termed “buckets” – using cosine similarity calculated from their word embeddings. The core method involves iteratively assigning words to existing buckets based on a similarity threshold, or creating new buckets if the threshold isn’t met. The authors also propose more sophisticated variations utilizing eigenvectors and topic reorganization. Evaluation across three benchmark datasets and a weakly supervised text classification task demonstrates performance competitive with, and often exceeding, current state-of-the-art methods (Röder et al., 2015).

While the underlying concept is straightforward and possesses a degree of *ad hoc* character, the paper presents promising results. The rationale connecting topic quality to word-word similarity isn’t fully articulated; the differing contextual foundations of topics (document-level) and word embeddings (sequential context) raise questions about this connection. Nevertheless, the method demonstrably performs well. I recommend the inclusion of statistical significance testing for the results presented in Table 1.

The paper is generally well-written, though some linguistic refinements would be beneficial. Section 3, detailing the techniques, proved somewhat difficult to follow. This appears largely attributable to the frequent use of passive voice when describing design decisions ("the threshold is computed as").  I suggest restructuring this section with a dedicated subsection for each method, which would improve clarity. The authors have not fully utilized the available page limit, and the “trace” of the method on page 3 could be condensed to accommodate this expansion. 

Finally, it would be valuable to understand the method’s sensitivity to the choice of word embeddings. Investigating the impact of alternative embeddings, such as word2vec, in place of GloVe would provide further insight.
