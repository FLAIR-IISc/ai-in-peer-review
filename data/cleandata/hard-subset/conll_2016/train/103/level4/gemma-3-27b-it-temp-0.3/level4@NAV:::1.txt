This paper introduces a novel approach to topic quality evaluation, leveraging word embeddings and similarity calculations – both directly and through matrix factorization – to achieve strong performance on established datasets. The work represents a logical and valuable progression in topic evaluation research. However, a key concern arises from the variability in performance across different methods and datasets. While state-of-the-art results are attained on all three datasets, the relative ranking of methods is inconsistent; no single proposed method consistently outperforms existing approaches. Specifically, the SVD-based methods demonstrate significantly weaker performance on the genomics dataset, which raises practical concerns for users seeking a broadly applicable solution. It is hypothesized that this discrepancy may be linked to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, and the possibility of predicting optimal method selection based on vocabulary overlap with the pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe) warrants investigation, though is not currently discussed.

Further considerations:

- The proposed methodology shares notable similarities with techniques found in the lexical chaining literature, and the authors are encouraged to explore and cite this related work.
- While the paper highlights the lack of explicit parameters in the proposed method, it’s important to acknowledge that the underlying word embedding models themselves contain a substantial number of parameters.
- The handling of OOV terms – particularly relevant for datasets like genomics where terms may be absent from the pre-trained GloVe embeddings – requires clarification. Specifically, how are these terms treated, and what is the resulting impact on the method’s performance?

Minor points:

- Section 2.1’s description of word embeddings implicitly assumes vector length is irrelevant when using cosine similarity. While valid for unit-length vectors, standard word2vec outputs do not inherently possess this property (normalization is often applied post-training). This is a subtle but important detail.
- The figures, particularly Figure 1, are currently too small for clear readability.
