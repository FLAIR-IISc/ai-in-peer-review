This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality using word embeddings to calculate similarity, either directly or via matrix factorization. The approach achieves impressive results on standard datasets and represents a natural and important step forward in research on topic evaluation.

My primary concern is the inconsistency of the results across the different datasets. While the paper reports state-of-the-art results on all three datasets, there are large variations in the relative performance of the proposed methods. In practice, no single method consistently outperforms the state of the art, and the SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. For a practitioner seeking to apply this method to an arbitrary dataset, this lack of consistency is a considerable drawback. I suspect the poor performance of the SVD-based methods on the genomics dataset is related to the high proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms (see comment below). It might be possible to automatically select the best-performing method based on vocabulary overlap with the pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe), but the paper does not explore this possibility or offer guidance on method selection.

Other issues:
- The proposed method bears a strong resemblance to work in the lexical chaining literature. I would encourage the authors to review this literature and discuss the connections in a future version of the paper.
- The authors emphasize that their method is parameter-free. However, the underlying word embedding models have a large number of parameters that are implicitly inherited by this method. While this is a minor point, it would be more accurate to acknowledge these implicit parameters.
- The paper does not specify how out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms are handled, particularly in a domain-specific corpus like the genomics dataset, where many terms are likely to be absent from the pre-trained GloVe embeddings. Are these terms simply ignored? A discussion of the OOV handling strategy and its potential impact on performance is needed.

Low-level issues:
- The description of word embeddings in Section 2.1 implies that vector length is unimportant by stating that cosine similarity measures the similarity between two vectors. This is only strictly true if the vectors are unit length. While some pre-trained embeddings are normalized post-hoc, word2vec itself does not necessarily produce unit-length vectors. This is a minor detail, but the description could be more precise.
- The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be easily readable.
