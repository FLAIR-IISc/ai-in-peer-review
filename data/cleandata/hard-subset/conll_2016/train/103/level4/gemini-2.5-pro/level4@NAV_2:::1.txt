### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel method for evaluating the quality of topics generated by models such as Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core concept involves grouping a topic's most probable words into semantic "buckets" using pre-trained word embeddings. A topic is considered coherent if its words form a single large bucket. The authors present three techniques for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering, another utilizing Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to identify sub-themes, and a third that refines the SVD output with a reorganization step. The proposed methods are evaluated against human judgments on three public datasets and their utility is demonstrated in a downstream weakly-supervised text classification task, where selecting more coherent topic sets leads to improved performance.

### Main Contributions
1.  The primary contribution is a novel and intuitive framework (TBuckets) for topic coherence evaluation. The method operationalizes the idea of thematic consistency by grouping words based on their vector-space similarity. The proposed SVD-based variants are notably parameter-free, which presents a significant practical advantage over existing methods that require tuning.
2.  The paper provides a strong empirical evaluation, demonstrating that the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two of the three benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG).
3.  The work demonstrates the practical value of the proposed metric by applying it to a weakly supervised text classification task. By using TBuckets to pre-select higher-quality topic sets for human annotation, the authors show an improvement in final classification accuracy.

### Strengths
1.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The proposed methodology, specifically the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg variant, achieves impressive results that surpass the state-of-the-art on the NYT and 20NG benchmark datasets. The high Pearson correlation with human coherence judgments on these standard datasets provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness.
2.  **Novel and Intuitive Approach:** The central idea of grouping words into thematic "buckets" is an intuitive and well-motivated advancement in topic evaluation research. It represents a logical step beyond pairwise word similarity metrics towards a more holistic, group-based assessment of coherence.
3.  **Parameter-Free SVD Methods:** A significant practical strength of the TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg techniques is that they are parameter-free. This contrasts favorably with many competing coherence measures that require careful and often computationally expensive tuning of multiple parameters, making the proposed methods more accessible and easier to apply "off-the-shelf".

### Weaknesses
1.  **Inconsistent Performance Across Datasets:** The main concern is the method's inconsistent performance. While the SVD-based approaches excel on the NYT and 20NG datasets, their performance drops significantly below the state-of-the-art on the Genomics dataset. This inconsistency raises questions about the generalizability and robustness of the method when applied to new or domain-specific corpora.
2.  **Lack of Guidance for Method Selection:** Following from the first point, the paper offers no analysis as to why performance varies so dramatically between datasets. For a user wishing to apply this method, it is unclear which TBuckets variant to choose. The paper would be substantially stronger if it investigated the reasons for these discrepancies (e.g., the proportion of out-of-vocabulary terms) and provided practical guidance on how to select the appropriate variant for a given task.
3.  **"Parameter-Free" Claim Qualification:** The claim that the SVD-based methods are parameter-free is only partially true. While the bucketing algorithms themselves do not have tunable parameters, they are critically dependent on pre-trained word embeddings. The process of generating these embeddings involves a large number of hyperparameters (e.g., vector dimensionality, context window size, training corpus). This implicit dependency should be acknowledged.
4.  **Missing Related Work:** The proposed technique of grouping semantically similar words shares conceptual similarities with work in the lexical chaining literature. A discussion of this related area would help to better situate the paper's contribution within the broader field.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you clarify how your method handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms—words that are not present in the pre-trained GloVe embeddings? Are they simply ignored? Given the specialized vocabulary of the Genomics dataset, could the handling of OOV terms explain the significant performance drop observed for the SVD-based methods on this dataset?
2.  Could you provide some insight into the performance disparities across datasets? What characteristics of a corpus or its vocabulary might make one TBuckets variant superior to another? Is it possible to automatically predict which variant will perform best, perhaps based on vocabulary overlap with the embedding's training corpus, without requiring a labeled set of topics for evaluation?

### Additional Comments
-   In Section 2.1, the description of word embeddings suggests that vector magnitude is unimportant by focusing on cosine similarity. However, models like word2vec do not necessarily produce unit-length vectors. Please clarify if the GloVe vectors were normalized prior to use.
