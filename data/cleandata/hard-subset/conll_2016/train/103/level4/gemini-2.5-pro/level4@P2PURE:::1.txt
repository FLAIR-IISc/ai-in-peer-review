This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality using word embeddings to calculate semantic similarity, either directly or via matrix factorization. The approach achieves impressive results on standard datasets and represents a natural and important step in the evolution of topic evaluation research.

My primary concern with the results is the inconsistency in performance across the different methods proposed. While the paper reports state-of-the-art results on all three datasets, no single method is consistently the top performer. In fact, the SVD-based methods underperform significantly on the genomics dataset. This lack of consistency is a significant concern for practitioners seeking to apply these methods to new, arbitrary datasets. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this issue. It is possible that the poor performance on the genomics dataset is related to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. If so, it might be possible to automatically select the best-performing method based on the vocabulary overlap with the pre-trained embeddings, but this is not explored.

Other issues:
- The proposed method shares strong similarities with approaches in the lexical chaining literature. The authors are encouraged to review this body of work and discuss the connections in a future version of the paper.
- The authors emphasize that the proposed method has no parameters. However, the underlying word embedding models contain a large number of parameters that are implicitly inherited. While this is a minor point, it is worth acknowledging for clarity.
- The paper should clarify how it handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, particularly for a domain-specific dataset like genomics. Are terms not found in the pre-trained GloVe embeddings simply ignored, and what is the resulting impact on performance?

Low-level issues:
- The description of word embeddings in Section 2.1 states that cosine similarity can measure the similarity between two vectors, which implicitly assumes vector length is unimportant. This is true for unit-length vectors, but standard word2vec embeddings are not guaranteed to have uniform length. This is a minor but important technical detail that should be clarified.
- The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be easily readable.
