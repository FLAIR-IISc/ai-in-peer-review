This paper proposes a new method for evaluating topic models. The core idea is to partition the top *n* words of a given topic into semantic clusters, or "buckets," based on the cosine similarity of their associated word embeddings. The authors present three variants of this method. In the simplest version, words are processed sequentially and either placed into an existing bucket—if their cosine similarity with the words already in it exceeds a threshold—or used to create a new bucket. Two more complex variants based on eigenvectors and a reorganization step are also introduced. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setting, where its performance is shown to be on par with or superior to the current state-of-the-art coherence metric by Röder et al. (2015).

**Strengths**

The primary strength of this work lies in its empirical results. The proposed metric, despite its relative simplicity, achieves strong performance and appears to be a very effective measure of topic quality. The paper is generally well-written and the evaluation is sound, using standard datasets and a relevant downstream task to demonstrate the method's utility.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Conceptual Justification:** While the proposed method is empirically effective, its conceptual underpinnings could be significantly strengthened. The approach feels somewhat ad hoc, and the paper does not offer a deep explanation for why word-to-word similarity, as captured by word embeddings, should be a definitive measure of topic quality. There is a fundamental difference between the contexts used to train topic models (document-level word co-occurrence) and word embeddings (local, sequential word co-occurrence). A more thorough discussion of why these two different notions of context can be productively combined for evaluation would provide a stronger theoretical foundation for the work.

2.  **Clarity of Methodology:** The description of the techniques in Section 3 is difficult to follow at times. This is partly due to the frequent use of the passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as..."), which obscures the authors' specific design choices and makes it hard to distinguish them from standard procedures. I strongly recommend that the authors revise this section to improve clarity. Dedicating a separate, clearly-labeled subsection to each of the three methods would significantly enhance readability and allow for a more explicit explanation of each algorithm's mechanics and motivation. There appears to be sufficient space for this expansion; for example, the detailed "trace" of the method on page 3 is not highly informative and could be condensed or moved to an appendix to make room for a clearer exposition of the core methodology.

3.  **Statistical Rigor:** The empirical comparison in Table 1 would be more convincing with the inclusion of statistical significance tests. This would help clarify whether the observed performance gains over the baseline are statistically meaningful.

**Questions for the Authors**

One key question is the sensitivity of the proposed metric to the choice of word embeddings. The experiments are conducted using GloVe embeddings; how would the results change if a different model, such as word2vec or FastText, were used? An analysis of this sensitivity would provide valuable insight into the robustness and generalizability of the method.
