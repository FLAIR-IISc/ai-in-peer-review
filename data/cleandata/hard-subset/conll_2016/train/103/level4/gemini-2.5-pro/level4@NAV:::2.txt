This paper introduces a novel approach for topic model evaluation. The method works by grouping the top n words of a given topic into semantic clusters (termed "buckets") based on the cosine similarity of their word vectors. In its most basic form, the algorithm iteratively processes each top word, assigning it to an existing bucket if it is sufficiently similar to the words already present, or creating a new bucket for it otherwise. The authors also present two more sophisticated variants, one leveraging eigenvectors and another involving a reorganization step. The proposed technique is assessed on three standard datasets and a weakly-supervised text classification task, where its performance is shown to be comparable to or better than the current state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015).

The fundamental concept is straightforward, though it appears somewhat heuristic in nature. The paper lacks a strong theoretical justification for why word-word similarity should be a valid proxy for topic quality. This connection is not immediately apparent, particularly since topic models and word embeddings operate on different notions of context (document-level versus local sequential context, respectively). Nevertheless, the empirical results are compelling, and the proposed method demonstrates strong performance. To further substantiate these findings, the inclusion of statistical significance tests for the results in Table 1 would be beneficial.

Overall, the manuscript is well-written, albeit with some minor language issues. The description of the methods in Section 3, however, is difficult to follow. This lack of clarity seems to arise from the frequent use of passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as") in places that describe specific design choices made by the authors. I suggest the authors restructure this section to explain each method in a dedicated subsection. Sufficient space appears to be available for this, as the paper does not use the full 8 pages, and the illustrative "trace" of the method on page 3 could be condensed, as it provides limited insight.

One point of inquiry is the sensitivity of the proposed technique to the underlying word embeddings. For instance, it would be valuable to see how the evaluation scores change if the authors were to use word2vec embeddings instead of the current GloVe vectors.
