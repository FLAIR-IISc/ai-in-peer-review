This paper proposes a new method for evaluating topic models by partitioning the top *n* words of each topic into clusters (or "buckets") based on the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. In the simplest variant, words are processed sequentially. Each word is either placed into an existing bucket—if its cosine similarity to the words already in that bucket exceeds a certain threshold—or it is used to create a new bucket. The authors also introduce two more complex methods based on eigenvectors and reorganization. The proposed approach is evaluated on three standard datasets and a weakly supervised text classification task, where it achieves performance that is on par with or surpasses the current state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015).

The paper's core idea is straightforward, though it appears somewhat ad hoc. The authors do not offer a novel theoretical justification for why topic quality should be measurable by word-word similarity. It is not obvious why this should be the case, given that topic models and word embeddings rely on two different notions of context (document-level vs. local sequential context, respectively). Despite this conceptual gap, the proposed method appears to perform well empirically. However, the claims in Table 1 would be more convincing with the inclusion of statistical significance tests.

Overall, the paper is clearly written, although it contains some grammatical errors and awkward phrasing. The description of the techniques in Section 3 is particularly difficult to follow. This lack of clarity seems to stem from the authors' use of the passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as") to describe what are, in fact, their own methodological design choices. The authors should explain the different methods more clearly, ideally by dedicating a separate subsection to each. This restructuring seems feasible within the page limits, as the paper does not use the full 8 pages of content and could condense the largely uninformative execution "trace" on page 3.

A remaining question is the sensitivity of the proposed technique to the choice of word embeddings. For example, how would the scores change if the authors had used word2vec instead of GloVe?
