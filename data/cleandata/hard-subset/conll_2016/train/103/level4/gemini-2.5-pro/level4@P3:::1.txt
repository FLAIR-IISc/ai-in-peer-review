### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics learned by models like LDA. The core idea is to group the top words of a topic into semantic "buckets" using their word vector representations. The authors posit that a coherent topic will result in a single large bucket, while an incoherent topic will be fragmented into multiple smaller buckets. The paper explores three methods for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), with the latter being presented as parameter-free. The proposed coherence scores are evaluated by correlating them with human judgments on three standard datasets.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel and Intuitive Framework for Topic Coherence:** The paper presents a new perspective on topic evaluation. Moving beyond pairwise word similarity scores, the TBuckets framework attempts to discover underlying sub-themes (or the lack thereof) within a topic, which aligns well with the human intuition of what makes a topic coherent. The SVD-based approach for identifying these sub-themes is particularly elegant.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The proposed method, specifically the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg variant, achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two out of the three benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG). This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach on general-domain corpora.

3.  **Demonstration of Practical Utility:** The authors show that their coherence metric can be used to improve a downstream task. By using TBuckets to select higher-quality topic sets for annotation in a weakly supervised text classification scenario, they achieve better classification performance, reinforcing the practical value of the proposed metric.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Novelty and Soundness:** The TBuckets concept is a natural and important step forward in topic coherence research. It leverages modern, powerful word embeddings in a more holistic way than simply averaging pairwise similarities. The use of SVD to identify orthogonal semantic dimensions within a set of topic words is a clever technique for implementing the "bucketing" idea without requiring explicit clustering parameters like distance thresholds.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance on General-Domain Datasets:** The method's ability to outperform a strong, recently established state-of-the-art baseline (Röder et al., 2015) on the NYT and 20NG datasets is a significant achievement. As shown in Table 1, the correlation scores for TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are compelling and validate the overall approach.

3.  **Parameter-Free Variants:** The SVD-based methods are presented as being parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage. Many existing coherence measures require tuning multiple parameters or rely on external resources that introduce their own complexities. A robust, parameter-free metric is highly desirable for practitioners who wish to apply topic modeling "off-the-shelf."

4.  **Well-Executed Downstream Task Evaluation:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 adds significant weight to the paper's claims. It provides concrete evidence that the proposed metric is not just better at mimicking human scores in isolation, but that this improvement translates into tangible benefits for a real-world application. The comparison between using the top 5 vs. bottom 5 topic sets (Table 3) effectively highlights the metric's utility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent Performance and Lack of Robustness:** The most significant weakness is the inconsistent performance of the proposed methods across different datasets. While the SVD-based methods excel on NYT and 20NG, they perform notably worse than the state-of-the-art on the Genomics dataset. In fact, the simpler clustering-based method performs better on this specialized domain. This lack of robustness is a serious concern for a general-purpose coherence metric. For a user with an arbitrary new dataset, it is unclear which TBuckets variant to choose, or whether to trust the method at all.

2.  **Absence of Analysis for Failure Cases:** The paper presents the poor results on the Genomics dataset but provides no discussion or analysis as to *why* the SVD-based methods fail here. The performance of any embedding-based method is highly dependent on the quality of the embeddings for the domain in question. The pre-trained GloVe embeddings (trained on Wikipedia and Gigaword) are likely to have poor coverage and/or representations for specialized genomics terminology. An analysis of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rates and their impact would be critical here. This omission leaves a major gap in the evaluation and in the reader's understanding of the method's limitations.

3.  **Missing Connections to Relevant Literature:** The core idea of grouping semantically related words has strong parallels with established research in lexical chaining. This body of work also deals with identifying and grouping coherent sets of words within texts. Situating the TBuckets approach within this context and discussing similarities and differences would strengthen the paper's contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the significant performance drop of the SVD-based methods on the Genomics dataset? Is this connected to the suitability of the pre-trained GloVe embeddings for this specialized domain? Specifically, how does your method handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, and what was the OOV rate for each of the three datasets?

2.  You emphasize that the SVD-based methods are parameter-free. However, they are fundamentally dependent on the choice of pre-trained word embeddings (model, dimensionality, training corpus, etc.), which represents a critical, implicit hyperparameter. How sensitive is the method's performance to different embedding models (e.g., word2vec vs. GloVe, or embeddings trained on a domain-specific corpus)? Acknowledging this dependency seems crucial for a complete picture of the method.

### Additional Comments (Minor Issues)

*   In the description of word embeddings in Section 2.1, the text implies that cosine similarity is always the appropriate measure. This is true if vectors are unit-normalized, but models like word2vec do not necessarily produce unit-length vectors. This is a minor detail but could be stated more precisely.
*   The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be easily readable in the submitted PDF. Please consider enlarging them for the final version.
