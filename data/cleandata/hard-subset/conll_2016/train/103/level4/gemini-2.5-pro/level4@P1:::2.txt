### **Summary**

This paper proposes TBuckets, a new method for evaluating the quality of topics learned by models like LDA. The core idea is to partition the top N words of a topic into clusters, or "buckets," based on the cosine similarity of their pre-trained word embeddings. The intuition is that a coherent topic will result in a single large bucket. The paper presents three variations of this approach: one based on agglomerative clustering, one using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), and a third that refines the SVD-based buckets with a reorganization step. The proposed methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets and by demonstrating their utility in a weakly supervised text classification task. The results show that the proposed techniques, particularly the SVD-based variants, outperform or perform on par with the current state of the art.

The primary contribution of this work is an effective, and in some variants parameter-free, method for measuring topic coherence. While the conceptual basis is simple, the empirical results demonstrate its strong performance, making it a valuable practical tool for topic model evaluation.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The main strength of the paper is its strong empirical results. The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two out of the three benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG), as shown in Table 1. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach for automatically measuring topic quality.

2.  **Simplicity and Intuitiveness:** The underlying "bucketing" concept is simple, intuitive, and easy to grasp. This makes the method appealing and straightforward to implement. The demonstration of its utility in filtering topic models for a downstream weakly supervised classification task further highlights its practical value.

3.  **Parameter-Free Variants:** The SVD-based methods are presented as being parameter-free, which is a significant advantage over competing coherence measures that often require tuning multiple parameters (as noted in the discussion of Röder et al., 2015). This makes the method more robust, generalizable, and easier for other researchers to apply.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Theoretical Justification:** While empirically successful, the method's theoretical underpinnings are not deeply explored. The approach has an ad-hoc quality, and the paper does not offer a new explanation for why semantic similarity from word embeddings (derived from local, sequential word contexts) should be a reliable proxy for topic coherence (derived from a global, document-level context). A more thorough discussion connecting these two different notions of context would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

2.  **Clarity of Method Description:** The description of the techniques in Section 3 is difficult to follow. The presentation would be clearer if each of the three methods (Clustering, SVD, and SVD-Reorg) was detailed in its own subsection. Furthermore, the use of passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as...") obscures the fact that these are important design choices made by the authors. The space used for the lengthy manual "trace" of the bucketing process on page 3 could be better utilized for a more precise and clear technical explanation of the algorithms.

3.  **Incomplete Experimental Analysis:** The empirical results in Table 1 are promising, but their statistical significance is not reported. To make a convincing claim of outperforming the state of the art, the authors should provide results from statistical significance tests comparing their method's correlation scores with those of the baseline.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The experiments rely exclusively on 300-dimensional GloVe embeddings trained on Wikipedia and Gigaword. How sensitive is the performance of the TBuckets methods to the choice of word embeddings? For example, have you experimented with other models (e.g., word2vec) or embeddings trained on different corpora or with different dimensionalities? It would be valuable to understand how robust the method is to this choice.
