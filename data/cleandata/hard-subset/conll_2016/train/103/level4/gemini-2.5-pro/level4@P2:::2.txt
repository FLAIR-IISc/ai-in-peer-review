### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel method for automatically evaluating the coherence of topics generated by models like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to group the top words of a topic into thematic "buckets" based on the semantic similarity of their pre-trained word embeddings. A topic is considered coherent if most of its words fall into a single large bucket. The authors propose three variants for creating these buckets: one based on agglomerative clustering and two more sophisticated, parameter-free methods based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), one of which includes a reorganization step. The proposed methods are evaluated against human judgments on three standard datasets and are also applied to a weakly supervised text classification task to demonstrate their practical utility.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel, parameter-free method for topic coherence evaluation that achieves state-of-the-art performance.** The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg methods offer a new way to measure topic quality that, unlike many competing methods, does not require parameter tuning. The empirical results show that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015) on two of the three benchmark datasets.

2.  **A demonstration of the practical utility of the proposed coherence metric in a downstream application.** The authors show that their metric can be used to select higher-quality topic models for annotation in a weakly supervised text classification setting, leading to improved classification performance. This provides evidence that the metric captures a meaningful aspect of model quality that has tangible benefits.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this paper is its impressive empirical results. The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves a higher correlation with human judgments than the best-performing systems from the comprehensive framework of Röder et al. (2015) on the NYT and 20NG datasets. Given that the Röder et al. framework explores a vast configuration space, outperforming it is a significant achievement.

2.  **Parameter-Free Method:** The SVD-based variants of TBuckets are presented as being parameter-free. This is a major practical advantage over many existing coherence measures that require tuning multiple parameters (e.g., window sizes, confirmation measures), which can be computationally expensive and may not generalize across datasets. The dynamic threshold designed for the reorganization step is a clever approach to avoid manual hyperparameter setting. This makes the method more robust and easier to adopt.

3.  **Intuitive Conceptual Framework:** The underlying idea of grouping semantically related topic words into buckets is simple and intuitive. This conceptual clarity makes the approach easy to understand, even if the SVD implementation details are more complex. The "dry run" examples in Section 3 effectively illustrate this core intuition.

4.  **Demonstrated Downstream Utility:** The experiment in Section 4.3 successfully connects the proposed coherence metric to a practical application. By showing that selecting topic models with higher TBuckets scores leads to better performance in weakly supervised classification, the authors validate that their metric is not just an academic exercise in correlating with human scores but can be used to improve real-world systems.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Clarity in Methodological Description:** The description of the proposed techniques in Section 3, particularly for the SVD-based methods, is difficult to follow and likely insufficient for replication. The logic for using the V matrix from SVD as "bucket identifiers" and the procedure for the reorganization step (Section 3.1) are explained in dense prose. Using passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as...") obscures design choices made by the authors. The paper would be significantly improved by including clearer, step-by-step descriptions or pseudocode for each method in dedicated subsections.

2.  **Limited Analysis and Theoretical Grounding:** While empirically successful, the paper offers little explanation for *why* this approach should work so well. Word embeddings are typically trained on local, sequential word contexts, whereas topic models are derived from global, document-level co-occurrence. The paper does not discuss the potential mismatch between these two notions of context or offer insights into why embedding-based similarity is such an effective proxy for topic coherence. This lack of analysis makes the contribution feel more like a successful engineering result than a finding that advances our fundamental understanding of topic models.

3.  **Absence of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper claims to outperform the state-of-the-art based on the Pearson's r values in Table 1. However, no statistical significance tests are reported. Without them, it is unclear whether the observed improvements are statistically significant or potentially due to chance. This is a critical omission for a paper making strong empirical claims.

4.  **Insufficient Sensitivity Analysis:** The experiments rely exclusively on a single set of 300-dimensional GloVe embeddings. The robustness of the TBuckets approach to different choices of word embeddings (e.g., word2vec, FastText), different dimensionalities, or embeddings trained on different corpora is not explored. This leaves open the question of how generalizable the results are and how dependent they are on this specific embedding choice.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the methodology for the TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg techniques? Specifically, providing pseudocode for the bucket creation and reorganization steps would greatly aid in understanding and replication. What is the precise formula for the dynamic threshold, and how is it derived from `AWES` and `AWPS`?

2.  Have you performed any statistical significance tests for the results presented in Table 1 to confirm that your improvements over the state-of-the-art are statistically significant?

3.  How sensitive is your method to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with other pre-trained embeddings, such as word2vec, or with different embedding dimensions? Understanding this would help assess the generalizability of your approach.
