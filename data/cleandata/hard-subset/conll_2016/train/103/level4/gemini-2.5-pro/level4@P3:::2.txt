### **Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for automatically evaluating the coherence of topics generated by models like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to partition the top words of a given topic into thematic groups, or "buckets," based on the semantic similarity of their pre-trained word embeddings. A topic is considered coherent if its words cluster into a single large bucket.

The authors propose and evaluate three specific techniques for this partitioning:
1.  An agglomerative clustering-based method (TBuckets-Clustering).
2.  A parameter-free method using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to identify sub-themes (TBuckets-SVD).
3.  An extension of the SVD method that includes a dynamic reorganization step to refine the buckets (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg).

The approach is evaluated by correlating its coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets. It is also applied to a weakly supervised text classification task to demonstrate its practical utility.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective method for measuring topic coherence.** The paper presents an intuitive approach that achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on standard benchmarks. The SVD-based variants are particularly notable for being parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage over methods that require extensive tuning.
2.  **An empirical demonstration of the method's utility in a downstream application.** The authors show that selecting topic models based on the proposed coherence score can improve performance in weakly supervised text classification, providing evidence that the metric captures a meaningful aspect of topic quality.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed method, particularly the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg variant, achieves state-of-the-art results on two of the three benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG) and is competitive on the third, as shown in Table 1. This strong empirical performance is the primary argument in favor of acceptance.

2.  **Parameter-Free SVD-based Methods:** A significant advantage of the SVD-based variants (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) is that they require no parameter tuning. This contrasts sharply with many existing coherence measures, including the state-of-the-art by Röder et al. (2015), which often require searching through a large configuration space. This makes the proposed method more practical, robust, and easier to apply for practitioners.

3.  **Intuitive and Simple Core Idea:** The underlying intuition of grouping semantically similar words into "buckets" to assess coherence is simple, elegant, and easy to understand. While the paper could benefit from a deeper theoretical discussion, the straightforwardness of the concept is a strength.

4.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The paper successfully demonstrates the utility of the proposed metric in a weakly supervised text classification setting (Section 4.3). By using TBuckets to pre-select higher-quality topic sets for human annotation, the authors show improved classification accuracy. This provides valuable evidence that the metric captures a meaningful aspect of topic quality that translates to better performance in downstream applications.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The paper lacks a strong justification for why semantic similarity derived from word embeddings (typically learned from local, sequential word contexts) should be an effective measure for the coherence of topics (derived from global, document-level co-occurrence). While empirically successful, the work would be substantially strengthened by a more in-depth discussion of this conceptual gap and an analysis of why this cross-contextual approach works so well.

2.  **Clarity and Structure of the Methodology Section:** The description of the proposed techniques in Section 3 is difficult to follow. The three methods are presented in a dense, continuous narrative. The paper would be significantly clearer if each technique (TBuckets-Clustering, TBuckets-SVD, and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) were presented in its own dedicated subsection with a more structured explanation. The use of passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as...") obscures key design choices made by the authors, making the methods harder to replicate and understand. Furthermore, the lengthy trace of the bucketing process on page 3 could be condensed to create space for a clearer, more formal description of the algorithms.

3.  **Omission of Statistical Significance Testing:** The key results in Table 1, which compare the proposed methods to the state-of-the-art, are presented without any statistical significance tests. It is therefore unclear whether the observed improvements over the baseline are statistically significant. The authors should include such tests to substantiate their claims of outperforming prior work.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The experiments rely exclusively on 300-dimensional GloVe embeddings. How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? For instance, have you experimented with other models like word2vec, or with different embedding dimensionalities? This would help clarify the robustness of the TBuckets approach.

2.  Could you provide a more detailed, step-by-step algorithmic description or pseudo-code for the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method? The current prose description of the reorganization conditions (based on LBPW, NBPE, etc.) and the dynamic threshold calculation is particularly dense and would benefit from a more formal presentation.
