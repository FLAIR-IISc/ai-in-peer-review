This paper proposes a new coherence metric for topic models. The core idea is to partition the top N words of a given topic into clusters (termed "buckets") based on the cosine similarity of their pre-trained word embeddings. The paper presents three variations of this method. In the simplest version, words are processed sequentially; each word is either added to an existing cluster if its similarity to the words already in that cluster is above a certain threshold, or it is used to seed a new cluster. The paper also introduces two more complex variations based on eigenvectors and cluster reorganization. The proposed metric is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setting, where it demonstrates performance that is on par with or superior to the current state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015).

The paper is generally well-written and presents an interesting, practical contribution. The primary strength of this work lies in its empirical performance. The proposed method appears to be an effective and competitive way to measure topic coherence automatically. However, the paper could be significantly improved in several areas.

My main concern is the lack of theoretical motivation for the approach. The basic idea feels somewhat ad hoc, and the authors do not offer a deep explanation for why word-word similarity, derived from word embeddings, should be a reliable measure of topic quality. There is a conceptual gap between topic models, which define topics based on document-level co-occurrence, and word embeddings, which capture semantic relationships from local, sequential word contexts. It is not immediately obvious why one should be a proxy for the other, and the paper would be stronger if it addressed this point more directly.

Furthermore, the presentation of the methodology in Section 3 could be substantially improved. I found the description of the techniques difficult to follow. This is partly due to the use of passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as...") in places where the authors are describing a specific design choice they made. Using active voice ("we compute the threshold as...") would make the algorithmic decisions clearer and more explicit. I strongly recommend that the authors restructure this section, perhaps dedicating a separate, clearly delineated subsection to each of the three proposed methods. This would significantly improve readability and allow for a more thorough explanation of each variant. There appears to be ample space for this, as the paper does not currently use the full 8 pages. The authors could, for instance, downsize or remove the rather uninformative "trace" of the method on page 3 to create more room for these clearer explanations.

Regarding the experiments, the results presented in Table 1 are promising; however, their impact is difficult to assess without statistical significance tests. The authors should include these tests to substantiate their claims of outperforming baseline methods and to provide a more rigorous basis for comparison.

Finally, I have a question regarding the robustness of the method. The experiments rely exclusively on GloVe vectors. How sensitive is the proposed technique to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? For example, how would the coherence scores change if a different embedding model, such as word2vec (CBOW or Skip-gram), were used? An analysis of this sensitivity would strengthen the paper’s conclusions and provide valuable insight into the method's general applicability.

In summary, this is a promising paper with a practical contribution. The method works well empirically, but the paper would benefit from a stronger theoretical grounding, a much clearer description of the methodology, and more rigorous statistical analysis of its experimental results.
