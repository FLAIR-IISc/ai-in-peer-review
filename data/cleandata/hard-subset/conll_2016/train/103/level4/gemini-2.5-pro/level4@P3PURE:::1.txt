This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality that uses word embeddings to calculate semantic similarity, either directly or via matrix factorization. The approach achieves impressive results on standard datasets and represents a natural and important next step in the evolution of topic evaluation research.

My primary concern with the results is the inconsistent performance of the proposed methods across the three datasets. While the paper reports state-of-the-art results overall, no single proposed method consistently outperforms the existing state of the art. The SVD-based methods, in particular, perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. This variability is a considerable drawback for a practitioner who wishes to use this method "off the shelf" on an arbitrary dataset. I suspect that the lower performance of SVD on the genomics data is related to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. The paper would be substantially strengthened by a discussion of this issue. It may even be possible to automatically predict which method will perform best for a given dataset based on vocabulary overlap with the pre-trained embeddings, but no such analysis is present.

Other issues:

*   The proposed method has strong similarities to techniques in the lexical chaining literature. The authors are encouraged to review this related work and discuss it in a future version of the paper.
*   The paper emphasizes that the proposed method has no parameters. However, the underlying word embedding models rely on a large number of hyperparameters, which are implicit in this method. While not a major flaw, this should be acknowledged.
*   The paper does not explain how it handles out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, which are likely to be prevalent in specialized corpora like the genomics dataset. Are these terms simply ignored? An analysis of the impact of OOV terms on performance would be a valuable addition.

Minor issues:

*   In Section 2.1, the description of word embeddings suggests that vector length is unimportant when using cosine similarity. This is only true if the vectors are unit length. Since pre-trained word2vec vectors are not necessarily unit-normalized, this point requires clarification.
*   The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable.
