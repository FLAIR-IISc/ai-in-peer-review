### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for automatically evaluating the quality of topics generated by models such as Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to group a topic's most probable words into semantic "buckets" using pre-trained word embeddings. The primary intuition is that a coherent topic will consist of a single large bucket, whereas an incoherent topic will fragment into multiple smaller buckets. The authors propose three distinct techniques for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering and two leveraging Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), with the final variant including a post-hoc reorganization step. The proposed methods are evaluated against human judgments on three public datasets, and their utility is further demonstrated in a weakly supervised text classification task.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel and Intuitive Framework for Topic Coherence:** The primary contribution is the TBuckets framework itself. It offers a conceptually simple and interpretable method for measuring topic coherence by formalizing the human process of identifying semantic groups within a list of words. This approach, which leverages the rich semantic information captured in word embeddings, is a valuable addition to the topic evaluation literature.
2.  **A Non-Parametric, High-Performing Coherence Metric:** A significant secondary contribution is the development of the SVD-based methods (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg), which achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance without requiring the explicit parameter tuning common to many existing coherence measures. This makes the method particularly attractive for practical, off-the-shelf use.
3.  **Demonstration of Downstream Task Improvement:** The paper successfully demonstrates that the proposed coherence metric can be used to improve a downstream application. By selecting topic sets with higher TBuckets scores, the authors show improved performance in a weakly supervised text classification scenario, providing practical validation for their method.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed methods achieve excellent results on standard benchmark datasets. The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique, in particular, surpasses the state-of-the-art on two of the three datasets (NYT and 20NG). This demonstrates that the bucketing approach is not only conceptually sound but also empirically effective and competitive with more complex, highly-tuned methods.
2.  **Conceptual Simplicity and Intuitiveness:** The underlying idea of forming semantic buckets is easy to grasp and aligns well with how humans assess topic coherence. This clarity makes the paper's contribution easy to understand and appreciate, and the resulting coherence scores are more interpretable than those from more abstract statistical measures.
3.  **Parameter-Free SVD-based Methods:** A key strength of the TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg techniques is their non-parametric nature. Many topic coherence metrics require tuning multiple parameters (e.g., window sizes, confirmation measures) which can be dataset-dependent and computationally expensive. By avoiding this tuning process, the SVD-based methods offer a robust and user-friendly solution for practitioners.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent Performance Across Datasets:** The most significant weakness is the inconsistent performance of the proposed methods across different domains. While TBuckets-SVD-Reorg excels on general corpora like NYT and 20NG, its performance drops substantially on the specialized Genomics dataset, where the clustering-based method performs best. This variability makes it difficult for a user to confidently select one of the proposed methods for a new, arbitrary dataset. The paper would be much stronger if it included an analysis of *why* this performance discrepancy exists and offered guidance on how to choose the appropriate TBuckets variant for a given corpus.
2.  **Unclear Handling of Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Words:** The paper relies on pre-trained GloVe embeddings but does not specify how it handles words that are not in the GloVe vocabulary (OOV terms). This is a critical omission, especially given the poor performance on the Genomics dataset, which likely contains many domain-specific terms absent from the general-domain training corpus of GloVe. If OOV words are simply ignored, this could severely degrade the quality of the coherence measure and may be the primary reason for the performance drop. A discussion of the OOV rate and its impact is necessary.
3.  **Missing Connection to Related Literature on Lexical Cohesion:** The method of grouping semantically related words to measure coherence is strongly reminiscent of work in lexical chaining and other measures of lexical cohesion. This body of work is highly relevant but is not discussed or cited. Situating the TBuckets approach within this broader context would better frame its novelty and acknowledge prior, related ideas.
4.  **Overstated "Parameter-Free" Claim:** The authors repeatedly emphasize that their SVD-based methods are parameter-free. While this is true for the bucketing algorithm itself, the overall approach is critically dependent on the choice of pre-trained word embeddings. These embeddings are the output of models (e.g., GloVe) that have many influential hyperparameters (vector dimension, window size, training corpus, etc.). The choice of embeddings is therefore a crucial, implicit parameter of the system. Acknowledging this dependency would provide a more nuanced and accurate description of the method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  How are out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words handled in your experiments? Are they ignored, or is there a fallback mechanism? Could you please report the OOV rates for each of the three datasets with respect to the GloVe embeddings used and analyze whether this correlates with the performance variations of your SVD-based methods?
2.  Given the inconsistent performance across the datasets, what is your hypothesis for why the SVD-based methods underperform on the Genomics dataset, while the clustering method is more effective there? Based on your findings, could you suggest a heuristic or a guiding principle for a practitioner to choose the most suitable TBuckets variant for a new domain?

### Additional Comments

*   In Section 2.1, your description of word embeddings states that "cosine similarity of word vectors in this space is hence, a good estimation of semantic similarity". This is generally true, but it's worth noting that this implicitly assumes vector length is not carrying information. While many pre-trained embeddings are unit-normalized, the raw output of models like word2vec is not. This is a minor detail but important for clarity and reproducibility.
*   The plots in Figure 1 are rendered at a very low resolution, making them blurry and difficult to read. Please consider providing higher-resolution figures in the final version.
