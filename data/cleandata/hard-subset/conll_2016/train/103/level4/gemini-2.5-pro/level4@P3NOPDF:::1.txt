This paper presents a novel method for evaluating the quality of topics by leveraging word embeddings to compute semantic similarity. The authors explore several variations of this approach, including direct similarity calculations and methods based on matrix factorization, and demonstrate impressive performance on several standard datasets. The application of word embeddings to the task of topic coherence evaluation is a natural and important step forward for the field. The authors are to be commended for systematically exploring this direction and showing its potential to outperform existing metrics.

However, while the overall direction is promising, I have some significant concerns about the consistency of the results and several other aspects of the paper that should be addressed.

**Major Concerns**

My primary concern lies in the inconsistency of the results across the different datasets. While the paper reports state-of-the-art performance on all three benchmarks, a closer look reveals that no single proposed method consistently outperforms the others or the existing state-of-the-art. For instance, the SVD-based methods perform particularly poorly on the genomics dataset. This lack of a clear "winner" among the proposed variants raises a significant practical concern: a user wishing to apply this method to a new, arbitrary dataset would have no clear guidance on which variant to choose. This variability undermines the method's utility as a general, "off-the-shelf" evaluation tool.

I suspect this performance variation may be linked to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms in each dataset, particularly for specialized domains like genomics. It is plausible that different methods (e.g., direct similarity vs. SVD) have varying sensitivity to OOV words. The paper would be substantially strengthened by an analysis of this issue. For instance, could the authors correlate performance with the vocabulary overlap between a dataset and the pre-trained GloVe embeddings? An even more valuable contribution would be a discussion on how one might automatically select the optimal method for a given dataset based on such characteristics. Without this guidance, the practical applicability of the work is limited.

**Other Suggestions for Improvement**

*   **Relation to Lexical Chaining:** The proposed method bears a strong resemblance to techniques used in the lexical chaining literature, where semantic similarity between words is used to identify coherent chains of related terms in a text. A discussion of this related work and a comparison of the conceptual underpinnings would provide valuable context and strengthen the paper's positioning.

*   **Parameter-Free Claim:** The paper emphasizes that the proposed methods are parameter-free. While this is true in terms of tunable parameters within the evaluation method itself, it is worth acknowledging that the approach implicitly inherits the numerous hyperparameters of the pre-trained word embedding model (e.g., vector dimensionality, context window size, training corpus). A brief acknowledgment of this dependency would provide a more complete picture.

*   **Handling of OOV Terms:** The paper does not specify how out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms—those not present in the pre-trained GloVe embeddings—are handled. Are they simply ignored? Given the specialized vocabulary of datasets like the genomics corpus, the treatment of OOV terms could have a significant impact on the results. Please clarify the methodology and discuss its potential effects.

**Minor Issues**

*   **Description of Cosine Similarity:** In the description of word embeddings (Section 2.1), the use of cosine similarity is presented as a straightforward measure of vector similarity. This implicitly assumes that vector magnitudes are unimportant. While this is true for unit-normalized vectors, common pre-trained models like word2vec do not necessarily produce unit-length vectors by default (though some pre-trained distributions are normalized post-hoc). This is a minor but important technical detail that should be clarified for precision.

*   **Figure Readability:** The plots in Figure 1 are too small to be easily read. Please increase their size for better legibility in the final version.
