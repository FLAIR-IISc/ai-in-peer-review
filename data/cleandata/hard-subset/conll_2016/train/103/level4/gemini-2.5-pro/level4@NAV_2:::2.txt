**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for automatically measuring the quality of topics generated by models like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to group the top words of a given topic into semantic "buckets" based on the cosine similarity of their pre-trained word embeddings. The coherence of a topic is then determined by the resulting distribution of words across these buckets; a topic is considered highly coherent if most of its words fall into a single, large bucket. The authors propose and evaluate three specific techniques for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering and two parameter-free variants that leverage Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), one of which includes a post-processing reorganization step. The method is evaluated by correlating its scores with human judgments on three standard datasets and by demonstrating its utility in a downstream, weakly-supervised text classification task.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The proposal of a novel and intuitive framework (TBuckets) for evaluating topic coherence. The SVD-based variants are a key contribution as they are parameter-free yet achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance compared to prior methods that often require significant parameter tuning.
2.  An empirical demonstration that the proposed coherence measure has practical utility. By using TBuckets to select higher-quality topic sets for human annotation, the authors show improved performance in a weakly supervised text classification scenario, validating that the measure effectively identifies more useful topics.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Parameter-Free Nature:** The proposed method, particularly the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg variant, achieves state-of-the-art performance on two of the three benchmark datasets (Table 1). This is a significant result, especially given that the SVD-based approaches are parameter-free. This contrasts favorably with many existing coherence measures, including the state-of-the-art baseline, which often rely on multiple parameters that require careful tuning.
2.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The paper goes beyond simply measuring correlation with human scores by showing the method's effectiveness in a downstream application. The text classification experiment (Section 4.3) provides compelling evidence that using TBuckets to pre-select topic models for annotation can improve final classification accuracy. This highlights the practical value of the proposed metric for improving real-world NLP pipelines.
3.  **Intuitive Core Concept:** The fundamental idea of grouping semantically related words to assess topic coherence is simple and easy to understand. While the paper's description of the implementation could be improved, the conceptual basis of the approach is sound and appealing.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Theoretical Justification:** The paper operates on the premise that semantic similarity, as captured by word embeddings, is a good proxy for topic coherence. However, it does not offer a deep explanation for why this should be the case, particularly given that topic models (like LDA) and word embedding models (like GloVe) learn from different notions of context (document-level co-occurrence vs. local window-based co-occurrence, respectively). The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of this conceptual link and the potential limitations of bridging these two types of models.
2.  **Clarity of Methodological Description:** The description of the techniques in Section 3 is difficult to follow, especially for the SVD-based methods and the dynamic threshold calculation in the reorganization step. The use of passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as") obscures the fact that these are important design choices. The section would be significantly improved by a clearer structure, perhaps with a dedicated subsection for each of the three techniques. The lengthy manual traces of the bucketing process on page 3 are not very informative and could be condensed to create space for a more rigorous technical explanation.
3.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The primary results in Table 1 are presented as raw correlation coefficients without any statistical tests for significance. This makes it difficult to determine whether the observed improvements over the state-of-the-art baseline are statistically meaningful or merely due to random variation. Including significance tests would provide stronger support for the paper's central claims.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The experiments rely exclusively on 300-dimensional GloVe embeddings. How sensitive is the performance of the TBuckets methods to the choice of word embeddings? For instance, have you experimented with other popular models like word2vec or FastText, or with different embedding dimensionalities? An analysis of this would help establish the robustness of your approach.
