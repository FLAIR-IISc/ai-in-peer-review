This paper proposes a new method for evaluating topic models. The technique works by partitioning the top *n* words of each topic into clusters, or "buckets," based on the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. In the simplest variant, words are processed sequentially. Each word is either placed into an existing bucket—if its cosine similarity to the other words in that bucket is below a certain threshold—or a new bucket is created for it. The authors also propose two more sophisticated methods based on eigenvectors and a reorganization step. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification task. The results show that the method performs on par with or outperforms the current state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

The paper's core idea is conceptually simple, although it has a somewhat ad-hoc quality. The authors do not provide a strong theoretical justification for why topic quality should be measurable by word-to-word similarity. This connection is not immediately obvious, given that topic models and word embeddings are derived from two very different notions of context (document-level vs. a local window). Despite this conceptual gap, the proposed method appears to work quite well in practice, though the authors should include significance tests for the results in Table 1 to strengthen their claims.

Overall, the paper is well-written, although there are some minor language issues that could be addressed. However, the description of the methods in Section 3 was somewhat difficult to follow. This is partly due to the use of passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as...") in places where the authors are describing their own design choices. I suggest that the authors restructure this section to explain each method more clearly, perhaps by dedicating a separate subsection to each. This seems feasible, as the paper does not use the full eight pages of available space. Furthermore, the authors could free up additional space by condensing the lengthy and relatively uninformative trace of the method's execution on page 3.

A remaining question is how sensitive the proposed technique is to the choice of word embeddings. For example, how would the results change if word2vec embeddings were used instead of GloVe?
