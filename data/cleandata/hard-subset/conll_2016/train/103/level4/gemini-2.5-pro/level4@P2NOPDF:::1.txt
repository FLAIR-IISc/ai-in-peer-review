This paper presents a method for evaluating topic quality by leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compute semantic similarity, either directly or via matrix factorization. The approach achieves impressive, state-of-the-art results on several standard datasets and represents a natural and important step forward in topic evaluation research.

My primary concern, however, lies with the inconsistency of the results across different datasets. While the paper reports state-of-the-art performance overall, no single proposed method consistently outperforms the baseline across all three testbeds. Different variants of the proposed method are the top performers on different datasets. Notably, the SVD-based methods underperform significantly on the genomics dataset. This variability is a considerable drawback for practical application, as it is unclear which method a practitioner should choose for a new, arbitrary dataset.

I suspect that this performance variation, particularly the poor results for SVD on the genomics corpus, may be linked to the prevalence of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. The paper would be substantially strengthened by a discussion of this issue and an analysis of whether dataset characteristics—such as vocabulary overlap with the pre-trained embeddings—could be used to predict the best-performing method variant.

**Other Issues:**

*   The proposed method bears a strong resemblance to techniques in the lexical chaining literature. The authors are encouraged to review this body of work and situate their contributions within that context in a future version of the paper.
*   The authors emphasize that their method is parameter-free. While this is true for the topic evaluation step itself, the method implicitly inherits the numerous hyperparameters of the underlying word embedding model (e.g., vector dimensionality, context window size). While not a major flaw, acknowledging these implicit parameters would provide a more complete description of the method.
*   The paper does not specify how out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms are handled. This is particularly relevant for specialized domains like the genomics dataset, where many terms are likely absent from the pre-trained GloVe vocabulary. Are these terms simply ignored? An analysis of the OOV rate for each dataset and its potential impact on the results would be a valuable addition.

**Minor Issues:**

*   In Section 2.1, the description of word embeddings and cosine similarity seems to assume that the vectors are unit length. While this may be true for some normalized pre-trained embeddings, it is not a general property of models like word2vec, where vector magnitude can carry information. Clarifying this minor technical point would improve the precision of the background section.
*   The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be legible and should be enlarged.
