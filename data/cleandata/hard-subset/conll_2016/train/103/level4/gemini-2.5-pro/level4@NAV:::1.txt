This paper introduces an approach for topic quality evaluation using word embeddings to calculate semantic similarity, employing both direct comparison and matrix factorization techniques. The method achieves strong performance on standard benchmark datasets and represents a logical and significant contribution to this area of research.

My primary concern pertains to the experimental results. While the paper reports state-of-the-art performance across all three datasets, there is considerable inconsistency in the relative effectiveness of the different proposed methods. Specifically, no single variant of the proposed method consistently outperforms the state of the art on all datasets. The SVD-based approach, in particular, exhibits a notable performance degradation on the genomics dataset. This variability presents a significant challenge for practitioners seeking to apply this work to new datasets, as there is no clear guidance on which method to choose. I hypothesize that the diminished performance of the SVD method on the genomics data may be attributable to a higher proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. It might be feasible to automatically select the optimal method based on a metric like vocabulary overlap with the pre-trained embeddings; however, the paper does not explore or discuss this possibility.

Other issues:
- The proposed method shares significant conceptual overlap with techniques from the lexical chaining literature. The authors are encouraged to review this related work and discuss the connections in a revised manuscript.
- The paper claims the method is parameter-free. While this is true for the proposed algorithm itself, it relies on pre-trained word embeddings, which are the product of models with a large number of hyperparameters. This implicit dependency is worth acknowledging.
- The manuscript should clarify the handling of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, particularly in specialized domains like the genomics dataset. Are terms not found in the pre-trained GloVe embeddings simply disregarded? A discussion of the potential impact of this decision on the model's performance is needed.

Low-level issues:
- In Section 2.1, the description of word embeddings implies that vector magnitude is irrelevant when using cosine similarity. This is only true for unit-length vectors. It is worth noting that standard word2vec models do not inherently produce unit-length vectors, though some pre-trained models are normalized as a post-processing step. This is a minor but important technical detail.
- The plots presented in Figure 1 are too small to be clearly legible.
