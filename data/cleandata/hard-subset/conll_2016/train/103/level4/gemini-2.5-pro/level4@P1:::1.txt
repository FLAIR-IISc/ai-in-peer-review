### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel and intuitive method for measuring the quality of topics produced by models like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to group a topic's most probable words into "buckets" based on their semantic similarity, which is derived from pre-trained word embeddings. A topic is considered coherent if its words cluster into a single large bucket. The authors propose three variants for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering and two parameter-free variants using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), with one including a reorganization step. The methods are evaluated by correlating their coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets. The primary contribution is a new framework for measuring topic coherence that leverages word embeddings for thematic grouping, achieving state-of-the-art results on two of the three datasets. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based methods is also a notable contribution.

### Strengths

1.  **An Intuitive and Novel Approach:** The central idea of grouping topic words into semantic "buckets" is highly intuitive and aligns well with how humans assess topic coherence. It represents a logical and powerful extension of previous work that focused primarily on pairwise word similarity. By operationalizing this concept using word embeddings and techniques like clustering and SVD, the paper offers a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art performance on the 20 Newsgroups and New York Times datasets, outperforming the extensive set of baselines evaluated by Röder et al. (2015). This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach on standard, general-domain corpora.

3.  **Parameter-Free Variants:** The SVD-based methods are presented as being parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage. Many existing coherence measures, including the state-of-the-art comparator, require tuning multiple parameters, which can be computationally expensive and may not generalize well. A robust, parameter-free method is highly desirable for practitioners, and this work makes a valuable contribution in that direction.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent Performance Across Datasets:** The paper's strongest argument is undermined by the inconsistent performance of its methods. While TBuckets-SVD-Reorg excels on the NYT and 20NG datasets, its performance drops significantly on the Genomics dataset, falling well below the state-of-the-art. Conversely, the clustering-based method performs best on the Genomics dataset. This lack of consistency is a major concern for practical application, as it is unclear which variant a user should choose for a new dataset without first comparing it to human judgments. The paper offers no discussion of why different methods perform better on different datasets or how one might select the optimal method a priori.

2.  **Unclear Handling of Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Words:** The proposed methods are fundamentally dependent on pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe). A likely explanation for the poor performance on the specialized Genomics dataset is a high rate of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. The paper fails to discuss how OOV words are handled. Are they simply ignored? If so, this could severely compromise the coherence score for topics with many domain-specific terms. This is a critical methodological detail that needs to be clarified, as it directly impacts the method's applicability to specialized domains.

3.  **Overstated "Parameter-Free" Claim:** The claim that the SVD-based methods are "completely parameter free" is an overstatement. While they do not introduce new parameters in their own right, they inherit the numerous implicit parameters of the pre-trained word embeddings they rely on (e.g., embedding dimension, context window size, training corpus). The choice of embeddings is arguably the single most important hyperparameter of the entire system, and the results are entirely contingent upon it. This dependency should be more explicitly acknowledged and discussed.

4.  **Missing Connections to Related Work:** The concept of grouping semantically related words has been extensively studied in the lexical chaining literature. The proposed "bucketing" approach bears a strong resemblance to methods for creating lexical chains. A discussion of this related work would provide valuable context and help to better situate the paper's contribution within the broader field.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify how your method handles out-of-vocabulary terms—words from a topic that are not present in the pre-trained GloVe embedding space? What is the OOV rate for each of the three test datasets, and how does your handling of these terms affect the final coherence score? Could the high OOV rate on the Genomics dataset explain the significant performance drop of the SVD-based methods?

2.  Given the inconsistent performance of the different TBuckets variants across the three datasets, do you have any recommendations for practitioners on how to select the best method for a new corpus? Is it possible to use characteristics of the dataset (e.g., domain specificity, vocabulary overlap with the GloVe training corpus) to automatically predict which variant is likely to perform best?

### Additional Comments

*   In Section 2.1, your description of word embeddings and cosine similarity is slightly imprecise. While cosine similarity is standard, it ignores vector magnitude, which can also carry semantic information in some embedding models. Although a minor point, clarifying that your method relies only on the direction of the vectors would be more accurate.
*   The plots in Figure 1 are too small to be clearly readable. Please consider enlarging them in the final version of the paper.
