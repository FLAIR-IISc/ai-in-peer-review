This paper proposes a new evaluation method for topic models, which operates by partitioning the top *n* words of each topic into clusters (or "buckets") based on the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. In its simplest form, the method iterates through the top words of a topic. Each word is either assigned to an existing bucket if its cosine similarity to the words in that bucket exceeds a given threshold, or it is used to create a new bucket. The paper also introduces two more complex variations of this method based on eigenvectors and reorganization. The proposed method is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly-supervised text classification setting. Results show that the method performs on par with or outperforms the current state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

The paper's core idea is straightforward, but feels somewhat ad-hoc. The authors do not provide a strong theoretical justification for why topic quality should be measurable by word-word similarity. It is not immediately obvious why this approach should be effective, given that topic models and word embeddings derive from two distinct notions of context: document-level co-occurrence for topics, and local, sequential co-occurrence for embeddings. Despite these conceptual concerns, the proposed method appears to perform well empirically. However, the results in Table 1 would be more convincing with the inclusion of significance tests.

Overall, the paper is well-written, though it contains some minor grammatical errors. I found the description of the techniques in Section 3 difficult to follow. This is primarily due to the use of the passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as") in places where the authors are describing their own design choices. Using the active voice would more clearly attribute these decisions to the authors and improve readability. I recommend that the authors restructure this section to explain each method in a dedicated subsection for improved clarity. Space for this expansion could be created, as the paper does not use the full 8 pages. For instance, the illustrative "trace" of the method on page 3 is not very informative and could be condensed or removed.

A remaining question is the sensitivity of the proposed technique to the choice of word embeddings. For example, how would the performance change if word2vec embeddings were used instead of the GloVe embeddings presented in the paper?
