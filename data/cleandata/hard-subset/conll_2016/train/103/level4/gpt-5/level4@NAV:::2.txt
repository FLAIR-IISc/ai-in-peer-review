This paper introduces a new approach for evaluating topic models that groups the top n words of each topic into clusters or “buckets” based on the cosine similarity of their corresponding word-embedding vectors. In the simplest variant, words are processed sequentially and each is either assigned to an existing bucket—if its cosine similarity to the words already in that bucket is below a fixed threshold—or a new bucket is created for it. Two more sophisticated variants based on eigenvectors and reorganisation are also proposed. The method is tested on three standard datasets and within a weakly supervised text classification setup. It matches or surpasses the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

The core idea is quite straightforward and has something of an ad hoc flavor. The authors do not provide new justification for why topic quality should be quantifiable via word–word similarity. This is not self-evident to me, given that topics and word embeddings are grounded in different notions of context (document vs. sequential). That said, the proposed method appears to perform well. (I would like to see significance testing for Table 1.)

Overall, the paper is clearly written, though there are some language issues. I also found the techniques in Section 3 somewhat difficult to follow; I believe this is largely because the authors use passive constructions (“the threshold is computed as”) in places where they are actually making design choices. The different methods should be explained more transparently, ideally with a dedicated subsection for each. There seems to be space for this: the 8 pages of content are not fully used, and the rather uninformative “trace” of the method on page 3 could be shortened.

One question I had concerns the sensitivity of the technique to the choice of word embeddings. For instance, how would the scores change if the authors used word2vec instead of GloVe?
