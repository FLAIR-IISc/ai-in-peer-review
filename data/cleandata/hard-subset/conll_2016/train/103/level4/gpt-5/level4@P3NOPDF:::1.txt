This paper proposes a family of metrics for evaluating topic quality that leverage word embeddings—either directly or via matrix factorisation—and reports strong results on standard datasets. The approach is a natural and timely next step in topic evaluation, moving beyond co-occurrence statistics to semantic similarity captured by distributional vectors.

Strengths:
- Clear, intuitive idea with a solid motivation: using embedding-based similarity to assess topic coherence.
- Strong empirical results: state-of-the-art performance is achieved across all three datasets.
- Conceptual simplicity and potential generality: the method can, in principle, be applied to topics from diverse models and domains.

Main concerns:
- Inconsistent performance across methods and datasets. While you reach SOTA on all datasets at least once, no single variant consistently outperforms the state of the art. In particular, SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. For practitioners seeking a reliable off-the-shelf metric, this variability is problematic.
- Likely sensitivity to vocabulary coverage and OOV terms. The genomics dataset likely contains many domain-specific terms absent from the pretrained embeddings (e.g., GloVe), which may explain the SVD underperformance. The paper does not quantify embedding coverage, analyze its impact, or provide guidance on when a method is likely to help or hurt.
- “No parameters” claim. While the proposed scoring function may have no tunable hyperparameters, the approach depends critically on pretrained embeddings that embody numerous design choices and parameters. This dependence should be acknowledged and discussed.

Requests for analysis and clarification:
- OOV handling: How are words without embeddings treated (ignored, zero vectors, random initialization, subword/backoff)? Please quantify OOV rates per dataset and report results stratified by coverage. An ablation showing performance as a function of coverage (e.g., by masking embeddings) would be highly informative.
- Method selection guidance: Can you predict which variant (direct similarity vs. SVD-based) will perform best for a given dataset? For example, can simple diagnostics—embedding coverage, domain similarity to the embedding training corpus, or lexical variability—serve as cues? A small meta-analysis or heuristic selection rule would improve practical usability.
- Embedding choices and preprocessing: How sensitive are results to the choice of embeddings (GloVe vs. word2vec vs. domain-specific embeddings) and to preprocessing (e.g., dimensionality, normalization)? Please clarify whether vectors are normalized before computing cosine similarity. Note that word2vec vectors are not unit-length by default; cosine similarity is scale-invariant only if vectors are explicitly normalized. Reporting whether you normalize and whether that affects results would address a subtle but important detail.
- Relation to lexical chaining: The proposed approach is closely related to lexical chaining and semantic proximity methods in classical NLP. Please discuss this literature, how your metrics differ (e.g., no sense inventory, continuous similarity), and whether insights from chaining (e.g., chaining strength, path structure) could inform your design.

Minor issues and presentation:
- Figure readability: The plots in Figure 1 are too small to be legible. Please increase font sizes, line widths, and resolution, and consider moving dense plots to the appendix.
- Terminology and claims: Rephrase “no parameters” to “no dataset-specific hyperparameters in the scoring function,” with an explicit note on the reliance on pretrained embeddings.
- Methodological details: Provide exact preprocessing steps (tokenization, lowercasing, lemmatization), vocabulary matching strategy (e.g., casing and OOV mapping), and any dimensionality reduction settings for SVD. Report runtime and memory costs for each variant to help practitioners choose among them.

Suggestions to strengthen the paper:
- Add a coverage analysis: report per-dataset embedding coverage, correlation between coverage and metric performance, and a controlled experiment where you vary coverage artificially.
- Include an automatic selection heuristic: e.g., choose the variant based on coverage threshold or cross-validated correlation on a small held-out set.
- Evaluate with domain-specific embeddings (e.g., biomedical embeddings for genomics) to test whether performance gaps are due to domain mismatch rather than the method itself.
- Provide an error analysis of topics where your metrics disagree with human judgments to expose failure modes (e.g., synonymy vs. topical diversity, rare terms, multiword expressions).
- Clarify normalization: state explicitly whether vectors are L2-normalized before computing cosine similarity and whether SVD inputs/outputs are normalized.

Overall assessment:
This is a promising and well-motivated contribution that advances topic quality evaluation by incorporating semantic information from embeddings. The empirical results are encouraging, but the method’s inconsistent performance across datasets and the lack of analysis on vocabulary coverage and OOV handling limit its reliability as an off-the-shelf tool. Addressing the coverage sensitivity, clarifying preprocessing and normalization, engaging with related lexical chaining work, and offering guidance on method selection would significantly improve the paper’s clarity, robustness, and practical impact.
