Summary and contributions
This paper introduces TBuckets, a family of methods for evaluating the coherence of LDA topics by grouping top topic words into “buckets” based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. The main contributions, as I see them, are:
1) A simple, intuitive bucket-based coherence framework operationalized via three techniques: agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering), SVD-based bucketing (TBuckets-SVD), and an SVD variant with automatic reorganization (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg).
2) Strong empirical performance on standard datasets, with SVD-Reorg outperforming prior work on 2 of 3 datasets (NYT and 20NG), and competitive results for clustering on Genomics.
3) A practical application showing that selecting higher-coherence topic models (as scored by TBuckets) improves weakly supervised text classification performance in most cases.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and interpretability: The bucketing idea closely mirrors how humans assess topic coherence (identifying one dominant semantic cluster). This makes the method easy to explain and reason about.
- Strong empirical results with minimal tuning: SVD-based variants are parameter-free at inference time and achieve or surpass the state of the art on two datasets, which is impressive given that competing methods typically require nontrivial parameter tuning.
- Practical utility for weak supervision: The text classification experiment demonstrates a tangible benefit—filtering topic sets by TBuckets coherence generally improves macro-F1, especially on harder subsets (e.g., PC vs. MAC).
- Sensible scoring choices: Using the size of the largest bucket, weighting by topic word order, and leveraging eigenvector similarities/singular values are reasonable, complementary signals aligned with the method’s intuition.
- Resource-light and easy to adopt: The approach only needs the learned topic-word lists and off-the-shelf embeddings, making it easy to plug into existing topic modeling pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Inconsistent performance across datasets/variants: While the methods often perform well, no single variant consistently beats the state of the art across all datasets. The SVD-based methods notably underperform on Genomics, raising concerns about off-the-shelf robustness. This inconsistency needs analysis and guidance for users choosing among the variants.
- Dependence on pre-trained embeddings and OOV handling: Results likely hinge on coverage and domain match of the embeddings. The Genomics dataset is prone to out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, which may explain SVD’s poorer performance. The paper does not describe how OOV words are handled (e.g., dropped, random vectors, special token), nor quantify OOV rates per dataset or the impact on bucket formation and scores.
- “Parameter-free” claim is somewhat overstated: While the SVD variants require no tunable parameters during bucketing, the approach depends on word embeddings that embody numerous design and training hyperparameters. This implicit dependency should be acknowledged, along with domain mismatch risks and potential variability across embedding choices.
- Missing connection to related literature on lexical chaining: The method’s core idea—grouping semantically related words into chains/buckets—bears strong resemblance to lexical chaining work. Positioning TBuckets relative to this literature would strengthen the framing and clarify novelty.
- Clarity and presentation issues: Figure 1 is too small to read. Section 2.1 implicitly treats cosine similarity as invariant to vector length, but word2vec embeddings are not guaranteed to be unit-normalized unless explicitly normalized; this matters for reproducibility and correctness.

Questions to authors
- OOV handling and coverage:
  - How are OOV words treated in each TBuckets variant? Are they ignored or mapped to a default vector? How does this affect bucket sizes and the largest-bucket score?
  - What are the OOV rates per dataset (especially Genomics), and how do the results change if OOV words are excluded vs. included with a proxy vector?
  - Have you tried domain-specific embeddings (e.g., biomedical embeddings or fastText subword embeddings) for Genomics to mitigate OOV and domain mismatch?
- Variant selection and robustness:
  - Can you provide guidance or an automatic criterion to choose among TBuckets-Clustering, SVD, and SVD-Reorg (e.g., based on vocabulary coverage or estimated domain match to the embedding space)?
  - Do the SVD variants improve if vectors are normalized or if you use different similarity metrics? Are bucket assignments stable across random seeds?
- Implementation details and ablations:
  - Do you L2-normalize word vectors before computing cosine similarities and before SVD? Are eigenvectors normalized prior to similarity computation?
  - How sensitive are results to the choice of embedding model (GloVe vs. word2vec vs. fastText) and dimensionality?
  - For the scoring, what is the incremental value of WL and EVS over bucket size alone? An ablation would help interpretability.
- Reproducibility and presentation:
  - Please enlarge Figure 1 and provide numeric results in the caption or main text.
  - Specify exactly which pre-trained embeddings were used (corpus, dimension, normalization), and any preprocessing (lowercasing, tokenization) for matching topic words to embeddings.

Additional comments
- The idea that performance might be predictable from vocabulary overlap with the embedding vocabulary is appealing. Reporting simple predictors (e.g., OOV rate, average IDF, domain-specific term ratio) and their correlation with your methods’ relative performance would make the work more actionable.
- Given the close conceptual alignment with lexical chaining, a brief discussion contrasting TBuckets’ use of modern embeddings/SVD with earlier chaining methods (which often rely on thesauri or WordNet) would help situate the contribution.
- Please clarify the discussion in Section 2.1 regarding cosine similarity and vector normalization for correctness and reproducibility.
