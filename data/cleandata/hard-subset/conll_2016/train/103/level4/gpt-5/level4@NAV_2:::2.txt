This paper introduces a new approach for assessing topic model quality by partitioning the top n words of each topic into clusters (“buckets”) using cosine similarity over pre-trained word embeddings. In its most straightforward variant, words are processed sequentially: each word is assigned to an existing bucket if it meets a similarity threshold with that bucket’s contents; otherwise, a new bucket is created. The authors also propose two more advanced variants built around SVD-derived eigenvectors and a subsequent reorganization step. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification scenario, showing performance that matches or surpasses the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

The core idea is simple and somewhat ad hoc. The paper does not provide a fresh rationale for why topic quality should be captured by word–word similarity. Given that topics and embeddings reflect different contextual assumptions (document-level vs. sequential co-occurrence), this connection is not self-evident. That said, the proposed metrics appear to work well in practice. (I would, however, like to see significance testing for the differences reported in Table 1.)

Overall, the paper is readable, though there are a few language issues. I found parts of Section 3 difficult to follow, likely because passive constructions (“the threshold is computed as”) obscure where design choices are being made. The exposition would benefit from clearer, method-by-method subsections. There seems to be room for this: the paper does not fully use the 8 pages of content and could trim the relatively uninformative procedural “trace” on page 3.

One question I had concerns sensitivity to the choice of embeddings. For instance, how would results change if word2vec were used instead of GloVe?
