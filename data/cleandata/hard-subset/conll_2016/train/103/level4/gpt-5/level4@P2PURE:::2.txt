This paper introduces a new evaluation method for topic models that groups the top-n words of each topic into clusters (“buckets”) using cosine similarity between their word-embedding vectors. In the simplest variant, words are processed sequentially and assigned to an existing bucket if they satisfy a cosine-similarity threshold relative to the words already in that bucket; otherwise, a new bucket is created. The authors also propose two more elaborate variants based on eigenvectors and reorganization. They evaluate the approach on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text-classification setting, where it outperforms or matches the current state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

The core idea is fairly simple and somewhat ad hoc. The paper does not provide a new rationale for why topic quality should be captured by word–word similarity. This is not self-evident, given that topics and word embeddings are grounded in different notions of context (document-level vs. sequential context). Nonetheless, the method appears to perform well. I would, however, like to see significance tests for Table 1.

Overall, the paper is clearly written, though there are some language issues. I found the description of the techniques in Section 3 somewhat hard to follow, likely because the authors use passive constructions (e.g., “the threshold is computed as”) where they are in fact making design choices. The methods would benefit from clearer exposition, with a dedicated subsection for each. There seems to be space for this: the paper does not fully use the eight pages of content, and the rather uninformative “trace” of the method on page 3 could be shortened.

One open question is the sensitivity of the technique to the choice of word embeddings. For example, how would the scores change if word2vec were used instead of GloVe?
