This paper proposes a new method for evaluating topic models that groups the top n words of each topic into clusters (“buckets”) based on cosine similarity computed from pre-trained word embeddings. In the simplest variant, words are processed sequentially: each word is either assigned to an existing bucket if it meets a cosine-similarity threshold with the words already in that bucket, or it starts a new bucket. The authors also propose two more complex variants that incorporate eigenvectors and a reorganization step. The approach is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setting, and it outperforms or is on par with the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

The core idea is straightforward and somewhat ad hoc. The paper does not provide a new justification for why topic quality should be captured by word–word similarity in an embedding space. This is not self-evident, since topics and word embeddings are induced from fundamentally different notions of context (document-level co-occurrence versus local sequential context). Despite this conceptual gap, the proposed method performs well empirically. I would, however, like to see statistical significance tests for the comparisons in Table 1.

Overall, the paper is clearly written, though there are occasional language issues. I also found parts of Section 3 difficult to follow. In several places, the use of the passive voice (e.g., “the threshold is computed as …”) obscures that the authors are making specific design choices. Presenting each variant in a dedicated subsection, with explicit statements of the decisions made and their motivations, would improve clarity. There appears to be room to do so: the paper does not fully use the 8-page limit, and the relatively uninformative “trace” of the method on page 3 could be condensed to free up space for clearer algorithmic descriptions.

One important question is the sensitivity of the proposed metric to the choice of word embeddings. How robust are the scores across different embedding models and training regimes? For example, how would the results change if the authors used word2vec instead of GloVe? Including such an analysis (or at least a discussion) would strengthen the empirical claims and help readers assess the generality of the approach.
