This paper proposes a new intrinsic evaluation method for topic models that groups the top-n words of each topic into clusters (“buckets”) based on cosine similarity of their word embeddings. In its simplest, greedy variant, words are processed sequentially and either assigned to an existing bucket if their similarity to words already in the bucket exceeds a threshold, or placed in a new bucket. Two more sophisticated variants—one leveraging eigenvectors and one involving post-hoc reorganization—are also introduced. The approach is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setting, and is reported to outperform or match the coherence metric of Röder et al. (2015).

Strengths
- The method is simple, easy to implement, and computationally light. Its reliance on pre-trained embeddings makes it broadly applicable without extensive corpus-specific estimation.
- Empirical results are promising: across several datasets and a downstream weakly supervised classification task, the proposed scores are competitive with, and often better than, a strong baseline.
- The paper is generally clear and well structured, and it addresses an important problem: automatic, scalable evaluation of topic quality.

Concerns and weaknesses
- Conceptual justification is limited. The paper assumes that higher word-word semantic similarity (as captured by embeddings trained on sequential context) implies greater topic quality (often grounded in document-level co-occurrence). The mismatch between the contexts that define topics (documents) and those that define embeddings (local sequential windows) deserves a deeper discussion. Why should embedding-based similarity be the right proxy, and under what conditions might it fail (e.g., topics driven by discourse-level associations rather than lexical semantics)?
- The approach has an ad hoc flavor: the bucket formation, thresholding, and the choice among the three variants appear design-driven rather than theoretically motivated. That need not be a problem if the empirical evidence is strong, but it increases the importance of careful analysis and ablations.
- Statistical rigor: Table 1 would benefit from significance testing (e.g., paired tests across topics or runs) to support claims of superiority or parity with Röder et al. (2015).
- Sensitivity to embeddings is not explored. Results may depend on the embedding model (GloVe vs. word2vec vs. fastText), training corpus, dimensionality, and preprocessing. Without this, it is hard to know whether the gains generalize.

Clarity and presentation
- Section 3 is difficult to follow in places, partly due to passive constructions that obscure design choices (e.g., “the threshold is computed as…”). Please clarify who chooses what, and why.
- I recommend restructuring Section 3 with one subsection per method (greedy, eigenvector-based, reorganization), each with: algorithmic steps, hyperparameters, and computational complexity. Pseudocode or a concise algorithm box would help.
- The “trace” on p. 3 is relatively uninformative and could be compressed or moved to the appendix; the saved space could support clearer method explanations and ablations. The paper does not fully use the 8-page limit; additional detail would improve readability.
- Minor language issues appear throughout; a careful proofreading pass would help.

Requests for clarification and additional analysis
- Thresholding:
  - How is the similarity threshold chosen (globally, per-topic, or per-dataset)? Is it tuned on a development set? Provide its range, selection procedure, and sensitivity analysis.
  - What similarity aggregator determines bucket membership (minimum, mean, or median similarity to current bucket members)? How sensitive are results to this choice?
  - Is the greedy algorithm order-sensitive? If so, how do you mitigate that (e.g., sort by frequency or centrality), and what is the variance across random orderings?
- Variants and ablations:
  - Please report ablations comparing the basic greedy method, the eigenvector-based variant, and the reorganization step, isolating each component’s contribution.
  - Provide per-dataset breakdowns and standard deviations across runs.
- Embeddings:
  - How would results change with word2vec or fastText embeddings, different dimensionalities, or embeddings trained on different corpora? If GloVe is trained on a large external corpus, discuss domain mismatch effects.
  - Are embeddings fine-tuned or kept fixed? Are OOV or rare words dropped, and with what impact on topics that include infrequent terms?
- Scope and parameters:
  - Analyze sensitivity to the choice of top-n words per topic and to the number of topics. Do bucket counts or coherence scores behave monotonically with n?
  - Discuss runtime and scalability for large topic sets and vocabularies.
- Evaluation:
  - In addition to the weakly supervised classification experiment, consider correlating your metric with human topic coherence judgments (if available) or providing a small human study to ground the metric’s validity.
  - For Table 1 and downstream results, include confidence intervals and appropriate significance tests.

Overall assessment
This paper presents a practical and effective embedding-based approach to topic evaluation. While the core idea is straightforward and somewhat heuristic, the empirical findings are encouraging. Strengthening the paper along four axes—(i) clearer algorithmic exposition with explicit design rationales, (ii) statistical testing, (iii) sensitivity analyses (especially embedding dependence and thresholding), and (iv) streamlined presentation—would substantially improve its rigor and impact. I am cautiously positive about the contribution, provided the authors address these clarity and evaluation concerns.
