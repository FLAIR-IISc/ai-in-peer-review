Summary
This paper introduces TBuckets, a method for evaluating topic quality by grouping topic words into “buckets” using word embeddings. It implements three variants: agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering), SVD-based bucketing (TBuckets-SVD), and SVD with a post-hoc reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). Topic coherence is estimated primarily from the size and composition of the largest bucket, optionally weighted by LDA word order and eigenvector similarity. On three public datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), TBuckets achieves strong correlations with human judgments, and the authors further demonstrate gains in weakly supervised text classification when selecting higher-coherence topic runs.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, interpretable bucket-based coherence measure grounded in distributional semantics, with a parameter-free SVD formulation and an effective reorganization step that improves coherence scoring.
2) An empirical study showing that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg matches or exceeds prior best coherence correlations on two datasets and is competitive on the third, alongside a comparison to Röder et al.’s framework.
3) A practical demonstration that coherence-based selection of topic runs can improve weakly supervised classification performance.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and interpretability: The “largest coherent bucket” intuition is natural and closely aligned with how humans assess topic coherence. The reorganization step is well-motivated by observed failure modes.
- Strong empirical results with minimal tuning: The SVD-based methods are parameter-free and still match or surpass prior best correlations on NYT and 20NG. The clustering variant requires only one threshold parameter and performs competitively on Genomics.
- Practical utility: The application to weakly supervised text classification is valuable and shows that coherence estimation can translate into measurable downstream gains by prioritizing better topic runs.
- Solid baselines and evaluation: The comparison against Röder et al.’s configurations on standard datasets and the use of Pearson correlation with human ratings follow established practice.

Weaknesses
- Inconsistent performance across datasets and variants: The best-performing TBuckets variant changes by dataset; notably, the SVD-based methods underperform on Genomics. This undermines the “off-the-shelf” appeal and suggests sensitivity to domain and vocabulary coverage.
- Dependence on pretrained embeddings and OOV handling: The approach relies on GloVe vectors trained on general corpora, which may poorly cover specialized domains (e.g., Genomics). The paper does not specify how out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words are treated (ignored, random, UNK, retrofitted), nor quantify per-topic coverage. This likely contributes to the SVD variants’ weaker Genomics results.
- “No parameters” claim needs nuance: While SVD-based TBuckets has no explicit tuning within the method, performance depends on upstream choices (embedding model, training corpus, dimensionality, normalization). A brief acknowledgment and sensitivity analysis (e.g., GloVe vs word2vec vs domain-specific vectors) would strengthen the claim.
- Related work gap: The method is closely related in spirit to lexical chaining and topic interpretability via semantic grouping. The paper would benefit from positioning against lexical chaining and other bucketing/grouping strategies beyond distributional measures.
- Generalization and robustness: The evaluation uses the top-10 words/topic on fixed datasets. It would help to assess sensitivity to the number of topic words, alternative topic models, and topic sparsity, as well as report run-time/complexity for large-scale use.

Questions to the Authors
- OOV handling: How do you handle topic words missing from the embedding vocabulary? Are they dropped, mapped to a special token, or estimated somehow? What proportion of topic words are OOV per dataset, and how does coverage correlate with performance?
- Variant selection: Can you predict, a priori, which TBuckets variant will work best on a new corpus using simple diagnostics (e.g., embedding coverage, average pairwise similarity, domain match to the embedding training corpus)?
- Embedding sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the choice of embeddings (GloVe vs word2vec vs fastText, general vs domain-specific, dimensionality, normalization)? Did you try domain-specific vectors for Genomics?
- Robustness to topic size: How does performance change when using more than the top-10 words per topic, or when topics are noisier/denser?
- Reorganization threshold: The dynamic threshold uses AWES and AWPS. Could you provide intuition about its stability across datasets and whether any failure cases were observed?

Additional comments
- Section 2.1: The text implies cosine similarity is generally applicable; however, cosine is scale-invariant only if vectors are consistently normalized. Pretrained word2vec vectors are not unit length by default. Please clarify whether you normalize vectors before computing similarities.
- Figure 1 readability: The plots are too small to read comfortably; please enlarge and/or use higher-resolution axes labels and legends.
- Minor phrasing: Rather than “no parameters,” consider “no method-internal hyperparameters beyond the choice of pretrained embeddings,” to avoid confusion.
