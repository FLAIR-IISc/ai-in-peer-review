Summary and contributions
- This paper introduces TBuckets, a method to evaluate topic quality by grouping the top n words of a topic into “buckets” based on cosine similarity of their word embeddings. The intuition is that a topic forming one large, coherent bucket is of higher quality.
- The authors propose three variants: a clustering-based method with a similarity threshold; an SVD-based method that assigns words to buckets via eigenvectors; and an SVD-based method with a subsequent reorganization step to refine bucket assignments.
- They evaluate on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setup, showing performance that is on par with or better than Röder et al. (2015). Notably, the SVD-based methods are parameter-free.

Strengths
- Empirical performance: TBuckets performs competitively, and in several cases better than the state of the art in correlating with human judgments of topic coherence (Table 1). The gains on NYT and 20NG are particularly compelling.
- Practical utility: The application to weakly supervised text classification is a useful validation. Selecting topic sets by coherence improves downstream classification performance in most settings, illustrating the method’s practical impact.
- Simplicity and intuition: The bucket-based rationale is straightforward and easy to implement. The SVD variants, especially being parameter-free, are attractive for practitioners who want robust defaults without tuning.
- Clear evaluation protocol: The authors follow the established evaluation scheme from Röder et al. (2015), facilitating direct comparison and reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Conceptual justification: The core idea has an ad hoc flavor, and the paper does not provide a new or convincing rationale for why topic quality should be captured by word–word similarity in embedding space. This is nontrivial because LDA topics and word embeddings are grounded in different types of context (document-level vs. local sequential context). Some discussion or evidence addressing this mismatch would strengthen the contribution.
- Statistical significance: The paper reports Pearson correlations but does not present significance tests to support claims of superiority over Röder et al. (2015). Confidence intervals or tests for differences in correlations (e.g., Williams’ test or Steiger’s test) would substantiate the reported improvements in Table 1.
- Clarity and presentation of methods: Section 3 is harder to follow than necessary. The heavy use of passive voice obscures design choices (e.g., “the threshold is computed as”). A clearer exposition with one subsection per method (Clustering, SVD, SVD+Reorg), explicit statements of decisions and assumptions, and a concise description of the reorganization criteria would improve readability.
- Space allocation: The “trace” example on page 3 is lengthy and adds limited insight; the space would be better used to clarify the methods. The paper does not fill the full 8 pages, so there is room for a more structured and detailed explanation.
- Sensitivity to embeddings: The approach relies on word embeddings trained on specific corpora (GloVe on Wikipedia/Gigaword). The paper does not examine how sensitive TBuckets is to the choice of embeddings (e.g., word2vec vs. GloVe, training corpora, dimensionality), which could materially affect results across domains.

Questions to the authors
- How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? For example, what happens if you use word2vec instead of GloVe, or embeddings trained on different corpora or with different dimensionalities?
- Can you report statistical significance for the differences in Pearson correlations in Table 1 (e.g., confidence intervals or formal tests comparing correlations)?
- For the clustering variant, how is the similarity threshold chosen in practice? Is performance stable across reasonable threshold ranges?
- For the SVD+Reorg method, could you provide more intuition for the dynamic threshold formula and an ablation showing the impact of the reorganization step relative to plain SVD?

Overall assessment
While the method is simple and somewhat ad hoc, the empirical results are strong and the parameter-free SVD variants are appealing. The paper would benefit from clearer exposition of the methods, significance testing, and a sensitivity analysis to the choice of embeddings.
