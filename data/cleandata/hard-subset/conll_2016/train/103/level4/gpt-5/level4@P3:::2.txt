Summary and contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using distributional similarity from word embeddings. The core intuition is that a coherent topic should yield a single dominant bucket with most words belonging to one theme. The paper introduces three variants:
- TBuckets-Clustering: agglomerative clustering of word embeddings with a maximum distance threshold (tested with single/average/complete linkage).
- TBuckets-SVD: assigns words to buckets using eigenvectors from an SVD of the topic’s word-embedding matrix; the first n eigenvectors (for n topic words) serve as bucket identifiers; parameter-free.
- TBuckets-SVD-Reorg: a reorganization heuristic that moves words into or out of the largest bucket using a dynamic, data-derived threshold based on average word–word and word–eigenvector similarities; parameter-free.

Topics are scored using properties of the largest bucket (size; word-order weights from LDA; and eigenvector similarity for SVD variants), with results weighted by the top singular value for SVD methods. Evaluation on three datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics) reports Pearson correlations with human coherence ratings and shows TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforming a strong baseline (Röder et al., 2015) on two datasets and being competitive on the third. The paper also demonstrates an application: selecting higher-coherence topic models to improve weakly supervised text classification.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, interpretable, and largely parameter-free coherence family (especially the SVD-based variants) that achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two datasets.
2) A reorganization heuristic that measurably improves SVD-based bucketing without manual tuning.
3) A practical application showing that coherence-driven model selection can improve weakly supervised classification.

Strengths
- Strong empirical performance with low complexity and minimal tuning: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg attains Pearson r of 0.837 (NYT) and 0.869 (20NG), surpassing the best reported settings in Röder et al. (0.806 and 0.859, respectively), while remaining competitive on Genomics. The SVD-based methods require no parameter tuning, a practical advantage over many coherence measures that need corpus-specific calibration.
- Clear and intuitive rationale with interpretable outputs: The “largest bucket” notion aligns with human intuition about single-theme coherence, and the produced buckets are easy to inspect. The method’s transparency is a plus for diagnostic use and downstream selection of topic models.
- Demonstrated downstream utility: Using TBuckets scores to select topic-model runs improves macro-F1 in 3 of 4 20NG binary tasks, with especially notable gains on harder tasks (e.g., PC vs MAC), supporting the practical value of the coherence scores.
- Systematic ablations of scoring choices: Table 1 reports multiple scoring combinations (size, WL, EVS, and their products), giving insight into which components drive performance.
- Reproducibility potential: The method uses public datasets and off-the-shelf embeddings (GloVe 300D), making replication and adoption feasible.

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical grounding for the embedding-based proxy: The paper does not justify why word–word similarity from distributional embeddings (trained on sequential context) should reliably reflect topic coherence (rooted in document-level co-occurrence). This conceptual gap is especially relevant when embeddings and topic-training corpora differ, or when topics are multi-faceted yet still coherent to humans.
- Sensitivity to embedding choice and domain mismatch is not analyzed: Results rely on a single embedding type (GloVe 300D trained on Wikipedia/Gigaword). Performance drops on the Genomics dataset (best r ≈ 0.691 vs 0.773 for Röder et al.), plausibly due to domain vocabulary. There is no study of alternative embeddings (word2vec, fastText, domain-specific embeddings), dimensionality, or OOV handling.
- Statistical rigor of comparisons: The paper reports Pearson r but provides no confidence intervals, significance tests, or tests for differences between dependent correlations. Given only 100 topics per dataset, margins can matter; formal significance testing (e.g., Fisher r-to-z, Williams test, or bootstrap) is needed to substantiate gains over Röder et al.
- Method description and organization: Section 3 is difficult to follow in places. Key design choices (e.g., using the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers; rationale for weighting by the top singular value; specifics of the reorganization thresholds) would benefit from a clearer, step-by-step algorithmic presentation and reduced reliance on long “trace” examples. Language issues (frequent passive voice, some inconsistencies) further obscure the flow.
- Potential metric bias toward single-theme topics: Scoring hinges on the largest bucket, which may penalize topics that are interpretable but legitimately multi-thematic (e.g., compound topics or curated mixtures). The method also fixes N=10 words but does not analyze robustness to N, even though N can affect bucket structure and human interpretability.

Questions to authors
- Embedding robustness: How sensitive are results to the embedding type, dimensionality, and training corpus? Have you tried word2vec or fastText, or domain-specific embeddings for Genomics? How are OOV words handled?
- Statistical testing: Are the improvements over Röder et al. statistically significant? Please provide confidence intervals and appropriate tests for differences between dependent correlations.
- Algorithmic details: For TBuckets-SVD, why use the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers (rather than selecting by singular values or using k<n)? What is the computational cost per topic for SVD and reorganization?
- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the derivation and intuition behind the dynamic threshold AWES − (AWPS/AWES). How often does reorganization move words into the largest bucket versus evict them, and how does each operation affect correlation (ablation)?
- Choice of N and scoring: How robust are results to the number of top words (e.g., N=10 vs 20)? Have you considered combining bucket size with measures that capture multiple sub-themes (e.g., entropy across buckets) to avoid penalizing coherent multi-theme topics?

Overall, this is a promising and practical contribution. With clearer exposition, embedding sensitivity analyses, and statistical testing, the paper would be significantly stronger.
