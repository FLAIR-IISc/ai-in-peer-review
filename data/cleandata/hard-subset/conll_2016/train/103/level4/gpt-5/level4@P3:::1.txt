Summary and contributions
The paper introduces TBuckets, a set of methods for estimating topic quality by grouping a topic’s top words into “buckets” based on distributional similarity in embedding space. The central intuition is that a coherent topic should yield one dominant bucket capturing a single theme. Three realizations are proposed: (i) TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering of word embeddings), (ii) TBuckets-SVD (using eigenvectors from an SVD of the topic word embedding matrix as bucket identifiers), and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a post-hoc reorganization step to move words into or out of the largest bucket using dynamic thresholds). Topic coherence is scored from properties of the largest bucket and correlates are reported against human judgments on three standard datasets. The paper also illustrates an application to weakly supervised text classification by filtering runs with higher coherence.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, embedding-based bucketing framework for topic coherence that aims to capture single-theme dominance via the largest bucket, with a parameter-free SVD variant and a reorganization heuristic.
2) Empirical evidence that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg matches or outperforms prior best results (Röder et al., 2015) on two datasets (NYT, 20NG), and that coherence-based selection can improve weakly supervised classification performance.
3) A practical, interpretable diagnostic (bucket distributions) that makes error analysis of topics more tangible.

Strengths
- Clear and intuitive formulation: The bucketing metaphor closely mirrors how humans informally assess topics. The examples show how coherent topics collapse into a single dominant bucket, whereas mixed topics split across multiple buckets. This interpretability is a genuine practical advantage over opaque coherence scores.
- Strong performance on two datasets with minimal tuning: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves state-of-the-art correlations on NYT and 20NG while avoiding extensive parameter sweeps required by many coherence configurations (as in Röder et al.). This makes the approach appealing for practitioners.
- Parameter-free SVD variants: The SVD-based methods avoid explicit thresholds and hyperparameters during bucket formation, which reduces the burden of tuning and the risk of overfitting to a particular dataset.
- Useful application to weak supervision: The classification experiment demonstrates that selecting topic sets with higher TBuckets coherence can boost downstream performance (notably on more challenging splits like PC vs MAC). This strengthens the case that the score is not only correlated with human judgments but also actionable.
- Reorganization step improves robustness: The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg procedure addresses a realistic failure mode (words attracted to non-dominant eigenvectors despite being thematically closer to the largest bucket). The dynamic threshold based on AWPS and AWES is a thoughtful design to avoid additional hyperparameters.

Weaknesses
- Inconsistent performance across datasets and methods: While the approach looks promising overall, no single variant consistently dominates. In particular, SVD-based methods underperform on Genomics, and TBuckets-Clustering requires a max-distance parameter that changes optimal performance depending on linkage. For off-the-shelf use on arbitrary domains, this instability is a concern.
- Dependence on pretrained embeddings and OOV handling: The approach relies on GloVe embeddings trained on general-domain corpora. Domain-specific datasets like Genomics are likely to contain many OOV or poorly represented terms, which probably explains the drop in performance. The paper does not state how OOVs are handled (ignored, averaged, random, or otherwise), nor does it quantify OOV rates or their impact on the score.
- “No parameters” claim is somewhat overstated: Although the SVD pipeline avoids tunable thresholds, the method inherits design choices and implicit parameters from the embedding space (dimension, training corpus, context window, etc.), and the reorganization step’s threshold is derived but still a modeling choice. This should be acknowledged to avoid misleading readers.
- Largest-bucket bias and multi-theme topics: Scoring heavily based on the largest bucket may penalize topics that legitimately contain two coherent sub-themes (e.g., a topic that systematically blends two facets). The framework could conflate “multi-faceted but coherent” with “incoherent,” and the paper does not quantify how often this harms correlation.
- Limited statistical analysis and ablations: The paper reports Pearson correlations but does not provide confidence intervals, significance tests between methods, or ablations (e.g., effect of WL vs EVS vs singular-value weighting, and the net gain from Reorg). This makes it hard to judge robustness and the specific contribution of each component.

Questions to authors
- How are OOV words handled in each method (Clustering, SVD, Reorg)? Are such words dropped, assigned zero vectors, or mapped via subword/backoff methods? Please report OOV rates per dataset and their effect on correlations.
- Can you provide significance testing (e.g., Williams’ test) for correlation differences versus baselines and among your variants, plus confidence intervals?
- How sensitive are results to the choice of embeddings (e.g., GloVe vs word2vec vs fastText), and to domain-specific embeddings for Genomics? A small sensitivity study would strengthen the generality claims.
- For TBuckets-SVD: you use the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers for n topic words. Given rank constraints and sign indeterminacy of eigenvectors, how do you ensure stability of bucket identities? Are eigenvectors normalized before similarity?
- For the weak supervision experiment, can you report variance across runs and statistical tests for S1 vs S2? Also, what is the annotation-time saving when labeling only the top-5 runs by coherence?

Additional comments
- Related work: Please discuss connections to lexical chaining and related semantic grouping literature, which share the intuition of clustering semantically associated words.
- Clarify the discussion in Section 2.1: cosine similarity assumes vector magnitudes are either normalized or not informative; pretrained word2vec vectors are not inherently unit length, and normalization details matter for reproducibility.
- Figure 1 is too small to read; please enlarge and ensure axis labels and legends are legible.
- Consider reporting an automatic rule to select among TBuckets variants (e.g., based on OOV rate or vocabulary overlap with the embedding model), which would reduce the risk of choosing a suboptimal variant for a new domain.
- Releasing code and evaluation scripts would aid reproducibility and facilitate adoption.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-motivated and practical approach with competitive results on two datasets, a compelling intuition, and a promising downstream application. Addressing the OOV/embedding dependency, clarifying robustness across domains, and adding statistical and ablation analyses would substantially strengthen the paper.
