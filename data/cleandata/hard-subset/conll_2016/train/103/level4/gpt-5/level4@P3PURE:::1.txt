This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality using word embeddings to compute similarity (either directly or indirectly via matrix factorization), and reports impressive results on standard datasets. The approach feels like a natural and important next step in the evolution of research on topic evaluation.

My main concern is the inconsistency of results across variants and datasets. Although you report state-of-the-art performance on all three datasets, the specific methods that perform best vary substantially, and several variants fall below prior state of the art. In practice, no single proposed method consistently outperforms the state of the art across datasets, and the SVD-based variants perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. For practitioners seeking an off-the-shelf solution applicable to arbitrary datasets, this variability is worrisome. I suspect the weaker SVD results on genomics are related to the proportion of OOV terms (see below). It may be possible to predict automatically which variant will perform best based on vocabulary match with the pretrained embeddings (e.g., GloVe), but the paper does not discuss this.

Other issues:
- The proposed method has strong similarities to work in the lexical chaining literature; I encourage the authors to review and cite that line of research in a future revision.
- You emphasize that the method has no parameters, but the word embedding models introduce many hyperparameters that are implicit in your approach. This is not a major issue, but it is worth acknowledging explicitly.
- How does the method handle OOV terms, especially in the genomics dataset (i.e., terms not present in the pretrained GloVe embeddings)? Are such terms ignored, and if so, what is the impact on the scores and overall reliability?

Low-level issues:
- In Section 2.1, the description of word embeddings and the use of cosine similarity implicitly assumes that vector length is unimportant. If vectors are unit length, this is fine; however, word2vec does not inherently produce unit-length vectors (pretrained vectors are often normalized post hoc, and vectors from a fresh word2vec run are not uniformly normalized). This is a small but important technical detail that should be clarified.
- The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable; please enlarge them.

Overall, this is a promising and timely contribution with impressive results, but the paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of robustness across datasets, an analysis of OOV effects (particularly for genomics), guidance on variant selection (potentially via vocabulary overlap with GloVe), and connections to the lexical chaining literature.
