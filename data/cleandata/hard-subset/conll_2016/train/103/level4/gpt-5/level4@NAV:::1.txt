This paper presents a method for assessing topic quality by leveraging word embeddings to compute similarity (either directly or indirectly via matrix factorisation), and reports strong performance on standard datasets. The approach feels like a natural and important next step in the progression of research on topic evaluation. What concerned me most, however, is that although you report state-of-the-art results across all three datasets, there are substantial inconsistencies in which variants perform well and which fall below the state of the art. In practice, none of the proposed variants consistently surpasses the state of the art, and the SVD-based methods perform particularly poorly on the genomics dataset. For a user hoping to deploy the method off the shelf on arbitrary data, this is a significant concern. I suspect the weaker SVD results on genomics are tied to the proportion of OOV terms (see below), and that one might automatically predict which variant will work best based on vocabulary overlap with GloVe, etc., but the paper does not discuss this.

Other issues:
- The proposed approach bears strong resemblance to methods in the lexical chaining literature; I encourage the authors to review and incorporate this line of work in a future version.
- You emphasise that the method has no parameters, yet the underlying word embedding models carry many parameters that are implicit in your approach. Not a major issue, but it should be acknowledged.
- How are OOV terms handled, especially in the genomics dataset (i.e., terms missing from the pretrained GloVe embeddings)? Are they simply omitted? What effect does this have on performance?

Low-level issues:
- In Section 2.1, the discussion of word embeddings implicitly treats vector length as unimportant when invoking cosine similarity. This is fine if vectors are unit normalised, but word2vec does not inherently produce unit-length vectors (pretrained sets are normalised post hoc, and self-trained vectors generally have non-uniform norms). A small detail, but important.
- The plots in Figure 1 are too small to be readable.
