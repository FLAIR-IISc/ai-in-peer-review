This paper introduces a method for evaluating topic quality by leveraging word embeddings to compute semantic similarity, either directly or via matrix factorization. The approach is a natural and meaningful next step in the evolution of topic evaluation, and it achieves strong results on standard datasets.

My main concern is the inconsistency of performance across datasets and variants. Although the paper reports state-of-the-art results on all three datasets, no single proposed variant consistently outperforms the prior best across the board. In particular, the SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. This variability raises concerns for practitioners seeking a reliable, off-the-shelf solution. I suspect the weaker SVD results on genomics are tied to a higher proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms (see below). It may be feasible to predict which variant will work best by examining vocabulary coverage with the chosen embeddings (e.g., GloVe), but the paper does not discuss this possibility. Adding an analysis of OOV rates, vocabulary match, and an automatic selection strategy would strengthen the contribution.

Other issues:
- The method closely resembles ideas from the lexical chaining literature. I encourage the authors to review and cite this work, and to clarify how their approach differs or builds upon it.
- The paper emphasizes that the method has no parameters, yet the underlying word embeddings embody numerous parameters and training choices that implicitly affect performance. This is not a major flaw, but it should be acknowledged to avoid confusion.
- Please clarify how OOV terms are handled (e.g., in the genomics dataset when using pretrained GloVe). Are such terms discarded, assigned default vectors, or treated in another way? What is the quantitative impact on the metric?

Low-level issues:
- In Section 2.1, the discussion of cosine similarity implicitly assumes vector length is irrelevant. This is only appropriate if vectors are normalized to unit length. Word2vec vectors are not unit-length by default (pretrained distributions are often normalized post hoc; if trained locally, norms vary). Please state explicitly whether you normalize vectors and ensure the formulation is consistent with that assumption.
- The plots in Figure 1 are too small to read comfortably. Please enlarge them and consider improving axis labels and legends for clarity.

Overall, the paper presents a timely, well-motivated idea with promising results. Addressing the performance variability, OOV handling, and connections to related work would substantially improve its reliability and impact.
