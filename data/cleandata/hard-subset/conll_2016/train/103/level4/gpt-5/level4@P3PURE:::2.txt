This paper proposes a new method for evaluating topic models that partitions the top n words of each topic into clusters (“buckets”) based on cosine similarity of their associated word embeddings. In the simplest setup, words are processed one by one and are either assigned to an existing bucket—according to a threshold on cosine similarity to the words already in that bucket—or a new bucket is created. Two more complex variants based on eigenvectors and reorganization are also introduced. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setting, and it outperforms or is on par with the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

The basic idea is simple and has a somewhat ad hoc flavor. The authors do not provide a new justification for why topic quality should be measurable in terms of word–word similarity. This is not obvious, given that topics and word embeddings rely on different notions of context (document-level vs. sequential context). That said, the proposed method appears to work well empirically. I would, however, like to see significance tests for Table 1.

Overall, the paper is clearly written, though there are some language issues. I also found the description of the techniques in Section 3 hard to follow; this seems largely due to the use of passive voice (e.g., “the threshold is computed as”) in places where the authors are in fact making design choices. The different methods would be clearer with one subsection per method. There appears to be room for this: the authors did not use the full eight pages of content, and they could downsize the rather uninformative “trace” of the method on page 3.

One question I had is how sensitive the technique is to the choice of word embeddings. For example, how would the scores change if the authors used word2vec instead of GloVe?
