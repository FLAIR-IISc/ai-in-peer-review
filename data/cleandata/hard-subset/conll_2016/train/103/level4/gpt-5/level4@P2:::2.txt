Summary
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-n words of a topic into semantically consistent “buckets” using word embeddings. The intuition is that a coherent topic should produce one dominant bucket. The authors instantiate this idea in three ways: (i) agglomerative clustering over word embeddings with a distance threshold, (ii) an SVD-based assignment of words to orthogonal directions in embedding space, and (iii) an SVD variant with a reorganization step that moves words into or out of the largest bucket using similarity-based rules. Coherence is scored using properties of the largest bucket (size, word order weights, and in SVD variants, similarity to eigenvectors). The methods are evaluated by correlating with human coherence judgments on three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), and are also applied to weakly supervised text classification by selecting topic runs with higher coherence. The SVD-based methods are parameter-free and reported to outperform or match the best configurations in Röder et al. (2015) on two of three datasets.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- A simple, embedding-based bucketization framework for topic coherence, with two parameter-free SVD variants and a lightweight clustering baseline.
- An empirical comparison showing competitive or superior Pearson correlations with human judgments on NYT and 20NG, and parity on Genomics.
- A practical downstream application: using TBuckets to choose better topic runs improves weakly supervised classification in most settings tested.

Strengths
- Practical and simple methods: The SVD-based variants are parameter-free and easy to implement, making them attractive alternatives to coherence measures that require extensive tuning. This enhances reproducibility and deployment.
- Empirical gains on standard benchmarks: On NYT and 20NG, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves higher Pearson r than the best settings in Röder et al. (2015), and clustering is competitive on Genomics. This supports the effectiveness of the “single dominant bucket” intuition.
- Sensible use of word embeddings: Leveraging off-the-shelf GloVe vectors is pragmatic; the approach works with widely available resources and avoids reliance on external statistics or corpora alignment beyond embeddings.
- Downstream utility: The application to weakly supervised classification is compelling. Selecting topic runs by TBuckets coherence improves macro-F1 in 3/4 tasks, illustrating that the measure is not only correlated with human judgments but also useful for guiding annotation effort.

Weaknesses
- Limited conceptual justification: The paper assumes that word–word semantic proximity (in embedding space) captures topic coherence, but provides little theoretical or empirical analysis of why this should align with human interpretability, especially given the mismatch between embedding context (local co-occurrence) and topic context (document-level co-occurrence).
- Methodological ad hocness and clarity: Several design choices (e.g., specific reorganization rules and thresholds derived from AWPS/AWES) feel ad hoc without ablations or sensitivity analyses. Section 3 is difficult to follow; the passive voice obscures decisions, and the long “trace” example could be shortened in favor of clearer, per-method subsections.
- Missing statistical tests and robustness checks: Table 1 reports correlations but no significance testing (e.g., Fisher r-to-z) to establish whether improvements over Röder et al. (2015) are statistically reliable. Robustness to the choice of embeddings (e.g., word2vec, fastText; domain-specific vectors for Genomics) is not explored.
- Evaluation scope and metrics: The evaluation uses Pearson r only; Spearman or Kendall correlations could test rank robustness. Results are limited to top-10 words per topic and three datasets; it would help to know how performance scales with different n, alternative topic models, or noisier topics.
- Incomplete analysis of the clustering variant: The agglomerative clustering method depends on a distance threshold and linkage type; while some settings are shown, a more principled way to set these parameters or a discussion of their sensitivity would strengthen this component.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive are results to the choice of embeddings? Have you tested word2vec, fastText, or domain-specific embeddings (especially for Genomics), and does performance change materially?
- Are the improvements in Table 1 statistically significant compared to Röder et al. (2015)? Please report significance tests (e.g., Fisher’s z) and confidence intervals.
- Can you provide ablations of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (e.g., into-only vs from-only moves; alternative threshold formulations) to clarify which parts drive the gains?
- How does performance vary with the number of top words per topic (e.g., top 5, 15, 20)? Do bucket properties remain informative as n changes?
- What is the computational cost of each variant (per topic), and how does it scale with topic size and embedding dimension?
- For the clustering variant, can you motivate or automate the choice of Max Dist and linkage? Would a density-based method (e.g., HDBSCAN) reduce parameter sensitivity?
- Did you evaluate rank-based correlations (Spearman/Kendall)? If not, can you report them to assess robustness?
- Is code and a reproducible pipeline available (including the exact pre-processing and GloVe vectors used)?
