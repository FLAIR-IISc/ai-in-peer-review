This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality by leveraging word embeddings to compute similarity—either directly or indirectly via matrix factorisation—and reports impressive results on standard datasets. The approach is a natural and important next step in the evolution of topic evaluation research.

My main concern is the inconsistency across datasets and variants. Although you report state-of-the-art results on all three datasets, the identities of the best-performing methods vary substantially, and several variants fall below the prior state of the art. None of the proposed methods consistently outperforms existing metrics, and the SVD-based variants perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. For practitioners seeking an off-the-shelf solution that works reliably across domains, this lack of robustness is worrisome.

I suspect the weak SVD results on the genomics dataset are linked to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms (see comment below). It also seems plausible that one could automatically predict which variant will work best from the corpus–embedding vocabulary overlap (e.g., with GloVe), yet the paper does not discuss such selection strategies.

Other issues:
- The proposed method has strong similarities to techniques in the lexical chaining literature. I encourage the authors to review and cite this work, and to situate the contribution relative to it in a future revision.
- You emphasise that your method has no parameters, but the word embedding models introduce many hyperparameters and design choices that implicitly affect your results. This is not a major issue, but it is worth acknowledging explicitly.
- How does your method handle OOV terms, especially in the genomics dataset (i.e., words not present in the pretrained GloVe embeddings)? Are such terms ignored? If so, what is the impact on the metric’s stability and accuracy?

Low-level issues:
- In Section 2.1, the description of word embeddings seems to assume that vector length is unimportant when using cosine similarity. That assumption holds for unit-normalised vectors, but word2vec does not produce unit-length vectors by default; popular pretrained vectors are normalised post hoc, and vectors from a fresh word2vec run are not uniform in length. Please clarify whether and how you normalise vectors before computing cosine similarity. It is a small detail, but important for reproducibility.
- The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable; please enlarge or reformat them for clarity.
