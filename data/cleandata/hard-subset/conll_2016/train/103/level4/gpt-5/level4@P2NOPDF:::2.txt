This paper introduces a method for evaluating topic models by clustering the top n words of each topic into “buckets” based on cosine similarity computed over their word embeddings. In the simplest variant, words are processed sequentially and either assigned to an existing bucket if they meet a predefined similarity threshold with its members, or placed in a new bucket. Two more advanced variants—one leveraging eigenvectors and another that reorganizes assignments—are also proposed. The approach is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification scenario, and it matches or outperforms the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

The core idea is straightforward and carries an ad hoc flavor. The paper offers little justification for why topic quality should be captured by pairwise word similarity. This is nontrivial, given that topics are defined over document-level co-occurrence while embeddings reflect sequential (or local) context. That said, the proposed method appears to work well in practice. I would, however, like to see statistical significance tests for the results in Table 1.

Overall, the paper is clearly written, though there are some language issues. I found Section 3 difficult to follow, largely because the use of passive voice obscures where the authors are making design choices (e.g., “the threshold is computed as”). I recommend rewriting the section with one subsection per method, clearly articulating the decisions involved in each variant. There seems to be room for this restructuring: the paper does not use the full eight-page content limit, and the relatively uninformative “trace” of the method on page 3 could be shortened to make space for clearer exposition.

One open question is the sensitivity of the evaluation to the choice of word embeddings. How would the results change if the authors used word2vec instead of GloVe (or other embedding models)? An analysis of robustness across embedding types would strengthen the claims.
