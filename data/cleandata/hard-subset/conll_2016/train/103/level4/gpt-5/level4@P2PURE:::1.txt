This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality that uses word embeddings to compute similarity—either directly or via matrix factorization—and reports impressive results on standard datasets. The approach is a natural and important next step in the evolution of research on topic evaluation.

My main concern is the inconsistency of performance across variants and datasets. Although you achieve state-of-the-art results on all three datasets, different variants alternately outperform and underperform the prior state of the art; in practice, no single proposed method consistently surpasses existing baselines across datasets. The SVD-based variants, in particular, perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. For practitioners seeking an off-the-shelf solution for arbitrary corpora, this variability is worrisome. I suspect the weak SVD results on genomics are related to the proportion of OOV terms (see below). It may be possible to predict automatically which variant will work best based on vocabulary overlap with GloVe (or similar), but the paper does not discuss this.

Other issues:
- The method has strong parallels with work on lexical chaining; I encourage the authors to review and cite this literature in a future revision.
- While you emphasize that the method has no parameters, the underlying word embedding models do have many parameters, which are implicit in your approach. Not a major issue, but it merits acknowledgment.
- How are OOV terms handled (e.g., in the genomics dataset when terms are missing from pretrained GloVe)? Are such terms ignored, and what is the impact on the metric?

Low-level issues:
- In Section 2.1, the description of word embeddings appears to assume vector length is unimportant when using cosine similarity. This is fine for unit-normalized vectors, but word2vec does not inherently produce unit-length vectors (pretrained vectors are often normalized post hoc, and self-trained word2vec typically yields non-uniform norms). A small detail, but important.
- The plots in Figure 1 are too small to be readable.
