This paper proposes a novel method for evaluating topic models that partitions the top *n* words of each topic into clusters, or "buckets," based on the cosine similarity of their corresponding word embeddings. In the simplest approach, words are considered one by one and either added to an existing bucket – if their cosine similarity to the words already in the bucket exceeds a defined threshold – or placed in a new bucket. Two more sophisticated methods leveraging singular value decomposition (SVD) and reorganization are also presented. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets and within a weakly supervised text classification setting, demonstrating performance on par with or exceeding the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

The core idea behind this paper is relatively straightforward and possesses a somewhat *ad hoc* character. The authors do not provide novel justification for why topic quality should be measurable in terms of word-to-word similarity. It’s unclear why this should hold, given that topics and word embeddings are defined with respect to distinct notions of context – documents versus sequential contexts, respectively. Nevertheless, the proposed method appears to perform effectively. (I would appreciate seeing significance tests reported for Table 1.)

Overall, the paper is clearly written, though some language refinements would be beneficial. I found the descriptions of the techniques in Section 3 somewhat difficult to follow, largely due to the authors’ frequent use of passive voice (“the threshold is computed as”) where they were actually describing design choices. I suggest the authors clarify each method with a dedicated subsection. There’s ample opportunity to do so; the authors did not fully utilize the eight-page limit, and they could condense the detailed walkthrough of the method on page 3.

I am curious about the sensitivity of the proposed technique to different word embeddings. For example, how would the scores change if the authors had employed word2vec instead of GloVe?
