This paper proposes a novel method for evaluating topic models by partitioning the top *n* words of each topic into clusters, termed "buckets," based on the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. The approach encompasses three techniques: a basic clustering method, one leveraging singular value decomposition (SVD), and a refined SVD-based method with reorganization. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets and within a weakly supervised text classification task, demonstrating performance competitive with or exceeding the state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015).

The core idea, while conceptually straightforward, possesses a somewhat *ad hoc* quality. The authors do not provide novel justification for measuring topic quality through word-word similarity. The differing contextual definitions underlying topics (document-level) and word embeddings (sequential context) raise questions about the validity of this approach. Nevertheless, the method appears to perform effectively – though I would appreciate the inclusion of significance tests for the results presented in Table 1.

Overall, the paper is well-written, despite minor language imperfections. The presentation of the techniques in Section 3 is somewhat challenging to follow; this is likely attributable to the frequent use of passive voice (“the threshold is computed as”) where the authors are articulating design decisions. Clarifying the description of each method with a dedicated subsection per approach would enhance readability. The paper could benefit from streamlining the illustrative "trace" on page 3, allowing for more detailed explanations.

A key question arises regarding the sensitivity of the proposed technique to the choice of word embeddings. Specifically, how would the results differ if word2vec were employed instead of GloVe?
