This paper introduces a novel approach to evaluating topic models by clustering the top *n* words of each topic based on the cosine similarity of their word embeddings. The core idea involves assigning each word to an existing “bucket” – a cluster of semantically similar words – if its cosine similarity to the bucket’s constituent words exceeds a defined threshold; otherwise, a new bucket is created. The authors also propose two more sophisticated variations employing eigenvector analysis and bucket reorganization. The method is evaluated on three benchmark datasets and within a weakly supervised text classification task, achieving performance comparable to, or exceeding, the state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015).

The underlying principle of the proposed method is straightforward, yet feels somewhat *ad hoc*. While the technique demonstrates effectiveness, the paper lacks a theoretical justification for measuring topic quality through word-word similarity. The connection isn't immediately clear, considering topics are typically defined by document-level context, while word embeddings capture sequential context. Despite this theoretical gap, the empirical results are promising. I recommend the inclusion of significance tests for the results presented in Table 1 to strengthen these findings.

Overall, the paper is well-written, though minor language imperfections are present. I found Section 3, detailing the technical aspects of the method, somewhat difficult to follow. This readability issue stems largely from the authors' frequent use of passive voice when describing design choices ("the threshold is computed as…") rather than actively stating them. To improve clarity, I suggest restructuring Section 3 with a dedicated subsection for each method. The authors have not fully utilized the available page limit (8 pages), and the “trace” of the method on page 3 – which provides limited informative value – could be condensed to accommodate this restructuring.

A crucial aspect for future work is exploring the sensitivity of the proposed technique to variations in word embeddings. Specifically, it would be valuable to investigate how the performance metrics change when using alternative embedding models, such as word2vec, instead of GloVe. This analysis would provide insights into the robustness and generalizability of the method.
