This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality based on using word embeddings to calculate similarity (either directly or indirectly via matrix factorization), achieving impressive results over standard datasets.
The proposed method represents a natural but important next step in the evolutionary path of research on topic evaluation. A key observation is that while the method achieves state-of-the-art results for all three datasets, performance varies considerably across different methods and datasets. Specifically, no single proposed method consistently outperforms the state of the art, and the SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. This inconsistency raises concerns for practitioners seeking a reliable, off-the-shelf solution applicable to arbitrary datasets. It is plausible that the lower results for SVD on genomics relate to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, and that predicting the best method based on vocabulary overlap with GloVe or similar resources might be possible. However, the paper does not discuss this possibility.
Other issues:
- The proposed method shares similarities with techniques found in the lexical chaining literature, and the authors are encouraged to explore and acknowledge this connection in future work.
- While the authors emphasize the parameter-free nature of their method, it relies on pre-trained word embeddings, which themselves have a large number of parameters. This implicit dependence on pre-trained models should be acknowledged.
- The paper does not detail how the method handles OOV terms, such as those encountered in the genomics dataset. Are these terms simply ignored? What impact does this have on performance?
Minor points:
- In Section 2.1, the description of word embeddings implicitly assumes the length of the vector is unimportant when using cosine similarity. While cosine similarity works well with unit-length vectors, standard word2vec implementations do not necessarily produce vectors with uniform length. This is a minor detail, but worth noting.
- The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be easily readable.
