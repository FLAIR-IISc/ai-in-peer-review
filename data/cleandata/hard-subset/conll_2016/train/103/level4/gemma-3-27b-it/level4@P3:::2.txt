This paper proposes a novel method for evaluating topic models, partitioning the top *n* words of each topic into clusters – termed "buckets" – based on the cosine similarity of their corresponding word embeddings. Three methods are presented: a basic clustering approach, one leveraging singular value decomposition (SVD), and a refined SVD-based method incorporating reorganization. The technique is evaluated on three standard datasets and within a weakly supervised text classification task, demonstrating performance competitive with or exceeding the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

The core contribution of this work lies in the TBuckets approach itself, providing a new and empirically successful metric for topic coherence. While the underlying intuition connecting word similarity and topic quality isn’t explicitly justified, the demonstrated correlation with human judgement and improved performance in a downstream task suggest practical value. A secondary, though notable, contribution is the parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques, offering a streamlined alternative to methods requiring extensive parameter tuning. Finally, the successful application of TBuckets to weakly supervised text classification showcases its utility beyond a purely evaluative role.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates that TBuckets outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two of the three datasets used in the evaluation (NYT and 20NG). This strong empirical showing provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The results presented in Table 1, while needing statistical significance testing (see questions to authors), suggest a genuine improvement over existing techniques.
2. **Parameter-Free Methods:** The SVD-based TBuckets techniques require no parameter tuning, a significant advantage over methods like Röder et al. (2015) that demand careful optimization. This ease of use increases the accessibility and practicality of the method for researchers and practitioners.
3. **Practical Application:** The demonstration of TBuckets’ usefulness in weakly supervised text classification adds to its value. This application highlights the potential for using TBuckets as a feature in broader NLP pipelines, rather than solely as a standalone evaluation metric.  The results in Table 3 show that selecting topics based on TBuckets coherence scores improves classification accuracy.
4. **Clear Presentation of the Core Idea:** The core concept of grouping topic words into buckets to assess coherence is intuitively understandable and well-explained, even if the deeper theoretical justification is lacking. The example illustrating the bucket-creation process for both coherent and incoherent topics (Section 3) is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper does not offer a strong theoretical rationale for why word similarity, as captured by word embeddings, should directly correlate with topic coherence. The connection between document-level context (topics) and sequential context (embeddings) is not adequately addressed. This weakens the argument for the method’s fundamental validity.
2. **Clarity of Method Descriptions:** The description of the techniques in Section 3 is convoluted and difficult to follow. The frequent use of passive voice obscures design choices and makes it challenging to understand the algorithmic steps.  A more structured presentation, with dedicated subsections for each method, would greatly improve readability. The example trace in Section 3 is verbose and doesn’t contribute significantly to understanding the core algorithm.
3. **Missing Statistical Significance:** The performance comparisons in Table 1 lack statistical significance tests. While the observed improvements are promising, the absence of significance testing prevents a definitive conclusion about the superiority of TBuckets. It's unclear whether the observed differences are statistically meaningful or due to chance.
4. **Limited Ablation Study:** The paper doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to understand the contribution of each component of the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique (size, WL, EVS). Understanding the individual impact of these components would provide valuable insights into the method's workings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Statistical Significance:** Could you please provide p-values or confidence intervals for the results reported in Table 1 to demonstrate the statistical significance of the improvements over the baseline methods?
2. **Embedding Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding models (e.g., word2vec) and, if so, how did the performance change?
3. **Ablation Study:** Can you provide an ablation study for TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, showing the performance contribution of each component (size, WL, EVS) individually and in combination?
4. **Justification of Reorganization:** The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg seems heuristic. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the reorganization criteria and the parameters used to control it?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity of the different TBuckets techniques.
*   Consider using more active voice throughout the paper, particularly in Section 3, to improve clarity and readability.
*   The term “buckets” is somewhat informal; consider whether a more technical term might be appropriate.
