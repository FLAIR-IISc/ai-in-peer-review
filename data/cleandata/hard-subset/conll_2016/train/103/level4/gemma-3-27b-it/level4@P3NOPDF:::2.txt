This paper introduces a novel method for evaluating topic models, partitioning the top *n* words of each topic into clusters – termed “buckets” – based on the cosine similarity of their corresponding word embeddings. The core approach considers words individually, assigning each to an existing bucket if its cosine similarity to the bucket’s constituent words exceeds a defined threshold, or creating a new bucket otherwise. The authors also propose two more sophisticated variations employing eigenvector analysis and bucket reorganization. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets, and within a weakly supervised text classification task, demonstrating performance competitive with, and often exceeding, the state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015).

The underlying principle of this work is straightforward, and possesses a degree of *ad hoc* quality. While effective, the paper lacks theoretical justification for measuring topic quality based solely on word-to-word similarity. The connection isn’t immediately apparent, given the fundamentally different contextual definitions inherent in topic models (document-level context) versus word embeddings (sequential context). Despite this conceptual gap, the method demonstrably performs well. However, the results presented in Table 1 would be strengthened by the inclusion of statistical significance tests.

Overall, the paper is clearly written, though exhibits some minor language imperfections. Section 3, detailing the technical methodologies, proved somewhat difficult to follow. This is largely attributable to the frequent use of passive voice ("the threshold is computed as") where active voice would more clearly convey the authors’ design choices. A restructuring of Section 3, dedicating a distinct subsection to each method, would significantly improve clarity. The authors have not fully utilized the allotted eight pages, and could readily accommodate this reorganization by condensing the largely descriptive “trace” of the method presented on page 3. 

A key question arises regarding the sensitivity of the proposed technique to the choice of word embeddings. Specifically, how would the results vary if word2vec were employed instead of GloVe? Exploring this sensitivity would provide valuable insight into the robustness and generalizability of the approach.
