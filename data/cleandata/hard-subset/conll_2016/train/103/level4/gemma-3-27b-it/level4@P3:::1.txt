This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality based on using word embeddings to calculate similarity (either directly or indirectly via matrix factorization), achieving impressive results over standard datasets. The proposed method represents a natural but important next step in the evolutionary path of research on topic evaluation. While the results demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on several datasets, inconsistencies exist across them regarding which method performs best. Specifically, no single proposed method consistently outperforms existing approaches, and the SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. This lack of robustness raises concerns for practical application, as a user would ideally want a method that reliably performs well across diverse datasets. The paper does not discuss potential reasons for these discrepancies, such as vocabulary overlap with the pre-trained word embeddings, nor does it explore ways to predict optimal method selection based on dataset characteristics.

**Main Contributions:** I identify two key contributions of this work. First, the *TBuckets* framework offers a novel perspective on topic coherence by framing it as the formation of thematic groups within topic word sets. This is a conceptually sound approach, leveraging the semantic relationships captured by word embeddings. Second, the empirical evaluation, despite the inconsistencies noted above, demonstrates that the *TBuckets-SVD-Reorg* technique achieves state-of-the-art performance on two of the three tested datasets (NYT and 20NG), and does so without requiring parameter tuning, which is a significant advantage. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the TBuckets Framework:** The core idea of grouping topic words into buckets based on semantic similarity, assessed through word embeddings, is a fresh and intuitive approach to quantifying topic coherence. It moves beyond purely statistical measures and attempts to capture the human interpretability aspect of topic quality, aligning with the goal articulated in the introduction and cited works (Chang et al., 2009).

2. **State-of-the-Art Performance:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on the NYT and 20NG datasets is a strong indicator of the method’s effectiveness in certain scenarios.  This suggests that the TBuckets approach is capturing meaningful signals related to topic coherence, at least for those datasets.

3. **Parameter-Free SVD-based Methods:** The fact that the SVD-based techniques require no parameter tuning is a significant practical benefit.  Many existing topic coherence measures involve complex parameter optimization, making them less accessible and more prone to overfitting.  The simplicity of TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is appealing.

4. **Demonstrated Utility in Downstream Task:**  The application of TBuckets to weakly supervised text classification and the resulting performance improvements (as demonstrated in Table 3) bolster the paper's value, showcasing a practical application beyond simply measuring coherence. This demonstrates the coherence score’s ability to guide topic selection for improved classification. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Inconsistent Performance Across Datasets:** The fluctuating performance of different TBuckets techniques across the three datasets is a major concern.  The significant underperformance of SVD-based methods on the genomics dataset, in particular, raises questions about the method's generalizability and robustness. The paper lacks a discussion of these discrepancies and potential underlying reasons.

2. **Lack of Comparison to Related Work on Lexical Chaining:** The authors do not engage with relevant work in lexical chaining, a field with strong similarities to their approach. Recognizing and differentiating TBuckets from existing lexical chaining techniques would strengthen the novelty claim and position the work more effectively within the broader research landscape.

3. **Implicit Parameter Dependence:** While the authors emphasize the parameter-free nature of their methods, they overlook the substantial number of parameters inherent in the pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe). These parameters significantly influence the performance of TBuckets, and this dependency should be acknowledged.

4. **Handling of Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Terms:** The paper does not address how OOV terms (words not present in the pre-trained GloVe embeddings) are handled. Ignoring such terms, especially in specialized datasets like genomics, could significantly impact the accuracy of coherence scores and limit the method's applicability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What potential factors contribute to the inconsistent performance of the different TBuckets methods across datasets? Specifically, could vocabulary overlap with the pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., GloVe) explain the poor performance of the SVD-based methods on the genomics dataset?
2. How are out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms handled when calculating word embeddings and coherence scores? Does the method simply ignore them, and if so, what is the expected impact on performance, particularly in specialized domains like genomics?
3. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the relationship between the TBuckets framework and existing work on lexical chaining? What are the key differences and advantages of your approach?
4. Is there any investigation of the potential to predict which TBuckets technique would perform best on a given dataset, based on its characteristics (e.g., vocabulary size, domain specificity)?
