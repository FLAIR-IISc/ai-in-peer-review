This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel method for evaluating topic models. The approach groups the top *n* words of each topic into “buckets” based on the cosine similarity of their corresponding word embeddings. Three techniques are proposed: a basic clustering approach, a method leveraging singular value decomposition (SVD), and a refinement of the SVD method incorporating reorganization. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets and demonstrates competitive performance in a weakly supervised text classification task, achieving results comparable to or exceeding the state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015).

The core idea – measuring topic quality through word similarity within buckets – is straightforward but somewhat *ad hoc*. While the paper doesn’t provide a strong theoretical justification for why word-word similarity should directly correlate with topic quality, given the differing contexts underlying word embeddings and topic models (sequential vs. document-level), the empirical results suggest effectiveness. The method demonstrably performs well, though statistical significance testing for the results in Table 1 would strengthen the findings.

Overall, the paper is well-written, despite some minor language imperfections. Section 3, detailing the techniques, could be improved in clarity. Specifically, a dedicated subsection for each method, replacing the current interwoven description, would enhance understanding. The authors frequently employ passive voice (“the threshold is computed as”) where a description of a design choice would be more effective. Streamlining the illustrative example on page 3, which feels overly detailed, could create space for these clearer explanations without exceeding the page limit.

A key area for further investigation is the sensitivity of the technique to different word embedding models. Exploring the performance of TBuckets with alternatives like word2vec, rather than solely relying on GloVe, would provide valuable insight into its robustness.

**Contributions:**

1.  **A novel approach to topic coherence evaluation:** TBuckets offers a new methodology for assessing topic quality by grouping topic words based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings.
2.  **Empirically validated performance:** The paper demonstrates through experiments on three public datasets that TBuckets achieves competitive, and in some cases superior, results compared to existing state-of-the-art methods.
3.  **Practical application in weakly supervised learning:** The study illustrates the usefulness of TBuckets by successfully applying it to improve performance in a weakly supervised text classification task.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, providing a clear explanation of the proposed methodology.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation demonstrates that TBuckets performs competitively against established methods for topic coherence assessment. The fact that the SVD-based techniques are parameter-free is a notable advantage.
3.  **Practical Relevance:** The application of TBuckets to weakly supervised text classification highlights its potential for real-world applications, demonstrating a tangible benefit beyond theoretical evaluation.
4.  **Comprehensive Comparison:** The comparison to Roder et al. (2015) provides a solid benchmark and context for the performance of TBuckets.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper lacks a strong theoretical argument explaining why word-word similarity, as measured through embeddings, should directly correlate with human judgments of topic coherence.
2.  **Clarity of Section 3:** The description of the three TBuckets techniques in Section 3 is somewhat convoluted and could be improved by dedicating a separate subsection to each method and employing more active voice.
3.  **Absence of Statistical Significance Testing:** The results presented in Table 1 would be more convincing with the inclusion of statistical significance tests to demonstrate the robustness of the observed performance differences.
4.  **Limited Exploration of Embedding Sensitivity:** The study relies exclusively on GloVe embeddings. Investigating the performance of TBuckets with other embedding models (e.g., word2vec) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide statistical significance tests for the results presented in Table 1?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of TBuckets to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with other embedding models besides GloVe?
3.  Could you elaborate on the design choices behind the specific formulas and parameters used in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique, particularly the dynamic threshold calculation?

**Additional Comments:**

The trace of the method execution with example topics, while illustrative, feels somewhat lengthy and could be condensed. Consider streamlining this section to focus on the key principles of the TBuckets approach.
