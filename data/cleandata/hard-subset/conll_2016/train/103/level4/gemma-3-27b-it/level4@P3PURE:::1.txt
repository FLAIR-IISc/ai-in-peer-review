This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality based on word embeddings, calculating similarity either directly or indirectly via matrix factorisation, and achieves impressive results on standard datasets. The proposed method represents a natural and important next step in the evolution of research on topic evaluation. However, a key concern regarding the results is the inconsistency in method performance across datasets. While state-of-the-art results are achieved for all three datasets, the relative performance of different methods varies significantly; no single proposed method consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art approaches. Notably, the SVD-based methods perform comparatively poorly on the genomics dataset. This lack of robustness is a considerable practical limitation for anyone seeking to apply this method to an arbitrary new dataset. I suspect this lower performance with SVD on genomics may be related to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, and it might be possible to predict the best-performing method based on vocabulary overlap with GloVe or similar embeddings. However, the paper does not discuss this possibility.

Other issues:

- The proposed method shares strong similarities with techniques from the lexical chaining literature, which I recommend the authors investigate and acknowledge in future revisions.
- The paper emphasizes that the method has no parameters, but the underlying word embedding methods do possess a large number of parameters that are inherent to its operation. While not a critical issue, this should be acknowledged.
- The paper does not address how the method handles OOV terms, such as those encountered in the genomics dataset (i.e., terms not present in the pre-trained GloVe embeddings). Are these terms simply ignored? What impact does this have on the method's performance?

Low-level issues:

- In Section 2.1, the description of word embeddings implicitly assumes that vector length is unimportant when stating that cosine similarity can be used to measure similarity. This is valid if vectors are unit length, but word2vec does not natively return unit-length vectors (pre-trained vectors are normalized post hoc, and vectors generated by running word2vec independently will not be uniformly scaled). This is a minor detail, but important for clarity.
- The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be easily readable.
