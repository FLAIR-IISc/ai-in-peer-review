This paper introduces a novel method for evaluating topic models by partitioning the top *n* words of each topic into clusters – termed “buckets” – based on the cosine similarity of their corresponding word embeddings. The core approach considers words individually, assigning each to an existing bucket if its cosine similarity to the bucket’s words exceeds a defined threshold, otherwise creating a new bucket. Two more sophisticated variations, employing eigenvectors and topic reorganisation, are also presented. The method is evaluated on three benchmark datasets and within a weakly supervised text classification task, achieving performance comparable to or exceeding the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

The underlying principle of this work is straightforward but feels somewhat *ad hoc*. The authors do not provide justification for why topic quality should be assessed through word-word similarity; the connection is not self-evident, considering topics are defined by document context while word embeddings capture sequential context. Despite this, the method demonstrates surprisingly effective performance. (Significance testing for the results in Table 1 would be beneficial.)

Overall, the paper is well-written, although some linguistic refinements are needed. I found the description of the techniques in Section 3 difficult to follow, largely due to the frequent use of passive voice ("the threshold is computed as") when describing design decisions. The authors should clarify the different methods, potentially dedicating a separate subsection to each. This expansion is feasible; the paper does not fully utilise the allocated eight pages, and the “trace” of the method on page 3 is rather uninformative and could be condensed.

A further investigation I would recommend is an analysis of the technique's sensitivity to different word embeddings. Specifically, it would be valuable to understand how the scores would change if alternative embeddings, such as word2vec, were used instead of GloVe.
