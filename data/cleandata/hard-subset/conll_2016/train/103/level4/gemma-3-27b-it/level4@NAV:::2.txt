This paper introduces a novel approach to topic model evaluation, grouping the top *n* words for each topic into clusters – termed “buckets” – based on the cosine similarity of their corresponding word embeddings. The core methodology begins with individual word assessment, assigning each to an existing bucket if its cosine similarity to the bucket's words remains below a defined threshold, otherwise establishing a new bucket.  The authors further propose two more refined techniques leveraging eigenvectors and topic reorganization. Evaluation across three benchmark datasets and a weakly supervised text classification task demonstrates performance comparable to, or exceeding, current state-of-the-art methods (Röder et al., 2015).

While the underlying principle is straightforward and possesses a degree of heuristic character, the paper successfully demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the method. A key limitation is the lack of theoretical justification linking topic quality to word-to-word similarity; the rationale for this connection isn't fully clear, considering the disparate contextual definitions underpinning topics (document-level) and word embeddings (sequential context).  Despite this, the empirical results are promising, and I recommend the inclusion of significance testing for the data presented in Table 1.

The paper is generally well-written, although some linguistic refinements would be beneficial. Section 3, detailing the techniques, proved somewhat challenging to follow. This difficulty stems largely from the frequent use of passive voice to describe design decisions (“the threshold is computed as…”).  I suggest restructuring this section with a dedicated subsection for each method to enhance clarity.  There’s ample opportunity for this expansion, as the current content doesn’t fully utilize the allocated eight pages, and the “trace” information on page 3 could be condensed. 

Finally, the sensitivity of the proposed technique to varying word embedding models warrants investigation. Specifically, it would be valuable to understand how performance is affected when utilizing alternatives like word2vec instead of GloVe.
