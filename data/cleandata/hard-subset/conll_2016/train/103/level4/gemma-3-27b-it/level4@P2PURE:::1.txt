This paper presents a method for topic quality evaluation leveraging word embeddings to compute similarity, either directly or via matrix factorisation, and reports strong performance on standard datasets. The proposed approach represents a logical and valuable progression in topic evaluation research. However, a key concern regarding the results is the inconsistency in method performance across the three datasets; while state-of-the-art results are achieved overall, the relative ranking of methods varies considerably. Critically, no single proposed method consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art techniques, and the SVD-based methods notably underperform on the genomics dataset. This lack of robustness is a significant practical limitation for users seeking a generalizable solution applicable to arbitrary datasets. I hypothesize that the lower SVD performance on genomics may be attributable to a higher proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, and suggest that it might be possible to predict optimal method selection based on vocabulary overlap with the pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe). However, this potential relationship is not discussed in the paper.

Other points to consider:
- The proposed method shares considerable overlap with techniques from the lexical chaining literature, and the authors are encouraged to review this work and incorporate relevant citations in future revisions.
- While the authors emphasize the parameter-free nature of their method, it relies on word embeddings which themselves possess a substantial number of parameters. This dependency, though not a major issue, warrants acknowledgement.
- The paper does not address the handling of OOV terms, particularly relevant in datasets such as genomics where terms may be absent from the pre-trained GloVe embeddings. Clarification is needed regarding whether OOV terms are ignored and, if so, the potential impact on performance. 

Minor issues:
- Section 2.1 describes word embeddings while implicitly assuming vector length is inconsequential when using cosine similarity. While valid for unit-length vectors, standard word2vec embeddings are not inherently unit length (pre-trained vectors are often normalised post hoc, and self-trained vectors are typically not uniform in length). This is a subtle but important detail.
- The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be easily readable.
