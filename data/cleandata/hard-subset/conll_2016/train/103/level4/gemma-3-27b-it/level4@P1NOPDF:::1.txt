This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality based on word embeddings – calculating similarity either directly or indirectly via matrix factorisation – and achieves impressive results on standard datasets. The proposed method represents a natural and important step forward in research on topic evaluation. However, a key concern regarding the results is that, while state-of-the-art performance is achieved on all three datasets, there are substantial inconsistencies in which methods perform well and which fall short. In practice, none of the proposed methods consistently outperforms the state of the art, and the SVD-based methods perform particularly poorly on the genomics dataset. This is a considerable drawback for anyone wishing to apply this method directly to an arbitrary dataset. I suspect that the lower results for SVD on genomics may relate to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, and that it might be possible to automatically predict the best-performing method based on vocabulary overlap with GloVe or similar embeddings. However, this possibility is not discussed in the paper.

Other issues include:

- The proposed method shares strong similarities with approaches in the lexical chaining literature, which the authors should explore and acknowledge in future revisions.
- The paper emphasizes that the method has no parameters, but the underlying word embedding methods contain a large number of parameters which are, in effect, inherent to the approach. While not a critical point, it is worth acknowledging this.
- The paper does not detail how the method handles OOV terms, such as those encountered in the genomics dataset (terms not present in the pre-trained GloVe embeddings). Are these terms simply ignored? What impact does this have on the method’s performance?

Minor points:

- The description of word embeddings in Section 2.1 implicitly assumes that vector length is unimportant when stating that cosine similarity can be used to measure similarity. This is valid if vectors are unit length, but word2vec does not inherently produce unit-length vectors (pre-trained vectors are typically normalised post hoc, and running word2vec independently results in non-uniform vector lengths). This is a small but important detail.
- The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be easily readable.
