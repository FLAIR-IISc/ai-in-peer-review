This paper presents a novel and promising method for evaluating topic quality, leveraging word embeddings to calculate similarity – both directly and via matrix factorization. The results achieved on standard datasets are impressive, and the approach represents a logical and valuable advancement in the field of topic evaluation.

However, while the paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on all three datasets, a significant concern arises from the inconsistency in method performance *across* those datasets. No single proposed method consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art approaches, and the SVD-based methods, in particular, exhibit notably weaker results on the genomics dataset. This variability poses a practical challenge for users seeking a robust, off-the-shelf solution applicable to arbitrary datasets. I hypothesize that the lower performance of SVD on the genomics data may be attributable to a higher proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. Exploring the possibility of automatically predicting the optimal method based on vocabulary overlap with the pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe) would be a valuable extension, but is currently absent from the discussion.

Several further points warrant consideration. 

First, the proposed method bears strong resemblance to techniques developed within the lexical chaining literature. I strongly encourage the authors to familiarize themselves with this work and to discuss its connection to their approach in a revised version of the paper.

Second, the authors emphasize the lack of explicit parameters in their method. However, the underlying word embedding models *do* possess a substantial number of parameters. While not a critical flaw, this implicit dependence should be acknowledged for clarity and completeness. 

Third, the paper does not explicitly address the handling of OOV terms, such as those encountered in the genomics dataset. Specifically, it is unclear whether OOV terms are simply ignored, and what impact this has on the overall performance of the method. A discussion of this issue, and potential mitigation strategies, would strengthen the paper.

Finally, a few minor points require attention. In Section 2.1, the description of word embeddings implicitly assumes that vector length is irrelevant when using cosine similarity. While this holds true for unit-length vectors, standard word2vec implementations do not consistently produce normalized vectors. Though a subtle point, it is important to note that pre-trained vectors are often normalized *post hoc*, and self-trained vectors will generally not be of uniform length. Additionally, the graphs presented in Figure 1 are too small to be easily readable and should be enlarged for improved clarity.
