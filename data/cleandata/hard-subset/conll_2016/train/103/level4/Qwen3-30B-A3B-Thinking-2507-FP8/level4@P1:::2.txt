

**Summary of Paper and Contributions**  
This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic quality in LDA-based topic models by grouping topic words into thematic "buckets" based on their semantic similarity. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are: (1) a parameter-free method for topic coherence evaluation using SVD-based bucketing (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that outperforms state-of-the-art techniques on two out of three datasets; (2) three distinct techniques for bucket formation (clustering-based, SVD-based, and SVD with reorganization), with the latter two requiring no parameter tuning; and (3) a practical demonstration of TBuckets' utility in weakly supervised text classification, where selecting high-coherence topics improves classification accuracy.

**Strengths**  
1. **Superior performance with parameter-free methods**: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves state-of-the-art results on two datasets (NYT and 20NG), outperforming the best methods reported in Röder et al. (2015). Crucially, these SVD-based techniques require no parameter tuning—unlike the state-of-the-art, which demands extensive hyperparameter optimization. This represents a significant practical advantage for real-world applications.  
2. **Demonstrated utility in text classification**: The paper compellingly shows that selecting high-coherence topics via TBuckets improves weakly supervised text classification accuracy. For example, in the PC vs. MAC dataset (one of the most challenging subsets of 20NG), the classification accuracy of the best 5 coherence-ranked topic sets (85.61%) significantly outperforms the worst 5 sets (82.85%), underscoring the practical value of their coherence measure.  
3. **Clear evaluation framework**: The experimental design is robust, using three standard datasets with human-annotated coherence scores and Pearson's r correlation as the evaluation metric. The comparison with prior work is thorough, and the inclusion of ablation studies (e.g., varying Max Dist in TBuckets-Clustering) strengthens the claims.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper assumes that topic coherence can be measured via word-word semantic similarity without addressing why this approach aligns with human interpretation of topics. Topics are defined through document context (LDA's generative process), while word embeddings capture sequential context. The disconnect between these two notions is not explained, making the core premise feel ad hoc.  
2. **Insufficient methodological clarity**: The description of TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (Sections 3.1–3.2) is overly technical and difficult to follow. Key design choices (e.g., the dynamic threshold computation) are buried in passive constructions ("the threshold is computed as..."), obscuring the authors' reasoning. A clearer presentation with one subsection per technique would improve accessibility.  
3. **Missing sensitivity analysis**: The paper does not evaluate how TBuckets' performance depends on the choice of word embeddings. For instance, would results change if word2vec were used instead of GloVe? This omission is notable given the growing diversity of embedding models.  
4. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The results in Table 1 (e.g., TBuckets-SVD-Reorg's 0.837 correlation on NYT) lack statistical significance tests, making it unclear whether the improvements over state-of-the-art are meaningful or due to chance.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does the performance of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg vary when using alternative word embedding models (e.g., word2vec or fastText) instead of GloVe?  
2. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation for why semantic similarity (via word embeddings) correlates with human-perceived topic coherence, particularly given the differing contexts underlying topics and embeddings?  
3. The dynamic threshold in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (Section 3.1) depends on AWPS and AWES. How does this threshold behave for topics with very low word-word similarity (e.g., highly fragmented topics)?
