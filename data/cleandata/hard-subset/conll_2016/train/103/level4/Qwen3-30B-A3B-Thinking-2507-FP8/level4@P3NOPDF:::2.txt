

This paper introduces a novel approach to evaluating topic models by clustering the top words of each topic using cosine similarity of word embeddings. The core method sequentially assigns words to existing "buckets" (clusters) if their similarity to the bucket's centroid falls below a threshold, otherwise creating a new bucket. The authors also propose two extensions leveraging eigenvectors and reorganization. Evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification task, the method achieves results comparable to or exceeding the state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015).

While the empirical results are promising, the paper’s foundational rationale requires strengthening. The approach assumes that topic quality can be meaningfully assessed through word–word similarity, yet it fails to justify why this metric aligns with topic coherence—especially given that topics and word embeddings derive from fundamentally distinct contexts (document-level vs. sequential). Without theoretical grounding, the method risks appearing ad hoc. The authors should explicitly address this gap, perhaps by linking word embedding semantics to topic interpretability or citing relevant prior work on embedding-topic relationships.

The paper is generally well-structured but suffers from minor language issues and unclear exposition in Section 3. The description of the three methods (basic, eigenvector-based, reorganization) is difficult to follow, partly due to excessive passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as..."). This obscures the authors' design choices. A clearer presentation would involve dedicating one subsection per method, explicitly stating the rationale and parameters for each. Additionally, the authors could utilize the space left by their 8-page limit to expand these explanations rather than including the currently redundant "trace" of the method on page 3.

A significant oversight is the lack of statistical validation. The results in Table 1 (and likely elsewhere) should include significance tests to confirm that observed improvements over baselines are non-random. The authors should report p-values or confidence intervals for key comparisons.

The paper also leaves an important question unanswered: How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? For instance, would using word2vec instead of GloVe yield substantially different scores? Given that embedding quality directly impacts the similarity metric, this robustness check is essential for establishing the method’s generalizability. The authors should include a brief ablation study comparing embedding models.

Despite these concerns, the method itself is conceptually straightforward and empirically effective. The results demonstrate clear utility for topic model evaluation, particularly in the weakly supervised classification task where the approach outperforms prior work. The technical contribution is modest but practical, offering a readily implementable alternative to existing metrics.

In summary, the paper presents a useful empirical contribution with strong results but requires substantial revision to address theoretical justification, clarity, and robustness. Specifically:  
1. **Theoretical justification**: Explain *why* word similarity correlates with topic quality.  
2. **Clarity**: Rewrite Section 3 using active voice and dedicated subsections per method.  
3. **Validation**: Add significance tests for Table 1.  
4. **Robustness**: Test sensitivity to embedding choice (e.g., GloVe vs. word2vec).  

With these revisions, the paper would make a solid contribution. As it stands, it is acceptable for publication but requires significant polishing to meet the standards of CONLL. The current version risks being dismissed as a technical recipe without deeper insight. The authors should prioritize addressing the theoretical and methodological gaps over minor presentation tweaks.
