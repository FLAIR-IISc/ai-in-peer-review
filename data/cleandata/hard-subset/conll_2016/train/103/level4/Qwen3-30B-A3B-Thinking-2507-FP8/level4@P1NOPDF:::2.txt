

This paper proposes a novel evaluation method for topic models that clusters the top n words per topic based on cosine similarity of their word embeddings. The core approach sequentially assigns each word to an existing "bucket" (if its similarity to all words in the bucket falls below a threshold) or creates a new bucket. Two additional variants—using eigenvectors and iterative reorganization—are also introduced. The method is tested on three standard datasets and a weakly supervised text classification task, outperforming or matching the state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015).  

While the core idea is straightforward, it lacks theoretical grounding. The authors provide no justification for why topic quality should correlate with word–word similarity, despite topics and word embeddings being defined over fundamentally different contexts (document-level vs. sequential). This ad hoc approach feels disconnected from the underlying semantics, making its validity questionable. The empirical success is notable, but Table 1 would benefit from significance tests to strengthen the claims.  

The paper is generally well-written, though minor language issues persist. Section 3’s technical descriptions are occasionally difficult to follow, primarily due to overuse of passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as" instead of explicitly stating the authors’ design choice). Clearer exposition would help—each method deserves its own subsection to improve readability. The authors also leave room for improvement: the 8-page limit isn’t fully utilized, and the "trace" of the method on page 3 is overly verbose and uninformative. Streamlining this section would free space for deeper methodological explanations.  

A key unresolved question concerns the method’s sensitivity to word embedding choices. For instance, how would results change if word2vec replaced GloVe? The paper uses GloVe exclusively, omitting analysis of this critical hyperparameter. Given that embedding quality directly impacts similarity metrics, this omission weakens the evaluation’s robustness.  

Overall, the method is empirically promising but lacks conceptual justification. The authors should clarify their design choices, address embedding sensitivity, and strengthen statistical validation. With these revisions, the work could make a meaningful contribution to topic model evaluation.
