

This paper introduces a topic quality evaluation method leveraging word embeddings to compute similarity (either directly or through matrix factorization), achieving strong performance on standard benchmarks. The approach represents a natural and significant advancement in topic evaluation research. However, a major concern is the substantial inconsistency in method performance across datasets despite state-of-the-art results on all three. Specifically, no single proposed method consistently outperforms the state of the art, and SVD-based approaches exhibit particularly poor performance on the genomics dataset. This variability poses a significant concern for practitioners seeking to deploy the method on arbitrary datasets. I suspect the SVD underperformance on genomics relates to high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) term rates (see below), and the authors might automatically select optimal methods based on vocabulary overlap with embeddings like GloVe. However, this potential explanation and method selection strategy are not addressed in the paper.  

Additional concerns:  
- The method bears strong resemblance to lexical chaining approaches; the authors should incorporate this literature in any revision.  
- While the paper emphasizes the absence of explicit parameters, the underlying word embedding techniques inherently involve numerous parameters. This should be acknowledged, though it constitutes a minor point.  
- The handling of OOV terms (e.g., in the genomics dataset, where terms lack GloVe embeddings) is not discussed. Are such terms ignored? What impact does this have on evaluation?  

Low-level issues:  
- Section 2.1 implicitly assumes vector length is irrelevant for cosine similarity. However, word2vec does not produce unit-length vectors (pre-trained vectors are normalized post hoc; self-trained vectors lack uniform length). This requires clarification.  
- Figure 1 is insufficiently sized for readability.
