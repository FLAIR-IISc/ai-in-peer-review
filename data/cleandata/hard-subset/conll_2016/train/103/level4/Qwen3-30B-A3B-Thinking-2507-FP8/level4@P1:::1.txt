

**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic quality in LDA-based topic models by grouping topic words into thematic buckets based on semantic similarity. The authors propose three techniques for creating these buckets: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (with reorganization of buckets). The main contribution is the demonstration that these techniques, particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, outperform state-of-the-art methods on two of three datasets (NYT and 20NG), while being completely parameter-free.

**Strengths**

1. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves the best results on two out of three datasets (NYT and 20NG), outperforming the state-of-the-art. This is particularly impressive given that the method requires no parameter tuning, unlike previous approaches that require significant parameter optimization.

2. The paper provides a clear, intuitive justification for the approach based on how humans assess topic coherence, with compelling examples demonstrating how the bucketing process reflects human judgment of topic coherence.

3. The application to weakly supervised text classification demonstrates practical utility, showing that selecting high-quality topics using TBuckets improves classification accuracy compared to selecting topics without quality assessment.

**Weaknesses**

1. The results show inconsistent performance across datasets: while TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art on NYT and 20NG, it performs worse than the best methods on the Genomics dataset. This inconsistency is problematic for practitioners who might want to apply the method to arbitrary datasets without knowing which technique will work best.

2. The authors do not address the issue of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, which is particularly relevant for the Genomics dataset. The poor performance on Genomics might be related to the high proportion of OOV terms (not present in GloVe embeddings), but the paper makes no mention of this or how OOV terms are handled (e.g., ignored or treated differently).

3. The claim that the method is "completely parameter-free" is misleading. While the TBuckets techniques themselves have no explicit parameters, they rely on word embeddings that have implicit parameters (e.g., the training of GloVe embeddings). The authors should acknowledge this limitation.

4. The paper fails to address the relationship between vocabulary coverage and method performance. It would be valuable to discuss whether the method's performance can be predicted based on vocabulary overlap with the embedding space (e.g., GloVe's vocabulary), which might explain the inconsistent results across datasets.

**Questions to Authors**

1. The Genomics dataset appears to have a high proportion of domain-specific terms that may not be present in GloVe embeddings. How does the method handle OOV terms? Are they simply ignored, and if so, what is the impact on the coherence scores?

2. Given the inconsistent performance across datasets, is there a way to automatically predict which TBuckets technique will perform best for a given dataset based on characteristics like vocabulary coverage with the embedding space?

3. The paper mentions similarities with lexical chaining approaches but doesn't discuss them. Could the authors elaborate on how their approach relates to or differs from existing lexical chaining methods?

4. The word embedding section (Section 2.1) states that cosine similarity can be used without considering vector length, but this is only true for unit-length vectors. The paper should clarify whether the GloVe embeddings used are unit-normalized or whether the authors adjusted for vector length.
