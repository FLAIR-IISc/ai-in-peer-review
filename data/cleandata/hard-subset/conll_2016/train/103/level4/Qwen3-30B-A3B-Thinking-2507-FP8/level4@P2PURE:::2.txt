

This paper introduces a novel approach for evaluating topic models by clustering the top n words per topic into "buckets" based on cosine similarity of their word embeddings. The core method processes words sequentially: each word is assigned to an existing bucket if its similarity to other words in the bucket falls below a threshold, or a new bucket is created. Two additional variants—using eigenvectors and reorganization—are also proposed. The method is evaluated across three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification task, achieving results that outperform or match the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

While the core idea is straightforward, it possesses an ad hoc quality. The authors provide no theoretical justification for measuring topic quality via word-word similarity, particularly given that topics and word embeddings derive from fundamentally distinct contexts (document-level vs. sequential). It remains unclear why this approach should be valid. Despite this, the method demonstrates strong empirical performance (I request significance tests for Table 1).  

The paper is clearly written overall, though minor language issues persist. Section 3's technical descriptions are occasionally difficult to follow, largely due to excessive passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as" instead of explicit design choices). The authors should clarify each method more thoroughly, with dedicated subsections per approach. Space for this exists: the 8-page limit is not fully utilized, and the largely uninformative "trace" description on page 3 could be condensed.  

A key unresolved question is the method's sensitivity to word embedding choices. For instance, how would results differ using word2vec instead of GloVe?
