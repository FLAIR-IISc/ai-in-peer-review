

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic quality in LDA-based topic models by grouping topic words into thematic buckets based on semantic similarity. The authors propose three techniques: (1) TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering of words), (2) TBuckets-SVD (using SVD to identify orthogonal sub-themes), and (3) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (reorganizing buckets to improve coherence). The primary contributions are: (1) a simple yet effective framework for topic coherence measurement grounded in human interpretability, (2) parameter-free SVD-based techniques that outperform state-of-the-art methods, and (3) empirical validation of the approach in weakly supervised text classification. The work is notable for its practical utility and clear demonstration of how topic quality directly impacts downstream applications.

**Strengths**  
1. **Strong empirical results with practical advantages**: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015) on two of three datasets (NYT, 20NG) while requiring no parameter tuning. This is a significant practical advantage over methods that demand extensive hyperparameter optimization. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques is particularly compelling for real-world applications where tuning is resource-intensive.  

2. **Demonstrated utility in downstream tasks**: The paper convincingly shows that using TBuckets to filter high-quality topics significantly improves weakly supervised text classification (e.g., 68.47% vs. 66.98% macroF1 in PC vs. MAC). The performance gap (e.g., 96.19% vs. 91.09% in POLITICS vs. SCIENCE) underscores the real-world impact of topic quality assessment—a contribution that transcends mere evaluation metrics.  

3. **Conceptual clarity and human-centered motivation**: The approach is intuitively grounded in how humans assess topic coherence (e.g., "single large bucket = coherent topic"). This aligns with the literature’s emphasis on human interpretability over purely statistical measures (Chang et al., 2009), making the method both accessible and theoretically justified.  

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of theoretical justification for word embeddings**: The paper assumes that semantic similarity (via word embeddings) correlates with topic coherence but fails to explain why this connection holds. Word embeddings capture sequential context, while topics are defined by document-level co-occurrence—these are fundamentally different contexts. Without addressing this disconnect, the method’s validity remains empirically supported but conceptually tenuous.  

2. **Insufficient robustness analysis**: The evaluation relies exclusively on GloVe embeddings without testing alternative models (e.g., word2vec), despite claims of semantic similarity being central to the approach. A sensitivity analysis across embedding models (e.g., Table 1 with GloVe vs. word2vec) would strengthen the argument. The draft review rightly questions this omission.  

3. **Ambiguous technical description**: The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique (Section 3.1) is overly complex and obscured by passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as..."). The explanation lacks clear design rationale, making it difficult to replicate or extend. A revised presentation with explicit subsections for each technique would improve clarity.  

4. **Limited evaluation scope**: Results are confined to three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics), all drawn from Röder et al. (2015). The method’s performance on non-English corpora, domain-specific topics (e.g., medical or legal texts), or non-LDA topic models remains unexplored. Broader validation would better establish generalizability.  

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How sensitive are your results to the choice of word embedding model (e.g., GloVe vs. word2vec)? Could you provide a comparison on a subset of your datasets?  
2. Are the performance differences in Table 1 statistically significant? If so, could you report p-values or confidence intervals?  
3. Could you clarify why the "dynamic threshold" (Section 3.1) is necessary for reorganization, rather than using a fixed threshold?  

**Additional Comments**  
The paper is well-structured and clearly written, though minor grammatical issues (e.g., "a single large bucket signifies a single coherent theme" in the abstract) could be refined. The authors could streamline Section 3 by replacing the current "trace" description with a concise algorithmic summary for each technique. The 8-page limit is underutilized; the current content could be tightened without sacrificing clarity.  

**Overall Recommendation**  
This paper presents a practical, empirically validated method for topic quality assessment with clear downstream benefits. While the theoretical foundation requires strengthening, the strong results, parameter-free techniques, and demonstrated utility in text classification make it a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance pending responses to the questions above.
