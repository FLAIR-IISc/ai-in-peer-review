

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel method for measuring topic quality in LDA-based topic models. The authors propose grouping topic words into thematic buckets (TBuckets) where a single large bucket indicates high topic coherence. They explore three techniques for bucket formation: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify orthogonal sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step to improve bucket assignments). The main contributions, in order of significance, are:

1. A novel topic coherence measurement approach based on grouping topic words into thematic buckets, which effectively correlates with human judgments of topic quality.
2. Parameter-free techniques (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that outperform state-of-the-art methods on two of three benchmark datasets, addressing a key limitation of existing approaches that require extensive parameter tuning.
3. Demonstration of practical utility for weakly supervised text classification, showing improved classification performance when using TBuckets to select high-quality topics.

**Strengths**

1. The TBuckets approach is conceptually sound and directly addresses a critical problem in topic modeling: measuring topic quality. The intuitive connection between bucket formation and human interpretation of topic coherence is well-motivated and effectively demonstrated through examples. The method provides a clear, interpretable way to assess topic quality that aligns with human judgment.

2. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques is a significant advantage over existing methods. As the authors correctly point out, state-of-the-art techniques typically require careful tuning of multiple parameters, which is impractical for many users. TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning, making them immediately deployable for practitioners.

3. The empirical evaluation demonstrates strong performance, particularly on the NYT and 20NG datasets where TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art. The application to text classification is particularly compelling, showing that using TBuckets to select high-quality topics leads to significant improvements in classification performance (e.g., 85.61% vs 85.23% on POLITICS vs RELIGION) compared to using all topics indiscriminately.

**Weaknesses**

1. The performance of the methods is inconsistent across datasets, with SVD-based techniques performing notably worse on the Genomics dataset (0.68 vs 0.86 for NYT and 0.86 for 20NG). This inconsistency is concerning for practitioners who would need to deploy the method across different domains. The paper does not address why this discrepancy occurs or provide guidance on selecting the appropriate technique for a given dataset. The authors should investigate whether this relates to out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms in the Genomics dataset (which may not be well-covered by GloVe embeddings), and whether the method's performance could be improved through vocabulary matching.

2. The claim that the method is parameter-free is misleading. While the authors avoid explicit parameters for the bucketing process, the method relies on pre-trained word embeddings with implicit parameters. The paper should acknowledge this limitation and clarify the parameter dependencies of the overall approach.

3. The paper does not adequately address the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) problem, which could significantly impact performance on domain-specific datasets like Genomics. The authors should specify how OOV terms are handled (e.g., ignored, replaced with similar words, or excluded from analysis) and provide evidence of the impact on performance. This is particularly important given the Genomics dataset's performance issues.

**Questions to Authors**

1. The Genomics dataset shows significantly lower performance for all methods compared to the other datasets. Could the authors investigate whether this is related to the proportion of OOV terms in the Genomics vocabulary (compared to GloVe's training data) and whether this could be mitigated by using domain-specific embeddings?

2. The paper claims the SVD-based methods are parameter-free, but the word embeddings themselves have implicit parameters. How do the authors reconcile this with the claim of being parameter-free, and what are the implications for reproducibility and generalizability?

3. The authors should clarify how OOV terms are handled in the Genomics dataset. Are they simply ignored? If so, what is the impact on the coherence scores, and how might this affect the method's performance in domain-specific applications?

**Additional Comments**

The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work in lexical chaining and topic coherence measurement, which shares similarities with the proposed approach. The authors should acknowledge these connections to better position their contribution.

The description of word embeddings in Section 2.1 should clarify that cosine similarity assumes unit-length vectors, which is not always the case with standard word embedding models. The paper should specify whether the authors normalized the vectors before computing similarities.

The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable; the authors should provide larger, clearer visualizations.
