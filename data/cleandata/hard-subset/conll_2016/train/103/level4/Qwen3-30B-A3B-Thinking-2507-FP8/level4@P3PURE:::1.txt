

This paper proposes a novel method for evaluating topic quality using word embeddings to compute similarity (via direct comparison or matrix factorization), achieving state-of-the-art results on standard datasets. The approach represents a natural and significant advancement in topic evaluation research. However, a major concern arises from inconsistent performance across datasets: while the method achieves SOTA results overall, it fails to consistently outperform existing approaches. Specifically, SVD-based methods exhibit notably poor performance on the genomics dataset. This inconsistency poses a significant concern for practitioners seeking to deploy the method across arbitrary datasets without further customization. I suspect the SVD underperformance on genomics stems from high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) term rates (see below), and the paper should discuss whether method selection could be automated based on vocabulary overlap with pre-trained embeddings like GloVe—this analysis is notably absent.

Additional concerns:
- The method shares substantial conceptual overlap with lexical chaining approaches; the authors should engage with this literature in any revision.
- The claim that the method "has no parameters" overlooks the implicit parameters inherent in word embedding models (e.g., GloVe), which require acknowledgment.
- The handling of OOV terms (e.g., in genomics) is unclear: are they ignored, and what is their impact on evaluation accuracy? This critical detail is unaddressed.

Minor issues:
- Section 2.1 states that cosine similarity applies generally without noting that it assumes unit-length vectors, which is not guaranteed by standard word2vec (pre-trained vectors are normalized, but self-trained vectors are not). This oversight affects the technical foundation.
- Figure 1’s graphs are insufficiently sized for clear interpretation.
