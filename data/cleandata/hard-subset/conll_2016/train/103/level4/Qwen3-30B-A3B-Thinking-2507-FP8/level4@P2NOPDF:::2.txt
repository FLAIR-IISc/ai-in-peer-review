

This paper introduces a novel method for evaluating topic models by clustering top words per topic using cosine similarity of word embeddings. The approach partitions words into "buckets" based on a similarity threshold: each word is assigned to an existing bucket if its similarity to the bucket's words falls below the threshold, or a new bucket is created otherwise. Two extensions—eigenvector-based and reorganization methods—are also proposed. Evaluated on three standard datasets and a weakly supervised text classification task, the method matches or exceeds prior state-of-the-art results (Röder et al., 2015).

While the core idea is conceptually simple, its theoretical foundation is underdeveloped. The authors provide no justification for why topic quality should correlate with word–word similarity, particularly given that topics (defined via document context) and word embeddings (derived from sequential context) operate on fundamentally distinct semantic frameworks. Despite this gap, the method demonstrates strong empirical performance. I recommend including significance tests for Table 1 to validate the statistical robustness of the results.

The paper is generally well-written, though minor language issues occasionally impede clarity. Section 3's technical descriptions are challenging to follow, partly due to excessive passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed"), which obscures design decisions. The authors should explicitly state methodological choices and adopt active voice. Additionally, the manuscript does not fully utilize the 8-page limit; the uninformative "trace" description on page 3 could be condensed to free space for clearer, subsection-level explanations of each method.

A critical unresolved question concerns sensitivity to word embedding choices. How would results change if word2vec replaced GloVe? This is essential for assessing the method's robustness, as embedding models significantly influence semantic similarity calculations. Addressing this would strengthen the paper's validity and applicability.

Overall, the work offers a practical evaluation tool with compelling empirical results. However, it would benefit from a clearer theoretical rationale, improved methodological transparency, and exploration of embedding dependencies. The current presentation, while functional, risks appearing ad hoc without deeper justification for its core premise. With these refinements, the paper could make a valuable contribution to topic model evaluation.
