

This paper introduces a novel approach for evaluating topic quality using word embeddings to compute similarity (either directly or via matrix factorization), demonstrating strong performance on standard datasets. The method represents a logical and valuable progression in topic evaluation research. However, the most concerning aspect is the significant inconsistency in results across the three datasets: while the authors achieve state-of-the-art scores overall, no single method consistently outperforms the current best approaches. Specifically, SVD-based methods exhibit notably poor performance on the genomics dataset. For practitioners seeking to deploy this method broadly across diverse datasets, this lack of consistent superiority presents a substantial practical limitation. I suspect the SVD underperformance on genomics stems from the high proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms relative to GloVe embeddings (as noted below), and the authors might explore predicting optimal method selection based on vocabulary overlap. However, this critical insight and potential mitigation strategy are absent from the paper.

Additional concerns require attention:
1.  **Methodological Overlap:** The core approach bears strong resemblance to techniques in lexical chaining literature. The authors should explicitly engage with this body of work and incorporate relevant citations in any revised version to position their contribution accurately.
2.  **Parameter Clarification:** While the authors emphasize their method has "no parameters," the underlying word embedding models (e.g., GloVe) inherently possess significant parameters. This implicit dependency should be acknowledged to avoid misleading readers about the method's simplicity.
3.  **OOV Handling:** The paper does not address how the method handles OOV terms, particularly prevalent in the genomics dataset (e.g., terms absent from GloVe). Are these terms simply ignored? What is their quantitative impact on the evaluation results? This omission is critical for understanding the method's applicability to domains with specialized vocabularies.

Several low-level technical points require correction:
*   **Vector Normalization:** Section 2.1 states that cosine similarity can be used to measure vector similarity without noting that this assumes unit-length vectors. Word2vec embeddings (and pretrained GloVe *before* post-hoc normalization) are not unit-length, making this assumption technically incorrect. The description should clarify this nuance or specify the normalization step applied.
*   **Figure Readability:** Figure 1 contains graphs that are too small to be legible, hindering the reader's ability to interpret the results.

Overall, the proposed method is promising and builds logically on existing work. The core idea of leveraging embeddings for topic evaluation is sound and demonstrates strong empirical results. However, the inconsistent performance across datasets, the lack of discussion on OOV terms (a likely key factor in the genomics results), the omission of relevant lexical chaining literature, the parameter ambiguity, and the technical inaccuracies in the vector description significantly undermine the paper's contribution and practical utility. Addressing these points—particularly the OOV issue, the methodological comparison, and the vector normalization detail—is essential for a robust contribution. The current presentation does not sufficiently justify the method's consistent superiority or provide a clear basis for its general adoption across diverse domains.
