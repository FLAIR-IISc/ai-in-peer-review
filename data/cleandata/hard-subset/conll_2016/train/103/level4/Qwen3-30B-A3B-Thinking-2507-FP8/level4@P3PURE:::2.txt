

This paper introduces a novel method for evaluating topic models by partitioning the top n words of each topic into clusters ("buckets") based on cosine similarity of their word embeddings. In the simplest variant, words are processed sequentially: each word is assigned to an existing bucket if its cosine similarity to other words in the bucket falls below a threshold, or a new bucket is created otherwise. Two additional approaches—based on eigenvectors and reorganization—are also proposed. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets and a weakly supervised text classification task, where it outperforms or matches the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

While the core idea is straightforward, it possesses an ad hoc character. The authors provide no theoretical justification for why topic quality should be measurable via word–word similarity, which is unclear given that topics and word embeddings rely on fundamentally different contextual frameworks (document-level vs. sequential context). Despite this, the method demonstrates strong empirical performance (though significance tests for Table 1 would strengthen this claim).

The paper is clearly written overall, though minor language issues persist. Section 3’s technical description is somewhat difficult to follow, largely due to excessive passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as" where a design choice is being made). The authors should clarify the methods by dedicating one subsection per approach. The 8-page limit offers ample room for this; for instance, the uninformative "trace" of the method on page 3 could be reduced.

A key unresolved question is the method’s sensitivity to word embeddings. How would results change if word2vec were used instead of GloVe?
