

This paper introduces a topic quality evaluation approach leveraging word embeddings to compute similarity (via direct comparison or matrix factorization), demonstrating strong performance on standard benchmarks. The method constitutes a logical and significant advancement in topic evaluation research. However, the most concerning aspect of the results is the substantial inconsistency in method performance across the three datasets: while all proposed approaches achieve state-of-the-art results overall, no single method consistently outperforms the current state of the art. Notably, SVD-based methods exhibit significantly poorer performance on the genomics dataset. For practitioners seeking to deploy this method across arbitrary datasets, this lack of robustness represents a significant concern. I suspect the SVD underperformance on genomics stems from the high proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms (see below), and it may be feasible to predict optimal method selection based on vocabulary overlap with pre-trained embeddings like GloVe; however, this critical insight is absent from the paper.  

Additional concerns:  
- The method bears strong resemblance to techniques in lexical chaining literature; I recommend the authors incorporate relevant prior work in any revision.  
- While the paper emphasizes the absence of parameters in the proposed method, the underlying word embedding approaches inherently involve substantial implicit parameters (e.g., GloVe training parameters). This should be acknowledged.  
- The handling of OOV terms (e.g., in the genomics dataset, where terms may lack GloVe embeddings) is unclear. Are such terms discarded? What is their impact on evaluation robustness?  

Low-level issues:  
- Section 2.1 implicitly assumes vector length is irrelevant for cosine similarity, but word2vec does not produce unit-length vectors by default (pre-trained GloVe vectors are normalized post hoc, while self-trained vectors vary in length). This technical oversight requires clarification.  
- Figure 1’s graphs are too small for legibility.
