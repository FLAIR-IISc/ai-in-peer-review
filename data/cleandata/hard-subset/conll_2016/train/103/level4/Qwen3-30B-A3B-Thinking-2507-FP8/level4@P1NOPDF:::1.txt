

This paper introduces a novel approach to topic quality evaluation using word embeddings to compute similarity (either directly or through matrix factorization), demonstrating strong performance on standard benchmarks. The method represents a logical and valuable progression in topic evaluation research. However, the most concerning aspect is the significant inconsistency in results across datasets: while the authors achieve state-of-the-art scores on all three benchmarks, no single method consistently outperforms prior work. Specifically, SVD-based approaches perform notably worse on the genomics dataset. For practitioners seeking to deploy this method broadly, this variability presents a significant concern. I suspect the poor SVD performance on genomics stems from high out-of-vocabulary (OOV) term rates (see below), and the paper might benefit from exploring whether method selection could be automated based on vocabulary overlap with pretrained embeddings like GloVe. This potential avenue is not addressed, however.

Other key concerns:

1.  **Lexical Chaining Similarity:** The core methodology bears strong resemblance to techniques in lexical chaining literature. The authors should incorporate relevant prior work in any revision.
2.  **Parameterization Claim:** While the paper emphasizes the method's "no parameters" nature, the underlying word embedding models (e.g., GloVe) inherently contain numerous parameters. This implicit dependency warrants acknowledgment, though it is a minor point.
3.  **OOV Term Handling:** The paper does not specify how OOV terms (e.g., prevalent in the genomics dataset) are managed. Are they simply excluded? What is the impact of this on evaluation robustness? This gap needs clarification.

Minor technical points:

*   Section 2.1 states that cosine similarity can measure vector similarity without noting that it assumes unit-length vectors. Standard word2vec outputs (unlike normalized pre-trained vectors) are not unit-length, making this assumption inaccurate. A brief note on normalization would be helpful.
*   Figure 1's visualizations are too small for clear interpretation; the resolution should be improved.

The core contribution is promising, and the results are generally strong. Addressing the inconsistencies—particularly the SVD performance on genomics and the OOV handling question—and clarifying the embedding dependency would substantially strengthen the paper's practical utility and theoretical grounding. The proposed method is a meaningful step forward, but its reliability across diverse domains requires further discussion.
