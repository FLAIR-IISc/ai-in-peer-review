

**Summary**  
This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic quality in LDA-based topic models by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The authors propose three techniques for bucket formation: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step for improved bucket formation). The method leverages word embeddings to assess semantic similarity between topic words, with coherence scores derived from the size and composition of the largest bucket. TBuckets achieves state-of-the-art performance on two out of three datasets (NYT and 20NG) while being parameter-free for its SVD-based techniques, contrasting with existing methods that require extensive parameter tuning.

**Strengths**  
1. **Parameter-free SVD-based techniques**: TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no hyperparameter tuning, a significant practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods that demand extensive configuration. This addresses a key pain point in topic coherence evaluation.  
2. **Strong empirical results**: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms prior work on NYT and 20NG datasets, with Pearson's r correlations exceeding the best reported results. The method's consistency on these datasets demonstrates its effectiveness.  
3. **Practical application**: The paper convincingly demonstrates TBuckets' utility in weakly supervised text classification, where filtering topics by coherence improves classification accuracy by 1-2% on challenging datasets like PC vs. MAC. This shows real-world value beyond theoretical evaluation.  
4. **Intuitive coherence measure**: The bucketing approach aligns well with human interpretation of topic coherence, where a single dominant theme indicates high quality. The method's design directly mirrors how humans assess topic coherence.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Inconsistent cross-dataset performance**: While TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves state-of-the-art results on two datasets, it performs significantly worse than the state-of-the-art on Genomics (0.68 vs. 0.773). The SVD-based techniques' poor performance on Genomics is unexplained and raises concerns about the method's generalizability.  
2. **Lack of OOV handling discussion**: The paper does not address how out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms in Genomics (e.g., domain-specific terms absent from GloVe embeddings) are handled. Given Genomics' lower performance, OOV terms likely contribute to this discrepancy, yet this critical issue is unaddressed.  
3. **Misleading "parameter-free" claim**: While TBuckets itself requires no parameters, it relies on pre-trained GloVe embeddings with implicit parameters. This oversight misrepresents the method's simplicity and could mislead practitioners.  
4. **Missing connection to lexical chaining**: The approach bears strong similarities to lexical chaining methods (e.g., grouping words by semantic similarity), yet this literature is not acknowledged in the related work. This omission weakens the paper's novelty claim.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How does TBuckets handle OOV terms in datasets like Genomics? Are they ignored, replaced, or otherwise processed? What impact does this have on coherence scores?  
2. Given the inconsistent performance across datasets (particularly SVD's poor results on Genomics), could you identify characteristics of a dataset that would determine the most effective TBuckets technique (e.g., vocabulary overlap with GloVe)?  
3. How does your method relate to lexical chaining literature (e.g., [1]), and why was this not included in the related work?  

**Additional Comments**  
- The description of word embeddings (Section 2.1) incorrectly assumes uniform vector length for cosine similarity. Word embeddings (e.g., from word2vec) are not unit-length unless normalized, which affects similarity calculations.  
- Figure 1 is too small to read, making it difficult to assess the effect of varying Max Dist for TBuckets-Clustering.  
- The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of when to use each technique (e.g., TBuckets-Clustering vs. SVD-based methods) based on dataset characteristics.  

**Overall Recommendation**  
The paper presents a compelling approach to topic quality evaluation with strong empirical results on two datasets and clear practical utility. However, the inconsistent performance on Genomics, lack of OOV handling discussion, and misleading parameter-free claim significantly undermine the work's generalizability and practicality. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the contribution. I recommend acceptance with major revisions.
