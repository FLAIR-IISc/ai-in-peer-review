

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic quality by grouping topic words into thematic buckets based on word embeddings. The authors propose three techniques: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (enhancing SVD with reorganization). The method demonstrates strong empirical performance across three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) and shows utility in weakly supervised text classification.

The primary contribution is the TBuckets framework itself, which provides a simple yet effective way to measure topic coherence by analyzing word groupings. The key insight is that a single dominant bucket indicates high topic coherence, while multiple medium-sized buckets suggest lower coherence. The authors' second contribution is their parameter-free SVD-based techniques, which outperform state-of-the-art methods without requiring parameter tuning—a significant advantage over existing approaches. The third contribution is the practical demonstration of TBuckets' utility in weakly supervised text classification, where selecting high-quality topics based on TBuckets scores leads to improved classification performance.

Strengths: The paper makes a strong empirical case for TBuckets. The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique achieves the highest correlation with human judgments on two of three datasets (NYT and 20NG), outperforming the state-of-the-art (Roder et al., 2015). Crucially, the SVD-based methods require no parameter tuning, unlike previous approaches that need extensive parameter optimization. This parameter-free advantage is well-documented in Table 1 and Figure 1, where TBuckets-SVD-Reorg consistently outperforms other methods without any hyperparameter adjustment. The paper also demonstrates practical value through the text classification experiments in Section 4.3, where using TBuckets to select high-quality topics for human annotation significantly improves classification accuracy (e.g., 96.19% vs. 95.95% on POLITICS vs. SCIENCE), with particularly dramatic improvements on challenging datasets like PC vs. MAC (68.47% vs. 66.98%).

Weaknesses: The paper lacks theoretical justification for why word embeddings should correlate with topic coherence. The authors assume that semantic similarity (via embeddings) aligns with human topic coherence, but they do not explain why document-based topics and sequential-context word embeddings should be related. This is a significant gap, as the two representations capture fundamentally different notions of context. The paper would benefit from a discussion of this theoretical disconnect. The description of the techniques, particularly in Section 3.2, is difficult to follow due to passive voice and insufficient explanation of design choices. For instance, the explanation of the reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) is opaque: the authors state "the threshold is computed as" without explaining the rationale behind the specific formula. This could be clarified by presenting each technique in a dedicated subsection with clear design decisions. The paper also fails to evaluate the method's sensitivity to different word embedding models. The authors use GloVe embeddings exclusively, but it's unclear how the results would change with alternative embeddings like word2vec or fastText, which could affect the coherence scores significantly. This omission is particularly notable given the authors' emphasis on word embeddings as a core component.

Questions to Authors: 
1. How sensitive are the coherence scores to the choice of word embeddings? Could you provide results using alternative embeddings (e.g., word2vec) on the same datasets?
2. Can you clarify the theoretical justification for why semantic similarity of words (via embeddings) should correlate with human topic coherence, given that topics are defined with respect to document context while embeddings capture sequential context?
3. The paper states that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg "requires absolutely no parameter tuning." Could you elaborate on how the dynamic threshold (AWES - AWPS/AWES) is computed without empirical parameter adjustment, and whether this threshold would need to be adapted for different domains or datasets?

The paper is well-written overall, though some language issues persist (e.g., "a single large bucket signifies a single coherent theme" appears twice in the abstract). The authors could improve clarity by restructuring Section 3 to present each technique in a dedicated subsection with clear design rationale, rather than discussing all three techniques in a single dense section. The current structure makes it difficult to distinguish between the core ideas and implementation details. The authors have room to reduce the length of their description of the basic bucketing procedure on page 3, which is overly detailed for the contribution being made.
