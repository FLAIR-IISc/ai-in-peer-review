

This paper introduces a novel approach for evaluating topic models by clustering the top n words per topic into "buckets" using cosine similarity of word embeddings. The core method processes words sequentially, assigning each to an existing bucket if its similarity to bucket members falls below a threshold, or creating a new bucket otherwise. Two additional variants—based on eigenvectors and reorganization—are also proposed. The method is assessed across three standard datasets and a weakly supervised text classification task, achieving results that either surpass or match the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).  

The core concept is straightforward but exhibits an ad hoc quality. The authors provide no theoretical justification for measuring topic quality via word–word similarity, particularly given the divergent contextual foundations of topics (document-level context) and word embeddings (sequential context). Despite this, the method demonstrates practical effectiveness (though significance tests for Table 1 would strengthen the claims).  

The paper is well-written overall, though minor language issues persist. Section 3’s technical descriptions are occasionally opaque, largely due to excessive passive voice (e.g., "the threshold is computed as" instead of clarifying design choices). The authors should enhance clarity by dedicating one subsection per method, which would be feasible given the paper’s current content (8 pages are not fully utilized; the "trace" section on page 3 is particularly uninformative).  

A key unresolved question is the method’s sensitivity to word embedding choices—e.g., how would results differ using word2vec instead of GloVe?
