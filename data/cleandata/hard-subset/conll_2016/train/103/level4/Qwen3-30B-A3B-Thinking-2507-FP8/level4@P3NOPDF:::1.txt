

Review:  
This paper introduces a novel method for topic quality evaluation leveraging word embeddings to compute similarity (via direct comparison or matrix factorization), demonstrating strong performance on standard benchmarks. The approach represents a natural and valuable progression in topic evaluation research. However, significant concerns regarding methodological consistency and implementation details warrant attention before acceptance.  

The primary issue is the inconsistent performance across datasets. While the method achieves state-of-the-art results on all three benchmarks, no single approach consistently outperforms all baselines. Crucially, SVD-based variants exhibit notably poor performance on the genomics dataset—a discrepancy that undermines the method’s practical applicability. Given that the genomics domain contains a high proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms (e.g., domain-specific terminology absent from GloVe), it is plausible that OOV handling directly contributes to this failure. The authors do not address this connection or explore whether method selection could be automated based on vocabulary overlap with pre-trained embeddings. For practitioners seeking a generalizable solution, this inconsistency presents a substantial risk, and the absence of such analysis weakens the paper’s core contribution.  

Additional concerns include:  
1. **Related Work**: The method bears strong resemblance to lexical chaining techniques (e.g., in *Cao et al., 2012* or *Liu et al., 2015*). The authors should explicitly engage with this literature to clarify their novel contribution.  
2. **Parameterization**: The claim that the method "has no parameters" is misleading. While the evaluation framework itself lacks hyperparameters, it implicitly relies on pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe), which entail substantial implicit parameterization. This should be acknowledged.  
3. **OOV Handling**: The paper does not specify how OOV terms (e.g., in genomics) are processed. Are they ignored? Does their exclusion bias results? This gap is critical for reproducibility and generalizability.  

Low-level issues further detract from clarity:  
- Section 2.1 incorrectly assumes word embedding vectors are unit-length for cosine similarity. Pre-trained word2vec vectors (and many others) are not normalized by default, making vector magnitude relevant. This oversight affects the technical validity of the similarity measure.  
- Figure 1’s graphs are too small for clear interpretation, hindering the assessment of results.  

In summary, the paper proposes a promising direction but requires substantial revisions. The inconsistent cross-dataset performance—particularly the SVD failure on genomics—demands deeper analysis of OOV effects and vocabulary alignment. Addressing the lexical chaining literature, clarifying the role of embedding parameters, and resolving the OOV handling question are essential. The technical note on vector normalization must also be corrected. Without these, the method’s reliability for real-world deployment remains questionable.  

The core idea has merit, but the current presentation does not sufficiently establish it as a robust, generalizable tool. I recommend major revision to address these points before publication.
