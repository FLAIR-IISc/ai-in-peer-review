This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality using word embeddings to calculate similarity, either directly or indirectly via matrix factorization, and achieves impressive results on standard datasets. The proposed method represents a natural yet important next step in the evolutionary path of research on topic evaluation.

However, the results raise some concerns. Although the method achieves state-of-the-art results on all three datasets, there are significant inconsistencies in the performance of different methods. In practice, none of the proposed methods consistently outperform the state of the art, and the SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. This inconsistency is a considerable worry for someone who wants to apply the method to an arbitrary dataset. I suspect that the poor performance of SVD on the genomics dataset may be related to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, and it may be possible to automatically predict which method will perform best based on vocabulary match with GloVe. However, this discussion is absent in the paper.

Other issues with the paper include:

* The proposed method bears strong similarities with methods proposed in the lexical chaining literature, which the authors may want to explore and include in future versions of the paper.
* Although the authors emphasize that their method has no parameters, the word embedding methods used have a large number of implicit parameters, which is worth acknowledging.
* The paper does not discuss how the method handles OOV terms, such as those in the genomics dataset that are not present in the pre-trained GloVe embeddings. Are these terms simply ignored, and what impact does this have on the method?

On a more technical level:

* The description of word embeddings in Section 2.1 assumes that the vector length is unimportant, which is only true if the vectors are unit-length. However, word2vec does not return unit-length vectors, and the pre-trained vectors have been normalized post-hoc. This is a small but important detail.
* The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable, which makes it difficult to interpret the results.

Overall, while the proposed method shows promise, it requires further refinement and discussion to address these concerns and inconsistencies.
