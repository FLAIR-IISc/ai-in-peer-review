This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality based on using word embeddings to calculate similarity, either directly or indirectly via matrix factorization, achieving impressive results over standard datasets. The proposed method represents a natural but important next step in the evolutionary path of research on topic evaluation.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel approach, called TBuckets, to measure the quality of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) based topics by grouping topic words into thematic groups (buckets). The method uses word embeddings and employs three techniques for creating buckets of words: clustering-based, singular value decomposition (SVD), and SVD with reorganization. The authors evaluate their techniques on three publicly available datasets and demonstrate the usefulness of TBuckets for the task of weakly supervised text classification.

The main contributions of this paper are:

1. A novel approach to measuring topic quality based on grouping topic words into thematic groups (buckets).
2. The proposal of three techniques for creating buckets of words: clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization.
3. The evaluation of the proposed techniques on three publicly available datasets, demonstrating their effectiveness in measuring topic quality.

**Strengths**

1. The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on two out of three datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in measuring topic quality.
2. The method is based on a simple yet intuitive idea of grouping topic words into thematic groups, making it easy to understand and implement.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their method on multiple datasets, demonstrating its robustness and generalizability.

**Weaknesses**

1. The results show large inconsistencies in which methods perform well and which methods perform poorly, making it difficult to recommend a single method for use on arbitrary datasets.
2. The SVD-based methods perform notably badly on the genomics dataset, which may be related to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms.
3. The authors do not discuss how their method deals with OOV terms, which may impact the method's performance.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of large inconsistencies in method performance across datasets?
2. Can you provide more insight into why the SVD-based methods perform poorly on the genomics dataset, and how you plan to improve their performance?
3. How does your method deal with OOV terms, and what impact does this have on the method's performance?
4. Have you considered exploring other techniques for creating buckets of words, such as lexical chaining methods?
5. Can you provide more information on how you plan to extend your method to other applications beyond text classification?
