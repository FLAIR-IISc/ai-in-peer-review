This paper presents a novel approach to evaluating topic quality by leveraging word embeddings to compute similarity, either directly or through matrix factorization, yielding impressive results on standard datasets. The proposed method marks a logical yet significant advancement in the field of topic evaluation research.

However, the results raised some concerns, particularly the inconsistencies in method performance across datasets. Although the proposed methods achieved state-of-the-art results for all three datasets, there were notable variations in their relative performance. In practice, none of the proposed methods consistently outperformed the state of the art, and the SVD-based methods performed particularly poorly on the genomics dataset. This inconsistency is a significant concern for users who may want to apply the method to arbitrary datasets.

I suspect that the poor performance of SVD on the genomics dataset may be attributed to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, and it may be possible to predict the best-performing method based on vocabulary match with GloVe. However, this aspect is not discussed in the paper.

Other issues that require attention include:

- The proposed method bears similarities with approaches in the lexical chaining literature, which the authors may want to explore and incorporate into future versions of the paper.
- Although the method is claimed to be parameter-free, it relies on word embedding methods with a large number of implicit parameters, which is worth acknowledging.
- The paper does not discuss how the method handles OOV terms, such as those in the genomics dataset, and their potential impact on the method's performance.

Additionally, some low-level issues were noted:

- The description of word embeddings in Section 2.1 assumes that vector length is insignificant when using cosine similarity. However, this assumption is problematic since word2vec does not produce unit-length vectors, and the pre-trained vectors have been normalized post hoc.
- The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable, hindering the interpretation of the results.
