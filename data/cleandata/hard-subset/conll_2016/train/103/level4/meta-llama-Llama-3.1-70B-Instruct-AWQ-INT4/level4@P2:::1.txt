This paper proposes a novel approach to measuring topic quality using word embeddings to calculate similarity, achieving impressive results over standard datasets. The proposed method represents a natural but important next step in the evolutionary path of research on topic evaluation.

**Summary of the Paper:**
The paper proposes a method called TBuckets, which groups topic words into thematic groups (buckets) using word embeddings and three different techniques: clustering-based, singular value decomposition (SVD), and SVD with reorganization. The method is evaluated on three publicly available datasets and compared to state-of-the-art results. The authors also demonstrate the usefulness of TBuckets for weakly supervised text classification.

**Main Contributions:**

1. A novel approach to measuring topic quality using word embeddings and bucketing techniques.
2. The proposal of three different techniques for creating buckets of words: clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization.
3. The evaluation of the proposed method on three publicly available datasets and comparison to state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths:**

1. The proposed method achieves state-of-the-art results on two out of three datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in measuring topic quality.
2. The method is parameter-free, making it easy to use and interpret.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, including a comparison to state-of-the-art results and an analysis of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The performance of the proposed method is inconsistent across datasets, with the SVD-based methods performing poorly on the genomics dataset.
2. The method does not handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms explicitly, which may impact its performance on certain datasets.
3. The authors do not discuss the potential impact of the word embedding method's parameters on the proposed method's performance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do you plan to address the inconsistent performance of the proposed method across datasets?
2. Can you provide more details on how the method handles OOV terms, and what impact this has on its performance?
3. Have you considered using other word embedding methods or techniques to improve the performance of the proposed method?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work, including the lexical chaining literature.
* The authors should acknowledge the implicit parameters of the word embedding method and discuss their potential impact on the proposed method's performance.
* The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable and should be enlarged or reformatted for better readability.
