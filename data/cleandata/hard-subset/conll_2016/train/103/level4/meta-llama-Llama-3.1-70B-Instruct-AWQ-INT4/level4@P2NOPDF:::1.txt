This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality based on word embeddings, which calculates similarity either directly or indirectly via matrix factorization, yielding impressive results on standard datasets. The proposed method represents a natural yet important next step in the evolutionary path of research on topic evaluation.

While the results are promising, with state-of-the-art performance achieved on all three datasets, there are notable inconsistencies in the methods' performance. In practice, none of the proposed methods consistently outperform the state-of-the-art, and the SVD-based methods perform poorly on the genomics dataset. This inconsistency raises concerns for potential users who may want to apply the method to arbitrary datasets. The subpar performance of SVD on the genomics dataset may be related to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. It is possible that the method's performance could be improved by automatically predicting the best-performing method based on vocabulary match with GloVe, but this discussion is absent from the paper.

Several issues warrant attention. Firstly, the proposed method bears strong similarities with methods in the lexical chaining literature, which the authors may benefit from exploring and incorporating into future versions of the paper. Additionally, while the authors emphasize that their method has no parameters, it is worth acknowledging that the word embedding methods used have a large number of implicit parameters. Furthermore, the paper does not adequately address how the method handles OOV terms, such as those in the genomics dataset, which are not present in the pre-trained GloVe embeddings. Are these terms simply ignored, and what impact does this have on the method's performance?

On a more technical level, the description of word embeddings in Section 2.1 assumes that vector length is unimportant when using cosine similarity to measure vector similarity. However, this assumption is problematic, as word2vec does not return unit-length vectors. While the pre-trained vectors have been normalized post-hoc, running word2vec independently would yield vectors of non-uniform length. Although a minor detail, it is essential to acknowledge this aspect. Lastly, the graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable, which hinders the clarity of the results.

Overall, the proposed method shows promise, but addressing these concerns and inconsistencies will be crucial to strengthening the paper and increasing its potential for real-world applications.
