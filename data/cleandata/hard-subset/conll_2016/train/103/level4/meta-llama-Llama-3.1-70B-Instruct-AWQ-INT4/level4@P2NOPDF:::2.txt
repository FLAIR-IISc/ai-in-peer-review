This paper proposes a novel method for evaluating topic models by partitioning the top n words of each topic into clusters, or "buckets," based on the cosine similarity of their associated word embeddings. The simplest setup involves considering each word individually and either assigning it to an existing bucket if its cosine similarity to other words in the bucket is below a certain threshold or creating a new bucket for the word. Two more complex methods, based on eigenvectors and reorganization, are also suggested. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setting, where it outperforms or is on par with the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

While the basic idea behind the paper is straightforward, it has an ad hoc flavor, and the authors do not provide new explanations for why topic quality should be measurable in terms of word-word similarity. Given that topics and word embeddings are defined with respect to two different notions of context (document vs. sequential context), it is not immediately clear why this approach should be effective. Nevertheless, the proposed method appears to work well, although I would like to see significance tests for Table 1 to confirm this.

The paper is generally well-written, despite some minor language issues. However, I found the description of the techniques in Section 3 to be somewhat difficult to follow, which I attribute to the authors' use of passive voice in places where they were making design choices. To improve clarity, I suggest that the authors explain each method in a separate subsection, which would be feasible given the available space. The 8-page content limit has not been fully utilized, and the rather uninformative "trace" of the method on page 3 could be downsized.

One question that arose during my review is the sensitivity of the proposed technique to different word embeddings. For instance, how would the scores be affected if the authors had used word2vec instead of GloVe? Addressing this question could provide valuable insights into the method's robustness and generalizability.
