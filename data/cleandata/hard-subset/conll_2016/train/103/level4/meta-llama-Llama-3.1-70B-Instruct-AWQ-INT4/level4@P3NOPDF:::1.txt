This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality using word embeddings to calculate similarity, either directly or indirectly via matrix factorization, achieving impressive results on standard datasets. The proposed method represents a natural yet important next step in the evolutionary path of research on topic evaluation.

However, the results raise some concerns. While the authors achieve state-of-the-art results for all three datasets, there are large inconsistencies in the performance of different methods. In practice, none of the proposed methods consistently outperform the state of the art, and the SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. This inconsistency is a considerable worry for someone who wants to use the proposed method off the shelf on any arbitrary dataset.

I suspect that the lower results for SVD on the genomics dataset may be related to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. It may be possible to automatically predict which method will perform best based on vocabulary match with GloVe, but there is no discussion on this topic in the paper.

Several issues need to be addressed:

1. The proposed method bears strong similarities with methods proposed in the lexical chaining literature. I encourage the authors to explore this connection and include it in any future version of the paper.
2. The authors emphasize that their method has no parameters, but the word embedding methods used have a large number of implicit parameters. While not a significant issue, it is worth acknowledging.
3. The paper does not discuss how the method deals with OOV terms, such as those in the genomics dataset that are not present in the pre-trained GloVe embeddings. Are they simply ignored? What impact does this have on the method?

On a lower level, a few details need clarification:

1. In the description of word embeddings in Section 2.1, the authors implicitly assume that the length of the vector is unimportant when using cosine similarity to measure the similarity between two vectors. However, this assumption is problematic since word2vec does not return unit-length vectors. The pre-trained vectors have been normalized post hoc, and if one runs word2vec themselves, the vector length is not uniform. This is a small but important detail.
2. The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable, making it difficult to interpret the results.

Overall, while the proposed method shows promise, it is essential to address these concerns to increase its reliability and applicability to various datasets.
