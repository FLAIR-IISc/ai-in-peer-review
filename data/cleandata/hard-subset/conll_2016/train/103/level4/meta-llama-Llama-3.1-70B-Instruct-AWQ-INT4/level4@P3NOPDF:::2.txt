This paper proposes a novel method for evaluating topic models by partitioning the top n words of each topic into clusters or "buckets" based on the cosine similarity of their associated word embeddings. In its simplest setup, the method considers each word individually, either assigning it to an existing "bucket" if its cosine similarity to the other words in the bucket is below a certain threshold or creating a new bucket for the word. Two more complex methods, based on eigenvectors and reorganization, are also suggested. The authors evaluate their method on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setting, demonstrating performance comparable to or surpassing the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

The core idea behind the paper is relatively straightforward, but it has an ad hoc flavor to it. The authors do not provide new explanations for why topic quality should be measurable in terms of word-word similarity. Given that topics and word embeddings are defined with respect to two distinct notions of context (document vs. sequential context), it is not immediately clear why this approach should be effective. Nevertheless, the proposed method appears to work well in practice. However, I would like to see some significance tests for Table 1 to further support the results.

The paper is generally well-written, although some language issues are present. I found the description of the techniques in Section 3 somewhat difficult to follow, primarily due to the authors' use of passive voice ("the threshold is computed as") in places where they were making design choices. I recommend that the authors rephrase these sections to clearly explain the different methods, potentially dedicating one subsection to each. There appears to be sufficient space for this, as the authors did not fully utilize the 8 pages of content, and the rather uninformative "trace" of the method on page 3 could be condensed.

One question I had was how sensitive the proposed technique is to different word embeddings. For instance, how would the scores change if the authors had used word2vec instead of GloVe? Investigating this could provide valuable insights into the method's robustness and generalizability.

Overall, the paper presents an interesting approach to evaluating topic models, and with some revisions to address the mentioned concerns, it has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field.
