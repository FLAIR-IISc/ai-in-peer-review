This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality by leveraging word embeddings to calculate similarity, either directly or indirectly via matrix factorization, and achieves impressive results on standard datasets. The proposed method represents a natural yet significant next step in the evolution of research on topic evaluation.

However, upon examining the results, I was struck by the large inconsistencies in the performance of different methods across the datasets. While the paper reports state-of-the-art results for all three datasets, none of the proposed methods consistently outperforms the state of the art. Moreover, the SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. This inconsistency raises concerns for potential users who may want to adopt the proposed method for use on arbitrary datasets. I suspect that the poor performance of SVD on the genomics dataset may be related to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, and it may be possible to automatically predict which method will perform best based on vocabulary match with GloVe. However, this discussion is absent from the paper.

Other issues with the paper include:

* The proposed method bears strong similarities with methods proposed in the lexical chaining literature, which the authors may find useful to explore and incorporate into future versions of the paper.
* Although the authors emphasize that their method has no parameters, it is worth acknowledging that the word embedding methods used have a large number of implicit parameters.
* The paper does not adequately address how the method handles OOV terms, such as those found in the genomics dataset (i.e., terms not present in the pre-trained GloVe embeddings). Are these terms simply ignored, and if so, what impact does this have on the method?

Additionally, there are a few low-level issues that should be addressed:

* In the description of word embeddings in Section 2.1, the authors implicitly assume that the length of the vector is unimportant when using cosine similarity to measure the similarity between two vectors. However, this assumption is problematic since word2vec does not return unit-length vectors. Although the pre-trained vectors have been normalized post-hoc, this detail is worth noting.
* The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable and should be resized or reformatted for clarity.

By addressing these concerns and providing further discussion on the limitations and potential applications of the proposed method, the authors can strengthen their contribution to the field of topic evaluation.
