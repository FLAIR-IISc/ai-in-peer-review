This paper proposes a novel approach for evaluating topic models by partitioning the top n words of each topic into thematic groups, referred to as "buckets," based on the cosine similarity of their associated word embeddings. The method, termed TBuckets, is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setting, outperforming or matching the state-of-the-art results.

The paper's core idea is straightforward, but it lacks a clear explanation for why topic quality should be measurable in terms of word-word similarity, given that topics and word embeddings are defined with respect to different notions of context (document vs. sequential context). Nevertheless, the proposed method demonstrates promising results.

The paper is well-written, although some language issues are present. The description of the techniques in Section 3 is somewhat challenging to follow, primarily due to the use of passive voice in places where design choices were made. To improve clarity, the authors could consider reorganizing the section into separate subsections for each method, providing more detailed explanations.

The authors should also consider adding significance tests for Table 1 to strengthen their claims. Furthermore, exploring the sensitivity of the proposed technique to different word embeddings, such as word2vec instead of GloVe, would be beneficial in understanding the method's robustness.

**Summary of the paper:** The paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for evaluating topic models by partitioning the top n words of each topic into thematic groups based on the cosine similarity of their associated word embeddings. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setting.

**Main contributions:**

1. The TBuckets approach, which groups topic words into thematic groups based on word embeddings, outperforms or matches the state-of-the-art results in evaluating topic models.
2. The method is effective in a weakly supervised text classification setting, demonstrating its potential in real-world applications.
3. The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their approach on three standard datasets, showcasing its robustness and generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper proposes a novel and intuitive approach for evaluating topic models, which is easy to understand and implement.
2. The method demonstrates promising results, outperforming or matching the state-of-the-art results in various settings.
3. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including a comprehensive analysis of the results and a discussion of the method's limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper lacks a clear explanation for why topic quality should be measurable in terms of word-word similarity, given the different notions of context used in topics and word embeddings.
2. The description of the techniques in Section 3 is somewhat challenging to follow, primarily due to the use of passive voice in places where design choices were made.
3. The authors do not provide significance tests for Table 1, which would strengthen their claims and provide more robust results.

**Questions to authors:**

1. Can you provide a clear explanation for why topic quality should be measurable in terms of word-word similarity, given the different notions of context used in topics and word embeddings?
