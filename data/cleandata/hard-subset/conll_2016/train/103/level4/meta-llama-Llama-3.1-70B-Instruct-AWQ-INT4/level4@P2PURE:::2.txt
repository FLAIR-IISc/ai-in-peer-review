This paper introduces a novel method for evaluating topic models by partitioning the top n words of each topic into clusters, or "buckets," based on the cosine similarity of their associated word embeddings. In its simplest form, the approach considers each word individually and either assigns it to an existing "bucket" if its cosine similarity to the other words in the bucket falls below a certain threshold, or creates a new bucket for the word. The authors also propose two more complex methods that utilize eigenvectors and reorganization. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setting, demonstrating performance comparable to or surpassing the state-of-the-art results reported by Röder et al. (2015).

While the underlying idea is straightforward, it has a somewhat ad-hoc nature. The authors do not provide a new explanation for why topic quality should be measurable in terms of word-word similarity, which is not immediately clear given that topics and word embeddings are defined with respect to distinct notions of context (document vs. sequential context). Nevertheless, the proposed method appears to be effective. However, I would like to see significance tests for the results presented in Table 1.

The paper is generally well-written, although some language issues are present. I found the description of the techniques in Section 3 somewhat challenging to follow, which I attribute to the authors' use of passive voice in places where they were making design choices. To improve clarity, I suggest that the authors explain each method in a separate subsection. There appears to be sufficient space for this, as the authors did not fully utilize the 8-page content limit and could potentially condense the less informative "trace" of the method on page 3.

One question that arose during my review is the sensitivity of the proposed technique to different word embeddings. For instance, how would the results change if the authors had used word2vec instead of GloVe?
