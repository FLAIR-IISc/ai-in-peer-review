This paper proposes a novel method, TBuckets, for evaluating topic models by partitioning the top n words of each topic into clusters or "buckets" based on the cosine similarity of their associated word embeddings. The approach is intuitive, and its performance is evaluated on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setting, where it outperforms or is on par with the state of the art (Röder et al., 2015).

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a new method for evaluating topic models by grouping topic words into thematic groups (buckets) using word embeddings. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel approach to measuring topic quality based on word embeddings and bucketing.
2. Three techniques for creating buckets of words: clustering-based, SVD-based, and SVD-based with reorganization.
3. Evaluation of the proposed techniques on three publicly available datasets, demonstrating better performance than the state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths**

1. The proposed method is simple and intuitive, making it easy to understand and implement.
2. The use of word embeddings and bucketing allows for a more nuanced evaluation of topic quality.
3. The evaluation on three standard datasets and in a weakly supervised text classification setting demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses**

1. The basic idea behind the paper is relatively simple and may not offer new insights into why topic quality should be measurable in terms of word-word similarity.
2. The paper does not provide a clear explanation for why topics and word embeddings, defined with respect to different notions of context, should be related.
3. The description of the techniques in Section 3 is sometimes hard to follow, and the use of passive voice may obscure design choices.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How sensitive is the proposed technique to different word embeddings, such as word2vec instead of GloVe?
2. Can the authors provide more insight into why topics and word embeddings, defined with respect to different notions of context, should be related?
3. How do the authors plan to address the potential limitations of their method, such as its simplicity and reliance on word embeddings?
