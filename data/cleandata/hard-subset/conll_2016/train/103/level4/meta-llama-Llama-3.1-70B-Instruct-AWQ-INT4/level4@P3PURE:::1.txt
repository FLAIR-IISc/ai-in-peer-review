This paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality based on using word embeddings to calculate similarity, either directly or indirectly via matrix factorization, achieving impressive results over standard datasets. The proposed method represents a natural but important next step in the evolutionary path of research on topic evaluation.

However, upon examining the results, I was struck by the large inconsistencies in the performance of different methods across the three datasets. Although the authors achieve state-of-the-art results for all three datasets, none of the proposed methods consistently outperform the state of the art. Furthermore, the SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset. This inconsistency raises concerns for practitioners who may want to apply the proposed method to an arbitrary dataset.

I suspect that the poor performance of SVD on the genomics dataset may be related to the proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. It is possible that the method's performance could be improved by automatically predicting which method will perform best based on vocabulary match with GloVe. However, this discussion is absent from the paper.

Other issues with the paper include:

* The proposed method bears strong similarities with methods proposed in the lexical chaining literature, which the authors would benefit from exploring and incorporating into future versions of the paper.
* While the authors emphasize that their method has no parameters, they overlook the fact that the word embedding methods used have a large number of implicit parameters. Although this is not a major concern, it is worth acknowledging.
* The paper does not adequately address how the method handles OOV terms, such as those present in the genomics dataset that are not included in the pre-trained GloVe embeddings. Are these terms simply ignored, and what impact does this have on the method's performance?

On a more technical level, I have two minor issues with the paper:

* In Section 2.1, the description of word embeddings implicitly assumes that the length of the vector is unimportant, as it suggests that cosine similarity can be used to measure the similarity between two vectors. However, this assumption is problematic, as word2vec does not return unit-length vectors. While the pre-trained vectors have been normalized post hoc, running word2vec itself would result in vectors of non-uniform length.
* The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable, which hinders the understanding of the results.

Overall, while the paper proposes a promising method for evaluating topic quality, it requires further refinement and discussion to address the concerns raised above.
