This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for evaluating topic quality based on grouping topic words into thematic groups (buckets) using word embeddings. The method achieves impressive results on standard datasets, outperforming state-of-the-art techniques on two out of three datasets. The proposed approach represents a natural next step in the evolution of research on topic evaluation.

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions:**

The paper proposes a method for evaluating topic quality based on grouping topic words into buckets using word embeddings. The main contributions of the paper are:

1. A novel approach, TBuckets, for evaluating topic quality using word embeddings.
2. Three techniques for creating buckets of words: clustering-based, singular value decomposition (SVD)-based, and SVD-based with reorganization.
3. Experimental evaluation on three publicly available datasets, demonstrating better performance than state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths:**

1. The proposed approach achieves state-of-the-art results on two out of three datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in evaluating topic quality.
2. The method is based on word embeddings, which capture semantic relationships between words, making it a natural fit for topic evaluation.
3. The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach, including a comparison with state-of-the-art techniques and an analysis of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The results are inconsistent across datasets, with the SVD-based methods performing poorly on the Genomics dataset. This raises concerns about the robustness of the method.
2. The paper does not discuss how to handle out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, which may impact the performance of the method.
3. The method relies on pre-trained word embeddings, which have a large number of implicit parameters.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do you plan to address the inconsistency in results across datasets?
2. Can you provide more details on how to handle OOV terms, and what impact this has on the method?
3. How do you plan to extend the method to handle domains with limited vocabulary overlap with pre-trained word embeddings?

**Additional Comments:**

1. The paper has strong similarities with methods proposed in the lexical chaining literature, which may be worth exploring further.
2. The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable, and may benefit from being enlarged or re-formatted.
3. The paper assumes that the length of the word embedding vectors is unimportant, but this may not be the case in practice.
