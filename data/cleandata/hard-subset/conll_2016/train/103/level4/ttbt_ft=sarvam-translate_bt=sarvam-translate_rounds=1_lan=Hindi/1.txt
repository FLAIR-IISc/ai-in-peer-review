This paper proposes a method for evaluating the quality of a subject using word embedding, which is for computing similarity (either directly or indirectly through matrix factorization), achieving impressive results on standard datasets. The proposed method is a natural but important next step in the development path of research on subject evaluation. What most troubled me with the results was that, while you achieve state-of-the-art results for all three datasets, there are large inconsistencies between the methods that perform and those that perform less well (below state-of-the-art). In practice, none of the proposed methods consistently beat state-of-the-art, and SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on genomics datasets. This is a significant concern for anyone who wants to take your method off the shelf and use it on any arbitrary dataset. I suspect that the low results for SVD on genomics are related to the ratio of OOV words (see comments below), and it may be possible to automatically predict which method will perform best based on vocabulary matching with GloVe, etc., but there is no such discussion in the paper.

Other issues:
- The proposed method has strong similarities with methods proposed in Lexical Channing's literature, which I would encourage the authors to read and incorporate into any future version of the paper.
- You emphasize that your method has no parameters, but word embedding methods have a large number of parameters, which are inherent in your method. This is not a big deal, but acceptable.
- How does your method deal with OOV words, for example in genomics datasets (i.e., not present in pre-trained GloVe embeddings)? Are they simply ignored? What impact does this have on the method?

Low-level issues:
- In your description of word embedding in Section 2.1, you implicitly assume that vector length is unimportant (in saying that cosine similarity can be used to measure similarity between two vectors). If the vectors are unit length, this is not problematic, but word2vec does not actually return unit-length vectors (pre-trained vectors are post-hoked generalized, and if you run word2vec yourself, the vector length is certainly not the same). It's a small thing, but important.
- The graphs in Figure 1 are so small that they cannot be read.
