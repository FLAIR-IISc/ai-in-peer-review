Summary and main contributions
- Introduces TBuckets, a novel perspective on topic coherence that explicitly groups top topic words into thematic “buckets,” and scores coherence via properties of the largest bucket. This shifts from pairwise association-based measures (e.g., PMI/NPMI) to an interpretable structure-level grouping criterion.
- Proposes two largely parameter-free algorithms for bucketing based on SVD of topic-word embeddings: TBuckets-SVD (use eigenvectors of ATA as “sub-theme” directions and assign words by similarity) and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a dynamic reorganization heuristic guided by topic-specific thresholds derived from word–word and word–eigenvector similarities).
- Empirical evidence that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg correlates with human judgements at or above state of the art on 2/3 benchmarks (NYT, 20NG), with competitive performance for clustering-based variant, and demonstrates downstream utility by improving weakly supervised text classification when selecting topic runs by TBuckets scores.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and clarity: The bucketing view of topic coherence is intuitive, interpretable, and different from dominant pairwise/co-occurrence paradigms. The use of SVD directions as orthogonal sub-themes is a neat, bold idea that provides a simple, parameter-free mechanism to induce buckets.
- Competitive to superior empirical performance with minimal tuning: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the best configurations of Röder et al. (2015) on NYT and 20NG while requiring no tuning beyond choosing word embeddings. The clustering variant is surprisingly strong with just one parameter.
- Practical impact and downstream validation: Using TBuckets to select topic runs in weakly supervised classification improves macro-F1 on 3/4 settings, illustrating that the coherence scores are not only correlated with human ratings but also actionable.
- Interpretability of outputs: Producing explicit word buckets is useful for human inspection, debugging topic models, and guiding annotators. Examples show that the method captures obvious sub-themes and their consolidation.
- Simplicity and portability: The approach leverages off-the-shelf embeddings and linear algebra; it should be easy to adopt in practice compared to frameworks with large configuration spaces and multiple tunables.

Weaknesses
- Heuristic design and limited justification: Several core choices (assigning to the most similar eigenvector, using the first n eigenvectors, the reorganization rules, and the dynamic threshold AWES − AWPS/AWES) lack theoretical grounding or empirical ablations. It is unclear why weighting by the square of the top singular value is appropriate. These design decisions seem ad hoc and may limit robustness.
- Reproducibility and missing details: Important implementation specifics are omitted: exact similarity metric (cosine vs dot), normalization of vectors, handling of eigenvector sign ambiguity, tie-breaking, the order and stopping criteria for reorganization, and whether bucketing is single-pass or iterative until convergence. For TBuckets-Clustering, how Max Dist was selected for reported numbers is unclear. Code availability is not mentioned.
- Evaluation gaps and significance: Only three datasets are used, with no confidence intervals or significance tests. Gains over Röder et al. are small on 20NG and the approach underperforms notably on Genomics, undermining claims of broad applicability. There is no comparison against strong embedding-based baselines such as average pairwise cosine, simple KMeans bucketing with largest-cluster size, or silhouette-based scoring.
- Dependence on external embeddings and domain mismatch: Using general GloVe likely hurts in specialized domains (e.g., Genomics), yet the method is presented as parameter-free. The reliance on an external resource is a meaningful choice that should be studied (e.g., domain-specific embeddings vs general).
- Related work positioning: While prior work with distributional semantics is discussed, the paper does not situate itself against clustering-based topic word coherence measures (or simple embedding-based coherence scores). The novelty of clustering/bucketing per se could be seen as incremental without clearer differentiation.

Questions to authors
- TBuckets-SVD specifics: Please clarify the exact SVD shapes and assignment. For A ∈ R^{n×d} (n words, d embedding dims), you state using V (d×d). Do you take the first n columns of V? Are word vectors normalized and is cosine similarity used? How do you handle eigenvector sign ambiguity when computing similarities?
- Reorganization procedure: Is reorganization iterative? In what order are words considered, and what is the stopping criterion? Why is the threshold defined as AWES − AWPS/AWES (unit-mismatched subtraction of a ratio from a similarity)? Please report sensitivity to this formula.
- Scoring choices: Why weight size/WL/EVS by the square of the largest singular value? What happens without this weighting? Can you report ablations for WL, EVS, and their product?
- Baselines: Can you compare against (a) average pairwise cosine among top-N words, (b) KMeans on word embeddings with largest-cluster size as the score, and (c) silhouette-based cluster quality? This would help isolate the contribution of SVD-based bucketing vs generic clustering.
- Genomics performance: Can you evaluate with domain-specific embeddings (e.g., biomedical) to test whether the drop is due to embedding mismatch? Also, does performance improve with top-20 words per topic?
- TBuckets-Clustering parameter: How was Max Dist chosen for the reported best results? Is there a principled/default setting practitioners can use without tuning?
- Practicalities: What is the runtime complexity for typical settings (e.g., 100 topics, top-10/20 words)? Will the approach scale to longer word lists or multilingual embeddings? Is code planned for release?

Overall, I find the bucketing perspective and SVD-based, parameter-light implementation to be a fresh and promising direction for topic coherence, with encouraging results and practical utility. Strengthening justification, details, and comparisons would materially improve the paper.
