Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic-coherence measures that group the top-N words of an LDA topic into “buckets” of semantically related words using pre-trained word embeddings. Three variants are presented: (i) clustering-based bucketing via agglomerative clustering; (ii) SVD-based bucketing that assigns each word to the most similar right-singular vector of the word-embedding matrix; and (iii) an SVD-based method with a parameter-free reorganization heuristic that moves words into/from the largest bucket using a dynamic threshold derived from average word–word and word–eigenvector similarities. Topic coherence is then scored primarily by properties of the largest bucket (size, LDA-order weighted sum, and average similarity to the bucket’s eigenvector), optionally weighted by the top singular value. On three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics; 100 topics each, 10 words per topic), the best TBuckets variant correlates with human judgments better than Röder et al. (2015) on two datasets and worse on Genomics. The authors also show a downstream use case where selecting topic-model runs with higher TBuckets coherence improves weakly-supervised text classification.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple, embedding-based, largely parameter-free coherence scoring paradigm that operationalizes human “bucketing” intuition via SVD directions and a reorganization heuristic.
2) Empirical evidence of competitive or better Pearson correlations with human coherence judgments compared to a strong baseline (Röder et al., 2015) on two datasets.
3) An application showing that selecting topic runs by TBuckets coherence can modestly improve topic-labeling-based classification.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and interpretability: The “largest bucket indicates a single theme” intuition is easy to grasp and inspect. The bucketing outputs are human-interpretable, which is useful for qualitative diagnostics.
- Largely parameter-free SVD variants: Avoiding global tuning is attractive in practice, and the SVD-Reorg variant yields the strongest results on two datasets.
- Competitive empirical performance: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves r = 0.837 (NYT) and 0.869 (20NG), surpassing Röder et al.’s best reported configurations. The reorganization step appears to substantially improve correlations relative to raw SVD bucketing.
- Efficiency and practicality: Operating on only the top-10 words with pre-trained 300d embeddings makes the approach fast, easy to deploy, and independent of external co-occurrence counting infrastructure.
- Downstream relevance: The classification experiment demonstrates that coherence scores can help select better topic runs for annotation, with noticeable gains in some settings and large gaps between “best” and “worst” coherence runs.

Weaknesses
- Theoretical grounding of SVD bucketing is weak/unclear: Assigning words to the most similar right-singular vector of A (n×d word–embedding matrix) treats eigenvectors of A^T A (directions in embedding feature space) as bucket prototypes. This choice is not justified theoretically. Important questions are unaddressed: sign indeterminacy of eigenvectors (do you use absolute cosine?), why use the first n eigenvectors (instead of ranked by explained variance or rank), and why use similarity to V rather than using U S (word loadings) or directly clustering in the projected space. Without this, the method can appear ad hoc.
- Reorganization threshold is underspecified and possibly mis-specified: The formula “AWES − AWPSAWES” lacks parentheses and dimensional clarity (do you mean AWES − AWPS/AWES or (AWES − AWPS)/AWES?). It is unclear whether the threshold can be negative, how sensitive results are to this threshold, and whether the heuristic overfits to the largest bucket. No ablation is provided to isolate the contributions of the into/from moves or the threshold.
- Scoring choice and post-hoc selection: Multiple scoring variants are reported (size, WL, EVS, WL*EVS), and the narrative highlights whichever variant performs best per dataset. This risks post-hoc cherry-picking. A single, pre-specified primary metric (with ablations) and statistical significance tests are needed. The improvements over Röder et al. are small (e.g., 0.869 vs 0.859 on 20NG) and may not be significant on 100 topics; no confidence intervals or tests are reported.
- Fairness and tuning: For clustering, the MaxDist threshold is varied and the best setting reported; for SVD variants, scores are optionally weighted by the top singular value. Röder et al.’s framework spans many configurations but, to be comparable, TBuckets should fix its scoring variant a priori, or perform a controlled model selection using a dev set. Otherwise the comparison is not entirely fair.
- Domain robustness and missing baselines: Performance drops sharply on Genomics (SVD variants ~0.69). While the authors suggest domain-specific embeddings, they do not test this straightforward fix. Moreover, comparison to strong embedding-based coherence variants (e.g., C_v, C_uci, or contextual-embedding baselines) is limited to Röder et al.’s reported best, without a more thorough contemporary set of embedding-based baselines.
- Reproducibility and details: Important implementation details are missing: exact similarity (cosine?) and normalization; handling OOV/rare tokens (e.g., “000”); selection of N (always 10?); procedures for words not in GloVe; sign handling for eigenvectors; whether eigenvectors are constrained. The paper contains a few inconsistencies/typos in bucket examples that reduce clarity.
- Downstream evaluation is thin: The classification gains are modest and mixed (one setting degrades), no significance tests are provided, and the labeling protocol (who annotated, how many annotators, inter-annotator agreement) is not documented. It is unclear whether the findings generalize beyond these four binary subsets.

Questions to authors
1) SVD bucketing:
- Do you use cosine similarity between normalized word embeddings and columns of V? Do you handle eigenvector sign indeterminacy (e.g., absolute cosine)? Why use the first n eigenvectors specifically? Have you tried using word loadings (U S) or clustering in the low-rank projected space?
2) Reorganization heuristic:
- Please clarify the exact threshold formula with parentheses and rationale. Is the threshold guaranteed non-negative? Provide sensitivity analyses and ablations for the into vs from moves.
3) Scoring and selection:
- Which single scoring variant do you consider the primary metric? Were the reported best-per-dataset variants chosen post hoc? Please report statistical significance (e.g., via bootstrap over topics).
4) Fairness and baselines:
- Can you compare against standard C_v (NPMI-based) and embedding-based coherence measures on the same datasets and report results with a fixed configuration?
5) Domain robustness:
- What happens on Genomics with domain-specific embeddings? Do TBuckets variants close the gap to 0.77?
6) Reproducibility:
- How do you handle OOV topic words, multiword tokens, and token normalization? Will you release code and data splits?

Overall: The idea is appealing and results are promising, but the SVD justification, thresholding heuristic, scoring selection, and evaluation rigor need strengthening. I lean toward rejection in the current form, with a path to acceptance after clarifying and tightening the methodology, comparisons, and statistical analysis.
