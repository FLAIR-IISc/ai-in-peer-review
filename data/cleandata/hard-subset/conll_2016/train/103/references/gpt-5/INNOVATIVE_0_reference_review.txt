Summary and claimed contributions
- Proposes TBuckets, a coherence estimator that mimics human “bucketing” of a topic’s top words into thematic groups using word embeddings. The core hypothesis is that coherent topics yield one dominant bucket.
- Introduces two parameter-free SVD-based variants: TBuckets-SVD (assigns words to the top singular vectors of the topic’s word-embedding matrix) and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a post-hoc reorganization step driven by word–word and word–eigenvector similarities with a dynamic, data-derived threshold).
- Empirically shows higher Pearson correlation with human coherence judgments than Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets (NYT, 20NG), competitive performance of the clustering variant on Genomics, and demonstrates downstream utility by selecting better topic sets for weakly supervised classification.

Strengths
- Conceptually novel, simple, and human-aligned: Turning “one big bucket = coherent topic” into an operational metric is a refreshing, interpretable departure from PMI/NPMI-only approaches. Using SVD directions as sub-theme identifiers is a bold, interesting idea.
- Parameter-free core variants: The SVD-based methods avoid the heavy configuration tuning typical in coherence frameworks (e.g., Röder et al.). This is practically valuable and likely to generalize across datasets without costly calibration.
- Competitive empirical results with minimal machinery: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg improves over Röder et al.’s best-reported settings on two datasets, and the clustering variant is strong on Genomics. Given the simplicity, these are impressive numbers.
- Interpretable diagnostics: Buckets are human-readable groupings of topic words. The examples show how the method surfaces sub-themes and errors (e.g., “girl” in a drink bucket), which can aid topic debugging and selection.
- Downstream impact: The experiment selecting higher-coherence topic sets for annotation improves macro-F1 in 3/4 20NG binary subsets, supporting the practical claim that this coherence metric is useful beyond intrinsic correlation.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity and rigor: Key details are underspecified or ambiguous. For TBuckets-SVD: (i) A is n×d (n words, d-dim vectors), V is d×d; the paper says “use the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers”—please clarify the exact k and why k=n is principled. (ii) Justification for using right singular vectors as bucket “centroids” is intuitive but not theoretically grounded; a baseline like k-means in the embedding space would help contextualize SVD’s benefit. For TBuckets-SVD-Reorg: the threshold formula “AWES − AWPSAWES” lacks parentheses and dimensional clarity; a derivation or ablation is needed. The WL/EVS combination and the multiplication by the square of the top singular value are ad hoc; no ablation shows each term’s contribution.
- Evaluation limitations and missing analyses: Only three datasets with 100 topics each; no statistical significance tests on correlations; no analysis of variance across random seeds. The downstream classification gains are sometimes small; one subset shows S2-Highest5 slightly worse than S1. It would help to report confidence intervals or paired tests, and quantify the correlation–performance relationship.
- Domain mismatch and OOV handling: The poor performance on Genomics likely stems from using general-purpose GloVe embeddings. The paper does not discuss OOV words or how many topic words lack embeddings. A domain-appropriate embedding (or retrofitting) study seems essential to validate generality.
- Sensitivity and robustness: Results depend on the chosen embedding model and dimensionality; this is not explored. The clustering variant’s single hyperparameter is shown in one plot, but there is no broader sensitivity analysis (e.g., linkage choice impact across datasets). For SVD-based methods, robustness to the number of topic words (N=10 here) is unknown.
- Reproducibility and polish: There is no mention of code/data release. Several typos/inconsistencies (e.g., bucket trace typo, formula formatting) hinder clarity. Normalization to [0,1] for TBuckets scores is claimed but not specified, complicating replication.

Questions to authors
- TBuckets-SVD specifics: Given A ∈ R^{n×d}, do you use the top n right singular vectors (columns of V) as bucket identifiers? Why choose k=n buckets rather than learning k from data? Did you try left singular vectors (U) or simply clustering in the span of the top r singular directions?
- Reorganization threshold: Please provide the exact formula with parentheses and rationale. Is it Threshold = AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? How sensitive are results to this construction? Can you share an ablation that removes/varies the threshold?
- Similarity computations: How do you handle OOV words in embeddings? What fraction of topic words are OOV per dataset, especially Genomics? Do domain-specific embeddings materially change the Genomics result?
- Score composition: Why multiply by the square of the largest singular value? Did you try normalizing singular values or removing this factor? Please include an ablation (size vs. WL vs. EVS vs. combinations).
- Statistical testing: Are the gains over Röder et al. significant (e.g., Fisher z-transform tests on Pearson r)? For the classification experiment, can you report variance over runs and significance of S2 vs S1?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and exact configurations (embedding version, token handling, normalization details, clustering params)? This will greatly help the community adopt and scrutinize the approach.

Overall recommendation: Weak accept. The bucketing-by-SVD idea is novel, intuitive, and practically appealing, with strong results on two datasets and a useful downstream demonstration. Addressing the clarity gaps (especially SVD/threshold details and ablations) and the domain-embedding issue would significantly strengthen the paper.
