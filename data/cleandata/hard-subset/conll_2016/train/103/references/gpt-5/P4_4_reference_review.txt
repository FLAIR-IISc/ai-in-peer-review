Summary
This paper addresses automatic evaluation of topic quality (coherence) by grouping the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using word embeddings. The core hypothesis is that a coherent topic yields one dominant bucket (a single theme) plus possibly small residual buckets. Three variants are proposed: (1) TBuckets-Clustering: agglomerative clustering of word embeddings with a maximum-distance threshold; (2) TBuckets-SVD: computing SVD over the topic’s word-embedding matrix and assigning each word to the most similar right singular vector (principal direction), treating these orthogonal directions as sub-theme identifiers; (3) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg: a heuristic reorganization step that moves words into or out of the largest bucket based on word–word and word–eigenvector similarities with a dynamic threshold. Coherence scores are derived from properties of the largest bucket (size, LDA-rank weighting, eigenvector similarity), sometimes weighted by the square of the top singular value. Experiments on 100-topic subsets of NYT, 20NG, and Genomics show Pearson correlations with human ratings competitive with or above the best configurations of Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets. A small application shows that selecting topic runs by TBuckets coherence can improve weakly supervised classification.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, embedding-based bucketing framework for measuring topic coherence, including an SVD-based, parameter-free instantiation and a reorganization heuristic to align buckets with the largest coherent theme.
2) Empirical evidence that the SVD-based methods achieve strong correlations with human judgments, outperforming a state-of-the-art configuration on NYT and 20NG while using no tuned parameters.
3) Demonstration that coherence-based selection of topic runs can improve downstream topic-labeling classification performance on 20NG subsets.

Strengths
- Strong empirical correlations without parameter tuning: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves r=0.837 (NYT) and r=0.869 (20NG), exceeding Röder et al. (2015) best settings (0.806 and 0.859 respectively). The parameter-free nature of SVD/SVD-Reorg reduces the configuration burden compared to frameworks with large search spaces.
- Intuitive and interpretable diagnostic artifacts: Buckets (especially the dominant bucket) are easy to inspect and align well with human intuitions about thematic coherence, which is valuable for qualitative analysis and debugging topic models.
- Practical utility for weakly supervised classification: Selecting runs by TBuckets coherence improved macro-F1 in 3/4 binary 20NG subsets (e.g., PC vs MAC: 66.98 → 68.47 for top-5 coherent runs), and clearly distinguished “best” vs “worst” runs, highlighting usefulness in human-in-the-loop pipelines.
- Simplicity and computational efficiency: Methods rely on off-the-shelf word embeddings and small per-topic computations (10×300 matrices), suggesting scalability and easy adoption. No corpus-wide co-occurrence statistics are required.

Weaknesses
- Limited technical justification and missing details for SVD-based bucketing: Assigning words to the first n right singular vectors (n = number of topic words) is heuristic. There is no analysis of how many meaningful components exist, how sign indeterminacy is handled, or the impact of near-zero singular values. The choice to use principal directions as “bucket identifiers” is not theoretically motivated beyond intuition.
- Reorganization heuristic is under-specified and ad hoc: The threshold formula appears ambiguous (printed as “AWES − AWPSAWES”; likely AWES − AWPS/AWES). The approach mixes word–word and word–eigenvector similarities with minimal justification, and no ablation isolating the effect of each term or the final weighting by the top singular value. Reported gains from reorganization are modest and inconsistent across datasets.
- Evaluation scope and statistical rigor: Results are reported only with Pearson r; no Spearman rank correlations or statistical significance tests (e.g., Fisher r-to-z, bootstrap) are provided for comparisons against baselines and between variants. For TBuckets-Clustering, the parameter (MaxDist) exploration is partially shown but selection criteria and the parenthesized values in Table 1 are unclear.
- Domain generality concerns: Performance drops substantially on Genomics (best r≈0.691 vs 0.773 for Röder), plausibly due to domain mismatch of general-purpose embeddings (GloVe). This undermines the general claim; no domain-adapted embeddings or robustness analysis are provided.
- Clarity and reproducibility issues: Several typographical errors and inconsistencies (e.g., a bucketization example mistakenly shows “storm, weather” in the Afghanistan/Wikileaks topic; unclear equation formatting; missing details on cosine normalization, handling of negative similarities, and exact score normalization). No code or detailed hyperparameter settings (for clustering) are provided, hampering reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
1) SVD bucketing design:
- Why use exactly the first n right singular vectors as bucket identifiers? Did you try selecting K by explained variance, scree test, or cross-validated clustering (e.g., K-means on word embeddings)?
- How do you handle the sign ambiguity of singular vectors and potential negative cosine similarities? Are vectors normalized prior to similarity computations?

2) Reorganization step:
- Please provide the exact threshold formula with parentheses and justify the use of AWES and AWPS. How sensitive are results to this threshold design?
- Could you report an ablation: TBuckets-SVD vs SVD with only “into” moves vs only “from” moves vs full reorg, and the effect of the singular-value weighting?

3) Evaluation:
- Can you report Spearman correlations and statistical significance tests for differences vs the Röder best settings and between your variants?
- For TBuckets-Clustering, what do the parenthesized values in Table 1 denote? How was MaxDist chosen for the reported “best” configurations?

4) Robustness and scope:
- How does performance vary with the number of top-N words (e.g., N=5, 15, 20)? Are results stable across different embedding sets (e.g., word2vec, fastText) and with domain-specific embeddings for Genomics?
- What is the runtime per 100 topics and how does it compare to representative baselines in Röder et al.?

5) Reproducibility:
- Will you release code, the exact GloVe version, and preprocessing details (tokenization, OOV handling) to facilitate replication?

Overall, the approach is simple and promising, with competitive empirical results and practical value, but would benefit from clearer specification, stronger justification for the SVD/reorganization choices, and more rigorous evaluation and analysis.
