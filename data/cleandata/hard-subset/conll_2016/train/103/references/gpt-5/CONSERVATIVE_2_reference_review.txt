Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures based on grouping a topic’s top words into “buckets” using semantic similarity from word embeddings. Three variants are introduced: agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering), SVD-based bucketing (TBuckets-SVD), and SVD followed by a heuristic reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg).
- The authors evaluate by Pearson correlation with human coherence ratings on three datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), claiming SVD-Reorg outperforms the best configurations reported by Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets. They further show a small downstream benefit in a weakly supervised text classification setup when selecting topic models with higher TBuckets scores.

Strengths
- Simple, fast, and broadly applicable: The methods operate on the top-N words per topic and pre-trained embeddings, making them easy to run and interpret. The SVD variant is computationally trivial for N≈10 and does not require complex tuning.
- Competitive correlations on standard datasets: On NYT and 20NG, reported Pearson’s r is slightly higher than the best settings from Röder et al. (2015), with clear, reproducible resources (GloVe).
- Intuitive interpretability: The “largest bucket” intuition mirrors how humans cluster words thematically. The examples show sensible groupings, offering a transparent interpretation of coherence.
- Practical tie-in: The demonstration that selecting topic sets with higher TBuckets scores can improve (slightly) weakly supervised text classification on several 20NG splits suggests potential utility beyond intrinsic evaluation.

Weaknesses
- Limited and inconclusive evaluation rigor: Results are reported on only three datasets with 100 topics each; no statistical significance testing is provided for differences in correlation with respect to prior work. Gains are small (≈0.01–0.03) and could be within noise. There is no robustness analysis across different K, different topic models, or different numbers of top words (N).
- Methodological clarity and soundness concerns:
  - The SVD rationale is underspecified. Using the first n eigenvectors of ATA (feature-feature directions) as “bucket identifiers” and assigning each word to its most similar eigenvector is heuristic and not clearly justified theoretically. It is unclear how sensitive the approach is to d, N, or the geometry of the embedding space.
  - The reorganization step is ad hoc. The threshold formula is ambiguously written (AWES − AWPSAWES vs AWES − AWPS/AWES), and the use of maximum pairwise similarities (rather than averages) may be unstable. This step appears tuned to increase the largest bucket, potentially inflating the score without principled grounding.
  - The final scoring mixes several choices (largest-bucket size, WL, EVS, squared top singular value as weight) without ablations to establish which components matter and why.
- Baselines are not sufficiently comprehensive or controlled:
  - Beyond comparing to “best of” Röder et al. (2015), there is no comparison to strong embedding-based coherence baselines such as average pairwise cosine similarity, centroid similarity, or normalized variants. Given the method’s reliance on embeddings, such baselines are essential.
  - No comparison to simple graph-based bucketing (e.g., connected components under a cosine threshold) or to PCA-based variants; the clustering baseline is only agglomerative with one parameter and does not explore other reasonable clustering paradigms.
- Domain robustness is weak: Performance drops substantially on the Genomics dataset (r≈0.69 vs 0.77 for Röder). This highlights the dependency on general-domain embeddings and undermines the “parameter-free” advantage. The claim is deferred to future work rather than addressed experimentally (e.g., with domain embeddings).
- Downstream application evidence is thin:
  - The text classification improvements are small (e.g., +0.16 to +1.49 macro-F1 on three splits; one split degrades). No variance estimates or significance tests are reported. Details on annotation protocol, annotator agreement, and whether annotators were blinded to run quality are missing. The number of topics per run and topic-labeling budget are not clearly specified.
- “Parameter-free” claim is overstated: The method depends on substantial design choices: number of top words (N=10), embedding model and dimensionality, cosine as similarity, using first n eigenvectors, the precise scoring combination, and the reorganization rules. These are de facto hyperparameters/design knobs even if no numeric thresholds are tuned on validation data.
- Reproducibility issues: Important implementation details are missing (exact preprocessing, handling OOV words, what happens when n>d, seed control, code availability). Some formulas and references are poorly formatted, which complicates replication.

Questions to authors
- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the exact formula. Is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? How sensitive are results to this choice? Provide an ablation removing reorg and varying the threshold design.
- SVD details: Why use the first n eigenvectors of ATA as bucket identifiers? Did you try PCA on the word vectors restricted to topic words and using components from U instead? How does performance vary with the number of components/buckets? What happens if multiple words map to the same eigenvector vs distributing across many?
- Scoring ablations: Report correlations for size-only, WL-only, EVS-only, and with/without squaring the largest singular value. Which component drives the gains? Provide confidence intervals or permutation tests for differences vs Röder’s best.
- Baseline coverage: How does TBuckets compare to average pairwise cosine among topic words, centroid similarity, and a simple graph thresholding baseline? These are natural embedding-based alternatives with minimal parameters.
- Robustness to N and embeddings: How do results change for N=5/15/20? For different embedding sources (word2vec, fastText; in-domain embeddings for Genomics)? Can domain-specific embeddings close the Genomics gap?
- Downstream setup: How many topics per run? How many annotators per run, agreement metrics, and were annotators blinded to run order/quality? Provide variance over multiple repetitions and statistical tests for macro-F1 differences.

Overall recommendation: The idea is intuitive and results are competitive on two datasets, but the evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous, key algorithmic choices are heuristic and under-explained, and baselines/ablations are lacking. I would lean toward rejection in the current form; a revised version with clearer methodology, stronger baselines, ablations, significance testing, and domain-robustness experiments would be more compelling.
