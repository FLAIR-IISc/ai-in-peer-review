Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using semantic similarity from pre-trained word embeddings. The main idea is that coherent topics yield one dominant bucket.
- Three instantiations are presented: (i) TBuckets-Clustering via agglomerative clustering of word vectors; (ii) TBuckets-SVD, which assigns words to buckets represented by principal axes (right singular vectors) of the topic’s word-embedding matrix; (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, which post-processes SVD buckets by moving words into or out of the largest bucket using a dynamic threshold based on average word-word and word-eigenvector similarities.
- The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation with human coherence judgments on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics), claiming to outperform the best configurations reported by Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets. A small application shows that selecting more “coherent” topic sets can improve weakly supervised text classification.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive framing: The “bucket” metaphor closely mirrors how humans assess topic interpretability. Reducing coherence to dominance of a single semantic cluster is easy to reason about and inspect, and bucket assignments can be audited.
- Embedding-based operationalization: Leveraging distributional semantics avoids expensive co-occurrence counting and can capture semantic relatedness beyond raw co-document frequencies. The use of off-the-shelf GloVe makes the approach practical.
- SVD-based variants are largely parameter-free: TBuckets-SVD and the dynamic-threshold reorganization avoid per-dataset tuning, which is appealing compared to prior work (e.g., the configuration space in Röder et al.).
- Empirical performance: On NYT and 20NG, SVD-Reorg shows higher Pearson r than the best settings in Röder et al. The clustering variant is competitive on Genomics, suggesting the paradigm is not brittle.
- Practical utility: The application to topic labeling for classification, while modest, provides evidence that coherence scores can be used to select better topic sets for annotation, which aligns with real workflows.

Weaknesses
- Methodological ambiguities and theoretical gaps in SVD assignment: Assigning words to buckets via cosine similarity to right singular vectors (principal axes) is unconventional and under-justified. Principal axes are directions capturing variance, not cluster prototypes. Important details are missing: normalization/centering of embeddings before SVD, handling of eigenvector sign indeterminacy, whether cosine similarities can be negative, and why “first n eigenvectors” is the right choice when topics have n words but embedding dimensionality is much larger. A more principled approach (e.g., clustering in the principal subspace, eigengap-based determination of bucket count) would strengthen the method.
- Reorganization rule lacks rigor and clarity: The dynamic threshold formula (AWES − AWPS/AWES) is insufficiently motivated, has unclear parentheses, and could be negative. The algorithmic procedure (one-pass vs iterative, convergence criteria, tie-breaking) is unspecified. Without ablations showing sensitivity to this threshold and to the number of reorg passes, it is hard to assess robustness.
- Overemphasis on largest bucket may penalize legitimate multi-facet but still coherent topics: Coherence is reduced to the mass in the largest bucket. Human judgments can consider topics coherent even with two balanced sub-themes (e.g., “economy and politics” in news). The proposed scoring will systematically under-rate such topics, yet the paper does not analyze failure modes or present per-topic qualitative comparisons against human labels to validate this assumption.
- Evaluation limitations and statistical analysis: The paper reports Pearson correlations but no statistical significance tests for dependent correlations (e.g., Williams/Steiger tests) to support claims of outperforming Röder et al. Differences (e.g., 0.869 vs 0.859) may not be significant on 100 topics. Spearman rank correlation is not reported, though it is standard for coherence. It is unclear whether Röder’s baselines were recomputed under the same corpora/resources or copied from the paper; this impacts fairness.
- Dependence on external embeddings and domain mismatch: Results on Genomics degrade for SVD variants, likely due to mismatched embeddings (Wikipedia/Gigaword). This undercuts the “parameter-free” claim; choice of embedding model/domain is a key hyperparameter. No sensitivity analyses across embedding models (word2vec, fastText), dimensionalities, or fine-tuning are reported.
- Reproducibility and clarity issues: Many details are missing (e.g., exact distance thresholds for clustering, whether word vectors were normalized for cosine, weighting by top singular value squared rationale, runtime). There are textual errors (e.g., an example bucket mistakenly includes “storm, weather” in the Afghanistan topic), and references are inconsistently formatted. No code is provided.

Questions to authors
- SVD details: Were word vectors centered/standardized before SVD? Are similarities computed with normalized vectors? How is the sign ambiguity of eigenvectors handled when using cosine similarity for assignment? Do you take absolute cosine? Why use exactly the first n eigenvectors (n = number of topic words) instead of selecting k by explained variance or eigengap?
- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the threshold formula unambiguously (parentheses) and justify its form. Can it be negative? How many reorganization passes do you perform, and how do you decide convergence? Any sensitivity analysis?
- Scoring rationale: Why weight coherence by the square of the top singular value? Does this overweight topics with high variance irrespective of coherence? Have you tried centering and removing the first component (often a mean/“common” direction) before scoring?
- Evaluation protocol: Did you recompute Röder et al.’s best configurations on the same datasets/corpora or reuse published numbers? Please report Spearman correlations and statistical significance for improvements over baselines. Also report variability across random seeds for TBuckets-Clustering.
- Domain embeddings: Can you provide results with domain-specific embeddings for Genomics? How sensitive are results to the embedding model and dimension?
- Failure analysis: Provide qualitative examples where TBuckets-SVD-Reorg disagrees with human ratings, especially for topics with two sub-themes that humans deem coherent.

Overall recommendation: Borderline reject. The core idea is appealing and results are promising, but key methodological choices are under-justified, important algorithmic details are missing, and the evaluation lacks significance testing and robustness analyses. With clearer algorithm specification, stronger theoretical grounding for the SVD-based assignments/reorganization, significance tests, and sensitivity analyses (especially wrt embeddings), this work could be substantially strengthened.
