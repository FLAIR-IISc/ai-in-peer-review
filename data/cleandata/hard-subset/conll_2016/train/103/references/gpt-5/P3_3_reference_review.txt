Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic-coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using distributional semantics, with the hypothesis that coherent topics yield a single dominant bucket. Three instantiations are presented: (i) TBuckets-Clustering via agglomerative clustering of word embeddings; (ii) TBuckets-SVD, which uses SVD over the topic’s word-embedding matrix and assigns words to principal directions; and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, which heuristically reorganizes bucket assignments using a dynamic threshold based on intra-topic similarities.
- The authors evaluate by Pearson correlation with human coherence ratings on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics), reporting improvements over best settings from Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets, with no hyperparameter tuning for SVD-based variants. They also demonstrate a downstream application: using TBuckets coherence to select topic runs for weakly supervised text classification, showing modest gains.
- As I see it, the main contributions are: (1) a simple, embedding-based bucketing scheme for topic coherence that emphasizes the size/quality of the largest bucket; (2) an SVD-based variant with a “parameter-free” reorganization heuristic; (3) an empirical study against a popular coherence benchmark and a small application study for topic-labeling-based classification.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and interpretability. The bucket lens provides an intuitive diagnostic of topic coherence that aligns with how humans often reason about topics. The focus on the largest bucket is a clear, testable proxy for single-theme coherence.
- Efficient, lightweight, and easy to reproduce in principle. Computing SVD on a 10×300 matrix per topic and cosine similarities is trivial computationally. The method uses standard, publicly available GloVe embeddings and does not require external co-occurrence corpora or complex pipelines.
- Competitive empirical results. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg matches or exceeds the best correlations reported by Röder et al. (2015) on NYT and 20NG, and clustering performs on par on Genomics. Even if margins are small, this places the approach among strong baselines.
- Downstream utility. Selecting topic runs for annotation by TBuckets coherence improves or matches weakly supervised classification performance in 3/4 subsets and shows a clear gap between “best 5” vs “worst 5” runs on harder tasks (e.g., POLITICS vs SCIENCE), supporting the practical value of coherence-guided selection.
- No manual tuning for SVD-based variants. Avoiding per-dataset parameter sweeps is appealing given coherence evaluation’s sensitivity and expense.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity and justification. The SVD bucketing step needs more rigorous grounding. With A∈R^{n×d} (n words, d=300), V contains eigenvectors of A^T A (feature space). The paper assigns each word vector to the most similar column of V (first n eigenvectors). This effectively buckets words by principal axes, but (a) the sign and magnitude of loadings are ignored; (b) orthogonal axes need not correspond to semantic sub-themes; and (c) taking “first n” axes is arbitrary when d≫n. A formal argument or empirical analysis (e.g., projections/loadings, sign handling, ablation on number of axes) is needed.
- Reorganization heuristic appears ad hoc and is underspecified. The threshold formula is written as AWES − AWPSAWES, which is ambiguous (is it AWES − AWPS/AWES?). Its range, stability, and sensitivity are not analyzed. The conditions compare word–eigenvector and word–word similarities on different scales without calibration beyond the heuristic subtraction. Without ablations (into-only vs from-only) or sensitivity analysis, it’s hard to assess robustness.
- Comparisons and significance. The paper compares against the best numbers reported in Röder et al. (2015) but does not re-run strong modern baselines on the same topics, notably C_v, C_npmi, and embedding-based measures (e.g., w2v-based coherence) that are now standard. Reported gains are small (e.g., +0.01 on 20NG), with no confidence intervals or significance testing; differences may not be statistically meaningful given 100 topics/dataset.
- Potential leakage and tuning in clustering. Table 1 reports correlations for TBuckets-Clustering at specific “Max Dist” values (in parentheses). It appears these were selected post hoc to maximize correlation on the same human labels used for evaluation. If so, this overestimates performance. A proper dev/test split or nested CV is needed; otherwise, the “parameter-free” advantage of SVD variants further undermines the fairness of this comparison.
- Domain dependence and OOV handling. Genomics underperforms, likely due to out-of-domain embeddings. The paper acknowledges this but does not specify OOV handling, token normalization, or stopword control. Since embedding coverage strongly influences bucketing, reproducibility and generality require these details and experiments with domain-specific embeddings.
- Limited evaluation breadth. Only Pearson r is reported; Spearman ρ is often preferred for ranking coherence. No per-topic error analysis or qualitative failure modes are given beyond a few examples. Generalization to other topic models (e.g., NMF, neural topic models) and to different N (top-5/top-20 words) is not explored.
- Text classification study lacks controls. While promising, the selection strategy is not accompanied by statistical tests, annotator effort/time, or inter-annotator agreement. The improvement over S1 is modest in 2/4 cases and reversed in one (POLITICS vs RELIGION). It’s unclear how many topics per run were labeled and whether selection truly reduces annotation cost.

Questions to authors
- SVD assignment details: Do you use cosine similarity between word vectors and columns of V (which are orthonormal in R^d)? How do you handle the sign of principal axes and words with strong negative loadings? Why choose exactly the first n axes rather than tuning k≤n or using explained variance?
- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the exact formula (parentheses) and its range. How sensitive are results to this thresholding scheme? Can you report ablations for into-only, from-only, and no-threshold variants?
- Baselines: Did you re-compute C_v, C_npmi, and word-embedding-based coherence baselines on the same topics and corpora? If not, can you add these with confidence intervals and significance tests vs TBuckets?
- Clustering parameter selection: Were Max Dist values chosen on a held-out dev set, or optimized on the test labels? If the latter, can you re-run with proper tuning separation?
- Embedding coverage: What fraction of topic tokens were OOV per dataset, and how were OOVs handled? Did you try domain-specific embeddings for Genomics, and if so, how did results change?
- Robustness: How do results vary with the number of top words (e.g., 5/15/20)? Do Spearman correlations tell a consistent story?

Additional comments
- Consider evaluating with contextualized word representations aggregated to type embeddings (e.g., average SIF, or static projections from BERT) and comparing to GloVe.
- Provide code and data splits to aid reproducibility.
- Several typos and inconsistencies in examples (e.g., “Word Seen: official. Bucket-1: {storm, weather}”) should be corrected for clarity.
