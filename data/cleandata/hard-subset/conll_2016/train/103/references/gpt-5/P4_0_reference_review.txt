Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic-coherence measures that group a topic’s top words into “buckets” of semantically related words using distributional word vectors. The core intuition is that coherent topics yield one dominant bucket (single theme), while incoherent topics split into multiple medium-sized buckets. Three variants are introduced: (i) TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering on word embeddings with a distance threshold), (ii) TBuckets-SVD (using right singular vectors of the topic word-embedding matrix as orthogonal “sub-theme” directions and assigning words to the most similar direction), and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a parameter-free reorganization step that moves words into/from the largest bucket using a dynamic threshold derived from average word–word and word–eigenvector similarities). Topic coherence is scored primarily from the largest bucket (size and optionally word-order and eigenvector-similarity weights). The methods are evaluated via Pearson correlation with human judgments on three datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), compared against best settings from Röder et al. (2015), and a small downstream experiment demonstrates utility for selecting topic-model runs for weakly supervised text classification.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A simple, largely parameter-free SVD-based bucketing method with an automatic reorganization criterion to produce a coherence score based on the largest bucket. This is the main technical contribution.
2) Empirical evaluation showing competitive or improved correlation with human topic coherence on two of three datasets relative to Röder et al. (2015), and analysis of a clustering variant.
3) A practical use case showing that selecting topic-model runs by TBuckets scores can improve weakly supervised classification performance.

Strengths
- Practical, parameter-free SVD variant with clear intuition: The SVD-based approach requires no hyperparameter tuning, in contrast to many coherence measures that depend on corpus choices, window sizes, and normalization. The reorganization threshold is derived from topic statistics (AWPS/AWES), avoiding global tuning. This makes the method attractive for practitioners.

- Competitive empirical results with state of the art: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves higher Pearson’s r than Röder et al. (2015) on NYT (0.837 vs. 0.806) and 20NG (up to 0.869 vs. 0.859), while being parameter-free. Even the naive SVD variant is competitive (e.g., WL 0.772 on NYT and 0.868 on 20NG). These results support the central intuition that a dominant bucket indicates coherence.

- Efficiency and simplicity: Operating on only the top topic words (n=10) with 300d embeddings makes SVD and pairwise similarity computations trivial at scale. The method does not require external count statistics or large reference corpora for PMI/NPMI, which simplifies deployment.

- Utility in downstream selection of topic runs: The weakly supervised classification experiment illustrates that selecting topic runs by TBuckets coherence can materially affect performance (e.g., POLITICS vs SCIENCE macro-F1 96.19 for top-5 coherence vs 91.09 for bottom-5), highlighting practical value beyond intrinsic correlation.

Weaknesses
- Insufficient technical justification and clarity for the SVD bucketing mechanism: The method uses V (right singular vectors of AᵀA) as bucket “directions” and assigns each word to the most similar eigenvector. The rationale for using V instead of U, the number of eigenvectors taken (“first n eigenvectors” when n is the number of words), and how cosine similarity to eigenvectors yields meaningful clusters are not theoretically justified. Important implementation details are missing: whether embeddings and eigenvectors are normalized before cosine similarity, whether d>n handling affects bucket counts, and whether sign indeterminacy of eigenvectors matters. These gaps hinder reproducibility and raise questions about soundness.

- Evaluation limitations and lack of statistical rigor: Improvements over Röder et al. (2015) are modest on 20NG (e.g., 0.869 vs. 0.859) and there is a substantial drop on Genomics (e.g., 0.691 vs. 0.773). No statistical significance testing or confidence intervals are reported, so it is unclear whether differences are meaningful. Moreover, only the “best-performing settings” from Röder et al. are used as a single baseline; more comprehensive baselines (e.g., NPMI-based measures, embedding-based measures from Aletras & Stevenson) are not reproduced under the same setup, limiting comparative strength.

- Potential tuning on test data for clustering variant: In Table 1, the clustering results include values in parentheses that appear to be the max-distance threshold (e.g., 0.63, 0.7), suggesting per-dataset tuning. If tuned against the same test sets used for reporting correlations, this risks overfitting and inflates performance claims for TBuckets-Clustering.

- Ambiguities and errors in exposition: There are typographical errors (e.g., bucket traces with incorrect words), unclear formulas (the threshold “AWES − AWPSAWES” lacks parentheses; presumably AWES − AWPS/AWES), and inconsistent notation. The weighting of scores by the square of the largest singular value is asserted without ablation or justification. Such issues reduce clarity and make it hard to verify or reimplement the approach.

- Limited analysis of failure cases and domain mismatch: Performance is notably lower on Genomics. The paper acknowledges domain-specific embeddings might help but does not analyze out-of-vocabulary handling, coverage of GloVe on technical terms, or provide ablations with domain-trained embeddings. A deeper error analysis would clarify when the bucket approach struggles (e.g., polysemy, named entities, mixed themes).

Questions to authors
- SVD bucketing details: Please clarify (i) the exact shapes and normalization (row/column) of A, U, S, V, (ii) whether you use full or truncated SVD, (iii) why V (feature space) is preferred over U (entity/topic-word space) for defining buckets, (iv) how many eigenvectors are actually used when d >> n, and (v) whether vectors are normalized before cosine computations. How do you address eigenvector sign indeterminacy?

- Reorganization threshold: Please provide the exact formula with parentheses and units. Is the intended threshold AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? How sensitive are results to this form? Any ablation comparing fixed thresholds vs the proposed dynamic one?

- Clustering variant tuning: How were max-distance thresholds selected? Were they tuned per dataset on the same test sets used for reporting Pearson’s r, or via a separate validation split? Please clarify to rule out selection bias.

- Baselines and significance: Can you include additional baselines (e.g., NPMI-based coherence, the distributional semantics approach of Aletras & Stevenson) computed under your setup with GloVe, and report statistical significance or confidence intervals for Pearson correlations?

- OOV and domain coverage: How many topic words lacked embeddings per dataset, and how were they handled? Have you tried domain-specific embeddings for Genomics, and if so, how did performance change?

Overall, the paper presents a simple, implementable idea with promising results and practical appeal. Strengthening technical justification, clarifying details, and expanding the comparative and statistical evaluation would substantially improve the submission.
