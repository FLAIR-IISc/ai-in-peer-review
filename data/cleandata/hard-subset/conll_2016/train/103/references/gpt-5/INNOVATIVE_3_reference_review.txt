Summary and contributions
- Introduces TBuckets, a conceptually simple, human-aligned approach to topic quality that groups a topic’s top words into “buckets” and scores coherence by how much mass concentrates in the largest bucket. This is a new direction compared to PMI/NPMI-style measures and distributional-centroid scores.
- Proposes an SVD-based, parameter-free mechanism to induce buckets from word embeddings by assigning words to the most similar eigenvector directions, plus an automatic “reorganization” step with a dynamic threshold to correct misassignments. Empirically, this variant outperforms the best configurations in Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets.
- Demonstrates downstream utility: using TBuckets to select higher-quality topic sets for weakly supervised text classification yields consistent gains on several 20NG subsets.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and interpretability: The “largest-bucket principle” is an appealing, human-intuitive reframing of topic coherence that differs from pairwise PMI/NPMI and centroid-based measures. The SVD-based derivation of orthogonal sub-themes as bucket identifiers is a bold, fresh angle. The produced buckets are themselves interpretable artifacts that facilitate error analysis, which is rare among coherence measures.
- Parameter-free SVD variant with strong results: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is essentially parameter-free (given fixed embeddings) yet matches or exceeds a heavily tuned state-of-the-art configuration space. The improvements on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and competitiveness on 20NG are meaningful given the simplicity and generality of the method.
- Practical downstream impact: Using TBuckets to triage multiple topic-model runs for labeling leads to better classification macro-F1 in 3/4 tasks, and the gap between “best 5” and “worst 5” coherence sets is substantial on the harder PC vs. MAC setup. This is a compelling, concrete payoff for practitioners.
- Clear problem framing and thorough variant exploration: The paper articulates three realizations (clustering, SVD, SVD+reorg) and reports multiple scoring variants (size, WL, EVS), making it easy to see where performance comes from. The clustering variant’s single-parameter exploration and sensitivity figure add credibility.
- Reproducibility prospects: The approach relies on widely available embeddings and standard linear algebra; per-topic computation is trivial. Given the description, reproducing the main results seems feasible.

Weaknesses
- SVD formulation is underspecified and potentially misaligned: The choice to use V (feature eigenvectors of A^T A) as bucket identifiers for words is counterintuitive; U (left singular vectors) directly spans the word (entity) space and would more naturally support clustering/assignment of rows. The paper should justify why V is preferable and clarify the exact similarity computation (cosine vs dot, normalization). Without this, the core mechanism feels heuristic rather than principled.
- Reorganization heuristic lacks clarity and ablation: The dynamic threshold formula is ambiguously written (AWES − AWPSAWES; likely AWES − AWPS/AWES) and its behavior is not analyzed. There is no ablation showing the separate benefit of “into” and “from” steps or the effect of the threshold magnitude. Given this step drives the best results, more rigor (or at least clarity) is needed.
- Dependence on general-purpose embeddings harms domain transfer: The method underperforms on Genomics, likely due to domain mismatch and OOV issues, but there is no analysis of coverage, OOV handling, or experiments with domain-specific embeddings. Since embeddings are the sole semantic backbone, robustness to embedding choice should be examined.
- Limited comparative analysis and significance testing: The comparison is to the best numbers in Röder et al. (2015) but lacks statistical significance testing, confidence intervals, or re-running key baselines under identical settings. Additional simple baselines (e.g., average pairwise cosine similarity, clustering with a standard model order selection, or U-based SVD assignment) would contextualize gains.
- Minor clarity/quality issues: A few examples contain apparent typos or copy-paste errors (e.g., “Word Seen: official. Bucket-1: {storm, weather}”). The scoring section mixes several weighting schemes (size, WL, EVS, singular value weighting) without a clear motivation or theoretical link; it reads as ad hoc. Some implementation details are missing (e.g., word normalization, cosine vs. raw dot, handling of sign ambiguity in SVD components).

Questions to authors
- SVD choice and assignment: Why use V instead of U for bucket identification? Did you test U-based assignment or clustering in the U-space? How do you handle sign indeterminacy of singular vectors, and do you normalize vectors before cosine similarity?
- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the exact formula (parentheses) and give intuition for its scale. How sensitive are results to this threshold’s form? Can you report ablations for “into only,” “from only,” and “no reorg” on the two strongest datasets?
- Embedding dependence: What is the OOV rate on each dataset? How do you handle OOV words? Can you report results with domain-specific embeddings for Genomics and with different general embeddings (e.g., word2vec, fastText) to assess robustness?
- Scoring components: You sometimes multiply scores by the square of the top singular value. Why square, and why only the top singular value? Could you report correlations for each component and combinations with significance tests?
- Baselines and fairness: Were Röder et al. numbers recomputed with your exact word lists and preprocessing? If not, can you re-run a strong NPMI baseline under identical conditions for a fair comparison?

Overall, this is a creative and promising direction with tangible benefits and competitive performance. With clearer justification of the SVD mechanics and the reorganization step, plus modest robustness analyses, it would be a strong contribution. I lean positive given the novelty and practicality.
