Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, a bucket-based perspective on topic coherence: given the top-N words of a topic and pretrained word embeddings, words are grouped into “buckets” representing sub-themes; coherence is then a function of the largest bucket. Three instantiations are explored: (i) agglomerative clustering with a distance threshold, (ii) an SVD-based assignment that uses the principal directions (V) of the topic word-embedding matrix as bucket identifiers, and (iii) a parameter-free “reorganization” step that moves words into/out of the largest bucket using a dynamic threshold derived from intra-topic similarities. The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation with human judgments on 20NG, NYT, and Genomics, and applied to topic-labeled weakly supervised text classification, where selecting topic runs with higher TBuckets scores improves downstream performance.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A novel, conceptually simple, parameter-free SVD-based bucketing paradigm (with dynamic reorganization) for topic coherence that reframes coherence as concentration into a dominant semantic cluster.
2) Empirical evidence that this approach is competitive or superior to state-of-the-art coherence (Röder et al., 2015) on two of three datasets, with practical downstream utility in topic-labeling-based classification.
3) A minimal-parameter alternative (particularly TBuckets-SVD/-SVD-Reorg) to heavily tuned coherence frameworks, with strong interpretability via the largest-bucket rationale.

Strengths
- Novel conceptual framing: The “bucketization” view, operationalized by aligning topic words to orthogonal directions (V) of the topic’s embedding matrix and then reorganizing, is a fresh angle on coherence compared to PMI/NPMI and prior distributional similarity approaches. Using eigenvectors as sub-theme identifiers is an interesting and bold idea.
- Parameter-free core with simple computation: The SVD variants avoid hyperparameter tuning, yet show strong performance. With only 10 words and 300-d embeddings, the method is computationally trivial, making it appealing for practical pipelines.
- Competitive to SOTA with clear wins: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg improves Pearson r over Röder et al. (2015) on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859), while remaining reasonable on Genomics. The improvement on NYT is sizable; the 20NG improvement, though modest, is notable given no tuning.
- Transparent interpretability: Reporting actual buckets (Table 2) demonstrates that the method captures intuitive sub-themes and that the largest bucket is often the “core meaning,” aligning with human inspection. The reorganization step corrects reasonable misassignments (e.g., news/fox/series folded into the TV bucket).
- Downstream utility: Selecting topic runs by TBuckets scores improves topic-labeled classification in 3/4 20NG subsets and clearly separates “highest” vs “lowest” coherence runs on harder tasks (e.g., PC vs MAC), supporting the method’s practical impact beyond intrinsic correlation.

Weaknesses
- Limited and uneven evaluation: Performance on Genomics is clearly worse (e.g., 0.691 vs 0.773). This appears due to domain mismatch of GloVe embeddings, but the paper does not quantify OOV rates or embedding coverage per dataset. A more rigorous evaluation with domain-specific embeddings (or at least coverage stats) is needed.
- Lack of statistical testing and stronger baselines: Differences in Pearson r (e.g., +0.010 on 20NG) are not tested for significance. Also, comparison does not include widely used c_v (from Röder’s framework) instantiated with modern corpora, or other embedding-based coherence measures beyond Aletras & Stevenson. This weakens the claim of outperforming SOTA.
- Ad hoc aspects and insufficient ablations: The dynamic threshold for reorganization (AWES − AWPS/AWES) is heuristic and under-justified; its sensitivity and potential negativity/scale issues are not analyzed. Ablations are missing: SVD vs clustering; SVD without reorg; U vs V; using absolute cosine to mitigate eigenvector sign; effect of weighting by squared top singular value; number of top words (N) beyond 10.
- Methodological clarity issues: Several details are ambiguous or error-prone: (i) “use the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers” where n is the number of words—why n, given d eigenvectors exist? (ii) tie-breaking when multiple eigenvectors are similarly close; (iii) normalization/sign of eigenvectors; (iv) assignment similarity function and whether absolute cosine is used; (v) what happens with ties for the largest bucket. There are also apparent typos in the bucket dry run (e.g., “Word Seen: official. Bucket-1: {storm, weather}”).
- Scope and generality: Only top-10 words are used; many coherence metrics consider sensitivity to N. It remains unclear if TBuckets is robust across N, across embedding families (word2vec, fastText), and to OOV handling. Code is not provided, limiting reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Significance: Are the improvements over Röder et al. statistically significant (e.g., using Williams test for dependent correlations)? Please report p-values, confidence intervals, and variance across topics.
- Coverage/OOV: What fraction of topic words are OOV for each dataset, and how are OOV words handled? Can you report results with domain-specific embeddings for Genomics?
- Algorithmic details: 
  • Why choose the first n eigenvectors of V as bucket identifiers? Did you try using U’s columns (entity space) or a learned k < n? 
  • Do you normalize eigenvectors and use cosine similarity? Do you use absolute cosine to address sign indeterminacy? How are ties resolved?
  • How many effective buckets typically result (distribution of bucket counts and sizes)?
- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the formula (parentheses), its range, and provide an ablation varying this threshold or replacing it with a learned margin. How sensitive are results to this component?
- Robustness: How does performance vary with the number of top words (e.g., N=10/15/20) and with different embedding types? Can you include c_v and other embedding-based baselines from the Röder framework for a stronger comparison?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and scripts to reproduce the reported results, including the text classification pipeline?

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. Despite some methodological rough edges and limited analysis, the bucketing-by-SVD perspective is a fresh and appealingly simple idea that yields competitive or better correlations with no tuning, and it demonstrates downstream value. With clarifications, significance testing, and a few additional baselines/ablations, this could be a solid and influential contribution to topic coherence measurement.
