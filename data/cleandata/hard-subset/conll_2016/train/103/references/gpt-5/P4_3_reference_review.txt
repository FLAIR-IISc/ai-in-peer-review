Summary:
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group a topic’s top words into “buckets” representing sub-themes. The central hypothesis is that coherent topics produce one large bucket (dominant theme) plus a few small ones, whereas incoherent topics split into several comparable buckets. Three instantiations are presented: (i) TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering of word embeddings), (ii) TBuckets-SVD (using SVD on the word-embedding matrix and assigning words to directions/eigenvectors), and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a parameter-free reorganization heuristic that moves words into or out of the largest bucket using similarity-based criteria with a dynamically computed threshold). Topic coherence is then scored primarily from the largest bucket (size, LDA word-order weights, and eigenvector similarity), with SVD variants additionally weighted by the top singular value. On three datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), Pearson’s r correlations with human ratings are compared to the best settings from Röder et al. (2015). TBuckets-SVD-Reorg slightly outperforms the state-of-the-art on NYT and 20NG, but trails on Genomics. The paper also illustrates utility for weakly supervised text classification by selecting runs with higher TBuckets coherence before topic labeling.

Main contributions (as assessed):
1) A new coherence-measure family based on bucketizing topic words using word embeddings, including a novel SVD-based assignment plus a parameter-free reorganization heuristic focusing on the largest bucket.
2) Empirical evidence that the SVD-based TBuckets variant correlates competitively or better with human judgments on two standard datasets, while being essentially parameter-free, and a small downstream case study showing that selecting high-coherence topic sets can improve weakly supervised classification.

Strengths:
- Clear, intuitive core idea: The “largest-bucket dominance” hypothesis offers a human-interpretable signal of topic coherence. Examples make the intuition concrete, and the approach aligns with how readers assess topical consistency.
- Competitive empirical results with minimal tuning: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg attains higher Pearson correlations than the best Röder et al. configuration on NYT and 20NG, with no dataset-specific parameter tuning. This is practically valuable given the large configuration/tuning space in prior frameworks.
- Sensible use of distributional semantics: Leveraging off-the-shelf embeddings is a simple, widely applicable way to encode semantic relatedness, avoiding dependence on external corpus co-occurrence statistics or taxonomies.
- Practical utility demonstrated: The weakly supervised classification experiment, though limited, indicates that filtering topic runs by TBuckets coherence can reduce annotator burden and improve or maintain accuracy on several splits.
- Computationally lightweight: Operating on 10 words × 300 dimensions per topic makes the clustering and SVD steps inexpensive, making the method easy to deploy.

Weaknesses:
- Incomplete technical justification and clarity for SVD design choices: The method assigns words to the most similar eigenvector from V (feature space) and uses the first n eigenvectors as “bucket identifiers,” where n is the number of topic words. The rationale for using V instead of U (entity space), the choice of exactly n eigenvectors, and the implications of rank and spectral decay are not theoretically motivated. This weakens the technical soundness of the SVD variant.
- Heuristic reorganization lacks formal grounding and has unclear threshold formula: The “into/from largest bucket” rules are intuitive but ad hoc. The dynamic threshold is defined as AWES − AWPSAWES (ambiguous missing operator/parentheses), and no derivation or sensitivity analysis is provided. Claims of being “parameter free” are somewhat offset by these heuristic design choices and the weighting by the square of the leading singular value, which is also not justified.
- Evaluation limitations and missing significance tests: Correlations are reported on three datasets of 100 topics each, with no statistical tests comparing TBuckets and Röder et al. results (e.g., Steiger’s test on dependent correlations). The performance on Genomics is notably worse than Röder’s best, and the paper does not probe causes (e.g., OOV rates, domain mismatch in embeddings). The classification case study is small and shows mixed results (one split slightly degrades), with no confidence intervals.
- Reproducibility gaps and underspecified details: For TBuckets-Clustering, the distance metric (cosine vs Euclidean), linkage choice per result, and how Max Dist maps to the reported parentheses values are not clearly documented. For SVD methods, the exact normalization and similarity computations, and handling of OOV words are not specified. No code or release plan is mentioned.
- Minor issues with clarity and consistency: There are typographical errors in bucket walkthroughs and formulas; some notation is inconsistent; and several references contain formatting errors. These detract from clarity and could hinder replication.

Questions to authors:
- SVD design choices:
  - Why use eigenvectors from V (feature space) rather than U (entity space), given that U captures directions among the words themselves? Did you compare the two?
  - Why restrict to the first n eigenvectors (n = number of words), as opposed to selecting a number based on explained variance or rank? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Reorganization threshold:
  - Please clarify the exact formula (is it AWES − AWPS/AWES?). What is the rationale behind this form, and how sensitive are results to this threshold? Any ablations without reorg or with alternative thresholds?
- Similarity and clustering details:
  - What exact similarity/distance metric is used throughout (cosine vs Euclidean)? For TBuckets-Clustering, how are the Max Dist values chosen per dataset, and what do the numbers in parentheses in Table 1 represent?
- Fairness and significance:
  - Are Röder et al. (2015) best-setting correlations reproduced under your environment, or taken from the paper? Did you conduct statistical tests on correlation differences on the same topic sets?
- Embedding dependence and domain mismatch:
  - What is the OOV rate per dataset with GloVe? Do domain-specific embeddings (e.g., biomedical) improve Genomics results? Any experiments varying embedding sources or dimensions?
- Scoring design:
  - Why weight TBuckets-SVD(-Reorg) scores by the square of the top singular value? Does this improve correlation consistently? Could it bias toward topics with general high-variance words?
- Application study:
  - In Table 3, one split shows a small degradation when selecting highest coherence sets. Can you provide variance/confidence intervals across runs to assess robustness? How many topics per class were labeled, and what was annotator effort saved?
