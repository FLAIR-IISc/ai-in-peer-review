Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic-coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using word embeddings. The central hypothesis is that coherent topics form a single large bucket. The authors instantiate this via:
- TBuckets-Clustering: agglomerative clustering of word embeddings with a distance threshold.
- TBuckets-SVD: SVD on the topic word embedding matrix; assign each word to the most similar right singular vector; coherence derives mainly from the size/weighted size of the largest bucket.
- TBuckets-SVD-Reorg: a “parameter-free” reorganization step moving words into/out of the largest bucket using a dynamic threshold computed from word–word and word–eigenvector similarities.

They evaluate by Pearson correlation with human coherence scores on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics; 100 topics each), and report improvements over Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets. They also demonstrate a weakly supervised text classification use-case by selecting topic sets with higher TBuckets coherence.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, embedding-based bucketization approach for topic coherence that reduces to measuring the size/weighted size of the largest cluster among topic words.
2) An SVD-based variant with a reorganization heuristic meant to be “parameter-free,” claiming competitive or superior correlation with human judgments vs. C_v-style measures.
3) A small application showing that selecting more “coherent” topic sets can yield modest improvements in weakly supervised classification.

Strengths
- Practical simplicity and interpretability: The largest-bucket notion is intuitive and easy to compute. SVD on a 10×300 matrix is cheap; the methods are usable in practice.
- Competitive correlations on standard benchmarks: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg matches or exceeds Röder et al. (2015) on two datasets (NYT, 20NG). Even small gains over strong baselines are not trivial in this space.
- Minimal tuning for SVD variants: The SVD-based methods do not depend on window sizes or external corpora statistics, which can be attractive when those resources are unavailable or domain-mismatched.
- Some analysis of clustering sensitivity: The paper varies the agglomerative distance threshold and reports its effect (albeit briefly).
- Application signal: The weakly supervised classification experiment suggests the coherence measure can be operationalized to filter topic runs, with non-trivial gains for harder subsets (e.g., PC vs. MAC).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior embedding-based coherence: The core idea—use embeddings to assess topic word relatedness—has been explored (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson 2013; later embedding-based coherence variants). Mapping to “largest bucket size” is a straightforward clustering proxy; the SVD bucketization (assign to closest singular vector) is unconventional and insufficiently justified.
- Methodological clarity and soundness issues in SVD variants:
  - It is unclear why right singular vectors (global basis of the embedding space) should serve as “sub-theme” prototypes, and assigning words by cosine similarity to these vectors is non-standard. Typically, one would use projections or clustering in the low-rank subspace.
  - The paper states “use the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers” when n is number of words; with n ≪ d this is odd and suggests overfitting or trivial partitions.
  - The reorganization threshold formula is ambiguous (AWES − AWPSAWES lacks parentheses), ad hoc, and not theoretically motivated. Despite the “parameter-free” claim, this is still a tunable design (derived from data statistics).
  - Additional arbitrary choices (e.g., squaring the top singular value to weight scores) are not justified or ablated.
- Insufficient baselines and statistical analysis:
  - No comparison against simple, strong embedding baselines (e.g., average pairwise cosine similarity among topic words; spherical k-means largest-cluster size; spectral clustering), or against later embedding-based coherence measures. Comparing only to Röder’s best config risks overstating gains.
  - No statistical significance testing on correlation differences; with 100 topics per dataset, improvements like 0.869 vs. 0.859 may not be significant.
- Reproducibility gaps:
  - Missing details: embedding normalization, distance metric for clustering, OOV handling (Table shows odd tokens like “000”), whether stop words were filtered, exact SVD implementation, handling of rank deficiency, and whether N (top-10) is fixed or robust to other N.
  - Pseudocode for bucketization and reorganization would help; currently the SVD assignment is underspecified.
- Evaluation scope and generality:
  - Results depend on general-domain GloVe vectors; poor performance on Genomics underscores domain mismatch. The claim of parameter-free generality is weakened without domain-specific embeddings or adaptation.
  - The text classification study is confounded: selecting “best 5 of 10” by the same metric used to claim utility introduces selection bias; human labeling effort differs across settings; no inter-annotator agreement, and gains are small/inconsistent.

Questions to authors
1) Please clarify the SVD assignment: for an n×d matrix (n words, d=300), do you use the first n right singular vectors (columns of V) or only those corresponding to non-zero singular values? Are embeddings normalized? Why cosine similarity to basis vectors, rather than projection magnitudes in the reduced subspace?
2) Can you provide ablations for: (a) largest-bucket size vs. WL vs. EVS; (b) with/without the “singular value squared” weighting; and (c) SVD-Reorg with different threshold formulas? How sensitive are results?
3) How do TBuckets compare to a simple baseline: average pairwise cosine similarity among the top-N words? And to spherical k-means largest-cluster size? Please include significance tests on r.
4) What is the exact formula for the reorganization threshold? Is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? Why this form? Any theoretical or empirical rationale beyond anecdotal examples?
5) How do you handle OOV words, stopwords, and rare tokens? Are results robust when N varies (e.g., top-5, top-20)?
6) For the classification study, can you control labeling effort (same number of runs and topics) and report statistical significance? Also report inter-annotator agreement.

Overall assessment
The idea is practical and the empirical results are promising, but the methodological justification is weak, key design choices are ad hoc, important baselines are missing, and significance/reproducibility are not adequately addressed. I am not convinced this sufficiently advances the state of the art over established coherence measures. I recommend rejection unless the authors can substantially strengthen the theoretical motivation, comparisons, and statistical analysis.
