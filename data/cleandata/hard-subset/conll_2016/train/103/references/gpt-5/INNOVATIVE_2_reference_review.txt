Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” intended to reflect sub-themes, and then score coherence by properties of the largest bucket. Three variants are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering over word embeddings (with a distance threshold), (ii) SVD-based bucketing that uses eigenvectors of A^T A (A is the topic word embedding matrix) as orthogonal “concept” directions and assigns words to their most similar eigenvector, and (iii) a parameter-free reorganization heuristic that moves words into/from the largest bucket using a per-topic dynamic threshold derived from average word–word and word–eigenvector similarities. On three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), TBuckets-SVD-Reorg attains higher Pearson correlation with human coherence judgments than the best configurations in Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets. The paper also illustrates utility for weakly supervised classification by selecting topic runs with higher TBuckets scores.

Contributions (as I see them; strongest first)
1) A novel SVD-based bucketing mechanism for topic coherence that treats eigenvectors of the topic word embedding matrix as interpretable “bucket identifiers,” yielding a simple, largely parameter-free procedure that aligns with human intuition about coherent topics having a dominant theme.
2) A dynamic, per-topic reorganization rule (no tuned hyperparameters) that adjusts assignments between the largest bucket and others based on relative pulls from words and eigenvectors.
3) Empirical evidence that these measures correlate competitively (and sometimes better) with human judgments, plus a downstream demonstration that selecting higher-coherence topic runs improves weakly supervised text classification.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and boldness: Using per-topic SVD on the word embedding matrix and treating (top) eigenvectors as theme directions is a fresh, elegant idea in the coherence literature. The “largest bucket” heuristic is intuitive and explainable.
- Parameter-free SVD variants: In contrast to many coherence measures requiring nontrivial tuning or external corpora/calibration, the SVD-based methods are simple to run, reproducible in principle, and reduce the burden of hyperparameter search.
- Competitive to SOTA: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg improves Pearson r over Röder et al. (2015)’s best configurations on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859). Even the clustering variant performs at par on Genomics, indicating robustness of the “bucketing” idea.
- Practical impact: The classification experiment shows that selecting high-coherence runs (by TBuckets) can improve macro-F1 versus averaging over arbitrary runs, especially on harder 20NG subsets (e.g., PC vs MAC), supporting real utility beyond correlations.
- Interpretability and diagnostics: The bucket outputs are human-readable and can surface when a topic has multiple sub-themes vs a single dominant one, potentially useful for model inspection and refinement.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity and correctness: Key details are underspecified or confusing. For TBuckets-SVD: how many eigenvectors are used (first n, but n≤rank?), what similarity metric is used, and how ties are handled. For the reorg threshold, the formula AWES − AWPSAWES lacks parentheses and dimensional clarity; rationale for subtracting from AWES is not well justified. Some examples contain apparent typos (e.g., “official” step swaps in “storm, weather”), undermining confidence.
- Limited evaluation scope and statistical rigor: Only three datasets with N=10 top words are used; no sensitivity to N, no ablations (e.g., SVD without weighting by top singular value, reorg on/off), no tests of significance for differences, and no Spearman correlations. The downstream classification gains are small-to-moderate and lack significance testing.
- Domain sensitivity and embeddings dependence: Performance drops notably on Genomics (≤0.691 vs 0.773 SOTA), likely due to out-of-domain GloVe vectors. The paper does not analyze OOV handling, alternative embeddings (e.g., domain-specific, fastText), or how sensitive results are to embedding choice—a central dependency of the method.
- Comparisons and baselines: The paper compares to “best” Röder et al. but does not include strong embedding-based coherence baselines (e.g., NPMI with embeddings-derived co-occurrence, post-2015 measures), nor report resource requirements vs PMI/NPMI methods using Wikipedia.
- “Parameter-free” claim is somewhat overstated: While thresholds are derived dynamically, the approach still encodes design choices (e.g., using first n eigenvectors, the exact reorg inequalities, weighting by top singular value squared). These choices could be varied and may act like hidden hyperparameters; ablations are needed.

Questions to authors
1) TBuckets-SVD specifics: For a topic with n words and d-dimensional embeddings, you take A ∈ R^{n×d} and use the first n eigenvectors of A^T A as bucket identifiers. Given rank(A) ≤ n, do you cap at rank(A)? What happens if singular values decay quickly? Why V (feature space) rather than U (entity space)?
2) Similarity and assignment: Do you use cosine similarity for word–eigenvector comparisons? Are vectors normalized? How do you break ties or handle negative cosine values?
3) Reorg threshold: Please clarify the threshold formula with parentheses and intuition. Is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? Why subtract from AWES, and what are the typical ranges? Does reorganization iterate to convergence or is it single-pass?
4) Weighting by σ1^2: Why square of the top singular value? Did you try no weighting or alternative normalizations? Provide an ablation table.
5) Embeddings and OOV: How do you handle words missing from GloVe? Have you tried domain-specific embeddings (e.g., BioWordVec) for Genomics, or fastText (subword) to reduce OOV? Sensitivity to embedding choice?
6) Robustness: How does performance vary with N (top-5, top-20)? With different ordering assumptions (since you also weight by LDA order)? Any effect of using topic-word probabilities directly (not just order)?
7) Statistical tests: Are improvements over Röder et al. significant? Please report confidence intervals or permutation tests for Pearson r, and significance for downstream classification.
8) Resources and reproducibility: Will you release code and scripts to reproduce the tables? What’s the runtime complexity compared to NPMI-based coherence?

Overall, I find the central idea novel and promising, with encouraging empirical results and useful interpretability. With improved clarity, ablations, and robustness analyses, this could be a valuable contribution. I lean toward acceptance given the conceptual novelty and practical potential despite presentation and evaluation gaps.
