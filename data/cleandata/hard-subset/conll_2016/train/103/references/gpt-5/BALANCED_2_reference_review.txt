Summary
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” of semantically related words using pre-trained word embeddings. The key intuition is that coherent topics should yield one dominant bucket reflecting a single theme. Three variants are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering), (ii) SVD-based assignment of words to eigenvector “directions” (TBuckets-SVD), and (iii) a reorganization heuristic (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that moves words into/out of the largest bucket using a dynamic threshold derived from average word–word and word–eigenvector similarities. Coherence scores are computed from properties of the largest bucket (size, order-weighted size, eigenvector similarity), with SVD-based scores further scaled by the top singular value squared. On three datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics; 100 topics each) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg reports higher Pearson correlation with human judgments than the best configurations in Röder et al. (2015) on two datasets. A small application shows that selecting topic model runs with higher TBuckets coherence can improve weakly supervised text classification.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, embedding-based, largely parameter-light coherence measure grounded in the “largest coherent bucket” hypothesis, with competitive or improved correlation to human judgments versus strong baselines (primary).
2) A reorganization heuristic that adaptively corrects SVD-based bucketing without dataset-level tuning, contributing to the best-performing variant.
3) An application study indicating the practical utility of coherence-driven run selection for topic-labeling-based classification.

Strengths
- Clear and intuitive idea, efficient implementation: Representing topic words in an embedding space and scoring coherence via the size/quality of the largest bucket aligns with how humans often interpret topics. The SVD variant is computationally trivial at topic scale (10×300 matrices) and conceptually neat.
- Competitive empirical results: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves r=0.837 (NYT) and r≈0.869 (20NG), exceeding the reported best from Röder et al. (2015) (0.806, 0.859) and performing reasonably on Genomics. This suggests the approach is viable across domains, at least for news-like text.
- Minimal tuning for the best variant: The SVD-based methods avoid manual parameter selection; the dynamic reorganization threshold is computed per topic, which is attractive for deployment and reproducibility.
- Practical downstream utility: Selecting runs by TBuckets coherence improves or maintains macro-F1 in 3/4 classification tasks, with large gains when coherence differs substantially (e.g., POLITICS vs SCIENCE: 96.19 vs 91.09), supporting the usefulness of the metric in active/weak supervision settings.
- Qualitative analyses: The bucketing examples (e.g., “television” topic) are illustrative and help interpret how the method behaves.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility gaps: Important details are underspecified. For TBuckets-SVD: why use the first n eigenvectors of V (feature space) as bucket identifiers rather than components in U (entity/topic-word space)? How are eigenvectors normalized? Is cosine similarity used throughout? The reorganization threshold formula is ambiguous (AWES − AWPSAWES likely means AWES − AWPS/AWES). OOV handling and coverage with GloVe are not discussed. These omissions hinder reproducibility and understanding.
- Limited and partly incomplete evaluation: Results are reported only as Pearson’s r on three 100-topic sets without statistical significance tests (e.g., Fisher z-transforms or bootstrapping). Some gains are small (e.g., +0.01 on 20NG), and it is unclear if they are significant. Comparisons omit other embedding-based coherence baselines (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson’s distributional/embedding measures, C_V variants using embedding-based confirmation) and more recent word-embedding topic coherence measures; an ablation study (largest-bucket size vs WL vs EVS vs singular value scaling) is also missing.
- “Parameter-free” claim is overstated: The approach makes several design choices that act as implicit hyperparameters (number of top-N words, using first n eigenvectors, cosine vs dot product, scaling by top singular value squared, dynamic threshold functional form). While not tuned per dataset, their effects should be analyzed for robustness.
- Domain mismatch and external resource bias: The Genomics results lag, likely due to out-of-domain GloVe vectors. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify OOV rates or test domain-adapted embeddings (e.g., PubMed, fastText). Since embedding choice crucially affects performance, a sensitivity analysis is needed.
- Metric assumption may penalize legitimate multi-faceted topics: The largest-bucket objective presumes a single-theme topic; however, topics can be interpretable yet multi-modal (e.g., subthemes or compound concepts). The method might under-score such topics. A discussion or variant that rewards a dominant bucket while allowing a meaningful second bucket would improve generality.
- Application study under-specified: The classification experiments lack necessary details (number of topics per run, labeling protocol, annotator count/agreements, selection criteria, train/test splits, statistical tests). Reported gains are modest in 2/4 cases; without significance testing, conclusions remain tentative.

Questions to Authors
1) SVD design: Why choose eigenvectors from V (feature-feature) as bucket identifiers instead of U (word-word)? How many eigenvectors are used when d < n or n varies? Are eigenvectors and word vectors L2-normalized before cosine similarity?
2) Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the exact formula (parentheses), provide ranges of AWPS and AWES, and justify the functional form. How sensitive are results to this form?
3) Similarity metric and preprocessing: Is cosine used throughout? Are word vectors centered/whitened? Are word embedding dimensions weighted by singular values during assignment?
4) OOV and embedding sensitivity: What fraction of topic words are OOV per dataset? How are they handled? Can you report results with alternative embeddings (word2vec, fastText; PubMed for Genomics) and discuss variance?
5) Ablations and robustness: Please report correlations for (i) size only, (ii) WL only, (iii) EVS only, (iv) with/without singular-value scaling, (v) SVD without reorg vs with reorg, and (vi) varying N (top-5/10/20 words).
6) Significance: Are the improvements over Röder et al. statistically significant? Please include CIs or p-values for r via Fisher z-transform and/or bootstrap over topics.
7) Baselines: Can you compare against embedding-based coherence measures (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson variants, C_V with NPMI/embeddings, more recent W2V/fastText-based coherence) to situate TBuckets fairly?
8) Application details: How many topics per run and per class? How were labels collected (annotators, time, agreement)? Are the differences between “highest 5” and “lowest 5” statistically significant?

Additional comments
- There are minor typos/formatting issues (e.g., bucket example for “official”; ambiguous threshold formula). Cleaning these will aid clarity.
- Releasing code and trained settings would significantly improve impact and reproducibility.

Overall, the idea is appealing and the empirical results are promising, but the paper would benefit from clearer algorithmic specification, stronger and broader comparisons, statistical testing, and robustness analyses.
