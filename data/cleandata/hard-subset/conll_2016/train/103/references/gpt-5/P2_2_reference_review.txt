Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group top-N topic words into “buckets” using semantic similarity from pre-trained word embeddings. The central hypothesis is that coherent topics yield one dominant bucket. Three instantiations are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering), (ii) SVD-based bucketing by assigning each word to the most similar right-singular vector (TBuckets-SVD), and (iii) a parameter-free reorganization step that moves words into/from the largest bucket based on dynamic thresholds derived from average word–word and word–eigenvector similarities (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg).
- Evaluation on three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics) reports Pearson correlation with human coherence ratings, showing TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforming Röder et al. (2015)’s best settings on 2/3 datasets. A secondary application demonstrates that selecting topic model runs with higher TBuckets scores can improve weakly supervised text classification performance in some settings.

Contributions as I see them (in order of strength)
1) A simple, largely parameter-free SVD-based bucketing heuristic for topic coherence with a dynamic reorganization step, yielding competitive or better correlations than strong baselines on public datasets.
2) An interpretable “largest-bucket” scoring perspective that operationalizes human grouping behavior and complements existing PMI/NPMI-style measures.
3) A preliminary application showing that coherence-based selection of topic runs can improve downstream topic-labeled classification in several subsets.

Strengths
- Empirical competitiveness with strong baselines: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms Röder et al.’s best setting on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859), with clear ablations (size, WL, EVS) showing consistent performance across scoring variants.
- Parameter-light and efficient: The SVD-based variants avoid expensive corpus-wide co-occurrence estimation and extensive configuration searches (as in Röder’s framework), relying only on pre-trained embeddings and SVD of a small n×d matrix (n≈10, d=300), making the method fast and practical.
- Interpretability: The bucketing outputs are easy to inspect and reason about; examples demonstrate face validity (e.g., weather and TV-related topics) and the rationale for the reorganization step.
- Sensible failure analysis and domain awareness: The Genomics underperformance plausibly reflects domain mismatch and OOV/coverage issues with generic GloVe embeddings; the authors explicitly propose domain-adapted similarities as future work.
- Downstream utility signals: Although preliminary, the experiment selecting higher-coherence topic sets improves topic-labeling-based classification in difficult settings (e.g., 20NG PC vs MAC), supporting practical value beyond intrinsic correlation.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior embedding-based coherence: Using distributional semantics to assess topic coherence is well established (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson 2013; Lau et al. 2014; Röder et al. 2015 C_v uses NPMI with word embeddings also explored later). The main novelty here is the SVD-derived “bucket identifiers” and dynamic reorganization. This is interesting but incremental; the paper should position itself more sharply against simple embedding baselines (e.g., mean pairwise cosine, centroid similarity) with direct comparisons.
- Ambiguity and under-specification in the reorganization rule: The threshold formula “AWES − AWPSAWES” is unclear (missing parentheses). The rationale for subtracting from AWES and how ranges are matched is not rigorously justified. More detail on similarity normalization, OOV handling, and stability is needed for reproducibility.
- Statistical rigor: The reported improvements lack significance testing for dependent correlations (e.g., Williams/Steiger tests) and confidence intervals. Some gains are modest (e.g., 20NG 0.859→0.869), so significance matters for claims over state-of-the-art.
- Baseline coverage and ablations: Only Röder’s best configuration is used as a reference. Missing are direct baselines using (i) UMass, UCI/NPMI, C_v on the same resources, and (ii) simple embedding coherence measures (pairwise cosine average, centroid-based) to isolate the added value of bucketing/SVD. An ablation varying top-N words (N=5/10/20), different embeddings (word2vec, fastText), and without weighting by the top singular value would strengthen the claims.
- Mixed downstream results: In Table 3, improvements are inconsistent (e.g., POLITICS vs RELIGION highest set is slightly worse than S1; MED vs SPACE lowest set outperforms the highest). The experimental design (select 10 runs, annotate top-5 vs bottom-5 by coherence) is reasonable, but a larger sample and statistical testing would clarify the practical impact.

Questions to authors
- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the exact formula (is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES) or (AWES − AWPS)/AWES?), the intuition behind it, and the typical numeric ranges of AWPS and AWES. How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Baselines: How does TBuckets-SVD-Reorg compare against a simple embedding-based coherence such as average pairwise cosine among top-N words, or centroid similarity (as in Aletras & Stevenson)? Can you include these as ablations?
- Sensitivity to N and embeddings: Did you evaluate different values of N (e.g., 5, 15, 20) and different pre-trained embeddings or OOV handling strategies? Does Genomics improve with domain-specific embeddings?
- Significance: Are the differences vs Röder et al. statistically significant on NYT and 20NG? Please report confidence intervals and significance tests for correlations.
- Implementation details: How are cosine similarities computed (normalized vectors?), how are ties handled when assigning to eigenvectors, and what happens if the first n singular vectors collapse (e.g., near-duplicate directions)? Why use the square of the top singular value as a weight?

Overall recommendation
This is a neat, interpretable, and efficient approach with promising results on two datasets. However, the methodological novelty is moderate, key details are underspecified, baseline coverage is incomplete, and statistical analysis is missing. I lean toward rejection for the main venue in its current form, but I believe the work could become competitive with clarified reorganization mechanics, stronger baselines and significance testing, and a more thorough analysis (including sensitivity to N and embeddings, and domain-specific embeddings for Genomics).
