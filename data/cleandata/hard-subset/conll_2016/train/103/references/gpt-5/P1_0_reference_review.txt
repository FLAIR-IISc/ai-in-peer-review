Summary
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using pretrained word embeddings. The core intuition is that coherent topics should yield one dominant semantic group (a large bucket), while incoherent topics split into multiple small buckets. Three implementations are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering over word embeddings (TBuckets-Clustering), (ii) SVD-based bucketing where principal directions in embedding space act as “bucket identifiers” (TBuckets-SVD), and (iii) an SVD variant with an automatic reorganization step to move words into/out of the largest bucket (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). Topic coherence is scored primarily via properties of the largest bucket (size, LDA word-order weighting, and eigenvector similarity), optionally weighted by the top singular value. On three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics; 100 topics each, top-10 words), TBuckets-SVD-Reorg attains higher Pearson correlation with human ratings than the best configurations reported by Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets. A small application shows that selecting topic models with higher TBuckets coherence before human labeling yields better weakly supervised classification.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, embedding-based, largely parameter-free SVD bucketing method for topic coherence, with an automatic reorganization heuristic to better align words with the main theme.
2) Empirical evidence that the approach correlates competitively with human judgments (exceeding a strong baseline on 2 datasets) and a practical demonstration that it can help triage topic models for annotation.
3) A coherent operationalization of the “largest-bucket” idea, combining word-order and spectral weighting.

Strengths
- Practical, simple, and fast method: The SVD approach is computationally light (10×300 matrices), requires only pretrained embeddings and topic word lists, and does not require corpus-specific co-occurrence statistics. This makes it easy to deploy in real pipelines.
- Intuitive signal: The largest-bucket heuristic matches how humans inspect topics (seeking a single dominant theme). The examples illustrate why a single large cluster is a useful proxy for interpretability.
- Competitive empirical performance: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg exceeds Röder et al.’s best configurations on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859), and is close on Genomics despite clear domain mismatch with general-domain embeddings.
- Parameter economy: The SVD variants are effectively parameter-free at inference time; the reorganization threshold is computed from the data, avoiding tuning on target datasets.
- Application value: The topic-model selection experiment demonstrates that TBuckets scores can guide labeling effort toward higher-yield topic models, improving or maintaining classification performance with the same annotation budget.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and theoretical grounding: Using distributional semantics/embeddings for topic coherence is known (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson, 2013; variants since), and clustering topic words is a straightforward baseline. The specific SVD bucketing (assigning words to eigenvectors of ATA) is interesting but feels ad hoc without theoretical justification that principal directions in the embedding subspace correspond to semantic sub-themes. Why use the first n eigenvectors (n = number of topic words) rather than selecting k < n by variance explained or stability?
- Evaluation rigor and multiple comparisons: The paper reports many scoring variants (size, WL, EVS, WL*EVS; with/without reorg; three linkage types; threshold sweeps) and compares the best numbers to Röder et al.’s best. No statistical significance tests or confidence intervals are provided, and no pre-specified primary metric is declared. With only 100 topics per dataset, differences of 0.01–0.03 in r may not be robust.
- Domain sensitivity and generality: Performance on Genomics is substantially worse than the baseline (≈0.69 vs 0.773), plausibly due to domain mismatch of GloVe embeddings. This undercuts the “parameter-free” advantage in specialized domains; the method may require domain-specific embeddings or adaptations, which are not explored.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility: Key details are missing or ambiguous: exact similarity function (cosine assumed), normalization, handling of OOV topic words, SVD implementation details, tie-breaking, whether V-columns are normalized before similarity, and the rationale for weighting by the square of the largest singular value. The reorganization threshold formula appears malformed in the text (“AWES − AWPSAWES”): is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? Code availability is not discussed.
- Baseline coverage: Given the reliance on embeddings, stronger, simpler baselines are natural and missing: e.g., average pairwise cosine similarity between topic words; clustering-based largest-cluster size using cosine with a standard method; or embedding-based variants of NPMI. Comparing only to Röder et al.’s best configuration limits the interpretability of gains specifically attributable to bucketing/SVD.

Questions to authors
- SVD bucketing details: Do you use cosine similarity between a word embedding and each column of V to assign the word’s bucket? Are columns normalized? Why use the first n eigenvectors rather than choosing k by cumulative variance or stability across runs? How sensitive are results to k?
- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the exact formula (is it AWES − AWPS/AWES?) and motivate it. Did you consider a margin proportional to the variance of similarities? How many words typically move in or out, and how does reorg affect correlation per dataset?
- Clustering setup: What exact distance metric and linkage parameters were used in Table 1? How were MaxDist values chosen for the reported numbers (tuned per dataset)? In Figure 1, which value was selected for the table and was tuning performed on the test topics?
- Embedding details: How do you handle OOV words (e.g., Genomics)? Any preprocessing (lemmatization, lowercasing)? Did you try domain-specific embeddings for Genomics, and if so, how did performance change?
- Statistical testing: Please provide confidence intervals for Pearson r (e.g., via bootstrapping topics) and test whether improvements over Röder et al. are statistically significant.
- Text classification application: How many topics per run, how many annotators, and what was the labeling protocol and IAA? Are the improvements significant? Does selecting top-5 runs after computing TBuckets add computational overhead that offsets annotation savings?

Overall, the paper presents a neat, implementable idea that performs competitively and could be useful in practice. Strengthening the theoretical motivation, clarifying/releasing implementation details, expanding baselines, and adding statistical rigor would substantially improve its impact.
