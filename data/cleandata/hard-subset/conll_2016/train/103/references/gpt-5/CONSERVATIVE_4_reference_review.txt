Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of methods to assess topic coherence by grouping top-N topic words into “buckets” using word embeddings. Three variants are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering), (ii) SVD-based assignment of words to principal directions (TBuckets-SVD), and (iii) a heuristic reorganization step over SVD assignments (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg).
- The methods are evaluated on three datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics) by correlating automatic scores with human coherence ratings (Pearson’s r), and compared to the best settings reported in Röder et al. (2015). TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves higher r on 2/3 datasets. A small application shows that selecting topic runs by TBuckets coherence can slightly improve downstream weakly supervised classification.
- The authors emphasize that the SVD variants are parameter-free and simpler to use than prior work requiring extensive parameter tuning.

Strengths
- Empirical competitiveness on standard benchmarks: On NYT and 20NG, TBuckets-SVD(-Reorg) achieves Pearson correlations on par with, and marginally above, the best configurations in Röder et al. (2015). Using widely recognized datasets with human annotations is appropriate and strengthens external validity.
- Simplicity and low tuning overhead: The SVD variants use off-the-shelf embeddings and avoid the heavy configuration search of coherence measures (as in Röder’s framework). This practicality is valuable for end-users who want a quick, reproducible heuristic.
- Clear, human-interpretable rationale: The bucket metaphor aligns with how practitioners informally inspect topics. Linking the coherence score to the size/weight of the largest bucket is intuitive.
- Small application to labeling-based text classification: The idea of screening multiple topic model runs using a coherence proxy to reduce annotator burden is sensible. The reported results indicate the approach is at least not harmful and can help in the harder PC–MAC setting.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and methodological justification: Embedding-based coherence measures and clustering in distributional space are established (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson, 2013). The SVD approach of assigning words to eigenvector “directions” is unconventional and insufficiently justified: (a) principal axes are sign-indeterminate; (b) assigning to a direction rather than to components or loadings is nonstandard; (c) using the first n eigenvectors (n=number of words) as “bucket identifiers” is arbitrary; (d) no discussion of negative projections, sign flips, or robustness. The reorganization heuristics further compound ad hoc choices without theoretical grounding.
- Evaluation rigor: The improvements over Röder et al. (2015) are small (e.g., 0.869 vs. 0.859 on 20NG; 0.837 vs. 0.806 on NYT) with only 100 topics per dataset. There is no statistical significance analysis (e.g., Fisher r-to-z), and with n≈100, these deltas are unlikely to be significant. The paper also reports only Pearson’s r; Spearman rank correlation is commonly used and would be informative. Claims of “outperforming the state of the art” are therefore too strong.
- Incomplete baselines and ablations: The paper does not compare against simple, strong embedding-based baselines (e.g., mean pairwise cosine, centroid similarity) nor reproduce NPMI/UMass with the same corpora. No ablations for key design choices (largest-bucket size vs. WL vs. EVS; with/without σ1^2 weighting; SVD vs. clustering; reorg on/off; varying number of top words N). The “parameter-free” claim is not fully convincing without sensitivity analyses to embedding choice (GloVe vs word2vec vs fastText), OOV handling, and N.
- Heuristic scoring details are under-specified: It is unclear how cosine similarity with eigenvectors is computed and whether negative values are treated; sign indeterminacy of eigenvectors is not addressed; the motivation for weighting by the square of the top singular value is not justified. The reorganization threshold formula (AWES − AWPS/AWES) lacks clear parentheses and rationale; no analysis of stability or failure cases is given.
- Domain robustness and reproducibility: Performance drops on Genomics, and OOV/biomedical terminology is a likely factor. Yet the approach relies on general-domain embeddings without domain adaptation; no alternative embeddings or domain calibration are tried. The description contains inconsistencies/typos (e.g., bucket trace example errors, unclear parentheses in Table 1), and it is unclear whether code and exact evaluation scripts will be released, making reproducibility uncertain.
- Classification application is weakly supported: Gains are small/inconsistent across four binary subsets; no statistical testing; annotator process, time, and agreement are not reported. Selecting the “best 5 of 10” runs by the proposed metric risks confirmation bias; a more controlled study is needed to substantiate practical utility.

Questions to authors
- SVD assignment:
  - How do you handle the sign indeterminacy of eigenvectors? Are similarities computed with signed cosines or absolute values? What happens to words with large negative loadings on the principal axis?
  - Why choose the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers? Did you try k < n (e.g., selected by explained variance) or an automatic selection? How sensitive are results to k?
  - What is the empirical effect of weighting by σ1^2? Please provide ablations.
- Reorganization:
  - Please clarify the threshold formula: is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES) or (AWES − AWPS)/AWES? What is the intuition for this exact form?
  - How many words are typically moved in each direction, and how does reorganization change correlations relative to SVD alone across datasets? Any stability analysis?
- Evaluation:
  - Please report Spearman correlations and statistical significance (Fisher r-to-z) against Röder’s best to substantiate “outperforms” claims.
  - Provide comparisons to simple embedding baselines (average pairwise cosine among top-N words; centroid cohesion) and to NPMI using the same reference corpus.
  - How are OOV words handled? Did you try domain-specific embeddings for Genomics or character/subword models (e.g., fastText)?
  - Sensitivity to N (top 5, 10, 20 words per topic) and to embedding type?
- Classification application:
  - How many annotators, what protocol, and were annotations controlled for time/order effects? Are differences statistically significant?
  - Can you correlate TBuckets scores with annotation time/agreement to demonstrate practical benefit?

Overall, while the idea is intuitive and results are competitive, the methodological underpinnings and evaluation rigor are not yet at a level I would endorse over established coherence measures without further analyses.
