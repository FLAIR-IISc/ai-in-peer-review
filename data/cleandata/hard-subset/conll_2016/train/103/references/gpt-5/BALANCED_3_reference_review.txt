Summary
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of embedding-based methods to assess topic quality by grouping the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” of semantically related terms. The core intuition is that a coherent topic will yield one dominant bucket (a single theme) and few small buckets. Three variants are presented: (i) TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering over word embeddings with a distance threshold), (ii) TBuckets-SVD (using the right singular vectors of the word-embedding matrix as orthogonal “sub-theme” directions and assigning words to the nearest eigenvector), and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a post-hoc reorganization that moves words into/out of the largest bucket using similarity-based criteria with a dynamic threshold). Topic coherence is scored primarily by properties of the largest bucket (size and optionally weights from word order and eigenvector similarity). The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation with human topic ratings on three 100-topic datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), compared against the best configurations reported in Röder et al. (2015). TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms prior work on NYT and 20NG, but underperforms on Genomics. A small application study suggests that selecting topic runs with higher TBuckets coherence can improve weakly supervised text classification.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, largely parameter-free embedding-based coherence family that operationalizes topic coherence as the size/quality of the largest semantic bucket, with an SVD-based variant and a data-driven reorganization step.
2) An empirical comparison to strong baseline configurations (Röder et al., 2015) showing competitive or better correlations on two general-domain datasets, and a negative case on a domain-mismatched dataset, along with a small downstream classification study.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive idea with practical appeal: The “largest bucket” heuristic captures the human intuition that coherent topics tend to have a single dominant theme. The approach is computationally light (n≈10 words, d≈300) and easy to implement with off-the-shelf embeddings.
- Competitive performance without heavy configuration: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg attains higher Pearson r than the best reported Röder configurations on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859), despite not relying on external co-occurrence statistics or parameter sweeps. This is a useful empirical point in favor of embedding-based coherence.
- Sensible negative result highlighting domain mismatch: The marked drop on Genomics (0.691 vs 0.773) is consistent with known limitations of general-purpose embeddings in specialized domains, and the paper acknowledges this and proposes domain-tailored similarities as future work.
- Practical downstream utility: The classification experiment, while modest, indicates that selecting topic runs with higher TBuckets coherence can improve weakly supervised labeling in some settings (e.g., PC vs MAC +1.5 pts, POLITICS vs SCIENCE +0.24 pts), aligning intrinsic and extrinsic evaluation.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental and theoretical grounding is thin: Using embeddings to assess topic coherence and grouping words by similarity is well explored (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson, 2013; subsequent work). The SVD variant treats right singular vectors of the word-embedding matrix as sub-themes, but this connection is not theoretically justified: with n<<d, these directions reflect variance in a small set of word vectors, and treating each of the top-n right singular vectors as “bucket identifiers” (yielding up to n buckets) seems ad hoc. A deeper analysis or ablation is needed to establish why SVD helps beyond straightforward clustering or centroid-based measures.
- Ambiguities and potential errors in the reorganization threshold: The dynamic threshold is given as AWES − AWPSAWES, which is unclear (is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)?). The rationale for mixing word-word and word-eigenvector similarities and for the exact inequalities is not well justified. This undermines the “parameter-free” claim: the method still relies on design choices and a non-trivial threshold formula that was not derived from first principles or validated systematically.
- Evaluation scope and statistical rigor: The datasets have only 100 topics each; differences of 0.01–0.02 in r may not be statistically significant. No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported. It is also unclear whether the reported TBuckets result per dataset is the single best among the four scoring variants (size, WL, EVS, WL*EVS), which would amount to post-hoc model selection.
- Reproducibility details are insufficient: Key details are missing, including preprocessing of topic words (lemmatization, OOV handling), exact cosine similarity computation and normalization, the agglomerative clustering setup (distance metric, threshold units), the number of top words N (assumed 10), and code availability. Figure 1 lacks axis definitions/units. The TBuckets-SVD step should clarify matrix shapes and which subset of V is used when rank<n.
- Downstream study mixed results and potential confounds: On MEDICAL vs SPACE, “lowest coherence” topic sets perform best; on POLITICS vs RELIGION the “high coherence” sets underperform S1. The setup (choosing best 5 of 10 runs) risks selection bias; it would be more convincing to correlate coherence with accuracy across all runs and report correlation/statistics.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the TBuckets-SVD mechanics precisely: given A∈R^{n×d} (n words, d dims), do you use the first n columns of V (right singular vectors) as bucket centroids? How many non-zero singular values were typically observed, and what happens if rank<n?
- Can you formalize the reorganization threshold? Is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? What are the ranges of AWES and AWPS, and why is this form appropriate? Did you assess sensitivity to this formula?
- Which specific Röder configurations are used for the “best performing settings”? Is it CV with NPMI on Wikipedia (Palmetto)? Please list the exact baselines to ensure a fair comparison.
- Are the reported r values per TBuckets variant chosen per dataset (best among size/WL/EVS/WL*EVS), or fixed a priori? If selected, please provide a protocol to avoid overfitting.
- Could you provide significance tests (e.g., Fisher z-transform) for differences in r and make code/data available?

Decision
Reject. The idea is intuitive and the empirical results on two datasets are promising, but the methodological novelty over prior embedding-based coherence measures is limited; key algorithmic components (SVD-based bucketing and reorganization) lack theoretical grounding and clarity; statistical and reproducibility aspects are insufficient; and the downstream application evidence is mixed. Strengthening the paper along the lines below could make it competitive.

Suggestions for improvement
- Provide a clear, formal description of TBuckets-SVD (matrix shapes, ranks, choice of eigenvectors) and the reorganization step with an unambiguous threshold formula, accompanied by sensitivity/ablation analyses (e.g., removing reorg, varying number of eigenvectors, replacing eigenvectors with PCA centroids, clustering baselines).
- Expand baselines to include strong embedding-based coherence measures (e.g., NPMI-based CV, Sheffield variants, and modern word-embedding topic coherence baselines) and report statistical significance.
- Analyze robustness: vary N (top words), swap in-domain embeddings for Genomics, test alternative similarity functions, and report results across multiple corpora/runs without post-hoc selection.
- For the classification study, correlate coherence with accuracy across all runs; avoid selecting “best” runs; and control the number and size of topics to remove confounds.
- Improve reproducibility: detail preprocessing, OOV handling, clustering settings, and release code.
