Summary and contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that bucket top-N topic words into thematic groups using pre-trained word embeddings. The key idea is that coherent topics should yield one dominant bucket. Three variants are introduced: (i) TBuckets-Clustering using agglomerative clustering with a distance threshold; (ii) TBuckets-SVD using SVD of the word-embedding matrix and assigning words to the most similar eigenvector (“sub-theme”); and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, a heuristic reorganization step that moves words into/out of the largest bucket based on similarities to words and eigenvectors with a data-driven threshold. Coherence is computed mainly from properties of the largest bucket (size, LDA word-order weights, and eigenvector similarity), optionally scaled by the top singular value. The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation to human coherence ratings on three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics) and applied to selecting topic models for weakly supervised text classification.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, largely parameter-free, embedding/SVD-based topic coherence measure grounded in the “largest bucket” heuristic, with a reorganization mechanism (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg).
2) Empirical evidence that TBuckets variants achieve correlations competitive with or slightly better than the best configurations reported by Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets, and a small downstream study showing utility in selecting topic sets for topic-labeling-based classification.
3) A practical framing of topic coherence via bucketing that is easy to implement and computationally cheap.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and efficiency: The bucketing intuition maps well to how humans inspect topics. The SVD-based implementation is lightweight (SVD on a 10×d matrix), requires no tuning, and uses only off-the-shelf embeddings. This makes the approach attractive for practitioners.

- Competitive performance: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg slightly outperforms the best configurations reported by Röder et al. (2015) on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and matches/exceeds on 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859), while using far fewer parameters. The clustering variant performs reasonably and shows robustness across linkage strategies.

- Clear ablations and variants: The paper reports multiple scoring choices (size, WL, EVS, and combinations) and shows the effect of the reorganization step. This helps isolate where the gains come from and increases credibility.

- Downstream utility: The topic selection experiment for weakly supervised classification is a sensible use case; selecting the more coherent topic sets typically improves macro-F1 over naive averaging and highlights the practical value of coherence estimation.

- Intuitive diagnostics: Worked “dry runs” and qualitative bucket examples are helpful for understanding behavior (e.g., detecting sub-themes, moving words like “news/fox/series” into the dominant television bucket).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and ad-hoc design choices: Using distributional representations and grouping/clustering for coherence has been explored (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson 2013; variants in Röder et al. 2015). The specific use of SVD eigenvectors as sub-theme axes and scoring by the largest bucket plus top singular value feels heuristic and is not theoretically justified. The reorganization threshold formula is unclear and appears ad-hoc.

- Evaluation gaps and significance: Improvements over Röder et al. are modest (e.g., +0.01 on 20NG), and statistical significance is not reported (e.g., Williams/Steiger tests on dependent correlations). Only Pearson’s r is used, though human ratings are on a 1–3 ordinal scale; Spearman/Kendall would be informative. It is unclear whether the Röder baselines were re-run under identical conditions or copied from the paper.

- Domain mismatch and limited generality: Performance drops notably on Genomics (0.691 vs 0.773), likely due to general-domain GloVe embeddings. The method’s dependence on off-the-shelf embeddings undermines the “parameter-free” claim in specialized domains; evaluating with domain-specific embeddings (or subword models) is essential.

- Largest-bucket bias: Reducing coherence to properties of the largest bucket may unfairly penalize multi-faceted but interpretable topics (e.g., two cohesive sub-themes). This design could also be brittle for topics with near-ties between sub-themes or for small N (10 words). There is no analysis of sensitivity to the number of top words.

- Reproducibility and clarity: Key details are underspecified: how similarity to eigenvectors is computed (cosine?), why “first n eigenvectors” are used (n = number of words), and what happens with OOV words. For clustering, the choice and tuning of distance threshold per dataset (parenthesized values in Table 1) suggest dataset-specific tuning, which weakens claims of parsimony. The reorganization threshold formula “AWES − AWPSAWES” is ambiguous (is it AWES − AWPS/AWES?). Some typos and citation formatting issues are present.

- Downstream study underdeveloped: The classification experiment lacks comparisons to selecting topic sets using standard coherence scores (e.g., c_v), does not report statistical significance, and the gains are small for some subsets. Details about annotation protocol, number of topics per run, inter-annotator agreement, and cost savings are missing.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the exact reorganization threshold formula and its derivation. Is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? How sensitive are results to this choice?

- In TBuckets-SVD, why select the first n eigenvectors (n = number of words)? Have you tried selecting k < n (e.g., based on an energy threshold) and does that change bucket sizes or performance?

- What similarity metric is used between a word and an eigenvector (cosine of normalized vectors)? Are word vectors centered/whitened before SVD?

- For TBuckets-Clustering, what distance metric is used and how were the MaxDist values chosen (the numbers in parentheses in Table 1)? Were they tuned on the evaluation sets?

- How are OOV topic words handled? What fraction of topic words per dataset lacked embeddings?

- Did you test Spearman/Kendall correlations or significance tests for Pearson differences?

- For the classification study, how many topics per run, how many annotators, any agreement measures, and how were class-topic mappings derived? Can you compare selection by TBuckets to selection by c_v or other baselines?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add statistical significance testing for correlation differences and report both Pearson and Spearman. Re-run Röder baselines under identical settings where possible, and include embedding-based baselines (e.g., average pairwise cosine among top-N words).

- Evaluate with domain-specific or subword embeddings (e.g., fastText, BioWordVec) to address the Genomics gap; discuss OOV handling.

- Explore scoring that accounts for multiple cohesive buckets (e.g., entropy of bucket distribution, weighted sum of top-2 buckets) to avoid over-penalizing multi-faceted but interpretable topics.

- Clarify and justify SVD design choices (number of eigenvectors, scaling by top singular value), and provide ablations. Consider PCA/whitening and assessing stability across random seeds.

- Strengthen the downstream study: compare against selecting topic sets via c_v (and others), report significance, detail the annotation protocol, and quantify annotation-time savings.

- Release code and detailed instructions to improve reproducibility.

Overall recommendation: Borderline reject. The idea is intuitive and results are promising, but the methodological novelty is limited, several design choices are heuristic and under-justified, and the evaluation needs strengthening (significance, baselines, domain coverage, clarity). With the suggested revisions, this work could become a solid contribution.
