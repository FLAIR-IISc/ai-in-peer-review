Summary and contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using word embeddings, with the intuition that coherent topics form one dominant bucket. Three instantiations are evaluated: (i) agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering), (ii) SVD-based assignment to eigenvector “directions” (TBuckets-SVD), and (iii) SVD plus a heuristic reorganization step that shifts words into/out of the largest bucket based on word–word and word–eigenvector similarities (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). Topic coherence is scored using properties of the largest bucket (size, LDA word-order weighting, and, for SVD variants, eigenvector similarity), optionally weighted by the top singular value. Correlation with human coherence ratings is reported on NYT, 20NG, and Genomics benchmarks from Röder et al. (2015). An application to weakly supervised topic-labeling-based text classification shows small gains when selecting high-coherence runs.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) A simple, embedding-driven “bucketization” framework for topic coherence that is largely parameter-free (SVD variants) and operationalizes the intuition of a dominant thematic group within a topic.
2) Empirical results showing TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves higher Pearson correlations with human judgments than the best settings of Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets, and a practical use case for selecting runs in topic-labeling-based classification.
3) A heuristic reorganization procedure (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that adjusts SVD assignments to favor a coherent largest bucket.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and interpretability: The “largest bucket implies coherence” idea is intuitive and easy to inspect qualitatively. The SVD-based approach is elegant in using orthogonal directions as latent sub-themes and requires no tuned hyperparameters, which is appealing compared to the configuration-heavy Röder et al. framework.

- Competitive to state of the art: On NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859), TBuckets-SVD-Reorg correlates as well or better than Röder et al.’s best setting. The improvements, while modest on 20NG, are meaningful on NYT and demonstrate viability of embedding-based coherence without external co-occurrence statistics.

- Practical utility demonstration: The selection of high-coherence runs improves macro-F1 in 3/4 binary 20NG classification subsets and notably separates “highest-5” vs “lowest-5” coherence sets (e.g., large gap for PC vs MAC), supporting the value of coherence measures in weakly supervised workflows.

- Minimal reliance on external resources: Using off-the-shelf GloVe embeddings keeps the method broadly applicable; SVD variants are parameter-free, improving robustness and reproducibility in principle.

- Clear operationalization and variants: The paper describes three concrete techniques and several scoring variants (size, WL, EVS), giving a reasonable exploration within the proposed framework.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior embedding-based coherence: Prior work (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson, 2013) already uses distributional semantics for coherence. The main novelty here is the “largest bucket” proxy and SVD-based direction assignment. While neat, it is a relatively small conceptual step and relies on heuristics (choice of top-n eigenvectors, sigma1^2 weighting, reorg thresholds) that need stronger justification and ablation.

- Heuristic and under-specified reorganization: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg introduces multiple derived quantities (NBPW, NBPE, LBPW, OBPW) and a “dynamic threshold” formula that is ambiguous as written (AWES − AWPSAWES likely means AWES − AWPS/AWES). The rationale for preferring the largest bucket (biasing “into” moves) could over-merge semantically distinct but interpretable subthemes. No ablations quantify the impact of reorg or sigma1^2 weighting on correlations.

- Incomplete evaluation rigor: Improvements over Röder et al. (2015) are not accompanied by statistical significance tests or confidence intervals. Only Pearson correlation is reported; Spearman rank correlation would be informative given the use case (ranking topics/runs). The classification gains are small and inconsistent (e.g., POLITICS vs RELIGION highest-5 coherence slightly underperforms S1), without statistical testing.

- Weak performance and analysis on Genomics: Results are substantially worse than Röder et al. (0.691 vs 0.773). The likely cause (domain mismatch in embeddings) is stated as future work, but the paper would benefit from trying domain-specific embeddings or analyzing OOV handling and its effect. This casts doubt on generalization beyond news-like domains.

- Methodological details and reproducibility: Important choices are under-specified: whether word vectors are normalized; cosine vs dot-product for eigenvector similarity; handling of OOV words; justification for using the first n eigenvectors (n words per topic) when V has d eigenvectors; why score weighting by sigma1^2; how Max Dist in clustering was selected (numbers in parentheses appear tuned on test data). No code or reproducibility plan is provided.

- Metric design may penalize multi-aspect yet interpretable topics: A single large bucket is rewarded, but topics with two clear sub-themes (still interpretable to humans) may be unfairly penalized. It would be useful to test against human judgments designed to distinguish “single-theme” vs “multi-theme but coherent.”

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the reorganization threshold: is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? How sensitive are results to this formula? Provide an ablation with and without reorg and with alternative thresholds.

- In TBuckets-SVD, why use the first n eigenvectors (n = number of topic words) rather than selecting k by explained variance or another criterion? What happens if n > rank or if multiple words map to the same eigenvector?

- How are similarities computed (cosine with normalized vectors)? Are eigenvectors (columns of V) used as-is (orthonormal) or scaled? Are word vectors centered/whitened before SVD?

- How do you handle OOV words in GloVe? What fraction of topic words are OOV in Genomics, and how does this affect results?

- Did you run statistical significance tests on correlation improvements and classification gains? Can you report Spearman correlations as well?

- For TBuckets-Clustering, how was Max Dist chosen (values in parentheses)? Was there a dev set, or was it tuned on test topics?

Additional comments
- Consider reporting ablations for the scoring components (size vs WL vs EVS, with/without sigma1^2 weighting). 
- Comparing against more recent coherence measures (e.g., C_v, C_uci, C_npmi variants already in Röder’s framework) with the same reference corpora would strengthen the claims.
- Releasing code and configurations would improve reproducibility.
