Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using semantic similarity from pre-trained word embeddings. The central idea is that a coherent topic should yield one dominant bucket. Three variants are introduced: (i) TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering with a max-distance parameter), (ii) TBuckets-SVD (assign each topic word to the most similar principal direction from an SVD of the topic word embedding matrix), and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a parameter-free post-hoc reorganization of words into/out of the largest bucket based on dynamic thresholds).
- Coherence scores are derived from the largest bucket’s size and optionally weighted by word order, eigenvector similarity, and the top singular value. The measures are evaluated via Pearson correlation with human coherence ratings on three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), and also used to select better topic sets for weakly supervised text classification.
- My view of the main contributions (in order): (1) A simple, interpretable, largely parameter-free SVD-based “bucketing” approach for topic coherence using word embeddings; (2) Empirical evidence that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves competitive-to-better correlations with human judgments vs. strong baselines; (3) A small application showing that selecting topic sets by TBuckets can improve downstream topic-labelling-based classification in some cases.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and interpretability: The “largest bucket = dominant theme” intuition is easy to grasp and aligns with how humans inspect topics. The approach yields interpretable artifacts (buckets) rather than opaque scalar scores.
- Mostly parameter-free SVD variants: TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no tuning, which is attractive in practice and compares favorably to coherence families that need multiple hyperparameters and external corpora.
- Competitive empirical performance: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the best configuration reported by Röder et al. (2015) on NYT and 20NG and is close on Genomics. Correlations in the 0.83–0.87 range on two datasets are strong for a simple method using only pre-trained embeddings.
- Practical utility beyond scoring: The classification experiment suggests that filtering topic runs by TBuckets can improve or maintain performance with the same human annotation budget (notably on POLITICS vs SCIENCE and PC vs MAC).
- Clear connection to distributional semantics: Leveraging off-the-shelf GloVe embeddings makes the approach easy to reproduce in principle and avoids domain-specific co-occurrence counting.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental relative to embedding-based coherence: Prior work has used word vectors to quantify topic coherence (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson, 2013; simple pairwise cosine/NPMI baselines). The specific SVD “eigenvector as bucket identifier” idea is interesting but not clearly justified theoretically or contrasted against simpler and natural baselines (e.g., average pairwise cosine similarity across top-N words, spectral clustering, k-means on embeddings). Such baselines should be included.
- Methodological clarity and justification gaps:
  - TBuckets-SVD assigns words to the most similar eigenvector from V, using the first n eigenvectors (n = number of topic words). Why V (feature space) and not U (word space), which directly gives word loadings? Are vectors length-normalized before cosine? How is “similarity to an eigenvector” defined when components can flip sign?
  - The reorganization rule and threshold formula are ad hoc and under-explained. The threshold AWES − AWPS/AWES is dimensionally unusual (and may be missing parentheses). No sensitivity or ablation is provided to show its necessity or robustness.
  - Weighting scores by the square of the top singular value is asserted without justification; it could reward burstiness or variance unrelated to semantic coherence.
- Evaluation omissions and inconsistencies:
  - No statistical significance testing on correlation differences; gains over the Röder baseline are modest and may not be significant with only 100 topics per dataset.
  - Missing comparisons to widely used coherence variants (e.g., CV) beyond quoting “best settings” from Röder, and missing embedding-based cosine/NPMI baselines.
  - The classification results are mixed: on MED vs SPACE, the “lowest-coherence” sets outperform the “highest,” contradicting the claimed utility; on POLITICS vs RELIGION, S2-highest underperforms S1. The paper’s interpretation is unconvincing without error bars and more runs.
- Domain mismatch and generality: Using general-domain GloVe for Genomics likely hurts performance; the paper acknowledges this but does not test domain-specific embeddings or analyze OOV handling, limiting claims of generality.
- Reproducibility and detail: Important experimental details are missing (normalization choices, distance/linkage specifics, MaxDist selection, number of topic words N beyond the datasets, OOV treatment). No code or data links are provided, and several references are incomplete.

Questions to authors
- TBuckets-SVD details: Do you L2-normalize word embeddings and eigenvectors before computing cosine similarity? How do you handle the sign ambiguity of eigenvectors? Why choose the first n eigenvectors from V instead of using U (which captures word scores) or PCA loadings on words directly?
- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the exact formula (parentheses) and provide intuition. Did you try fixed margins or simpler criteria? How sensitive are results to this step (ablation)?
- Baselines: How does TBuckets compare to a simple baseline that averages pairwise cosine similarities among top-N words, or to k-means/spectral clustering buckets? Can you include CV and NPMI-based measures computed on the same external corpus for fairness?
- Statistical testing: Are gains over Röder et al. significant (e.g., Steiger’s test for dependent correlations)?
- Practical settings: What is N (number of topic words) in all experiments, and how do results vary with N? How do you handle OOV words in embeddings? Any results with domain-specific embeddings for Genomics?

Overall recommendation
- Weak reject. The paper presents a neat, interpretable idea with promising empirical results and low parameter overhead. However, the methodological novelty over existing embedding-based coherence is limited; key algorithmic choices (SVD space, reorg threshold, singular-value weighting) lack justification; and the evaluation omits critical baselines and significance analyses, with mixed downstream evidence. With stronger baselines, clearer exposition, ablations, and significance testing, this work could be a solid contribution.
