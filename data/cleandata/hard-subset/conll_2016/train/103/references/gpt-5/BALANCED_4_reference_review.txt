Summary
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic-coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” of semantically related words using pre-trained word embeddings. The key intuition is that a coherent topic will produce one dominant bucket. Three realizations are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering with a distance threshold; (ii) an SVD-based approach that uses eigenvectors of ATA (A: matrix of topic word embeddings) as orthogonal “sub-theme” directions and assigns each word to its most similar eigenvector; and (iii) a reorganization step (SVD-Reorg) that moves words into/out of the largest bucket using a dynamic threshold derived from average word–word and word–eigenvector similarities. Topic coherence is computed from properties of the largest bucket (size, LDA word-order weighting, and eigenvector similarity, optionally weighted by the top singular value). On three standard datasets with human coherence ratings (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), SVD-Reorg correlates better than Röder et al. (2015)’s best setting on 2/3 datasets. As an application, selecting topic runs with higher TBuckets coherence improves weakly supervised text classification in most cases.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, parameter-free SVD-based bucketing method for topic coherence, including a reorganization heuristic that emphasizes a dominant bucket.
2) An empirical evaluation on standard benchmarks showing competitive/better Pearson correlations with human judgments versus a strong prior framework, and a small application showing that filtering by coherence can improve topic-labeling-based classification.
3) A comparison to a clustering-based variant, illustrating the generality of the bucketing idea.

Strengths
- Practical, intuitively grounded formulation: The “largest coherent bucket” intuition maps well to how humans assess topics. Using off-the-shelf embeddings and small per-topic SVD makes the method easy to implement and computationally light.
- Parameter-free SVD variants: SVD and SVD-Reorg avoid corpus-specific tuning (a frequent pain point for PMI/NPMI-based measures). The reorganization step is well-motivated by observed assignment errors and improves results on two datasets.
- Competitive results on standard data: SVD-Reorg exceeds Röder et al.’s best on NYT and 20NG and is close on Genomics. The paper reports multiple scoring variants (size, WL, EVS), showing robustness and the effect of reorganization.
- Demonstrated downstream utility: Selecting higher-coherence topic runs yields better macro-F1 in 3/4 20NG binary tasks and highlights the risk of low-quality topics in weak supervision. This increases the practical relevance beyond intrinsic correlation.

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical/methodological justification: Assigning words to eigenvectors of ATA as “sub-themes” is novel but under-explained. Why should eigenvectors in embedding space correspond to coherent semantic directions for small n? The weighting by the square of the largest singular value also lacks justification or ablation.
- Ambiguity and missing details: The dynamic threshold formula appears as “AWES − AWPSAWES,” which is ambiguous (is it AWES − AWPS/AWES?). Ranges and behavior (e.g., negative thresholds) are not discussed. Handling of negative cosine similarities to eigenvectors, tie-breaking, and whether vectors are normalized are not specified. It’s unclear how many buckets are allowed (up to n?) and whether empty/singleton buckets are treated specially.
- Evaluation gaps and significance: Correlation improvements are modest in places; no statistical significance tests or confidence intervals are provided. Only Pearson’s r is reported; Spearman’s ρ would be appropriate for ranking-based use. Sensitivity to the number of top words (here fixed at 10) is not studied, though coherence metrics can be N-dependent.
- Domain robustness: Performance drops on Genomics relative to Röder et al. Using general GloVe vectors likely hurts domain topics. The method’s dependence on embedding coverage and domain mismatch is only noted in future work rather than addressed experimentally (e.g., in-domain embeddings).
- Clustering variant comparability: The agglomerative approach requires a distance threshold; the reported best thresholds differ across datasets (values in parentheses). This contrasts with the parameter-free claim and complicates fair comparison to Röder et al., whose “best” setting also reflects configuration choices. More care is needed to control tuning across datasets.
- Reproducibility and presentation: Several typos/inconsistencies (e.g., a bucket example mistakenly referencing “storm, weather”), lack of LDA training details (topic count, priors, preprocessing), OOV handling for embeddings, and no code release reduce reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the reorganization threshold formula precisely (with parentheses) and its numerical behavior (range, sign). How sensitive are results to this choice? Any failure cases?
- Are cosine similarities computed on L2-normalized word vectors and eigenvectors? How are negative cosines handled in assignment and scoring?
- How many buckets are permitted in SVD (n? fewer?) and how are ties resolved? Do singleton buckets influence reorganization decisions?
- Why weight coherence scores by the square of the top singular value? Did you evaluate without this weighting or with alternative normalizations?
- How sensitive are results to the number of top words per topic (e.g., N=5/10/15) and to the choice of embeddings (e.g., word2vec, fastText, in-domain vectors)?
- Can you report statistical significance (e.g., Fisher z test) for differences vs. Röder et al. and include confidence intervals? Also, could you add Spearman correlations?

Overall, the paper presents a simple, appealing idea with promising results and practical utility. Strengthening the methodological clarity (especially around SVD assignment and reorganization), expanding the evaluation (significance, sensitivity analyses, domain embeddings), and improving reproducibility would substantially increase its impact.
