Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using word embeddings. The intuition is that coherent topics yield one dominant bucket. Three variants are explored: (i) agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering), (ii) SVD on the topic word embedding matrix with assignment to the most similar right singular vector (TBuckets-SVD), and (iii) an automatic reorganization heuristic that moves words into/from the largest bucket using derived, topic-specific thresholds (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The methods produce topic coherence scores based primarily on the size and weighted properties of the largest bucket. On three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics) with human coherence annotations (Röder et al., 2015), TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves higher Pearson correlation with human scores than the best configuration of Röder et al. on 2/3 datasets. A small application shows that selecting topic runs with higher TBuckets coherence can improve weakly supervised text classification.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, largely parameter-free SVD-based bucketing approach for topic coherence using off-the-shelf word embeddings, with competitive-to-better correlation with human judgments on standard datasets.
2) An automatic reorganization heuristic that adapts per-topic without tuning, modestly improving SVD bucketing.
3) A practical demonstration that coherence-based selection of topic runs can benefit weakly supervised classification.

Strengths
- Competitive empirical performance with minimal tuning: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg improves correlation over Röder et al.’s best (NYT 0.833 vs 0.806; 20NG 0.869 vs 0.859), using only pre-trained GloVe and no tuned hyperparameters. This is appealing in practice, given the heavy parameterization and search space in coherence frameworks like Röder et al.
- Interpretable intermediate artifacts: The “bucket” outputs mirror how humans group words, offering qualitative insight (e.g., identifying the dominant subtheme) rather than an opaque scalar. The examples provided illustrate this interpretability.
- Sound intuition leveraging distributional semantics: Using embeddings to cluster/assign topic words is a well-motivated way to capture semantic relatedness beyond co-occurrence statistics. The SVD view (principal directions in embedding space) is a reasonable way to model orthogonal subthemes within a topic.
- Practical utility beyond benchmarking: The classification experiment, though limited, shows that selecting high-coherence topic runs can improve weakly supervised labeling outcomes in 3/4 subsets, reinforcing the usefulness of the proposed scores for downstream workflows.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior embedding-based coherence: Using word embeddings to assess topic coherence (pairwise similarity, centroid similarity, clustering) has been studied (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson, 2013). The core idea—grouping topic words in embedding space and scoring concentration—is incremental. The specific SVD-based assignment to right singular vectors is the most novel piece, but still conceptually close to PCA-based directionality and clustering.
- Methodological clarity and specification gaps: Several definitions are ambiguous or underspecified, hindering reproducibility.
  • The reorganization threshold formula is unclear (typesetting suggests AWES − AWPSAWES; is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)?). The rationale for this exact form is not justified.
  • The scoring pipeline is vague: how WL, EVS, and “weighted by square of the highest singular value” are combined (e.g., WL*EVS vs linear weighting), normalization to [0,1], and tie-breaking rules are not precisely defined.
  • “Use the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers” (n = topic words) is ad hoc; why not top-k tuned by explained variance? How are sign/orientation issues handled when comparing words to singular vectors via cosine similarity?
  • Examples contain errors (e.g., “Bucket-1: {storm, weather}” appears in the Afghanistan example), which undermines confidence.
- Evaluation scope and rigor: 
  • Comparisons are against reported best settings from Röder et al., not re-run under the same environment; no direct comparison to other embedding-based measures (e.g., NPMI-based distributional methods). This makes it hard to attribute gains to the proposed mechanisms vs baseline choice.
  • No statistical significance tests, confidence intervals, or sensitivity analyses (e.g., to number of topic words N, embedding choice/dimension, OOV handling). The 20NG gain (0.869 vs 0.859) may or may not be significant.
  • Performance drops notably on Genomics (0.691 vs 0.773), likely due to domain mismatch of embeddings, but this key limitation is not addressed experimentally (e.g., using domain embeddings).
  • The classification application lacks comparison against selecting topics by other coherence measures (e.g., NPMI), so it does not establish advantage over alternatives—only that selecting high-coherence sets helps.
- Lack of ablations and analysis of reorganization: The “into/from largest bucket” steps are combined; there is no ablation to quantify each step’s contribution or to justify the threshold design. Many design choices (number of eigenvectors, bucket assignment criterion, role of the top singular value) are not probed.
- Writing and presentation quality: Numerous typos, inconsistent notation, incomplete references, and occasional confusing phrasing make the paper harder to follow. There is no algorithmic pseudocode or complexity/run-time discussion; code/data availability is not stated, limiting reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Please provide exact, unambiguous formulas:
  • The reorganization threshold; the conditions for into/from moves with the exact RHS; how AWPS and AWES are computed; and why this function is appropriate.
  • The final scoring: how size, WL, EVS, and “weighted by the square of the highest singular value” are combined; normalization details; tie-breaking; handling of negative cosine similarities to singular vectors.
- Why choose the first n right singular vectors (n = topic size) as bucket identifiers? Did you try using k determined by explained variance or cross-validated k? How sensitive are results to k?
- How are OOV topic words handled with GloVe? What fraction of topic words per dataset were OOV?
- Are improvements over Röder et al. significant? Please report CIs or significance tests across topics.
- Can you compare against at least one strong embedding-based baseline (e.g., NPMI-based distributional measures from Aletras & Stevenson, or topic-word centroid similarity) under the same setup?
- For Genomics, can you report results with in-domain embeddings? This would help assess whether the method is fundamentally domain-robust or embedding-dependent.
- Please provide code and exact settings to ensure reproducibility.

Overall assessment relative to reviewing criteria
- Originality: Moderate; conceptually incremental over prior embedding-based coherence work, with a novel but simple SVD-based bucketing and reorg heuristic.
- Soundness/correctness: Plausible but underspecified; several ad hoc choices lack justification/ablation.
- Empirical evaluation: Promising headline numbers but limited baseline coverage and robustness analysis; no significance testing.
- Clarity: Below average due to ambiguities and typos.
- Significance: Moderate practical appeal if clarified and validated more rigorously.

Recommendation: Borderline reject; the idea is interesting and results are promising, but the paper needs clearer specification, stronger baselines, significance/robustness analyses, and improved presentation to meet CoNLL standards.
