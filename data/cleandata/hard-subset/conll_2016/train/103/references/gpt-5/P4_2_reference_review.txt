Summary
The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” intended to reflect underlying sub-themes. Three instantiations are presented: (i) TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering on word embeddings with a maximum distance threshold), (ii) TBuckets-SVD (use SVD of the matrix of topic word embeddings; the first n eigenvectors of V are treated as orthogonal bucket identifiers, each word is assigned to its most similar eigenvector), and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step that moves words into or out of the largest bucket using a data-dependent threshold derived from average word–word and word–eigenvector similarities). Topic coherence is computed primarily from properties of the largest bucket (its cardinality, an LDA-probability-based word-order weight, and word–eigenvector similarity), with SVD-based scores further scaled by the square of the leading singular value. Experiments on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) report Pearson correlations with human coherence judgments, showing improvements over the best settings in Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets. A downstream application shows that selecting topic model runs by TBuckets scores can improve weakly supervised text classification.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A new bucket-based coherence framework that uses word embeddings and SVD directions to induce thematic groups and scores topics based on the size and quality of the largest bucket; two variants (SVD, SVD-Reorg) are effectively parameter-free.
2) Empirical evidence that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves state-of-the-art or better Pearson correlation with human judgments on two datasets, and competitive performance on the third.
3) A practical demonstration that TBuckets scores can guide selection of topic model runs to improve weakly supervised classification performance on 20NG subsets.

Strengths
- Strong empirical correlations with human judgments on standard benchmarks. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg improves Pearson’s r over Röder et al. on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859), with competitive values on Genomics. This directly addresses the core objective (human interpretability) using established evaluation protocols.
- Parameter-free SVD variants with simple, efficient computation. The SVD-based methods need no external corpora for co-occurrence counting, no window-size tuning, and (barring embedding choice) no thresholds, which reduces tuning complexity compared to many coherence measures that require parameter search.
- Conceptual alignment with human judgment via “largest bucket” intuition. The bucketing procedure operationalizes a plausible human heuristic: a single dominant theme indicates higher coherence. The reorganization step further aligns assignments with semantic neighborhood structure.
- Practical utility in downstream tasks. The selection experiment shows that choosing higher-coherence topic sets (by TBuckets) yields higher or comparable macro-F1 in 3/4 binary 20NG tasks and substantially outperforms low-coherence selections, indicating usefulness beyond intrinsic evaluation.
- Clear comparison breadth. The paper reports multiple scoring variants (size, WL, EVS, products) and three bucketing strategies, offering transparency about which components drive performance.

Weaknesses
- Methodological ambiguities and reproducibility gaps. Key definitions need clarification: (a) why V (feature–feature) rather than U (word–word) is used as bucket basis; (b) exact threshold formula for reorganization (“AWES − AWPSAWES” is ambiguous; likely AWES − AWPS/AWES); (c) handling of empty buckets and assignment ties; (d) distance metric and normalization for clustering; (e) OOV handling for embeddings. Lack of code or pseudocode impedes reproducibility.
- Limited theoretical justification for SVD bucketing. Treating the first n eigenvectors of ATA as thematic directions and assigning each word to its most similar eigenvector is heuristic. There is no analysis of when this is expected to recover semantically coherent groupings, nor ablations comparing U vs V, different numbers of eigenvectors, or alternative dimensionality reductions.
- Selectivity and multiple-comparisons concerns. The results table reports several TBuckets scoring variants; it is unclear whether a single fixed variant was selected a priori for all datasets or chosen post hoc per dataset. Röder et al.’s “best” setting is also a choice, but a fair comparison would predefine a single TBuckets configuration or use a dev/test split. No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported.
- Domain sensitivity and embeddings. Performance degrades on Genomics, likely due to domain mismatch of GloVe embeddings. The approach relies on embedding coverage and quality; the paper does not quantify OOV rates or test domain-specific embeddings, which limits claims of generality.
- Coherence definition may penalize multi-faceted yet interpretable topics. Optimizing for a single large bucket could undervalue topics that intentionally combine two tightly coherent sub-themes, which some applications may still regard as interpretable. No analysis is provided on such cases.
- Downstream validation is limited. The selection study does not compare against selection by alternative coherence measures (e.g., NPMI-based), and lacks statistical significance analysis. Reported gains are modest in two tasks.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the reorganization threshold formula and its rationale: is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? How sensitive are results to this formulation?
- Why use V (eigenvectors of ATA) rather than U (eigenvectors of AAT), given that U directly models word–word structure? Have you compared U-based bucketing?
- How many eigenvectors are actually used when n < d? Is n fixed to the number of topic words, and do you cap the number of buckets or prune empty ones?
- How are assignment ties handled (equal similarity to multiple eigenvectors)? Are similarities cosine, and are vectors normalized?
- For TBuckets-Clustering, what distance metric and linkage parameters were used precisely? How was the MaxDist range chosen for Figure 1?
- Do you fix a single TBuckets scoring variant when comparing to Röder et al., or select per-dataset? Can you report results with a single predefined variant across all datasets?
- Are the correlations statistically significant vs. the Röder et al. baseline (e.g., Williams’ test)? Please provide confidence intervals.
- What is the OOV rate per dataset with GloVe, and how are OOV words handled? Have you tried domain-specific embeddings for Genomics?
- Why weight SVD-based scores by the square of the top singular value? Please justify and provide an ablation without this weighting.
- Can you compare topic-run selection using TBuckets vs. a strong NPMI-based coherence for the classification experiments, with significance tests?

Overall, the paper presents a simple, computationally light approach with competitive results and practical utility, but it would benefit from clarifying the method, strengthening justification and ablations, and tightening the evaluation protocol.
