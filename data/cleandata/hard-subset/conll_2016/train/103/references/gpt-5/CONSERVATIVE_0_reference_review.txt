Summary
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic-coherence measures that group a topic’s top words into “buckets” intended to reflect sub-themes. Three variants are introduced: (i) TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering over pre-trained word embeddings); (ii) TBuckets-SVD (assign words to eigenvector “directions” obtained from SVD of the topic word-embedding matrix); and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a heuristic reorganization step that moves words into and out of the largest bucket using similarity-based criteria with a dynamic threshold). Topic coherence is computed primarily from properties of the largest bucket (size, word-order weights, and similarity to eigenvectors), sometimes scaled by the top singular value. The methods are evaluated via Pearson correlation with human coherence judgments from three datasets used by Röder et al. (2015), and a small downstream demonstration where sets of topics with higher TBuckets scores are preferentially labeled for weakly supervised text classification.

Contributions
1) A bucket-based coherence family that leverages off-the-shelf embeddings; two “parameter-free” SVD-based variants and a simple clustering baseline.
2) Empirical results suggesting modest improvements over the best configurations reported in Röder et al. (2015) on two datasets (NYT, 20NG), and parity/underperformance on Genomics; a small application showing that selecting more “coherent” topic sets can marginally improve weakly supervised classification.

Strengths
- Pragmatic, computationally simple approach: The SVD variants are efficient for typical topic sizes (n≈10, d≈300) and easy to reproduce in principle using public embeddings. This makes them potentially attractive for practitioners who want quick, off-the-shelf coherence estimates without heavy parameter search.
- Embedding-based coherence independent of reference corpora: Unlike PMI/LCP-based measures that require co-occurrence counts, TBuckets only needs pre-trained vectors, which can be convenient when reference corpora are unavailable.
- Some empirical gains vs. established measures: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves slightly higher Pearson r than the best Röder configuration on 20NG and NYT, indicating that the basic “largest coherent bucket” intuition has merit. The clustering variant performs reasonably on Genomics, suggesting robustness of the general bucketing idea.
- Attempted utility beyond intrinsic correlation: The classification case study, while limited, at least explores whether the coherence measure is useful in a downstream selection scenario.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and weak theoretical grounding: The central idea—group top-N words by semantic similarity and score the largest group—closely mirrors existing coherence intuitions (distributional similarity, sub-theme consistency). The SVD mapping from words to eigenvector “directions” as bucket identifiers lacks a principled justification; using the first n eigenvectors (where n is the number of topic words) is arbitrary, and the “eigenvector as concept” assumption is not validated. The reorganization rules and threshold are ad hoc and derived without theoretical support.
- Insufficient and potentially unfair baseline comparisons: The paper compares to “best performing settings” from Röder et al. (2015) rather than recomputing baseline coherences under the same resources used by TBuckets (e.g., same corpora/embeddings). Embedding-based methods depend on the embedding corpus (Wikipedia+Gigaword), while baseline coherence scores may depend on different co-occurrence sources, raising fairness/replicability concerns. No comparisons to more recent or alternative embedding-based coherence variants beyond Aletras & Stevenson are provided.
- Overstated “parameter-free” claim and unreported design choices: The approach depends on multiple implicit choices (top-N words per topic fixed at 10, embedding type/dimension, cosine similarity, using the top singular value squared for weighting, choice among scoring combinations like WL, EVS, WL*EVS). The reorganization uses a “dynamic threshold” formula whose derivation is unclear and even ambiguously written (AWES − AWPSAWES). These are effectively parameters/design decisions that could influence results, yet no ablations or sensitivity analyses are reported.
- Statistical rigor and significance: The reported differences in Pearson r are small (e.g., 0.869 vs. 0.859 on 20NG), with no statistical significance tests or confidence intervals. Given the modest margins and the small number of topics per dataset (100), it’s not clear that improvements are robust.
- Limited generality and scope of evaluation: Only three datasets, one of which (Genomics) shows underperformance for SVD variants, and only LDA topics with top-10 words are considered. It is unclear how methods scale to more words per topic, other topic models, or domain-specific embeddings. OOV handling and preprocessing details are missing.
- Downstream application is underpowered: The classification study shows small gains, lacks significance testing, and provides minimal details on labeling protocol, inter-annotator agreement, and controls for confounds. Selecting the “best 5” of 10 runs introduces selection bias; without robust experimental controls, it is hard to attribute gains to the coherence measure.

Questions to Authors
1) Justification of SVD design: Why select the first n eigenvectors (n=number of words) as bucket identifiers instead of, e.g., selecting by explained variance or clustering in the low-rank subspace? Have you compared SVD-based bucketing to k-means or spectral clustering over word vectors?
2) Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the threshold formula (parentheses, normalization). How sensitive are results to this formula? Did you consider simpler margins (e.g., fixed epsilon) and evaluate robustness?
3) Scoring choices: Why square the largest singular value for weighting? How do results change if you do not weight, or if you use cumulative explained variance?
4) Baseline parity: Can you recompute competitive baselines (e.g., NPMI/CV/UMass) using the same corpora/resources used by your method to ensure fairness? Also, please include statistical tests for r differences.
5) Generalization: How does TBuckets perform with top-20/30 words per topic? How do you handle OOV terms and multi-word expressions? Did you test other embeddings (e.g., domain-specific for Genomics)?
6) Downstream study: Provide details on topic labeling protocol, annotator agreement, number of topics per run, and significance tests. Can you randomize selection to control for run difficulty and reduce selection bias?
7) Reproducibility: Will you release code, configurations, and data splits for all experiments, including the exact Röder datasets and embeddings used?

Additional comments
- There are several typos/inconsistencies (e.g., an erroneous bucket trace with {storm, weather} under “official”), and the math notation could be clearer. Improving clarity and adding ablations/significance would strengthen the submission.
