Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic-coherence measures that group the top words of a topic into “buckets” based on semantic similarity. Three instantiations are presented: (i) TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering of word embeddings), (ii) TBuckets-SVD (assigning words to orthogonal directions from SVD of the word-embedding matrix), and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a heuristic reorganization step that moves words into/out of the largest bucket using a dynamic threshold).
- Topic coherence is computed primarily from properties of the largest bucket (size and optional weighting by LDA word order and eigenvector similarity, further scaled by the square of the top singular value for SVD-based variants).
- Experiments correlate TBuckets scores with human coherence ratings on three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), reporting higher Pearson r than the best settings in Röder et al. (2015) on two datasets and slightly lower on Genomics. A small application shows that selecting topic-model runs with higher TBuckets scores can modestly improve weakly supervised text classification.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, embedding-based operationalization of “single-theme dominance” via largest-bucket size for topic coherence, with a parameter-light SVD-based variant.
2) Empirical evidence that the approach correlates well with human ratings on two public datasets and can be useful for selecting topic models for human labeling.
3) A reorganization heuristic that attempts to correct mismatches between SVD-assigned buckets and human-perceived clusters without manual tuning.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and intuitiveness: Modeling human inspection as grouping semantically related words and using the largest group as a proxy for coherence is sensible. It captures the “one main theme” intuition that many other coherence metrics only approximate indirectly.
- Practicality and efficiency: The SVD-based method uses off-the-shelf embeddings and small SVDs (10×300), no external corpora or PMI estimation, and minimal hyperparameters (and claims to be parameter free for the SVD variants). This lowers the barrier to adoption.
- Competitive empirical results: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg yields higher Pearson r than the best settings in Röder et al. (2015) on NYT (0.837 vs. 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs. 0.859). Even the clustering variant is competitive in some settings (e.g., Genomics single-linkage “size” 0.774 vs. 0.773).
- Cross-task utility: The classification study, although modest, indicates that selecting runs based on TBuckets coherence can improve downstream performance in 3/4 subsets and clearly separates high- vs low-coherence runs in difficult settings (e.g., large drop for POLITICS vs SCIENCE when choosing low-coherence runs).
- Reorganization idea: Recognizes a real failure mode of assigning words by SVD directions and proposes an automatic fix inspired by word-word vs word-eigenvector similarities.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty vs prior embedding-based coherence: Using word embeddings to assess topic coherence and clustering words has been explored (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson, 2013; Röder’s framework includes vector-space confirmation/aggregation); the main novelty here is emphasizing largest-bucket size and an SVD-based assignment via right singular vectors. The rationale for using V (feature-space directions) as “bucket identifiers” is intuitive but ad hoc; mapping words directly via U or standard clustering on embeddings is more straightforward. No theoretical justification or empirical ablation shows why the V-based assignment is superior to simpler baselines (e.g., k-means/clustering with fixed k; centroid similarity; average pairwise cosine).
- Evaluation rigor and significance: Improvements in Pearson r are modest (often ~0.01–0.03) with only 100 topics per dataset, yet no confidence intervals or significance tests are reported. The method underperforms by a wide margin on Genomics for SVD variants. There is limited error analysis to understand when/why the approach fails (e.g., domain mismatch, OOV).
- Tuning and fairness concerns: TBuckets-Clustering requires a MaxDist threshold; Table 1 shows different values per dataset (in parentheses), but the selection protocol is unclear and may have used test labels, risking optimistic results. SVD-Reorg introduces a dynamic threshold formula without theoretical grounding; scaling by the square of the largest singular value is also arbitrary. There is no ablation demonstrating the impact of these design choices.
- Reproducibility and clarity: Important details are missing or ambiguous: exact similarity function (presumably cosine), OOV handling, proportion of OOVs (especially in Genomics), effect of number of top words (N=10 seems fixed), and the precise threshold formula (AWES − AWPSAWES is ambiguous). Several typos/inconsistencies reduce clarity (e.g., the “official” example accidentally references “storm, weather”; notation switches; limited motivation for WL*EVS combination).
- Baselines and breadth: Comparisons are limited to Röder et al.’s “best” configuration. There is no comparison to strong, simple embedding-based baselines (e.g., average pairwise cosine; centroid similarity; WETC-like measures) or to more recent coherence metrics. The classification study shows contradictory behavior on MED-SPACE (lowest-coherence runs outperform highest) and a drop on POLITICS vs RELIGION under S2, which weakens the downstream claim.

Questions to authors
- Why use V (right singular vectors) as bucket identifiers rather than U (word loadings) or projections onto principal components? Did you compare assignments based on U or on projecting word vectors onto the top components and clustering those scores?
- How were MaxDist values chosen for TBuckets-Clustering? Was a development split used, or were the test human ratings used for parameter selection?
- Are the reported r differences statistically significant? Please provide confidence intervals or permutation tests for Table 1.
- Clarify the dynamic threshold: is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES) or (AWES − AWPS)/AWES? What is its typical range, can it be negative, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- What similarity metric is used (cosine?), how are OOV topic words handled, and what fraction of topic words are OOV per dataset (especially Genomics)? Did you try domain-specific embeddings for Genomics?
- Why weight scores by the square of the top singular value? Provide ablations for WL, EVS, WL*EVS, with/without singular value weighting, and with/without reorganization.
- In Table 3, MED-SPACE shows “Lowest coherence sets” outperform “Highest.” How do you reconcile this with your narrative? Also, POLITICS vs RELIGION degrades under S2. Please clarify the selection protocol (number of topics, runs, annotator consistency) and provide statistical testing.
