Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes TBuckets, an embedding-based family of topic coherence measures that form “buckets” of semantically related topic words and score a topic by the size/quality of its largest bucket. Three realizations are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering on word embeddings; (ii) SVD on the topic’s embedding matrix with buckets defined by right singular vectors; and (iii) a parameter-free reorganization step that moves words into/from the largest bucket based on dynamic thresholds. The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation with human coherence judgements on three datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics) used in Röder et al. (2015), and a small downstream study shows that selecting topic-model runs by TBuckets coherence can improve weakly supervised classification.

Contributions as I see them:
1) A simple, computationally cheap, largely parameter-free embedding-based coherence measure (TBuckets-SVD and -SVD-Reorg) that achieves state-of-the-art or better correlations on two benchmark datasets.
2) A heuristic reorganization procedure that modestly improves the SVD variant without external tuning.
3) A demonstration that coherence-based selection of topic runs can aid topic-labeling-based text classification, with mixed but sometimes substantial gains.

Strengths
- Empirical performance and simplicity: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg attains higher Pearson r than the best configurations reported by Röder et al. on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859), using only off-the-shelf embeddings and no parameter tuning. The methods are extremely lightweight (SVD of ~10×300 matrices), practical for large-scale use.

- Clear intuition and interpretability: The “bucketization” metaphor aligns with how humans assess topic coherence (one dominant theme vs several medium sub-themes). The scoring focuses on the largest bucket and optionally respects LDA word order, which is intuitive and easy to explain to practitioners.

- Reasonable ablations and variants: The paper evaluates clustering vs SVD vs SVD-Reorg, considers multiple scoring variants (size, WL, EVS), and gives qualitative bucket examples, which helps unpack what the method is doing.

- Downstream relevance: The weakly supervised classification study shows that selecting higher-coherence topic runs sometimes yields better macro-F1 (e.g., +0.24 for POLITICS vs SCIENCE and +1.49 for PC vs MAC), supporting the practical utility of coherence scoring beyond correlation numbers.

- Reproducibility resources: Evaluation uses standard datasets and publicly available GloVe embeddings, facilitating replication in principle.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior embedding-based coherence: Earlier work already leverages distributional semantics to assess coherence (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson 2013; Lau et al. 2014; Röder et al.’s CV measure). TBuckets builds on these ideas but mainly rephrases them as clustering around principal directions and counting the largest cluster. The theoretical justification for using right singular vectors as “bucket identifiers” is thin; effectively, the method measures alignment with the first few principal components. It would be important to compare with simpler baselines such as:
  - Average pairwise cosine similarity among top-N words;
  - Cosine similarity to the centroid (or variance explained by PC1);
  - Largest connected component under a cosine-threshold graph.
  Without such baselines, it is unclear how much the SVD/bucketing adds.

- Statistical significance and robustness: The reported improvements (e.g., +0.010 r on 20NG) may be within sampling noise for n=100 topics. No confidence intervals, significance tests (e.g., Williams’ or Steiger’s tests for dependent correlations), or variance across runs are provided. The results may not be statistically different from strong baselines. Sensitivity to top-N (10 words only), to embedding choice (GloVe vs word2vec/fastText), and to preprocessing (stop words, MWEs) is not examined.

- Heuristic reorganization step lacks clarity and validation: The dynamic threshold formula “AWES − AWPSAWES” is ambiguous (is it AWES − AWPS/AWES?) and its rationale is only loosely motivated. The step appears ad hoc; ablations showing how often words move, and how much each move contributes to correlation gains, are needed. The “parameter-free” claim is somewhat overstated since the reorganization still depends on a crafted formula.

- Baseline coverage and fairness: The comparison cites “best” Röder settings but does not report multiple widely used measures (UMass, UCI, CV, C_A) under recommended defaults, nor newer embedding-based coherence variants. Given TBuckets uses Wikipedia/Gigaword embeddings, the edge on NYT may partially reflect domain alignment; the strong underperformance on Genomics underscores domain mismatch but is not further analyzed (e.g., with in-domain embeddings).

- Downstream study design is under-specified and mixed: The topic-labeling experiment provides little detail (number and expertise of annotators, inter-annotator agreement, topic counts/hyperparameters, seeds, annotation protocol). Gains are inconsistent: in MEDICAL vs SPACE, “lowest coherence sets” outperformed “highest,” and in POLITICS vs RELIGION the “highest” underperformed S1. Without statistical testing and stronger controls, it is hard to attribute improvements to TBuckets rather than run variance.

- Clarity and presentation issues: Some SVD notation and shapes are imprecise (e.g., selecting the “first n eigenvectors” from V), and the logic linking singular values to the final score could be better grounded (e.g., comparing to explained variance ratios). There are minor typos and formatting glitches; references are inconsistently formatted.

Questions to authors
- Significance: Are the gains over Röder et al. statistically significant? Please provide confidence intervals and appropriate tests for dependent correlations on the same topics.

- Baselines: How does TBuckets-SVD(-Reorg) compare to (a) average pairwise cosine of embeddings, (b) centroid similarity, (c) PC1 explained variance, and (d) largest connected component under cosine thresholds? These are natural, parameter-light baselines.

- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the exact formula (parentheses) and provide intuition/derivation. How sensitive are results to this choice, and how often do words move?

- Robustness: Do results hold with different embeddings (word2vec, fastText), varying top-N words (e.g., 5, 15, 20), and different domains (e.g., in-domain embeddings for Genomics)?

- Downstream study: Please detail the annotation protocol, annotator count/IAAs, topic modeling settings (K, α/β, inference algorithm, seeds), and provide statistical tests. Can you replicate the gains with fixed labeling budgets and identical annotators across conditions?

Overall assessment
The paper addresses an important problem and proposes a simple, fast, and interpretable approach that achieves competitive-to-strong correlations. However, the conceptual novelty is moderate, key comparisons and significance analyses are missing, and the reorganization step is heuristic and under-explained. With stronger baselines, statistical rigor, clarified methodology, and improved presentation (plus code release), this would be a solid contribution. In its current form, it is promising but not yet fully convincing.
