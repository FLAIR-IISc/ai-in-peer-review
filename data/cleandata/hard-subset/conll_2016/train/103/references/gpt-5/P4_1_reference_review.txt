Summary and claimed contributions
This paper introduces TBuckets, a family of topic-coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” intended to reflect sub-themes. The key idea is that a topic with one dominant bucket is more coherent. Three implementations are proposed:
- TBuckets-Clustering: agglomerative clustering over word embeddings with a maximum distance threshold.
- TBuckets-SVD: compute SVD of the topic word embedding matrix; treat leading eigenvectors of ATA as sub-theme directions and assign each word to its most similar eigenvector.
- TBuckets-SVD-Reorg: a parameter-free reorganization heuristic that moves words into or out of the largest bucket based on dynamic thresholds derived from average word–word and word–eigenvector similarities.

Coherence is scored primarily by properties of the largest bucket (size; word-order weighting; and, for SVD-based methods, similarity to eigenvectors, optionally weighted by the top singular value). On three datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics; 100 topics each with top-10 words and human coherence labels), Pearson correlation with human judgments shows TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforming the best configuration from Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets and being competitive otherwise. The paper also shows a downstream use case: selecting topic-model runs by TBuckets coherence yields improved weakly supervised text classification.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) A novel, parameter-free SVD-based coherence estimator that treats eigenvectors as sub-theme directions and uses a dynamic reorganization heuristic to consolidate the largest bucket; achieves strong correlations with human judgments (primary).
2) An intuitive bucketing perspective for topic coherence that operationalizes “one dominant theme” via bucket size and related scores, unifying clustering and SVD approaches (secondary).
3) An application showing that model-run selection by TBuckets coherence can improve weakly supervised classification performance (ancillary).

Strengths
- Empirical gains over a strong baseline with minimal tuning: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves higher Pearson r than the best Röder et al. configuration on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859), using fixed pretrained embeddings and no parameter tuning. This is practically attractive given Röder’s large configuration space and tuning costs.
- Clear, testable intuition: The “dominant bucket indicates coherence” aligns with human judgments and is straightforward to compute. Using word embeddings provides a semantic signal beyond mere co-occurrence and complements traditional PMI/NPMI approaches.
- Parameter-free SVD variant and dynamic thresholds: The reorganization rule based on AWPS and AWES removes manual thresholds while adapting to each topic. This addresses sensitivity concerns common in clustering-based coherence.
- Reasonable computational profile: For N=10 words and d=300 embeddings, per-topic SVD is inexpensive, facilitating deployment in pipelines that score many topic models.
- Initial downstream validation: The experiment selecting higher-coherence topic sets leads to better or comparable macro-F1 in 3/4 20NG binary tasks, suggesting practical utility of the coherence estimates for human-in-the-loop labeling.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation breadth and statistical rigor: Results are on three datasets with 100 topics each (N=100 per correlation). No confidence intervals or significance tests (e.g., Fisher r-to-z) are reported; improvements of ~0.01–0.03 may not be statistically significant. The downstream gains are small in some cases; standard deviations and significance are missing.
- Ambiguities and errors in method description: There are apparent typographical errors in the bucket walkthrough (e.g., “Word Seen: official. Bucket-1: {storm, weather}”), and the threshold formula is unclear (“AWES − AWPSAWES”). Clarification is needed on whether the threshold is AWES − (AWPS/AWES), and on normalization for cosine computations with eigenvectors. The choice to use “first n eigenvectors” as bucket IDs (when rank ≤ n and V is d×d) needs more justification and clarity.
- Heuristic design choices without ablation: The scoring weights (e.g., squaring the top singular value; combining WL and EVS) are ad hoc. No ablation shows the incremental effect of reorganization, singular-value weighting, or largest-bucket size vs. WL vs. EVS. It is unclear whether the improvements mainly come from bucket size alone.
- Sensitivity to embeddings and domain mismatch: Performance drops on the Genomics dataset, plausibly due to using general-domain GloVe. The method’s dependence on embedding quality and domain fit is not systematically evaluated (e.g., domain-specific embeddings, contextual embeddings, or retrofitting).
- Clustering variant tuning and fairness of comparison: TBuckets-Clustering requires a Max Dist parameter and shows results at specific thresholds (values given in parentheses). It is unclear how this parameter was selected per dataset and whether ground-truth labels informed tuning. Meanwhile, the comparison to Röder et al. cites “best configurations” without reproducing under identical resources, complicating fairness.
- Scope limited to top-10 topic words: Many coherence measures study sensitivity to the number of top words (N). The proposed methods and scores are tailored to N=10; no analysis is provided for other N, which may affect bucket distributions and correlations.

Questions to authors
1) Please clarify the reorganization threshold formula and normalization: Is the threshold AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? Are word vectors and eigenvectors L2-normalized before computing cosine similarities?
2) Why use the first n eigenvectors from V as bucket identifiers? Given rank ≤ n and V ∈ R^{d×d}, what is the rationale for n buckets rather than using the r non-zero singular directions? Have you tried r only?
3) Can you provide ablations quantifying the contribution of: (a) reorganization vs. no reorganization; (b) singular-value weighting vs. none; (c) largest-bucket size vs. WL vs. EVS; and (d) using only bucket size?
4) How sensitive are results to the choice of embeddings (e.g., GloVe vs. word2vec vs. fastText; domain-specific embeddings; contextual embeddings reduced to type embeddings)? Does Genomics improve with domain-trained vectors?
5) For TBuckets-Clustering, how was the Max Dist chosen? Was any validation set used, and was tuning done without peeking at human labels?
6) Please report confidence intervals or significance tests for Pearson correlations and for macro-F1 improvements in the classification experiments.
7) How does performance vary with the number of top words N (e.g., 5, 10, 20)? Does the bucketing approach remain robust?
8) Will code and data preprocessing scripts be released to facilitate reproducibility?
