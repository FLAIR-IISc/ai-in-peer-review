Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic-coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using semantic similarity from pre-trained word embeddings. The core intuition is that coherent topics should produce one dominant bucket (single theme), whereas incoherent topics split into several medium-sized buckets. Three realizations are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering over word embeddings (TBuckets-Clustering), (ii) an SVD-based bucketing that uses right-singular vectors as “bucket identifiers” (TBuckets-SVD), and (iii) an SVD variant with an automatic reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that moves words into/out of the largest bucket based on word–word and word–eigenvector similarities with a topic-specific threshold. Topic coherence is scored mainly by properties of the largest bucket (size, LDA rank weighting, eigenvector similarity), sometimes scaled by the top singular value. The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation against human ratings on three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), and a small downstream demo shows selecting topic models with higher TBuckets coherence can modestly improve weakly supervised classification.

Main contributions, in order of strength:
1) A simple, parameter-light framework (especially SVD/SVD-Reorg) for measuring topic coherence using pretrained embeddings, achieving correlations competitive with or exceeding the best configurations from Röder et al. (2015) on two datasets.
2) A novel SVD-based bucketing plus an automatic, topic-specific reorganization heuristic to better align words with the dominant theme.
3) A small application showing TBuckets can filter topic model runs to improve topic-labelling-based classification.

Strengths
- Practical and lightweight: The SVD-based variants require no parameter tuning beyond access to embeddings, are computationally cheap (no external corpus counts or windowed co-occurrence estimation), and are easy to integrate into topic model pipelines. This is attractive relative to coherence frameworks that require multiple configuration choices and external corpora.
- Competitive empirical performance: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves r=0.837 on NYT and r=0.869 on 20NG, modestly above the reported best from Röder et al. (2015), with strong performance from clustering variants as well. Even when not best (Genomics), performance is reasonable given domain mismatch of general embeddings.
- Clear intuition and interpretability: The “dominant bucket” notion closely mirrors how practitioners scan top words for a single theme. The bucket outputs are human-interpretable artifacts (examples in Table 2), more so than opaque scalar scores.
- Useful downstream demonstration: The classification experiment, while small, is a plausible use case: selecting topic model runs by coherence before manual labelling. The difference between highest- and lowest-coherence sets (e.g., PC–MAC) supports the utility of the measure in practice.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity and soundness (SVD bucketing): Using V’s columns (feature-space eigenvectors) as “bucket identifiers” is not well justified. Right singular vectors are orthogonal axes, not cluster centroids; assigning words to the nearest axis can be geometrically unintuitive and may be brittle. The choice “first n eigenvectors for n words” is arbitrary and risks overfragmentation; why should the number of potential buckets scale with the number of words rather than the intrinsic dimensionality or number of sub-themes?
- Reorganization threshold and scoring details are underspecified/odd: The dynamic threshold formula (AWES − AWPS/AWES) is ambiguous (missing parentheses) and may be negative; its scale relative to similarities is unclear. Weighting coherence by the square of the largest singular value can conflate vector norm/anisotropy effects with coherence and impedes comparability across topics; no normalization of embeddings or singular values is described. The precise similarity metric (cosine/cosine distance) and embedding normalization are not consistently stated.
- Evaluation rigor and fairness: The reported improvements in r are small; no statistical significance testing (e.g., Fisher r-to-z) is presented. Only 100 topics per dataset limits power. Comparisons rely on best numbers from Röder et al. rather than re-running under identical preprocessing, which may confound fairness. The Genomics shortfall is attributed to domain mismatch, but no experiments with in-domain embeddings (e.g., PubMed/BioWordVec) are provided to validate this.
- Metric design may be too narrow: Scoring depends heavily on the largest bucket (size, WL, EVS), despite the initial motivation that multiple-medium buckets indicate low coherence. Using only the largest bucket ignores the full bucket size distribution; measures like normalized entropy or Gini of bucket sizes could better capture fragmentation. No ablation isolating the contribution of reorg “into” vs “from” steps, or of the WL/EVS components, is provided.
- Presentation and reproducibility issues: There are typos and inconsistencies (e.g., the “official” line in the dry-run example mistakenly repeats “storm, weather”; unclear parentheses and definitions for CUMASS; references are messy). Important implementation details are missing: OOV handling for embeddings, whether vectors are centered/whitened, linkage distance metric in clustering, topic word count sensitivity (N=10 only), code availability.

Questions to authors
- SVD design: Why use right singular vectors as bucket identifiers rather than (a) clustering in the PCA-reduced space, (b) spectral clustering on a word similarity graph, or (c) using left singular vectors (entity space) or principal components as features then k-means? Do you have empirical comparisons?
- Bucket cardinality: Why set the number of candidate “buckets” to n (number of words)? Did you observe many singleton buckets pre-reorg? Have you tried selecting the number of buckets by an eigenvalue drop or explained variance threshold?
- Threshold formula: Please clarify the exact threshold expression (parentheses, ranges) and provide intuition for its scale. How often is it negative? Sensitivity analysis?
- Similarity and normalization: Are embeddings L2-normalized before SVD and similarity computations? Is cosine used everywhere? Did you consider embedding centering or debiasing to mitigate hubness/anisotropy?
- Statistical significance: Are the differences in Pearson’s r vs Röder et al. significant (Fisher’s z)? Can you provide confidence intervals?
- Generality: How does TBuckets perform for other topic models (e.g., NMF, neural topic models) and for different N (top 5, 15, 20 words)?

Additional comments/suggestions
- Consider bucket-distribution measures (e.g., 1 − normalized entropy) in addition to largest-bucket features; this may better capture multi-theme fragmentation.
- Add ablations: SVD vs SVD-Reorg; “into” vs “from” reorg; WL vs EVS; with/without singular-value weighting.
- Evaluate with in-domain biomedical embeddings for Genomics; this likely improves the observed deficit and strengthens your claims about domain portability.
- Include significance testing and re-run a strong Röder configuration under your exact preprocessing for a fairer head-to-head.
- Clean up typos, define all metrics precisely, and release code to improve reproducibility.

Overall: Interesting, practical idea with promising results and nice interpretability, but the SVD bucketing rationale and evaluation rigor need strengthening. I lean borderline accept if clarity and evaluation issues are addressed.
