Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic-coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” intended to reflect sub-themes. The core intuition is that a more coherent topic will yield a single large bucket and few small ones. Three instantiations are presented:
- TBuckets-Clustering: agglomerative clustering of word embeddings with a distance threshold and various linkages.
- TBuckets-SVD: SVD of the topic’s word-embedding matrix; words are assigned to the eigenvector (in embedding space) they are most similar to; the largest bucket’s properties yield the coherence score.
- TBuckets-SVD-Reorg: a parameter-free reorganization heuristic that moves words into or out of the largest bucket based on word–word and word–eigenvector similarities with a dynamic threshold derived from topic-level statistics.

Evaluation uses Pearson correlation between automatic scores and human coherence ratings on three datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics) from Röder et al. (2015), and a small application study showing that selecting topic models with higher TBuckets-SVD-Reorg scores can improve weakly supervised text classification.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, embedding-based bucketization approach (especially the SVD/Reorg variants) to estimate topic coherence, which correlates competitively with human judgments and is largely free of tuned hyperparameters.  
2) An empirical study indicating that coherence-based selection of topic models can improve downstream weakly supervised classification.  
3) A practical perspective on using the size/quality of a single largest bucket as a coherence signal, with multiple scoring variants (size, order weighting, eigenvector similarity).

Strengths
- Competitive correlation with human judgments and partial SOTA improvements: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg yields r=0.837 (NYT) and r=0.869 (20NG), surpassing reported best settings from Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets. This suggests the approach captures topical interpretability reasonably well.
- Parameter-light and efficient: The SVD-based methods require no tuned thresholds or external co-occurrence statistics and operate on small matrices (e.g., 10×300), making them fast and easy to adopt. This is a pragmatic advantage over many coherence measures requiring large external corpora and parameter sweeps.
- Clear operational intuition: The “single big bucket” heuristic mirrors how humans inspect top words and cluster them thematically. The paper provides step-by-step examples that make the idea accessible and motivate the scoring functions.
- Useful diagnostic outputs: Producing bucket assignments provides interpretable artifacts (e.g., which words fall into the largest bucket) that practitioners can inspect to debug topics, a benefit over scalar-only coherence measures.
- Downstream utility: The classification case study shows that preferring topic models with higher TBuckets scores improves performance (notably on harder PC vs MAC). This adds applied value beyond correlation reporting.

Weaknesses
- Novelty and justification of SVD design choices are limited: Assigning words to directions in V (feature–feature eigenspace) rather than to word-space components (U) is under-justified; using the first n eigenvectors (where n is number of words) as bucket identifiers is ad hoc; similarity to eigenvectors (as proxies for sub-themes) is not theoretically motivated. Alternative, more direct formulations (e.g., clustering in the U-space or low-rank projections with k chosen by explained variance) are not explored.
- Reorganization heuristic is heuristic-heavy and insufficiently analyzed: The dynamic threshold AWES − AWPS/ AWES is introduced without derivation; behavior when AWES is small, negative thresholds, stability across topics, and sensitivity to embedding domains are not studied. No ablations quantify how much Reorg helps and which part (into vs from) matters.
- Comparisons may not be fully fair and lack significance testing: The paper compares to best numbers reported in Röder et al. (2015) rather than re-running baselines under the same environment; this can bias conclusions. Confidence intervals or tests for differences in correlations (e.g., Steiger’s test) are missing. With only 100 topics per dataset, some gains may not be statistically reliable.
- Limited treatment of the Genomics domain and OOV handling: The approach underperforms on Genomics (r≈0.69 vs 0.77). The likely cause—mismatch between general-domain GloVe and domain terminology—is acknowledged but not addressed experimentally (e.g., with domain-specific embeddings, OOV handling, or retrofitting). This weakens claims of generality.
- Many design choices lack justification or ablation: Number of topic words (N=10), WL’s 1/log(i+1) weighting, using the square of the top singular value to weight scores, cosine vs other similarities, linkage selection, and Max Dist tuning (for clustering) are not systematically evaluated. The “parameter-free” claim is undermined by several fixed but unexplained choices.

Questions to authors
- SVD formulation: Why use V (feature eigenvectors) rather than U (word components) for bucket identifiers? Did you try clustering rows of U or projecting words onto top-k components chosen by explained variance? How sensitive are results to k?
- Similarity definition and normalization: What similarity metric is used between words and eigenvectors (cosine)? Are eigenvectors normalized before similarity? Do you center or whiten embeddings before SVD?
- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the derivation and range of the threshold AWES − AWPS/ AWES. Can it become negative or unstable when AWES is small? Provide an ablation quantifying gains from Reorg and from each move type (into/from).
- Baselines and significance: Can you recompute strong baselines (e.g., C_v/NPMI variants from Röder) in your environment for these exact topic sets and report 95% CIs or significance tests for correlation differences?
- Domain sensitivity: Did you test domain-specific embeddings for Genomics (e.g., biomedical embeddings) or provide OOV handling? How does performance change with different embedding sources/dimensions?
- Robustness: How do results vary with the number of top topic words (N), and with alternative word-order weightings? Does Spearman correlation (rank-based) tell a different story than Pearson?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, preprocessing details (tokenization, stopwords), and the exact topics used, to facilitate replication?

Overall recommendation: Borderline reject. The intuitive idea and empirical correlations are promising, and the parameter-light, interpretable pipeline is appealing. However, key modeling choices (SVD usage, thresholding, scoring weights) are heuristic and under-justified, comparisons lack controlled reproduction and significance testing, and performance degrades on a specialized domain without remediation. With stronger theoretical grounding, thorough ablations, fair baseline re-evaluation, and domain-robust experiments, this work could become a solid contribution.
