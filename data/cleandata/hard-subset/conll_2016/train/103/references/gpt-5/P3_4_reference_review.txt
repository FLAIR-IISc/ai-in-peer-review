Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top words of a topic into “buckets” using word embeddings, with the intuition that high-quality topics produce one dominant, semantically coherent bucket. Three variants are introduced: (i) TBuckets-Clustering via agglomerative clustering on word embeddings (with a distance threshold), (ii) TBuckets-SVD that derives orthogonal “sub-themes” from an SVD of the topic’s word embedding matrix and assigns words to buckets based on cosine similarity to eigenvectors, and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg that post-processes assignments by moving words into/out of the largest bucket using a dynamic threshold based on average word-word and word-eigenvector similarities. Topic coherence is then scored from properties of the largest bucket (size, LDA word-order weights, eigenvector similarity), optionally weighted by the top singular value. On three benchmark datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), TBuckets-SVD-Reorg slightly outperforms the best settings reported in Röder et al. (2015) on NYT and 20NG but lags on Genomics. The paper also explores a weakly supervised classification scenario where selecting runs with higher TBuckets coherence yields modest accuracy gains.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, interpretable bucketing-based coherence proxy using word embeddings, with an SVD-based, nominally parameter-free instantiation and an automatic reorganization step.
2) Empirical evaluation against Palmetto-style coherence measures showing competitive correlations with human judgments on two general-domain datasets, and a small application demonstrating utility for selecting better topic-model runs for topic labeling.
3) A human-interpretable diagnostic (bucket distributions) that may aid qualitative analysis of topics.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and interpretability: The “largest bucket” intuition is easy to understand and aligns with how humans judge coherence. The bucket outputs provide useful qualitative diagnostics beyond scalar coherence numbers.
- Competitive performance without manual parameter tuning: The SVD/SVD-Reorg variants require no dataset-specific tuning and achieve r = 0.837 (NYT) and 0.869 (20NG), matching or outperforming Röder et al. (2015) while using readily available embeddings. This is practically attractive.
- Elegant use of representation learning: Leveraging off-the-shelf GloVe vectors avoids large external corpora or PMI table construction and generalizes across domains better than pointwise co-occurrence counts in some settings.
- Practical relevance: The topic-selection experiment shows that coherence can guide which runs to label, improving weakly supervised classification on several 20NG splits (notably +0.24 macro-F1 on POLITICS vs SCIENCE; larger gap between highest vs lowest coherence runs), illustrating downstream utility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior embedding-based coherence: Using embeddings to compute coherence via clustering/centroid similarity is well explored (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson 2013; Lau et al. 2014; and many post-2015 variants). The core idea of grouping semantically similar topic words is not new; the specific SVD-based bucketing and “largest bucket” score appears incremental. A stronger positioning against embedding-only baselines (e.g., average pairwise cosine, k-means with silhouette, PCA variance explained) is needed.
- Methodological clarity and justification: The SVD assignment uses V (eigenvectors of A^T A) and assigns words by cosine similarity to these component directions, using the first n eigenvectors where n = number of topic words. This is unusual: with n≈10, you effectively generate as many buckets as words, and cosine-to-direction is not the standard way to use PCA/SVD (projection magnitudes or loadings would be more conventional). The eigenvector sign ambiguity and component interpretability are not addressed. The reorganization thresholds are ad-hoc and the formula “AWES − AWPS/AWES” is ambiguous (missing parentheses) and insufficiently justified theoretically. Overall, choices like weighting by the square of the top singular value are not motivated or ablated.
- Evaluation gaps and statistical rigor: Correlation improvements are modest; significance testing for dependent correlations (e.g., Williams/Steiger tests) is missing. Multiple design variants are tried (size, WL, EVS, WL*EVS; different linkages/thresholds), but there is no correction for multiple comparisons or principled model selection. Only three datasets are used, with Genomics showing substantial underperformance—unsurprising given the embedding mismatch—but no attempt is made to use in-domain embeddings (e.g., PubMed/BioWordVec) to test the stated hypothesis.
- Baseline coverage: The comparison is only to the best Palmetto configuration from Röder et al. (2015). Missing are embedding-based baselines (avg pairwise cosine; CUCI-NPMI computed over Wikipedia; word2vec/NPMI hybrids), and more recent coherence measures. Without these, it is difficult to attribute gains to the bucketing/SVD design rather than simply to embedding similarity.
- Reproducibility and missing details: Important details are absent: handling OOV topic words, tie-breaking, dimensionality issues when rank < n, eigenvector sign handling, exact agglomerative settings, runtime complexity, and whether code/data will be released. The topic-labeling experiment lacks specifics (number of topics per run, labeling protocol, variance across annotators, statistical significance), and improvements are small on several splits.

Questions to authors
- Can you justify theoretically why assigning words by cosine to eigenvectors of A^T A is preferable to assigning by maximal absolute projection onto principal components (or clustering in the projected U-space)? Did you try the latter?
- Please clarify the dynamic threshold formula: is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES) or (AWES − AWPS)/AWES? What is its typical numeric range, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- How do you handle eigenvector sign indeterminacy? Does flipping signs change assignments? Do you normalize eigenvectors and word vectors before similarity?
- What happens if you restrict to the first k < n components (e.g., k=2–3) and assign by projections? Is performance stable? Why use n components when n≈10?
- Can you report significance tests for the differences in Pearson’s r versus Röder et al. (2015) and between your own variants?
- For Genomics, did you try domain-specific embeddings (e.g., PubMed/PMC)? This seems crucial to validate the approach’s dependence on representation quality.
- In the classification experiment, how many topics per run, and how many labels per topic were provided? Are the differences statistically significant? Are annotators blinded to run selection?

Overall, this is a clear, practically motivated paper with an interpretable idea and promising results on two datasets. However, the methodological novelty is limited, several design choices lack justification, and the evaluation omits key baselines and significance tests. Strengthening the empirical comparisons (especially against strong embedding baselines), clarifying and ablating the SVD/Reorg choices, and addressing domain embeddings would substantially improve the paper.
