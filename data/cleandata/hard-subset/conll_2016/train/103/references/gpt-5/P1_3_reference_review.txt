Summary
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of simple, embedding-based methods to estimate topic coherence by grouping a topic’s top words into “buckets” (thematic clusters) and scoring topics primarily by the size/quality of the largest bucket. Three variants are introduced: (i) agglomerative clustering over word embeddings, (ii) an SVD-based assignment of words to orthogonal directions (interpreted as sub-themes), and (iii) SVD plus an automatic “reorganization” step that moves words into or out of the largest bucket using a data-derived threshold. The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation with human coherence ratings on three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics); TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the best settings reported in Röder et al. (2015) on two datasets. A small downstream study suggests that selecting higher-coherence topic runs improves weakly supervised text classification.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, largely parameter-free coherence measure based on forming “buckets” of topic words using pre-trained word embeddings, with an SVD-based and a reorganization variant that achieve strong human-correlation on two datasets.
2) An empirical study benchmarking against the widely used coherence framework of Röder et al. (2015), plus a qualitative analysis of buckets and a small downstream application demonstrating practical utility for topic-label-based classification.
3) A conceptual framing of topic coherence as dominance of a single thematic group (largest-bucket emphasis), offering interpretability and computational efficiency.

Strengths
- Empirical gains over a strong baseline on two datasets. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves higher Pearson r than the best configurations from Röder et al. (2015) on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859). The improvements are non-trivial given Röder’s framework is a well-established reference for coherence evaluation.
- Simplicity and efficiency with interpretable outputs. The approach uses off-the-shelf embeddings and very light-weight operations (clustering or SVD on ≤10 words per topic), producing bucket assignments that are intuitive to inspect. This is attractive for practitioners who need quick diagnostics.
- Parameter-light design. The SVD-based methods avoid tuning hyperparameters; the reorganization threshold is computed from topic-level statistics (AWPS/AWES). This reduces exhaustive search that is typical in coherence frameworks with large configuration spaces.
- Qualitative introspection. The paper provides interpretable examples where reorganization corrects obvious mis-assignments (e.g., moving “news,” “fox,” “series” into a TV-related bucket), supporting the human-aligned rationale of the method.
- Downstream utility. Selecting topic runs with higher TBuckets coherence improves or maintains macro-F1 in 3/4 binary 20NG subsets; the large gap between best and worst coherence sets (e.g., POLITICS vs SCIENCE) highlights the practical value of coherence-based run selection.

Weaknesses
- Methodological clarity and rigor. The SVD formulation is underspecified and conceptually questionable: A is n×d (n words, d-dim embeddings), and the method uses the first n eigenvectors from V (eigenvectors of AᵀA) as “bucket identifiers.” In PCA terms, V spans feature directions, not word clusters; similarity of a word vector to eigenvectors conflates variance directions with semantic prototypes. No justification is provided for using V rather than U, for using up to n directions, or for the chosen assignment rule. This undermines the theoretical grounding of the SVD variant.
- Reorganization threshold is ambiguous. The threshold formula “AWES − AWPSAWES” is unclear (missing parentheses/operation). The statistical rationale for combining AWES and AWPS this way is not justified, and the sensitivity to embedding scale/normalization is not discussed. This hurts reproducibility and confidence in the “parameter-free” claim.
- Limited novelty relative to prior embedding/distributional approaches. The core idea—grouping topic words by similarity and using group cohesion for coherence—has strong overlap with prior work using distributional similarity or clustering-based coherence. The paper does not compare against a strong, simple embedding baseline (e.g., average pairwise cosine similarity of topic words), nor against Aletras and Stevenson (2013) implemented with modern embeddings, making it hard to isolate the unique value of “bucketization” vs simpler embedding-based coherence.
- Evaluation gaps and potential selection bias. The paper reports multiple TBuckets scoring variants (size, WL, EVS, WL*EVS) and highlights the best without pre-specifying a single metric, raising concerns about ex post selection. No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported; Spearman correlations are not provided. Table 2 shows noisy tokens (e.g., “000”) among top words, but there is no discussion of preprocessing or OOV handling—critical given Genomics underperformance likely stems from embedding coverage mismatch.
- Downstream section is under-specified. Details are missing about the number of topics per run, annotation protocol (number of annotators, agreement), labeling effort, and statistical testing. Gains are modest for most subsets, and the experimental setup (selecting top-5 vs bottom-5 runs) risks selection bias without stronger controls.
- Design bias toward single-theme topics. Scoring solely by properties of the largest bucket may penalize legitimate multi-faceted topics. No analysis shows how TBuckets behaves when topics intentionally mix two coherent sub-themes.

Questions to Authors
- SVD details: Why use V (feature directions) rather than U (word directions)? How many eigenvectors are actually used when d >> n? Is the assignment equivalent to projecting word vectors onto principal directions and taking the argmax? Please provide the exact algorithm (including normalization).
- Reorganization threshold: Please provide the exact formula with parentheses and a short derivation or justification. How sensitive are results to this threshold? Any ablation showing performance without reorg, and with alternative thresholds?
- Baselines: How does TBuckets compare to a simple average pairwise cosine baseline, and to Aletras and Stevenson (2013) implemented with GloVe? This would help quantify the added value of bucketization and SVD.
- Preprocessing and OOV handling: Which GloVe model is used? How are casing, numbers, and OOV tokens handled? Could you re-run Genomics with domain embeddings (e.g., biomedical) to test domain robustness?
- Statistical analysis: Please report confidence intervals or permutation tests for Pearson r differences vs Röder’s best, and include Spearman correlations.

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The approach is appealingly simple and shows promising empirical gains on two datasets, but key methodological choices (SVD variant and reorganization) are under-motivated and insufficiently specified, the novelty over existing embedding-based coherence measures is limited, and the evaluation lacks some critical baselines and statistical rigor. With clearer algorithms, stronger baselines, significance testing, and domain-appropriate embeddings, this work could be a solid contribution.
