Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of methods to estimate topic coherence by grouping the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using semantic similarity from word embeddings. Three variants are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering), (ii) SVD-based bucketing using eigenvectors of ATA as “bucket identifiers” (TBuckets-SVD), and (iii) a parameter-free reorganization step that moves words into/from the largest bucket based on dynamic thresholds (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg).
- Evaluation compares Pearson correlation with human judgements on three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics; 100 topics each) against best configurations reported in Röder et al. (2015). The SVD-Reorg variant slightly outperforms the reported best on NYT and 20NG but lags on Genomics.
- As an application, the authors use TBuckets to select higher-quality topic runs for weakly supervised text classification, reporting modest gains on 3/4 20NG binary subsets.

In my view, the claimed contributions are:
1) A simple embedding-based bucketing heuristic for topic coherence with a reorganization step intended to better approximate single-theme topics.
2) An empirical comparison against the Röder et al. framework on three datasets, showing small improvements on two general-domain sets.
3) A preliminary demonstration that selecting topic runs by TBuckets coherence can marginally improve weakly supervised text classification.

Strengths
- Simplicity and interpretability: The “largest bucket size” intuition is easy to understand and aligns with how humans often judge topic coherence. The method uses widely available word embeddings and standard similarity metrics.
- Some empirical gains on standard datasets: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves higher Pearson r than the best configurations reported in Röder et al. for NYT (0.837 vs. 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs. 0.859), suggesting the approach can be competitive in general-domain settings.
- Parameter efficiency: The SVD-based variants avoid broad configuration searches typical of coherence frameworks like Röder et al., which can be expensive and require tuning. Even the clustering variant uses a single threshold.
- Practical application signal: The selection-by-coherence idea for weakly supervised classification is reasonable; on harder splits (e.g., PC vs MAC), selecting higher-coherence runs yields noticeably better macro-F1, which supports potential utility beyond standalone coherence scoring.

Weaknesses
- Weak theoretical grounding for SVD bucketing: The use of V (feature eigenvectors of ATA) as “bucket identifiers” is not well justified. Eigenvectors in embedding feature space are global orthogonal directions, not topic-specific semantic axes. Assigning each word to its most similar eigenvector (and taking the first n eigenvectors) is ad hoc; the choice of n equals number of words is arbitrary, and the similarity between word vectors and eigenvectors (sign, normalization, centering) is unspecified. This undermines the conceptual soundness of the core variant.
- Incomplete and ambiguous methodological details: Key implementation details are missing or unclear, including whether word vectors are normalized/centered before SVD, which SVD variant is used, precise similarity computations to eigenvectors, handling of negative cosine, tie-breaking, and the exact formula for the dynamic threshold (printed as “AWES − AWPSAWES” without parentheses). The weighting by the square of the largest singular value also lacks justification. These omissions impede reproducibility and raise concerns about hidden sensitivities.
- Limited and uneven evaluation: Only three datasets with 100 topics each are used; no statistical significance testing is provided for correlation differences that are often small. The method underperforms substantially on Genomics, likely due to out-of-domain embeddings, but no remedy (e.g., domain embeddings) or robustness analysis is offered. There is no study of sensitivity to the number of topic words N (fixed at 10), embedding choice/dimension, or topic model type. Runtime/complexity is not reported.
- Baseline comparison and fairness: Results rely on “best performing settings” from Röder et al. rather than re-running baselines under the same resources. Strong embedding-based coherence baselines (e.g., NPMI variants, Sheffield-style distributional vectors, or more recent embedding coherence metrics) are not directly re-implemented or compared under a matched setup. The application study does not compare TBuckets-based selection against selecting by a standard coherence measure.
- “Parameter-free” claim is overstated: The reorganization step introduces a derived threshold and combines multiple signals (NBPW, NBPE, LBPW, OBPW) with hand-crafted conditions, plus weighting by singular values. While not tuned on dev data, these are still heuristic design choices with implicit parameters; a robustness or ablation study is needed to support the claim.

Questions to authors
- SVD-based method:
  - Do you center A or normalize word vectors before SVD? How is the similarity between word vectors and eigenvectors defined (cosine after L2-normalization)? How do you handle the sign ambiguity of eigenvectors? Why use the first n eigenvectors specifically, and did you explore k < n buckets?
  - What is the rationale for weighting scores by the square of the top singular value? Did you try without this weighting or with alternative normalizations?
- Reorganization step:
  - Please provide the exact threshold formula with parentheses and units: is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? What is the motivation for this form? How sensitive are results to this threshold? Any ablations removing reorg or individual pulls (NBPW/NBPE/LBPW/OBPW)?
- Evaluation and baselines:
  - Did you re-run the Röder et al. measures with the same external corpora and preprocessing, or do you copy their reported numbers? If the latter, how do you ensure comparability?
  - Are the correlation improvements statistically significant (e.g., using Fisher r-to-z)? Please report p-values and confidence intervals.
  - How sensitive are your results to the number of top words N and to the embedding choice (word2vec, fastText, domain-specific embeddings for Genomics)?
  - For the classification application, can you compare selection by TBuckets against selection by a strong baseline coherence (e.g., NPMI) and report significance across more datasets/runs? Why does “lowest 5 coherence sets” sometimes outperform S1?
- Reproducibility:
  - Will you release code and exact settings (including embedding vocabulary handling, OOV policy, linkage/threshold ranges for clustering)?
