The paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to evaluating topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. Three techniques – clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization – are explored. The authors claim TBuckets outperforms state-of-the-art methods, particularly its parameter-free SVD-based versions. The primary contribution, as presented, is a new metric for topic coherence. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of competitive performance on standard datasets. A tertiary contribution is the application of TBuckets for improved topic labeling in weakly supervised text classification.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of grouping words into buckets to assess topic coherence is intuitively sound and represents a different perspective compared to existing methods reliant on PMI or distributional semantics. The "bucket" metaphor aligns with how humans conceptually understand topics.
2. **Parameter-Free SVD Variants:** The claim of achieving competitive performance without parameter tuning is significant. Many existing topic coherence measures require careful parameter selection, making them less accessible and potentially prone to overfitting. The SVD-based approach’s simplicity is commendable.
3. **Experimental Evaluation:** The use of established datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) and a standard evaluation metric (Pearson’s r) allows for direct comparison with existing methods, lending credibility to the results. The inclusion of results for text classification is a positive extension.
4. **Detailed Methodology:** The paper provides a thorough description of the TBuckets approach, including the different techniques and the reorganization step. The explanation of the dynamic threshold in the SVD-Reorg method is detailed and demonstrates careful consideration of the underlying principles.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Improvement & Limited Significance:** While TBuckets outperforms the state-of-the-art on some datasets, the improvements are marginal, particularly considering the complexity introduced. A Pearson's r increase of a few points does not justify a major contribution, and the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a substantial practical impact. The claim of "outperforming" is overstated.
2. **Overly Detailed & Redundant Explanation:** The paper suffers from excessive detail, particularly in Section 3. The example dry runs, while illustrative, are lengthy and contribute little to understanding the core concepts. The level of detail often obscures the main ideas and makes the paper difficult to follow. The repeated explanation of the SVD process and its geometrical interpretations are unnecessary.
3. **Lack of Rigorous Ablation Study:** The paper presents three variations of TBuckets but doesn't provide a rigorous ablation study to justify the inclusion of all three. For example, what specific benefits does the reorganization step (SVD-Reorg) offer over the basic SVD approach? A more systematic evaluation of the components would strengthen the claims.
4. **Questionable Justification of the Reorganization Step:** The dynamic threshold calculation for reorganization relies on "average word pair similarity" and "average word eigenvector similarity." The rationale behind using these specific metrics and the formula for combining them feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks strong justification. The "flexibility" argument is not compelling.
5. **Formatting & Presentation Issues:** The paper contains several minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of boldface, equation numbering). More importantly, the table formatting is poor; values are presented within parentheses, making them difficult to read at a glance. Proper table presentation is critical for conveying results effectively. The reference list also contains formatting errors.
6. **Writing Style & Verbosity:** The writing style is verbose and repetitive. Many sentences could be condensed without losing meaning. The introduction, in particular, contains a lengthy literature review that could be streamlined.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more compelling justification for the marginal improvements observed with TBuckets, demonstrating its practical significance over existing methods?
2.  What is the computational cost of TBuckets (especially SVD-Reorg) compared to existing topic coherence measures?
3.  Could you conduct an ablation study to assess the individual contributions of each component (clustering, SVD, reorganization) within the TBuckets framework?
4.  What is the sensitivity of the reorganization step to different values of AWPS and AWES? Did you consider other methods for determining the reorganization threshold?
5.  Please address the formatting inconsistencies and table presentation issues in the revised manuscript.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list requires careful proofreading and standardization.
*   Consider removing some of the overly detailed explanations to improve readability.
*   A more concise and focused introduction would strengthen the paper.



**Recommendation:** Reject. While the idea behind TBuckets is interesting, the incremental improvements, excessive detail, and lack of rigorous evaluation do not justify acceptance. The numerous minor flaws further detract from the paper’s overall quality. Substantial revisions, including a focused ablation study, streamlined presentation, and improved justification for the method’s benefits, would be required for reconsideration.
