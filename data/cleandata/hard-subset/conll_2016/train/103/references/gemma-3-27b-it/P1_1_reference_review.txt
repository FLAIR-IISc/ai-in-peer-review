## Peer Review: Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for evaluating the coherence of topics generated by Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to group topic words into thematic “buckets” – a process the authors argue mirrors human assessment of topic coherence.  Three techniques are explored for creating these buckets: clustering-based, Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), and SVD with reorganization.  The authors evaluate these techniques by correlating their coherence scores with human-annotated scores on three datasets, demonstrating performance competitive with, and in some cases surpassing, state-of-the-art methods. A key claim is that the SVD-based techniques are parameter-free, offering a significant advantage. The paper also demonstrates the utility of TBuckets in improving weakly supervised text classification.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Coherence Metric:** The TBuckets approach offers a conceptually interesting and intuitively motivated new metric for topic coherence.  The idea of mimicking human grouping of words into themes is a valuable direction.
2. **Effective SVD-based Implementation:** The SVD-based techniques, particularly with reorganization, demonstrate strong empirical performance while avoiding the parameter tuning often required by other coherence measures. This is a practical benefit.
3. **Demonstrated Application to Downstream Task:** Showing improved performance in weakly supervised text classification provides a concrete application and justifies the importance of improved topic coherence measurement.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves competitive, and sometimes superior, correlation with human judgements of topic coherence, especially on the NYT and 20NG datasets. This is a significant finding.
2. **Parameter-Free SVD Techniques:** The absence of parameter tuning in the SVD-based methods is a significant strength, making them easier to apply and less susceptible to overfitting. This is a compelling advantage over existing methods.
3. **Clear and Intuitive Explanation:** The paper clearly explains the TBuckets concept and the rationale behind each technique. The example dry runs for coherent and incoherent topics are particularly helpful in understanding the approach.
4. **Practical Application:** The successful application of TBuckets to improve text classification performance showcases the practical value of the proposed coherence metric.  This moves beyond simply *measuring* coherence to *using* it to improve a downstream task.
5. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a good overview of existing topic coherence measures and positions TBuckets within the broader research landscape.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Idea:** While the implementation is novel, the core idea of assessing topic coherence by looking at semantic relationships between words isn't entirely new. Existing methods (like those discussed in the related work) already leverage semantic similarity, and TBuckets builds upon that foundation.  The novelty lies more in *how* these relationships are analyzed (through bucketing).
2. **Dependence on Word Embeddings:** The performance of TBuckets is heavily reliant on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe in this case). The paper doesn't explore the sensitivity of the results to different embedding models or the potential for domain-specific embeddings.  A weakness of this approach is that it may not perform well if the topic contains rare or out-of-vocabulary words not well represented in the embedding space.
3. **Reorganization Step’s Heuristics:** The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg relies on heuristics (AWPS, AWES) for determining the thresholds for moving words between buckets. While the authors justify this approach, it feels somewhat ad-hoc and could potentially be optimized further. A more systematic exploration of different thresholding strategies could strengthen the results.
4. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper compares different TBuckets variants, a more detailed ablation study could isolate the contribution of each component (clustering, SVD, reorganization) more clearly. For instance, how much does the reorganization step *actually* improve performance beyond the basic SVD approach?
5. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** Beyond the example topics, the paper lacks a more in-depth qualitative analysis of the buckets generated by TBuckets. Showing concrete examples of how TBuckets identifies and groups semantically related words in real-world topics would be more convincing.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to different choices of pre-trained word embeddings? Have you explored the use of domain-specific embeddings, and if so, what was the impact on performance?
2.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the AWPS and AWES based thresholding in the reorganization step? Have you considered other thresholding methods, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed qualitative analysis of the buckets generated by TBuckets on the datasets used in your experiments? Specifically, can you show examples where TBuckets effectively identifies meaningful thematic groupings of words?
4.  Would the authors comment on the computational cost of the different TBuckets techniques, particularly for very large topics or document collections?
5.  The paper states that the TBuckets approach aims to mimic human judgment. Have you performed any user studies to directly compare human assessments of topic coherence with the TBuckets scores?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise.
*   Consider adding a visualization of the bucket formation process for a few example topics.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
