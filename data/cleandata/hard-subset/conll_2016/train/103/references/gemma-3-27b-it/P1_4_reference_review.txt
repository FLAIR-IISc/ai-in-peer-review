## Peer Review: Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for measuring the quality (coherence) of topics inferred using Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to group topic words into thematic "buckets," with the assumption that a coherent topic will result in a single large bucket and few small ones. The authors explore three techniques for bucket creation: clustering-based, SVD-based, and SVD with reorganization. They demonstrate, through correlation with human annotations on standard datasets, that their SVD-based approach (particularly with reorganization) outperforms state-of-the-art methods, and importantly, is parameter-free. They also show the utility of their metric in improving weakly supervised text classification.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A Novel and Intuitive Topic Coherence Metric:** The TBuckets approach provides a conceptually sound and human-interpretable way to assess topic coherence. The idea of grouping words based on semantic similarity and assessing the distribution of bucket sizes is a valuable contribution to the field.
2. **Effective Parameter-Free SVD-Based Implementation:** The SVD-based techniques, particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, achieve strong performance *without* requiring parameter tuning, which is a significant advantage over existing methods. This makes the approach more readily applicable in a variety of scenarios.
3. **Demonstrated Utility in Weakly Supervised Learning:** The application of TBuckets to improve text classification performance using topic labeling provides a practical demonstration of the metric’s usefulness beyond simply evaluating topic model quality.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates statistically significant improvements over existing coherence metrics on multiple datasets. The correlation with human judgments is consistently high, supporting the validity of the TBuckets approach.
2. **Clear and Well-Explained Methodology:** The paper clearly describes the TBuckets methodology, including the three different techniques for bucket creation and the rationale behind them. The example dry runs illustrating the bucket procedure are particularly helpful for understanding the core idea.
3. **Practical Advantages of Parameter-Free Approach:** The lack of required parameter tuning for the SVD-based methods is a considerable advantage. It simplifies the application of the technique and reduces the risk of overfitting.
4. **Relevance to the Field:** Topic coherence is a critical aspect of topic modeling. Improved methods for assessing coherence directly contribute to building more interpretable and useful topic models.
5. **Application to a Downstream Task:** The inclusion of a text classification experiment strengthens the paper by demonstrating a tangible benefit of the proposed coherence metric.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Underlying Techniques:** While the *combination* and application of SVD and clustering to topic coherence is novel, the individual techniques themselves (word embeddings, SVD, clustering) are well-established. The core innovation lies in the specific application and the “bucket” concept, rather than fundamental algorithmic breakthroughs.
2. **Reorganization Step – Justification Could be Stronger:** The motivation for the reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) is explained with examples, but a more rigorous justification—perhaps exploring the impact of different threshold values or analyzing the types of errors corrected by reorganization—would strengthen the argument. The threshold calculation seems a bit ad-hoc.
3. **Reliance on GloVe Embeddings:** The use of pre-trained GloVe embeddings may limit the generalizability of the results. Performance could vary significantly with different embedding models, and the authors haven’t explored this sensitivity. A discussion of the potential impact of embedding choice would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the results show improved performance, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of *why* TBuckets performs better than existing methods. Investigating specific examples where TBuckets correctly identifies coherent topics and other methods fail would provide valuable insights.
5. **Presentation of Results:** Table 1 is quite dense and hard to parse at a glance. Breaking it down into smaller, more focused tables (e.g., one for each technique) would improve readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The threshold used for reorganization seems adaptive, based on AWPS and AWES. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how these values are calculated and why this specific formula was chosen? Is there sensitivity to these values, and how does performance change with different formulas?
2.  You mention the sensitivity of results to the choice of word embeddings. Have you considered evaluating TBuckets with other pre-trained embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, FastText) or even fine-tuning embeddings on the specific datasets?
3.  Could you provide examples of topics where TBuckets-SVD-Reorg significantly improved the coherence score, and illustrate *how* the reorganization step led to a more coherent grouping of words?
4.  In the text classification experiments, how were the topics labeled by human annotators? Was the TBuckets coherence score used to filter or prioritize topics presented to the annotators?
5. The paper mentions a “unifying framework” in Roder et al. (2015). How does TBuckets fit into that framework and what new dimensions or configurations does it explore?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and concise, but some sections could benefit from a slightly more structured presentation.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the different TBuckets techniques.
* The inclusion of a visualization of the bucket structure for a few example topics would further enhance understanding.
