This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to evaluating topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic "buckets." The authors explore three techniques – clustering, SVD, and SVD with reorganization – to create these buckets, and claim state-of-the-art performance on established datasets. The primary contribution, as presented, is the TBuckets approach itself, specifically the SVD-based reorganization technique which purportedly outperforms existing methods without parameter tuning. A secondary contribution lies in the application of topic coherence assessment to improve weakly supervised text classification.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Problem:** Evaluating topic coherence is a crucial aspect of topic modeling, and a reliable, automated metric is valuable. The motivation for improving upon existing measures is well-justified.
2. **Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a reasonable empirical evaluation, correlating TBuckets scores with human judgments on standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics). The comparison to Roder et al. (2015) provides a benchmark.
3. **Parameter-Free Advantage:** The claim that the SVD-based techniques require no parameter tuning is a significant advantage. Many existing coherence measures demand careful parameter optimization. This simplicity is appealing.
4. **Demonstrated Improvement in Downstream Task:** The application to weakly supervised text classification, and the subsequent improvement in accuracy through topic quality filtering, demonstrates a practical benefit of the proposed approach. This connection to a real-world task strengthens the paper’s value.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Core Idea:** The core idea of grouping words to assess coherence isn’t particularly novel. Existing approaches already consider word relationships and semantic similarity. While the specific implementation with SVD and reorganization is new, the fundamental concept feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
2. **Insufficient Justification for SVD:** The rationale for using SVD, particularly regarding the interpretation of eigenvectors, is somewhat hand-wavy. While SVD is a powerful technique, the authors don’t adequately explain *why* it’s ideally suited to capture thematic groups within topics.  The explanation focuses on dimensionality reduction but doesn't concretely link this to thematic coherence.
3. **Reorganization Step Feels Ad-Hoc:** The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, while demonstrating performance gains, appears somewhat arbitrary. The thresholds and logic for moving words between buckets (based on AWPS and AWES) lack a strong theoretical foundation and feel empirically derived. The dynamic threshold approach, while attempting to address this, introduces additional complexity without clear justification.
4. **Overstated Claims:** The claim of “outperforming the state-of-the-art” requires scrutiny. The gains reported in Table 1 are modest and not consistently observed across all datasets. While statistically significant, the practical impact may be limited.  Furthermore, the state-of-the-art, as represented by Roder et al. (2015), is a highly flexible framework *designed* to achieve strong performance through parameter tuning; comparing a parameter-free method to a highly optimized one isn’t entirely fair.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a thorough error analysis.  Why does TBuckets fail to identify coherent topics in certain cases? What types of topics are particularly challenging? A deeper understanding of the method’s limitations would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of *why* SVD is theoretically well-suited to identifying thematic groups in topic words? What properties of SVD specifically contribute to this capability?
2. Can you justify the AWPS/AWES-based threshold for reorganization? What is the intuition behind this formula, and have you explored alternative thresholding strategies?
3.  What types of topics consistently present challenges for TBuckets?  Can you provide examples where the method fails to accurately assess coherence, and explain *why* it fails?
4.  How does the performance of TBuckets compare to Roder et al. (2015) when Roder et al. is also run in a parameter-free mode (e.g., using default settings)?



**Additional Comments:**

The related work section is comprehensive. The writing is generally clear and concise. The code would be beneficial for reproducibility.
