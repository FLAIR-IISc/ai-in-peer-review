The paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for evaluating topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic “buckets.” Three techniques – clustering, SVD, and a reorganization step on top of SVD – are presented, with experimental validation against human judgment and comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods.

The primary contribution is the *idea* of TBuckets itself – framing topic coherence as a problem of identifying thematic groupings *within* a topic’s word distribution. This is a conceptually interesting shift from existing approaches focused on word-word relationships (PMI, distributional semantics) or overall topic distributions. The SVD-based methods, particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, demonstrate promising results, outperforming existing methods on some datasets *without* parameter tuning, which is a significant advantage.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The TBuckets concept provides a fresh perspective on topic coherence evaluation. It aligns well with how humans intuitively assess topic quality – by looking for underlying themes. This is a departure from relying solely on statistical measures and represents a valuable contribution to the field.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique achieves competitive and, in some cases, superior performance compared to established methods (Roder et al., 2015), indicating the method's practical effectiveness. The parameter-free nature of the SVD methods is particularly noteworthy.
3. **Intuitive and Explainable:** The “bucket” metaphor makes the approach relatively easy to understand and interpret. The process of grouping words into thematic buckets provides a more human-understandable representation of topic coherence than many existing metrics. The reorganization step further refines this interpretability.
4. **Demonstrated Applicability:** The paper goes beyond simply proposing a new metric and demonstrates its usefulness in a downstream task – weakly supervised text classification – showing a tangible benefit to employing the TBuckets approach for topic quality assessment.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** While the intuition behind TBuckets is strong, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical grounding for why this grouping approach should correlate with human judgment. The link between bucket structure and human interpretability isn't rigorously established. A more formal connection to cognitive science principles could strengthen the work.
2. **Sensitivity to Word Embeddings:** The performance of TBuckets is heavily reliant on the quality of the underlying word embeddings. The choice of GloVe embeddings is reasonable, but the paper doesn’t explore the sensitivity of the results to different embedding models or training corpora. This is a potential limitation.
3. **Reorganization Step – Heuristics:** The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg relies on dynamically calculated thresholds. While the explanation is clear, the method feels somewhat heuristic, and the justification for the specific formula used for the threshold could be more thoroughly explained. An ablation study focusing on the impact of this step would be beneficial.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The experiments rely on datasets created for previous work (Roder et al., 2015). While this allows for direct comparison, it would be beneficial to evaluate TBuckets on a more diverse range of datasets, including those from different domains and with varying topic coherence levels.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you elaborate on the theoretical motivation behind using bucket structure as a proxy for human interpretability? Are there any cognitive science principles that support this approach?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding models and training data?
3. Can you provide a more detailed justification for the formula used to calculate the reorganization threshold in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg?
4. Have you considered alternative methods for the reorganization step, beyond the dynamic threshold approach? Could reinforcement learning be used to optimize the reorganization process?



Overall, this is a promising paper with a novel and potentially impactful approach to topic coherence evaluation. While some theoretical and experimental refinements are needed, the strengths of the work – particularly the novelty of the TBuckets concept and the encouraging empirical results – outweigh the weaknesses. I lean towards **acceptance**, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses raised above in their response. The bold approach and potential for significant contribution justify overlooking minor flaws at this stage.
