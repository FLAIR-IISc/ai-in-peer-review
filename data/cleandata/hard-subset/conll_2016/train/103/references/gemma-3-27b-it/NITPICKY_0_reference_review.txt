The paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to evaluating topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic “buckets.” Three techniques – clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization – are explored. The core idea is that coherent topics will result in a single large bucket, while less coherent topics will distribute words across multiple buckets. The authors evaluate TBuckets on three datasets, demonstrating superior performance to state-of-the-art methods, particularly with the SVD-based reorganization technique. A final demonstration links coherence scores to improved performance in a weakly supervised text classification task.

The primary contribution is the TBuckets approach itself, offering a potentially intuitive way to assess topic quality. The SVD-based methods, being parameter-free, represent a secondary, though noteworthy, contribution. A tertiary contribution lies in demonstrating the link between TBuckets coherence scores and downstream task performance (text classification).

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The “bucket” metaphor for evaluating topic coherence is a fresh perspective, departing from purely statistical measures like PMI or perplexity. The idea of capturing human interpretability through thematic grouping is conceptually sound.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported correlations with human judgments are compelling, consistently outperforming the baseline reported in Roder et al. (2015) on two of the three datasets. This provides initial evidence of the method’s effectiveness.
3. **Parameter-Free SVD Methods:** The complete lack of parameter tuning required for the SVD-based techniques is a significant advantage, simplifying application and reducing the risk of overfitting. This addresses a known limitation of many existing coherence measures.
4. **Demonstration of Practical Utility:** Connecting TBuckets scores to improved text classification performance strengthens the argument for its practical relevance and demonstrates that the measure captures something meaningful about topic quality.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Ablation Study:** While three variations of TBuckets are presented, a comprehensive ablation study to determine the *specific* impact of the reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) is missing. It is unclear how much of the performance gain is due to SVD *versus* the reorganization. The explanation of the reorganization logic itself (threshold calculation based on AWPS and AWES) feels ad-hoc and lacks strong justification. Why those specific parameters, and how sensitive is performance to their values?
2. **Overly Detailed Methodology Obscures Clarity:** The paper is dense with methodological details, particularly in section 3.1 regarding the reorganization step. This detail, while potentially valuable, hinders readability and makes it difficult to grasp the core idea quickly. The numerous formulas and parameters distract from the overall argument. A more concise presentation of the reorganization logic would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Dataset Diversity:** The evaluation is restricted to three datasets, all of which are relatively standard in topic modeling research. Exploring TBuckets’ performance on more diverse datasets, particularly those with different characteristics (e.g., shorter documents, domain-specific terminology) is crucial to assess its generalizability.
4. **Word Embedding Dependence:** The performance is inherently tied to the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe). The paper doesn't explore the sensitivity of TBuckets to different embedding models or the potential for using domain-specific embeddings, which could be critical for certain applications. A sensitivity analysis is needed.
5. **Formatting and Style Issues:** The paper suffers from numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing, citation style) and occasionally awkward phrasing. While not major flaws, they detract from the overall professionalism of the presentation. The use of bold text is inconsistent and often unnecessary.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more rigorous ablation study specifically isolating the impact of the reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg?
2. What is the computational complexity of the different TBuckets techniques, and how does it scale with the number of topics and words per topic?
3. Have you explored the sensitivity of TBuckets to different pre-trained word embeddings?
4. Could you provide examples of topics where TBuckets significantly differs in its coherence assessment compared to existing methods, and explain the reasons for the divergence?
5. Why was the threshold for reorganization calculated as AWES − AWPS / AWES and not another formulation?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., “Blei et al., 2003” vs “Aletras and Stevenson, 2013”).
*   Consider providing a visual illustration of the “bucket” formation process for a coherent and incoherent topic to aid understanding.
*   The introduction could be streamlined. Several sentences are repetitive or overly descriptive.



Despite the novelty of the approach and promising empirical results, the numerous minor flaws – particularly the lack of a strong ablation study, excessive methodological detail, and formatting inconsistencies – raise concerns. While the work holds potential, a significant revision addressing these issues is required before it can be considered for acceptance. I lean towards **rejection** at this time.
