This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to evaluating topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic "buckets." Three techniques – clustering, SVD, and SVD with reorganization – are presented, evaluated on established datasets, and claimed to outperform existing state-of-the-art methods, with the SVD-based approaches being particularly appealing due to their parameter-free nature.

The primary contribution, as presented, is a new metric for topic coherence. A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration of competitive performance against existing methods on standard datasets. A tertiary, though noted, benefit is the parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques. However, I find the central claim of a truly *novel* metric questionable, and the performance gains, while present, do not appear substantial enough to warrant significant deviation from established approaches.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the TBuckets concept and its variations. The example walk-throughs illustrating the bucket creation process are helpful in understanding the intuition behind the method.
2. **Established Evaluation Framework:** The authors rightly leverage the evaluation scheme from Röder et al. (2015), allowing for direct comparison against established baselines and facilitating assessment of the method's relative performance.
3. **Focus on Parameter-Free Methods:** The emphasis on parameter-free methods (SVD-based techniques) is a valuable consideration.  Many existing coherence measures require careful parameter tuning, which can be a significant drawback. Demonstrating good performance without such tuning is a positive aspect.
4. **Application to Weakly Supervised Classification:** The extension of TBuckets to text classification and the demonstrated improvements in performance when using only high-coherence topics add a practical dimension to the work. This suggests a potential application beyond merely evaluating topic quality.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the *combination* of ideas – word embeddings, SVD, and bucket formation – is presented as novel, the individual components are well-established. The core idea of evaluating topic coherence based on semantic grouping is not entirely new; existing measures like C_V (Röder et al., 2015) already attempt to capture semantic relationships between topic words. The contribution feels more like an engineering of existing techniques rather than a fundamental breakthrough.
2. **Incremental Performance Gains:** The performance improvements over the state-of-the-art, as reported in Table 1, are modest. While statistically significant, the gains are not dramatic enough to suggest a paradigm shift in topic coherence evaluation. The authors highlight outperformance on NYT and 20NG, but performance on Genomics is comparable to or slightly worse than existing methods.
3. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper presents three variations of TBuckets (Clustering, SVD, SVD-Reorg). However, it lacks a thorough ablation study to dissect the contribution of each component. For example, how much does the "reorganization" step truly improve performance, and under what conditions? Is it always beneficial? A clearer understanding of the individual contributions of each step would strengthen the work.
4. **Reliance on GloVe Embeddings:** The performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the pre-trained GloVe word embeddings. The authors acknowledge this, but do not explore the sensitivity of the method to different embedding models or training corpora.  This introduces a potential bias and limits the generalizability of the results. The chosen embeddings may not be optimal for all datasets (e.g., Genomics).



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed justification for the claim of novelty? Specifically, how does TBuckets fundamentally differ from existing coherence measures like C_V or those based on distributional semantics?
2. Please conduct and report the results of an ablation study, systematically evaluating the contribution of the "reorganization" step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg.
3. How sensitive is TBuckets to the choice of word embeddings?  Have you experimented with other embedding models or considered fine-tuning the embeddings on the specific datasets used in your evaluation?
4. The threshold in the reorganization step is described as being dynamically determined. Could you provide more details about how this threshold behaves across different topics and datasets? Are there edge cases where it might lead to suboptimal bucket formation?
