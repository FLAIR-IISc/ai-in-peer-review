This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach to evaluating topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic "buckets." The core idea – that a coherent topic should yield a single dominant bucket – is intuitively appealing and represents a departure from existing coherence metrics. The paper explores three instantiation techniques (clustering, SVD, and SVD with reorganization) and demonstrates, through correlation with human judgments, that TBuckets, particularly the SVD-reorganization variant, outperforms state-of-the-art methods on several datasets. A secondary, but notable contribution, is the demonstration that parameter-free methods (the SVD approaches) can achieve competitive performance. Finally, the application of TBuckets for topic quality assessment in a weakly supervised text classification setting adds practical relevance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The "bucket" metaphor for assessing topic coherence is genuinely novel. Existing metrics often focus on word-word relationships or distributional similarity, whereas TBuckets directly addresses the human interpretability aspect by modeling how we *group* words when evaluating a topic. This represents a conceptual shift in how topic quality is considered.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling evidence, through Pearson correlation, that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg consistently outperforms established coherence measures. Outperforming the state-of-the-art, even modestly, is a significant achievement.
3. **Parameter-Free Performance:** The success of the SVD-based methods *without* parameter tuning is remarkable. This simplifies application and reduces the risk of overfitting compared to methods requiring extensive parameter search. This is a practical strength.
4. **Practical Application:** Demonstrating the utility of TBuckets in a weakly supervised text classification task grounds the work in a real-world application and showcases its potential impact. Showing an improvement in classification accuracy (even small) reinforces the value of improved topic quality assessment.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** While intuitively appealing, the connection between the bucket distribution and true topic coherence feels somewhat heuristic. The paper doesn’t delve deeply into *why* a single dominant bucket equates to a more interpretable or useful topic. A more formal theoretical grounding, perhaps linking it to information-theoretic concepts, would strengthen the work.
2. **SVD Reorganization Heuristics:** The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg relies on thresholds (AWES - AWPS) calculated based on the dataset, that introduces an element of empirical tuning even if not explicit parameter tuning.  The justification for this particular threshold calculation feels a bit ad-hoc. A more rigorous method for determining when to reorganize buckets would be beneficial.
3. **Dataset Limitations:** The use of only three datasets, all originally used in Roder et al. (2015), limits the generalizability of the findings. Testing TBuckets on a more diverse range of corpora, including domains with different word distributions and topic structures, would enhance confidence in its robustness.
4. **Word Embedding Dependence:** The performance of TBuckets heavily relies on the quality of the word embeddings. While GloVe embeddings are standard, a sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of different embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec, FastText) would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of the "bucket" metaphor? Is there a connection to concepts like topic granularity or conceptual density?
2.  What is the computational complexity of each TBuckets technique (Clustering, SVD, SVD-Reorg)? How does this scale with the number of topic words and the size of the corpus?
3.  Have you explored alternative methods for calculating the reorganization thresholds in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, beyond the AWES-AWPS approach?
4.  Did you consider other semantic similarity measures beyond cosine similarity for the bucket creation and reorganization steps?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The experimental setup is sound, and the results are convincingly demonstrated. Despite the minor weaknesses, the novelty and promising results strongly suggest acceptance. The boldness of the approach and the potential for a new perspective on topic coherence evaluation outweigh the limitations. I recommend acceptance, contingent on addressing the questions raised above.
