## Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

This paper proposes TBuckets, a new approach to evaluating topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The authors explore three techniques – clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization – and demonstrate improved correlation with human judgment on standard datasets compared to existing methods.

**Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A novel, intuitive approach to topic coherence assessment:** The core idea of "bucketing" words based on semantic similarity reflects a human-like process for judging topic quality, offering a potentially more interpretable measure than purely statistical approaches.
2. **Demonstrated empirical improvement:**  The SVD-based TBuckets approach, particularly with reorganization, demonstrates competitive or superior performance to state-of-the-art methods on established datasets, offering a valuable alternative for topic evaluation.
3. **Parameter-free SVD variants:** The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques is a practical advantage, reducing the burden of hyperparameter tuning.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of topic quality and the limitations of existing evaluation metrics. The motivation for developing TBuckets is well-founded.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The evaluation on multiple datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) using a standard evaluation protocol (Pearson correlation) is commendable.  The comparison against established baselines strengthens the claim of improved performance.
3. **Well-Explained Methodology:** The description of TBuckets, including the three techniques, is generally clear and detailed. The illustrative example of the bucket creation process for coherent and non-coherent topics is particularly helpful.
4. **Practical Application Demonstrations:** The exploration of using TBuckets for improving text classification through quality-aware topic selection demonstrates a potential real-world application. The improvement in classification accuracy, albeit modest, is a positive sign.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Insufficient Novelty in Core Components:**  While the *combination* of techniques is novel, the individual components (word embeddings, SVD, clustering) are well-established. The paper doesn’t sufficiently highlight *what* about the way these are combined is fundamentally different or innovative beyond the intuitive bucketing idea. The reorganization step, while effective, feels like a necessary correction rather than a core innovation.
2. **Overly Detailed Reorganization Methodology:** The description of the reorganization process (Section 3.1) is excessively complex and reliant on dynamically calculated thresholds (AWPS, AWES). This adds unnecessary complexity and reduces interpretability. The reasoning behind using these specific thresholds isn't fully convincing, and it feels like an attempt to over-engineer a relatively simple concept.  Why not simply move words to the largest bucket if their similarity to *any* word in that bucket exceeds their similarity to the closest word in their current bucket?
3. **Limited Ablation Study:** The paper doesn't provide a sufficiently detailed ablation study to understand the individual contribution of each component of TBuckets (clustering, SVD, reorganization).  For example, how much does the reorganization step *actually* contribute to the improved performance?  Presenting results for TBuckets-SVD *without* reorganization would be valuable.
4. **Formatting and Writing inconsistencies:** Numerous minor inconsistencies throughout the paper detract from its polish.  For example, some section headings are not consistently formatted (e.g., "2 Background" vs. "4 Experimental Analysis"). There are instances of redundant phrasing and slightly awkward wording.  The reference formatting is also inconsistent.
5. **Lack of Discussion of Limitations:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of the limitations of TBuckets. For example, how sensitive is the approach to the quality of the underlying word embeddings? Does it perform equally well across different domain-specific corpora?


**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed ablation study showing the contribution of each component (clustering, SVD, reorganization) to the overall performance of TBuckets?
2.  What is the computational cost of the reorganization step, particularly for large topics? Is it scalable to very large datasets?
3.  Can you simplify the reorganization methodology by removing the dynamic threshold calculation and implementing a more straightforward similarity-based criterion for moving words between buckets?
4.  Have you investigated the sensitivity of TBuckets to the choice of word embeddings?
5.  What are the potential failure cases of TBuckets, and what steps could be taken to mitigate them?



**Additional Comments:**

* Please carefully proofread the paper for formatting inconsistencies and grammatical errors.
*  The references should be checked for consistency and accuracy.
*  Consider adding a visual illustration of the bucketing process, perhaps a diagram showing how words are grouped for a sample topic.



**Overall Assessment:**

While the paper presents a potentially valuable contribution to the field of topic modeling evaluation, the lack of significant novelty in the individual components, the overly complex reorganization methodology, and the numerous minor flaws prevent me from recommending acceptance in its current form.  I recommend **rejection** but encourage the authors to address the weaknesses identified in this review and resubmit a revised version. A significantly simplified reorganization method and a more detailed ablation study are crucial for improving the paper's impact.
