This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measure topic quality by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core idea is that coherent topics should result in a single large bucket, while less coherent topics will have multiple medium-sized buckets. Three techniques – clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization – are explored for bucket creation. The method is evaluated on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) by correlating estimated coherence scores with human annotations and compared to state-of-the-art results. The authors also demonstrate the application of TBuckets for improving text classification with weak supervision.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **TBuckets Framework:** The proposal of the TBuckets framework itself constitutes the primary contribution. This offers a different perspective on topic coherence evaluation—assessing how well topic words cluster into thematic groups—which complements existing methods focused on word-word relationships (PMI, distributional semantics).
2. **SVD-based Techniques:** The exploration of SVD-based techniques (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) presents a practical contribution. These methods are parameter-free, a significant advantage over approaches requiring substantial tuning. The reorganization step addresses a limitation of the initial SVD approach.
3. **Application to Weakly Supervised Text Classification:** Demonstrating a practical application of the proposed coherence measure in improving text classification performance, by filtering lower-quality topics during annotation, increases the impact and relevance of the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The TBuckets approach presents a unique method for evaluating topic coherence based on bucket formation. It moves beyond direct word-word similarity and considers a more holistic grouping of words based on semantic relatedness.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two of the three datasets, indicating its effectiveness in capturing human judgments of topic coherence.
3. **Parameter-Free Methods:** The SVD-based techniques are completely parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage and simplifies their application. This avoids the often-challenging process of parameter tuning.
4. **Practical Application:** The successful application of TBuckets to improve weakly supervised text classification showcases the practical utility of the proposed approach beyond simply evaluating topic quality. The observed improvement in classification accuracy is a valuable result.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the methodology, including the different techniques for bucket creation and the coherence score computation. The example illustrating the bucket procedure is helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** While the intuition behind TBuckets is understandable, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical justification for why this grouping approach should inherently measure coherence. A more formal connection to established notions of topic quality would strengthen the work.
2. **Dependence on Word Embeddings:** The performance of TBuckets is heavily reliant on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. The choice of GloVe embeddings is mentioned, but a discussion of the potential impact of using different embeddings or training embeddings specifically for the target domain would be beneficial.
3. **Reorganization Heuristic:** The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg relies on a dynamic threshold calculated based on average word similarity. The rationale behind this specific threshold calculation is not fully explained. A more rigorous justification or sensitivity analysis of this threshold would be helpful.
4. **Limited Dataset Diversity:** The evaluation is performed on three datasets, which, while commonly used, may not fully represent the diversity of topic modeling applications. Testing on more diverse datasets, including those with different domain-specific vocabularies, would strengthen the generalizability of the findings.
5. **Incremental Improvement:** Although the SVD-Reorg method outperforms the state-of-the-art on some datasets, the improvement is not substantial. The authors should discuss the practical significance of this marginal gain.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the theoretical motivations behind the TBuckets framework? Is there a connection to existing theories of semantic similarity or topic structure that underpins your approach?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Did you experiment with other embeddings, and how did they affect the performance of TBuckets?
3. Could you provide a more detailed justification for the dynamic threshold calculation used in the reorganization step? What is the intuition behind using the difference between average word pair similarity and average word eigenvector similarity?
4. Do you have any insights into cases where TBuckets fails to accurately reflect human judgments of topic coherence? What types of topics are challenging for your approach?
5. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the different TBuckets techniques, particularly for large topic models?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The figure illustrating the effect of varying Max Dist for TBuckets-Clustering is not sufficiently informative. A clearer visual representation of the correlation values for different Max Dist values would be helpful.
*   The paper references a lot of previous work. While this demonstrates awareness of the field, it could be streamlined by focusing on the most directly relevant publications.
*   Consider discussing potential biases in the human annotations used for evaluation.
