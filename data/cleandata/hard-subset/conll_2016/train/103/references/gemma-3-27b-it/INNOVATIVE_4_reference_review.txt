This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach to evaluating topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic "buckets." The core idea – that a coherent topic should result in a single dominant bucket – is intuitively appealing and represents a refreshingly different perspective on topic quality assessment. The paper explores three bucket-creation techniques (clustering, SVD, and reorganized SVD) and demonstrates competitive performance, even outperforming state-of-the-art methods on some datasets. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based methods is a significant advantage.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Coherence Metric:** The TBuckets approach provides a new lens through which to assess topic coherence, moving beyond traditional measures like PMI and distributional similarity. This conceptual shift is the strongest contribution.
2. **Effective SVD-based Implementation:** The SVD-based techniques, particularly with reorganization, demonstrate promising results and offer a compelling trade-off between simplicity and effectiveness.
3. **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** Showing improved text classification performance when using TBuckets to filter topic quality prior to labeling highlights the potential real-world impact of this work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold Idea & Intuitive Approach:**  The core concept of “buckets” is immediately understandable and aligns with how humans naturally evaluate topic coherence. This intuitive foundation is a major strength.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates that TBuckets achieves competitive, and in some cases superior, results compared to established methods. The consistent performance of the SVD-based approaches is particularly noteworthy.
3. **Parameter-Free Methods:** The complete lack of parameter tuning required for the SVD-based methods is a significant advantage in practical applications, simplifying the workflow and reducing the risk of overfitting.
4. **Practical Application:**  The demonstrated improvement in text classification via pre-filtering topics based on TBuckets coherence scores showcases the applicability and value of the proposed method.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and explains the TBuckets concept and its variations in a clear and accessible manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper compares different TBuckets techniques, a more thorough ablation study examining the impact of individual components (e.g., the reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) would strengthen the analysis. What specific aspects of the reorganization contribute most to the performance gains?
2. **Reliance on GloVe Embeddings:** The performance is tied to the quality of the pre-trained GloVe embeddings. The paper doesn’t explore the sensitivity of the results to different embedding types or training data. While reasonable, it’s a potential limitation.
3. **Limited Dataset Diversity:** The evaluation relies on a relatively small number of datasets, all of which are text-based. Evaluating TBuckets on different data modalities or application domains would bolster its generalizability.
4. **Heuristic Threshold for Reorganization:** The dynamic threshold used in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg relies on the average word pair similarity and eigenvector similarity. While the rationale is explained, the heuristic nature of this approach could be a source of instability or sub-optimal performance in certain cases.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** Examining specific examples where TBuckets’ coherence scores diverge from human judgment could provide valuable insights for improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed ablation study analyzing the contribution of the reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg? Specifically, what is the impact of different weighting schemes or alternative reorganization criteria?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with different pre-trained embeddings or considered training embeddings specifically for the evaluation datasets?
3. Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the different TBuckets techniques, particularly for large-scale topic models?
4. Are there specific types of topics (e.g., highly abstract or nuanced topics) where TBuckets consistently performs poorly compared to human judgment? If so, can you provide examples and discuss potential solutions?



**Additional Comments:** The paper offers a genuinely novel and promising approach to topic coherence evaluation. While some aspects could benefit from further investigation, the strengths of the work—particularly the innovative concept and strong empirical results—outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance.
