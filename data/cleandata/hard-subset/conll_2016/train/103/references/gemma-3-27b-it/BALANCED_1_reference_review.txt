This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measure topic quality by grouping topic words into thematic “buckets”. The core idea is that a coherent topic will result in a single large bucket, while less coherent topics will have multiple smaller buckets. The authors explore three techniques for bucket creation: clustering-based, SVD-based, and SVD with reorganization, and demonstrate improved correlation with human judgements compared to state-of-the-art methods. The method is also touted for being parameter-free (SVD-based approaches).

**Contributions:**

1. **TBuckets Framework:** The central contribution is the TBuckets framework itself – a new perspective on topic coherence evaluation based on the thematic grouping of topic words. This provides an intuitive and potentially effective way to quantify topic quality.
2. **SVD-Based Techniques:** The SVD-based methods (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) represent a practical contribution due to their parameter-free nature. This addresses a common issue with existing coherence measures that require significant parameter tuning.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experimental evaluation on three datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of TBuckets, particularly the SVD-based reorganization technique (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg), which outperforms existing methods on two out of three datasets. This provides empirical support for the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Intuitive Approach:** The underlying intuition behind TBuckets – that coherent topics should have clear thematic groupings – is strong and aligns with how humans assess topic quality.
2. **Parameter-Free Methods:** The SVD-based methods’ lack of parameter tuning is a significant advantage. This makes the approach easier to apply and reduces the risk of overfitting.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are promising, demonstrating that TBuckets can achieve higher correlation with human judgements than existing methods on several datasets.  The results on the NYT and 20NG datasets are particularly notable.
4. **Well-Defined Methodology:** The paper clearly outlines the TBuckets framework and the different techniques for bucket creation. The description of the reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) is thorough and logical.
5. **Practical Application:** Demonstrating the utility of TBuckets for improving text classification performance via topic quality filtering adds to the practical relevance of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in SVD Application:** While the application of SVD to topic word grouping is reasonable, the SVD technique itself isn't novel. The core innovation lies in *how* SVD is applied within the TBuckets framework and the subsequent reorganization step. This should be emphasized more clearly in the paper.
2. **Reliance on Word Embeddings:** The performance of TBuckets is heavily reliant on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings.  The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the results to different embedding models or the potential impact of domain-specific embeddings.
3. **Reorganization Threshold:** The dynamic threshold calculation for the reorganization step, while motivated, appears somewhat ad-hoc. The rationale behind using `AWES - AWPS / AWES` isn't fully convincing and could benefit from further justification or exploration of alternative thresholding mechanisms.  A more rigorous sensitivity analysis of the threshold would be helpful.
4. **Presentation of Results:** Table 1 presents a lot of numbers. It would be beneficial to simplify this by highlighting the key improvements achieved by TBuckets and visually emphasizing the statistical significance of the improvements (e.g., using bolding or asterisks). The discussion of the results could also be more nuanced, discussing cases where TBuckets *doesn’t* outperform the state-of-the-art.
5. **Lack of Ablation Study:** There is no ablation study to understand the individual contributions of the different components of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (size of bucket, word order weighting, eigenvector similarity). Knowing which component contributes most to the performance gains would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you discuss the computational complexity of the TBuckets techniques, particularly SVD-based ones, and how they scale with the number of topics and the vocabulary size?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding models or fine-tuned embeddings on the specific datasets?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed justification for the threshold calculation in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique? Have you considered alternative thresholding strategies?
4.  What is the effect of the window size used for computing PMI in related work on the topic coherence?
5.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of your approach and potential scenarios where TBuckets might not perform well?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The related work section is thorough but could be slightly more concise.
*   Consider adding a visualization of the bucket creation process for a sample topic to illustrate the concept more clearly.
*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of potential applications beyond text classification.



**Overall:**

This paper presents a promising new approach to topic coherence evaluation. The TBuckets framework is well-motivated, and the SVD-based techniques offer a practical advantage due to their parameter-free nature. While the novelty of the SVD application is limited, the overall approach and experimental results are encouraging. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly concerning the threshold justification and sensitivity analysis, would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
