The paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for evaluating topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The authors present three techniques for bucket creation: clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization. They claim TBuckets outperforms state-of-the-art methods on several datasets and offers a parameter-free option with the SVD-based techniques.

The primary contribution is the TBuckets approach itself – a conceptually simple, yet potentially effective method for assessing topic quality. The SVD-based techniques, particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, demonstrating competitive performance *without* parameter tuning is a secondary, albeit important, contribution. The experimental evaluation using established datasets and comparison with existing methods is also a positive aspect.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The idea of measuring topic coherence through bucket formation is relatively novel and offers a different perspective compared to existing methods relying on PMI, distributional semantics, or word co-occurrence. While not revolutionary, it provides a fresh angle on a well-studied problem.
2. **Parameter-Free Option:** The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg methods' lack of reliance on parameter tuning is a significant advantage. Many existing topic coherence measures require careful parameter selection, which can be time-consuming and data-dependent. A parameter-free method offers ease of use and generalizability.
3. **Competitive Performance:** The experimental results show that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves competitive performance, surpassing the state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets. This demonstrates the potential of the approach and warrants further investigation.
4. **Clear Presentation of the TBuckets Idea:** The authors provide a clear and intuitive explanation of the TBuckets concept, illustrated with examples. This helps the reader understand the underlying intuition and how the different techniques operate.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Justification for Bucket Formation:** The core premise—that well-defined buckets equate to coherent topics—is asserted rather than rigorously justified. The examples provided are illustrative, but not proof. A more thorough theoretical analysis or a stronger connection to cognitive models of human topic understanding is needed.  The “human observation” analogy feels weak as justification.
2. **Ambiguity in SVD-Based Bucket Assignment:** The method of assigning words to buckets based on eigenvector similarity in TBuckets-SVD is somewhat vague. How is the "most similar" eigenvector determined? The paper doesn’t clearly define the similarity metric used (cosine similarity is mentioned later in relation to word embeddings, but its use here isn’t explicitly stated). This ambiguity hinders reproducibility.
3. **Overly Complex Reorganization Step:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg step, while potentially beneficial, introduces significant complexity with its four parameters (NBPW, NBPE, LBPW, OBPW) and dynamic threshold calculation. While the authors state the threshold is "not empirically decided," the dependence on AWPS and AWES introduces dataset-specific sensitivities. The justification for the specific formula used for the dynamic threshold is lacking.  It feels ad-hoc.
4. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses on demonstrating the success of TBuckets.  A discussion of *when* TBuckets fails, and *why*, is missing. Understanding the limitations is crucial for evaluating the overall usefulness of the method.  Are there types of topics where bucket formation is inherently problematic?
5. **Minor Formatting Issues and Redundancy:** The paper contains some minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing around equations) and redundancies in the descriptions of related work. The referencing style is also inconsistent. For example, some authors are listed with full names on first mention and initials thereafter (Blei et al., 2003), while others are consistently listed with full names.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for the assumption that well-defined buckets indicate high topic coherence?  Specifically, is there a connection to information theory or cognitive science principles?
2. Please explicitly state the similarity metric used to determine the "most similar" eigenvector in TBuckets-SVD.
3. What is the computational complexity of the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg step, particularly the threshold calculation? Could this complexity limit its scalability to large topics or datasets?
4. Can you provide examples of topics where TBuckets performs poorly, and analyze the reasons for the poor performance?
5. Will you address the formatting inconsistencies and redundancies in the manuscript?

**Additional Comments:**

* The discussion of related work is comprehensive but could be more concise.
* The notation (e.g., using "wi" to represent a word) is generally clear, but could be more consistent throughout the paper.
* The application to text classification seems somewhat tacked on and could be strengthened with a more detailed analysis.



Despite the novel idea and competitive performance, the lack of theoretical justification, ambiguities in implementation details, and the complexity of the reorganization step raise significant concerns. While the potential is there, the paper needs substantial revision to address these weaknesses before it can be considered for acceptance. I lean towards **rejection** at this stage.
