This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measure topic quality by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core intuition is that coherent topics will result in a single large bucket, while less coherent topics will exhibit several smaller buckets. The authors explore three techniques for bucket creation: clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization. They evaluate TBuckets on three datasets, comparing its performance to state-of-the-art methods and demonstrating improved correlation with human judgments, particularly with the SVD-based techniques. A secondary application to weakly supervised text classification is also presented.

The primary contribution of this work is the TBuckets approach itself – a conceptually simple and seemingly effective method for assessing topic coherence based on the distribution of words into thematic buckets. The SVD-based techniques, especially TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, demonstrate strong performance without requiring parameter tuning, offering a practical advantage. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of TBuckets’ utility in improving text classification performance in a weakly supervised setting. The experimental evaluation provides convincing evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The TBuckets approach offers a fresh perspective on topic coherence evaluation. Existing methods often rely on statistical measures or distributional semantics. TBuckets, by explicitly attempting to mimic human interpretation of topics (grouping related words), introduces a conceptually distinct method.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results, particularly the outperformance of state-of-the-art methods on the NYT and 20NG datasets, demonstrate the practical effectiveness of TBuckets. The consistent performance of the SVD-based techniques is particularly noteworthy.
3. **Parameter-Free Techniques:** The SVD-based TBuckets techniques require no parameter tuning, which simplifies application and reduces the risk of overfitting. This is a significant advantage over methods that demand extensive parameter optimization.
4. **Demonstrated Applicability:** Showing the usefulness of TBuckets in improving performance on a downstream task, weakly supervised text classification, adds practical value and highlights the potential impact of the proposed method. This moves beyond just a coherence metric to demonstrating usefulness.
5. **Clear Explanation and Motivation:** The paper clearly explains the underlying intuition behind TBuckets and provides illustrative examples to clarify the bucket creation process. The motivation for the reorganization step within TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates the empirical effectiveness of TBuckets, it lacks a rigorous theoretical analysis of why and when the method performs well. A deeper understanding of the relationship between bucket structure and topic coherence would strengthen the contribution.
2. **Dependence on Word Embeddings:** The performance of TBuckets, particularly the SVD-based techniques, heavily relies on the quality of the underlying word embeddings. The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the results to different embedding models or the impact of domain-specific embeddings.
3. **Reorganization Parameterization (AWPS/AWES):** Although touted as parameter-free, the reorganization step within TBuckets-SVD-Reorg uses dynamic thresholds based on AWPS and AWES. While not *set* by the user, these values *are* determined by the data and influence the reorganization process. A clearer discussion on the robustness of this dynamic thresholding would be beneficial.
4. **Comparisons with Baselines:** The comparison to related work primarily relies on the results reported in Roder et al. (2015). A direct comparison of TBuckets with more recent coherence measures or implementations would be more compelling.
5. **Dataset Limitations:** The use of only three datasets, all previously used in the literature, limits the generalizability of the findings. Evaluating TBuckets on more diverse and challenging datasets would increase confidence in its robustness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the performance results of the SVD-based techniques to different pre-trained word embeddings? Did you experiment with other embedding models (e.g., fastText, word2vec)?
2. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the dynamic thresholding mechanism used in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg? What is the typical range of values for AWPS and AWES across different datasets, and how does this affect the reorganization process?
3. Have you investigated the computational complexity of TBuckets, particularly the SVD-based techniques, for very large topics or document collections?
4. The paper mentions future work involving devising better ways to compute word similarities for specific domains. Could you elaborate on the strategies you are considering?
5. The application to text classification is limited. Could you provide some error analysis on the cases where TBuckets improves classification accuracy?

**Additional Comments:**
