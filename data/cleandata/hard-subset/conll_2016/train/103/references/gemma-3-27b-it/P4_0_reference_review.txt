This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measure topic quality by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core idea is that a coherent topic will result in a single large bucket, while incoherent topics will have multiple smaller buckets. Three techniques for bucket creation are explored: clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization. The authors evaluate TBuckets on three datasets, correlating their coherence scores with human annotations and comparing against state-of-the-art results. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of their method for text classification by selecting high-quality topics for human labeling.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **The TBuckets Framework:** The primary contribution is the TBuckets framework itself – a conceptually simple yet potentially effective approach to quantifying topic coherence based on the distribution of words into thematic buckets. The idea of reflecting coherence through bucket distribution is intuitive and offers a different perspective compared to existing methods.
2. **SVD-based Techniques:** The use of SVD, particularly the parameter-free SVD-based techniques (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg), is a significant aspect of the work. Offering parameter-free alternatives in a field often requiring extensive tuning is valuable.
3. **Demonstration of Utility in Weakly Supervised Learning:** Showing that the coherence scores generated by TBuckets can be used to select better topics for human labeling in a weakly supervised text classification scenario is a practical contribution, demonstrating the real-world applicability of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The TBuckets approach offers a fresh perspective on topic coherence measurement, diverging from traditional methods based on PMI, distributional semantics, or word co-occurrence. The bucket-based representation is conceptually appealing.
2. **Parameter-Free Methods:** The SVD-based techniques are a substantial advantage. In many topic modeling applications, parameter tuning is a significant hurdle. Providing methods that require no parameter tuning is valuable for usability.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two of the three datasets tested, which is a strong indicator of its effectiveness.
4. **Practical Application:**  Demonstrating how TBuckets can improve text classification performance via topic selection for human annotation highlights its practical relevance and provides a clear application beyond pure coherence evaluation.
5. **Clear Explanation and Implementation Details:** The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the TBuckets framework and its variations, enabling reproducibility. The inclusion of the reorganization step based on LDA order weight and eigenvector similarity is well-motivated and explained.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** While the intuition behind TBuckets is strong, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical justification for why bucket distributions should correlate with human judgments of coherence. The connection between bucket characteristics and interpretability could be further explored.
2. **Sensitivity to Word Embeddings:** The performance of TBuckets heavily relies on the quality of the word embeddings used. The authors use pre-trained GloVe embeddings, but the choice of embeddings could influence the results. A brief discussion of the potential impact of different embedding models would be beneficial.
3. **Reorganization Step Complexity:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique introduces a dynamic threshold for reorganization based on AWPS and AWES. While the motivation for this is clear, the complexity of this calculation could potentially introduce instability or sensitivity to specific topic characteristics. Further analysis of the impact of this dynamic threshold would be useful.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The datasets used (20NG, NYT, Genomics) are standard but may not fully represent the diversity of topic modeling applications. Evaluating the method on more diverse and challenging datasets would strengthen the findings.
5. **Comparison Depth:** While comparisons to state-of-the-art are provided, a more granular comparison detailing *why* TBuckets outperforms other methods in specific scenarios (e.g., types of topics where it excels) would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the theoretical justification for using bucket distributions to measure topic coherence? Are there any connections to information-theoretic principles or cognitive models of topic understanding?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of TBuckets to the choice of word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embedding models, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide more insights into the behavior of the dynamic threshold in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg? How does it vary across different topics and datasets, and what impact does it have on the resulting bucket distributions?
4.  Could you discuss the computational complexity of the different TBuckets techniques, particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, and their scalability to larger datasets and topics?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The organization of the paper is generally clear and logical.
*   The related work section provides a good overview of existing topic coherence measures.
*   The experimental setup is well-defined, and the results are presented clearly.
*   The conclusion summarizes the key contributions and suggests promising directions for future work.
