This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measuring topic quality by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core intuition is that coherent topics will result in a single large bucket, while incoherent topics will have multiple smaller buckets. The authors explore three techniques for bucket creation – clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization – and demonstrate improved performance compared to state-of-the-art methods on standard datasets.

**Contributions:**

1. **TBuckets Framework:** The primary contribution is the TBuckets framework itself, offering a new perspective on topic coherence assessment based on the distribution of words into thematic buckets. This provides an interpretable way to quantify topic quality, aligning with human intuition about topic coherence.
2. **SVD-based Techniques:** The SVD-based techniques (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) are a significant contribution, particularly due to their parameter-free nature. This is a notable advantage over existing methods that require extensive parameter tuning. The reorganization step within TBuckets-SVD-Reorg shows a refinement of the initial SVD approach.
3. **Empirical Validation and Application:** The thorough experimental evaluation on multiple datasets, demonstrating superior performance to existing methods, strengthens the work. The application to weakly supervised text classification further demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed coherence measure.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The concept of grouping topic words into buckets to assess coherence is a novel approach. It’s a clever idea that resonates with how humans assess topic quality.
2. **Parameter-Free Methods:** The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg techniques stand out due to their lack of parameters. This makes them significantly easier to apply and reduces the risk of overfitting.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported results on the 20NG, NYT, and Genomics datasets demonstrate that TBuckets, particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods.  The consistency across multiple datasets adds weight to these findings.
4. **Clear Explanation and Motivation:** The paper is well-written and the motivation for the work is clearly articulated. The example illustrating the bucket creation process is particularly helpful in understanding the core idea.
5. **Practical Application:** Demonstrating the usefulness of the coherence measure within a text classification task provides a compelling practical application and illustrates the benefit of using TBuckets to filter high-quality topics.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Bucket Creation Nuances:** While the paper explains the general process of bucket creation, it lacks a detailed discussion of how the semantic similarity is determined. Specifically, what threshold for similarity is used, and how sensitive are the results to changes in this threshold? A more in-depth exploration of this aspect would strengthen the work.
2. **Reliance on Pre-trained Word Embeddings:** The performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe). The paper doesn't explore the impact of using different word embeddings or training embeddings specifically for the target domain. This is a limitation as the performance might not generalize well to domains with different vocabulary or semantic relationships.
3. **Reorganization Step Heuristic:** The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, while effective, relies on heuristics (AWPS, AWES) to determine the reorganization threshold. While the authors justify this approach, a more systematic exploration of different thresholding strategies could potentially improve performance. The explanation of how the threshold is determined could be more detailed and easier to follow.
4. **Dataset Limitations:** The datasets used (20NG, NYT, Genomics) are somewhat standard. Exploring the performance on more challenging or diverse datasets (e.g., datasets with noisy text, specialized domains) would provide a more robust evaluation of the method.
5. **Computational Complexity:** Although the parameter-free nature is a strength, the computational complexity of SVD, especially for large vocabularies, isn't discussed. Providing an analysis of the computational cost would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of semantic similarity metric used for bucket creation (e.g., cosine similarity, other embedding-based similarity measures)?
2.  Have you explored the impact of using different pre-trained word embeddings or training embeddings specifically for the target domain? If so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the logic behind the dynamic threshold calculation in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique? Could you perhaps illustrate it with an example?
4.  What is the computational complexity of the TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg techniques, and how does it scale with the size of the vocabulary and the number of topic words?
5.  How would the method perform on datasets with significantly different characteristics (e.g. short texts like tweets, or highly specialized domains)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a visualization of the bucket creation process for a few example topics.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach.
*   The formatting of Table 1 could be improved for readability (e.g., using consistent decimal places).



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and well-motivated approach to topic coherence assessment. The empirical results are convincing, and the parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques is a significant advantage. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
