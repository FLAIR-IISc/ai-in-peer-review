This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to evaluating topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic “buckets.” Three techniques – clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization – are presented and evaluated against human annotations and existing state-of-the-art methods. The primary claimed contribution is an improved coherence measure, specifically the SVD-based approach (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) which purportedly outperforms existing methods *without* parameter tuning. A secondary contribution is the application of this coherence measure to improve weakly supervised text classification.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Problem & Motivation:** Assessing topic quality is a crucial, ongoing challenge in topic modeling. The authors correctly identify the need for better automated evaluation metrics, particularly those aligning with human interpretability.
2. **Empirical Validation:** The authors conduct a reasonably thorough empirical evaluation on three datasets, using a standard evaluation protocol (Pearson correlation with human judgments). The reported results are encouraging, with TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieving competitive or superior performance on two out of three datasets.
3. **Parameter-Free Advantage:** The claim of a parameter-free SVD-based approach is a legitimate strength. Many existing coherence measures require significant parameter tuning, which can be time-consuming and sensitive to the dataset. Removing this burden is a practical benefit.
4. **Application to Downstream Task:** Demonstrating the utility of the proposed metric in a downstream task (weakly supervised text classification) adds value and shows a potential real-world application beyond simply measuring coherence. The observed improvement, even if modest, is a positive sign.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Novelty is Incremental:** While the TBuckets approach is presented as novel, it largely builds upon established techniques (word embeddings, SVD, clustering) in a relatively straightforward manner. The core idea of grouping words into buckets based on semantic similarity is not fundamentally new; it’s a reasonable heuristic but lacks a strong theoretical underpinning. The reorganization step feels similarly ad-hoc.
2. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** The paper lacks a strong theoretical justification for *why* this bucketing approach should correlate with human judgments of coherence. The authors rely heavily on intuition (“how we humans generally observe a topic…”) without providing a more rigorous explanation. Why does the size of the largest bucket necessarily equate to higher coherence? The link between the bucket structure and human interpretability remains weakly established.
3. **Over-Reliance on GloVe Embeddings:** The entire approach relies heavily on the quality of pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe). The performance of TBuckets is therefore inherently tied to the limitations and biases of these embeddings. The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the results to different embedding models or the potential for domain-specific embeddings.
4. **Modest Improvements in Classification:** The gains in text classification performance are relatively small. While statistically significant, the practical impact of using TBuckets for topic selection in this setting is questionable. The authors do not provide a thorough analysis of *why* the coherence measure leads to improved classification.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* TBuckets succeeds and fails, and *why*, is crucial for assessing its limitations and guiding future research. Providing examples of topics where TBuckets' coherence scores deviate significantly from human judgments would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more theoretical justification for the link between the bucket structure generated by TBuckets and human perceptions of topic coherence?
2.  How sensitive are your results to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with other embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec, FastText) or domain-specific embeddings?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, highlighting specific examples of topics where TBuckets' coherence scores are misaligned with human judgments?
4.  In the reorganization step, the threshold is dynamically calculated. Could you provide more insight into how sensitive the results are to the AWPS and AWES values? Are there situations where this dynamic threshold might be inappropriate?
5.  The application to text classification shows modest improvement. What is the contribution of TBuckets compared to simply using LDA perplexity as a topic quality metric for topic selection?



**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear and well-organized. The related work section is comprehensive. However, the paper would benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach. The formatting of Table 1 is a bit difficult to read.
