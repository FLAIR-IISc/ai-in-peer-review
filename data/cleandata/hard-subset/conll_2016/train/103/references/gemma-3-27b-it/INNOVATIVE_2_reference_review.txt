This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach to evaluating topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic "buckets." The core idea – that a coherent topic should yield a single dominant bucket with smaller supporting ones – is intuitive and offers a fresh perspective on topic quality assessment. The paper explores three instantiation techniques (clustering, SVD, and SVD with reorganization) and demonstrates promising results, outperforming state-of-the-art methods on two out of three datasets, notably with the parameter-free SVD-based approaches. The application to weakly supervised text classification further underscores potential practical benefits.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novelty of the TBuckets Concept:** The central contribution is the TBuckets framework itself. Moving beyond existing coherence measures, it proposes a fundamentally different way to assess topic quality based on the distribution of words into thematic groups. This is a significant conceptual leap.
2. **Effective SVD-Based Implementation:** The SVD-based techniques (especially with reorganization) are particularly impressive. Achieving competitive performance *without* parameter tuning is a strong advantage, highlighting the inherent effectiveness of the approach.
3. **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The application to text classification, showing improved performance when using TBuckets to filter topic quality, adds credibility and demonstrates the practical relevance of the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Intuitive and Human-Aligned Approach:** The core principle of TBuckets – that coherent topics should exhibit a clear thematic focus – aligns well with how humans intuitively assess topic quality. This provides a strong justification for the proposed method.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported performance, particularly the outperformance on NYT and 20NG datasets, is a significant achievement. This provides strong evidence of the effectiveness of the TBuckets approach.
3. **Parameter-Free Methods:** The SVD-based techniques are a major strength. The lack of parameter tuning simplifies the application of the method and reduces the risk of overfitting or suboptimal configurations. This also suggests inherent robustness of the underlying principle.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and explains the TBuckets concept, the different implementation techniques, and the experimental setup clearly. The inclusion of examples illustrating the bucket formation process is helpful.
5. **Relevance to a Core NLP Problem:** Topic model evaluation is a crucial aspect of topic modeling, and a more effective evaluation metric like TBuckets is valuable for the research community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** While the intuition behind TBuckets is strong, the paper lacks a more formal theoretical grounding.  A deeper exploration of why a single dominant bucket corresponds to a “good” topic would strengthen the work.  Is there a connection to information theory or other established concepts?
2. **Sensitivity to Word Embeddings:** The performance of TBuckets heavily relies on the quality of the word embeddings. The authors acknowledge this but don't extensively explore the impact of different embedding models or training data.
3. **Reorganization Heuristic:** The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, while effective, relies on somewhat arbitrary thresholds based on AWPS and AWES. While the authors justify this dynamic approach, a more systematic analysis of this threshold’s impact would be beneficial. How robust is the performance to variations in this threshold?
4. **Dataset Limitations:** While the chosen datasets are standard, the analysis relies on pre-defined topics with human coherence scores. It would be valuable to demonstrate TBuckets’ performance on topics learned directly from unlabeled data.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you elaborate on the theoretical motivations behind the TBuckets approach?  Is there a connection to existing concepts in information theory or semantic coherence?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of TBuckets to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding models or training data?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of the AWPS/AWES threshold in the reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg)?  How does varying this threshold affect performance?
4.   Have you considered extending TBuckets to evaluate hierarchical topic models, where topics may have sub-topics?
