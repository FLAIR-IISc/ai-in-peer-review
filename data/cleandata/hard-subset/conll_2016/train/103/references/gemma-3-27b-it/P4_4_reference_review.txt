This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measure topic quality by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. Three techniques—clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization—are explored for bucket creation. The method is evaluated on three datasets, demonstrating performance competitive with, and in some cases surpassing, state-of-the-art coherence measures. An application to text classification further highlights the utility of the approach.

The primary contribution is the TBuckets approach itself, providing an alternative method for assessing topic coherence based on the thematic grouping of words. The SVD-based techniques (particularly with reorganization) offer a parameter-free approach which is a significant practical advantage. A secondary contribution lies in the experimental evaluation, demonstrating the effectiveness of TBuckets and providing a comparative benchmark against existing methods. The successful application of the coherence measure to improve text classification performance also adds value.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The idea of assessing topic coherence by forming thematic 'buckets' of words is a relatively novel perspective. It moves beyond simple word co-occurrence or distributional similarity metrics, attempting to capture a more holistic understanding of topic cohesion as humans might perceive it.

2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates, through rigorous experimentation on multiple datasets, that TBuckets, particularly the SVD-Reorg version, achieves competitive or superior results compared to established coherence measures like CUCI and CUMASS.  The consistent outperformance on NYT and 20NG is particularly noteworthy.

3. **Parameter-Free SVD Techniques:** The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg methods’ lack of reliance on parameter tuning is a strong advantage. This simplifies application and reduces the risk of overfitting or suboptimal performance due to parameter selection.  This is explicitly highlighted and justified within the paper.

4. **Practical Application:**  The demonstration of TBuckets' utility in improving weakly supervised text classification is valuable. This showcases the practical relevance of the coherence measure beyond just an evaluation metric. The results showing improvements in classification accuracy when using quality-filtered topics are compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** While the intuition behind TBuckets is explained, a more formal theoretical grounding for why bucket formation accurately reflects topic coherence would strengthen the work. The connection between bucket structure (size and distribution) and human interpretability feels somewhat intuitive, but a more rigorous analysis would be beneficial.

2. **Complexity of SVD-Reorg:** The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, while effective, introduces complexity and relies on several parameters (NBPW, NBPE, LBPW, OBPW) calculated dynamically. The rationale for the dynamic threshold calculation, while explained, is somewhat intricate and potentially sensitive to parameter choices within the calculation itself. The reliance on the “average word pair similarity” and “average word eigenvector similarity” requires further scrutiny, as these averages might mask important nuances in word relationships.

3. **Dependence on Word Embeddings:** The performance of TBuckets heavily relies on the quality of the underlying word embeddings (GloVe in this case). The paper doesn't adequately discuss the potential impact of different embedding models or the sensitivity of TBuckets to embedding choice.  A brief analysis exploring this dependency would be valuable.

4. **Limited Ablation Study:** While the paper presents results for different TBuckets techniques, a more detailed ablation study exploring the contribution of each component of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (e.g., the impact of just the reorganization step) would provide further insights into the method’s inner workings and justify its complexity.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the theoretical underpinnings of why the bucket structure, specifically the size and distribution of buckets, correlates with human interpretability of a topic?
2. How sensitive is TBuckets (particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) to the choice of word embeddings?  Have you experimented with different embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec, FastText) and how do the results compare?
3. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the dynamic threshold calculation in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg? What is the impact of variations in the "average word pair similarity" and "average word eigenvector similarity" values on the reorganization process and the resulting coherence scores?
4.  Could you provide more examples of topics where the reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg significantly improves coherence and explain how it accomplishes this?

**Additional Comments:**

The related work section is comprehensive. It would be useful to briefly discuss the computational cost of each of the TBuckets techniques, especially in the context of large-scale topic modeling applications. The notation throughout the paper is generally clear.
