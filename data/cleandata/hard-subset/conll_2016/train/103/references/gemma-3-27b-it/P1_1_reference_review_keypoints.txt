- Proposes TBuckets, grouping topic words into thematic “buckets” to measure LDA topic coherence.  
- Explores three bucket-creation techniques: clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization.  
- Evaluates methods by correlating TBuckets scores with human annotations across three datasets.  
- Claims SVD-based methods are parameter-free and often outperform state-of-the-art coherence measures.  
- Demonstrates TBuckets can improve weakly supervised text classification as a downstream application.  
- Novelty: conceptually interesting metric that mirrors human grouping of words into themes.  
- Empirical strength: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg shows competitive or superior correlation with human judgments on NYT and 20NG.  
- Practical benefit: SVD-based methods avoid parameter tuning, easing application and reducing overfitting risk.  
- Clear presentation with helpful example dry runs illustrating coherent vs. incoherent topics.  
- Comprehensive related work situating TBuckets among existing coherence measures.  
- Core idea not entirely novel—builds on existing semantic-similarity approaches; novelty is in the bucketing method.  
- Heavy reliance on pre-trained embeddings (GloVe) raises sensitivity and OOV/rare-word concerns; domain-specific embeddings not explored.  
- Reorganization step uses heuristic thresholds (AWPS, AWES) that feel ad-hoc and could be better justified or optimized.  
- Limited ablation study; need to quantify each component’s contribution (clustering, SVD, reorganization).  
- Lacks in-depth qualitative analysis showing concrete bucket examples on real topics.  
- Questions on sensitivity to different embeddings and effect of domain-specific embeddings.  
- Requests rationale and alternatives for AWPS/AWES thresholding and exploration of other methods.  
- Asks for more qualitative examples demonstrating meaningful thematic groupings by TBuckets.  
- Seeks information on computational cost and scalability for large topics or corpora.  
- Inquires whether user studies were done to compare TBuckets scores directly with human judgments.  
- Suggests adding visualizations of the bucket formation process for illustrative topics.  
- Notes clear, concise writing and a comprehensive reference list.
