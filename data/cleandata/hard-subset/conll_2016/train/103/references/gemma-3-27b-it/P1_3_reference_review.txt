## Peer Review: Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for measuring topic coherence in Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) models. The core idea is to group topic words into thematic "buckets" – a process mirroring human interpretation of topic coherence.  The authors explore three techniques for bucket creation: clustering-based, Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), and SVD with reorganization. They evaluate TBuckets on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics), demonstrating improved correlation with human judgments compared to state-of-the-art methods, particularly with the SVD-Reorg variant. They also showcase its utility in improving weakly supervised text classification.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Intuitive and Motivated Approach:** The TBuckets concept, based on mimicking human assessment of topic coherence, provides a strong intuitive foundation for a new evaluation metric.  This is a valuable contribution as many existing metrics are mathematically defined but lack direct interpretability.
2. **Effective SVD-based Technique:** The SVD-based bucket creation, especially with the reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg), demonstrably outperforms existing methods on two of the three datasets and does so without parameter tuning, a significant practical advantage.
3. **Demonstration of Practical Impact:** The application of TBuckets to improve weakly supervised text classification highlights its potential utility beyond simply evaluating topic models, showcasing its value in downstream NLP tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The SVD-Reorg technique's performance on the NYT and 20NG datasets, exceeding reported state-of-the-art results, provides compelling evidence of its effectiveness.  The statistical significance of these improvements should be clearly stated (e.g., p-values).
2. **Parameter-Free Advantage:** The lack of parameter tuning required for the SVD-based methods (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) is a significant strength. This makes the approach readily applicable without extensive optimization.
3. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper provides a thorough explanation of the TBuckets approach, including the rationale behind bucket creation, the different techniques employed, and the coherence score calculation. The example dry runs help to understand the methodology.
4. **Practical Application:**  The inclusion of a text classification experiment provides a tangible demonstration of the benefits of using TBuckets to select higher-quality topics for weakly supervised learning.
5. **Well-Organized Structure:**  The paper is logically structured, with a clear introduction, background, method description, experimental evaluation, and discussion of related work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of ideas is novel, the individual techniques – word embeddings, SVD, clustering – are well-established. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how TBuckets builds upon these existing techniques and contributes something truly new.  The core novelty appears to be in the specific *application* of these techniques to bucket creation and the reorganization step.
2. **Dataset Limitations:**  The evaluation is performed on only three datasets, which might not be representative of all types of text corpora.  Expanding the evaluation to include datasets with different characteristics (e.g., longer documents, different domains) would strengthen the findings.
3. **Reorganization Threshold Details:** The dynamic threshold for reorganization (AWES − AWPS / AWES) is described, but its sensitivity and impact on performance are not thoroughly investigated.  A more detailed ablation study, examining the effects of different thresholding strategies, would be valuable.  The rationale behind *subtracting* AWPS from AWES is not entirely clear and could be better explained.
4. **Comparison Depth:** The comparison to Roder et al. (2015) is somewhat limited.  A more in-depth analysis of *why* TBuckets outperforms these methods, beyond simply stating that it does, would be insightful.  Specifically, discussing the strengths and weaknesses of TBuckets relative to the different configurations within the Roder et al. framework would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Statistical Significance:**  Please provide statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values) for the performance improvements reported in Table 1.
2. **Reorganization Threshold Sensitivity:**  How sensitive is the performance of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg to the dynamic threshold value?  Can you provide a sensitivity analysis, showing how performance changes with different threshold values?
3. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost (time complexity) of the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg approach compared to other coherence measures, especially considering the SVD operation?
4. **Threshold Rationale:**  Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind the AWES − AWPS / AWES formulation for the reorganization threshold?  Why is subtraction used, and what is the intuition behind the ratio?
5. **Generalizability:** Do you anticipate the performance of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg to be consistent across different embedding spaces (e.g., fastText, BERT embeddings)? Have you experimented with different embeddings?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a visual example of the bucket creation process for a specific topic.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the TBuckets approach.
*   The related work section is comprehensive, but could be more critically evaluated.  For instance, explicitly state how TBuckets differs from and improves upon existing methods.



Overall, this is a promising paper with a novel and well-motivated approach to topic coherence evaluation. The strong empirical results and clear presentation make it a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
