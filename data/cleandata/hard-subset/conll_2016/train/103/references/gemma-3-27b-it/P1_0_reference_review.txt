## Peer Review for "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for measuring the coherence and quality of topics inferred from Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) models. The core idea involves grouping topic words into thematic "buckets" – a process the authors argue mirrors human assessment of topic coherence. Three techniques are explored for bucket creation: clustering-based, Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), and SVD with reorganization. The authors demonstrate through correlation with human-annotated scores that TBuckets, particularly the SVD-based variant with reorganization, outperforms state-of-the-art methods on several datasets.  A key selling point is the parameter-free nature of the SVD techniques.  They also showcase an application of TBuckets in improving weakly supervised text classification.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A Novel Coherence Metric:** The TBuckets approach offers a conceptually intuitive and potentially valuable new metric for evaluating topic coherence, moving beyond purely statistical measures like PMI. The idea of mimicking the human cognitive process of grouping related words is promising.
2. **Effective SVD-based Implementation:** The SVD-based techniques (particularly with reorganization) demonstrate strong empirical performance, outperforming existing state-of-the-art methods in the reported experiments. The parameter-free nature of this method is a significant practical advantage.
3. **Practical Application to Weakly Supervised Learning:**  Demonstrating the utility of TBuckets for selecting high-quality topics in a weakly supervised text classification setting adds practical value and demonstrates a concrete application of the research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:**  The paper presents compelling empirical evidence showing that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg consistently achieves higher correlation with human judgments compared to existing methods on the tested datasets. This is a strong indicator of the metric's validity.
2. **Intuitive and Well-Motivated Approach:**  The core idea of TBuckets – reflecting how humans assess topic coherence – is easy to understand and logically sound. The paper effectively motivates this approach by contrasting coherent and incoherent topics.
3. **Parameter-Free SVD Methods:** The complete lack of parameter tuning required for the SVD-based techniques is a significant advantage in terms of usability and reproducibility. This distinguishes it from many other coherence measures that require careful parameter optimization.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation utilizes established datasets and a well-defined evaluation metric (Pearson correlation) commonly used in the field, allowing for fair comparison with existing work. The inclusion of a practical application strengthens the evaluation.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the proposed methodology and experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Underlying Techniques:** While the *combination* of techniques and the application to topic coherence are novel, the individual components (word embeddings, SVD, clustering) are well-established.  The paper needs to more strongly articulate what specifically makes the integration and application unique and impactful.
2. **Reorganization Step – Lack of Detailed Justification & Potential Sensitivity:** The “reorganization” step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg feels somewhat ad-hoc. The justification relies on observed examples, but the thresholding mechanism (based on AWPS and AWES) feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks a rigorous theoretical grounding. Further analysis into the sensitivity of this threshold to different datasets/topic characteristics would be valuable.
3. **Reliance on GloVe Embeddings:** The use of pre-trained GloVe embeddings limits the generalizability of the results.  The performance of TBuckets might be tied to the quality and characteristics of these embeddings. Investigating the performance with different embedding models or even domain-specific embeddings would strengthen the findings.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates improved correlation with human judgements, it doesn’t delve into *why* TBuckets succeeds where other methods fail. A more detailed error analysis, examining specific topic examples where TBuckets performs well/poorly compared to baselines, would provide valuable insights.
5. **Narrow Scope of Application:** The application to text classification, while positive, is relatively limited. Exploring other potential applications of TBuckets, such as topic model selection or hyperparameter optimization, could further demonstrate its broader utility.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the theoretical motivations behind the AWPS and AWES-based thresholding mechanism in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg step?  Are there alternative thresholding strategies you considered, and why did you choose this approach?
2.  How sensitive are the results of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg to different values of AWPS and AWES? Could you present a sensitivity analysis demonstrating the impact of varying these parameters on the correlation scores?
3.  Did you experiment with other pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, FastText) or explore training word embeddings specifically on your datasets? If so, how did the performance of TBuckets change?
4.  Could you provide a qualitative error analysis, highlighting specific examples of topics where TBuckets performs significantly better or worse than the baseline methods, and explaining the potential reasons for these differences?
5.  Beyond text classification, what other applications do you envision for TBuckets, and are you planning to explore them in future work?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity of the different TBuckets techniques.
*  It would be helpful to include a visual representation of the "buckets" formed by TBuckets-SVD-Reorg for a few example topics, to better illustrate the process and the resulting thematic groupings.
*  The reference list is extensive, but a few references are missing publication details (e.g., Aletras and Stevenson, 2013).
