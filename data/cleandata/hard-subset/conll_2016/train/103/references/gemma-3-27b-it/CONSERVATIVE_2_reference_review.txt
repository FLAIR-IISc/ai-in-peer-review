This paper proposes TBuckets, a new approach to evaluating topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic "buckets." The core idea is that a coherent topic will result in a single large bucket, while incoherent topics will produce many smaller ones. Three techniques – clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization – are explored for bucket creation, and the SVD-based approach is claimed to outperform state-of-the-art methods without requiring parameter tuning. The authors also demonstrate application to weakly supervised text classification.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a novel *approach* to topic coherence evaluation, even if the specific techniques presented feel somewhat incremental. While the idea of grouping words thematically is intuitive, the formalization and attempts at automated implementation are the key contribution. A secondary, though less substantial, contribution is the demonstration that an SVD-based method *can* achieve competitive performance without extensive parameter tuning – a practical consideration. A minor contribution lies in showing the utility of the TBuckets score for improving topic selection in a weakly supervised classification scenario.

**Strengths:**

1. **Intuitive Concept:** The core idea behind TBuckets – that coherent topics should map to a limited number of well-defined themes – is sound and aligns with human understanding of topic quality. This provides a reasonable basis for the proposed method.
2. **Competitive Performance:** The SVD-based approach (particularly with reorganization) demonstrates competitive performance against established coherence measures on the chosen datasets. Achieving comparable results without parameter tuning is a positive aspect.
3. **Practical Application:**  The demonstration of TBuckets’ utility in improving weakly supervised text classification is a worthwhile extension. Showing how the coherence score can be practically employed adds value beyond purely intrinsic evaluation.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the TBuckets methodology. The examples illustrating the bucket creation process are particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Techniques:** While the overall *approach* is novel, the individual techniques employed (clustering, SVD) are well-established. The authors don’t present any fundamentally new algorithmic advances within these techniques; they are applied in a specific way to the problem of topic coherence. The reorganization step is a sensible addition, but not a breakthrough.
2. **Limited Empirical Rigor:** The experimental evaluation, while showing competitive performance, lacks depth. The datasets are standard, but the analysis feels cursory. A more thorough comparison to a wider range of baselines (including simpler coherence measures) would strengthen the claims. Specifically, while they *mention* Roder et al. (2015), the comparison feels limited to simply stating outperformance without a deeper dive into *where* and *why* TBuckets succeeds.
3. **Sensitivity to Word Embeddings:** The reliance on pre-trained word embeddings introduces a potential point of failure. The performance of TBuckets is intrinsically linked to the quality of these embeddings. The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the results to different embedding models or domain-specific embeddings.
4. **Parameter Sensitivity in Clustering:** While the SVD methods are parameter-free, the clustering approach *does* require parameter tuning (the distance threshold). The paper briefly mentions varying this parameter, but the analysis of the impact feels insufficient.  The best setting appears to be somewhat dataset dependent.
5. **Overstated Claims:** The claim of "outperforming the state-of-the-art" feels slightly overstated, given the limitations in the experimental analysis.  The improvements are modest, and the lack of a comprehensive comparison weakens this claim.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the specific cases where TBuckets outperforms existing coherence measures, and conversely, the cases where it falls short? What characteristics of the topics or datasets seem to influence the performance of TBuckets?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding models or fine-tuned embeddings on domain-specific corpora?
3.  For the clustering-based approach, can you justify the choice of the agglomerative clustering method and the linkage criteria used?  Have you considered other clustering algorithms?
4.  The dynamic threshold for reorganization seems somewhat arbitrarily defined.  Could you provide a more theoretical justification for this threshold, or explore alternative approaches to determining when to reorganize buckets?
5.  In the text classification experiments, what is the statistical significance of the performance improvements observed with TBuckets-informed topic selection?



**Additional Comments:**

The references are comprehensive. The paper is generally well-organized, but could benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach. The potential computational cost of SVD, especially for large topics, should be briefly addressed.
