This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic quality by grouping topic words into thematic “buckets.” The core intuition is that coherent topics will result in a single large bucket, while less coherent topics will have multiple smaller buckets. Three techniques are explored for bucket creation: clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization. The authors evaluate their approach on three datasets, comparing correlation with human-annotated coherence scores to state-of-the-art results, and also demonstrate its utility in a text classification task.

The primary contribution of this work is the TBuckets approach itself – a new method for assessing topic coherence based on the organization of words within a topic. While the underlying concepts (word embeddings, SVD, clustering) are established, combining them in this specific way, focused on bucket formation to represent topic coherence, constitutes a worthwhile contribution. The second contribution is the demonstration that the parameter-free SVD-based techniques achieve competitive performance compared to existing approaches, offering a simpler and more readily applicable method. Finally, showcasing the practical application of TBuckets to improve weakly supervised text classification adds value by illustrating its potential impact beyond just topic quality evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** TBuckets presents a fresh perspective on topic coherence assessment. Unlike many existing methods relying on statistical measures or distributional semantics directly, it focuses on *structuring* the topic’s word list to reflect human interpretability. This shift in focus is conceptually sound and offers a different lens through which to evaluate topic quality.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing. The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique consistently outperforms the state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets, indicating its effectiveness. This demonstrates that the method isn’t just theoretically appealing but also performs well in practice.
3. **Parameter-Free Techniques:** The SVD-based approaches are a significant advantage. Many existing coherence measures require careful parameter tuning, which can be time-consuming and dataset-dependent. The fact that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no such tuning makes them easier to apply and more broadly useful.
4. **Practical Application:** Demonstrating the use of TBuckets in a text classification scenario solidifies its relevance and highlights a potential real-world application. The improvement in classification accuracy using topics filtered by TBuckets coherence scores further supports the value of the proposed method.
5. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper is well-written and provides a clear and detailed explanation of the TBuckets approach, including the different techniques for bucket creation and the rationale behind them. The example illustrating the bucket procedure with coherent and non-coherent topics is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Reliance on Word Embeddings:** The method's performance is heavily reliant on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe in this case). While GloVe is a strong baseline, the choice of embeddings could significantly impact results. The paper doesn't explore the sensitivity of TBuckets to different embedding models.
2. **Reorganization Step Heuristic:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique's reorganization step, while improving performance, relies on empirically derived thresholds based on average word pair similarity and eigenvector similarity. While the authors justify this approach, it introduces a degree of heuristic dependence. A more rigorous analysis or adaptation of these thresholds could strengthen the method.
3. **Limited Scope of Datasets:** The evaluation is performed on only three datasets. While these datasets are commonly used in topic modeling research, it would be beneficial to evaluate TBuckets on a wider range of datasets, including those with different characteristics and domain specifics.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Baselines:**  While the paper compares against state-of-the-art methods, it lacks comparison to simpler baselines for topic coherence, such as simply using the number of words in the largest bucket as a coherence score. Establishing performance against such baselines could further highlight the benefits of the TBuckets approach.
5. **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper largely focuses on successes. Discussing specific examples where TBuckets *fails* to accurately assess coherence, and analyzing the reasons for these failures, would provide a more balanced and insightful evaluation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of TBuckets to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with different pre-trained embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, FastText) or fine-tuning embeddings on the specific datasets?
2. Can you provide a more detailed justification for the formulas used to compute the thresholds in the reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg)? What is the theoretical basis for using the ratio of AWES to AWPS?
3. Could you analyze the computational complexity of the different TBuckets techniques (clustering, SVD, SVD-Reorg)?
4. What types of topics or datasets might be particularly challenging for TBuckets, and why?
5. Do you have any insights into what causes the performance difference between TBuckets on the NYT/20NG datasets versus the Genomics dataset?

**Additional Comments:**

* The related work section is comprehensive but could be slightly more focused on coherence measures specifically.
* The figures are well-presented and easy to understand.
* Consider adding a discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future research in the conclusion.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach to topic coherence assessment. The experimental results are encouraging, and the parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques is a significant advantage. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, pending minor revisions addressing the questions and concerns raised.
