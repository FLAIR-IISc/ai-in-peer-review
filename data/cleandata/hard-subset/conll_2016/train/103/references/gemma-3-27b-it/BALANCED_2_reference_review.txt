This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measure topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core idea is that a coherent topic will result in a single large bucket, while incoherent topics will produce multiple smaller buckets. The authors explore three techniques – clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization – for bucket creation and demonstrate, through correlation with human judgments, that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods on several datasets. They further showcase the utility of TBuckets in improving text classification performance.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Topic Coherence Measure (TBuckets):** The central contribution is the TBuckets approach itself, which provides a conceptually intuitive way to assess topic quality based on the thematic grouping of words. This shifts the focus from word-level statistics to a more holistic, bucket-level analysis.
2. **Parameter-Free SVD-based Techniques:** The SVD-based methods (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) offer a significant advantage by requiring no parameter tuning, unlike many existing coherence measures. This makes them readily applicable and reduces the burden on users.
3. **Demonstrated Improvement in Text Classification:** The application of TBuckets to enhance text classification with weak supervision demonstrates the practical value of the proposed coherence measure beyond simply evaluating topic models.  Specifically, using TBuckets to pre-filter topics for annotation yields improved classification accuracy.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Intuitive Approach:** The core idea behind TBuckets – that coherent topics should group into a single thematic bucket – is easy to understand and resonates with how humans intuitively assess topic quality.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents strong empirical evidence demonstrating that TBuckets, particularly the SVD-based variants, outperform existing state-of-the-art coherence measures on multiple datasets. The correlation with human judgments is compelling.
3. **Practical Applicability:**  The demonstration of improved text classification performance adds significant value.  Showing how the coherence measure can be utilized in a downstream task strengthens the paper's impact.
4. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors use established datasets and evaluation metrics, ensuring a fair comparison with existing methods.  The ablation study of reorganization further strengthens their findings.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow.  The background information is sufficient, and the methodology is explained in detail.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Dependence on Word Embeddings:** The performance of TBuckets relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings used. While GloVe is a standard choice, the results might be sensitive to the specific embedding model and training corpus. The impact of different embedding choices isn’t investigated.
2. **Reorganization Step Complexity:** While the reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg improves performance, the explanation of the dynamic threshold calculation is somewhat complex and relies on several parameters (AWPS, AWES). It isn’t immediately clear how these parameters would generalize to different datasets or domains.  A more detailed justification or sensitivity analysis of these parameters would be beneficial.
3. **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily focuses on showcasing the successes of TBuckets. It would be valuable to include a discussion of cases where the method performs poorly, and to explore potential reasons for these failures.
4. **Comparison Scope:** While the paper compares against Roder et al. (2015), the field of topic coherence evaluation is rapidly evolving. A more comprehensive comparison with more recent, state-of-the-art methods would strengthen the paper's claim of superiority.
5. **Granularity of Buckets:** The paper doesn't explore the impact of different similarity thresholds or clustering algorithms on the granularity of the buckets formed. Investigating this aspect could provide further insights into the optimal configuration of TBuckets.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with other embedding models, and how do the results compare?
2. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the dynamic threshold calculation in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg? Perhaps an example illustrating how the threshold adapts to different topics would be helpful.
3. Can you discuss specific cases where TBuckets performs poorly, and analyze the potential reasons for these failures?
4. Have you considered comparing TBuckets with more recent topic coherence measures published after Roder et al. (2015)?
5. Did you explore alternative similarity measures beyond cosine similarity for determining bucket membership?

**Additional Comments:**

* The table showing the effect of varying Max Dist in TBuckets-Clustering is helpful.
* The examples of buckets created by TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are illustrative, but more examples would be beneficial.
* It would be useful to discuss the computational cost of the different TBuckets techniques.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to topic coherence evaluation. The TBuckets method is conceptually sound, empirically well-supported, and has demonstrated practical value. While some weaknesses exist, they are relatively minor and do not detract significantly from the overall contribution. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above. The paper has the potential to be a valuable addition to the literature on topic modeling and evaluation.
