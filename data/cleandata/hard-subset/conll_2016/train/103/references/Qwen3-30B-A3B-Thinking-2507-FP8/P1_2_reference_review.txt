

# Peer Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel method called TBuckets for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core idea is to group topic words into thematic "buckets" based on semantic similarity, with the assumption that a coherent topic will form a single dominant bucket. The authors propose three techniques for creating these buckets: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify orthogonal sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step to improve the bucket assignments from TBuckets-SVD). The authors evaluate their methods on three standard datasets (20NewsGroups, New York Times, and Genomics) using Pearson correlation with human-annotated coherence scores, demonstrating that their TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique outperforms the state-of-the-art on two of the three datasets. They further show that using their coherence measure to select high-quality topics improves weakly supervised text classification performance.

## Main Contributions

1. **A novel coherence measure based on thematic bucketing**: The TBuckets approach provides an intuitive framework for topic coherence assessment that aligns with human interpretation of topics.
2. **Parameter-free topic coherence techniques**: The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg methods require no parameter tuning, which is a significant advantage over existing state-of-the-art methods.
3. **Demonstrated utility for text classification**: The paper successfully shows that using TBuckets to select high-quality topics improves text classification performance in weakly supervised settings.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and intuitive approach to topic coherence**: The TBuckets concept is well-motivated by how humans interpret topics. The bucketing process mirrors human cognitive processes of grouping related words into themes, which provides a strong theoretical foundation for the method. The paper's examples of coherent vs. non-coherent topics and their corresponding bucket distributions effectively illustrate the core idea.

2. **Superior performance with parameter-free methods**: The paper demonstrates that their TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method outperforms the state-of-the-art on two of three datasets (NYT and 20NG) while requiring no parameter tuning. This is a significant contribution, as many existing methods require extensive parameter tuning to achieve good performance. The fact that their method achieves state-of-the-art results without tuning parameters makes it particularly valuable for practical applications.

3. **Practical application in text classification**: The paper provides a compelling demonstration of the utility of their coherence measure by showing that using TBuckets to select high-quality topics improves classification performance in weakly supervised text classification tasks. The results in Table 3 clearly show that selecting topics with higher coherence scores leads to better classification accuracy, especially for more challenging datasets like PC vs. MAC.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares only with the results reported in Roder et al. (2015), which is from 2015. However, there have been several important advances in topic coherence measurement since then, including work by Zhao et al. (2017), Ding et al. (2019), and others. Without comparison to these more recent methods, it's difficult to assess how much the proposed method advances the current state of the art. The paper should compare with at least the most recent state-of-the-art methods to establish the true contribution.

2. **Lack of sufficient explanation for the reorganization step**: While the paper describes the reorganization process in detail, it doesn't provide a clear explanation for *why* this step is necessary or what specific properties of topics it addresses. The paper states "many such similar examples were observed" but doesn't analyze these examples to demonstrate the necessity of the reorganization step. An ablation study showing the contribution of the reorganization step alone would strengthen the argument.

3. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations of the TBuckets approach. For instance:
   - How does the method handle topics with very few words (e.g., topics with only 3-5 words)?
   - How does it perform on topics that are coherent but have words with low semantic similarity (e.g., "war," "peace," "armistice")?
   - What are the performance characteristics of the method on domain-specific topics (e.g., in scientific or medical domains)?
   - How does the method handle topics with words that have multiple meanings (polysemy)?

4. **Inconsistent parameter-free claim**: The paper claims that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter-free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold that depends on AWPS and AWES. This threshold calculation isn't truly parameter-free as it requires calculating these metrics, which might be sensitive to the dataset. The paper should clarify why this threshold is considered parameter-free.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015), but doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods like Zhao et al. (2017) or Ding et al. (2019). Could you provide results comparing your method with these more recent approaches?

2. The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg uses a dynamic threshold calculated as AWES - AWPS*AWES. Could you clarify why this specific formula is used and why it's considered parameter-free? How sensitive is the performance to this threshold calculation?

3. In Table 3, the performance improvement for MEDICAL vs SPACE is minimal (94.67% vs 94.83% for highest vs lowest coherence sets). Why is this dataset different from the others? Is it because the topic model naturally generates more coherent topics for this dataset, or are there other factors at play?

4. The paper mentions that "the word embeddings of a large set of words, trained on various corpora, are now available publicly." However, it's not clear whether the GloVe embeddings used in the experiments are appropriate for all three datasets. Could you discuss whether domain-specific embeddings might be needed for some of these datasets, particularly Genomics?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and clearly presents the motivation and methodology. The examples provided in Section 3 are helpful for understanding the bucketing process. The experimental setup is appropriate and the evaluation metrics are standard for this research area.

I would recommend that the authors revise the paper to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly by adding comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art methods and providing a more thorough analysis of the limitations of their approach. The parameter-free claim should also be clarified, as the dynamic threshold calculation may introduce hidden parameters.

Overall, the paper presents a novel and promising approach to topic coherence measurement with strong empirical results, but would benefit from more comprehensive comparisons with recent work and a clearer discussion of the method's limitations.
