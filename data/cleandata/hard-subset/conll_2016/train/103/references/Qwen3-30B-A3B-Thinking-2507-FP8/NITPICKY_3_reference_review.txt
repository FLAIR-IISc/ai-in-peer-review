

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of Paper and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach called TBuckets for measuring topic quality in topic models. The core idea is to group topic words into thematic buckets (logical clusters) and use properties of these buckets (particularly the size and composition of the largest bucket) to compute a coherence score. The paper describes three techniques for creating these buckets: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify orthogonal dimensions), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (an improved version with a reorganization step). The authors claim that their TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique outperforms state-of-the-art on two of three datasets while being completely parameter-free.

## Strengths

1. **Intuitive Conceptual Framework**: The TBuckets approach aligns well with how humans intuitively assess topic coherence. The idea of grouping topic words into thematic buckets and evaluating based on the size of the largest bucket is conceptually sound and easy to understand, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper evaluates the approach on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) using the same evaluation metrics as Roder et al. (2015), providing a fair comparison to state-of-the-art. The demonstration that their approach outperforms existing methods on two out of three datasets is a meaningful contribution.

3. **Practical Application to Text Classification**: The paper demonstrates a valuable application of topic quality measurement to weakly supervised text classification, showing that using TBuckets to filter high-quality topics before human annotation leads to better classification performance. This practical utility strengthens the paper's contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Technical Inaccuracies in SVD Explanation (Section 2.2)**: The paper contains a fundamental error in describing SVD. It states "the matrix V contains the eigenvectors of ATA" which is correct, but then claims "the V matrix helps to identify important dimensions among the entities in terms of their features." This is misleading - V^T (not V) contains the eigenvectors of ATA, and these eigenvectors correspond to the feature dimensions, not the entity dimensions. This is a basic linear algebra error that undermines the technical credibility of the paper.

2. **Mathematical Inconsistency in Reorganization Threshold (Section 3.1)**: The paper defines the dynamic threshold as "AWES − AWPSAWES" which would be a negative value since both AWPS and AWES are between 0 and 1. This appears to be a typo, but as written, the formula doesn't make mathematical sense. The authors should clarify whether they meant "AWES - (AWPS * AWES)" or something else, and provide justification for the formula.

3. **Misleading Claim about "Parameter-Free" Techniques**: The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but this is inaccurate. The SVD-based techniques implicitly rely on the dimensionality of word embeddings (300 in their case), which is a parameter. Additionally, the reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg uses a threshold that depends on dataset-specific values (AWPS and AWES), which effectively makes it parameterized. The claim of being "completely parameter-free" is misleading and contradicts the paper's own description.

4. **Inconsistent Dataset Description and Results (Section 4.1 & Table 2)**: The paper states that human coherence scores range from 1 to 3, but in Table 2, the human scores shown are 0.542, 2.3125, 0.028, 1.23, 0.796, 2.82, and 0.071, 1.25. These values are inconsistent with the stated range. The paper needs to clarify whether these are normalized scores (as suggested in the table note) or if there's a fundamental error in the dataset description.

5. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper reports marginal improvements (e.g., 0.869 vs. 0.859 for 20NG), but doesn't provide statistical significance testing to determine whether these differences are meaningful. Without this, it's difficult to assess whether the claimed improvements are substantial or merely due to random variation.

## Questions to Authors

1. The SVD explanation in Section 2.2 contains a fundamental error regarding which matrix corresponds to feature dimensions. Can you clarify the correct mathematical relationship between the SVD components and the feature dimensions?

2. The threshold formula in Section 3.1 is mathematically inconsistent (AWES - AWPSAWES). Can you clarify the intended formula and provide justification for it?

3. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter-free." Given that they rely on 300-dimensional word embeddings, how do you consider this dimensionality not a parameter? Can you provide a clear definition of what constitutes a "parameter" in this context?

4. The human coherence scores in Table 2 (0.542, 2.3125, etc.) are inconsistent with the stated range of 1-3. Are these normalized scores, and if so, how were they normalized?

5. The paper reports marginal improvements over state-of-the-art. Have you conducted statistical significance tests to determine whether these differences are meaningful?

## Additional Comments

- The reference list has inconsistent formatting (e.g., "Aletras and Stevenson.,? 2013" instead of "Aletras and Stevenson, 2013").
- The paper mentions "Figure 1" but doesn't include the figure in the submission.
- There are minor typos throughout, such as "im-proving" in the introduction.
- The paper refers to "GloVe framework" but doesn't specify which version was used or provide a proper citation for the specific embeddings.

## Overall Recommendation

While the TBuckets concept is intuitive and the evaluation provides some promising results, the paper contains several fundamental technical errors and misleading claims that significantly undermine its credibility. The technical inaccuracies in the SVD explanation, the mathematical inconsistency in the threshold formula, and the misleading claim about parameter-free techniques are particularly concerning. These issues suggest a lack of rigorous technical review of the paper before submission.

The marginal improvements over state-of-the-art (which are not statistically validated) do not outweigh the substantial methodological flaws. As a perfectionist reviewer, I must recommend rejection due to these serious technical issues that would mislead the research community if published. The paper needs substantial revision to address these fundamental problems before it can be considered for publication.
