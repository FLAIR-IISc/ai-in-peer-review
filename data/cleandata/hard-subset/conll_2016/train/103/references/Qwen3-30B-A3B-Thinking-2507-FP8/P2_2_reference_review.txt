

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core idea is that coherent topics should form a single dominant bucket (indicating a single coherent theme), while incoherent topics will have words distributed across multiple buckets. The authors propose three techniques: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (reorganizing buckets from SVD for improved coherence). They evaluate their methods on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) by correlating their coherence scores with human annotations, demonstrating that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on two datasets. They also show that using their coherence scores to select high-quality topics improves weakly supervised text classification performance.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **TBuckets-SVD-Reorg**: A parameter-free topic coherence measure that outperforms state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets. This is the strongest contribution as it combines novelty with strong empirical results and practical advantages (no parameter tuning).

2. **TBuckets-SVD**: A parameter-free technique for topic coherence measurement using SVD to discover thematic sub-themes. This is significant as it provides a theoretically sound approach that requires no parameter tuning, unlike most existing methods.

3. **Application to text classification**: Demonstrating that using TBuckets to filter high-quality topics improves weakly supervised text classification performance. This shows practical utility beyond just coherence measurement.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and compelling approach**: The "bucket" concept provides a fresh perspective on topic coherence that aligns well with human intuition. The paper clearly explains this with well-chosen examples (e.g., the weather topic forming one bucket versus the Afghanistan/Wikileaks topic forming multiple buckets), making the contribution highly understandable.

2. **Parameter-free nature of SVD-based methods**: The fact that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning is a major practical advantage. The authors correctly emphasize this as a significant benefit over state-of-the-art methods that require extensive parameter tuning (as noted in Table 1 where their methods require no tuning while others need "Max Dist" or other parameters).

3. **Strong empirical results**: The results are well-presented with clear tables (Table 1) showing that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets (NYT and 20NG) with statistically significant improvements (e.g., 0.837 vs. 0.806 on NYT). The text classification application in Section 4.3 further validates the practical utility of their method.

4. **Clear presentation of methodology**: The paper provides detailed explanations of all three techniques with clear examples (e.g., the dry run examples in Section 3). The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is particularly well-motivated by specific examples where the basic SVD approach failed.

5. **Use of GloVe embeddings**: The authors correctly use standardized word embeddings (GloVe) that are widely available, making their approach easily reproducible and applicable to any topic model.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares only with Roder et al. (2015), but since then, there have been significant advances in topic coherence measurement (e.g., Lau et al. 2014, Meng et al. 2018). This is a serious omission as the authors claim to "outperform state-of-the-art," but their comparison is not with the most recent and strongest baselines. For example, the paper doesn't compare with the "CUCI with NPMI" method which Aletras and Stevenson (2013) showed correlates well with human judgments.

2. **Inadequate explanation of SVD's theoretical justification**: While the paper explains how TBuckets-SVD works, it doesn't provide a strong theoretical foundation for why SVD would be effective for this task. The claim that "the V matrix, provides eigenvectors ordered according to most significant singular values first" is somewhat hand-wavy. The paper would be stronger with a more rigorous explanation of why the top singular vectors correspond to meaningful thematic dimensions.

3. **Poor performance on Genomics dataset**: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg performs worse than state-of-the-art on the Genomics dataset (0.68 vs. 0.773), while outperforming on the other two. The paper doesn't adequately address why this discrepancy occurs or what limitations this suggests about their method. This is a significant weakness as it suggests their method may not generalize well to all topic distributions.

4. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to show the contribution of each component of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg. For example, how much of the improvement comes from the reorganization step versus the SVD step? This makes it difficult to assess what aspects of their method are truly novel and valuable.

5. **No computational complexity analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of their methods compared to existing approaches. This is particularly important since topic modeling is often applied to large-scale datasets, and the computational cost of coherence measurement can be a bottleneck.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to outperform state-of-the-art, but only compares with Roder et al. (2015). How does your method compare with more recent coherence measures like those proposed in Lau et al. (2014) or Meng et al. (2018)? Have you considered these in your evaluation?

2. The paper states that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on AWES and AWPS. Is this threshold truly parameter-free, or does it require some form of tuning based on the dataset?

3. Why does TBuckets-SVD-Reorg perform worse than state-of-the-art on the Genomics dataset, while outperforming on the other two? What characteristics of the Genomics topics might explain this discrepancy?

4. Could you provide more details on the computational complexity of your methods compared to existing approaches? How would your method scale to very large topic models?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper with a novel and intuitive approach to topic coherence measurement. The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method is particularly compelling due to its parameter-free nature and strong empirical performance. However, the lack of comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods and the poor performance on the Genomics dataset are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. The paper would be substantially stronger with a more comprehensive comparison to current methods and a deeper analysis of why their method works (or doesn't work) in different scenarios.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions (particularly addressing the weaknesses noted above), as the core contribution is valuable and well-executed, but the comparison to state-of-the-art needs to be updated to reflect more recent advances in the field.
