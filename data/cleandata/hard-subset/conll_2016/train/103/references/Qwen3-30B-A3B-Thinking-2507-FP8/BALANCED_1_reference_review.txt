

# Review for "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary
This paper introduces a novel approach called TBuckets for measuring the coherence of topics in topic models. The core idea is to group topic words into thematic "buckets" (logical groups), where a topic with a single dominant bucket is considered more coherent. The authors propose three techniques for creating these buckets: (1) TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering on word embeddings), (2) TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify orthogonal sub-themes), and (3) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step to improve bucket formation). They evaluate their methods on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) by correlating their coherence scores with human-annotated scores and demonstrate that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two of the three datasets. The paper also shows that using TBuckets for selecting high-quality topics improves performance in weakly supervised text classification.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **A novel topic coherence measurement framework based on thematic grouping (buckets) of topic words.** This represents a fundamentally different perspective on topic coherence compared to existing pairwise similarity-based approaches.

2. **Parameter-free topic coherence measurement techniques (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg).** The authors emphasize that these SVD-based techniques require no parameter tuning, which is a significant practical advantage over existing methods that often require extensive parameter tuning.

3. **Demonstration of practical utility in weakly supervised text classification.** The paper shows that using TBuckets to select high-quality topics for human annotation leads to improved classification performance, providing strong evidence of the method's real-world applicability.

## Strengths
1. **Novel and intuitive approach to topic coherence:** The paper presents a fresh perspective on topic coherence that aligns well with how humans perceive topics. The bucketing analogy (grouping words into thematic clusters as humans would) is compelling and well-illustrated with clear examples. This represents a meaningful conceptual shift from existing pairwise similarity-based approaches.

2. **Strong empirical results with practical advantages:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method outperforms the state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets (NYT and 20NG), which is significant. Crucially, the authors demonstrate that their SVD-based techniques are completely parameter-free, while state-of-the-art methods require considerable parameter tuning. This is a substantial practical advantage that the authors effectively highlight.

3. **Clear demonstration of real-world utility:** The application to weakly supervised text classification is particularly strong. The results in Table 3 show that selecting high-coherence topics (using TBuckets) leads to better classification performance than selecting randomly (S1 vs S2), with the difference being especially pronounced for challenging datasets like PC vs MAC. This provides strong evidence of the method's practical value beyond just coherence measurement.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of theoretical foundation for the bucketing approach:** The paper presents the bucketing idea as intuitive but provides no formal justification for why grouping words into buckets would correlate with human coherence judgments. There's no discussion of how this approach relates to existing coherence measures or why it should work better theoretically. This is a significant gap in the paper's argumentation.

2. **Insufficient comparison with related work:** While the authors compare with state-of-the-art methods, they don't thoroughly discuss how their approach relates to or differs from existing coherence measures. For example, they don't explain why their bucketing approach would work better than CUSheffield (which also uses word embeddings and topic-specific context vectors) or how their approach might complement existing methods.

3. **Implementation details and reproducibility concerns:** The paper mentions using GloVe embeddings but doesn't provide details about how they were used (e.g., whether they were pre-trained or fine-tuned). The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is complex but not well-explained, and the paper lacks sufficient detail to fully reproduce this method. The dynamic threshold calculation in the reorganization step is particularly opaque.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does the bucketing approach relate to or differ from existing coherence measures like CUSheffield (which also uses word embeddings)? Why should we expect bucketing to correlate better with human judgments?

2. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on AWPS and AWES. Could you clarify exactly how this threshold is computed and why it's considered parameter-free?

3. In Table 2, the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg coherence scores are normalized to [0,1] while human scores are [1,3]. How did this normalization affect the correlation results, and would the relative performance of the methods change if the scores were not normalized?

4. For the text classification experiments, why did the authors use 5 runs for S1 (without quality consideration) and 10 runs for S2 (with quality consideration)? Would the results change if they had used the same number of runs for both strategies?

## Overall Recommendation
I recommend **weak reject** for this paper. While the paper presents a novel approach to topic coherence measurement with strong empirical results and clear practical applications, it has significant weaknesses in theoretical justification and comparison with related work that prevent it from meeting the high standards expected for this venue. The parameter-free aspect of their method is a strong practical advantage, but the lack of theoretical grounding and insufficient comparison with related work makes it difficult to fully assess the significance of their contribution.

## Suggestions for Improvement
1. **Strengthen theoretical justification:** Provide a more rigorous explanation for why bucketing words into thematic groups would correlate with human coherence judgments. How does this approach relate to the underlying semantics of topics?

2. **Expand comparison with related work:** Conduct a more thorough comparison with existing coherence measures, particularly those that use word embeddings (like CUSheffield). Explain why the bucketing approach should outperform these methods.

3. **Improve implementation details:** Provide more complete details about the word embeddings used (including dimensionality and training corpus), the exact procedure for computing the dynamic threshold, and any other implementation details necessary for reproducibility.

4. **Clarify the parameter-free claim:** If the reorganization step does involve implicit parameters, be explicit about this and explain why it's still considered parameter-free in practice.

The paper has a strong foundation and the TBuckets approach shows real promise, but addressing these weaknesses would significantly strengthen the contribution and make it suitable for publication at a top-tier conference.
