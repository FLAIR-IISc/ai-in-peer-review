

# Peer Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic "buckets" based on semantic similarity. The authors present three techniques: TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering of word embeddings), TBuckets-SVD (using SVD to identify orthogonal sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (reorganizing buckets from TBuckets-SVD). The core contribution is the insight that topic coherence can be measured by the distribution of words across buckets, with a single large bucket indicating high coherence.

The main contributions I see (in order of strength):
1. The intuitive "word bucketing" approach that aligns with human interpretation of topic coherence, supported by clear examples
2. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques, which is a significant practical advantage over existing methods
3. The demonstrated utility of the coherence measure in improving weakly supervised text classification performance

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation and Intuitive Approach**: The paper effectively demonstrates how humans intuitively group topic words into thematic buckets when assessing coherence, making the TBuckets concept highly relatable. The examples in Section 3 (e.g., the weather topic vs. the Afghanistan/Wikileaks topic) clearly illustrate how bucket distribution correlates with human judgment of coherence. This strong motivation elevates the work beyond just another coherence metric.

2. **Parameter-Free SVD Techniques**: The authors correctly highlight that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning, unlike most state-of-the-art methods. This is a significant practical advantage, as parameter tuning is often time-consuming and requires expertise. The paper shows that these parameter-free techniques outperform the best state-of-the-art results on 2 out of 3 datasets (NYT and 20NG), which is compelling evidence of their value.

3. **Effective Experimental Evaluation**: The evaluation on the same datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) and using the same metrics (Pearson's r with human annotations) as Roder et al. (2015) provides a fair comparison. The results in Table 1 are convincing, with TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieving the highest correlation scores on NYT (0.837) and 20NG (0.863), surpassing the previous best (0.806 and 0.859 respectively). The additional text classification experiment in Section 4.3 further demonstrates practical utility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Novelty**: While the TBuckets concept is presented as novel, it bears significant resemblance to existing distributional semantic approaches. The clustering-based method is nearly identical to Aletras and Stevenson's (2013) CUSheffield metric, and the SVD approach resembles other dimensionality reduction techniques used for topic coherence. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate TBuckets from these established methods in the related work section.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares only with Roder et al. (2015), which is from 2015. Since then, there have been several advances in topic coherence measurement (e.g., Cao et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2019). The authors should have compared with these more recent methods to establish the current state-of-the-art and demonstrate the lasting relevance of their approach.

3. **Misrepresentation of "Parameter-Free" Claim**: The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on topic-specific values (AWPS and AWES). This is not truly parameter-free, as the threshold calculation depends on topic characteristics. The authors should clarify this point and explain why they consider it parameter-free.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why the SVD-based approach would work better than simpler alternatives. For instance, why not just use the top k words and compute a standard coherence measure (like CUCI) on them? The authors should explain the theoretical underpinnings of their method to strengthen their contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter free," but the reorganization threshold depends on topic-specific values (AWPS and AWES). Could you clarify why this is considered parameter-free, and how this differs from other methods that use dynamic thresholds?

2. How does the TBuckets approach fundamentally differ from Aletras and Stevenson's (2013) CUSheffield metric, which also uses distributional semantics and word embeddings? What specific improvements does TBuckets offer over this established method?

3. The paper compares only with Roder et al. (2015), but there have been more recent advances in topic coherence measurement. Could you explain why you chose not to compare with these more recent methods, and how your approach would perform against them?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated and intuitively appealing approach to topic coherence measurement that demonstrates strong empirical results. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques is a significant practical contribution, and the text classification application provides compelling evidence of real-world utility. However, the novelty is somewhat limited, and the paper would be strengthened by comparing with more recent state-of-the-art methods and providing clearer theoretical justification for the approach.

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a meaningful contribution to the topic coherence evaluation field. With some clarifications about the "parameter-free" claim and better differentiation from existing methods, this paper would be even stronger. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
