

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a novel approach called TBuckets for measuring topic coherence in probabilistic topic models. The core idea is to group topic words into thematic buckets, with the intuition that a single large bucket indicates high coherence. The authors propose three techniques for bucket creation: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (reorganizing buckets from TBuckets-SVD). They evaluate these techniques on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) by correlating their coherence scores with human-annotated scores and show that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two datasets.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Conceptual novelty of topic coherence measurement**: The bucket-based approach to topic coherence measurement is intuitive and aligns with human perception of topic coherence.
2. **Parameter-free techniques**: The SVD-based approaches (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) require no parameter tuning, which is a significant practical advantage over existing methods.
3. **Demonstrated utility in text classification**: The paper shows that using TBuckets for topic quality assessment improves weakly supervised text classification performance.

## Strengths

1. **Intuitive concept of topic coherence measurement**: The TBuckets approach is conceptually simple and well-motivated by how humans perceive topic coherence. The examples provided in Section 3 make the concept immediately understandable, which is a strong point for a paper in this field.

2. **Parameter-free implementation**: The paper successfully demonstrates that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning, which is a significant advantage over existing methods that often require careful parameter selection (as noted in Roder et al., 2015). This practical advantage is well-documented in Table 1.

3. **Demonstrated utility in text classification**: The application to weakly supervised text classification (Section 4.3) is well-executed and provides concrete evidence of the practical value of the coherence measure. The results show clear improvements when using high-coherence topics for annotation, particularly on difficult classification tasks like PC vs MAC.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical technical inconsistencies and typos**: The paper contains several critical technical errors that undermine its credibility. Most notably:
   - In Section 3.2, the LDA Order Weight formula is written as "LDA Order Weight(wi) = 1 log(i+ 1) if i > 0 = 1 otherwise" without a division symbol. This is a major typo that would confuse readers (should be "1/log(i+1)").
   - In Section 3.1, the dynamic threshold formula is written as "AWES − AWPSAWES" which is ambiguous and mathematically unclear. It should specify whether it's "AWES - (AWPS/AWES)" or "AWES - (AWPS * AWES)".
   - In Table 2, the human coherence scores include values like 0.028 and 0.071, but the paper states "Human assigned coherence scores lie between 1 and 3." The normalization process is not clearly explained, creating a contradiction.

2. **Inconsistent parameter claims**: The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg uses a dynamic threshold based on topic-specific calculations (AWPS and AWES). This is effectively a parameter that varies per topic, contradicting the "completely parameter free" claim. The paper fails to clarify this distinction.

3. **Marginal performance improvements with unclear significance**: The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets, but the improvements are marginal (e.g., 0.837 vs 0.806 for NYT). The paper does not provide statistical significance tests for these improvements, making it difficult to assess whether the differences are meaningful. The claim of "outperforming the state-of-the-art" is overstated given the small differences.

4. **Inconsistent evaluation setup**: The paper compares to Roder et al. (2015) but doesn't clearly explain how the comparison is set up. In Table 1, the "Roder et al. (2015) Best performing settings" column shows values like 0.806, 0.859, 0.773, but it's unclear whether these are the best results reported by Roder et al. or the authors' own results. This inconsistency undermines the evaluation.

5. **Inadequate discussion of limitations**: The paper fails to discuss important limitations of the TBuckets approach. For example:
   - How does the method perform on topics with very few words (e.g., fewer than 5 words)?
   - How does it handle topics that are coherent but have multiple equally important themes (e.g., a topic about "climate change and politics")?
   - What are the computational costs of the SVD-based approaches compared to simpler methods?

## Questions to Authors

1. In Section 3.2, the LDA Order Weight formula is written as "1 log(i+ 1)" without a division symbol. Is this a typo, and should it be "1/log(i+1)"?

2. In Section 3.1, the dynamic threshold is written as "AWES − AWPSAWES." What operation is intended between AWPS and AWES? Is it "AWES - (AWPS/AWES)" or "AWES - (AWPS * AWES)"?

3. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on topic-specific calculations. How does this differ from having a parameter? Is the threshold calculation considered a "parameter" in the context of the paper?

4. In Table 2, the human coherence scores include values like 0.028 and 0.071, but the paper states "Human assigned coherence scores lie between 1 and 3." How were these scores normalized to lie between 0 and 1, and why are some of the normalized scores below 1?

5. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art on two datasets, but the improvements are small (e.g., 0.837 vs 0.806 for NYT). What is the statistical significance of these improvements? Were any statistical tests performed?

## Recommendation

I recommend **rejection**. While the paper presents a conceptually interesting approach to topic coherence measurement, it contains multiple critical technical inconsistencies that undermine its credibility. The paper's claims about being "completely parameter free" are contradicted by the dynamic threshold in the reorganization step, and the marginal performance improvements are not statistically validated. The technical errors (particularly the missing division symbol in the LDA Order Weight formula) are serious enough to suggest the paper was not carefully proofread. These minor but critical flaws outweigh the paper's conceptual contributions, and I believe they would prevent the paper from being published in a top-tier conference like CONLL.
