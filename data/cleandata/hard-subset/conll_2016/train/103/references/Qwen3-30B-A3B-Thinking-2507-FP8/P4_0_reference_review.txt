

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel approach called TBuckets for measuring topic coherence in topic models. The core idea is that a coherent topic should form a single dominant thematic bucket when topic words are grouped based on semantic similarity, while incoherent topics would form multiple medium-sized buckets. The authors propose three techniques for creating these buckets: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to discover sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step for the SVD-based method). They evaluate these techniques on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) by correlating their coherence scores with human-annotated scores and demonstrate that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two of the three datasets. The paper also shows the utility of their approach for weakly supervised text classification by filtering low-quality topics before human annotation.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Novel conceptual framework for topic coherence measurement**: The paper introduces a new way of conceptualizing topic coherence based on thematic grouping of words into buckets, grounded in human perception of coherence rather than purely statistical measures.

2. **Parameter-free topic coherence measurement**: The SVD-based techniques (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) are presented as completely parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage over existing methods that require extensive parameter tuning.

3. **Demonstration of practical utility**: The paper provides evidence that using TBuckets for topic quality assessment improves performance in weakly supervised text classification tasks by filtering out low-quality topics before human annotation.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptually novel approach**: The TBuckets idea offers a fresh perspective on topic coherence measurement that aligns with human perception of topic coherence (grouping words into thematic buckets). This is distinct from existing approaches that primarily rely on statistical measures like PMI or distributional similarity. The examples provided (weather topic vs. non-coherent topic) effectively illustrate the intuition behind the approach.

2. **Parameter-free advantage**: The claim that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are completely parameter-free is a significant practical contribution. As the authors note, state-of-the-art methods require "considerable tuning of multiple parameters," while their SVD-based techniques require none. This makes the approach more accessible and easier to apply in practice without requiring expertise in parameter tuning.

3. **Strong empirical validation**: The evaluation on three different datasets with standard metrics (Pearson's r correlation with human judgments) shows consistent improvement over state-of-the-art methods on two out of three datasets. The results are clearly presented in Table 1, and the comparison with Roder et al. (2015) provides a meaningful benchmark.

4. **Demonstrated practical application**: The text classification experiment (Table 3) effectively shows how the coherence measurement can be used to improve real-world applications. The results show that using TBuckets to filter topics before human annotation leads to better classification performance (higher macroF1 scores) in most cases, with particularly notable improvements on difficult classification tasks like PC vs MAC.

5. **Clear and well-structured methodology**: The paper provides detailed explanations of the three techniques, including the reorganization step, with clear examples of how the bucketing process works. The mathematical formulation of the coherence score (Section 3.2) is well-motivated and aligned with the core intuition of the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in technical implementation**: While the TBuckets concept is novel, the specific techniques used (clustering, SVD) are not new to NLP. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its application of these techniques from prior work that has used similar approaches for related tasks. The SVD-based technique, in particular, builds directly on standard matrix factorization methods without clear innovation in the application to topic coherence.

2. **Questionable claim about being "completely parameter-free"**: The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on average word pair similarity (AWPS) and average word eigenvector similarity (AWES). This threshold calculation involves a formula (AWES - AWPS*AWES) that effectively introduces a form of parameterization, even if it's not user-specified. The authors should clarify how this threshold is computed and why it's considered parameter-free.

3. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) versus the SVD step alone (TBuckets-SVD). It's unclear how much of the performance improvement comes from the reorganization step versus the SVD technique. A proper ablation study would strengthen the claims about the effectiveness of the reorganization step.

4. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't adequately address limitations of the approach. For example, it doesn't discuss how the method performs with topics containing very few words (e.g., fewer than 5 words), which is common in topic modeling. The paper also doesn't address how the method handles topics that are coherent but have multiple sub-topics (e.g., a topic about "cars" that includes both "sports cars" and "electric cars").

5. **Comparison with more recent work**: The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015) as the state-of-the-art, but doesn't compare with more recent work on topic coherence measurement that may have improved upon the state-of-the-art since 2015. This limits the significance of the claimed performance improvements.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on AWPS and AWES. Can the authors clarify how this threshold is computed and why it's considered parameter-free, given that it involves a mathematical formula?

2. The paper shows that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on two of three datasets. Can the authors provide insight into why it performs better on some datasets (NYT, 20NG) but not on Genomics? Are there specific characteristics of these datasets that make TBuckets more effective?

3. The paper mentions that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg "carries merit for being completely parameter free," but the clustering technique (TBuckets-Clustering) requires a single parameter (Max Dist). How does the authors' approach to parameter-free methods compare with other methods that might require minimal parameter tuning, and why is this distinction important for practical applications?

4. The paper doesn't discuss how the method handles topics with very few words (e.g., fewer than 5 words). How would TBuckets perform on such topics, and would the bucketing approach still be meaningful with limited words?
