

# Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes "TBuckets," a novel approach for measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core idea is that a single large bucket with few small buckets indicates high coherence, while multiple medium-sized buckets indicate low coherence. The authors propose three techniques for creating these buckets: clustering-based (TBuckets-Clustering), SVD-based (TBuckets-SVD), and SVD with reorganization (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. The TBuckets concept itself, which offers a fresh human-interpretable perspective on topic coherence measurement.
2. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques, a significant practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods requiring parameter tuning.
3. The application demonstrating how TBuckets improves weakly supervised text classification by filtering for high-quality topics.

## Strengths

1. **Intuitive and well-motivated approach**: The paper effectively demonstrates the TBuckets concept through clear examples of coherent vs. non-coherent topics. The bucketing process aligns well with how humans assess topic coherence, making the approach highly interpretable. This human-centric perspective is a significant contribution to the field, as most coherence measures focus on statistical properties rather than human interpretability.

2. **Strong empirical results with practical advantages**: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art on two of three datasets (NYT and 20NG) while being completely parameter-free. This dual advantage—better performance and no parameter tuning—represents a meaningful contribution to the field. The parameter-free nature is particularly valuable for practical applications where tuning multiple parameters can be time-consuming and require expertise.

3. **Demonstrated practical utility**: The application to text classification is compelling. The paper shows that filtering topics by coherence score (using TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) significantly improves classification performance in weakly supervised settings, especially on challenging datasets like PC vs. MAC. This practical validation strengthens the paper's contribution beyond just coherence measurement.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of comparison**: The paper only compares with the best-performing methods reported in Roder et al. (2015), not with all state-of-the-art methods. This makes it difficult to assess whether TBuckets represents a genuine advancement over the entire landscape of coherence measures or just the specific methods evaluated in that paper.

2. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the reorganization step. Without these, it's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the reorganization versus the SVD approach itself. For example, does TBucks-SVD alone perform well enough, or is the reorganization essential?

3. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to only three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics), all of which are standard but relatively homogeneous. A broader evaluation across more diverse datasets would strengthen the claim that TBuckets generalizes well across different domains.

4. **Ad-hoc reorganization threshold**: The dynamic threshold for reorganization (AWES - AWPS*AWES) seems somewhat arbitrary and lacks theoretical justification. While the paper claims it avoids parameter tuning, the threshold calculation itself is complex and not clearly motivated, making it difficult to understand why this specific formulation works better.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify whether you compared TBuckets with all methods in Roder et al. (2015), or only with the best-performing methods reported in that paper? This would help contextualize your performance claims.

2. Would it be possible to provide ablation studies showing the performance of TBuckets-SVD without reorganization? This would help isolate the contribution of the reorganization step.

3. How would your approach perform on larger topics (e.g., 20 words instead of 10)? The current evaluation only considers topics with 10 words, which may not reflect real-world applications where topics often have more words.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that presents a novel and intuitively appealing approach to topic coherence measurement. The TBuckets concept is grounded in human interpretation, and the parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques is a significant practical advantage. The empirical results are strong, with TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforming state-of-the-art on two datasets while requiring no parameter tuning. The application to text classification further demonstrates the practical utility of the approach.

While the paper has some limitations in scope and evaluation, these are not severe enough to undermine the core contribution. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of topic modeling by offering a human-centered coherence measure that is both effective and easy to apply. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions above.

The paper meets the reviewing criteria well, with clear contributions, strong empirical results, and a compelling application. The strengths outweigh the weaknesses, making this a valuable contribution to the field of topic modeling and evaluation.
