

# Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary
The paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic coherence in topic models (specifically LDA) by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The authors claim that their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two out of three datasets while requiring no parameter tuning for their SVD-based techniques. The paper evaluates three specific implementations: TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering of word embeddings), TBuckets-SVD (using SVD to discover sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (reorganizing buckets from TBuckets-SVD).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel framework for measuring topic coherence based on grouping topic words into thematic buckets (TBuckets), which provides a fresh perspective on topic coherence that aligns well with human judgment.
2. Three specific techniques for implementing the TBuckets framework, particularly the parameter-free TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, which demonstrate superior performance over state-of-the-art methods on two out of three datasets.
3. A clear practical application of their coherence measure in weakly supervised text classification, showing that filtering topics by coherence leads to improved classification performance.

## Strengths

1. **Intuitive and well-motivated framework**: The TBuckets concept is conceptually sound and aligns closely with how humans judge topic coherence. The paper effectively demonstrates this through clear, illustrative examples (e.g., the weather vs. Afghanistan topics) that show how bucket formation correlates with human judgment. This intuitive connection is a significant strength that makes the approach immediately understandable.

2. **Strong empirical results with practical advantages**: The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique achieves the highest correlation with human judgments on two out of three datasets (NYT: 0.837 vs. 0.806, 20NG: 0.869 vs. 0.859), outperforming the state-of-the-art. Crucially, this improvement comes with the significant practical advantage of being completely parameter-free, unlike methods requiring extensive parameter tuning as noted in the paper.

3. **Clear demonstration of practical utility**: The application to text classification (Section 4.3) is well-executed and compelling. The paper shows that using TBuckets to filter high-quality topics before human annotation leads to significantly better classification performance (e.g., 96.19% vs. 95.95% for POLITICS vs SCIENCE), demonstrating clear real-world value.

4. **Rigorous evaluation methodology**: The paper follows standard evaluation practices in the field, using Pearson's r correlation with human-annotated coherence scores across three well-established datasets. The results are presented clearly in Table 1 with appropriate comparisons to the state-of-the-art.

5. **Parameter-free approach**: The fact that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning is a substantial practical advantage over many existing methods, which is highlighted effectively in the paper. This is particularly valuable for practitioners who may not have the expertise or resources to tune multiple parameters.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical foundation**: While the paper provides intuitive examples of how bucketing aligns with human judgment, it lacks a strong theoretical justification for why this approach should work better than existing methods. The connection between bucket formation and coherence is presented as intuitive rather than rigorously justified, which weakens the conceptual contribution.

2. **Limited comparison with recent work**: The paper compares exclusively with Roder et al. (2015), which is a good baseline, but fails to compare with more recent methods published since 2015. The field has advanced considerably since then, and the paper doesn't establish its position relative to these newer approaches.

3. **Insufficient analysis of why TBuckets works**: The paper demonstrates that TBuckets performs well but doesn't provide deep analysis of *why* it works better than existing methods. For instance, why does the SVD-based approach outperform clustering? What specific properties of the SVD decomposition make it effective for this task? This lack of mechanistic understanding limits the contribution.

4. **Ad hoc reorganization step**: The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg seems to be a post-hoc adjustment to improve performance. The dynamic threshold calculation (AWES - AWPS*AWES) lacks strong theoretical justification and appears somewhat arbitrary, which weakens the methodological contribution.

5. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper doesn't thoroughly discuss the limitations of the TBuckets approach. For example, how does it perform on topics with very few words (e.g., fewer than 5)? What happens when topic words are highly ambiguous? These limitations would be important for practitioners to understand.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a comparison with more recent topic coherence methods published after 2015 (e.g., from 2016-2023) to better establish the state-of-the-art and demonstrate the novelty of your approach?

2. Can you provide more theoretical justification for why the SVD-based approach works better than clustering for this task? What specific properties of SVD make it particularly suitable for topic coherence measurement?

3. How does the TBuckets approach handle topics with very few words (e.g., fewer than 5 words)? What is the minimum number of words required for the approach to work effectively, and how does performance degrade as topic word count decreases?

4. Could you provide more details on the dynamic threshold calculation in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (AWES - AWPS*AWES)? Why was this specific formula chosen rather than alternative formulations?

5. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is parameter-free, but the reorganization step involves several calculations. How do you ensure this remains truly parameter-free across different datasets and topic distributions?

## Overall Recommendation

While the paper presents a conceptually intuitive approach with strong empirical results, the lack of theoretical foundation and limited comparison with more recent work are significant weaknesses that prevent this from being a strong contribution to the field. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques is a notable advantage, and the application to text classification is compelling.

**Decision: Weak Accept**

The paper has several strengths that make it a potentially valuable contribution, particularly the intuitive TBuckets framework and the strong empirical results on standard datasets. However, the lack of theoretical justification and limited comparison with more recent work prevents it from being a strong acceptance. With revisions addressing the theoretical foundation, comparison with more recent methods, and deeper analysis of why the approach works, this could become a strong contribution to the field.

The authors should revise the paper to:
1. Provide stronger theoretical justification for the bucketing approach
2. Compare with recent methods published since 2015
3. Deepen the analysis of why TBuckets works better than existing approaches
4. More thoroughly discuss the limitations of the approach
5. Provide clearer justification for the dynamic threshold calculation

These revisions would significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more compelling contribution to the field of topic modeling evaluation.
