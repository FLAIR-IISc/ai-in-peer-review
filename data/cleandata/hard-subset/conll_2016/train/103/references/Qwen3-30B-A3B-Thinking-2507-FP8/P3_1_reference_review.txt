

# Peer Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach called TBuckets for measuring topic coherence in topic models. The core idea is to group topic words into thematic buckets (groups) and use the properties of these buckets (particularly the size of the largest bucket) to determine topic coherence. The authors present three specific techniques for creating these buckets: (1) TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering of word embeddings), (2) TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to find orthogonal dimensions/sub-themes), and (3) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step to improve the SVD-based approach). They evaluate their methods on three datasets using Pearson correlation with human-annotated coherence scores, demonstrating that their best method (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) outperforms state-of-the-art results on two out of three datasets. The paper also shows that using their coherence scores to filter high-quality topics improves text classification performance.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel topic coherence measurement approach based on thematic grouping of words into buckets, with intuitive human-like assessment properties.
2. A parameter-free implementation (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that outperforms state-of-the-art methods requiring multiple parameter tunings.
3. Demonstration of practical utility for improving weakly supervised text classification by filtering high-quality topics.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and Intuitive Approach**: The TBuckets concept is well-motivated and aligns with how humans assess topic coherence. The idea that coherent topics should have most words in a single thematic group is straightforward and compelling. The examples provided (e.g., the weather vs. Afghanistan topics) effectively illustrate the concept. This is a fresh perspective on topic coherence that moves beyond statistical measures like PMI or NPMI.

2. **Parameter-Free Nature**: The most significant contribution of this work is the parameter-free nature of TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg. As the authors correctly point out, most state-of-the-art coherence measures require substantial parameter tuning (e.g., Roder et al. 2015), which can be time-consuming and non-trivial for practitioners. The fact that their SVD-based methods require no parameters at all is a major practical advantage that deserves emphasis.

3. **Strong Empirical Results**: The evaluation demonstrates clear improvements over state-of-the-art. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves Pearson's r = 0.837 on NYT (vs. 0.806 for state-of-the-art) and 0.863 on 20NG (vs. 0.859). These are meaningful improvements, especially considering the parameter-free nature of the method. The text classification application further validates the practical utility of the approach.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across three datasets with multiple scoring metrics (size, word order, eigenvector similarity). The ablation study of different linkage configurations for TBuckets-Clustering (Figure 1) demonstrates careful analysis of the parameter space. The text classification experiment also adds practical validation beyond the coherence measurement task.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Foundation**: While the approach is intuitive, the paper lacks a strong theoretical justification for why the bucketing approach correlates with human coherence judgments. There's no discussion of how the bucketing process relates to semantic coherence or why the properties of the largest bucket (e.g., size) should correlate with human ratings. This weakens the scientific contribution, as it's unclear whether the method would generalize to other coherence dimensions beyond what's measured in the datasets.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims to outperform state-of-the-art methods but only compares against the reported results in Roder et al. (2015). It would be significantly more convincing to implement and directly compare against the actual state-of-the-art methods rather than relying on reported numbers. The paper doesn't address whether the improvements are statistically significant or if they might be due to dataset differences.

3. **Implementation Details for Reorganization**: The dynamic threshold calculation (AWES - AWPS*AWES) for the reorganization step is described but lacks sufficient justification. The paper claims "we do not decide the threshold empirically" but doesn't explain why this specific formula was chosen or how it was derived. The formula appears arbitrary without theoretical backing or empirical validation of its superiority over other thresholding methods.

4. **Lack of Scalability Analysis**: The paper makes no mention of computational complexity or scalability. For large topic models with many topics and words per topic, the SVD-based methods could become computationally expensive. The paper doesn't address whether the method can be scaled to large corpora or topic models with hundreds of topics, which would be critical for real-world applications.

5. **Inadequate Ablation Study**: The paper doesn't provide a clear ablation study to isolate the contribution of the reorganization step. It's difficult to determine how much TBuckets-SVD-Reorg improves over TBuckets-SVD, which is already a strong method. The text classification results (Table 3) don't clearly attribute the improvement to the coherence measure versus the reorganization step.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "TBuckets-SVD-Reorg" outperforms state-of-the-art, but the improvement on Genomics is minimal (0.691 vs. 0.773). Why does the method work well on some datasets but not others? What characteristics of the datasets (e.g., topic diversity, word embeddings) might explain this variation?

2. The dynamic threshold formula (AWES - AWPS*AWES) appears to be a heuristic without theoretical justification. Can you provide more insight into how this formula was derived and why it's better than alternatives (e.g., fixed thresholds, different combinations)?

3. How does the computational complexity of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg compare to the state-of-the-art coherence measures? For a topic model with 100 topics and 10 words per topic, what would be the expected runtime for your method versus, say, CUCI with NPMI?

4. The text classification experiment shows that using high-coherence topics improves performance, but it's not clear how much time or effort the human annotators save by only labeling high-quality topics. Could you provide more details about the practical benefits for real-world applications?

5. The paper mentions that the SVD-based techniques are "completely parameter free," but the TBuckets-Clustering method requires a "maximum distance threshold" for agglomerative clustering. How does this parameter affect performance, and why is it acceptable to have this parameter while the SVD-based methods are considered parameter-free?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper presents an interesting and novel approach to topic coherence measurement with practical advantages due to its parameter-free nature. The empirical results are strong, particularly for the SVD-based methods, and the text classification application adds practical relevance. However, the lack of theoretical foundation, insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art implementations, and inadequate explanation of the reorganization step are significant weaknesses that prevent this from being a strong contribution to the field.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with major revisions. The authors should address the theoretical justification for their approach, provide direct comparisons with state-of-the-art implementations, clarify the reorganization step, and include scalability analysis. With these improvements, the paper could make a meaningful contribution to the topic modeling literature.
