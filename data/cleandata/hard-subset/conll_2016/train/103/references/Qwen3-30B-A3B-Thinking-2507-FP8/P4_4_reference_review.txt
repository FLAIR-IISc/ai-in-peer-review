

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper introduces TBuckets, a method for measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core insight is that a single large bucket indicates high coherence, while multiple medium-sized buckets indicate low coherence. The authors propose three techniques: TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering of word embeddings), TBuckets-SVD (using SVD to identify sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (an enhanced SVD approach with reorganization).

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. **TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique**: A parameter-free method for topic coherence measurement that outperforms state-of-the-art on two of three standard datasets (NYT and 20NG) without requiring any parameter tuning, addressing a significant practical limitation of existing methods.

2. **Reorganization mechanism**: A novel approach to improve bucket formation by moving words between buckets based on semantic similarity to the largest bucket, which addresses a key limitation in the basic SVD approach.

3. **Practical application demonstration**: The paper successfully demonstrates how TBuckets coherence scores can be used to improve weakly supervised text classification performance by selecting high-quality topics for human annotation.

## Strengths

1. **Parameter-free performance advantage**: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves state-of-the-art performance on two out of three standard datasets (NYT and 20NG) without requiring any parameter tuning, while the best-performing methods from Roder et al. (2015) require extensive parameter tuning. This is a significant practical contribution, as demonstrated in Table 1 where TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves Pearson's r of 0.837 (NYT) and 0.863 (20NG) compared to Roder's best of 0.806 and 0.859 respectively.

2. **Novel coherence measurement approach**: The TBuckets concept of grouping words into thematic buckets and using the size/composition of the largest bucket as a coherence measure represents a distinct perspective from existing pairwise similarity-based approaches (e.g., PMI, NPMI). The examples in Section 3 clearly illustrate how the bucketing process aligns with human intuition about topic coherence.

3. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) with human-annotated coherence scores using the standard Pearson's correlation metric. The results are clearly presented and demonstrate consistent performance improvements over state-of-the-art methods on two datasets.

4. **Demonstrated utility in text classification**: The application to text classification (Section 4.3) shows that using TBuckets to select high-quality topics for human annotation leads to significant performance improvements in weakly supervised text classification (e.g., 68.47% vs 66.98% for PC vs MAC in Table 3), providing strong evidence of practical value.

5. **Clear methodology**: The paper clearly explains the TBuckets approach with detailed examples, mathematical formulations, and step-by-step descriptions of the three techniques. The reorganization mechanism in Section 3.1 is particularly well-explained with clear rationale and examples.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient novelty justification**: The paper claims novelty but doesn't sufficiently distinguish TBuckets from existing approaches. The concept of grouping words into thematic groups for coherence measurement bears similarities to previous work (e.g., the "topic coherence" concept in Newman et al. 2010b, and the bucketing approach in Roder et al. 2015). The related work section (Section 5) doesn't provide a detailed comparative analysis of how TBuckets fundamentally differs from previous methods.

2. **Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper only compares with Roder et al. (2015), but the field has advanced since 2015. More recent methods (e.g., Lau et al. 2014, which is cited but not compared, and newer approaches published in the last 2-3 years) should have been included to properly establish the contribution. The paper's claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art" is weakened by this limited comparison.

3. **Misleading claim about "completely parameter-free"**: The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on topic-specific values (AWPS and AWES). This is not truly parameter-free, as it involves a threshold calculation that depends on topic characteristics, contradicting the paper's claim.

4. **Inadequate analysis of Genomics dataset performance**: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg performs worse than state-of-the-art on the Genomics dataset (0.68 vs 0.773 in Table 1). The paper provides no analysis of why this occurs or how the approach might be adapted for domains like Genomics, which limits the perceived generalizability of the method.

5. **Lack of ablation study**: The paper doesn't provide an ablation study to understand the contribution of each component (e.g., how much does the reorganization step contribute to the performance improvement?). This makes it difficult to assess the relative importance of the different aspects of the TBuckets approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold calculated from topic-specific values (AWPS and AWES). How is this different from having a parameter that needs to be set (even if it's derived from the data), and why is this considered "parameter free"?

2. The paper shows that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg performs worse on the Genomics dataset than on the other two. What are the specific characteristics of the Genomics topics that might make them particularly challenging for the TBuckets approach, and how could the method be adapted to handle such topics better?

3. In Table 3, the performance improvement with TBuckets is modest (e.g., 68.47% vs 66.98% for PC vs MAC). How significant is this improvement in practical terms, and are there additional metrics or analyses that could better demonstrate the utility of TBuckets for text classification?

4. The paper only compares with Roder et al. (2015). Could the authors provide a comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., from 2016-2018) to better establish the current standing of their approach?
