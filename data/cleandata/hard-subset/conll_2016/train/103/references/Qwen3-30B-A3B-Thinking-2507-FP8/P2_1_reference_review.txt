

# Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary
This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic coherence in topic modeling. The core idea is to group topic words into thematic "buckets" (logical clusters) and use properties of these buckets (particularly the size and composition of the largest bucket) as a coherence measure. The authors propose three techniques for creating these buckets: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step for the SVD-based approach). The paper evaluates these techniques on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) using Pearson's r correlation with human-annotated coherence scores, demonstrating that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on two of the three datasets while requiring no parameter tuning.

## Main Contributions
1. **Novel topic coherence framework based on word bucketing**: The paper introduces a fundamentally new perspective on topic coherence measurement by grouping words into thematic buckets, with coherence determined by the distribution of words across these buckets (particularly the size of the largest coherent bucket).
2. **Parameter-free topic coherence measurement**: The authors demonstrate that their SVD-based techniques (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) require absolutely no parameter tuning, which is a significant practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods that require substantial parameter tuning.
3. **Demonstrated effectiveness in both coherence measurement and text classification**: The paper not only shows improved coherence measurement but also demonstrates the practical utility of their approach in weakly supervised text classification, where using high-quality topics (as measured by TBuckets) leads to better classification performance.

## Strengths

1. **Intuitive and well-motivated approach**: The TBuckets concept is clearly explained with compelling examples that align with how humans naturally assess topic coherence. The paper effectively demonstrates through examples that the bucket distribution (single large bucket vs. multiple medium-sized buckets) correlates strongly with human perception of topic coherence.

2. **Parameter-free implementation**: The claim that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are completely parameter-free is a significant strength. This is a major practical advantage over existing methods that require careful tuning of multiple parameters (e.g., Newman et al., 2010b; Roder et al., 2015). The paper's emphasis on this advantage is well-justified by the experimental results showing that their parameter-free methods outperform parameter-tuned state-of-the-art approaches.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The evaluation is thorough and well-designed, using the standard Pearson's r correlation metric with human annotations as in Roder et al. (2015). The results clearly show that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on two of the three datasets (NYT and 20NG), while TBuckets-Clustering performs comparably on Genomics. The results are presented clearly with appropriate tables and figures.

4. **Practical application validation**: The text classification experiment provides compelling evidence of the practical utility of the approach. The paper demonstrates that using TBuckets to select high-quality topics for human annotation leads to better classification performance (3 out of 4 datasets) and that the difference between high-quality and low-quality topics is substantial (e.g., 4.9% improvement for PC vs. MAC).

5. **Clear organization and presentation**: The paper is well-structured with clear explanations of the methodology, thorough experimental evaluation, and appropriate comparison with prior work. The examples provided for the bucketing process are particularly helpful for understanding the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient differentiation from prior work**: While the bucketing approach is novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish it from related work that uses word embeddings for topic coherence (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson, 2013; Lau et al., 2014). The paper claims TBuckets is novel but doesn't clearly articulate why the bucketing approach is fundamentally different from existing methods that use word embeddings to measure coherence. The connection to prior work is underdeveloped.

2. **Incomplete comparison with state-of-the-art**: The paper only compares with the best results from Roder et al. (2015), but doesn't compare with the most recent and relevant work in topic coherence measurement. For example, it doesn't compare with the more recent work by Lau et al. (2014) that introduced NPMI-based coherence measures, which have become standard in the field. This limits the paper's claim of being state-of-the-art.

3. **Insufficient theoretical justification**: While the paper demonstrates that TBuckets works well empirically, it lacks a strong theoretical justification for why the size of the largest bucket correlates with human judgments of coherence. The paper states this correlation is intuitive but doesn't provide deeper analysis or explanation for why this metric works better than alternatives.

4. **Limited dataset diversity**: The evaluation is conducted on only three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics), which are all text corpora but represent different domains. The paper doesn't discuss whether the approach generalizes to other types of text (e.g., social media, medical texts) or whether the performance varies significantly across different domain types.

5. **Questionable "parameter-free" claim**: While the paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," the description of the reorganization threshold (AWES - AWPS*AWES) appears to involve parameters that are computed from the data. The paper doesn't clarify whether this is truly parameter-free or just parameter-free in the sense that no user-specified parameters are required.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free." However, the threshold calculation for reorganization involves AWES and AWPS, which are computed from the topic data. Can you clarify whether this is truly parameter-free, and if so, how the threshold calculation avoids requiring any parameters?

2. The paper shows that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on NYT and 20NG but performs worse than state-of-the-art on Genomics. Can you provide insights into why the approach might not generalize as well to Genomics topics, which may have different characteristics?

3. How does the TBuckets approach handle topics that are inherently multi-themed (e.g., a topic about both "technology" and "business")? The paper seems to assume that good topics should have a single coherent theme, but many real-world topics might naturally cover multiple related themes.

4. The paper doesn't compare with the most recent topic coherence measures (e.g., those from Lau et al., 2014, or more recent work). Have you considered comparing with these more recent approaches, and if so, why weren't they included in the comparison?

5. The paper states that the largest bucket is the most important for coherence measurement. However, some topics might have multiple equally important themes. How would your approach handle such cases, and what would the bucket distribution look like for these topics?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to topic coherence measurement with strong empirical results. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques is a significant practical advantage that is well-supported by the experimental results. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of topic modeling evaluation.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by better differentiation from prior work, more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and deeper analysis of why the bucketing approach works well. The weaknesses, particularly regarding the novelty claim and limited comparison, prevent this paper from being a strong candidate for acceptance at CoNLL.

**Rating: Accept with Minor Revision** (if the authors can address the weaknesses, particularly the comparison with state-of-the-art and clarification on the parameter-free claim)
