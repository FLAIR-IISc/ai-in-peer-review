

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary
This paper proposes "TBuckets," a novel approach to measure topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core idea is that coherent topics should have a single dominant bucket containing most words, while incoherent topics will have multiple medium-sized buckets. The authors propose three techniques: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step for the SVD-based approach). They evaluate their techniques on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) using Pearson's correlation with human-annotated coherence scores, claiming superior performance to state-of-the-art methods, particularly for their SVD-based techniques which require no parameter tuning.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A novel approach to topic coherence measurement based on word bucketing
2. Three techniques for creating word buckets (Clustering, SVD, and SVD with reorganization)
3. Demonstration of parameter-free SVD-based techniques

## Strengths
1. **Intuitive core concept**: The idea that coherent topics should have a single dominant thematic bucket aligns well with human perception of topic coherence, as demonstrated by the clear examples (weather vs. Afghanistan/Wikileaks topics).

2. **Sound evaluation methodology**: The use of standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) and standard metrics (Pearson's r correlation with human judgments) provides a valid basis for comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

3. **Practical application demonstration**: The application of TBuckets to text classification shows tangible utility, with results demonstrating improved classification performance when using high-quality topics identified by TBuckets.

## Weaknesses
I must recommend rejection due to the following substantial flaws that undermine the paper's technical validity:

1. **Critical mathematical error in SVD description**: This is the most serious flaw. The paper states: "We use the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers to attach words to." However, for a topic with n words (each represented by d-dimensional embeddings), the SVD of the n × d matrix A produces a V matrix of size d × d (not n × d). Therefore, V contains only d eigenvectors, not n. The paper's description implies they use n eigenvectors, which is impossible if n > d (which is almost always true, since d is typically 300). This mathematical error renders the entire TBuckets-SVD technique as described impossible to implement. For example, if they have 10 words in a topic (n=10) and use 300-dimensional word embeddings (d=300), V is 300×300, so there are 300 eigenvectors, not 10. The paper incorrectly claims they use "the first n eigenvectors" when they can only use up to d eigenvectors.

2. **Ambiguous reorganization threshold formula**: The paper states "The threshold is computed as AWES − AWPSAWES." This mathematical expression is completely ambiguous. It could mean:
   - AWES - (AWPS × AWES)
   - (AWES - AWPS) / AWES
   - AWES - (AWPS × AWES) [which simplifies to AWES(1 - AWPS)]
   - Or something else entirely.

   Without clarification, it is impossible to reproduce the reorganization step, which is a critical component of the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique. The authors claim to have removed parameter tuning, but this ambiguous formula effectively introduces an unexplained parameter.

3. **Inconsistent citation formatting throughout**: The paper exhibits numerous inconsistent citation formats:
   - "Blei et al., 2003" vs. "Blei, 2012"
   - "David M. Blei, 2007" vs. "Blei, 2007"
   - "Roder et al. (2015)" vs. "Röder et al., 2015" (note the umlaut)
   - In the reference list, inconsistent use of "et al." and year placement (e.g., "Blei et al\.,? 2003" vs. "Blei, 2012")

   This level of inconsistency is unacceptable in a technical paper, especially given the paper's claim to be rigorous.

4. **Missing details about human evaluation**: The paper states that each topic is associated with a human coherence score between 1 and 3, but fails to explain how these scores were obtained. The number of human annotators, inter-annotator agreement, and methodology for obtaining the scores are all missing. Without this information, it's impossible to assess the reliability of the evaluation.

5. **Inconsistent terminology**: The paper uses "topic quality" and "topic coherence" interchangeably, despite these being distinct concepts in the literature. Topic quality might encompass coherence, but also other aspects like coverage, diversity, and relevance. This ambiguity weakens the paper's claims.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please clarify the mathematical error in the SVD description. How many eigenvectors are actually used in TBuckets-SVD, and how is this number determined (is it min(n,d) or something else)?

2. Please provide a precise mathematical definition for the reorganization threshold formula "AWES − AWPSAWES" that allows for exact reproduction of the method.

3. Please explain how the human coherence scores were obtained (number of annotators, annotation process, inter-annotator agreement).

4. Why does the paper claim their SVD-based techniques are "completely parameter free" when the SVD technique requires a dimensionality parameter (d) that is fixed by the word embeddings, and the reorganization step has an ambiguous threshold?

## Recommendation
**Reject**

The paper presents an interesting conceptual approach to topic coherence measurement, but contains fundamental technical errors that undermine the entire methodology. The critical mathematical error in the SVD description (using n eigenvectors when only d exist) makes the TBuckets-SVD technique impossible to implement as described. Combined with the ambiguous threshold formula, these issues make the paper's results unreliable and the method impossible to reproduce. The inconsistent citation formatting and missing details about human evaluation further indicate a lack of rigor that is unacceptable for a technical paper. While the core idea has potential, the technical flaws are so severe that they cannot be overlooked. The authors should address these fundamental issues before considering publication.
