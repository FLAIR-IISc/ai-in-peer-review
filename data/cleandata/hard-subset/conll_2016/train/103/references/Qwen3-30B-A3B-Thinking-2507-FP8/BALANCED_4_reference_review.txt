

# Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic quality in topic models by grouping topic words into thematic buckets (thematic groups). The core idea is that a topic with a single dominant bucket (representing a coherent theme) is more coherent than a topic with multiple medium-sized buckets. The authors present three techniques: (1) TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering of word embeddings), (2) TBuckets-SVD (using SVD to discover orthogonal sub-themes), and (3) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step for the SVD-based technique).

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. **Novel TBuckets Conceptual Approach**: A fresh perspective on topic coherence that aligns with human assessment of topic quality through thematic grouping of words.
2. **Parameter-Free Techniques**: TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning, a significant practical advantage over existing methods that require careful parameter selection.
3. **Demonstrated Utility in Text Classification**: The paper shows how the coherence measure can be practically applied to improve weakly supervised text classification by filtering low-quality topics before human annotation.

## Strengths

1. **Conceptually Strong and Intuitive Approach**: The TBuckets idea is well-motivated and directly mirrors how humans assess topic coherence. The paper provides excellent examples (e.g., the weather topic vs. the Afghanistan/Wikileaks topic) that clearly illustrate how bucket distribution correlates with human intuition about coherence. This conceptual novelty is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Parameter-Free Nature of Key Techniques**: The fact that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning is a major practical advantage over existing methods (e.g., Roder et al., 2015), which require extensive parameter tuning. This makes the approach more accessible to practitioners who may lack expertise in topic model tuning. The paper demonstrates that these parameter-free techniques outperform state-of-the-art methods on two out of three datasets (NYT and 20NG), which is compelling evidence of their value.

3. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The evaluation on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) with human-annotated coherence scores is thorough and well-executed. The results show that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves the highest Pearson's r correlation (0.837 on NYT, 0.863 on 20NG) compared to state-of-the-art methods. The ablation study (Table 1) clearly demonstrates the incremental improvements from the different techniques.

4. **Practical Application Demonstration**: The text classification experiment (Table 3) provides strong evidence of the real-world utility of the coherence measure. The results show that using TBuckets to filter topics before human annotation significantly improves classification performance (e.g., 68.47% vs. 66.98% for PC vs. MAC), especially on difficult datasets. This bridges the gap between theoretical coherence measurement and practical application.

5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the methods and thorough experimental evaluation. The examples (Tables 1 and 2) effectively illustrate the bucketing process and the resulting coherence scores.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015), but there have been significant developments in topic coherence measurement since then. For example, Lau et al. (2014) proposed CUCI with NPMI, and Zhao et al. (2015) introduced a new coherence measure. The authors should compare their method with these more recent approaches to establish their standing in the current literature.

2. **Insufficient Justification for Reorganization Step**: The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique shows significant improvement over TBuckets-SVD, but the paper doesn't provide a strong theoretical justification for the reorganization step. The threshold (AWES - AWPS*AWES) appears somewhat ad hoc, and the authors don't explain why this specific formulation is optimal or how it generalizes beyond the datasets tested. A more principled approach to the reorganization step would strengthen the paper.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Analysis**: While the empirical results are strong, the paper lacks theoretical analysis of why the TBuckets approach works well. For instance, it would be valuable to explore the relationship between the number of buckets and topic coherence, or how the dimensionality of word embeddings affects the bucketing process. The paper would benefit from more theoretical grounding.

4. **Limited Generalization to Other Domains and Models**: The evaluation is limited to three datasets and LDA-based topic models. It's unclear how well the approach would generalize to other topic models (e.g., dynamic topic models, correlated topic models, neural topic models) or to domains with specialized vocabulary (e.g., biomedical, legal). The paper should address how the method might need adaptation for different domains.

5. **Insufficient Ablation Study**: While the paper presents results for different techniques, it lacks a thorough ablation study to understand the contribution of each component. For example, it's not clear how much of the improvement in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg comes from the reorganization step versus the SVD-based bucketing itself. A more detailed ablation would strengthen the claims about the method's components.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015), but there have been several topic coherence measures proposed since then (e.g., Lau et al., 2014; Zhao et al., 2015; etc.). How do your methods compare with these more recent approaches?

2. The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg appears critical to the method's success. Can you provide a more theoretical justification for the specific threshold you use (AWES - AWPS*AWES), rather than relying solely on empirical observations?

3. The paper focuses on LDA-based topic models. How would your approach generalize to other topic models, such as dynamic topic models or neural topic models, which are becoming increasingly popular?

4. The evaluation is limited to three datasets. How do you expect your method to perform on domains with specialized vocabulary (e.g., biomedical, legal) where general-purpose word embeddings might not capture domain-specific semantics as well?

5. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free." However, the word embeddings used are pre-trained on Wikipedia and Gigaword. How would the method perform with different word embedding sources or domain-specific embeddings?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to topic coherence measurement that aligns with human intuition and provides practical benefits for text classification. The parameter-free nature of the key techniques is a significant advantage over existing methods. The empirical results are strong, and the practical application to text classification is well-executed.

However, the limited comparison with more recent work, insufficient theoretical justification for the reorganization step, and lack of generalization analysis to other domains and models represent significant weaknesses. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the paper.

**Decision: Weak Accept**

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of topic modeling evaluation with a novel and practical approach. While the current version has some weaknesses, the core idea is strong and the empirical results are compelling. With the suggested revisions (particularly addressing the comparison with recent work and providing more theoretical justification for the reorganization step), this paper would be an excellent fit for CONLL.
