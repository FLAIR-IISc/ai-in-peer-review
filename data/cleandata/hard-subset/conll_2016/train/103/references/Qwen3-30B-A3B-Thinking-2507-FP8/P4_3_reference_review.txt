

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core insight is that coherent topics should form a single dominant theme (large bucket) rather than multiple distinct themes (multiple medium-sized buckets). The authors propose three techniques for creating these buckets: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering of word embeddings), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify orthogonal dimensions), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (adding a reorganization step to improve bucket formation).

The main contributions of this work are:

1. **TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique**: A parameter-free coherence measure that outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two of three datasets (NYT and 20NG) with higher Pearson's correlation with human judgments. The key novelty lies in the reorganization step that improves bucket formation without requiring parameter tuning.

2. **TBuckets-SVD technique**: A novel application of SVD to topic coherence measurement that identifies orthogonal dimensions (sub-themes) in topic words, enabling a parameter-free approach to topic coherence assessment.

3. **Demonstrated utility for weakly supervised text classification**: The paper shows that using TBuckets to select high-quality topics for human annotation leads to significantly better text classification performance, particularly in challenging domains like PC vs MAC.

## Strengths

1. **Parameter-free approach with strong empirical results**: The paper presents a compelling case for the parameter-free nature of TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, which outperforms state-of-the-art methods that require significant parameter tuning. The results in Table 1 demonstrate clear improvements over Roder et al. (2015) on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.863 vs 0.859) with Pearson's r correlation, while requiring no parameter tuning. This is a significant practical advantage for real-world applications.

2. **Strong application demonstration**: The text classification experiments (Table 3) provide compelling evidence of the practical utility of the coherence measure. The 1.5-5.0% improvement in classification accuracy when using TBuckets to select high-quality topics (S2 vs S1) demonstrates tangible value beyond just coherence measurement. The dramatic difference in performance between highest and lowest coherence topic sets for PC vs MAC (68.47% vs 67.96%) particularly highlights the utility of the method.

3. **Clear conceptual framework**: The paper effectively illustrates the core idea of "bucketing" with concrete examples (e.g., the weather topic forming a single large bucket versus the Afghanistan/Wikileaks topic forming multiple buckets). This makes the approach intuitive and easy to understand, which is valuable for both researchers and practitioners.

4. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper evaluates on three diverse datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) and uses the same evaluation scheme as Roder et al. (2015), enabling direct comparison. The inclusion of both coherence measurement and application to text classification strengthens the contribution beyond just a new metric.

5. **Technical soundness of the approach**: The mathematical formulation of the reorganization step (Section 3.1) is well-justified, with clear definitions of the four parameters (NBPW, NBPE, LBPW, OBPW) and a dynamic threshold that avoids manual parameter tuning. The SVD-based approach (Section 3.2) is technically sound and appropriately applied to the problem.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to existing work**: While the bucketing concept is novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from existing semantic clustering methods (e.g., Aletras and Stevenson, 2013). The core idea of using semantic similarity to group words is not entirely new, and the paper doesn't adequately explain how TBuckets differs from or improves upon this prior work. This weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of ablation study for TBuckets-SVD-Reorg**: The paper doesn't provide an ablation study to show the contribution of each component (SVD vs. reorganization) to the final performance. It's unclear how much the reorganization step specifically contributes to the improved performance over TBuckets-SVD alone. Without this, it's difficult to assess the relative importance of the different components.

3. **Inconsistent claims about parameter-free nature**: The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter-free," but TBuckets-Clustering requires a Max Dist parameter (as shown in Figure 1 and Table 1). This inconsistency undermines the strong claim about parameter-free methods, as one of the three techniques does require parameter tuning.

4. **Limited analysis of failure cases**: The paper shows TBuckets-SVD-Reorg underperforms on Genomics (0.68 vs 0.773 for state-of-the-art), but doesn't analyze why this occurs. Understanding why the method fails on certain datasets would provide valuable insights and strengthen the paper's contribution.

5. **Inadequate comparison with recent work**: The paper compares only with Roder et al. (2015), but doesn't compare with more recent coherence measures published after 2015 (e.g., Lau et al., 2014; other follow-up studies). This limits the evaluation of the method's standing in the current state of the art.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter-free," but TBuckets-Clustering requires a Max Dist parameter. How do you reconcile this inconsistency in your claims about parameter-free methods?

2. How does the TBuckets approach specifically differ from the semantic clustering approach used in Aletras and Stevenson (2013), which also uses word embeddings to group topic words based on semantic similarity?

3. Could you provide an ablation study showing the contribution of the reorganization step to TBuckets-SVD-Reorg's performance, compared to just TBuckets-SVD?

4. Why does TBuckets-SVD-Reorg underperform on the Genomics dataset compared to the other two datasets? Is there a specific characteristic of Genomics topics that makes them more challenging for the bucketing approach?

5. The paper doesn't compare with more recent coherence measures beyond Roder et al. (2015). How does TBuckets-SVD-Reorg compare with methods proposed after 2015, such as those in Lau et al. (2014)?
