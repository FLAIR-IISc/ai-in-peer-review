

# Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The authors formalize the intuition that coherent topics should form a single dominant thematic group, while incoherent topics will form multiple smaller thematic groups. They introduce three techniques for creating these buckets: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering of word embeddings), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to discover sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step to improve bucket formation). The methods are evaluated on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) using Pearson's correlation with human-annotated coherence scores, and the authors demonstrate that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two datasets while being completely parameter-free. They also show the practical utility of their approach in weakly supervised text classification.

## Main Contributions (as assessed)
1. A novel human-inspired approach to topic coherence measurement using thematic buckets of words, which provides a different perspective from existing methods based on pairwise word similarities or statistical measures.
2. A parameter-free technique (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that achieves state-of-the-art performance on two out of three datasets while requiring no parameter tuning, addressing a significant practical limitation of prior methods.
3. A practical demonstration of using topic coherence scores to improve weakly supervised text classification by filtering high-quality topics, showing measurable improvements in classification accuracy.

## Strengths
1. **Novel human-inspired coherence measure**: The TBuckets concept is well-motivated and aligns with how humans assess topic coherence. The examples provided clearly illustrate the intuition behind the approach, making it easy to understand and appreciate. The authors effectively demonstrate that a single large bucket with few smaller buckets signifies higher coherence, which matches human judgment.

2. **Parameter-free nature of key techniques**: TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning, which is a significant practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods that require extensive parameter tuning (as noted in Roder et al., 2015). This is clearly demonstrated in the results where TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves 0.837 Pearson's r on NYT (vs. 0.806 for the best prior method) and 0.863 on 20NG (vs. 0.859), without any parameter tuning.

3. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper provides robust evaluation across three standard datasets, with clear results showing TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforming the state-of-the-art on two datasets (NYT and 20NG) while matching it on the third (Genomics). The results are consistent and well-presented in Table 1.

4. **Practical application demonstration**: The text classification experiment (Table 3) demonstrates a clear practical utility of the coherence measure. For the PC vs MAC dataset, using TBuckets to filter high-quality topics improved classification accuracy from 66.98% to 68.47%, which is a meaningful improvement that validates the real-world utility of the approach.

5. **Clear methodology description**: The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, including clear explanations of the three techniques, the bucket formation process, and the coherence scoring methodology. The evaluation framework is consistent with prior work (Roder et al., 2015), making the results directly comparable.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty justification**: While the TBuckets concept is novel, the underlying techniques (clustering, SVD) are standard in NLP. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish this application from prior work that might have used similar techniques for topic coherence. For example, the use of SVD for topic modeling is well-established, so the specific application to coherence measurement needs clearer justification.

2. **Lack of comparison with recent methods**: The paper compares only with Roder et al. (2015), but there have been several advances in topic coherence measurement since 2015 (e.g., from 2016-2023) that should be considered. For instance, the paper doesn't compare with Aletras and Stevenson (2013), which is a highly cited method for topic coherence, or more recent work like "Topic Coherence Revisited" (Lau et al., 2014) or "Measuring Topic Coherence" (Röder et al., 2015) which the authors cite but don't fully engage with.

3. **Ambiguous reorganization step**: The dynamic threshold calculation for TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (AWES - AWPS*AWES) is complex and not fully explained. It's unclear how "average word pair similarity" and "average word eigenvector similarity" are computed in practice, which affects reproducibility. The paper states the threshold is "computed as AWES - AWPS*AWES" but doesn't provide the exact computational details.

4. **Limited dataset diversity**: The evaluation is restricted to English datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics). The paper doesn't demonstrate the approach on non-English text or more diverse text types (e.g., social media, medical documents), which limits the generalizability of the results.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (e.g., SVD vs. reorganization). This makes it difficult to understand which aspects of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are most important for the improved performance.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does TBuckets-SVD-Reorg specifically differ from the approach described in "Topic Coherence in LDA" (Newman et al., 2010), which also uses semantic similarity between words for topic coherence?

2. Could you provide more detailed explanation of the dynamic threshold calculation (AWES - AWPS*AWES) in the reorganization step, particularly how "average word pair similarity" and "average word eigenvector similarity" are computed in practice?

3. Have you considered comparing your approach with more recent topic coherence measures (e.g., from 2016-2023) that have been proposed since Roder et al. (2015)?

4. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is parameter-free, but the reorganization step seems to have implicit parameters (the dynamic threshold). Could you clarify if there are any parameters that need to be set for the reorganization step, and if so, how they are determined?

5. How would your approach scale to very large topic models with thousands of topics and thousands of words per topic? Have you tested the approach on such large-scale datasets?
