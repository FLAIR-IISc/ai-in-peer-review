

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a novel approach called "TBuckets" for measuring topic coherence in topic models (e.g., LDA). The core idea is that coherent topics should have words that naturally group into a single dominant thematic bucket, while incoherent topics will have words distributed across multiple buckets. The authors develop three techniques for creating these buckets:
1. TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering of word embeddings)
2. TBuckets-SVD (using SVD to identify orthogonal sub-themes)
3. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (reorganizing buckets from TBuckets-SVD)

The primary contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. **The word bucket concept for topic coherence measurement**: A novel, intuitive approach that aligns with how humans assess topic coherence, demonstrated through clear examples.

2. **Parameter-free SVD-based techniques**: TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning, addressing a significant limitation of previous methods that required extensive hyperparameter tuning.

3. **Empirical validation showing state-of-the-art performance**: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms previous methods on 2 out of 3 datasets (NYT and 20NG) while being completely parameter-free.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and intuitive concept**: The "word bucket" approach is a fresh perspective on topic coherence that directly aligns with human cognitive processes for topic assessment. The examples provided (weather vs. Afghanistan topics) effectively demonstrate how the bucketing mechanism captures coherence differences. This is a strong conceptual contribution that makes the approach immediately understandable.

2. **Practical parameter-free implementation**: The paper's claim that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter-free" is a significant practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods that require careful parameter tuning. The authors provide evidence that their SVD-based techniques achieve superior results without any parameter adjustment, which is rare in this field and addresses a major pain point for practitioners.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves Pearson's r correlations of 0.837 (NYT) and 0.863 (20NG), outperforming the previous state-of-the-art (0.806 and 0.859 respectively). The results are statistically significant and well-presented with clear comparison tables. The fact that their method outperforms state-of-the-art on two datasets while requiring no parameter tuning is compelling evidence of its value.

4. **Demonstrated practical application**: The paper shows a concrete, valuable application of the coherence measure in weakly supervised text classification. By using TBuckets to select high-quality topics for human labeling, they achieve improved classification accuracy (e.g., 68.47% vs. 66.98% for PC vs. MAC). This demonstrates the real-world utility of their coherence measure beyond just theoretical evaluation.

5. **Clear methodology and presentation**: The paper provides sufficient mathematical detail, clear examples, and well-structured explanations of the three techniques. The evaluation methodology is standard and well-executed, making the results easy to understand and verify.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with recent methods**: The paper compares only with Roder et al. (2015), which is a 2015 paper. The field has advanced significantly since then, with more recent methods (e.g., Lau et al. 2014, Bouma 2019, or methods in ACL/EMNLP 2020-2023) that should be included for a proper state-of-the-art comparison. Without this, the claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art" is questionable.

2. **Insufficient ablation study**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to understand the contribution of each component in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg. Specifically, it doesn't quantify how much the reorganization step improves performance over the basic TBuckets-SVD approach. The paper states "reorganization step was desirable" but doesn't provide evidence of the magnitude of improvement.

3. **Questionable "parameter-free" claim**: While the authors claim the SVD-based techniques are "completely parameter-free," the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold that depends on calculated values (AWES and AWPS). This threshold calculation is not truly parameter-free as it relies on topic-specific computations that could potentially be seen as implicit parameters. The paper doesn't adequately explain why this is considered parameter-free.

4. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper provides empirical evidence but lacks theoretical justification for why the largest bucket size or the reorganization step correlates with human coherence judgments. Without this, it's unclear whether the approach is fundamentally sound or if it's simply coincidentally performing well on the specific datasets used.

5. **Limited generalizability evidence**: The evaluation is limited to three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics). The paper doesn't demonstrate how the approach performs on other types of text (e.g., social media, medical texts) or non-English languages, which would strengthen the claim that the method is broadly applicable.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a comparison with more recent topic coherence measures published after Roder et al. (2015), such as those in the last 3 years (e.g., from ACL, EMNLP, or NAACL)? This would better establish your method's position in the current state-of-the-art.

2. Regarding the "parameter-free" claim for TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, could you clarify how the dynamic threshold (AWES - AWPS*AWES) is truly parameter-free? Is there a risk that this threshold calculation might require adjustment for different types of text or domains?

3. In the text classification application, you observed improved performance when using high-coherence topics for labeling. Could you provide more insight into why higher coherence topics lead to better classification performance? Is it because they are more discriminative, or is there another underlying reason?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend **weak acceptance** for this paper. The core idea of word buckets for topic coherence is novel and intuitive, and the parameter-free SVD-based techniques represent a significant practical advantage. The empirical results are strong on two of the three datasets, and the application to text classification demonstrates clear utility.

However, the paper has significant weaknesses that need to be addressed before it can be considered for acceptance. The limited comparison with recent methods, questionable parameter-free claim, and lack of theoretical justification are substantial concerns. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the paper.

## Suggestions for Improvement

1. Add a comparison with at least 2-3 recent topic coherence measures (2019-2023) to properly establish the state-of-the-art.

2. Conduct a thorough ablation study to quantify the contribution of each component in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (e.g., TBuckets-SVD vs. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, with and without reorganization).

3. Clarify the "parameter-free" claim by either removing the dynamic threshold (if possible) or providing a more rigorous justification for why it's considered parameter-free.

4. Add a theoretical discussion explaining why the largest bucket size or the reorganization step should correlate with human coherence judgments.

5. Extend the evaluation to at least one additional dataset (e.g., a non-English corpus or a different domain like medical texts) to demonstrate broader applicability.

With these improvements, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field of topic modeling evaluation. As it stands, it has good ideas but requires more comprehensive validation to be accepted at a top conference.
