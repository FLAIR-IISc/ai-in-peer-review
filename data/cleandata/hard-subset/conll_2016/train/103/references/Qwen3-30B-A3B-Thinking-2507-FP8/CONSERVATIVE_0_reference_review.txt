

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary
This paper proposes a novel approach called "TBuckets" for measuring topic coherence in topic models. The core idea is to group topic words into thematic buckets and use properties of these buckets (particularly the size of the largest bucket) to compute a coherence score. The authors present three techniques for creating these buckets: clustering-based (TBuckets-Clustering), SVD-based (TBuckets-SVD), and SVD with reorganization (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). They evaluate these techniques on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) against human-annotated coherence scores, claiming their best technique (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) outperforms state-of-the-art on two of the three datasets. They also demonstrate an application of their approach in text classification, showing that using high-coherence topics for human annotation leads to better classification performance.

## Main Contributions
1. A method for topic coherence measurement based on word grouping into thematic buckets (TBuckets)
2. Three techniques for bucket creation (clustering, SVD, SVD with reorganization)
3. Demonstration that their SVD-based techniques are parameter-free compared to state-of-the-art methods requiring multiple parameter tunings

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and motivation:** The paper effectively articulates why topic coherence is important and provides compelling examples of coherent versus non-coherent topics. The connection between human interpretation of topics and the bucketing concept is well-motivated and understandable.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation framework:** The evaluation on three different datasets against human-annotated coherence scores is appropriate and follows standard practice in the field. The comparison with Roder et al. (2015) as the state-of-the-art baseline is reasonable and provides a clear benchmark.

3. **Demonstrated practical utility:** The application to text classification showing improved performance when using high-coherence topics for human annotation is a strong practical contribution that goes beyond just measuring coherence. This demonstrates real-world value beyond theoretical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in core concept:** The fundamental idea of grouping topic words into coherent thematic groups is not novel. Similar approaches have been explored in existing coherence measures (e.g., CUSheffield uses grouping via centroid similarity, and clustering-based approaches have been used in other coherence measures). The paper fails to clearly differentiate TBuckets from these existing methods or establish how it fundamentally advances beyond them.

2. **Inadequate comparison with recent state-of-the-art:** The paper only compares with the results reported in Roder et al. (2015), which is now several years old. The field has advanced since then, and more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., those by Zhao et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020) should have been included for comparison to properly establish the novelty and significance of their approach.

3. **Questionable "parameter-free" claim:** The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter-free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on topic-specific calculations (AWES - AWPS*AWES). This is not truly parameter-free as the threshold calculation depends on the topic itself, which undermines the claim of being parameter-free. The paper should either clarify this or remove the claim.

4. **Lack of ablation studies:** The paper does not provide sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate the contribution of each component of their method. In particular, it's unclear how much the reorganization step contributes to the performance gain over TBuckets-SVD. The paper should have shown how performance degrades when the reorganization step is removed.

5. **No failure case analysis:** The paper presents strong results on two datasets but fails to analyze where TBuckets performs poorly. It's important to understand the limitations of the approach. The paper should have included examples where TBuckets fails to outperform state-of-the-art and why, to provide a more balanced evaluation.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does TBuckets fundamentally differ from existing coherence measures like CUSheffield or other clustering-based approaches? The paper doesn't provide a clear differentiation.

2. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter-free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold (AWES - AWPS*AWES) that depends on topic-specific calculations. Can you clarify how this is truly parameter-free?

3. Can you provide an ablation study showing the performance difference between TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg? How much of the improvement comes from the reorganization step?

4. Have you compared your approach with more recent state-of-the-art methods beyond those reported in Roder et al. (2015)? If not, why not?

5. What are the computational complexities of your approaches compared to existing methods? How does the computational cost scale with topic size and number of topics?

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a practical approach to topic coherence measurement with demonstrated utility in text classification, the lack of clear novelty, inadequate comparison with recent state-of-the-art, and questionable "parameter-free" claim significantly undermine its contribution. The field of topic coherence measurement has seen substantial development since Roder et al. (2015), and the paper would need to demonstrate clear advancement over more recent work to be considered significant. The core idea of grouping topic words into thematic buckets is not sufficiently novel, and the paper fails to establish how TBuckets fundamentally advances beyond existing methods. I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form without significant revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
