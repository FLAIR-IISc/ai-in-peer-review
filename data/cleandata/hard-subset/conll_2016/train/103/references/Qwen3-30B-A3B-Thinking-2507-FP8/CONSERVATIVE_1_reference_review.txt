

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a method called "TBuckets" for measuring topic coherence in topic modeling by grouping topic words into thematic "buckets" based on semantic similarity. The authors claim three techniques for creating these buckets: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to discover sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (reorganizing buckets from TBuckets-SVD). They claim their method outperforms state-of-the-art coherence measures on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) and that the SVD-based techniques are completely parameter-free.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Evaluation of a coherence measure based on semantic grouping of topic words** - This is the most concrete contribution, with thorough empirical validation on standard datasets.
2. **A reorganization step for SVD-based bucketing** - The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique is presented as an improvement over the basic SVD approach.
3. **Demonstration of utility for text classification** - Showing that using coherence scores to select high-quality topics improves weakly supervised text classification performance.

## Strengths

1. **Rigorous empirical evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation on three standard datasets using Pearson's r correlation with human judgments, following the evaluation protocol established by Roder et al. (2015). The results are clearly presented in Tables 1 and 2, with sufficient detail to understand the performance differences. The authors correctly benchmark against the state-of-the-art, making their claims of outperformance credible.

2. **Clear demonstration of practical utility**: The text classification experiments show a reasonable application of the coherence measure. The results (Table 3) demonstrate that selecting high-quality topics based on TBuckets-SVD-Reorg leads to better classification performance (68.47% vs 66.98% for PC vs MAC) in most cases, with the difference being particularly significant for difficult classification tasks like PC vs MAC. This provides a compelling real-world application of the coherence measure.

3. **Good presentation of the method**: The paper provides clear examples of how the bucketing procedure works for both coherent and non-coherent topics (Section 3.1). The explanation of the bucket formation process is intuitive and well-illustrated, making it easy for readers to understand the core idea.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty and insufficient differentiation from existing work**: The core idea of grouping topic words based on semantic similarity is not novel. The paper's approach bears strong resemblance to existing coherence measures like CUSheffield (Aletras and Stevenson, 2013), which also uses semantic similarity between words to measure topic coherence. The authors fail to adequately differentiate their method from these existing approaches or provide a compelling theoretical justification for why their specific implementation would outperform them. This is a significant weakness given the paper's claim of novelty.

2. **Misleading "parameter-free" claim**: The paper claims that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but this is misleading. The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg uses a dynamic threshold that is computed based on topic-specific properties (AWPS and AWES). This is effectively a parameter, albeit one that's automatically calculated. The paper doesn't explain why this is fundamentally different from the parameter tuning required by other methods (e.g., window size for PMI calculations), which is a key part of the authors' claim of superiority.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification and ablation studies**: The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation for why the bucketing approach should work better than existing semantic similarity-based methods. There are no ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of each component of the approach (e.g., the reorganization step). Without these, the paper's claims about the superiority of their method remain empirically supported but theoretically unsupported.

4. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper fails to address important limitations of the approach, such as:
   - How the method handles topics with very few words (the paper uses 10-word topics, but many applications use more)
   - How it performs on topics with uncommon or domain-specific vocabulary
   - Whether the method scales well with topic size (more than 10 words)
   - How it compares to methods that use domain-specific knowledge (e.g., WordNet hierarchies as in Musat et al., 2011)

5. **Marginal gains in practical application**: The text classification improvements are modest (e.g., 1.49% increase for PC vs MAC), and the paper doesn't provide sufficient explanation for why these gains occur. This weakens the practical significance of the contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter-free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on topic-specific properties. How is this fundamentally different from the parameter tuning required by existing methods (e.g., window size for PMI calculations), and why should this be considered a significant advantage?

2. The paper states that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms TBuckets-SVD on NYT and 20NG but not on Genomics. What specific characteristics of the Genomics dataset (or topics within it) prevent the reorganization step from being beneficial, and how does this impact the generalizability of the approach?

3. The paper's approach bears strong resemblance to CUSheffield (Aletras and Stevenson, 2013). What specific aspects of the TBuckets method make it superior to CUSheffield, and how does the paper's approach differ in terms of the underlying semantic similarity calculation?

4. The paper uses GloVe word embeddings. Have the authors tested the robustness of their method using different embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec, fastText), and how would results change with different embeddings?

5. The text classification experiments show marginal improvements. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of why coherence scores correlate with classification performance, and what specific aspects of topic quality are most predictive of good classification results?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a methodologically sound evaluation of a coherence measure based on semantic grouping of topic words, with thorough empirical validation. However, the core idea lacks sufficient novelty compared to existing work, and the claims of superiority are not adequately justified beyond empirical results. The "parameter-free" claim is misleading, and the paper fails to provide sufficient theoretical grounding or address important limitations.

I would recommend **weak rejection**. The paper has merit in its thorough evaluation and practical application demonstration, but the lack of significant novelty and theoretical justification makes it insufficiently compelling for publication at CoNLL, which emphasizes established methods and rigorous validation of new approaches. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to better differentiate their approach from existing work, provide a stronger theoretical foundation, and address the limitations mentioned above to make a compelling case for novelty.
