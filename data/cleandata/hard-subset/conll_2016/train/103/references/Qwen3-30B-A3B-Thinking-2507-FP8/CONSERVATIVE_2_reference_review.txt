

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper

The paper proposes "TBuckets," a novel approach for measuring topic coherence in topic models by grouping topic words into thematic "buckets." The authors claim their method outperforms state-of-the-art techniques on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) and that their SVD-based techniques require no parameter tuning. The core idea involves creating thematic groups of words within a topic and using the distribution of words across these buckets to determine coherence (single large bucket = high coherence).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. A new conceptual framework for topic coherence evaluation based on thematic bucketing of topic words
2. Three implementation techniques for the TBuckets approach (clustering, SVD, and SVD with reorganization)
3. Demonstration of practical utility in weakly supervised text classification

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem motivation**: The paper effectively establishes the importance of topic coherence evaluation with compelling examples of coherent vs. non-coherent topics. The authors clearly articulate why this problem matters for practical applications of topic modeling.

2. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation on three standard datasets using the standard Pearson's r correlation metric with human judgments. The comparison against Roder et al. (2015) is appropriate, and the results are presented clearly with supporting tables.

3. **Demonstrated practical utility**: The application to weakly supervised text classification (Section 4.3) is a strong practical contribution. The paper shows that using TBuckets to filter high-quality topics leads to improved classification performance in 3 out of 4 datasets, which is valuable evidence of real-world utility.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in core concept**: The fundamental idea of grouping topic words by semantic similarity is not novel. The paper cites several prior works (Newman et al., 2010b; Mimno et al., 2011; Aletras and Stevenson, 2013) that have already explored similar concepts. The "bucket" concept is essentially a rephrasing of existing approaches without sufficient differentiation. The paper fails to clearly articulate how TBuckets differs from these prior methods.

2. **Misleading claim about parameter-free approach**: The paper claims that "TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg carry merit for being completely parameter free," but this is inaccurate. The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg uses a dynamic threshold based on topic-specific calculations (AWPS and AWES), which is still a form of parameterization dependent on the topic being evaluated. The authors provide no evidence that this dynamic threshold will generalize well across different topics and datasets.

3. **Insufficient statistical validation**: The reported performance gains are modest (e.g., TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves 0.837 vs. 0.806 on NYT), but the paper provides no statistical significance testing to confirm these differences are meaningful. The claim that the approach "outperforms the state-of-the-art" is unsubstantiated without proper statistical validation.

4. **Inadequate comparison with relevant baselines**: The paper compares against Roder et al. (2015)'s "best performing settings" but fails to compare against specific methods from that framework that might be more relevant (e.g., CUCI with NPMI, CUSheffield). This makes the claimed superiority less convincing, as it's possible the state-of-the-art methods they're comparing against are not the most appropriate baselines.

5. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper doesn't provide a theoretical foundation for why the bucket distribution should correlate with human judgments of topic coherence. The examples given (like the weather topic) are illustrative but not rigorous. The authors don't explain why a single large bucket should indicate coherence, or why multiple medium-sized buckets indicate lower coherence.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the TBuckets approach fundamentally differ from existing methods like CUSheffield (Aletras and Stevenson, 2013), which also uses distributional semantics to evaluate topic coherence? The paper doesn't clearly distinguish these approaches.

2. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on topic-specific calculations (AWPS and AWES). How is this truly parameter-free, and what evidence do the authors have that this dynamic threshold will generalize well to topics not in their evaluation datasets?

3. The paper states that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on 2 out of 3 datasets. What statistical tests were performed to confirm these differences are significant, and what is the p-value for these comparisons?

4. The paper mentions that TBuckets-Clustering requires a "Max Dist" parameter, yet claims that "TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are completely parameter free." How do the authors justify this distinction, and why is TBuckets-SVD considered parameter-free when TBuckets-Clustering is not?

5. The paper doesn't address why their approach would be better suited for specific domains (e.g., Genomics) compared to existing methods. What specific advantages does TBuckets offer for domain-specific topic coherence evaluation?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a practical approach to topic coherence evaluation with some demonstrable utility in text classification. However, the core contribution lacks sufficient novelty to justify the claims of being a "novel approach" that "outperforms the state-of-the-art." The paper's claims about the parameter-free nature of their methods are misleading, and the performance gains reported are modest without statistical validation.

As a reviewer who prioritizes methodological rigor and established approaches, I find the paper's claims overstated. While the practical application to text classification is valuable, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support the claim that TBuckets represents a significant advance over existing methods. The paper would need to substantially strengthen its theoretical justification, provide a more rigorous comparison with relevant baselines, and clarify the true parameter requirements of their approach to be convincing.

I recommend rejection, with the understanding that the paper might be reconsidered with substantial revisions addressing the weaknesses identified above.
