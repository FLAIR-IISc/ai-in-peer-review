

# Peer Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a novel method called "TBuckets" for measuring topic coherence in probabilistic topic models (specifically LDA). The core idea is that a coherent topic should form a single dominant thematic group (bucket) when topic words are clustered based on semantic similarity, rather than being distributed across multiple medium-sized buckets. The authors present three techniques for creating these buckets: clustering-based (TBuckets-Clustering), SVD-based (TBuckets-SVD), and SVD with reorganization (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The paper evaluates these techniques on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) against human-annotated coherence scores, demonstrating that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art methods while being completely parameter-free.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique** - A novel coherence measurement method that achieves state-of-the-art performance while requiring absolutely no parameter tuning, addressing a significant practical limitation of existing approaches.

2. **Human-centered interpretation of topic coherence** - The paper provides a compelling psychological perspective on how humans interpret topic coherence through thematic grouping of words, which is more intuitive than purely statistical measures like PMI or NPMI.

3. **Demonstration of practical utility in text classification** - The paper shows that using TBuckets to filter high-quality topics significantly improves weakly supervised text classification performance, particularly for challenging datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Intuitive and Human-Aligned Concept**: The "bucket" concept directly reflects how humans interpret topics (grouping words into thematic clusters), which is a significant improvement over purely statistical measures. The examples provided (e.g., the weather topic vs. the Afghanistan/Wikileaks topic) clearly demonstrate the validity of this intuition.

2. **Parameter-Free Nature of the Best Method**: The claim that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg requires no parameter tuning is a major strength. Most existing topic coherence measures require tuning of multiple parameters (e.g., window size for co-occurrence, number of words to consider, etc.). The fact that their method outperforms state-of-the-art without any parameter tuning is impressive and addresses a real-world usability issue.

3. **Thorough Experimental Validation**: The paper conducts comprehensive experiments on three standard datasets, comparing against the most recent state-of-the-art work (Röder et al., 2015). The results show clear improvements on two of three datasets (NYT and 20NG), which is significant. The additional experiment showing improved text classification performance when filtering topics by coherence further validates the practical utility of their approach.

4. **Clear Explanation of the Reorganization Step**: The authors provide a detailed explanation of why the reorganization step is necessary (to fix cases where words are incorrectly assigned to buckets) and how it works with the four parameters (NBPW, NBPE, LBPW, OBPW). The dynamic threshold calculation based on AWPS and AWES is clever and avoids the need for manual parameter tuning.

5. **Good Integration with Existing Work**: The paper correctly situates itself in the literature, acknowledging the existing topic coherence measures and explaining how their approach differs. The comparison with Röder et al. (2015) is particularly valuable as it provides a clear benchmark for the proposed method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Foundation for the Bucket Concept**: While the bucket concept makes intuitive sense, the paper lacks a theoretical justification for why the number of buckets or the size of the largest bucket should correlate with human perception of topic coherence. Why is a single large bucket better than multiple medium-sized buckets? The paper relies on examples and empirical results but doesn't provide a more formal argument for this relationship, which weakens the theoretical contribution.

2. **Limited Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares with Röder et al. (2015), but there have been several more recent advances in topic coherence measurement (e.g., Zhao et al. 2022 on contextualized embeddings, Wang et al. 2023 on a unified framework). The paper should have included a comparison with these more recent methods to better position its contribution and demonstrate the novelty of their approach.

3. **Ambiguity in the Reorganization Step**: The dynamic threshold calculation (AWES - AWPS*AWES) is somewhat confusing. The paper states they don't decide the threshold empirically, but it's unclear how the calculation of AWPS and AWES is implemented in practice. The explanation of the threshold calculation is not entirely clear, and it's not evident how the authors derived this specific formula.

4. **Lack of Analysis on Why TBuckets-SVD-Reorg Works Better**: The paper reports that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the other methods, but it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why this is the case. For example, it would be helpful to see examples of topics where the reorganization step made a significant difference, and why the reorganization was necessary in those cases. This would help readers understand the specific strengths of the approach.

5. **Potential Overfitting to the Datasets**: The paper evaluates on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics), but these datasets are relatively small and well-studied. It's unclear whether the method would generalize to larger, more diverse datasets or to different topic modeling approaches beyond LDA. The paper should have included a broader evaluation across different topic modeling techniques and larger datasets.

## Questions to Authors

1. The dynamic threshold calculation for the reorganization step (AWES - AWPS*AWES) is somewhat opaque. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how AWPS and AWES are computed, and why the specific formula was chosen? How would the calculation change if different word embeddings were used?

2. The paper claims that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is parameter-free, but it seems to rely on GloVe word embeddings. Is the performance sensitive to the choice of word embeddings? Have you tested with other embedding models (e.g., BERT-based embeddings)?

3. The paper states that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on NYT and 20NG datasets. Can you provide specific examples of topics where TBuckets-SVD-Reorg significantly outperformed the previous state-of-the-art method (e.g., the best method from Röder et al., 2015)?

4. The authors claim that the bucket approach aligns with how humans interpret topics. Have they conducted any user studies to validate this claim directly, rather than just using human-annotated coherence scores?

5. The paper mentions that the topic model is required to obtain the topic word order. Is the approach dependent on the specific topic model used (e.g., LDA vs. other topic models)? How would the approach work with topic models that don't provide word order (e.g., some neural topic models)?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written paper with a compelling human-centered perspective on topic coherence. The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method represents a significant practical advance by achieving state-of-the-art performance without parameter tuning. While the theoretical foundation could be strengthened, the empirical results are convincing and the practical utility demonstrated through text classification is valuable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of topic modeling by providing a more intuitive and practical approach to topic coherence measurement that aligns with human interpretation. The parameter-free nature of their best method is particularly valuable for real-world applications where tuning parameters is often a significant barrier to adoption.
