

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary
The paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measuring topic quality by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core idea is that coherent topics will form a single dominant thematic group (bucket), while incoherent topics will form multiple medium-sized buckets. The authors present three techniques: TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using SVD to discover sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (adding a reorganization step to improve SVD-based results). They evaluate their approach on three standard datasets, demonstrating that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two of the three datasets while being completely parameter-free.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. **The novel "word buckets" concept** for measuring topic coherence, providing a fundamentally different perspective from existing methods that focus on word-word relationships.
2. **The parameter-free nature of TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg**, which outperform state-of-the-art methods while requiring no parameter tuning.
3. **Demonstration of practical utility** through text classification experiments showing improved performance when using TBuckets to select high-quality topics.

## Strengths
1. **Genuine novelty of the conceptual framework**: The "word buckets" approach is a bold, fresh perspective on topic coherence measurement that fundamentally differs from existing methods (which all focus on word-word relationships like PMI, NPMI, or semantic similarity). The paper's examples clearly illustrate how coherent topics form single dominant buckets while incoherent topics form multiple buckets, providing a compelling intuitive explanation that aligns with human perception of coherence.

2. **Parameter-free techniques with strong empirical results**: The fact that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are completely parameter-free is a significant advantage over existing methods that require careful tuning of multiple parameters. The empirical results are impressive - TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on two of three datasets (NYT and 20NG) while requiring no parameter tuning, which is a major practical advantage.

3. **Clear practical validation**: The text classification experiment demonstrates real-world utility by showing that using TBuckets to select high-quality topics improves classification performance (with significant gains on the most challenging dataset, PC vs MAC). This moves beyond just measuring coherence to showing tangible benefits for downstream applications.

4. **Strong empirical evaluation**: The paper provides thorough evaluation across three different datasets with standard metrics (Pearson's r correlation with human annotations), and the results are consistently strong across the board.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of theoretical grounding**: While the approach is intuitive and empirically effective, the paper doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why the bucketing approach should work better than existing methods. The connection between the bucket structure and human perception of coherence is somewhat hand-wavy, lacking deeper theoretical insights.

2. **Ad hoc nature of the reorganization step**: The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique adds a reorganization step that seems to be a post-hoc adjustment to fix issues with the SVD-based approach. The justification for the dynamic threshold (AWES - AWPS*AWES) feels somewhat ad hoc, and the paper doesn't clearly explain why this particular formulation is theoretically sound.

3. **Limited comparison with very recent methods**: While the paper compares with Röder et al. (2015), which is a good baseline, there might be more recent methods published after 2015 that could provide a more comprehensive comparison, potentially weakening the claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art."

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide more detail on why the parameter-free aspect is so valuable for practitioners? How common is it for topic modeling practitioners to struggle with parameter tuning in real-world applications?

2. The dynamic threshold used in the reorganization step (AWES - AWPS*AWES) depends on topic-specific calculations. How robust is this approach to variations in word embedding quality or topic quality across different domains?

3. Have you considered applying the TBuckets approach to non-English corpora or domain-specific corpora (e.g., medical or legal documents) where word embeddings might not capture the relevant semantic relationships as well?

## Overall Recommendation
**Accept**

This paper introduces a genuinely novel and bold approach to measuring topic quality that fundamentally shifts the perspective from word-word relationships to thematic grouping of words. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques is a significant practical advantage, and the empirical results are strong, demonstrating clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods on two out of three datasets. While there are some minor weaknesses (particularly regarding theoretical grounding), these are outweighed by the novelty of the conceptual framework and the strong empirical validation. The paper's focus on a fresh perspective on topic coherence aligns perfectly with the priorities of a reviewer who values novelty over methodological perfection. The practical application in text classification further strengthens the contribution.
