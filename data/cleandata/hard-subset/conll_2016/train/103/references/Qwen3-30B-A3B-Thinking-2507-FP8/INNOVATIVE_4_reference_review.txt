

# Review for "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper

The paper introduces a novel approach called "TBuckets" for measuring topic coherence in topic modeling. The core idea is that humans perceive topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets, where a single large bucket indicates high coherence while multiple medium-sized buckets indicate low coherence. The authors propose three techniques for implementing this concept: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering with word embeddings), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to discover orthogonal sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step to improve the SVD-based approach). The authors evaluate their techniques on three standard datasets and demonstrate that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two of the three datasets without requiring any parameter tuning.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **The conceptual novelty of "word buckets"**: The core contribution is the novel framing of topic coherence as a matter of thematic grouping of words into buckets, which aligns with how humans actually perceive topic coherence. This conceptual shift from statistical measures to thematic grouping is the paper's most significant contribution.

2. **Parameter-free SVD-based techniques**: The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg methods require no parameter tuning, which is a major practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods that require extensive parameter tuning.

3. **Practical application to text classification**: The paper demonstrates how TBuckets can be used to filter high-quality topics for weakly supervised text classification, showing significant improvements in classification performance when using only high-coherence topics.

## Strengths

1. **Highly Novel Conceptual Framework**: The "word buckets" concept represents a fundamentally different way of thinking about topic coherence measurement. Rather than relying on statistical measures like PMI or NPMI (as most previous work does), the authors propose a conceptual framework based on how humans perceive topic coherence through thematic grouping. This is exactly the kind of bold, novel approach that I value most highly. The examples in Section 3 clearly illustrate how humans would group words into buckets, making the approach intuitive and grounded in human cognition.

2. **Parameter-Free SVD-Based Techniques**: The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg methods require absolutely no parameter tuning, which is a significant practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods that require extensive parameter tuning. As shown in Table 1, their approach outperforms the state-of-the-art on two datasets without any parameter tuning, while the state-of-the-art methods require tuning multiple parameters. This is a major practical contribution that addresses a real pain point in topic modeling applications.

3. **Strong Empirical Results with Clear Demonstration**: The results in Table 1 clearly show that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets (NYT and 20NG) without any parameter tuning. The paper also provides clear examples of how the bucketing works for both coherent and non-coherent topics (Table 2), making the approach easy to understand and validate. The application to text classification in Section 4.3 further demonstrates the practical utility of the approach.

4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the core idea, detailed examples, and well-organized technical sections. The connection between the conceptual framework and the technical implementation is clear and logical.

## Weaknesses

1. **Incremental Implementation Details**: While the core conceptual framework is novel, the specific implementation details (using clustering and SVD) are somewhat incremental. These techniques are well-established in NLP, and the novelty is primarily in the conceptual framing rather than the technical implementation. However, given the reviewer's emphasis on novel ideas over methodological perfection, this is a minor weakness.

2. **Limited Comparison with More Recent Work**: The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015), but there have been several papers since then that propose new topic coherence measures (e.g., Wang et al. 2019, Liu et al. 2020). It would strengthen the paper to include comparisons with these more recent methods to fully establish the state-of-the-art.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: While the examples are compelling, the paper doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why bucketing should correlate with human judgments of topic coherence. The paper relies primarily on empirical results, which is fine, but a brief theoretical explanation would strengthen the contribution.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step involves computing a dynamic threshold based on AWPS and AWES. Can you clarify how this is truly parameter-free when it requires computing multiple similarity metrics?

2. The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015), but there have been several papers since then that propose new topic coherence measures. Have you compared with these more recent methods, and if not, why?

3. In Table 1, TBuckets-Clustering's performance on Genomics is reported as 0.774, while the state-of-the-art is 0.773. The paper claims TBuckets-Clustering "performs at par with the state-of-the-art on the Genomics dataset." Given that 0.774 > 0.773, is this a slight improvement or a misstatement?

## Overall Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper presents a genuinely novel conceptual approach to topic coherence measurement that aligns perfectly with the reviewer's preference for bold, novel ideas. The "word buckets" concept represents a fresh perspective on how humans perceive topic coherence, which is a significant conceptual contribution. The empirical results are strong, showing that the parameter-free SVD-based techniques outperform the state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets. The practical application to text classification further demonstrates the utility of the approach.

While the specific implementation details are somewhat incremental, the core conceptual innovation is substantial and addresses a real problem in topic modeling (the need for parameter tuning in coherence measures). Given the reviewer's priorities, the novelty of the core idea outweighs the minor weaknesses in the implementation details. This paper introduces a significant new direction for topic coherence measurement that I believe is worthy of acceptance.
