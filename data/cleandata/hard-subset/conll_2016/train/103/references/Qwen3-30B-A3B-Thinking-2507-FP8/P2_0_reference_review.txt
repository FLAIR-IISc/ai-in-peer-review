

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

The paper proposes a novel approach called "TBuckets" for measuring topic coherence in topic models. The core idea is to group topic words into thematic "buckets" and use the distribution of words across these buckets to assess coherence. A topic with a single dominant bucket (indicating a single coherent theme) is considered high quality, while a topic with multiple medium-sized buckets (indicating multiple themes) is considered low quality.

The main contributions are:
1. A fresh perspective on topic coherence based on human interpretation of topics as thematic groups
2. Three techniques for creating word buckets: TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using SVD to find orthogonal sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (reorganizing buckets for improved coherence)
3. A parameter-free SVD-based method that outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two of three datasets

## Strengths

1. **Novel and intuitive approach to topic coherence**: The paper presents a compelling human-centric perspective on topic coherence that aligns well with how humans actually interpret topics. The bucketing approach is refreshingly different from the typical semantic similarity-based methods used in prior work, and the examples provided clearly demonstrate the intuition behind the approach. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the field.

2. **Parameter-free SVD-based techniques**: The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg techniques are completely parameter-free, which is a major practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods that require extensive parameter tuning. This is particularly valuable for real-world applications where parameter tuning can be time-consuming and requires expert knowledge. The fact that these parameter-free methods outperform methods requiring parameter tuning on two of three datasets is a strong point.

3. **Strong empirical results with clear practical application**: The evaluation shows TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art on two of three datasets, with clear improvements in Pearson correlation. The application to text classification demonstrates a concrete, practical use case where coherence scores can improve performance in weakly supervised learning. The results show significant improvements in classification accuracy when high-quality topics are selected using TBuckets.

4. **Clear explanation of the bucketing process**: The paper provides detailed, step-by-step examples of the bucketing process for both coherent and non-coherent topics, which makes the approach very easy to understand and validates the intuition behind the method.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification for the bucket approach**: While the bucketing approach is intuitive and the examples are compelling, the paper doesn't provide a strong theoretical foundation for why the distribution of words across buckets correlates with human perception of topic coherence. The paper states that "the bucket procedure for this topic executes as follows" but doesn't explain why this particular metric (size of largest bucket) is the right way to measure coherence. This weakens the paper's contribution as a principled method.

2. **Limited comparison with other methods**: The paper only compares with the Roder et al. (2015) framework, but doesn't clearly identify what the current state-of-the-art is beyond this one framework. It would be valuable to compare with other recent topic coherence measures (e.g., Lau et al., 2014; Aletras and Stevenson, 2013) to better establish the novelty and significance of the proposed approach.

3. **Insufficient analysis of why the method works**: The paper shows that the method works well, but doesn't deeply analyze why it works better than other approaches. For instance, why does the SVD-based approach outperform clustering? What specific properties of topic word distributions make the bucket approach effective? This lack of analysis makes it difficult to understand the true contribution of the work.

4. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics), all of which are text corpora. It's unclear how generalizable the method is to other types of data or more specialized domains. The paper doesn't address whether the method would work well for topics with different semantic structures or in domains where word usage differs significantly from general text.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to show the contribution of each component (e.g., the reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). It's unclear how much improvement the reorganization step actually provides over TBuckets-SVD, which weakens the argument for the full TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with other recent topic coherence measures (beyond Roder et al., 2015) to better establish the novelty and significance of your approach? Specifically, how do your methods compare with the CUCI and CUSheffield measures?

2. Could you provide more analysis of why your method works better than alternatives? For instance, what specific properties of topic word distributions make the bucket approach effective, and how do these properties differ from those that make semantic similarity measures effective?

3. How would your method perform on topics from more specialized domains (e.g., medical or legal texts), where word usage and semantic relationships might differ significantly from general text corpora? Could you provide any preliminary results or analysis on this?

4. Could you provide ablation studies to show the contribution of the reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg? How much improvement does this step provide over TBuckets-SVD, and under what conditions is it most beneficial?

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a novel and intuitive approach to topic coherence measurement that aligns well with human interpretation of topics. The parameter-free SVD-based techniques are a significant practical contribution, and the empirical results are strong. However, the lack of theoretical justification, limited comparison with other methods, and insufficient analysis of why the method works well are notable weaknesses that prevent the paper from being a truly groundbreaking contribution.

The paper has significant potential for practical application in topic modeling and text classification, and with more thorough theoretical analysis and broader evaluation, it could make a substantial contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper is a solid contribution that advances the state of the art in topic coherence measurement, but its full potential is not yet realized.

**Recommendation**: Accept (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly the theoretical justification and broader comparison with other methods)
