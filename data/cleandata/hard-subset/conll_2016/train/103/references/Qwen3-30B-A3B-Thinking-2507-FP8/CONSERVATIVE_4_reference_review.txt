

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Contributions

The paper proposes "TBuckets," a method for measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core idea is that coherent topics will form a single large bucket, while incoherent topics will form multiple medium-sized buckets. The authors present three variants: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using SVD to find orthogonal dimensions), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (adding a reorganization step to the SVD approach). The main claimed contributions are: 1) a novel topic coherence measurement approach based on word bucketing, 2) three techniques for implementing this approach, and 3) superior performance over state-of-the-art on standard datasets.

## Contributions as I See Them (Ordered by Strength)

1. **Evaluation of topic coherence on standard datasets**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation on three well-established topic coherence datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) with human-annotated coherence scores. This is a solid contribution as it demonstrates the method's performance on standard benchmarks.

2. **Parameter-free approach for SVD-based techniques**: The claim that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning is a practical contribution, though I'll question its validity below.

3. **Application to text classification**: The demonstration of how topic coherence scores can improve weakly supervised text classification performance is a useful application that adds value beyond just topic coherence measurement.

## Strengths

1. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation on three standard datasets with multiple variants of the method. The comparison with Roder et al. (2015) is well-executed, and the results (Table 1) show clear improvements for TBuckets-SVD-Reorg on two of the three datasets. The text classification application (Table 3) provides a meaningful real-world validation of the coherence scores.

2. **Clear visualization of the bucketing procedure**: The paper effectively demonstrates the bucketing process with concrete examples (e.g., the weather topic vs. the Afghanistan topic), making the concept easy to understand. The examples in Table 2 provide good insight into how the method works.

3. **Practical parameter-free approach**: If the claim of "completely parameter-free" is valid, this is a significant practical advantage over methods requiring careful parameter tuning. This is particularly valuable for practitioners who might struggle with hyperparameter optimization.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty in the core approach**: The fundamental idea of grouping words into thematic clusters to measure coherence is not novel. Similar concepts have been explored in prior work: Newman et al. (2010b) used word co-occurrence patterns, Aletras and Stevenson (2013) used distributional similarity, and Roder et al. (2015) proposed a framework that already includes many similar elements. The paper fails to adequately distinguish its approach from these existing methods. The term "word buckets" is superficially different from previous clustering-based coherence measures, but the underlying idea is not new.

2. **Questionable "parameter-free" claim**: The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but this is misleading. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg explicitly uses a dynamic threshold calculated as "AWES − AWPS/AWES" which depends on two values (AWPS and AWES) that are computed for each topic. This is a form of parameterization, albeit dynamic, and the paper doesn't explain why this threshold calculation doesn't require any parameter tuning. The authors should clarify how this is truly parameter-free.

3. **Inadequate comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares only against Roder et al. (2015), which is from 2015, while there have been numerous improvements to topic coherence metrics since then (e.g., in 2018, 2020, and 2021). The authors don't compare against more recent work such as the work by Cao et al. (2020) or the more recent evaluation frameworks. This is a serious methodological weakness as it suggests the authors haven't kept up with the current state of the field.

4. **Lack of ablation study**: The paper doesn't provide an ablation study to determine which component of the method is actually responsible for the performance gains. Without this, it's impossible to know whether the bucketing idea, the SVD, or the reorganization step is truly contributing to the results. For example, the authors claim TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art, but we don't know if the reorganization step is necessary or if the basic SVD approach would have been sufficient.

5. **Weak theoretical justification**: The paper provides intuitive examples but lacks a strong theoretical argument for why the bucketing approach should correlate with human coherence judgments. The examples given are anecdotal and don't provide systematic evidence for why this specific method would work better than existing approaches.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free." However, the reorganization step uses a threshold calculated as "AWES - AWPS/AWES." Can you clarify how this calculation doesn't require any parameters, especially since the values of AWPS and AWES vary across topics?

2. The paper compares only against Roder et al. (2015), which is from 2015. Why not compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., from 2020-2023) that have improved upon the metrics used in Roder et al.?

3. The paper lacks an ablation study. Could you provide results showing the contribution of each component (bucketing, SVD, reorganization) to the final performance? Specifically, how much does TBuckets-SVD-Reorg improve over TBuckets-SVD?

4. The paper doesn't discuss how the method handles topics with very few words (e.g., less than 5 words). How would the bucketing approach work in such cases, and would it still be meaningful?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents an interesting approach to topic coherence measurement that is well-evaluated on standard datasets. However, the core idea lacks sufficient novelty compared to existing work, and the claim of "parameter-free" techniques is questionable. The paper would be significantly stronger with a more thorough comparison against recent state-of-the-art methods, a proper ablation study, and a clearer justification for why this approach should work better than existing methods.

While the application to text classification is promising, the main contribution of the paper (the coherence measurement method itself) does not seem to represent a substantial advancement over the field. The paper would be better positioned as a methodological improvement on existing coherence metrics rather than a novel approach.

I recommend rejection, as the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate novelty or significant methodological advancement over established approaches in the field. The lack of comparison with more recent work is particularly problematic for a paper claiming to outperform the state-of-the-art.
