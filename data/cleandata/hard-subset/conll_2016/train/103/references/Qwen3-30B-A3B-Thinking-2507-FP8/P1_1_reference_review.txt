

# Peer Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary

This paper proposes a novel approach called TBuckets for measuring the quality (coherence) of topics in topic modeling. The core idea is that coherent topics should group into a single dominant thematic bucket, while less coherent topics will form multiple smaller buckets. The authors present three techniques for creating these buckets: TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify orthogonal sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step to improve the SVD-based approach). The approach is evaluated on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) by correlating automated coherence scores with human-annotated scores, and is also applied to improve weakly supervised text classification.

## Main Contributions

1. **The TBuckets concept**: A novel, intuitive approach to topic coherence measurement based on grouping topic words into thematic buckets, with the assumption that a single dominant bucket indicates higher coherence.
2. **Parameter-free SVD-based techniques**: TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg that require no parameter tuning, a significant practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods.
3. **Demonstrated practical utility**: Successful application of the coherence measure to improve weakly supervised text classification by selecting only high-quality topics for human annotation.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and intuitive approach**: The TBuckets concept is genuinely novel and aligns well with how humans perceive topic coherence. The explanation of the bucket formation process (e.g., the weather vs. Afghanistan topics) is clear and compelling, making the approach easy to understand and motivating.

2. **Parameter-free SVD techniques**: The fact that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning is a major practical contribution. Most existing coherence measures require careful tuning of multiple parameters (as noted in the paper), which is a significant barrier to practical adoption. The paper demonstrates that their parameter-free approaches outperform state-of-the-art methods on two out of three datasets, which is a strong practical advantage.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The evaluation on three standard datasets shows clear improvements over the state-of-the-art (Roder et al., 2015) on two datasets (NYT and 20NG), with TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieving the highest Pearson's r correlation values. The results are well-presented in Table 1, and the performance is consistent across different topic sets.

4. **Practical application to text classification**: The demonstration of how TBuckets can improve weakly supervised text classification by selecting only high-quality topics for human annotation is a compelling practical contribution. The results in Table 3 show clear improvements in classification accuracy (3 out of 4 datasets) when using high-quality topics, with significant differences (up to 5% F1) in the most challenging datasets like PC vs MAC.

5. **Clear evaluation methodology**: The paper follows a well-established evaluation framework (using Pearson's correlation with human annotations) that allows for direct comparison with previous work, making the results transparent and comparable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical foundation**: While the approach is intuitive, the paper provides no theoretical justification for why the bucket structure correlates with coherence. There is no formal analysis explaining why grouping words into buckets would better capture topic coherence than existing methods based on PMI or semantic similarity. This makes it difficult to understand why the approach works, beyond empirical evidence.

2. **Limited comparison with recent work**: The paper only compares with Roder et al. (2015), which is a good baseline, but doesn't compare with more recent methods for topic coherence measurement. For example, methods like those proposed by Liu et al. (2015), Wang et al. (2016), or others that have emerged since Roder et al. would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the state-of-the-art.

3. **Ambiguity in the "parameter-free" claim**: The paper claims that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step involves a threshold calculation (AWES - AWPS*AWES) that depends on computed values (AWES and AWPS). While these values are derived from the topic itself, they effectively constitute a data-dependent parameter, which contradicts the "parameter-free" claim. The paper needs to clarify this point.

4. **Lack of analysis on why the approach works**: The paper shows that the approach works well, but doesn't provide deeper analysis on why it outperforms existing methods. For example, the paper doesn't analyze which types of topics benefit most from the bucket approach, or why the SVD-based method works better than clustering. This limits the broader understanding of the contribution.

5. **Potential domain limitations**: The use of GloVe embeddings trained on Wikipedia and Gigaword may not be optimal for specialized domains like Genomics, which is one of the datasets used. The paper doesn't explore how the approach might perform with domain-specific embeddings, which could limit its applicability to specialized text collections.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization threshold calculation (AWES - AWPS*AWES) depends on values computed from the topic. How is this considered parameter-free? Is there a way to make the threshold truly parameter-free without data-dependent calculations?

2. The paper doesn't compare with more recent topic coherence methods published after Roder et al. (2015). Could the authors provide a comparison with methods like those by Liu et al. (2015) or Wang et al. (2016) to better establish the state-of-the-art?

3. For the Genomics dataset, the GloVe embeddings were trained on general Wikipedia text, which may not capture domain-specific terminology well. How would the performance change if the authors used domain-specific word embeddings (e.g., trained on biomedical literature)?

4. The paper shows that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg improves text classification, but the human annotation process for topics is not described in detail. Could the authors provide more information about how the human annotators evaluated the topics, and how the selection of high-quality topics reduced the annotation effort?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a novel and intuitive approach to topic coherence measurement with strong empirical results. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques is a significant practical advantage over existing methods. While the paper has some weaknesses in theoretical justification and comparison scope, the practical contributions and strong experimental results make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions raised above, particularly regarding the "parameter-free" claim and comparison with more recent work.
