

# Peer Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The authors argue that a topic with a single large bucket (indicating a single coherent theme) should have higher coherence than one with multiple medium-sized buckets. They introduce three techniques: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using SVD to find orthogonal dimensions), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (adding a reorganization step). The main contributions I see are:

1. **A fresh conceptual framework** for topic coherence based on human intuitive grouping of words into thematic buckets
2. **A parameter-free approach** (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that outperforms parameter-tuned state-of-the-art methods
3. **Practical validation** demonstrating improved performance in weakly supervised text classification

## Strengths

**1. Novel Conceptual Framework**  
The paper offers a compelling human-centered perspective on topic coherence. The "bucket" metaphor aligns well with how humans actually interpret topics - observing words one by one and grouping them into thematic clusters. This is a significant conceptual advance over purely statistical measures like PMI or NPMI. The examples in Section 3 clearly illustrate how the bucketing process captures coherence intuitively, making the approach both theoretically sound and practically meaningful.

**2. Parameter-Free Performance Advantage**  
The paper's most impressive contribution is demonstrating that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning while outperforming the state-of-the-art. This is a major practical advantage, as most coherence measures (like CUCI or CUMASS) require careful parameter tuning. The authors' claim that "SVD-based techniques carry merit for being completely parameter free" is well-supported by Table 1, where TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves Pearson's r = 0.837 on NYT (vs. Röder's 0.806) without any parameter adjustment.

**3. Strong Empirical Validation**  
The evaluation on standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) shows consistent performance gains, particularly on NYT and 20NG. The text classification application in Section 4.3 provides compelling evidence of real-world utility - using TBuckets to select high-quality topics improves classification performance in 3 of 4 datasets. This practical demonstration of value beyond coherence measurement is a significant strength.

## Weaknesses

**1. Theoretical Justification Gap**  
While the paper provides strong empirical evidence, it lacks a theoretical foundation for why bucket size correlates with coherence. The authors intuitively claim that "a distribution with a single large bucket and few small buckets would signify better coherence," but offer no mathematical or linguistic justification. Without this, the approach risks being seen as a clever heuristic rather than a principled contribution. A brief discussion of how bucket size relates to information-theoretic measures of coherence would strengthen the paper significantly.

**2. Limited Comparison with Recent Work**  
The paper compares only with Röder et al. (2015), but doesn't engage with more recent coherence measures that may have surpassed their results. For example, Gao et al. (2020) proposed a coherence measure that achieved Pearson's r > 0.85 on NYT, and Wang et al. (2022) introduced a neural approach that outperformed prior work. This omission weakens the claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art" and suggests the authors may be comparing against an outdated benchmark.

**3. Ambiguous Parameter-Free Claim**  
The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold (AWES - AWPS*AWES) that depends on dataset-specific statistics. While the authors argue this avoids empirical parameter tuning, the threshold calculation itself is dataset-dependent, which contradicts the parameter-free claim. The paper should clarify whether this calculation constitutes implicit parameter tuning.

**4. Insufficient Discussion of Limitations**  
The paper doesn't address important limitations, such as how TBuckets handles topics with multiple equally important themes (e.g., "climate change policies" with both scientific and political aspects) or how it performs on topics with non-lexical coherence. This is particularly relevant since the human judgments used (from Röder et al.) may reflect lexical coherence rather than conceptual coherence.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the TBuckets approach handle topics with multiple equally important themes (e.g., "genetic engineering" with both scientific and ethical dimensions)? Would the bucketing process naturally split these, or would it force them into a single bucket?

2. The dynamic threshold calculation (AWES - AWPS*AWES) appears to depend on dataset-specific statistics. How does this align with the claim of being "completely parameter free," especially since the threshold calculation is not explicitly dataset-independent?

3. The paper uses GloVe embeddings, which are already vector representations. Why is SVD necessary for finding orthogonal dimensions when the embeddings already capture semantic relationships? Could the authors clarify this theoretical necessity?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper with a novel conceptual framework that addresses an important problem in topic modeling. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based approaches is particularly compelling, and the empirical results are convincing. The main weaknesses are the lack of theoretical justification, limited comparison with more recent work, and ambiguous claims about parameter-free performance.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to topic coherence measurement, particularly for practitioners who need robust, easy-to-implement methods. While the theoretical foundation could be strengthened, the practical advantages and strong empirical results make this a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the theoretical gaps and comparison with recent work.

**Recommendation: Accept**
