

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core idea is that coherent topics should form a single dominant bucket, while incoherent topics form multiple medium-sized buckets. The authors introduce three techniques: TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering of word embeddings), TBuckets-SVD (using SVD to identify orthogonal sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (adding a reorganization step to improve bucket formation). They evaluate their methods on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) by correlating their coherence scores with human-annotated scores, reporting that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two out of three datasets while being completely parameter-free.

## Main Contributions
1. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg: A parameter-free topic coherence measure that achieves state-of-the-art results on two out of three datasets, eliminating the need for manual parameter tuning required by existing methods.
2. The "word buckets" conceptual framework that provides an intuitive explanation for topic coherence aligned with human perception, demonstrated through clear examples.
3. Practical validation of the coherence measure through improved text classification performance when using high-coherence topics for human annotation.

## Strengths
1. **Parameter-free approach with superior performance**: The most significant strength is TBuckets-SVD-Reorg's ability to achieve state-of-the-art results without any parameter tuning. As shown in Table 1, it outperforms all previous methods on 20NG (0.869 vs. 0.859) and NYT (0.837 vs. 0.806) datasets while requiring no parameter configuration. This addresses a major practical limitation of existing methods that require careful parameter tuning.

2. **Intuitive conceptual framework**: The paper provides clear, well-motivated examples (e.g., the weather topic vs. the Afghanistan/Wikileaks topic in Section 3) that effectively demonstrate how the bucket concept aligns with human perception of topic coherence. This intuitive foundation makes the method accessible and understandable.

3. **Demonstrated practical utility**: The text classification experiment (Section 4.3) shows that using TBuckets to select high-coherence topics for human annotation leads to significantly better classification performance (e.g., 96.19% vs. 95.95% for POLITICS vs SCIENCE). This provides concrete evidence of the method's value beyond just coherence measurement.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of theoretical foundation**: The paper lacks a formal theoretical justification for why the bucket structure correlates with human-perceived topic coherence. While the examples are intuitive, there is no mathematical analysis or formal proof explaining the relationship between bucket formation and coherence, making it difficult to assess the generalizability of the approach.

2. **Limited comparison with recent methods**: The comparison is restricted to methods reported in Roder et al. (2015), which may not represent the current state-of-the-art. The paper does not compare with more recent coherence measures published after 2015, such as those in the last 2-3 years, which weakens the claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art."

3. **Insufficient justification for reorganization step**: The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique adds a reorganization step with a threshold based on "average word pair similarity" and "average word eigenvector similarity." However, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence or analysis to show why this step is necessary, or how it improves performance beyond the basic SVD approach. The threshold calculation appears somewhat ad-hoc without proper justification.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does TBuckets-SVD-Reorg compare with more recent topic coherence measures published after 2015 (e.g., in the last 2-3 years)? The current comparison is limited to methods from Roder et al. (2015), which may not represent the current state-of-the-art.

2. Could you provide a more formal explanation or mathematical analysis for why the bucket structure correlates with human-perceived topic coherence? This would strengthen the theoretical foundation of the approach.

3. What is the computational complexity of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg compared to existing methods, particularly considering the reorganization step? The paper doesn't discuss computational efficiency, which is important for practical application.
