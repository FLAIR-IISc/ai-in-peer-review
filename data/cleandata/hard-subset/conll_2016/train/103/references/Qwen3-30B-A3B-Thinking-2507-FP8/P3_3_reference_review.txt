

# Peer Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic coherence in probabilistic topic models by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The authors propose three techniques for creating these buckets: clustering-based (TBuckets-Clustering), SVD-based (TBuckets-SVD), and SVD with reorganization (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. **The TBuckets framework** for measuring topic coherence by grouping words into thematic buckets, which aligns with human cognitive processes for evaluating topic coherence.
2. **Parameter-free coherence measurement** through TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, which outperform state-of-the-art methods on two of three datasets without requiring parameter tuning.
3. **Demonstration of practical utility** in improving weakly supervised text classification by selecting high-coherence topics for human annotation.

## Strengths

1. **Intuitive and Human-Aligned Coherence Measurement**: The paper's core insight—grouping topic words into thematic buckets to reflect human perception of coherence—is highly intuitive and well-justified through clear examples. The authors demonstrate how a single dominant bucket correlates with human judgment of coherence, which is a significant conceptual contribution over purely statistical measures.

2. **Parameter-Free Approach**: The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg techniques are completely parameter-free, a major advantage over existing methods that require extensive parameter tuning. This makes the approach more practical for real-world applications. The paper shows TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on NYT (0.837 vs. 0.806) and 20NG (0.863 vs. 0.859) datasets, with a clear performance advantage in parameter efficiency.

3. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-structured, using three standard datasets with human-annotated coherence scores. The results consistently show TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieving top performance, and the application to text classification (where selecting high-coherence topics improved classification accuracy by 1-2% on difficult datasets) provides compelling evidence of practical utility.

4. **Clear Presentation and Methodology**: The paper is exceptionally well-written, with clear explanations of the TBuckets concept, the three techniques, and their implementation. The examples of bucket formation for coherent and non-coherent topics effectively illustrate the approach's rationale.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification for Bucketing**: While the bucketing approach is intuitive, the paper lacks a theoretical foundation for why the number of buckets or the size of the largest bucket should correlate with human perception of coherence. The authors observe the correlation but don't provide a formal explanation for why this relationship exists, which weakens the conceptual contribution.

2. **Incomplete Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015) but doesn't include comparisons with more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., the work by Lau et al. 2014 on NPMI, or subsequent improvements in topic coherence evaluation). This limits the significance of the claimed performance gains.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Failure Cases**: The method underperforms on Genomics (0.68 vs. 0.773), but the paper doesn't analyze why this happens. Without understanding these failure cases, it's unclear whether the approach is limited to certain topic types or datasets, which is important for assessing the method's generalizability.

4. **Ambiguity in Reorganization Threshold**: The dynamic threshold for reorganization (AWES - AWPS*AWES) is defined without clear derivation. While the authors claim it's parameter-free, the formula appears somewhat ad-hoc, and it's unclear how it was developed or why this specific form is optimal.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you elaborate on why TBuckets-SVD-Reorg underperforms on the Genomics dataset compared to other datasets? What specific characteristics of Genomics topics might cause this discrepancy?

2. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is completely parameter-free, but the reorganization threshold depends on AWES and AWPS. Could you clarify how these values are computed without introducing parameters, especially since they're derived from the topic itself?

3. How does the TBuckets approach interact with different topic model configurations (e.g., varying K, hyperparameters)? Would the coherence scores be affected by the underlying topic model quality, and how might this impact the selection of high-coherence topics for text classification?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to topic coherence measurement. The TBuckets framework is intuitive and well-validated, and the parameter-free SVD-based techniques represent a significant practical advantage over existing methods. While the theoretical justification could be strengthened and the comparison with more recent work expanded, the empirical results are compelling and the practical application to text classification is particularly convincing. The paper is well-written and makes a clear, meaningful contribution to the field.

The main weaknesses are the lack of theoretical grounding for the bucketing approach and insufficient analysis of failure cases. However, these do not significantly undermine the paper's core contributions, which are well-supported by the experimental results and practical demonstration. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions above.
