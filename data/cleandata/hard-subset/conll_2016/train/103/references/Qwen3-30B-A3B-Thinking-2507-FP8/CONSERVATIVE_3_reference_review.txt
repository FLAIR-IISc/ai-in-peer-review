

# Review for "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes "TBuckets," a novel approach for measuring topic coherence in topic models by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core idea is that coherent topics will have most words in a single dominant bucket, while incoherent topics will have words spread across multiple buckets. The authors present three techniques: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to find orthogonal dimensions), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step to improve the SVD approach). They evaluate their methods on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) using Pearson's r correlation with human judgments, claiming their approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods, particularly their SVD-based techniques which are "completely parameter free."

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **The TBuckets concept of grouping topic words into thematic buckets for coherence assessment** - This is the core novelty of the paper.
2. **The reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg)** - A thoughtful refinement to address limitations of the basic SVD approach.
3. **Empirical validation on standard datasets** - Demonstrating improved performance over prior work on established benchmarks.

## Strengths

1. **The reorganization step is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation**: The authors clearly identify a specific failure case of the basic SVD approach (words being placed in buckets based on high similarity to an eigenvector rather than the largest bucket) and provide a thoughtful solution. The examples they provide (e.g., the topic {show, television, tv, news, network, medium, fox, cable, channel, series}) are convincing and demonstrate a real improvement over the basic SVD approach.

2. **The parameter-free nature of the SVD approaches is genuinely advantageous**: The claim that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning is a strong point in their favor. Most existing topic coherence measures require careful tuning of multiple parameters (e.g., the number of words to consider, thresholds for similarity), which can be a significant barrier to practical adoption.

3. **The application to text classification demonstrates practical utility**: The paper shows that using TBuckets to select high-quality topics for weakly supervised text classification leads to improved performance over not using coherence measures. This is a compelling demonstration of the real-world value of their approach, particularly for the PC vs MAC dataset where the difference was substantial (68.47% vs. 66.98% accuracy).

## Weaknesses

1. **The core idea lacks sufficient novelty**: The paper's central concept—grouping topic words to assess coherence—is not as novel as claimed. Existing methods like "word and topic intrusions" (Chang et al., 2009) and "distributional semantics" (Aletras and Stevenson, 2013) operate on similar principles. The paper doesn't clearly differentiate TBuckets from these established approaches, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of their contribution. The bucketing approach is conceptually similar to existing methods that assess topic coherence through semantic grouping.

2. **The "parameter-free" claim is misleading**: While the authors claim TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," the reorganization step relies on a dynamic threshold calculated from "average word pair similarity (AWPS)" and "average word eigenvector similarity (AWES)." This threshold calculation is effectively a parameter that's computed on the fly for each topic, not a fixed value. The paper doesn't explain how this differs from having a parameter that's dynamically calculated rather than set by the user, which undermines their key claim of being parameter-free.

3. **Lack of thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art**: The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015), which is a good baseline, but doesn't compare with more recent methods published after 2015. The paper appears to have been written around 2015 (based on the Roder et al. reference), but the conference where it's being submitted would likely expect comparison with more recent work. Without this, it's difficult to assess whether their approach truly represents the current state-of-the-art.

4. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (clustering, SVD, reorganization). Without these, it's impossible to determine how much each component contributes to the final performance. For example, how much does the reorganization step improve performance over the basic SVD approach? How does clustering compare to the SVD approach? This omission makes it difficult to assess the true novelty and value of each component.

5. **Limited discussion of failure cases**: The paper only presents examples where TBuckets performs well (Tables 1 and 2), but doesn't discuss cases where it fails or underperforms. A rigorous evaluation should include analysis of failure cases to help readers understand the scope and limitations of the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization threshold is calculated using AWPS and AWES. How is this different from having a parameter that's computed dynamically rather than set by the user? If the threshold calculation is considered a parameter, what would be the impact on the method's performance if it were fixed (e.g., using a constant value instead of the dynamic calculation)?

2. The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015), but the conference where this paper is being submitted would expect comparison with more recent state-of-the-art methods. Have you compared your approach with methods published after 2015, and if not, why not?

3. The paper states that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on 2 out of 3 datasets. What are the specific characteristics of the datasets where it performs well vs. where it doesn't? Are there patterns in the topic types or dataset characteristics that explain the performance differences?

4. The paper mentions using GloVe word embeddings but doesn't discuss how the choice of word embeddings might affect results. Have you experimented with different embedding methods (e.g., word2vec, fastText) to see if the results are robust to embedding choice?

## Recommendation

**Weak Reject**

While the paper presents a well-motivated refinement to topic coherence assessment (particularly the reorganization step) and provides some useful empirical validation, the core idea lacks sufficient novelty compared to existing work. The "parameter-free" claim is misleading, and the paper fails to adequately compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods. The lack of ablation studies and failure case analysis further limits the paper's contribution. The practical application to text classification is a strong point, but it's not enough to overcome the methodological shortcomings. The paper would need significant revision to address these concerns before it could be considered for publication.
