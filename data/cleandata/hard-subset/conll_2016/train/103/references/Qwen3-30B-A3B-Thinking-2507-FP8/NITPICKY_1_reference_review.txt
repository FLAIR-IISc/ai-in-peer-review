

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Intuitive topic coherence measurement approach**: The paper proposes a novel method (TBuckets) that groups topic words into thematic buckets to measure topic coherence, which is based on how humans intuitively perceive topic coherence.
2. **Three implementation techniques**: The authors present three implementations of their approach: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (with a reorganization step for improved coherence).
3. **Parameter-free SVD-based techniques**: The paper claims that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," which they position as a significant advantage over state-of-the-art methods requiring multiple parameter tunings.

## Strengths

1. **Novel conceptual framework**: The TBuckets approach presents a fresh perspective on topic coherence measurement that aligns well with human intuition. The bucketing concept is intuitive and makes sense for evaluating topic coherence, as demonstrated in the examples provided.

2. **Clear experimental evaluation**: The paper thoroughly evaluates their approach on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) using the same evaluation protocol as Roder et al. (2015), allowing for direct comparison with state-of-the-art methods.

3. **Practical application demonstration**: The paper successfully demonstrates the utility of their coherence measure in a real-world application (weakly supervised text classification), showing that using high-quality topics (as measured by TBuckets) leads to better classification performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misleading claim about "completely parameter-free" techniques**: The paper repeatedly claims that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," which is technically inaccurate. The GloVe embeddings with 300 dimensions are a fixed parameter, and the reorganization step calculates a dynamic threshold based on topic-specific values (AWPS and AWES). The paper states "We do not decide the threshold empirically, instead devise it based on the topic under consideration," but this still involves a calculation that depends on the topic, making it a parameter of the method. This misrepresentation undermines the paper's credibility.

2. **Confusing technical description of reorganization threshold**: The paper states "A higher value of the ratio indicates better word-word similarities and lenient word-eigenvector similarities making reorganization meaningful." However, the ratio is calculated as (AWES - AWPS)/AWES. A higher value of this ratio would actually indicate *less* flexibility (since it's closer to 1), not more. This is a fundamental error in the explanation of the method that would confuse readers and potentially lead to incorrect implementation.

3. **Minor but pervasive formatting inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that suggest a lack of attention to detail:
   - References use inconsistent citation formats (e.g., "Blei et al\.,? 2003" instead of "Blei et al., 2003")
   - Inconsistent use of hyphens ("text-mining" vs. "text mining")
   - Inconsistent use of "Röder" (with umlaut) in text vs. "Roder" without umlaut in references
   - Section 3.1 contains an example with a typo: "Word Seen: weather. Bucket-1: {storm, weather}" when "weather" was the second word, not the first.

4. **Questionable significance of performance gains**: The paper claims "TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art on 2 out of 3 datasets," but the performance gains are marginal (e.g., 0.837 vs. 0.806 on NYT is only a 3.8% improvement). The paper does not provide statistical significance testing for these differences, making it difficult to assess whether the improvements are meaningful in practice.

5. **Incomplete explanation of coherence score combination**: In section 4.3, the paper states "we simply add the coherence scores of individual topics in the set" to compute the quality of a set of topics. However, it doesn't explain how the coherence scores (which range from 0 to 1 as shown in Table 2) are combined with the human scores (which range from 1 to 3). This omission makes the evaluation of the text classification application unclear.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free." Could you clarify what parameters you consider to be "free" (i.e., not requiring tuning), and why the fixed GloVe embedding dimensionality (300) is not considered a parameter?

2. In section 3.1, you state "A higher value of the ratio indicates better word-word similarities and lenient word-eigenvector similarities making reorganization meaningful." Given that the ratio is (AWES - AWPS)/AWES, a higher value would indicate *less* flexibility (since it's closer to 1), not more. Could you clarify this apparent contradiction?

3. The paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the performance differences between TBuckets-SVD-Reorg and the state-of-the-art. Could you provide p-values or confidence intervals for these comparisons?

4. In section 4.3, you state that coherence scores for individual topics are normalized to lie between 0 and 1 (as shown in Table 2), but human-assigned coherence scores lie between 1 and 3. How exactly do you combine these scores to evaluate the quality of a set of topics? Could you provide the specific formula used for this combination?

## Final Recommendation

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an intuitive approach to topic coherence measurement with some promising results, the numerous technical inaccuracies, misleading claims about parameter-free methods, and pervasive formatting inconsistencies undermine the paper's credibility. The marginal performance gains (3-4% improvement in correlation) are not sufficient to overcome these methodological and presentation flaws, especially when the paper makes strong claims about being "completely parameter free." A perfectionist reviewer would find these issues too significant to overlook, even if the core idea has some merit.
