

# Review: Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets

## Summary
This paper introduces "TBuckets," a novel approach for measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core idea is that a coherent topic will have a single dominant bucket containing most words, while an incoherent topic will have multiple medium-sized buckets. The authors propose three techniques for creating these buckets: clustering-based (TBuckets-Clustering), SVD-based (TBuckets-SVD), and a reorganized SVD approach (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The paper claims that their methods outperform state-of-the-art coherence measures, with the SVD-based techniques being completely parameter-free.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Novel conceptual framework for topic coherence**: The "bucket" concept represents a fundamentally new perspective on topic coherence, moving beyond statistical measures (PMI, NPMI) to a thematic grouping approach that aligns more directly with human intuition about topic coherence.

2. **Parameter-free coherence measurement**: The SVD-based techniques require no parameter tuning, which is a significant practical advantage over most state-of-the-art methods that require careful parameter optimization.

3. **Demonstrated effectiveness**: The method outperforms state-of-the-art on 2 out of 3 benchmark datasets, with the SVD-Reorg technique achieving the highest correlations with human judgments.

## Strengths

1. **Genuine conceptual novelty**: The paper introduces a completely fresh perspective on topic coherence measurement. While existing methods focus on pairwise word relationships or distributional similarity, TBuckets conceptualizes coherence as the thematic grouping of words within a topic. The examples provided (weather vs. Afghanistan topics) clearly demonstrate how this aligns with human intuition about coherence, which is a significant conceptual leap from existing work.

2. **Practical advantage of parameter-free approach**: The claim that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning is a major practical contribution. Most state-of-the-art methods require extensive parameter tuning (as evidenced by Roder et al., 2015), which makes them difficult to apply in practice. This is a significant advantage that would make the approach more accessible to practitioners.

3. **Strong empirical validation**: The results in Table 1 demonstrate clear superiority over state-of-the-art on 2 out of 3 datasets. The fact that the SVD-based techniques outperform methods that require parameter tuning is particularly compelling evidence for the value of the approach.

4. **Demonstrated utility in application**: The text classification experiment (Table 3) provides strong evidence that the coherence scores are practically useful, as selecting high-coherence topics leads to better classification performance in weakly supervised settings.

5. **Clear, intuitive explanation**: The paper's examples and step-by-step demonstration of the bucketing process make the approach easy to understand and visualize, which is a significant strength for a methodological paper.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical foundation**: While the paper provides intuitive examples, it doesn't establish a theoretical basis for why the bucket distribution should correlate with human judgments of coherence. The authors assume that a single dominant bucket indicates coherence, but don't provide a formal justification for this relationship.

2. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to just three datasets from the Roder et al. (2015) benchmark. A more comprehensive evaluation across diverse text domains (e.g., medical, social media, code) would strengthen the claims about the method's generalizability.

3. **Questionable "parameter-free" claim**: While the authors claim the SVD-based techniques are parameter-free, they use GloVe embeddings with a fixed 300-dimensional space. The choice of embedding dimensionality and the number of singular values used in SVD are effectively parameters that could affect results, but the paper doesn't address this.

4. **Ad-hoc reorganization step**: The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique introduces a dynamic threshold based on AWPS and AWES. While the authors claim this avoids parameter tuning, the specific formula (AWES - AWPS*AWES) seems somewhat arbitrary and lacks justification.

5. **Limited comparison with more recent methods**: The paper compares only with Roder et al. (2015), but doesn't compare with more recent coherence measures proposed after 2015 (e.g., methods from 2016-2023 that might have improved performance).

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter-free," but they rely on GloVe embeddings with a fixed dimensionality. How would the approach behave with different embedding dimensions, and why is 300 the appropriate choice?

2. The paper uses the same word embeddings for all datasets. How would the approach perform with domain-specific embeddings (e.g., using medical embeddings for the Genomics dataset)? Would this improve results?

3. The authors state that "a single large bucket" indicates coherence, but don't provide a formal justification for why this should correlate with human judgments. Can you provide a more rigorous theoretical explanation for this relationship?

4. The dynamic threshold in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (AWES - AWPS*AWES) seems ad-hoc. What evidence supports this specific formula over alternatives?

5. The paper evaluates on only three datasets. Could you provide results on additional datasets (e.g., from the 2015 Roder paper or more recent benchmarks) to demonstrate broader applicability?

## Recommendation

Accept

This paper presents a genuinely novel conceptual approach to topic coherence measurement that aligns well with human intuition about topic quality. The TBuckets framework is conceptually bold and offers a fresh perspective that moves beyond the standard statistical measures used in the field. While the methodological details have some minor weaknesses (particularly the theoretical foundation and evaluation scope), the novelty and practical advantages of the parameter-free SVD-based techniques outweigh these concerns. The strong empirical results and demonstrated utility in text classification provide compelling evidence for the value of the approach.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty and bold ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of topic modeling and topic coherence measurement. The TBuckets concept is sufficiently novel to warrant acceptance, and the practical advantages of the parameter-free techniques add significant value to the work.
