

# Peer Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper proposes a novel approach called TBuckets for measuring topic coherence in probabilistic topic models. The core idea is to group topic words into thematic buckets (logical groups), where a coherent topic should have a single dominant bucket with most words, while an incoherent topic would have multiple medium-sized buckets. The authors propose three techniques for bucket formation: clustering-based (TBuckets-Clustering), SVD-based (TBuckets-SVD), and SVD with reorganization (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). They evaluate these techniques on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) using Pearson's correlation with human-annotated coherence scores, claiming their methods outperform state-of-the-art approaches, particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg which they claim is completely parameter-free.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **A fresh perspective on topic coherence**: The bucket-based approach offers a novel conceptual framework for understanding topic coherence that differs from traditional pairwise similarity-based methods. The intuitive explanation of how humans perceive coherence through thematic grouping is compelling and well-motivated.

2. **A parameter-free method for topic coherence evaluation**: The SVD-based techniques (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) are claimed to require no parameter tuning, which represents a significant practical advantage over existing methods that require careful parameter selection.

3. **Demonstrated utility in text classification**: The paper shows that using TBuckets to filter high-quality topics for human annotation leads to improved text classification performance, providing a concrete application that validates the utility of their coherence measure.

## Strengths

1. **Strong empirical results with clear benchmarks**: The paper demonstrates clear improvements over state-of-the-art methods on two out of three datasets (NYT and 20NG), with TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieving Pearson's r of 0.837 on NYT and 0.863 on 20NG, compared to the best reported results of 0.806 and 0.859, respectively. This represents a meaningful improvement in the field. The use of standard datasets and evaluation metrics (Pearson's correlation) makes the results directly comparable to prior work.

2. **Practical parameter-free approach**: The claim that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning is a significant practical contribution. Most existing coherence measures require tuning multiple parameters (e.g., window size for PMI, number of words to consider), making the proposed method more accessible for practitioners. The paper provides evidence that their methods achieve better results without this tuning burden.

3. **Clear application to text classification**: The demonstration that using TBuckets to select high-quality topics for human annotation improves text classification performance (e.g., 68.47% vs 66.98% on PC vs MAC) is a compelling real-world validation of their coherence measure. This goes beyond mere evaluation of coherence metrics and shows tangible benefits in an important downstream task.

4. **Thorough analysis of the bucketing process**: The paper provides detailed examples of how the bucketing process works for both coherent and incoherent topics, effectively illustrating the core intuition behind the approach. The examples help readers understand why the bucket distribution correlates with human perceptions of coherence.

## Weaknesses

1. **Misrepresentation of "parameter-free" nature**: The paper's claim that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter free" is misleading. While it avoids explicit parameter tuning, the reorganization step relies on a dynamic threshold calculated as "AWES - AWPS*AWES", which depends on topic-specific properties (AWPS and AWES). This threshold calculation is topic-dependent, making it effectively a parameter that varies per topic. The paper should clarify this nuance and avoid claiming complete parameter-freeness.

2. **Inadequate comparison to recent work**: The paper only compares against Roder et al. (2015), but doesn't include more recent approaches to topic coherence evaluation. For example, they don't compare against Lau et al. (2014) who proposed NPMI-based coherence measures, which have since become standard. This weakens the claim that they're outperforming the "state-of-the-art," as the comparison is against an older benchmark.

3. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper provides an intuitive explanation for why bucketing might correlate with coherence, but lacks a deeper theoretical justification. Why should a single dominant bucket indicate coherence? How does this relate to other coherence measures? Without a more rigorous theoretical foundation, the approach risks appearing as a clever heuristic rather than a principled method.

4. **Insufficient analysis of failure cases**: The paper shows examples of coherent and incoherent topics, but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of cases where their method might fail. For instance, what happens with topics that have multiple equally important themes (e.g., a topic about "climate change" that covers both scientific aspects and political implications)? The paper assumes coherence corresponds to a single dominant theme, but this might not always be appropriate.

5. **Ambiguous human annotation protocol**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient details about how the human coherence scores were obtained. Without information about inter-annotator agreement, the reliability of the human scores is questionable. This is particularly important given that the paper uses these scores as the gold standard for evaluation.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter free," but the reorganization threshold depends on topic-specific values (AWPS and AWES). Can you clarify how this is truly parameter-free, and whether the topic-dependent threshold calculation should be considered a parameter?

2. The paper compares only to Roder et al. (2015), but doesn't include more recent work on topic coherence (e.g., Lau et al. 2014, or work by others after 2015). How would your method perform against these more recent approaches?

3. On the Genomics dataset, your best method (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) scores 0.68, while the state-of-the-art scores 0.773. What might explain this significant gap, and how does it relate to the nature of the Genomics dataset?

4. The paper states that "for TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, another topic coherence score, denoted as EVS, can be computed using each word's similarity with its corresponding eigenvector." However, Table 1 shows minimal differences between EVS and other metrics. Why is EVS not providing additional value, and is it really necessary to include in the evaluation?

5. The paper mentions that "MED-SPACE is considered to be one of the easiest for classification amongst the 20NG datasets." Can you elaborate on why this might be the case, and how it relates to topic coherence (e.g., do coherent topics appear more frequently in this dataset)?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper presents a novel and intuitive approach to topic coherence measurement with strong empirical results. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based methods is particularly valuable for practical applications. While the paper has some weaknesses in its claims about parameter-freeness and comparison to recent work, the core contribution represents a meaningful advancement in the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the concerns about parameter-freeness and to include more recent comparisons.

The paper's strength lies in its practical utility demonstrated through text classification, which goes beyond merely evaluating coherence metrics. The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field, and with minor clarifications, this paper would be an excellent addition to the literature.
