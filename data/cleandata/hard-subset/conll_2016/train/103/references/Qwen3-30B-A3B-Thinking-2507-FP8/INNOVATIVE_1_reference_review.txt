

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach called "TBuckets" for measuring topic quality in probabilistic topic models. The core idea is that human interpretation of topic coherence involves grouping topic words into thematic buckets, with a single large bucket indicating high coherence. The authors propose three techniques for creating these buckets: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify orthogonal sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a reorganization step to improve bucket assignments).

The main contributions, in order of strength as I see them:

1. **A fundamentally novel approach to topic coherence measurement** based on the human cognitive process of grouping words into thematic buckets, rather than relying on statistical or semantic similarity measures between words.
2. **Parameter-free techniques** (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that outperform state-of-the-art methods requiring extensive parameter tuning, with TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieving the best results on two of three datasets.
3. **Demonstrated practical utility** of the coherence measure in improving weakly supervised text classification by selecting high-quality topics for human annotation.

## Strengths

1. **Truly novel conceptual framework**: The paper introduces a fundamentally different perspective on topic coherence that is grounded in how humans actually assess topic quality. Most existing methods focus on pairwise word similarities or statistical measures, while this approach captures the human cognitive process of grouping words into thematic buckets. This is a bold, fresh direction that hasn't been explored before in topic coherence literature.

2. **Parameter-free advantage**: The fact that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning while outperforming state-of-the-art methods that require extensive parameter tuning is a significant practical advantage. This addresses a real pain point in topic modeling applications where parameter tuning is time-consuming and often requires expert knowledge.

3. **Strong empirical validation**: The method demonstrates clear superiority over state-of-the-art on two out of three standard datasets (NYT and 20NG), with TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieving the highest Pearson's r values. The authors also provide convincing evidence of the method's practical utility through the text classification application, where selecting high-coherence topics leads to significantly better classification performance.

4. **Clear theoretical justification**: The paper provides an intuitive explanation of why the bucket approach aligns with human judgment of topic coherence, using compelling examples that illustrate how coherent topics form single large buckets while incoherent topics form multiple medium-sized buckets. This human-centered explanation is stronger than many purely statistical approaches.

5. **Practical implementation**: The method is straightforward to implement and requires only standard resources (word embeddings and topic models), making it accessible for practitioners without requiring specialized knowledge or computational resources.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited dataset evaluation**: While the three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) are standard, the paper's evaluation is limited to these three datasets. More extensive evaluation across diverse domains would better establish the generalizability of the approach.

2. **Lack of detailed analysis of why SVD works better than clustering**: The paper demonstrates that SVD-based methods outperform clustering, but doesn't provide a deep theoretical or empirical explanation for why this is the case. A more thorough analysis of the SVD approach's advantages would strengthen the paper.

3. **Reorganization step feels somewhat ad-hoc**: While the reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) improves performance, the dynamic threshold calculation based on topic-specific metrics (AWPS and AWES) is somewhat ad-hoc and could benefit from more theoretical justification.

4. **Limited comparison with very recent methods**: The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015) as the state-of-the-art, but doesn't compare with more recent coherence measures published after 2015 that might have improved upon the methods they evaluated.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the TBuckets approach "can be characterized through three techniques," but doesn't explain why these three techniques were chosen over other possible approaches. Could you elaborate on the design space you considered for creating buckets?

2. The paper states that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter free," but the dynamic threshold calculation depends on topic-specific metrics (AWPS and AWES). Could you clarify how this is considered parameter-free, and whether these metrics could be computed in a way that doesn't depend on the specific topic?

3. In Figure 1, the performance of TBuckets-Clustering varies with Max Dist, but the paper doesn't discuss why this variation occurs. Could you provide more insight into the relationship between the clustering parameter and topic coherence?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The TBuckets approach introduces a genuinely novel perspective on topic coherence measurement that is both theoretically grounded in human cognition and empirically validated. The parameter-free nature of two of the techniques is a significant practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods. While the evaluation could be expanded, the core idea is bold, novel, and makes a significant contribution to the field. The paper clearly articulates why this approach is different from and superior to existing methods, and the practical application to text classification demonstrates real-world utility. Given my review criteria that prioritize novel ideas over methodological perfection, I believe this paper should be accepted.
