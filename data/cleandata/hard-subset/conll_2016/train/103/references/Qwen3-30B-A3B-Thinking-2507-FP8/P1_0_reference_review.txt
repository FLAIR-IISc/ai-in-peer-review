

# Peer Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach called "TBuckets" for measuring topic coherence in topic models (specifically LDA-based models). The core idea is to group topic words into thematic buckets, where a topic with a single dominant bucket (containing most words) is considered more coherent. The authors propose three techniques for creating these buckets: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify orthogonal sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (adding a reorganization step to improve bucket quality).

The main contributions of this work are:

1. **A novel coherence measure based on thematic grouping of topic words** - The paper introduces an intuitive approach to topic coherence that aligns with how humans perceive topic coherence by grouping words into thematic buckets.

2. **Parameter-free coherence measurement techniques** - The authors claim that their SVD-based approaches (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) are completely parameter-free, which is a significant advantage over existing methods that require careful parameter tuning.

3. **Demonstrated utility in practical applications** - The paper shows that using TBuckets for topic selection in weakly supervised text classification leads to improved performance, particularly for challenging classification tasks.

## Strengths

1. **Novel Perspective on Topic Coherence**: The paper offers a fresh perspective on topic coherence by modeling how humans actually group words into thematic buckets. The examples provided (e.g., the weather topic vs. the Afghanistan/Wikileaks topic) clearly demonstrate how the bucketing approach captures human intuition about topic coherence. This is a valuable conceptual contribution that complements existing statistical approaches to topic coherence.

2. **Parameter-Free Techniques with Strong Performance**: The claim that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning is well-supported by the experimental results. The paper demonstrates that these methods outperform the state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets (NYT and 20NG) without any parameter tuning, which is a significant practical advantage over existing methods that require careful parameter selection. This is particularly important for real-world applications where parameter tuning can be time-consuming and require expertise.

3. **Practical Application in Text Classification**: The paper provides a compelling real-world application of their coherence measure by demonstrating its utility in weakly supervised text classification. The results in Table 3 show that using TBuckets to select high-coherence topics leads to better classification performance across multiple datasets, with particularly significant improvements for challenging classification tasks like PC vs MAC. This demonstrates clear practical value beyond just theoretical contribution.

4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation**: The paper is well-organized with clear explanations of the methodology, including detailed examples of the bucketing process. The experimental setup is transparent, using standard datasets and evaluation metrics that allow for direct comparison with prior work.

5. **Thorough Evaluation of the Reorganization Step**: The authors provide a thoughtful analysis of why the reorganization step is necessary (with examples where words are misplaced in the initial SVD-based bucketing), and they develop a principled method for reorganization that doesn't require additional parameters. This shows careful consideration of the limitations of their initial approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Clear Differentiation from Existing Methods**: While the paper presents the bucketing approach as novel, it doesn't sufficiently differentiate it from existing coherence measures that also use semantic similarity between words. For example, CUSheffield (Aletras and Stevenson, 2013) uses semantic similarity between topic words, which is conceptually similar to the bucketing approach. The paper doesn't clearly explain how TBuckets differs from or improves upon these existing approaches beyond the specific implementation details.

2. **Limited Dataset Diversity**: The evaluation is restricted to three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics), which have been extensively used in topic modeling literature. The authors don't evaluate on more diverse or domain-specific datasets, making it difficult to assess whether the approach generalizes to other domains or text types. This limits the generalizability of the claimed superiority of TBuckets.

3. **Insufficient Comparison with Recent Work**: The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015), but doesn't compare with more recent topic coherence measures published after 2015. The state-of-the-art in topic coherence has continued to evolve since 2015, and it's unclear how TBuckets would compare with these newer methods. This weakens the claim that TBuckets outperforms the "state-of-the-art."

4. **Questionable Justification for Reorganization Threshold**: The paper introduces a dynamic threshold for reorganization (AWES - AWPS*AWES) but provides limited justification for this specific formula. The authors claim it's "not decided empirically" but don't provide sufficient theoretical or empirical evidence to support why this particular formula is optimal. This makes the reorganization step appear somewhat arbitrary.

5. **Lack of Ablation Study**: The paper doesn't provide a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of each component of the approach. For example, it's unclear how much the reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) contributes to the performance improvement over TBuckets-SVD. Without this, it's difficult to assess which aspects of the method are most valuable.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter-free," but the reorganization step uses a threshold that depends on topic-specific values (AWES and AWPS). How is this threshold calculated in practice, and why is it considered parameter-free? If it depends on the topic under consideration, doesn't this introduce some form of topic-specific parameter?

2. The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015), but doesn't discuss how TBuckets compares with more recent topic coherence measures published after 2015. Could the authors provide a comparison with some of these more recent methods (e.g., those from 2016-2023)?

3. The paper states that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets, but the results on Genomics are weaker. Could the authors provide an analysis of why TBuckets performs less well on Genomics compared to the other datasets, and whether this is related to the specific characteristics of the Genomics domain?

4. The paper claims that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg requires no parameter tuning, but the clustering-based approach (TBuckets-Clustering) requires a "Max Dist" parameter. The paper shows results for different values of Max Dist in Figure 1, but doesn't discuss how to choose this parameter in practice. How should this parameter be set for new datasets, and does the paper provide guidance for this?

5. In Table 2, the human coherence scores are between 1 and 3, while TBuckets-SVD-Reorg scores are normalized to between 0 and 1. How exactly was the normalization done, and how does this affect the correlation scores? Could the authors provide the exact normalization formula used?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and makes a meaningful contribution to the topic coherence literature. The practical demonstration of how coherence measurement can improve text classification is particularly valuable. However, the paper would be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing a more comprehensive comparison with recent work and a clearer explanation of how TBuckets differs from existing semantic similarity-based approaches.
