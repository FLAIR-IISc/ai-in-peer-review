### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core idea is to mimic the human process of grouping a topic's words into thematic clusters, or "buckets". The authors hypothesize that coherent topics will result in one large, dominant bucket, while incoherent topics will fragment into multiple smaller buckets. The paper proposes three methods for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering and two novel methods based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the topic words' embeddings. The final coherence score is derived from the properties of the largest resulting bucket.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel conceptual framework for topic coherence:** The "bucketing" metaphor is an intuitive and fresh alternative to the prevalent paradigm of aggregating pairwise word similarity scores (like PMI-based measures).
2.  **An effective SVD-based implementation:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD and its reorganized variant (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) are novel applications of SVD in this context. These methods achieve state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two of three standard benchmark datasets.
3.  **A parameter-free coherence metric:** A key claimed advantage of the SVD-based methods is their parameter-free nature, which makes them more practical and easier to deploy compared to methods that require extensive tuning, such as the framework proposed by Röder et al. (2015).
4.  **Demonstration of extrinsic utility:** The paper includes an application study showing that using the proposed metric to pre-select higher-quality topic models can lead to improved performance in a downstream weakly-supervised text classification task.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this work is its performance. The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves a new state-of-the-art on the NYT and 20NG datasets, outperforming the best configurations from the comprehensive study by Röder et al. (2015). This is a significant result, demonstrating the method's effectiveness.
2.  **Novelty and Intuition:** The core idea is elegant and well-motivated. The SVD approach to identify orthogonal "sub-themes" within a topic is a clever way to operationalize the bucketing intuition. This provides a distinct and valuable new direction for coherence research, moving beyond the simple aggregation of pairwise similarities.
3.  **Practicality and Simplicity:** The claimed parameter-free nature of the SVD-based methods is a major practical advantage. Users of topic models often need a reliable, off-the-shelf coherence metric without wanting to perform a complex search over a configuration space (as in Röder et al.) or tune parameters on a held-out set of human judgments. This work provides a strong candidate for such a metric.
4.  **Good Experimental Design:** The evaluation is sound. The authors use standard datasets and the established evaluation protocol (Pearson's correlation with human scores) from prior work, allowing for direct and fair comparison. The inclusion of the text classification experiment adds significant value by showing that the metric is not just correlated with human perception but is also useful for a practical downstream application.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Methodological Justification:** The paper's main weakness is the lack of deep justification for some key methodological choices.
    *   **Choice of V Matrix in SVD:** The paper states that the V matrix is used because it captures "feature-feature interactions" and represents "orthogonal d dimensional directions." This is a high-level description of SVD, not a specific justification for why these particular eigenvectors should serve as ideal "bucket identifiers" for semantic clustering of topic words. A more principled explanation connecting the properties of ATA to the task of semantic grouping is needed.
    *   **Reorganization Threshold Formula:** The formula for the dynamic threshold in the SVD-Reorg step, `AWES - AWPS/AWES`, feels arbitrary and heuristic. While the goal of a data-dependent threshold is commendable, the paper provides no rationale for this specific mathematical form. Why this combination of terms? Have alternatives been explored? Without this justification, it looks like a form of hidden parameter tuning where the "parameter" is the formula itself.

2.  **Inconsistent Performance and Lack of Analysis:** The method performs significantly worse than the state-of-the-art on the Genomics dataset (0.691 vs 0.773). The paper reports this result but fails to discuss or analyze it. This is a crucial omission. Is this failure due to the nature of the domain, where the semantics are not well-captured by general-purpose GloVe embeddings? Does it reveal a fundamental limitation of the bucketing approach for certain types of topics? Acknowledging and hypothesizing about this negative result is essential for understanding the method's boundaries.

3.  **Overstated "Parameter-Free" Claim:** While TBuckets-SVD itself is parameter-free, the best-performing model, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, relies on the aforementioned threshold formula, which is a fixed modeling choice that functions like a hyperparameter. Furthermore, the choice of word embeddings (model, dimensionality) is an extremely influential, untuned parameter that is external to the model but critical to its success. The claim of being "completely parameter less" should be softened to more accurately reflect these dependencies.

4.  **Limited Scope of Experiments:** All experiments are conducted on topics with exactly N=10 words. It is well known that the performance of coherence metrics can be sensitive to N. An analysis showing how the correlation scores vary as N changes (e.g., for N=5, 15, 20) would make the evaluation much more robust and provide a better understanding of the method's behavior.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the theoretical or empirical reasoning behind choosing the eigenvectors of ATA (the V matrix) as the basis for the buckets? What is the intuition that makes these vectors better representations of sub-themes than, for example, running K-Means on the word embeddings directly?
2.  The formula for the dynamic threshold in the reorganization step appears ad-hoc. Could you provide the rationale for this specific formulation (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`)? Did you experiment with simpler or alternative formulations, and if so, how did they perform?
3.  What is your main hypothesis for the significant underperformance of your methods on the Genomics dataset? Do you believe this points to a limitation of the underlying embeddings for specialized domains, or a weakness in the TBuckets approach itself when faced with technical vocabulary?
4.  The application study in Section 4.3 is a nice addition. However, in the MED-SPACE dataset, the topic sets with the lowest coherence scores achieve slightly better classification accuracy than the highest coherence sets. While this might be noise, it seems counterintuitive. Do you have any explanation for this specific result?
