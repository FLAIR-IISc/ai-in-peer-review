### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core idea is that a coherent topic's words should belong to a single overarching theme. The method operationalizes this by grouping the top words of a topic into "buckets" of semantically related words. A topic is considered coherent if most of its words fall into a single large bucket. The authors propose three techniques for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering of word embeddings (`TBuckets-Clustering`), a second using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to identify sub-themes (`TBuckets-SVD`), and a third that refines the SVD-based buckets through a reorganization step (`TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`). The proposed methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, and their utility is demonstrated in a downstream weakly-supervised text classification task.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and intuitive framework for topic coherence:** The "bucketing" approach is a conceptually clear and different way to think about topic coherence, moving beyond the standard pairwise word similarity aggregation methods (like PMI). The authors successfully translate this intuition into a working computational model.
2.  **A high-performing, parameter-free coherence metric:** The proposed `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method achieves state-of-the-art results on two of the three benchmark datasets. Crucially, this SVD-based approach is presented as parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage over the current state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015), which relies on exploring a vast and computationally expensive configuration space.
3.  **Demonstration of practical utility:** The paper includes an extrinsic evaluation (Section 4.3) showing that the proposed coherence metric can be used to select better topic sets for human annotation in a weakly-supervised classification pipeline, leading to improved downstream performance. This adds significant weight to the paper by showing the metric's value beyond intrinsic correlation with human scores.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Simplicity:** The underlying idea of TBuckets is elegant and easy to grasp. The dry-run examples in Section 3 effectively illustrate how grouping words into thematic buckets can distinguish between coherent and incoherent topics. This provides a strong, intuitive foundation for the more technical methods that follow.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method shows very strong results, outperforming the best-performing settings from a comprehensive study by Röder et al. (2015) on the NYT and 20NG datasets. Achieving state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments validates the effectiveness of the proposed bucketing framework.
3.  **Parameter-Free Method:** One of the most significant strengths is that the best-performing methods, `TBuckets-SVD` and `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`, require no parameter tuning. This is a major advantage for practitioners, as many existing coherence metrics require setting parameters (e.g., window size) or, in the case of the SOTA baseline, searching a large space of configurations. The authors' design of a dynamic, data-driven threshold in the reorganization step is a commendable effort to maintain this parameter-free quality.
4.  **Compelling Application Task:** The text classification experiment provides strong evidence for the metric's usefulness. By showing that selecting topic sets with higher coherence scores leads to better classification accuracy, the authors demonstrate that their metric captures a notion of quality that has a tangible impact on a downstream application. This is a valuable and often-missing piece in papers on evaluation metrics.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Justification of the SVD Method:** The motivation for using SVD is not fully convincing. The paper states that the V matrix (right-singular vectors) provides "orthogonal d dimensional directions we are interested in" which represent "sub-themes". This intuition is not well-justified. Why should the principal components of the word-embedding covariance matrix (AᵀA) correspond to meaningful thematic groups? A more detailed explanation, perhaps with a geometric or algebraic argument, is needed to make this core mechanism clearer and more convincing.
2.  **Complexity and Ad-Hoc Nature of the Reorganization Step:** While effective, the `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method introduces significant complexity that undermines the elegance of the initial SVD approach. The rules for reorganization are based on four different "pull" metrics and a complex, dynamically computed threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`). The justification for this specific formula feels ad-hoc ("The ratio...denotes flexibility...The subtraction from AWES manages the gap..."). This makes the final, best-performing method less transparent and potentially difficult to reproduce or build upon.
3.  **Inconsistent Results in Application Task:** In the text classification experiment (Table 3), the result for the `MEDICAL vs SPACE` dataset is counter-intuitive: the sets of topics with the *lowest* coherence scores yield slightly better classification performance than the sets with the *highest* scores. While the authors suggest this is because the dataset is "easy", this result still weakens the overall claim that higher coherence (as measured by their metric) consistently leads to better downstream performance. This anomaly deserves a more thorough analysis.
4.  **Minor Presentation and Writing Issues:** The paper could be improved with careful proofreading. There are several grammatical errors, awkward phrasings, and formatting issues (e.g., the reference list has inconsistent formatting and odd characters like question marks). In the dry run example for the incoherent topic in Section 3, there appears to be a copy-paste error in the fourth step. These issues, while minor, detract from the overall quality and professionalism of the submission.
