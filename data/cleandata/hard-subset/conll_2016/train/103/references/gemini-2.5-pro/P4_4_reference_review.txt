**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics generated by probabilistic topic models. The central idea is that a coherent topic's words can be grouped into a single large thematic "bucket," while an incoherent topic's words will be distributed across multiple smaller buckets. The authors propose three methods to implement this bucketing: an agglomerative clustering approach (TBuckets-Clustering), a method using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on the topic words' embeddings (TBuckets-SVD), and a refined version that reorganizes the SVD-generated buckets (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The final coherence score is calculated based on the properties of the largest resulting bucket. The methods are evaluated by calculating the Pearson correlation between their scores and human coherence judgments on three standard datasets. The results show that the parameter-free SVD-based methods, particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, achieve state-of-the-art performance. The paper also demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed metric in a weakly-supervised text classification task, showing that selecting topic models with higher coherence leads to better downstream performance.

**Contributions**

1.  **A novel, parameter-free topic coherence metric (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments.** The core technical contribution is the use of SVD on a matrix of word embeddings to identify orthogonal sub-themes within a topic. Words are assigned to "buckets" represented by these sub-themes (eigenvectors). A subsequent self-adjusting reorganization step refines these buckets. This approach outperforms prior work on two of three benchmark datasets without requiring any parameter tuning.
2.  **An extrinsic, application-based evaluation demonstrating the practical utility of the proposed metric.** The paper includes an experiment where the coherence metric is used to select the best set of topics from multiple LDA runs for a weakly-supervised text classification task. The results show that using higher-coherence topics for human annotation leads to improved classification accuracy, providing strong evidence for the metric's real-world value beyond intrinsic correlation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its experimental results. The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art Pearson correlation with human judgments on the 20NG and NYT datasets, outperforming the best results reported in the comprehensive study by Röder et al. (2015). This provides clear and compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2.  **Parameter-Free Method:** A significant advantage of the TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg methods is their lack of tunable parameters. This contrasts with many existing coherence measures, including the framework of Röder et al. (2015), which require exploring a large configuration space to find an optimal setting. The dynamic threshold used in the reorganization step, which is derived from properties of the topic itself, is particularly well-designed and enhances the method's robustness and ease of use.
3.  **Novel and Technically Sound Approach:** The conceptual framework of "bucketing" topic words is intuitive, and its implementation via SVD is novel in the context of topic coherence evaluation. The use of SVD to decompose the semantic space of a topic's words into orthogonal components (the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix) is a technically sound way to identify distinct sub-themes. The subsequent reorganization step is well-motivated by qualitative examples and empirically shown to improve performance.
4.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The paper reinforces its claims with an extrinsic evaluation in Section 4.3. By showing that the proposed coherence score can be used to select better topic models for a downstream classification task, the authors demonstrate that their metric captures a notion of quality that is not just correlated with human perception but is also beneficial for practical applications.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Clarity and Justification for the Reorganization Threshold:** The description of the reorganization step in Section 3.1 is difficult to follow, particularly the formulation of the dynamic threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`). The paper provides a high-level intuition but does not offer a principled justification for this specific mathematical form. The claims that the ratio `AWPS/AWES` denotes "flexibility" and the subtraction "manages the gap" are not rigorously substantiated. The technical soundness of this crucial component would be stronger with a more detailed derivation or an ablation study demonstrating its superiority over simpler thresholding schemes.
2.  **Ambiguity in Final Score Calculation:** Section 3.2 describes several potential scoring functions (size of the largest bucket, a word-order weighted score `WL`, an eigenvector similarity score `EVS`) and mentions that for SVD-based methods, these scores are weighted "with the square of the highest singular value." However, the paper does not commit to a single, final, principled metric. Table 1 reports results for multiple scoring variants, making it unclear which one constitutes the definitive "TBuckets-SVD-Reorg" metric. The rationale for weighting by the squared highest singular value is also not explained, making this part of the method appear somewhat ad-hoc and designed to maximize performance on the test sets.
3.  **Insufficient Technical Detail for SVD Implementation:** The paper states that for a topic with `n` words, it uses the "first `n` eigenvectors as bucket identifiers". Given that SVD on an `n x d` word embedding matrix (where `n=10`, `d=300`) produces `d` eigenvectors in the `V` matrix, the choice of using exactly `n` is not justified. A more standard approach would be to select the number of eigenvectors based on the amount of variance explained by the corresponding singular values. This lack of justification makes the choice seem arbitrary and could impact the method's generalizability.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed justification for the specific formula used for the dynamic threshold in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`)? An ablation study comparing this formulation to simpler alternatives (e.g., a constant threshold, or using only `AWPS` or `AWES`) would strengthen the paper's claims.
2.  In the SVD-based methods, you state that you use the "first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers". Could you clarify the reasoning behind choosing exactly `n` (the number of topic words) eigenvectors, rather than a different number determined by, for instance, the spectrum of singular values?
3.  Your final results in Table 1 are reported for multiple scoring metrics (size, WL, EVS, etc.), and you mention a weighting scheme based on the highest singular value. Could you specify which combination constitutes your single proposed metric? Furthermore, could you provide the intuition or a theoretical justification for why weighting the score by the square of the highest singular value is beneficial for measuring topic coherence?
