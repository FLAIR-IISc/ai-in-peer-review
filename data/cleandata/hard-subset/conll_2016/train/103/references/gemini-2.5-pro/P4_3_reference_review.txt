### Summary

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the quality and coherence of topics generated by probabilistic topic models. The core idea is to partition the top words of a given topic into semantic groups, or "buckets". The authors hypothesize that a coherent topic will be dominated by a single large bucket, whereas an incoherent topic will be fragmented into multiple smaller buckets. The coherence score is then derived from the properties of the largest resulting bucket.

The paper explores three methods for this bucketing process:
1.  **TBuckets-Clustering:** Uses agglomerative clustering on the word embeddings of the topic words.
2.  **TBuckets-SVD:** Applies Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to the matrix of word embeddings for the topic words. The right singular vectors are used as identifiers for orthogonal "sub-themes" (buckets), and each word is assigned to the bucket whose identifier is most similar.
3.  **TBuckets-SVD-Reorg:** An extension of the SVD method that applies a post-processing step to reorganize words between buckets, aiming to correct initial misassignments and improve the coherence of the largest bucket.

The methods are evaluated by calculating the Pearson correlation between their generated coherence scores and human judgments on three standard datasets. The results show that the proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method outperforms the state-of-the-art on two of the three datasets. Additionally, the paper demonstrates the practical utility of the metric in a weakly-supervised text classification task, showing that selecting topic sets with higher coherence scores leads to improved classification performance.

### Contributions

1.  **A novel, parameter-free method for topic coherence evaluation:** The paper proposes TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, which are based on applying Singular Value Decomposition to the embedding matrix of a topic's words. This is a novel approach for identifying latent sub-themes within a topic. The reorganization variant, in particular, achieves state-of-the-art performance while being presented as parameter-free, a significant practical advantage over methods that require extensive tuning.
2.  **Strong empirical validation against human judgments:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method is shown to achieve a higher correlation with human coherence judgments than previously reported state-of-the-art methods on the 20 Newsgroups and New York Times datasets. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed technique.
3.  **Demonstration of downstream task improvement:** The paper includes an application of the coherence metric to weakly-supervised text classification. The experiment shows that using the TBuckets score to select higher-quality sets of topics for human annotation can lead to better final classification accuracy. This successfully demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed metric beyond intrinsic evaluation.

### Strengths

1.  **State-of-the-art Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this work is its strong empirical results. As shown in Table 1, the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves Pearson correlation scores of 0.837 on NYT and 0.869 on 20NG. These results surpass the best-performing settings reported by Röder et al. (2015) (0.806 and 0.859, respectively). Achieving a new state-of-the-art on established benchmarks is a significant accomplishment.
2.  **Novel and Intuitive Approach:** The central idea of partitioning topic words into semantic "buckets" is intuitive and provides a clear conceptual model for what makes a topic coherent. The use of SVD to identify orthogonal sub-themes within the semantic space defined by the topic's words is a novel and technically interesting way to automate this partitioning, moving beyond simple pairwise similarity calculations common in other coherence metrics.
3.  **Parameter-Free Method:** The SVD-based methods are presented as being parameter-free. This is a substantial advantage over many existing coherence measures, including the complex framework of Röder et al. (2015), which requires selecting from a large configuration space of parameters (confirmation measures, aggregation methods, etc.). A robust, parameter-free method is easier to deploy and more likely to generalize across different domains and topic models, which significantly increases its practical value.
4.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 provides a compelling use case. By demonstrating that pre-filtering topic sets with their coherence score can improve the performance of a downstream task, the authors show that their metric is not just an academic exercise but has tangible benefits. The performance gap between the "Highest 5" and "Lowest 5" coherence sets in Table 3, especially for the more difficult datasets, strongly supports this claim.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for the Reorganization Step:** The paper's best-performing method, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, relies on a crucial reorganization step. However, the formulation of the dynamic threshold used for this step (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) is not well-justified. The description in Section 3.1 is based on intuition ("The ratio... denotes flexibility", "The subtraction... manages the gap") rather than a formal or empirical derivation. This makes the crucial component of the best method appear ad-hoc and undermines the technical soundness. While the threshold is dynamic and not tuned, its specific mathematical form lacks a clear rationale.
2.  **Lack of Clarity in the SVD Method Details:** The paper's explanation for why SVD is appropriate is somewhat brief. It states that the V matrix provides orthogonal directions representing "distinct bucket identifiers". However, it does not clearly justify why the right singular vectors of the word embedding matrix are the ideal representation for these sub-themes, or why exactly *n* eigenvectors should be used for *n* words. A more thorough explanation of the underlying theory would strengthen the paper's technical contribution.
3.  **Potential for Overly Optimistic Results in Clustering Baseline:** For the TBuckets-Clustering method, the performance is highly dependent on the `Max Dist` parameter, as shown in Figure 1. In Table 1, a single `Max Dist` value is reported in parentheses for each result. The paper does not specify how this value was selected. If these values were chosen post-hoc by picking the peak performance on the test set, then the reported correlation scores are optimistic and not comparable to methods that do not require such tuning. This lack of detail hinders the reproducibility and fair assessment of this baseline.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The formula for the dynamic threshold in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) appears to be a key element of its success. Could you provide a more rigorous justification for this specific formulation? Have alternative formulations been explored, and if so, how did they perform in comparison?
2.  For the TBuckets-Clustering results presented in Table 1, how were the `Max Dist` values (shown in parentheses) selected for each dataset and linkage configuration? Were these values tuned on a separate development set, or do they represent the peak performance on the test set itself?
3.  In the TBuckets-SVD method, you use the top *n* right singular vectors as bucket identifiers for the *n* topic words. What is the reasoning behind this one-to-one correspondence? Have you considered using a different number of vectors, for instance, by selecting only those corresponding to singular values above a certain threshold, to determine the number of "true" sub-themes?
