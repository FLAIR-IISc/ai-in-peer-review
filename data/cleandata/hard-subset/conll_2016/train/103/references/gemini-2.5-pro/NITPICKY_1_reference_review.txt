**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core intuition is that a coherent topic consists of words that can be grouped into a single large thematic "bucket." The authors propose three methods to implement this bucketing: an agglomerative clustering-based approach (TBuckets-Clustering), a method using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to identify sub-themes (TBuckets-SVD), and an extension that reorganizes the SVD-generated buckets (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The final coherence score is derived from properties of the largest bucket. The authors evaluate their methods by correlating the generated scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, claiming state-of-the-art performance. They also present an extrinsic evaluation showing that their metric can be used to select better topic sets for a weakly-supervised text classification task.

**Contributions**

1.  **A Novel Intuition for Topic Coherence:** The central idea of modeling coherence by grouping topic words into thematic "buckets" is intuitive, simple, and psychologically plausible. It provides a new perspective compared to existing methods that rely on pairwise word similarities.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The proposed `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two out of three benchmark datasets. The proposed methods are shown to be highly competitive with existing, more complex approaches.
3.  **An Application to an Extrinsic Task:** The paper demonstrates a practical use case for the proposed coherence metric by showing it can improve a downstream weakly-supervised text classification task by filtering out low-quality topic models before human annotation.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Intuitiveness of the Core Idea:** The "bucketing" approach is an elegant and simple concept for evaluating topic quality. It captures the human process of identifying a common theme among a set of words. This conceptual simplicity is a significant strength.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported correlations in Table 1 are impressive. `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` surpasses the best results from a comprehensive study by Röder et al. (2015) on the NYT and 20NG datasets. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3.  **Parameter-Free Claim:** The SVD-based methods are presented as being parameter-free, which is a highly desirable quality for an evaluation metric. If true, this would make the methods easily applicable without the need for expensive parameter tuning, a major advantage over many existing coherence measures.
4.  **Demonstration of Practical Utility:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is a valuable addition. It moves beyond intrinsic correlation and shows that the metric can have a tangible impact on a downstream application by helping to select higher-quality topic sets, potentially saving human annotation effort and improving performance.

**Weaknesses**

While the paper presents a promising direction, it is marred by a substantial number of methodological gaps, presentation flaws, and inconsistencies that collectively undermine the work's credibility.

1.  **Lack of Justification for Key Methodological Choices:** Several core components of the proposed methods are presented as ad-hoc heuristics without adequate justification.
    *   The "dynamic threshold" in `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` (Section 3.1) is based on the formula `AWES - AWPS/AWES`. This formula is highly specific and non-obvious, yet no theoretical or empirical justification is provided for its form. The claim that this makes the method "parameter-free" is questionable; it merely replaces a tunable parameter with a complex, unmotivated heuristic.
    *   The coherence scores for SVD-based methods are weighted by the "square of the highest singular value" (Section 3.2). The rationale for using the *square* and for using *only the highest* singular value is completely absent.
    *   In `TBuckets-SVD`, the authors state they use the "first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers." Given that the matrix `V` is `d x d`, it is unclear what "first n" refers to and why `n` (the number of topic words) is the correct number of identifiers to use. This critical detail is not explained.

2.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:** The manuscript exhibits a lack of care that is not acceptable for a conference publication.
    *   The **References** section is in disarray. It contains numerous formatting errors, inconsistent citation styles (e.g., `Blei.,? 2012`), strange artifacts (`AlSumait et al\.,?`), and duplicate entries. This suggests a rushed preparation and lack of proofreading.
    *   There is a clear copy-paste error in the example dry run in Section 3. For the non-coherent topic, the first step for `Word Seen: official` incorrectly lists `Bucket-1: {storm, weather}`.
    *   In-text citation styles are inconsistent (e.g., `(David M. Blei, 2007)` vs. the standard `(Blei, 2007)`).

3.  **Flaws in Experimental Analysis and Reporting:** The analysis of the text classification experiment is not rigorous.
    *   In Table 3, the `S2 (Highest 5)` strategy yields a lower macro-F1 score than the `S1` baseline for the `POLITICS vs RELIGION` dataset (85.23 vs 85.61). The authors completely ignore this negative result in their discussion, which constitutes biased reporting.
    *   The experimental comparison between S1 and S2 is questionable. S1 is the average of 5 runs, while S2 selects the best 5 runs from a pool of 10. A more direct comparison would be to compare the average of the 10 runs to the average of the best 5, or to compare the average of 5 random runs to the average of another 5 random runs. The current setup potentially inflates the benefit of the selection strategy.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Can you provide a detailed justification for the specific mathematical form of the dynamic threshold used in `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`? What alternatives were considered, and why is `AWES - AWPS/AWES` the optimal choice?
2.  Please explain the rationale for weighting the final coherence score by the *square* of the *highest* singular value. What is the intuition behind this, and what is the effect of this weighting on the results?
3.  In Section 4.3, your proposed topic selection strategy (S2) underperformed the baseline (S1) on one of the four datasets. Could you please address this result and discuss its implications for the general applicability of your metric?
4.  Could you clarify the experimental design for the text classification task? Why was the S1 baseline calculated over 5 runs, while the S2 selection was made from a larger pool of 10 runs?
