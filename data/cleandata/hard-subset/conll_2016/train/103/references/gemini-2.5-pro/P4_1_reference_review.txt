### Summary

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core intuition is that a coherent topic's constituent words should group into a single, dominant semantic "bucket". The paper proposes three methods to implement this idea: one based on agglomerative clustering of word embeddings (TBuckets-Clustering), and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the topic words' embedding matrix. The SVD-based methods, TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, identify orthogonal sub-themes within a topic and assign words to them. The final coherence score is derived from the size and composition of the largest resulting bucket. The methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three benchmark datasets, where the proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method shows state-of-the-art performance. The paper also demonstrates the practical utility of the metric by using it to select higher-quality topic sets for a weakly supervised text classification task, leading to improved downstream performance.

### Contributions

1.  A novel conceptual framework (TBuckets) for topic coherence evaluation that models the thematic structure of a topic by partitioning its words into semantic groups. This contrasts with common approaches that rely on aggregating pairwise word similarity scores.
2.  The development of two parameter-free coherence metrics (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) based on applying SVD to the word embedding matrix of a single topic. These methods achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive correlation with human judgments without requiring parameter tuning.
3.  An empirical demonstration of the proposed metric's practical utility. The authors show that selecting topic models based on their TBuckets coherence score can improve performance on a downstream weakly supervised text classification task.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Approach**: The "bucketing" concept is an original and well-motivated approach to measuring topic coherence. It directly operationalizes the idea that a good topic should be focused on a single theme. This provides a new and valuable perspective on the problem, moving beyond simple averages of pairwise similarities to consider the global structure of the topic's word set.
2.  **Development of a Parameter-Free Method**: A key strength is that the best-performing methods, TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, are parameter-free. This is a significant practical advantage over many coherence measures that depend on external corpora choices, window sizes, or other tunable parameters. The comprehensive framework they compare against (Röder et al., 2015) highlights the complexity of navigating a large parameter space, a problem this work effectively sidesteps. This makes the proposed method more robust and easier for practitioners to adopt.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance**: The paper presents compelling empirical results. On two out of three standard benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG), the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method surpasses the state-of-the-art performance reported by Röder et al. (2015). Achieving higher correlation with human judgments on established benchmarks provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness.
4.  **Demonstration of Downstream Utility**: The authors include an application experiment in weakly supervised text classification (Section 4.3). By showing that selecting topic sets with higher coherence scores leads to better classification accuracy, they demonstrate that the proposed metric has practical value beyond simply mimicking human ratings. This strengthens the paper's overall contribution and impact.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Justification for the Reorganization Heuristics**: The `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method, which provides the best results, relies on a set of reorganization rules and a dynamically computed threshold. The formula for this threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) is presented without a clear theoretical or empirical derivation. The logic is described intuitively, but the specific mathematical form appears ad-hoc. Without a more rigorous justification for these heuristics, the mechanism driving the performance improvement over the simpler SVD method is not fully transparent and may be difficult to generalize or build upon.
2.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis of SVD Interpretation**: The paper posits that the right singular vectors of the topic word embedding matrix represent orthogonal "sub-themes". This is a core and interesting claim, but it is not substantiated with qualitative or analytical evidence. An analysis of the eigenvectors for a few example topics could have provided valuable insight into what these latent dimensions capture and would have strengthened the technical contribution by validating the underlying intuition of the SVD-based approach.
3.  **Unclear Motivation for Scoring Details**: The paper lacks clarity on some details of the scoring function. In Section 3.2, it is stated that scores are weighted by the "square of the highest singular value" for the SVD-based methods. The motivation for this specific weighting scheme is not provided. It is unclear how critical this step is to the final performance and why this particular function was chosen over other alternatives. This omission affects the method's transparency and reproducibility.
4.  **Potential Selection Bias in Application Experiment**: The experimental design in the text classification task (Section 4.3) may contain selection bias. Strategy S2 selects the top 5 out of 10 runs based on the coherence metric and compares their average performance to S1's average of 5 random runs. By definition, S2 involves a selection step that S1 lacks. A more direct evaluation would be to compare the average accuracy of the 5 "best" runs against the average accuracy of the 5 "worst" runs (which the authors do report, strengthening their point) and also against the average of all 10 runs to show a net improvement over simply running the model more times.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed justification for the specific formula `AWES - AWPS/AWES` used as the dynamic threshold in the reorganization step? Was this formula derived empirically from a development set, or does it have a stronger theoretical grounding?
2.  In Section 3.2, the final coherence scores for the SVD-based methods are weighted by the "square of the highest singular value." Could you explain the motivation behind this specific weighting scheme and its impact on the final correlation scores?
3.  The paper interprets the eigenvectors from SVD as representing "sub-themes." Could you provide a brief qualitative analysis for an example topic to illustrate this? For instance, for a topic with two known sub-themes, does an inspection of the corresponding eigenvectors and their most aligned words support this interpretation?
