### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces "TBuckets," a novel framework for automatically measuring the coherence of topics from models like LDA. The core intuition is that a coherent topic's words belong to a single, dominant thematic group. The method operationalizes this by grouping topic words into "buckets" and using the resulting distribution—particularly the size and composition of the largest bucket—to derive a coherence score. The authors propose three bucketing techniques: one based on agglomerative clustering and two parameter-free methods based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the words' vector embeddings. The methods are evaluated against human judgments on three standard datasets and show state-of-the-art performance. The practical utility of the proposed metric is also demonstrated by showing it can be used to select higher-quality topic sets to improve performance on a downstream weakly-supervised text classification task.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and intuitive "bucketing" framework for topic coherence:** The central idea of partitioning topic words into thematic groups and scoring coherence based on the size of the dominant group is conceptually clear, easy to understand, and distinct from prevalent PMI-based or vector-centroid methods.
2.  **An effective, parameter-free coherence measure:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two of the three benchmark datasets without requiring the explicit parameter tuning common to many competing methods.
3.  **A clear demonstration of downstream utility:** The authors successfully show that their coherence measure can be used to improve a practical application (weakly supervised text classification) by filtering for higher-quality topic models, strengthening the claim that the metric captures a useful aspect of topic quality.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High correlation with human judgments and SOTA performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves excellent empirical results, outperforming the best-performing configurations from the comprehensive study by Röder et al. (2015) on the NYT and 20NG datasets. This provides strong validation for the effectiveness of the bucketing approach.
2.  **Novelty and simplicity of the core idea:** The bucketing metaphor is a fresh perspective on topic coherence that aligns well with human intuition. Rather than relying on aggregate pairwise statistics (like PMI), it attempts to discover the underlying thematic structure of the topic words, which is a compelling and powerful idea.
3.  **The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based methods:** A significant practical advantage of the TBuckets-SVD and SVD-Reorg techniques is their lack of user-defined hyperparameters. Many state-of-the-art coherence metrics require tuning parameters like window sizes or selecting external reference corpora, which can be brittle and computationally expensive. This work presents a robust, "out-of-the-box" solution.
4.  **Strong validation via a downstream task:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 provides a convincing use case. By showing that selecting topic sets with higher TBuckets scores leads to better classification accuracy, the paper demonstrates that the proposed metric has practical value beyond simply correlating with human annotations.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Unclear motivation for the SVD formulation:** The paper's justification for using SVD is vague. It states that the columns of the V matrix (right singular vectors) represent "orthogonal d dimensional directions" that can act as "bucket identifiers," but it does not explain *why* these vectors should correspond to meaningful sub-themes within a topic. A more principled explanation, perhaps linking SVD to Principal Component Analysis of the word vectors, would significantly strengthen the paper's technical foundation.
2.  **The ad-hoc nature of the reorganization step:** The best-performing model, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, relies on a complex reorganization procedure with several engineered rules and pull scores (NBPW, LBPW, etc.). Most critically, the "dynamic threshold" (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) is introduced without any derivation or justification. While the authors claim this avoids parameter tuning, this complex, non-obvious heuristic functions as a built-in parameter itself, undermining the claim that the method is "completely parameter less." The complexity of this step detracts from the elegance of the original SVD idea.
3.  **Insufficient analysis of scoring functions:** The paper proposes several scoring metrics (size, WL, EVS) and a weighting scheme (multiplying by the square of the highest singular value) but provides little analysis of their respective contributions. Why is weighting by the singular value beneficial? What is the intuition behind it? Why does considering word order (`WL`) sometimes help and sometimes not? A more detailed ablation or analysis of the scoring components would provide deeper insight into what makes the method work.
4.  **Limited baseline comparison in the application experiment:** The text classification experiment compares using the top 5 topic sets ranked by TBuckets against the bottom 5. While this demonstrates the metric's discriminative power, a more robust evaluation would compare against selecting the top 5 sets using a strong, established baseline coherence measure (e.g., C_V/NPMI). Such a comparison is necessary to demonstrate that TBuckets is genuinely better at identifying useful topics for this task, rather than just being one of many coherence measures that could achieve a similar result.
5.  **Minor presentation issues:** The paper contains several minor but distracting errors. The dry run example in Section 3 for the non-coherent topic contains a copy-paste error. The references section is poorly formatted, with inconsistent styling and stray characters (`?`, `\.,`), which should be corrected before publication.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed theoretical motivation for using the right singular vectors (from the V matrix) of the topic words' embedding matrix as representations of orthogonal sub-themes or "bucket identifiers"?
2.  The "dynamic threshold" (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) is a key component of your best-performing SVD-Reorg model, yet its derivation is not explained. Could you please elaborate on how this specific formula was developed and provide the intuition or justification behind its form?
3.  In your text classification experiment, did you consider comparing your topic set selection strategy against a strategy that uses a strong baseline coherence measure (like C_V or NPMI) to rank and select the topic sets? This would help clarify if your measure offers a unique advantage for this downstream task.
