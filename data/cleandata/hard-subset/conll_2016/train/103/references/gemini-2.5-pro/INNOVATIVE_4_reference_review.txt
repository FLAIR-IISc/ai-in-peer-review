### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces a novel approach, TBuckets, for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core intuition is that a coherent topic should consist of words that can be grouped into a single, dominant thematic "bucket". The paper proposes three methods to implement this bucketing idea: one based on agglomerative clustering of word embeddings, and two more sophisticated methods based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The SVD approaches treat the right singular vectors of a topic's word embedding matrix as latent sub-themes, assigning words to the "bucket" of the most similar vector. The best-performing variant, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, adds a parameter-free heuristic step to refine these buckets. The authors evaluate their methods by correlating the generated coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, demonstrating that their parameter-free SVD-based method outperforms previous state-of-the-art approaches on two of the three datasets. They also show the practical utility of their metric by using it to select higher-quality topic models for a weakly supervised text classification task, leading to improved downstream performance.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A New Conceptual Framework for Topic Coherence:** The primary contribution is the "bucketing" paradigm itself. It reframes coherence evaluation from the conventional approach of aggregating pairwise word similarity scores to a more holistic task of identifying the thematic structure within a topic. This is a conceptually novel and highly intuitive way to think about the problem.
2.  **A Parameter-Free, SVD-based Coherence Metric:** The paper introduces a creative and effective method (TBuckets-SVD and its reorganized variant) that uses SVD to operationalize the bucketing idea without requiring any parameter tuning. This stands in stark contrast to many existing metrics that depend on multiple, often hard-to-set, parameters.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance with a Downstream Application:** The proposed method is not just a theoretical novelty; it is shown to achieve state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on standard benchmarks. Furthermore, its practical value is demonstrated in a text classification task, showing that selecting topic models based on the TBuckets score can improve downstream results.

### Strengths
1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Intuition:** The core idea of this paper is refreshingly new and elegant. Moving away from the dominant paradigm of averaging pairwise PMI or cosine similarities is a bold step. The "bucketing" metaphor is powerful and aligns well with how a human might intuitively assess a topic's coherence by trying to find a single unifying theme. This conceptual shift is exactly the kind of contribution that pushes the field forward.
2.  **Creative and Effective Use of SVD:** The application of SVD to identify sub-themes within a small set of 10-20 topic words is a clever and non-obvious methodological contribution. Interpreting the right singular vectors as latent "bucket identifiers" is an inspired choice that provides a principled, linear-algebraic foundation for the partitioning process.
3.  **The Parameter-Free Nature of the Best Method:** One of the most significant practical strengths is that the best-performing methods, TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, are completely parameter-free. This is a massive advantage over the complex configuration space of measures explored by Roder et al. (2015), which can be computationally expensive and difficult to optimize for new domains. The authors' design of a dynamic, data-driven threshold for the reorganization step is particularly commendable, as it avoids introducing new hyperparameters.
4.  **Strong and Well-Rounded Evaluation:** The paper validates its claims convincingly. It not only achieves state-of-the-art results on standard correlation benchmarks but also includes an extrinsic evaluation on a downstream task. This application to weakly supervised classification demonstrates that the proposed metric has tangible utility beyond simply mimicking human scores, strengthening the overall impact of the work.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Heuristic Nature of the Reorganization Step:** While the results for TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are impressive, the reorganization logic itself feels somewhat ad-hoc. The conditions for moving words (e.g., `LBPW > NBPE`) are based on a set of intuitive but not formally justified comparisons. While the dynamic threshold is a nice touch, the underlying rules seem engineered for the task. The paper would be stronger if it could provide a more principled motivation for this specific set of reorganization heuristics.
2.  **Justification for SVD Interpretation is a Leap of Faith:** The paper asserts that the right singular vectors (V matrix) represent orthogonal sub-themes. While this is an appealing interpretation, it is presented without deep theoretical grounding. A more thorough explanation of why these specific vectors should correspond to meaningful semantic directions within the embedding space would bolster the method's foundations. As it stands, the connection feels more empirical than theoretical.
3.  **The Final Scoring Function is Under-motivated:** The paper proposes several ways to calculate the final coherence score from the buckets (e.g., size of the largest bucket, weighted by word order, weighted by the square of the highest singular value). The choice to use the *square* of the singular value, for instance, is not explained and feels arbitrary. A brief ablation or discussion on the choice of scoring function would help clarify why the chosen combinations are effective.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The reorganization step in SVD-Reorg is key to the improved performance. Could you provide an ablation study on the "into" (pulling words into the largest bucket) and "from" (evicting words from the largest bucket) steps? It would be interesting to see if one of these operations is more critical than the other.
2.  The dynamic threshold `AWES − AWPS/AWES` is a clever way to avoid a hyperparameter, but its form is not immediately obvious. Could you provide a more detailed intuition for why this specific formula, involving a ratio and a difference of average similarities, is a suitable choice?
3.  Have you considered the potential effect of the word embedding quality on your method? Since the entire approach is built on top of word vectors, how sensitive is TBuckets to the choice of embeddings (e.g., GloVe vs. Word2Vec, different dimensions, different training corpora)?
