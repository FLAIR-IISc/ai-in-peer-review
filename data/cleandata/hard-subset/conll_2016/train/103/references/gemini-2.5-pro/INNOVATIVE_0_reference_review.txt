### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel conceptual framework for measuring the coherence of topics generated by models like LDA. The core idea is to mimic the human process of judging topic quality by grouping a topic's most prominent words into semantically coherent "buckets." A topic is considered more coherent if its words fall into a single large bucket. The authors propose three methods for this automatic bucketing: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on a creative application of Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to word embeddings.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A novel conceptual framework for topic coherence:** The "TBuckets" idea itself is a significant contribution. It moves beyond aggregating pairwise word similarity scores (like PMI-based methods) towards a more holistic model of thematic grouping, which is a fresh and intuitive perspective on the problem.
2.  **A parameter-free, SVD-based implementation:** The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg methods are a bold and non-obvious way to realize the bucketing concept. Using the eigenvectors of the topic words' embedding matrix as identifiers for latent "sub-themes" is highly creative. The fact that this approach is parameter-free is a major practical advantage over prior state-of-the-art methods that require extensive tuning.
3.  **Strong empirical validation and a practical application:** The paper demonstrates that its best method (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) outperforms a strong, established baseline on two of three standard datasets. Furthermore, the authors show the practical utility of their metric in a downstream task, demonstrating that selecting topic models based on their proposed coherence score improves performance in weakly-supervised text classification.

### Strengths

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Intuition:** The paper's greatest strength lies in its central idea. The "bucketing" metaphor is a powerful and intuitive way to reframe the problem of topic coherence. It departs significantly from the dominant paradigm of summing pairwise similarity scores, which often fails to capture the multi-faceted nature of a topic's theme. This work proposes a new direction for coherence research, which is exactly the kind of contribution our field needs. I find this conceptual leap far more valuable than yet another incremental improvement on PMI calculations.

2.  **Bold and Unconventional Methodology:** The SVD-based approach is particularly impressive. Applying SVD to a matrix of word embeddings to identify orthogonal sub-themes is an elegant and inventive piece of technical work. It is not an obvious or straightforward extension of existing techniques. This willingness to apply a standard tool in a completely new context to solve a problem is commendable. That this bold approach yields a method that is not only effective but also parameter-free makes it a significant achievement.

3.  **Compelling Empirical Performance:** A novel idea is only as good as its results, and this paper delivers. The fact that the parameter-free TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method can outperform the best-performing (and heavily tuned) configuration from Roder et al. (2015) is a powerful statement. It suggests that the underlying conceptual model is not just elegant but also more aligned with human judgments of coherence than previous, more complex approaches.

4.  **Demonstrated Practical Impact:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is a crucial addition that elevates the paper beyond a purely academic exercise in metric design. By showing that their metric can be used to select better topic models for a real-world task (reducing human annotation effort on incoherent topics), the authors demonstrate the tangible value of their contribution. This provides a clear answer to the "so what?" question that often accompanies work on evaluation metrics.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few areas that could be strengthened. I view these as minor points that do not detract from the core contribution.

1.  **Heuristic Nature of the Reorganization Step:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method, while being the best-performing, relies on a set of rules and a dynamic threshold that feel somewhat heuristic. The formula for the threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) is presented without a deep theoretical justification. While its topic-dependent and parameter-free nature is clever, the mechanism feels like a set of patches designed to fix observed errors in the cleaner SVD method, rather than a principled extension. A more thorough ablation or analysis of this component would have been beneficial.

2.  **Justification for SVD as Sub-theme Discovery:** The paper provides an intuition that the orthogonal eigenvectors from the SVD correspond to different sub-themes within a topic. This is a plausible and appealing explanation, but it remains an intuition. The connection between the geometric orthogonality in the word embedding space and the semantic discreteness of themes could be explored more deeply. While the empirical results provide strong evidence that it works, a more formal grounding would make the argument unassailable.

3.  **Limited Exploration of the "Bucketing" Concept:** The paper proposes clustering and SVD as two ways to implement bucketing. However, the TBuckets framework itself could be much broader. One could imagine graph-based community detection algorithms or other unsupervised partitioning methods being used. The paper presents a great initial idea and a strong proof-of-concept, but it could have done more to position "bucketing" as a general framework and discuss the space of possible implementations.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more intuition or a more formal justification for the specific formula used to calculate the dynamic threshold in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method? While it clearly works well, understanding *why* this particular combination of AWES and AWPS is effective would strengthen the paper.

2.  In your text classification experiment, you select the top 5 models out of 10 runs based on your coherence score. Have you performed any analysis to see how stable this ranking is? For instance, if you ran the topic model 100 times, would the models in the top decile consistently outperform those in the bottom decile? This would further solidify the practical utility of the metric.
