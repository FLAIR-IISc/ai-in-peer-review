### **Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The central intuition is that a coherent topic consists of words that can be grouped into a single, large thematic "bucket." The authors propose three methods to automate this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering of word embeddings (TBuckets-Clustering), and two more sophisticated methods using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on the matrix of word embeddings (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The final scores are derived from properties of the largest resulting bucket. The authors evaluate their methods by correlating the generated scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, demonstrating that their best-performing method, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, achieves state-of-the-art results on two of the three datasets. They further show the practical utility of their metric by using it to select higher-quality topic sets for a weakly-supervised text classification task, leading to improved downstream performance.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Conceptual Framework for Coherence:** The "bucketing" approach is an intuitive and original way to model topic coherence, moving beyond the standard paradigm of aggregating pairwise word similarity scores.
2.  **An Effective SVD-based Implementation:** The use of SVD to identify latent sub-themes (buckets) within a small set of topic words is a clever application of the technique. The strong empirical results, particularly for the proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method, validate this approach.
3.  **Demonstration of Downstream Utility:** The text classification experiment provides a concrete and valuable demonstration that improving topic coherence metrics can have a tangible, positive impact on downstream NLP applications that rely on topic models.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Intuitive and Novel Idea:** The core concept of grouping topic words into buckets is highly intuitive and closely mirrors how a human might assess a topic's coherence. This provides a fresh and promising alternative to existing methods that rely on averaging pairwise PMI or cosine similarity, which may not fully capture the thematic structure of the word set.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper reports state-of-the-art results on two widely used benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG). Outperforming the extensive and highly-tuned framework of Röder et al. (2015) is a significant achievement and a strong argument for the method's effectiveness.
3.  **Parameter-Free Method:** A key advantage of the TBuckets-SVD and SVD-Reorg methods is their parameter-free nature. This contrasts sharply with many competing methods that require tuning (e.g., window sizes for PMI, thresholds) or rely on a complex configuration space. This makes the proposed method more robust, easier to apply, and more generalizable.
4.  **Valuable Application Study:** The inclusion of the weakly-supervised text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is a major strength. It moves the work beyond being a pure evaluation paper by showing that the proposed metric can be used to make practical improvements in a real-world task. The comparison between selecting the highest- and lowest-coherence topic sets provides compelling evidence for the metric's validity.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Arbitrariness of the Reorganization Step:** The best-performing method, `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`, relies on a complex reorganization step with a "dynamic threshold" given by the formula `AWES − AWPS/AWES`. This formula feels ad-hoc and is not theoretically justified. While the authors' intuition is explained, the specific mathematical form seems arbitrary. This makes the key component of the best method appear to be a carefully engineered heuristic, which somewhat undermines the elegance and the "parameter-free" claim of the core SVD approach.
2.  **Inconsistent Performance and Lack of Analysis:** The method's performance drops significantly on the Genomics dataset, where it underperforms the state-of-the-art by a considerable margin (0.691 vs. 0.773). The paper simply presents this result without any discussion or analysis. This is a crucial omission. Is this failure due to the general-purpose GloVe embeddings being ill-suited for a specialized domain? Does it reveal a fundamental limitation of the bucketing approach? Acknowledging and attempting to explain this poor performance is essential for understanding the method's limitations.
3.  **Scoring Metric Discards Information:** The final coherence score is calculated based only on the properties of the largest bucket. This approach ignores potentially useful information about the overall distribution of words into buckets. For example, a topic that splits into two moderately sized, internally consistent buckets (e.g., related to both politics and economics) is qualitatively different from a topic where every word is in its own singleton bucket. A scoring function that considers the entropy or Gini coefficient of the bucket size distribution could potentially provide a more nuanced and robust measure of coherence.
4.  **Minor Lack of Clarity in SVD Method Description:** The description of how `TBuckets-SVD` works could be slightly clearer. It states that the eigenvectors from the V matrix serve as "bucket identifiers," and a word is assigned to the bucket of its most similar eigenvector. A sentence explaining the intuition here—that these eigenvectors represent the principal orthogonal semantic axes within the topic's word embedding space—would strengthen the reader's understanding.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the design of the dynamic threshold for the `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method? Have you explored alternative formulations for this threshold, and how sensitive is the model's performance to its specific mathematical form? This would help clarify whether the current formula is a finely-tuned "sweet spot" or if the general principle is robust.
2.  What is your hypothesis for the significant performance gap on the Genomics dataset? Have you considered evaluating your method with domain-specific embeddings (e.g., trained on PubMed) to test whether the issue lies with the input representations rather than the bucketing method itself?
3.  Your current scoring metrics focus exclusively on the largest bucket. Did you experiment with metrics that capture the properties of the entire bucket distribution, such as the number of buckets or the entropy of their sizes? It seems this could differentiate between topics that are multi-themed versus those that are simply incoherent.
