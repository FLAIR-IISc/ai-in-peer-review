**Summary of the paper**
This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel family of methods for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core intuition is that a coherent topic's words should belong to a single semantic group, or "bucket." The paper proposes three concrete instantiations of this idea: (1) TBuckets-Clustering, which uses agglomerative clustering on word embeddings; (2) TBuckets-SVD, which uses Singular Value Decomposition on the topic words' embedding matrix to identify orthogonal sub-themes; and (3) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, an extension of the SVD method that reassigns words to buckets based on a set of heuristics. The authors evaluate these methods by correlating their coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, claiming state-of-the-art performance on two of them. A secondary experiment is presented to demonstrate the utility of the proposed metric in a weakly supervised text classification task.

**Main contributions**
1.  **A novel conceptual framework for topic coherence:** The paper introduces the "bucketing" metaphor, an intuitive and interpretable way to conceptualize topic coherence based on semantic grouping, which is a departure from traditional pairwise PMI or vector-averaging methods.
2.  **Parameter-free coherence metrics:** The primary contribution lies in the TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg methods, which are presented as parameter-free alternatives to existing metrics that often require significant tuning.
3.  **Strong empirical results:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method is shown to achieve higher correlation with human judgments than the reported state-of-the-art on the NYT and 20NG datasets.

**Strengths**
1.  **Intuitive and Novel Approach:** The central idea of grouping topic words into semantic "buckets" is elegant and easy to understand. It provides a fresh perspective on what constitutes a coherent topic, moving beyond simple pairwise word similarities to a more holistic, group-based assessment.
2.  **Strong Performance on Standard Benchmarks:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves impressive results, outperforming the strong baseline from Röder et al. (2015) on two of the three datasets. This suggests that the underlying bucketing idea is effective and captures aspects of coherence that align well with human intuition.
3.  **Parameter-Free Methods:** A significant strength is the parameter-free nature of the SVD-based approaches. Many existing coherence metrics, including the state-of-the-art baseline, rely on a configuration space with multiple parameters (e.g., window size, confirmation measure, probability estimation) that require tuning. A robust, parameter-free metric is a valuable contribution to the field, simplifying its application.
4.  **Demonstration of Practical Utility:** The inclusion of the text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is commendable. While the experiment is small, it attempts to ground the utility of the coherence metric in a downstream task, showing that selecting topic models based on the proposed score can lead to improved performance.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Methodological Opacity and Ambiguity:** The descriptions of the core methods lack precision and contain inconsistencies.
    *   The "dry run" examples in Section 3 are confusing and contain errors. In the second example, a line from the first example is incorrectly copy-pasted (`Bucket-1: {storm, weather}`). More importantly, the logic is hand-wavy; it's unclear why "high" and "northern" form new buckets while "cold" is assimilated into the main bucket in the first example. This undermines the intuitive explanation.
    *   The description of TBuckets-SVD (Section 3) is technically imprecise. For an `n x d` matrix of word embeddings, SVD yields a `d x d` V matrix. The paper states, "We use the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers." This is unclear. Does it mean the first n columns of V^T? This needs to be specified. The mechanism for assigning a word to a bucket ("the word’s most similar eigenvector") is also not fully defined (e.g., is similarity measured by cosine distance?).
    *   The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method, touted as parameter-free, relies on a complex, ad-hoc dynamic threshold: `AWES − AWPS/AWES`. The formula is presented without theoretical or strong empirical justification, making it seem arbitrary. This heuristic-driven complexity weakens the "parameter-free" claim, as it functions like a finely-tuned, data-dependent parameter itself.
    *   The final coherence score computation (Section 3.2) is also arbitrary. Why is the score weighted by the *square* of the highest singular value? This choice requires justification.

2.  **Weak Experimental Setup and Analysis:**
    *   The comparison to the baseline is weak. The authors only compare against a single number for the "best performing settings" from Röder et al. (2015). A rigorous evaluation would require reimplementing and running well-established coherence metrics like `C_v` or `C_npmi` on the same data and embeddings to provide a more direct and robust comparison.
    *   The analysis of the text classification experiment (Section 4.3) is flawed. No statistical significance tests are performed. Furthermore, the results are contradictory. For the MED-SPACE dataset, the "Lowest 5" coherence sets perform best, and for POLITICS-RELIGION, the S1 strategy outperforms S2, both of which challenge the central hypothesis of the experiment. These inconvenient results are not adequately discussed or explained.

3.  **Poor Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of formatting and typesetting errors, which detracts from its professional quality.
    *   The reference section is highly unprofessional. Citations are inconsistently formatted, contain extraneous characters like `?` and `\`, and are not compiled correctly (e.g., `Blei et al\.,? 2003, Blei et al\.,`). The in-text citations are also inconsistent (e.g., `(Blei, 2012)` vs. `(Röder et al., 2015)`).
    *   There are numerous small errors throughout the text, such as the malformed URL footnote on page 4 and the copy-paste error in Section 3 mentioned above.
    *   The placement of the Related Work section (Section 5) is unconventional for a research paper; it would be more effective after the introduction.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide a precise mathematical description of the TBuckets-SVD method? Specifically, for an `n x d` word embedding matrix, how are the `n` bucket-identifying eigenvectors derived from the `d x d` V matrix?
2.  Can you provide a stronger rationale for the dynamic threshold formula `AWES − AWPS/AWES`? Its construction seems arbitrary. How does this complex heuristic align with the claim that the method is "parameter-free"?
3.  In your text classification experiment (Table 3), your proposed strategy S2 underperforms the baseline S1 for the POLITICS vs RELIGION dataset, and the "Lowest 5" coherence sets outperform the "Highest 5" for the MEDICAL vs SPACE dataset. How do you reconcile these results with your claim that higher coherence scores lead to better downstream performance? Have you performed any significance testing on these results?
