### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel method for evaluating the coherence of topics generated by models like LDA. The core idea is that the words of a coherent topic should cluster into a single, large "bucket." The authors use pre-trained word embeddings to represent topic words and propose three methods for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The final coherence score is derived from the properties of the largest resulting bucket. The methods are evaluated by measuring the correlation of their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets. A secondary, extrinsic evaluation is also presented, suggesting that selecting topic models with higher coherence scores can improve performance on a weakly supervised text classification task.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  The proposal of a new, embedding-centric paradigm for topic coherence that models intra-topic structure through clustering, departing from the more common approach of aggregating pairwise word similarity scores.
2.  The application of SVD to this problem, creating a method that the authors claim is parameter-free and achieves competitive performance.
3.  An initial extrinsic evaluation that attempts to link the proposed coherence metric to a downstream application, providing a practical motivation for the work.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Novelty of Approach:** The paper's core idea of "bucketing" topic words is intuitive and presents a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem. It moves beyond simple pairwise comparisons (like in PMI-based measures) to consider the holistic thematic structure of a topic's word set.
2.  **Use of Standard Benchmarks:** The evaluation is performed on standard, publicly available datasets from Röder et al. (2015), which come with human coherence judgments. This is good practice and allows for direct comparison with a large body of existing work.
3.  **Extrinsic Evaluation:** The authors should be commended for including an extrinsic evaluation (Section 4.3). Too often, work on evaluation metrics is presented in a vacuum. Demonstrating that a higher score on their metric correlates with better performance on a downstream task, even with some limitations, strengthens the paper's argument for the metric's practical utility.
4.  **Clarity:** The paper is generally well-written and the proposed methods, particularly the high-level intuition, are easy to understand. The examples in Section 3 are helpful for illustrating the concept.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Methodological Justification:** The SVD-based methods, which are central to the paper's claims, are presented as "intuitive" but lack a rigorous theoretical foundation. It is not clear why the eigenvectors of the word embedding covariance matrix (ATA) should serve as meaningful "bucket identifiers" for a small set of 10 words. SVD is a powerful technique for dimensionality reduction on large matrices, but its application here feels ad-hoc. Without a more principled explanation, the method appears to be a complex heuristic rather than a well-grounded technique.
2.  **Questionable "Parameter-Free" Claim:** The best-performing method, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, is presented as parameter-free, but this is misleading. The reorganization step introduces a complex "dynamic threshold" based on a specific, non-obvious formula (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`). This formula is itself a fixed, un-tuned hyperparameter. Similarly, the final scoring function's decision to weight results by the "square of the highest singular value" is an arbitrary design choice. These critical components are not derived from first principles and require substantial justification, which is currently absent. Their presence undermines the claim that the method is truly parameter-free in spirit.
3.  **Inadequate Comparison to Baselines:** The paper compares its performance against a single number—the "best performing settings" reported in Röder et al. (2015). This is a weak and potentially unreliable form of comparison. Established topic coherence metrics like C_V or C_NPMI (which are part of the framework in Röder et al.) are the standard in this field. A rigorous evaluation would require a direct, head-to-head comparison against these specific, state-of-the-art baselines, reimplemented or run within the authors' exact experimental setup (using the same GloVe embeddings, etc.). Merely outperforming a number quoted from a prior publication is not sufficient evidence of superiority.
4.  **Inconclusive Results:** While TBuckets-SVD-Reorg shows improvements on two datasets, the gains are modest (e.g., 0.859 to 0.869 on 20NG), and it underperforms the reported state-of-the-art on the Genomics dataset. This suggests the method's effectiveness may not be universal. The extrinsic evaluation is similarly not entirely conclusive, with the proposed topic selection strategy failing to outperform the baseline on one of the four datasets.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Can you provide a more formal justification for using the eigenvectors of the ATA matrix as thematic "bucket identifiers"? What properties of SVD on a small n x d matrix (where n=10) make this a principled choice for partitioning topic words?
2.  The formula for the dynamic threshold in the reorganization step and the use of the squared highest singular value in the scoring function are crucial to your best results. Could you elaborate on the design process for these components? How sensitive is your model's performance to these specific mathematical forms?
3.  Given that Röder et al. (2015) provide a framework for creating and testing numerous coherence measures, why did you not include direct comparisons to top-performing individual measures from that work, such as C_V or C_NPMI, within your own controlled experimental environment? Such a comparison would make a much stronger case for the claimed improvements.
