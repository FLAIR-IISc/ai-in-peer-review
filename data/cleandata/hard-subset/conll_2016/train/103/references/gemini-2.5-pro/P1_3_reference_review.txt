### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics from topic models. The central idea is that a coherent topic's words should cluster into a single dominant thematic group, or "bucket." The authors operationalize this by grouping a topic's top N words based on their pre-trained word embeddings. They propose three specific methods: one using agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering) and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The SVD-based methods identify orthogonal sub-themes within a topic and assign words to them. The final and best-performing method, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, refines these initial buckets using a dynamic, parameter-free reorganization step. The authors evaluate their methods by measuring the correlation of their coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance. They also show the practical utility of their metric by using it to select higher-quality topic models for a downstream weakly-supervised text classification task, leading to improved performance.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective parameter-free method for topic coherence.** The use of SVD on topic word embeddings to identify and group words into thematic sub-dimensions is an intuitive and original approach. The fact that the best-performing variant (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) is parameter-free is a significant practical contribution, as it removes the need for expensive tuning that plagues many existing metrics.
2.  **Achieving state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks.** The proposed method outperforms the strong, search-based baseline from Röder et al. (2015) on two out of three widely-used datasets (NYT and 20NG), establishing it as a top-performing technique for topic coherence evaluation.
3.  **Demonstration of downstream task improvement.** The paper successfully demonstrates that its coherence metric has practical value beyond simply correlating with human scores. By using TBuckets to filter topic models, the authors show a tangible improvement in a weakly-supervised classification task, which strengthens the validity and usefulness of their proposed metric.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Strong Intuition:** The conceptual framing of coherence as the tendency of topic words to fall into a single "bucket" is clear and intuitive. The use of SVD to automatically discover these buckets as orthogonal semantic dimensions within the topic's embedding space is a clever and novel technique that distinguishes this work from dominant approaches based on aggregating pairwise word similarities (like PMI).
2.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The paper presents very strong results. `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` achieves state-of-the-art Pearson correlation with human judgments on the NYT and 20NG datasets. Surpassing the best-performing configuration from Röder et al. (2015), which represents an extensive search over a large space of coherence measures, is a significant achievement.
3.  **Parameter-Free Method:** This is a major practical strength. Many existing coherence metrics require setting parameters (e.g., window sizes for PMI, confirmation measures) which can be data-dependent and hard to tune without a labeled validation set. The proposed SVD-based methods are presented as parameter-free (barring the choice of embeddings), making them much easier to apply in practice.
4.  **Well-Designed Evaluation:** The evaluation is sound. The authors use standard datasets and evaluation protocols, comparing against a strong and relevant baseline. The inclusion of the text classification application (Section 4.3) is a commendable addition, as it provides compelling evidence for the metric's real-world utility in model selection.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity of the Reorganization Method:** The description of the `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method in Section 3.1 is the weakest part of the paper. While the "pull" scores are understandable, the logic and derivation of the dynamic threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) is opaque. It is introduced without sufficient justification, making it seem arbitrary. Since this reorganization is critical to achieving the paper's best results, a more thorough explanation of why this specific formula was chosen and how it works is necessary to support the claim of a principled, non-empirical threshold.
2.  **Limited Comparison to Baselines in Results:** The paper only compares its results to the single best-performing number reported in Röder et al. (2015). While this is a very strong SOTA baseline, the paper would be more comprehensive if it also directly compared against other widely-known individual coherence metrics (e.g., `C_v`, `C_npmi`, `C_umass`) in its main results table (Table 1). This would provide a clearer picture of where TBuckets stands in relation to the broader landscape of coherence measures, not just the "best-of-the-best" from a hyperparameter search.
3.  **Undeclared Dependency on Word Embeddings:** The performance of the method is fundamentally dependent on the choice of pre-trained word embeddings. This is a significant external dependency, and its impact is not analyzed. How do the results change with different embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec, FastText), different dimensions, or embeddings trained on the target corpus itself? Without this analysis, the robustness of the method is unclear.
4.  **Lack of Justification for Scoring Functions:** Section 3.2 introduces several scoring functions (size, WL, EVS) and a weighting scheme (multiplying by the square of the highest singular value) without strong motivation. It is unclear why these specific functions were chosen over other possibilities or why the singular value weighting is appropriate. The final results in Table 1 seem to cherry-pick the best score from among several options for each method, which could be seen as a form of implicit parameter tuning.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed motivation or derivation for the dynamic threshold formula `AWES - AWPS/AWES` used in the SVD-Reorg method? A clearer explanation would greatly strengthen the claim that this is a principled and robust parameter-free choice.
2.  Your SVD-based methods rely on pre-trained word embeddings. Have you investigated the sensitivity of your results to the choice of these embeddings? For example, how does performance vary when using different models (GloVe vs. word2vec), dimensions, or embeddings trained in-domain on the evaluation corpora?
3.  In Section 3.2, the coherence scores for the SVD methods are weighted by the "square of the highest singular value." What is the theoretical or empirical justification for this specific weighting scheme?
