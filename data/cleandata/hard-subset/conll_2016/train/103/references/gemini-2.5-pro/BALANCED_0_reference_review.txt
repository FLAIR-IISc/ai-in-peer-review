**Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics derived from topic models. The central idea is to automatically group the top words of a topic into semantic "buckets". The authors posit that a coherent topic will be dominated by a single large bucket, whereas an incoherent topic will fragment into multiple smaller buckets. Three bucketing techniques are explored: one based on agglomerative clustering of word embeddings (TBuckets-Clustering), and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The SVD-based methods, TBuckets-SVD and its refinement TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, identify orthogonal sub-themes within the topic words to form buckets and are presented as parameter-free. The final coherence score is derived from the properties of the largest resulting bucket. The authors evaluate their methods by correlating the generated scores with human judgments on three benchmark datasets. The results show that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms a strong state-of-the-art baseline on two of the three datasets. The paper also provides an extrinsic evaluation, demonstrating that using the proposed coherence measure to select higher-quality topic models improves performance on a downstream weakly supervised text classification task.

**Main Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is a novel, intuitive, and effective method for topic coherence evaluation (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). It operationalizes the idea of finding a dominant theme within a topic's words by using SVD on their embeddings to group them into sub-themes. This approach is conceptually different from prevalent PMI-based methods and achieves state-of-the-art results while being presented as parameter-free.
2.  A secondary but important contribution is the extrinsic validation of the proposed metric. The authors demonstrate that selecting topic sets with higher coherence scores, as measured by their method, leads to tangible improvements in a downstream text classification application. This showcases the practical utility of the work beyond simply correlating with human judgments.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Intuitive Methodology:** The core idea of "bucketing" topic words is conceptually simple and aligns well with the human process of interpreting topic coherence. Using SVD to identify orthogonal sub-themes is a clever and novel way to automate this process. It provides a fresh perspective on a well-studied problem, moving beyond the standard paradigm of aggregating pairwise word similarity scores.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance with a Simple Model:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on the NYT and 20NG datasets, surpassing the complex, multi-parameter baseline from Roder et al. (2015). Achieving this with a method that is ostensibly parameter-free is a significant strength, as it suggests the model is not over-tuned and would be easier for practitioners to adopt and apply.
3.  **Extrinsic Evaluation Demonstrates Practical Utility:** The application to weakly supervised text classification (Section 4.3) is a compelling addition. By showing that selecting topic sets based on their TBuckets score improves downstream F1-score, the authors provide strong evidence that their coherence metric captures a meaningful aspect of topic quality that has real-world impact. This goes a step beyond many papers in this area that rely solely on intrinsic evaluation.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **The "Parameter-Free" Claim and Heuristic Threshold:** The central claim that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is parameter-free is questionable. The reorganization step, which is critical for its strong performance, relies on a dynamic threshold defined by the formula `AWES - AWPS/AWES`. This formula appears heuristic and is not theoretically or empirically justified in the paper. It functions as a hidden, data-dependent parameter. The paper would be much stronger if the authors provided a principled derivation for this threshold, or at least an ablation study showing its impact and robustness compared to simpler alternatives. Without this, the parameter-free claim is weakened.
2.  **Lack of Justification for Scoring Components:** The final coherence scores for the SVD methods are computed using a combination of metrics (size, WL, EVS) and are then weighted by the "square of the highest singular value." The motivation for this specific weighting scheme is not explained. Why the square? What is the semantic intuition? An ablation study is needed to disentangle the contributions of the different scoring components (largest bucket size, word order weight, eigenvector similarity, and the singular value weighting). This would provide a clearer understanding of what aspects of the model are driving its success.
3.  **Insufficient Analysis of SVD Components and Cross-Dataset Performance:** The paper's explanation of why the V matrix from SVD is used to identify sub-themes is brief. A more detailed interpretation of what these eigenvectors represent in the embedding space for a small set of 10 words would be beneficial. Furthermore, the SVD-based methods perform significantly worse on the Genomics dataset than both the baseline and the authors' own clustering-based method. This is a critical result that is not sufficiently discussed. Is this failure due to the unsuitability of the general-purpose GloVe embeddings for a specialized domain? This has important implications for the method's generalizability, which should be addressed transparently.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the design process for the dynamic threshold in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg step? What is the justification for the specific formula `AWES - AWPS/AWES`, and how sensitive is the model's performance to variations in this formulation?
2.  What is the intuition behind weighting the final SVD-based coherence scores by the square of the highest singular value? Could you provide results from an ablation study showing the performance with and without this weighting, or with a linear weighting, to demonstrate its impact?
3.  Your SVD-based methods show a notable performance drop on the Genomics dataset. Do you have a hypothesis for why this might be the case? Specifically, do you believe this is a limitation of using general-domain word embeddings (GloVe) for specialized corpora, and if so, does this suggest your method's effectiveness is highly dependent on the choice of embeddings?
