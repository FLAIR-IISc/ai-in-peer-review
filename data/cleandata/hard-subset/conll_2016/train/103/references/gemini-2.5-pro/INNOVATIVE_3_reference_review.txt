### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper introduces "TBuckets," a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core idea is to mimic the human process of assessing a topic by grouping its constituent words into thematic "buckets." The coherence of a topic is then determined by the resulting distribution of words across these buckets, with a single, large bucket indicating high coherence. The authors propose and evaluate three methods to implement this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on a creative application of Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on the topic words' embeddings.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are, in order of importance:

1.  **A Novel Conceptual Framework for Topic Coherence:** The primary contribution is the "TBuckets" idea itself. It reframes the problem of coherence evaluation from aggregating pairwise word similarities (like in PMI-based metrics) to a partitioning problem. This is a fresh, intuitive, and conceptually appealing approach that opens a new direction for research in this area.
2.  **An Effective and Parameter-Free SVD-based Implementation:** The paper presents a clever and non-obvious method (TBuckets-SVD and its reorganized variant) that operationalizes the bucketing concept. By applying SVD to a topic's word embedding matrix and using the resulting singular vectors as identifiers for latent sub-themes, the authors develop a powerful, parameter-free coherence metric that achieves state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks.
3.  **Demonstration of Practical Downstream Utility:** The authors validate their metric not only by correlating it with human judgments but also by showing its utility in a downstream task. The weakly supervised text classification experiment provides compelling evidence that using TBuckets to pre-select high-quality topic models can lead to better real-world application performance.

### Strengths

1.  **High Conceptual Novelty:** The central "bucketing" metaphor is a significant departure from existing approaches to topic coherence. It is highly intuitive and provides a powerful new lens through which to view the problem. I find this kind of bold, conceptual leap to be exactly what the field needs. Rather than another incremental improvement on PMI calculations, this work proposes an entirely different way of thinking, which is a major strength.
2.  **Creative and Original Methodology:** The use of SVD to identify thematic sub-groups within a small set of 10 topic words is a creative and inspired methodological choice. Interpreting the right singular vectors of the topic's word embedding matrix as orthogonal "sub-themes" is an elegant idea that seems perfectly suited to the task. This is not a straightforward application of a standard tool but a genuinely novel technical contribution.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results Combined with Simplicity:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method is not just novel; it is highly effective, outperforming a strong, state-of-the-art baseline on two of the three datasets. That it achieves this while being completely parameter-free is a significant practical advantage. Competing methods often require extensive tuning or rely on large external corpora in complex ways, whereas the SVD-based approach is self-contained and easy to implement.
4.  **Grounding in a Practical Use Case:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is a commendable addition. It moves the evaluation beyond abstract correlation scores and demonstrates that the proposed metric can provide tangible benefits in a realistic application scenario. Showing that selecting for higher coherence scores leads to better classifier performance makes a strong case for the metric's real-world value.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper's core ideas, there are a few areas that could be strengthened. I view these as opportunities for refinement rather than fatal flaws.

1.  **Heuristic Nature of the Reorganization Step:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method, which delivers the best performance, relies on a set of rules and a dynamic threshold formula (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) that feel somewhat ad-hoc. While the authors' motivation to create a parameter-free method is laudable, the formula itself is not derived from a theoretical grounding and its specific form is not well justified. This makes the "reorganization" step feel more like a carefully engineered heuristic than a principled correction, which could limit its generalizability.
2.  **Unexplored Connection Between Buckets and Sub-topics:** The paper's intuition is that SVD finds "sub-themes," but this is not explicitly verified. A qualitative analysis showing that the words assigned to smaller buckets indeed form coherent, distinct sub-topics would have been very powerful. For example, in the incoherent topic `{karzai, afghan, miner...}`, does one SVD-defined bucket consistently group `{miner, mine}` and another `{assange, wikileaks}`? Such an analysis would provide stronger evidence for the mechanism behind the method's success.
3.  **Under-analyzed Performance on the Genomics Dataset:** The SVD-based methods show a notable drop in performance on the Genomics dataset, falling behind both the baseline and the paper's own simpler clustering method. The authors report this result but do not discuss potential reasons. This is a missed opportunity for a deeper analysis. Is this a failure of the general-purpose GloVe embeddings in a specialized domain, or does it point to a limitation of the SVD approach itself when topics are structured differently? A brief discussion of this point would strengthen the paper's claims.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the derivation of the dynamic threshold for the SVD-Reorg method? Was this formula found empirically, or is there a deeper theoretical or geometric intuition for why this specific combination of Average Word Pair Similarity and Average Word Eigenvector Similarity is effective for deciding when to move a word?
2.  Regarding the SVD-based methods, have you performed any qualitative analysis of the eigenvectors themselves? For example, what are the nearest words in the full GloVe vocabulary to the second or third right singular vectors for a given topic? Does this confirm that they represent coherent sub-themes as hypothesized?
3.  What is your primary hypothesis for the performance gap of the SVD-based methods on the Genomics dataset? Do you believe training domain-specific embeddings would close this gap, or might there be a more fundamental mismatch between the method's assumptions (e.g., orthogonal sub-themes) and the nature of topics in this domain?
