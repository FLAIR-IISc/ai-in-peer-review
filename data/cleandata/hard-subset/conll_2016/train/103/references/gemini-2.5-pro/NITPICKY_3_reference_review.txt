### Summary of the paper and contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics from topic models. The core idea is to simulate the human process of grouping a topic's words into thematic clusters, or "buckets". The authors hypothesize that a topic is coherent if its words predominantly fall into a single large bucket. The paper proposes three concrete methods to automate this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering) and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (TBuckets-SVD and a variant with a reorganization step, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A new conceptual approach (TBuckets) for topic coherence evaluation, based on thematic grouping of topic words rather than pairwise word similarity aggregation.
2.  A parameter-free implementation of this approach using SVD (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg), which is a significant practical advantage over existing heavily parameterized methods.
3.  An empirical demonstration that the proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two out of three benchmark datasets.

### Strengths

1.  **Intuitive and Novel Core Idea:** The central idea of "bucketing" words to measure coherence is appealingly intuitive and offers a fresh perspective. It attempts to model how humans might identify multiple conflicting sub-themes within a topic, which is a more nuanced view of coherence than simply averaging pairwise similarities.
2.  **Parameter-Free SVD Methods:** A major strength is that the SVD-based variants are presented as parameter-free. This is a substantial advantage in practice, as many existing coherence measures require tuning multiple parameters (e.g., window size, PMI variant, weighting schemes), as highlighted by the work of Röder et al. (2015). This makes the proposed method more robust and easier to apply.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The experimental results are compelling. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the reported state-of-the-art on the NYT and 20NG datasets (Table 1), demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The performance of TBuckets-Clustering is also competitive, particularly on the Genomics dataset.
4.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The application of the coherence metric to a downstream task (weakly supervised text classification in Section 4.3) is a valuable addition. The experiment shows that selecting topic sets with higher coherence scores can lead to improved classification performance, providing tangible evidence for the method's utility beyond correlation with human scores.

### Weaknesses

Despite the strong results, the paper is marred by a significant number of methodological ambiguities, unclear explanations, and careless errors in presentation.

1.  **Fundamental Misunderstanding or Poor Explanation of SVD:** The description of the SVD method in Section 3 contains a critical and confusing statement. For an n x d matrix A (where n=10 words, d=300 embedding dimension), the paper states it uses the "first n eigenvectors" from the V matrix as bucket identifiers. However, in the SVD of A, the V matrix contains the eigenvectors of A^T A, which is a d x d matrix. Therefore, V is d x d and contains d eigenvectors. Using the "first n" (i.e., first 10) of these 300 eigenvectors is a specific choice that is not explained or justified. Is this a typo? Does it mean the first n *principal components*? This ambiguity undermines the technical soundness and reproducibility of the core method.
2.  **Convoluted and Poorly Justified Reorganization Method:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method (Section 3.1) is described via a complex set of rules and a seemingly ad-hoc dynamic threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`). The justification provided for this formula is purely narrative ("denotes flexibility", "manages the gap") and lacks any theoretical or rigorous empirical grounding. This makes the "reorganization" step feel like arbitrary post-hoc engineering designed to fit the data, which detracts from the elegance of the parameter-free SVD approach.
3.  **Unmotivated Choices in Scoring:** The paper states that for the SVD-based methods, the final scores are weighted "with the square of the highest singular value" (Section 3.2). No justification is provided for this specific choice. Why the square? Why only the single highest value, which represents the most dominant component, rather than a combination of values that might capture the distribution of variance across components? This arbitrary choice further weakens the methodological rigor.
4.  **Disconnect Between Intuition and Implementation:** The paper motivates the TBuckets idea with a sequential, one-word-at-a-time process (Section 3). However, the actual implementations (agglomerative clustering and SVD) do not operate this way. They are global methods that consider all words simultaneously. This creates a disconnect between the motivating intuition and the technical realization of the methods.
5.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from numerous presentation flaws that signal a lack of care.
    *   The reference list is exceptionally poorly formatted, containing stray punctuation (`et al\.,?`), inconsistent citation styles, missing venue information, and question marks. This is far below the expected standard for a conference submission.
    *   There is a clear copy-paste error in the example dry run in Section 3, where the buckets from the coherent "storm" topic appear in the middle of the "karzai" topic example.
    *   The writing is often awkward ("The TBuckets approach powers us to do this...") and contains grammatical errors ("is a indication").
    *   Citation format is inconsistent within the text itself (e.g., `(David M. Blei, 2007)` vs. `(Blei and Lafferty, 2006)`).

While the core idea is promising and the results are strong, the sheer volume of minor-to-moderate flaws in the methodological description and presentation significantly undermines my confidence in the work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the dimensionality of the V matrix in your SVD setup? Given an n x d input matrix, V should be d x d. How and why do you select the "first n eigenvectors" from V as your bucket identifiers?
2.  The formula for the dynamic threshold in the SVD-Reorg method (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) appears ad-hoc. Can you provide a more rigorous justification for its specific functional form? Did you experiment with other forms?
3.  What is the motivation for weighting the SVD-based coherence scores by the *square* of the highest singular value? What is the impact on performance if this weighting is removed or changed (e.g., using the singular value directly)?
4.  In your text classification experiment (Table 3), the S1 strategy yields a better result than the S2 (Highest 5) strategy on the POLITICS vs RELIGION dataset. How does this finding align with your overall claim that selecting for higher coherence improves downstream task performance?
