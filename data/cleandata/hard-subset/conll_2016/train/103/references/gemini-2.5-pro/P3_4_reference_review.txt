### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics from probabilistic topic models. The core intuition is that words in a coherent topic should belong to a single overarching theme. The method operationalizes this by grouping a topic's top words into thematic "buckets" and using the properties of the resulting bucket distribution—particularly the largest bucket—to compute a coherence score. The authors propose three variants for creating these buckets: one based on agglomerative clustering and two more novel methods based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The most sophisticated method, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, uses SVD to identify orthogonal sub-themes and then applies a parameter-free reorganization step to refine the buckets. The proposed methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets and are shown to achieve state-of-the-art performance.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel SVD-based methodology for topic coherence:** The use of SVD on a topic's word embedding matrix to identify orthogonal sub-themes (represented by right singular vectors) is an elegant and conceptually new way to model topic coherence, moving beyond the standard paradigm of aggregating pairwise word similarities.
2.  **A parameter-free, state-of-the-art coherence metric:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves excellent empirical results, outperforming prior work on two of three benchmark datasets, with the significant practical advantage of being entirely parameter-free. This makes it a robust and readily applicable tool.
3.  **Demonstration of downstream utility:** The paper includes a compelling application study showing that using the proposed coherence metric to select higher-quality topic sets for weak supervision leads to improved performance in a text classification task. This provides concrete evidence of the metric's practical value beyond intrinsic evaluation.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Novelty and Strong Intuition:** The core idea of identifying sub-themes within a topic via SVD is highly innovative. While clustering is a natural first thought for grouping words, the SVD approach provides a more principled way to find the dominant and orthogonal "axes of meaning" spanned by the topic's words. The intuition that a coherent topic should be dominated by a single principal axis is compelling and well-motivated.

2.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method sets a new state-of-the-art on the widely used NYT and 20NG datasets from Röder et al. (2015). Achieving this without any dataset-specific parameter tuning is a very strong result and suggests that the method captures a fundamental aspect of semantic coherence that generalizes well.

3.  **Absence of Hyperparameters:** A major weakness of many coherence metrics, including the framework of Röder et al. (2015), is their reliance on multiple parameters that require tuning (e.g., window sizes, probability estimation methods, confirmation measures). The fact that TBuckets-SVD and its reorganization variant are parameter-free is a significant practical advantage, making them much easier to deploy and more reliable across different domains and topic models. The design of the "dynamic threshold" for the reorganization step is a clever contribution to maintaining this property.

4.  **Clear Application-Oriented Validation:** The experiment in Section 4.3 is a key strength. Many papers on evaluation metrics stop at showing correlation with human judgments. By demonstrating that their metric can be used to improve a downstream task (weakly supervised classification), the authors make a much stronger case for its real-world relevance. The significant performance gap between using high-coherence vs. low-coherence topic sets on the more difficult datasets powerfully illustrates the metric's utility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for the SVD Formulation:** While the intuition behind using SVD is appealing, the technical explanation could be more rigorous. The paper states that the `V` matrix (eigenvectors of A^T A) captures "feature-feature interactions" and represents "orthogonal d dimensional directions." A more formal connection to established concepts would be beneficial. For example, is this equivalent to performing PCA on the set of word vectors and using the principal components as bucket identifiers? Clarifying this would strengthen the theoretical underpinnings of the method.

2.  **Opacity of the Dynamic Reorganization Threshold:** The formula for the dynamic threshold used in the SVD-Reorg step, `AWES - AWPS/AWES`, is presented without a clear derivation or justification. It feels ad-hoc. Subtracting a unitless ratio (`AWPS/AWES`) from an average similarity score (`AWES`) is dimensionally questionable and its behavior is not immediately obvious. The paper would be stronger if it provided a more principled explanation for this specific functional form or showed an ablation study comparing it to simpler alternatives. Without this, the claim of being "completely parameter free" is slightly weakened, as this formula acts as a complex, built-in parameter.

3.  **Potential Computational Complexity:** The reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) appears to have non-trivial computational cost. For a word in the largest bucket, the algorithm checks its similarity against words in all other buckets. For a word outside the largest bucket, it checks against all words inside it. For a topic of N words, the complexity of this step could approach O(N^2), depending on the initial bucket distribution. While N=10 is small, a discussion of the method's computational complexity and scalability would be important, especially if one wanted to apply it to topics with more words.

4.  **Unexplored Dependency on Word Embeddings:** The entire method relies heavily on pre-trained word embeddings. All experiments use a single set of GloVe embeddings. The performance is notably lower on the Genomics dataset, a specialized domain where general-purpose embeddings may be less effective. The paper would benefit from a discussion on the sensitivity of the results to the choice of embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec vs. GloVe, domain-specific vs. general-purpose). This would provide a more complete picture of the method's robustness and limitations.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the relationship between your SVD approach and Principal Component Analysis (PCA)? Does using the right singular vectors of the word embedding matrix `A` as bucket identifiers have a direct interpretation in terms of the principal components of the set of word vectors?
2.  The formula for the dynamic threshold in the reorganization step (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) is central to the performance of your best model, yet its derivation is not discussed. Could you please provide the reasoning that led to this specific formula? Have you experimented with other formulations?
3.  The reorganization process is defined entirely in relation to the single largest bucket. Did you consider a more global optimization, where a word could move between any two buckets if it improved an overall objective function? What is the specific motivation for restricting movement to be only into or out of the largest bucket?
