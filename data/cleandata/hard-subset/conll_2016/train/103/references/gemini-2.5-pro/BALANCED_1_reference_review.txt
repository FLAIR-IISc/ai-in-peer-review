### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach, "TBuckets," for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core intuition is that words in a coherent topic should belong to a single overarching theme, which can be modeled by grouping them into "buckets." The paper proposes three methods to implement this: a clustering-based approach, a parameter-free method using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), and an extension of the SVD method that includes a reorganization step (SVD-Reorg). The authors evaluate their methods by correlating the generated scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, demonstrating that their SVD-Reorg method outperforms the state-of-the-art on two of them. Furthermore, they show the practical utility of their metric in a weakly supervised text classification task, where selecting topic models with higher coherence scores leads to improved performance.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and intuitive method for evaluating topic coherence based on partitioning topic words into thematic "buckets" using SVD on their word embeddings. This method is notably parameter-free, a significant practical advantage over many existing approaches.
2.  Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks, where the proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two of the three datasets evaluated.
3.  A demonstration of the proposed metric's practical utility in a downstream application, showing that it can be used to select higher-quality topic models to improve the performance of a weakly supervised text classification system.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Intuitiveness of the Method:** The "word buckets" concept provides a very clear and intuitive model for topic coherence. The operationalization of this idea using SVD is clever and novel in this domain. Rather than relying on pairwise co-occurrence statistics (like PMI-based measures), this approach leverages the geometric properties of word embeddings to identify orthogonal sub-themes within a topic. This represents a conceptually different and valuable approach to the problem.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Parameter-Free Design:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD and SVD-Reorg methods achieve excellent results, outperforming a strong state-of-the-art baseline on the NYT and 20NG datasets (Table 1). A key advantage highlighted by the authors is that these SVD-based methods are parameter-free. This is a significant practical benefit, as many high-performing coherence measures require tuning a complex set of parameters (e.g., the configuration space explored by Röder et al., 2015), making them difficult to apply off-the-shelf. The authors' design, especially the dynamic calculation of the reorganization threshold, is elegant in this regard.

3.  **Demonstration in a Downstream Task:** The inclusion of the text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is a major strength. It grounds the evaluation of the coherence metric in a practical application, moving beyond simple correlation with human scores. By showing that filtering topic models based on the TBuckets score can lead to more robust classification performance (specifically by avoiding low-quality models), the authors provide compelling evidence for the real-world utility of their work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Justification of the Reorganization Step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg):** The description of the reorganization methodology is the weakest part of the paper. While the intuition is understandable, the technical details are opaque and insufficiently justified.
    *   The formula for the dynamic threshold, `AWES − AWPS/AWES`, is presented without derivation or strong justification. The explanation provided is hand-wavy ("denotes flexibility among words"). This makes the process seem arbitrary and undermines the "parameter-free" claim, as this complex, fixed formula functions like a tuned parameter itself. An ablation study or sensitivity analysis on this formula would be necessary to validate its design.
    *   The rules for moving words (e.g., `OBPW > max(NBPW, NBPE)`) are stated but not thoroughly motivated. Why this specific combination of the four "pull" metrics?
    *   The results for this step are inconsistent: it provides a large boost on NYT, a marginal one on 20NG, and is detrimental on Genomics. The paper offers no discussion of this significant variance, which raises questions about the generalizability and robustness of the reorganization heuristic.

2.  **Insufficient Detail on the Core SVD Method:** The explanation of the TBuckets-SVD method is overly brief. For a reader to fully understand and reproduce the work, more detail is needed. For instance, the paper states that the first `n` eigenvectors of V are used as bucket identifiers for a topic with `n` words. It is unclear if this means there are always `n` initial buckets, and what happens if some eigenvectors are not the closest "identifier" for any word. A clearer explanation of the mapping from words to eigenvectors would be beneficial. A more explicit justification for using the right-singular vectors (V) over the left-singular vectors (U) in the context of the n x d word embedding matrix would also strengthen the methodological description.

3.  **Limited Comparison to Baselines:** The paper compares its performance only to the single best-performing result reported in Röder et al. (2015). While this is a strong SOTA baseline, the comparison would be more rigorous and informative if:
    *   Results for well-established baselines (e.g., `C_v`/NPMI, `C_UMASS`) were included directly in Table 1, ideally re-implemented within the authors' experimental framework for a controlled comparison.
    *   The specific SOTA configuration from Röder et al. (2015) that is being compared against was identified for each dataset, giving the reader a clearer sense of what is being outperformed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed motivation or derivation for the dynamic threshold formula `AWES − AWPS/AWES` used in the SVD-Reorg method? Given its complexity, how was this specific formulation chosen, and how sensitive are the results to variations in it?

2.  In the TBuckets-SVD method, when using `n` eigenvectors as bucket identifiers for `n` topic words, is it possible for some buckets to remain empty? If so, how does this affect the subsequent coherence score calculation?

3.  The performance impact of the SVD-Reorg step varies drastically across datasets (highly positive for NYT, neutral for 20NG, negative for Genomics). Do you have any hypotheses about why this heuristic is so dataset-dependent? Could this be related to domain characteristics or properties of the word embeddings for those domains?
