### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core intuition is that words in a coherent topic should belong to a single, dominant thematic "bucket," whereas words in an incoherent topic will fragment into multiple distinct buckets. The authors operationalize this idea in three ways: a baseline clustering approach and two more innovative methods based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the topic words' embeddings. The SVD-based methods treat the resulting eigenvectors as orthogonal sub-themes, assigning words to the most similar one. The best-performing variant, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, adds a parameter-free heuristic to refine these buckets. The final coherence score is derived from the size and composition of the largest bucket.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A New Conceptual Framework for Topic Coherence:** The primary contribution is the "bucketing" idea itself. This is a fresh and highly intuitive alternative to the dominant paradigm of aggregating pairwise word similarity scores (like PMI or cosine similarity). It offers a more holistic view of topic structure by attempting to discover latent sub-themes within a topic's word list.
2.  **A Novel, Parameter-Free Method using SVD:** The application of SVD to the word embedding matrix of a single topic to identify these "buckets" is a bold and creative methodological step. The fact that the best-performing versions of this method are completely parameter-free is a significant practical advantage over existing state-of-the-art methods that require extensive tuning.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation and a Practical Application:** The paper demonstrates that its novel approach not only works in principle but also outperforms a strong, established baseline on standard evaluation datasets. Furthermore, the authors show its utility in a downstream task, using the coherence score to select better topic models for weakly supervised text classification, leading to improved performance.

### Strengths

1.  **High Degree of Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The paper's central idea is its greatest strength. Moving away from simple pairwise comparisons towards a model of thematic grouping is a significant conceptual leap. The "bucketing" metaphor is powerful because it aligns well with the human cognitive process of evaluating a topic: we don't just check if every word is related to every other word; we try to see if they all fit under a single conceptual umbrella. The SVD implementation is an elegant way to formalize this hunt for underlying themes. This kind of bold, first-principles thinking is exactly what the field needs.

2.  **Parameter-Free Methodology:** The best-performing methods, TBuckets-SVD and SVD-Reorg, require no parameter tuning. This is a massive advantage in practice. Many existing coherence metrics, especially the state-of-the-art framework by Röder et al. (2015), involve a complex configuration space that can be computationally expensive to explore and may not generalize well. A method that works "out of the box" and still achieves state-of-the-art performance is a major contribution to the community.

3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The novel idea is backed by convincing results. Outperforming a heavily-tuned SoTA on two out of three standard benchmarks is a clear testament to the method's effectiveness. The gains are not marginal, and they are achieved without any of the parameter tuning required by the baseline. This provides strong evidence that the underlying "bucketing" hypothesis is sound.

4.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The application to weakly supervised text classification (Section 4.3) is a valuable addition. It moves the work from being a purely academic exercise in metric design to a tool with demonstrated real-world benefit. Showing that one can actively improve a downstream model's performance by using TBuckets to filter for higher-quality topic sets is a compelling argument for the metric's practical relevance.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few areas where the work could be strengthened. I view these as minor issues that do not detract from the core contribution, but they are worth noting.

1.  **Heuristic Nature of the Reorganization Step:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method, which yields the best results, relies on a reorganization step with a dynamically computed threshold. The formula for this threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the authors' intuition for it is explained, the paper would benefit from a more principled justification for this specific mathematical form. As it stands, it appears to be a well-crafted heuristic that works, but its theoretical grounding is unclear.

2.  **Limited Exploration of Scoring Functions:** A coherence score is computed based on the final buckets, using metrics like the size of the largest bucket, a word-order weighted score (WL), and an eigenvector similarity score (EVS). The paper presents results for several of these but provides little analysis or justification for choosing one over another. A deeper discussion of what aspect of coherence each scoring function captures would strengthen the paper. For instance, why is a combination of WL and EVS the best for the SVD-Reorg method on the 20NG dataset?

3.  **Dependence on Word Embedding Quality:** The entire method is predicated on the geometry of a pre-trained word embedding space (GloVe). The performance is therefore fundamentally tied to the quality of these embeddings and how well they capture the semantics of the domains in the test datasets. While this dependency is common in modern NLP, a brief discussion on how the method might behave with different embeddings (e.g., domain-specific ones for the Genomics dataset) would be welcome. This is more of a limitation to acknowledge than a core flaw.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more intuition or a more formal justification for the specific formula used to calculate the dynamic threshold in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method? Was this formula derived from empirical observation of similarity distributions, or is there a deeper reason for its structure?

2.  The SVD approach uses eigenvectors as "bucket identifiers," which you describe as orthogonal sub-themes. Have you attempted to qualitatively interpret these eigenvectors, for instance by looking at which other vocabulary words are closest to them in the embedding space? This could provide fascinating insight into what the SVD is actually discovering and why the method works so well.

3.  In the text classification application, the quality of a *set* of topics is calculated by simply summing the individual topic scores. This doesn't account for redundancy or diversity across the topics in the set. Could you comment on this choice? Do you think a more sophisticated set-level quality metric that considers inter-topic relationships might be even more effective for this downstream task?
