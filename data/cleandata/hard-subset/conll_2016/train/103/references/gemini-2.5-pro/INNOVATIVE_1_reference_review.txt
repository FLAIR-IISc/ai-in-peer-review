### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics generated by probabilistic topic models. The central and compelling intuition is that the words of a coherent topic should largely belong to a single, unified semantic theme. The authors operationalize this idea by proposing methods to automatically group a topic's words into thematic "buckets". The coherence score is then derived from the properties of the largest resulting bucket, such as its size.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A new conceptual framework for topic coherence:** The primary contribution is the "bucketing" metaphor itself. This moves beyond the dominant paradigm of aggregating pairwise word similarity scores (like PMI or cosine similarity) and instead evaluates the *structural coherence* of the set of topic words. This is a refreshing and significant shift in perspective on how to computationally model human judgments of topic quality.
2.  **A parameter-free, SVD-based method for measuring coherence:** The use of Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on the matrix of topic word embeddings to identify orthogonal "sub-themes" is a highly novel and elegant instantiation of the bucketing idea. The fact that this method and its reorganized variant (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) are parameter-free is a major practical advantage over many existing coherence measures that require significant tuning.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The "word buckets" idea is intuitive, well-motivated, and represents a genuinely new approach to topic coherence evaluation. It attempts to model a plausible human interpretation process, and its departure from simply averaging pairwise scores is a bold and welcome innovation. The conceptual clarity of the core idea is a major strength.
2.  **Innovative Use of Linear Algebra:** The application of SVD to find the principal axes of the semantic space defined by a small set of topic words is particularly clever. It provides a principled, algebraic foundation for identifying potential sub-themes (the buckets), represented by the eigenvectors. This is a much more sophisticated approach than standard clustering and provides a strong theoretical underpinning for the method.
3.  **Parameter-Free Implementation:** A standout feature of the SVD-based methods is that they are parameter-free. As the authors note, many state-of-the-art approaches are effectively large configuration spaces that require extensive tuning or reliance on external corpora statistics that may not be well-matched to the target domain. A high-performing method that works "out of the box" is a significant practical contribution to the field.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation and Demonstrated Utility:** The paper doesn't just propose a new idea; it shows that it works. The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two out of three standard datasets. Moreover, the application in Section 4.3, which shows that using TBuckets to pre-select higher-quality topic models improves downstream classification performance, provides compelling evidence of the method's real-world value.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper's core ideas, there are a few areas where the work could be strengthened.

1.  **Heuristics in the Reorganization Step:** The SVD-Reorg method, which provides the best results, relies on a reorganization step governed by a "dynamic threshold" (`AWES − AWPS/AWES`). While the authors' motivation to avoid a fixed, manually-tuned parameter is commendable, the formula itself feels somewhat ad-hoc. The paper would be more convincing with a deeper analysis of this threshold's behavior. Without it, one might argue that a complex, data-dependent heuristic has simply replaced a simpler, fixed parameter, and its robustness to different datasets or embedding spaces is unclear.
2.  **Underutilization of the Bucket Distribution:** The proposed scoring functions rely exclusively on the properties of the single largest bucket. This discards potentially useful information from the rest of the bucket distribution. For instance, a topic that yields one bucket of size 8 and two of size 1 is likely more coherent than a topic that yields two buckets of size 5. A scoring metric based on the entropy or Gini coefficient of the bucket sizes could capture this nuance and might provide a more robust signal of coherence. This feels like a missed opportunity within the paper's own novel framework.
3.  **Sensitivity to Word Embeddings:** The method is fundamentally dependent on the geometric properties of the pre-trained word embeddings. The paper uses GloVe but provides no discussion of how the choice of embeddings (e.g., word2vec, fastText, BERT-based), their dimensionality, or their training corpus might impact the SVD analysis and the final coherence scores. While using off-the-shelf embeddings is standard, the method's reliance on the vector space structure makes this an important, unaddressed variable.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide more analysis or intuition for the dynamic threshold formula in the SVD-Reorg method? For example, showing the distribution of AWES, AWPS, and the resulting threshold for topics judged by humans as highly coherent versus highly incoherent would help build confidence that this is a generally applicable principle rather than a dataset-specific heuristic.
2.  Have you experimented with coherence scores that consider the entire distribution of bucket sizes, rather than just the largest bucket? It seems that the shape of this distribution (e.g., its entropy) would be a strong indicator of whether a single dominant theme exists, which is the core intuition of your work.
3.  The SVD method identifies orthogonal bases for the sub-themes. How does the method perform in cases where a topic contains multiple, distinct, but non-orthogonal themes (e.g., a topic about "court" that contains words related to both law and basketball)? Does the orthogonality assumption pose a limitation?
