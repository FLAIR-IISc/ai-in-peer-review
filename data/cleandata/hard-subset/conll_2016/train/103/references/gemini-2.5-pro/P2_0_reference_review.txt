### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces "TBuckets," a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models like LDA. The core intuition is that a coherent topic should consist of words that belong to a single, dominant theme. The method operationalizes this by automatically grouping a topic's top N words into thematic "buckets." The distribution of words across these buckets—specifically, the size and composition of the largest bucket—is used to calculate a coherence score.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel, parameter-free method for topic coherence (TBuckets-SVD and SVD-Reorg):** The authors propose using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) on the matrix of word embeddings for a topic's words. The resulting right-singular vectors are treated as latent sub-themes, and words are bucketed based on their similarity to these vectors. This is an elegant and novel application of SVD. The SVD-Reorg variant, which refines these buckets using a data-driven dynamic threshold, is particularly strong as it remains parameter-free, a significant advantage over many existing methods.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method is shown to outperform the best-performing configurations from a comprehensive prior framework (Röder et al., 2015) on two out of three standard benchmark datasets. Achieving this with a parameter-free method is a significant result.
3.  **Demonstration of practical utility:** The paper goes beyond intrinsic evaluation (correlation with human scores) and shows that the proposed coherence metric can be used to improve a downstream task. By using TBuckets to select higher-quality topic models for annotation in a weakly supervised text classification setup, they demonstrate improved classification performance, grounding the work's utility.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Intuitive and Elegant Core Idea:** The central metaphor of grouping topic words into "buckets" to assess coherence is highly intuitive and aligns well with how a human might judge a topic. The SVD-based implementation of this idea is particularly elegant, framing the problem as one of identifying orthogonal semantic dimensions within the topic's word set. This provides a clean conceptual foundation for the work.

2.  **Strong and Well-Supported Empirical Results:** The evaluation is thorough and follows established protocols. The primary claim—that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves state-of-the-art performance—is well-supported by the results in Table 1. Outperforming the complex and heavily-tuned framework of Röder et al. (2015) on the NYT and 20NG datasets is a compelling achievement.

3.  **The Method is Parameter-Free:** This is a major practical strength. Many topic coherence metrics, including the state-of-the-art comparator, require tuning multiple parameters (e.g., window sizes, confirmation measures, probability estimations) which can be computationally expensive and may not generalize across datasets. The TBuckets-SVD and SVD-Reorg methods avoid this entirely. The "dynamic threshold" in the reorganization step is cleverly derived from the topic's own data, preserving the parameter-free nature of the approach. This makes the method highly attractive for practical use.

4.  **Validation on a Downstream Task:** The inclusion of the text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is a significant strength. It demonstrates that the proposed coherence score is not just an abstract metric that correlates with human judgment, but has tangible benefits. Showing that selecting topic models with higher coherence leads to better classification F1-scores provides strong evidence for the metric's practical value.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Justification for Methodological Choices:** While the SVD approach is elegant, the paper provides little theoretical justification for *why* the right-singular vectors of the word embedding matrix should correspond to meaningful sub-themes. The explanation is largely intuitive ("orthogonal d dimensional directions we are interested in"). A more formal connection or a clearer geometric interpretation would strengthen the paper. Similarly, the SVD-Reorg step, while effective, feels somewhat heuristic. The specific formulation of the dynamic threshold and the reorganization rules (e.g., LBPW > NBPE + threshold) are presented without a discussion of the design choices or alternatives considered.

2.  **Clarity and Presentation Issues:** The paper could benefit from another round of proofreading. For instance, in the second dry-run example in Section 3, a line from the first example is mistakenly copied ("Bucket-1: {storm, weather}"). More critically, the references section is poorly formatted, with inconsistent citation styles and extraneous characters (e.g., "Blei et al\\.,?"), which detracts from the overall professionalism of the submission. The explanation of the coherence score computation in Section 3.2 is also a bit dense; a formal equation combining the components (size, WL, EVS, singular value weighting) would improve clarity.

3.  **Evaluation is Limited to N=10 Words:** All experiments are conducted on topics represented by their top 10 words. Topic coherence can be sensitive to the number of words (N) considered. It is unclear how the TBuckets approach, particularly the SVD and reorganization steps, would perform or scale as N increases (e.g., to 15 or 20). An analysis of the method's sensitivity to N would make the evaluation more comprehensive.

4.  **Performance on Specialized Domains:** The method underperforms the state-of-the-art on the Genomics dataset. The authors rightly suggest that domain-specific embeddings might help in future work, but this points to a current limitation. The reliance on general-purpose embeddings (GloVe trained on Wikipedia/Gigaword) may make the method less effective for technical or specialized corpora where word semantics differ. This weakness should be more explicitly acknowledged and discussed.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a more formal justification or a deeper intuition for why the eigenvectors of the ATA matrix (from SVD of the word embeddings matrix A) serve as effective "bucket identifiers" or representations of sub-themes within a topic?
2.  The SVD-Reorg method's dynamic threshold is key to its performance. Could you elaborate on the design process for this threshold? Specifically, why was the `AWES − AWPS/AWES` formulation chosen over other potential data-driven alternatives?
3.  How does the performance of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg vary as you change the number of top words (N) used to represent a topic (e.g., N=5, 15, 20)? Is there a point where the SVD approach becomes less stable or the reorganization logic breaks down?
