### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics from probabilistic topic models. The core intuition is that the words of a coherent topic should belong to a single, unified theme. The method operationalizes this by grouping topic words into thematic "buckets". The coherence score is then derived from the properties of the largest resulting bucket. The paper introduces three specific techniques for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering of word embeddings (TBuckets-Clustering), one using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to identify orthogonal sub-themes (TBuckets-SVD), and a final refined version that reorganizes the SVD-generated buckets (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets and are shown to outperform a state-of-the-art baseline on two of them. The paper also demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed metric in a weakly supervised text classification task.

The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel, parameter-free method for topic coherence evaluation.** The proposed TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg techniques provide a new way to measure coherence by identifying latent sub-themes within a topic using SVD on word embeddings. A key advantage of this approach is that it does not require tuning of external parameters, unlike many existing methods.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results on benchmark datasets.** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves higher correlation with human judgments than the reported state-of-the-art on the NYT and 20NG datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness.
3.  **An extrinsic evaluation demonstrating the practical utility of the coherence metric.** The authors show that using the proposed metric to select higher-quality topic sets leads to improved performance in a downstream weakly supervised text classification task, providing evidence that the metric captures a meaningful aspect of topic quality that impacts application performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of the paper is the strong performance of the proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method. As shown in Table 1, it achieves Pearson's r correlations of 0.837 and 0.869 on the NYT and 20NG datasets, respectively. These results surpass the reported state-of-the-art baselines of 0.806 and 0.859 from Roder et al. (2015). This provides clear, quantitative evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2.  **Methodological Novelty and Soundness:** The conceptual framework of "bucketing" words into thematic groups is intuitive and offers a fresh perspective compared to prevalent methods that rely on aggregating pairwise semantic similarity scores (e.g., based on PMI or cosine distance). The use of SVD to identify orthogonal eigenvectors as representations of these thematic "buckets" is a technically sound and creative way to implement this idea.
3.  **Parameter-Free Implementation:** The best-performing methods, TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, are presented as being parameter-free. The reorganization step, for instance, uses a dynamically computed threshold based on the properties of the topic words themselves, rather than a fixed, user-defined hyperparameter. This is a significant practical advantage over complex frameworks like that of Roder et al. (2015), which the authors note requires "considerable tuning," making the proposed method more robust and easier to deploy.
4.  **Demonstrated Practical Application:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 adds significant value by providing an extrinsic evaluation of the metric. By showing that selecting topic sets with higher coherence (as measured by TBuckets) leads to better classification F1-scores, the paper demonstrates that the metric is not just an academic exercise in correlating with human ratings but has tangible benefits for downstream NLP applications.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Justification of Key Methodological Details:** The description of the reorganization step (Section 3.1) and the dynamic threshold calculation is insufficiently justified. The formula for the threshold, `AWES - AWPS/AWES`, is presented without a clear derivation or theoretical grounding. It is explained with a brief, high-level intuition ("manages the gap," "denotes flexibility"), which is not entirely convincing. The technical soundness of this crucial component would be substantially improved by a more rigorous explanation, an analysis of its properties, or an ablation study on its components.
2.  **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The experimental comparison is made only to the final "best performing" numbers reported in Roder et al. (2015). While this is a strong baseline, the paper would be more convincing if it included direct comparisons to other widely recognized coherence metrics such as C_NPMI (Lau et al., 2014) or C_V, which are also part of the Roder et al. framework. Running these established baselines within the same experimental setup would provide a more controlled and comprehensive comparison.
3.  **Inconsistent Performance Across Datasets:** The SVD-based methods perform significantly worse than the baseline on the Genomics dataset (e.g., 0.691 for TBuckets-SVD-Reorg vs. 0.773 for the baseline). This performance drop is not adequately analyzed or explained in the main body of the paper. This raises questions about the generalizability of the method, particularly for domain-specific corpora where general-purpose word embeddings (like the pre-trained GloVe used here) may be less effective. A discussion of this limitation is necessary for a balanced assessment of the method's applicability.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed derivation or justification for the specific mathematical form of the dynamic threshold used in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method? An empirical analysis showing how this threshold's value changes for topics with high vs. low coherence would strengthen the claim that it is a principled choice.
2.  Regarding the text classification experiment, Table 3 shows two cases (POLITICS vs RELIGION and MEDICAL vs SPACE) where the average performance of the top 5 coherent topic sets does not clearly outperform the alternative (either the S1 baseline or the bottom 5 sets). Could you elaborate on these results and how they impact the overall conclusion about the practical utility of your metric?
3.  What is your hypothesis for the significant performance drop of the SVD-based methods on the Genomics dataset? Does this point to a fundamental dependency on the quality of pre-trained, general-domain word embeddings, potentially limiting the method's effectiveness on specialized text without domain-specific embeddings?
