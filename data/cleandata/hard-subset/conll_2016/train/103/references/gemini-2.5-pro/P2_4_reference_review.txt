### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel, intuitive framework for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core idea is to mimic human judgment by automatically grouping a topic's words into semantically coherent "buckets." A topic is considered more coherent if its words cluster into a single large bucket. The authors propose three methods for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The SVD-based methods are presented as a key advantage due to being parameter-free. The proposed methods are evaluated by correlating their coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets and are also applied to a downstream task of weakly supervised text classification to demonstrate their utility.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel and Intuitive Framework (TBuckets):** The paper introduces a conceptually clear and appealing method for coherence evaluation. The "bucketing" metaphor is a departure from standard pairwise word similarity aggregation (like PMI-based methods) and provides a different perspective on what makes a topic coherent. The use of SVD to identify orthogonal sub-themes within a topic is a particularly creative application.
2.  **A High-Performing, Parameter-Free Coherence Metric:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg methods achieve state-of-the-art or near-state-of-the-art performance on standard benchmarks. The fact that these methods do not require parameter tuning is a significant practical advantage over competing methods like the one from Röder et al. (2015), which requires searching a large configuration space to find the best setting.
3.  **Demonstration of Downstream Utility:** The authors validate their coherence metric on a practical application—selecting high-quality topic sets for weakly supervised text classification. The results in Section 4.3 show that using TBuckets to filter topic models before human annotation can lead to improved classification performance, providing strong evidence for the metric's real-world value.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Intuition:** The paper is very well-motivated. The introduction clearly explains the need for topic quality evaluation and the core idea behind TBuckets is illustrated with compelling examples in Section 3. This makes the approach easy to understand and its underlying logic is sound.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method outperforms the reported state-of-the-art from Röder et al. (2015) on two out of three datasets (NYT and 20NG, as shown in Table 1). Achieving this with a novel method is a strong result. The fact that the simpler TBuckets-SVD is also highly competitive further strengthens the validity of the core SVD-based approach.
3.  **Parameter-Free Nature of SVD Methods:** This is a major strength. Many existing coherence metrics, and especially the state-of-the-art framework by Röder et al., rely on several parameters (e.g., window size, confirmation measure, probability estimation method). This often requires extensive tuning on a development set with human annotations, which may not be available. The TBuckets-SVD and SVD-Reorg methods offer a robust, "out-of-the-box" solution, which is highly desirable for practitioners.
4.  **Valuable Application Study:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is an excellent addition that significantly elevates the paper. It moves beyond simply correlating with human scores and demonstrates that the proposed metric can be used to make tangible improvements in a downstream NLP pipeline. The comparison between selecting the highest vs. lowest coherence topic sets provides a clear and convincing argument for the metric's utility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Justification of the Reorganization Step:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method relies on a "dynamic threshold" (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) to decide when to move words between buckets. While the authors' motivation to avoid a tunable parameter is commendable, the formulation of this threshold feels somewhat ad-hoc and is not well-justified. The paper would be stronger if it provided more intuition or an ablation study explaining why this specific formula was chosen over other potential heuristics. Without this, the "parameter-free" claim for the reorganization step feels slightly weakened, as it contains a complex, engineered component.
2.  **Lack of Analysis on Weaker Performance:** The proposed methods, particularly the SVD-based ones, underperform the state-of-the-art on the Genomics dataset. The paper reports this result but offers no discussion or analysis as to why this might be the case. Is it an issue with the general-domain GloVe embeddings being ill-suited for the specialized vocabulary of genomics? Or does the bucketing assumption fail for highly technical, specific topics? A brief discussion of this limitation would add depth to the analysis and provide valuable insights for future work.
3.  **Dependence on Pre-Trained Embeddings:** The method is fundamentally dependent on the quality and domain-appropriateness of the underlying word embeddings. While using pre-trained GloVe embeddings is a reasonable choice, the paper does not discuss how the choice of embeddings might impact performance. For instance, would embeddings trained on the target corpus itself improve results, especially for specialized domains like Genomics? Acknowledging this dependency and its implications would make the experimental analysis more complete.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the dynamic threshold in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method: Could you provide more intuition behind the formula `AWES - AWPS/AWES`? Did you experiment with alternative non-parametric heuristics for the reorganization step, and if so, how did they perform?
2.  Regarding the performance on the Genomics dataset: Do you have a hypothesis for why your SVD-based methods did not outperform the baseline on this particular dataset? Do you believe this points to a limitation of the bucketing approach for certain topic types, or is it more likely an artifact of using general-domain word embeddings?
