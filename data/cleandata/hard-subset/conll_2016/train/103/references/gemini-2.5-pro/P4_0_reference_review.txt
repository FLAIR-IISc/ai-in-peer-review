**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The central idea is to partition the words of a topic into semantic "buckets". The coherence of the topic is then determined by the resulting distribution of words into these buckets, with the intuition that a coherent topic will have most of its words in a single, large bucket. The paper proposes three methods to implement this bucketing: an agglomerative clustering approach (TBuckets-Clustering) and two methods based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The first SVD method (TBuckets-SVD) uses the right-singular vectors of the topic's word embedding matrix as identifiers for sub-themes. The second (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) adds a post-processing step to refine the buckets. The proposed methods are evaluated against human coherence judgments on three standard datasets, where TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is shown to achieve state-of-the-art correlation. The paper also demonstrates the practical utility of the metric in a weakly-supervised text classification task.

**Contributions**

1.  **A novel, parameter-free topic coherence metric based on SVD (TBuckets-SVD and SVD-Reorg).** The primary contribution is the application of SVD to the matrix of a topic's word embeddings to identify orthogonal sub-themes, which are then used to group words. This provides a principled way to quantify the thematic diversity within a topic. The SVD-based methods are presented as being parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage over many existing coherence measures that require tuning.
2.  **Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks.** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves a higher Pearson correlation with human judgments than previously reported state-of-the-art results on two out of three widely used datasets (NYT and 20NG). This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed technique.
3.  **A demonstration of the metric's utility in a downstream application.** The authors conduct an experiment in weakly supervised text classification, showing that using their coherence metric to select higher-quality topic sets for human annotation leads to improved classification performance. This successfully links the abstract goal of topic coherence with tangible benefits in a practical task.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and strong intuition.** The core idea of evaluating coherence by partitioning topic words into semantic groups is intuitive and well-motivated. Using SVD to discover these groups by identifying orthogonal dimensions (sub-themes) in the embedding space of the topic's words is a novel and technically sound approach to this problem. The "dry run" examples in Section 3 effectively illustrate this intuition.
2.  **Parameter-free methodology.** The main SVD-based methods are presented as being free of tunable hyper-parameters. The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, for instance, uses a dynamically computed threshold based on properties of the topic itself rather than a fixed, empirically chosen value. This makes the method more robust and easier to apply across different datasets and domains compared to methods that require extensive tuning.
3.  **State-of-the-art performance.** The experimental results in Table 1 show that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the best-performing configurations from the extensive study of Röder et al. (2015) on the NYT and 20NG datasets. Achieving this level of performance, especially with a parameter-free method, is a significant result.
4.  **Clear presentation.** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The motivation, methodology, and experimental setup are clearly described, making the work easy to understand and reproduce.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient justification for key methodological choices.** Several design choices lack rigorous motivation.
    *   The dynamic threshold in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) is introduced with a brief, high-level explanation. The specific functional form is not derived or justified, making it seem arbitrary. This undermines the claim of a fully principled, parameter-free method.
    *   The final scores for SVD methods are weighted by the "square of the highest singular value." The paper provides no justification for why this specific weighting is used or why the value is squared. An ablation study is needed to demonstrate the impact and necessity of this component.
    *   The reorganization conditions compare different types of similarity scores (e.g., word-word similarity `OBPW` vs. word-eigenvector similarity `NBPE`) without establishing that they are on a comparable scale, which could make the comparison unreliable.
2.  **Limited experimental analysis and comparison.** The paper compares its results to numbers reported in a previous study (Röder et al., 2015). While this is a strong baseline, it is unclear if the underlying resources (e.g., word embeddings) are comparable. A more direct comparison against a few well-known baselines (e.g., `C_v` or `C_uci`) implemented using the same GloVe embeddings would provide a more controlled evaluation. Furthermore, there is little analysis of the behavior of the methods themselves, such as how often the reorganization step is triggered or which scoring component (size, WL, EVS) is most critical.
3.  **Dependence on external word embeddings.** The method's performance is fundamentally tied to the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. The paper uses general-domain embeddings (trained on Wikipedia/Gigaword) for all experiments. The performance drop on the specialized Genomics dataset suggests that the method may not generalize well to domains where the pre-trained embeddings are a poor fit. While the authors acknowledge this as future work, it is a significant limitation of the current study.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the specific formula used to calculate the dynamic threshold in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method? Was this formula derived from theoretical principles, or were other functional forms considered and empirically evaluated?
2.  In the "from" reorganization step, the method compares word-word similarities (OBPW, NBPW) with a word-eigenvector similarity (NBPE). Could you clarify if these values are on a comparable scale and explain why it is sound to compare them directly in the `max` function and the inequality?
3.  The paper states that the SVD-based scores are weighted by the square of the highest singular value. Could you explain the rationale behind this weighting? An ablation showing performance without this weighting, or with a linear weighting, would be helpful to understand its contribution to the final results.
