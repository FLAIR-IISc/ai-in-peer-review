### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces "TBuckets," a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core intuition is that a coherent topic consists of words that can be grouped into a single, large thematic "bucket." The paper proposes three methods to automate this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering of word embeddings (TBuckets-Clustering), and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The basic SVD method (TBuckets-SVD) uses the principal components (eigenvectors from the V matrix) of the topic words' embedding matrix to define orthogonal sub-themes, assigning each word to its most similar sub-theme. An enhanced version (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) adds a heuristic-based reorganization step to refine the buckets. The final coherence score is derived from the size and composition of the largest bucket. The authors evaluate their methods by correlating the generated scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, demonstrating that their SVD-based methods, which they claim are parameter-free, outperform the state-of-the-art on two of the datasets. They also show the practical utility of their metric in a downstream task of weakly supervised text classification.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel SVD-based Method for Topic Coherence:** The primary contribution is the TBuckets-SVD approach, which operationalizes the idea of thematic grouping by using SVD on a matrix of word embeddings. Using the principal right-singular vectors as representations of latent "sub-themes" within a topic is an original and intuitive way to quantify topic coherence. The parameter-free nature of this method is a significant practical advantage over existing techniques that require extensive tuning.
2.  **Extrinsic Evaluation Demonstrating Practical Utility:** The paper goes beyond the standard intrinsic evaluation (correlation with human scores) by applying the coherence metric to a downstream task. Section 4.3 shows that using TBuckets to select higher-quality topic models leads to improved performance in weakly supervised text classification. This provides strong evidence that the proposed metric is not just academically interesting but practically useful for improving NLP pipelines that rely on topic models.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance and State-of-the-Art Results:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art results on two out of three widely used benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG). Outperforming the extensive framework of Roder et al. (2015) is a significant achievement and clearly demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2.  **Novelty and Intuitiveness of the Method:** The core idea of "bucketing" words is highly intuitive and well-motivated by the provided examples. The use of SVD to automatically discover these "buckets" as orthogonal dimensions in the embedding space is a novel and elegant formulation. It provides a principled way to identify whether a topic's words cluster around a single concept or are split across multiple, distinct sub-themes.
3.  **Practicality of Parameter-Free Variants:** The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg methods are presented as being parameter-free. In a field where many metrics rely on parameters that need to be tuned (e.g., window sizes for PMI, thresholds for clustering), a high-performing, parameter-free method is a substantial contribution. This makes the method easy to adopt and apply across different domains without requiring expensive parameter sweeps.
4.  **Clear Presentation and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and the methodology is explained clearly, especially with the illustrative dry-run examples in Section 3. The use of standard public datasets and pre-trained embeddings makes the work appear readily reproducible.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Heuristics and Lack of Justification in the Reorganization Step:** The performance improvement of TBuckets-SVD-Reorg over TBuckets-SVD is substantial, indicating the reorganization step is critical. However, this step is based on a set of complex, heuristic rules. In particular, the formula for the dynamic threshold, `AWES - AWPS/AWES`, is presented without any theoretical or empirical justification. It feels like a carefully engineered formula that works well on these datasets, which slightly undermines the "parameter-free" claim, as it contains hard-coded design choices that function like hidden parameters. The paper would be much stronger if it provided more intuition or an ablation study on the components of this threshold.
2.  **Limited Analysis of Results and Failures:** While the method performs well, the paper lacks a deep analysis of *why*. For instance, the performance on the Genomics dataset is significantly lower than the state-of-the-art. The authors do not offer a hypothesis for this discrepancy. It is likely due to the use of general-domain GloVe embeddings on a specialized corpus, but discussing this limitation and potential solutions (e.g., using domain-specific embeddings) would strengthen the paper. A qualitative analysis of topics where the metric agrees or disagrees with human scores would also provide valuable insights.
3.  **Unclear Contribution of Different Scoring Components:** The final coherence score is calculated using several components (size of largest bucket, word order weight WL, eigenvector similarity EVS) and, for SVD methods, is weighted by the highest singular value. The relative importance of these components is not analyzed. For example, how much does weighting by the singular value contribute to the final performance? An ablation study breaking down the contribution of each part of the scoring function would make the results more interpretable and the methodological choices more compelling.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is crucial for achieving state-of-the-art performance. Could you provide more intuition or justification for the specific formula used to calculate the dynamic threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`)? Were alternative formulations considered, and if so, how did they perform?
2.  Your method's performance on the Genomics dataset lags behind the state-of-the-art, unlike on the other two datasets. What is your hypothesis for this performance gap? Do you believe it is primarily due to the use of general-domain word embeddings on a specialized vocabulary, and have you considered experiments with domain-specific embeddings?
3.  The SVD-based scoring functions are weighted by the square of the highest singular value. Could you elaborate on the motivation for this weighting and quantify its impact on the final correlation scores? An ablation that removes this weighting would help clarify its importance.
