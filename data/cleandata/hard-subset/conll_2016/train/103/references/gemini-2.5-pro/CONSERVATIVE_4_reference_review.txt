### **Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel method for evaluating the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core intuition is that a coherent topic's constituent words should belong to a single, dominant semantic group, or "bucket". The paper proposes three techniques to implement this idea: one based on agglomerative clustering of word embeddings, and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the topic words' embedding matrix. The final and best-performing method, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, adds a heuristic-based reorganization step to refine the buckets. The authors evaluate their methods by measuring the correlation of the generated coherence scores with human judgments on three datasets, claiming state-of-the-art performance on two of them. They also present an extrinsic evaluation showing the utility of their metric for selecting high-quality topic sets to improve a downstream, weakly-supervised text classification task.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  The application of SVD to the matrix of word embeddings for a topic's top words as a method for identifying latent sub-themes and, consequently, measuring topic coherence.
2.  An empirical evaluation showing that this SVD-based approach, when combined with a heuristic reorganization step, can achieve competitive correlation with human coherence judgments on standard benchmarks.
3.  An application-oriented experiment demonstrating that the proposed coherence metric can be used to filter topic models to improve performance on a downstream task.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Clear Intuition:** The fundamental idea of grouping topic words into thematic "buckets" is intuitive and provides a tangible metaphor for what constitutes a coherent topic. It aligns well with how a human might assess a list of words for a common theme.

2.  **Strong Empirical Performance on Some Datasets:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method demonstrates strong empirical results, outperforming the reported state-of-the-art from Röder et al. (2015) on the NYT and 20NG datasets. This suggests the proposed approach is effective, at least for general domain corpora like news text.

3.  **Inclusion of Extrinsic Evaluation:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is a commendable addition. Too often, work on coherence metrics focuses solely on correlation with human ratings. Demonstrating that the metric can be used to select better models for a practical downstream task adds significant weight to the paper's claims of utility.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Methodological Complexity and Lack of Justification in Reorganization Step:** The best-performing method, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, introduces a complex, heuristic-driven reorganization step. The automatic threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) is presented without theoretical or strong empirical justification, appearing to be an ad-hoc formula engineered to solve observed deficiencies in the simpler SVD model. This lack of principled motivation undermines the soundness of the approach. While the authors frame this as being "parameter-free," it actually hides a complex, hard-coded decision process that is not transparent or easily interpretable.

2.  **Misleading "Parameter-Free" Claim:** The paper repeatedly claims its SVD-based methods are "completely parameter free," which is a significant overstatement. The entire approach is fundamentally dependent on several key (and unanalyzed) parameters: the choice of pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe), their dimensionality (300d), and the number of top words (N=10) selected from each topic. These are de facto hyperparameters that can have a substantial impact on performance, and their influence is not explored.

3.  **Insufficient Comparison with Established Baselines:** The evaluation compares performance against a single "best performing" value reported in the survey by Röder et al. (2015). This is inadequate for a paper proposing a new state-of-the-art method. A rigorous evaluation should include direct comparisons against well-established and widely used baselines (e.g., C_v, C_uci, C_umass) implemented within the same experimental framework. Without this, it is difficult to properly contextualize the performance of TBuckets.

4.  **Inconsistent Performance and Lack of Analysis:** The method's performance is inconsistent across datasets. It achieves strong results on news corpora (NYT, 20NG) but underperforms the baseline significantly on the Genomics dataset. The paper provides no discussion or analysis of this discrepancy. This is a critical omission, as it suggests the method may have limitations related to domain specificity, vocabulary, or the suitability of the general-purpose GloVe embeddings. Understanding the failure cases is as important as highlighting the successes.

5.  **Flaws in the Extrinsic Evaluation Setup:** The experiment in Section 4.3, while a good idea, is not designed for a clean comparison. Strategy S1 averages 5 separate runs, while S2 averages the best 5 selected from a pool of 10 runs. These are not directly comparable sets. A more robust evaluation would involve analyzing a single pool of runs (e.g., 10 runs) and comparing the average performance of the top-k models selected by the metric against the average of the bottom-k models and the average of all 10. This would provide a more direct and convincing measure of the metric's ability to discriminate between effective and ineffective topic models.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The formula for the dynamic threshold in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method appears ad-hoc. Can you provide a more rigorous justification for this specific formulation? How was it derived, and how sensitive are the results to changes in its structure?

2.  The SVD approach is predicated on the idea that eigenvectors of the ATA matrix represent distinct "sub-themes." What is the basis for this assumption? Could you provide a qualitative analysis showing what these eigenvectors represent for a few example topics? For instance, for the topic `{karzai, afghan, miner, ...}`, do different eigenvectors clearly correspond to the "Afghanistan" and "mining" sub-themes?

3.  Could you elaborate on why your method performs poorly on the Genomics dataset? Do you believe this is a fundamental limitation of the SVD-on-embeddings approach, or is it an issue with using general-domain word embeddings (trained on Wikipedia) for a specialized scientific corpus?

4.  Regarding the text classification experiment, would it be possible to re-frame the results by comparing the performance of the "Highest 5" vs. the "Lowest 5" coherence sets from the *same* pool of 10 runs? This would provide a clearer and more direct validation of your metric's utility.
