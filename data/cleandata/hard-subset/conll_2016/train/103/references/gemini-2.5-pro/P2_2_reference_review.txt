**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics generated by models like LDA. The core intuition is that coherent topics consist of words that can be grouped into a single, dominant thematic "bucket." The paper proposes three methods to implement this bucketing: an agglomerative clustering approach (TBuckets-Clustering), a method based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the topic words' embeddings (TBuckets-SVD), and a refined version that reorganizes the SVD-generated buckets (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The final coherence score is derived from the properties of the largest bucket formed. The authors evaluate their methods by correlating the generated scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, demonstrating that their TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method outperforms the state-of-the-art. They also showcase the practical utility of their metric in a weakly-supervised text classification task.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel, parameter-free topic coherence metric (TBuckets-SVD and SVD-Reorg):** The core contribution is the clever use of SVD on a matrix of word embeddings for a given topic. Using the resulting eigenvectors as representations of latent sub-themes ("buckets") is an original and intuitive idea. The fact that this method is parameter-free is a significant advantage over many existing metrics that require tuning.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves higher correlation with human judgments than previous best-performing methods on 2 out of 3 benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG). This provides strong evidence for the method's effectiveness.
3.  **Demonstration of practical utility:** The paper goes beyond simply reporting correlation scores by applying the metric to a downstream task. The experiment in Section 4.3 shows that using TBuckets to select higher-quality topic sets for human annotation can lead to improved performance in weakly-supervised text classification, strengthening the paper's overall impact.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper's primary strength lies in its results. As shown in Table 1, the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves Pearson's r values of 0.837 on NYT and 0.869 on 20NG, outperforming the SOTA reported by Roder et al. (2015) (0.806 and 0.859, respectively). This is a clear and significant improvement on standard benchmarks. The other proposed methods are also competitive.
2.  **Novelty and Intuition of the SVD-based approach:** The idea of performing SVD on the word embedding matrix of a single topic to identify its sub-themes is elegant and novel. The eigenvectors of the feature covariance matrix (ATA) intuitively represent orthogonal semantic directions within the topic, and using them as anchors for "buckets" is a well-motivated approach.
3.  **Parameter-Free Nature of the Best Method:** The SVD and SVD-Reorg techniques are presented as parameter-free. This is a highly desirable property for a coherence metric, as it removes the need for dataset-specific tuning (e.g., setting window sizes for PMI, distance thresholds for clustering) and makes the method more robust and generalizable. This stands in stark contrast to the large configuration space explored by Roder et al. (2015).
4.  **Clear Application to a Downstream Task:** The text classification experiment (Section 4.3) is a valuable addition. It demonstrates that the proposed metric is not just an abstract measure but a practical tool that can be used to improve real-world NLP pipelines by filtering out low-quality, difficult-to-annotate topics, thereby improving the efficiency and effectiveness of human-in-the-loop systems.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Justification for the Reorganization Heuristics:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method, which provides the best results, relies on a set of rules and a dynamically computed threshold that feel somewhat ad-hoc. The threshold formula (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) is presented without a strong theoretical or empirical justification. While the authors claim this makes the method parameter-free, the formula itself contains hard-coded operations (subtraction, division) that are not fully motivated. It is unclear how this specific formulation was derived and how sensitive the results are to variations of it. A more thorough ablation study or justification for these design choices is needed.
2.  **Limited Exploration of the Method's Properties:** The paper uses the top `n` eigenvectors to create `n` potential buckets for `n` words in a topic. This seems to imply that every word could potentially form its own sub-theme. It would be insightful to understand the behavior of the system if a lower-rank approximation was used (i.e., using only the top `k < n` eigenvectors to define buckets). This might provide a more principled way of determining the number of "true" sub-themes in a topic.
3.  **Insufficient Analysis:** The evaluation is sound but lacks depth. While overall correlation is high, there is no analysis of the types of topics where the metric fails or disagrees significantly with human raters. For example, does it struggle with topics containing polysemous words or topics that are abstract? A qualitative error analysis would strengthen the paper by providing a more nuanced understanding of the metric's limitations.
4.  **Minor Presentation Issues:** The paper could benefit from another proofreading pass. For instance, in the dry run for the non-coherent topic in Section 3, the first bucket for "official" is incorrectly listed as `{storm, weather}`. The formatting of the references is also inconsistent and contains errors (e.g., question marks, misplaced author names). While minor, these issues detract from the overall quality of the submission.

**Overall Recommendation:**

This is a strong paper with a novel, intuitive, and high-performing method for measuring topic coherence. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based approach and its demonstrated utility in a downstream task are significant strengths. The main weaknesses relate to the clarity and justification of the reorganization heuristics and a lack of deeper analysis. However, these weaknesses are addressable. I believe the contributions are significant enough for publication at CoNLL. My recommendation is **Accept**.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed motivation for the specific formula used to calculate the dynamic threshold in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method? Have you experimented with alternative formulations, and how did they perform?
2.  In the TBuckets-SVD method, you use the `n` eigenvectors corresponding to the `n` words in a topic. Have you considered using a smaller number of eigenvectors (e.g., based on a scree plot of singular values) to define the buckets? This might offer a more principled way to identify the number of latent sub-themes.
3.  All experiments use the top 10 words per topic. How does the performance of your SVD-based methods scale or change as the number of words per topic increases (e.g., to 20 or 30)? Does the assumption that each word is a potential bucket founder remain stable?
