### **Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics produced by models like LDA. The core intuition is that a coherent topic's constituent words should belong to a single, unified theme. The proposed method operationalizes this by automatically grouping a topic's top words into semantic "buckets". A topic is considered coherent if most of its words fall into a single large bucket. The authors propose three techniques for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the words' embedding matrix. The final coherence score is derived from the properties of the largest resulting bucket. The methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, where the parameter-free SVD-based variant with a reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) achieves state-of-the-art results on two of the datasets. The practical utility of the measure is also demonstrated in a weakly-supervised text classification task.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel and Intuitive Framework for Topic Coherence:** The paper presents a new conceptual approach to measuring coherence based on partitioning topic words into thematic groups ("buckets"). This is a departure from prevalent PMI-based or vector-averaging methods and offers a more interpretable model of what makes a topic coherent. The parameter-free SVD-based implementation is particularly valuable, removing the need for hyperparameter tuning common in other measures.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method demonstrates superior performance by achieving higher Pearson correlation with human judgments than prior work on the 20 Newsgroups and New York Times datasets, two standard benchmarks for this task.
3.  **Demonstration of Downstream Task Improvement:** The paper includes a practical application showing that using the proposed coherence measure to select higher-quality topic models leads to improved performance in a weakly supervised text classification task. This provides compelling evidence for the measure's real-world utility.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Strong Motivation:** The "bucketing" concept is original, intuitive, and well-motivated. The paper effectively uses examples to illustrate how this process mimics human evaluation of topics. This provides a strong conceptual foundation for the work, making it easy to understand and appreciate. The development of a parameter-free method based on SVD is a significant strength, addressing a major practical challenge in applying topic coherence metrics.
2.  **Excellent Empirical Results:** The paper reports very strong results, outperforming the best-performing configurations from the extensive study by Röder et al. (2015) on two out of three datasets. Achieving a new state-of-the-art is a significant accomplishment. The fact that this is achieved with a parameter-free method makes the results even more impressive.
3.  **Well-Designed Evaluation:** The evaluation is thorough. It follows the standard and rigorous methodology of correlating with human judgments on established public datasets. Crucially, the inclusion of the text classification experiment (Section 4.3) adds significant weight to the paper's claims. It demonstrates that the proposed metric is not just an academic exercise in matching human scores but a practical tool that can be used to improve downstream NLP applications by identifying more useful topic models.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The core idea of TBuckets is introduced clearly, the different implementations are described systematically, and the experimental setup and results are presented transparently. The examples in Section 3 are particularly helpful for building intuition.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Justification for the Reorganization Threshold:** While the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method yields the best results, the description of its key component—the dynamic threshold for reorganization—is not sufficiently justified. The formula `threshold = AWES − AWPS/AWES` is presented without a clear derivation or explanation of why this specific functional form was chosen. While it avoids a tunable hyperparameter, the design itself is an ad-hoc choice. This lack of theoretical or empirical grounding makes the reorganization step feel somewhat arbitrary and less principled than the core SVD approach. A discussion of the formula's properties or an ablation study on its components would significantly strengthen this part of the paper.
2.  **Unexplored Dependence on Word Embeddings:** The entire method is fundamentally dependent on the quality and characteristics of the input word embeddings. The experiments use a single set of pre-trained GloVe embeddings. The paper does not discuss or analyze how the choice of embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec vs. GloVe, different training corpora, dimensionality) might affect performance. This is a critical aspect, especially for domain-specific corpora like the Genomics dataset, where the model's performance is notably weaker. Using generic news- and web-trained embeddings for a specialized domain may be a primary reason for this performance gap, a limitation that warrants discussion.
3.  **Insufficient Intuition Behind SVD for Bucketing:** The paper states that the eigenvectors from SVD on the word embedding matrix serve as "bucket identifiers" representing orthogonal sub-themes. While this is an interesting idea, the intuition is not fully developed. A more detailed explanation of why the principal components of the word-word similarity matrix (A*A^T) should effectively capture distinct semantic themes within a topic would be beneficial. For example, one could argue that if a topic contains two sub-themes, the corresponding word vectors will primarily occupy a 2D subspace, and the top two eigenvectors will define the axes of this subspace. A more formal or illustrative explanation would make the SVD-based approach more compelling and less of a "black box."

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed rationale for the specific formulation of the dynamic threshold used in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method? Was this formula derived from theoretical principles, or were other formulations explored? How sensitive is the performance of the reorganization step to this specific choice?
2.  The method's performance appears to be highly dependent on the choice of word embeddings. Have you investigated how using different embeddings (e.g., domain-specific embeddings for the Genomics dataset, or contextual embeddings like BERT) impacts your results? A discussion on this dependency seems crucial for understanding the method's applicability.
3.  The SVD approach seems to implicitly assume that different sub-themes within a topic are orthogonal. How well does this assumption hold for real-world topics? Can you provide any examples of failure cases where this assumption might be violated (e.g., a topic with two closely related but non-orthogonal sub-themes) and discuss how your method behaves?
