### Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces a novel approach, TBuckets, for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core intuition is to mimic human judgment by grouping a topic's words into thematic "buckets." A topic is considered coherent if most of its words fall into a single large bucket. The authors propose and evaluate three automated bucketing techniques: (1) TBuckets-Clustering, which uses agglomerative clustering on word embeddings; (2) TBuckets-SVD, which applies Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to the topic's word embedding matrix to identify orthogonal sub-themes that act as buckets; and (3) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, an extension that refines the SVD-generated buckets based on word-to-word and word-to-bucket similarities. The methods are evaluated by computing the Pearson correlation between their coherence scores and human judgments on three standard datasets. The paper also demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed metric in a weakly-supervised text classification task.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel SVD-based Method for Topic Coherence:** The primary contribution is the TBuckets-SVD and its refined version, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg. The idea of applying SVD to the matrix of word embeddings for a *single topic* to discover its constituent sub-themes (represented by eigenvectors) is creative and distinct from prior work that typically relies on pairwise word similarities (like PMI) or vector centroids. Crucially, this SVD-based approach is presented as being parameter-free, a significant practical advantage.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance and Practical Demonstration:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms a comprehensive state-of-the-art baseline (Röder et al., 2015) on two out of three benchmark datasets. Furthermore, the authors demonstrate the real-world utility of their metric in a downstream application (Section 4.3), showing that selecting topic sets based on their coherence score improves the performance of a weakly-supervised text classifier.

### Strengths
1.  **Intuitive and Novel Method:** The core idea of "bucketing" is an intuitive and easily understandable proxy for thematic coherence. While the clustering-based implementation is straightforward, the SVD-based approach is particularly novel. It reframes the problem from measuring aggregate pairwise similarity to identifying the underlying dimensional structure of a topic's semantics, which is a conceptually appealing and powerful idea.
2.  **Parameter-Free Nature of the Main Method:** One of the most significant strengths of TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is that they require no parameter tuning. This contrasts sharply with many existing coherence measures, including the SOTA baseline which involves exploring a large configuration space. This makes the proposed method highly practical, easy to adopt, and more robust for use on new datasets without requiring extensive tuning.
3.  **Strong and Well-Validated Results:** The experimental evaluation is sound. The authors compare against a very strong and recent baseline on standard datasets, using the established evaluation protocol (Pearson's r with human scores). Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the 20NG and NYT datasets is a compelling result that validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4.  **Demonstration of Downstream Utility:** The inclusion of the text classification experiment (Section 4.3) is a major strength. It moves beyond simply correlating with human judgments and shows that the coherence metric can be used to make a tangible improvement in a practical NLP task. This provides strong evidence that the metric is not just an academic curiosity but a useful tool for practitioners working with topic models.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Clarity and Justification of the Reorganization Step:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method is the paper's best-performing technique, but its mechanism is complex and not fully justified. The conditions for moving words (e.g., LBPW > NBPE) are intuitive, but the dynamic threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) feels ad-hoc. The paper offers a brief explanation ("manages the gap in the ranges"), but a more rigorous justification or an ablation study on the threshold's formulation is needed to build confidence in its design. Without this, the claim of being "completely parameter-free" is weakened, as this complex, fixed formula functions like a highly engineered parameter.
2.  **Inconsistent Performance and Lack of Analysis:** The proposed SVD-based methods perform significantly worse than the baseline on the Genomics dataset. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg scores 0.691 while the baseline reaches 0.773. The paper does not analyze this discrepancy in the results section, only briefly mentioning it as a direction for future work (domain-specific similarities). This is a notable weakness. Is the method less suited for technical domains? Do the general-purpose GloVe embeddings fail here, and if so, does that reveal a brittleness in the approach? A discussion of this limitation is crucial for understanding the method's scope.
3.  **Insufficient Explanation of SVD Intuition:** The paper states that the V matrix from SVD provides orthogonal directions corresponding to sub-themes, but the intuition is not deeply explored. For a matrix A of size `n x d` where `n` is small (10 words) and `d` is large (300 dimensions), what does SVD practically capture? A more targeted explanation of why the principal components of the word vectors' covariance matrix (A^T A) should be interpreted as coherent "bucket identifiers" would strengthen the paper's theoretical grounding.
4.  **Ambiguity in the Final Scoring Function:** The coherence score for the SVD methods is calculated from properties of the largest bucket (size, WL, EVS) and then weighted by the "square of the highest singular value." The motivation for this specific weighting scheme is not provided. It is unclear how critical this weighting is to the final performance. An ablation study showing results with and without this weighting, or with a different weighting function (e.g., linear), would help clarify its contribution and the sensitivity of the results to this choice.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The formula for the dynamic threshold in the SVD-Reorg method is quite specific. Could you provide a more detailed justification for its form (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`)? Have you experimented with alternative formulations, and how sensitive is the model's performance to this choice?
2.  Your best-performing method, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, underperforms significantly on the Genomics dataset compared to the baseline and even your own clustering method. Do you have a concrete hypothesis for why this is the case? Does this suggest a limitation of using general-purpose embeddings like GloVe with your method in specialized domains, or a more fundamental issue with the SVD approach for certain types of vocabulary?
3.  For the SVD-based coherence scores, you weight the final score by the square of the highest singular value. What was the motivation for this particular weighting? How much does it contribute to the final correlation scores, and what is the performance without any weighting?
