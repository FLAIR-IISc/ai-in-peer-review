### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core intuition is that a coherent topic's constituent words should belong to a single, overarching theme. The method operationalizes this by grouping a topic's top words into "buckets" based on semantic similarity, derived from pre-trained word embeddings. A topic is considered more coherent if its words fall into one large bucket. The authors propose three variants for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the words' embedding matrix. The second SVD variant includes a "reorganization" step to refine the buckets. The methods are evaluated by correlating their coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, and their utility is demonstrated in a downstream text classification task.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel, SVD-based Approach to Topic Coherence:** The primary contribution is the TBuckets-SVD method, which cleverly uses SVD on a topic's word embedding matrix to identify orthogonal "sub-themes" (represented by right singular vectors) and assign words to them. This is a conceptually new way to model intra-topic structure for coherence evaluation.
2.  **A High-Performing and Parameter-Free Coherence Metric:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art or superior performance on 2 out of 3 benchmark datasets. Critically, the authors claim it is "completely parameter-free," which represents a significant practical advantage over existing methods (like those explored by Röder et al., 2015) that require extensive parameter tuning or configuration.
3.  **Demonstration of Downstream Task Improvement:** The paper shows that using TBuckets to pre-select higher-quality sets of topics leads to improved performance in a weakly supervised text classification task. This provides valuable extrinsic validation, demonstrating that the proposed coherence metric has practical utility beyond simply correlating with human scores.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method is shown to outperform the state-of-the-art results reported by Röder et al. (2015) on the NYT and 20NG datasets. Achieving this level of performance on established benchmarks is a significant strength and validates the effectiveness of the underlying "bucketing" idea.
2.  **Novelty and Intuition:** The conceptual framework of bucketing words is intuitive and well-explained with clear examples. The use of SVD to find orthogonal conceptual dimensions within a small set of word vectors is a novel and elegant application of the technique. It provides a principled way to identify and quantify sub-themes, which is a key source of incoherence.
3.  **Parameter-Free Design:** The main SVD-based methods are presented as parameter-free, which is a major advantage for usability and reproducibility. Many existing coherence metrics require choosing confirmation measures (PMI, NPMI), context windows, or external corpora, creating a complex configuration space. A method that works well "out of the box" is highly valuable to practitioners who want to evaluate their topic models without extensive tuning.
4.  **Extrinsic Evaluation:** The application to text classification (Section 4.3) is a strong point. It moves beyond intrinsic correlation with human scores and shows a tangible benefit: using the metric to filter topic model runs can improve downstream task performance. This makes a compelling case for the metric's real-world relevance.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The "Parameter-Free" Claim is Questionable:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method, which provides the best results, relies on a "dynamic threshold" for reorganizing words. The formula for this threshold, `AWES − AWPS/AWES`, is a specific, non-obvious heuristic. While it is "dynamic" in that it's calculated from the topic's data, the formula itself is a fixed design choice that functions like a hyperparameter. The paper provides no justification for this specific formulation over other potential ones. This lack of ablation or justification weakens the "completely parameter-free" claim, as a crucial, unevaluated modeling choice is central to the best-performing method.
2.  **Limited Baselines and Comparison:** The paper compares its results only to the single best-performing setting reported in Röder et al. (2015). It does not re-implement or compare against classic and widely-used baselines like `C_v`, `C_umass`, or `C_uci` within its own experimental setup. While comparing against the SOTA is important, providing a direct comparison to these foundational metrics would give a more complete picture of the method's performance and trade-offs. The Röder et al. work is a framework exploring a large space of measures, and comparing to a single point from that space may not be fully representative.
3.  **Lack of Complexity Analysis:** The proposed method requires computing an SVD for every topic being evaluated. While SVD on a 10x300 matrix is trivial, the paper does not discuss the computational complexity or scalability. How does the runtime compare to PMI-based measures, which rely on large pre-computed count tables? This becomes relevant if one wants to evaluate topics with more words (e.g., top 20) or evaluate thousands of topics during model selection. A brief discussion of computational costs would be beneficial.
4.  **Insufficient Justification for SVD Components:** The paper states it focuses on the V matrix from SVD because its vectors represent "orthogonal d dimensional directions." While this is technically correct, the link between these mathematical objects and the semantic concept of "sub-themes" is asserted rather than deeply explained. A more detailed justification for why the right singular vectors (as opposed to, for instance, clustering the word vectors directly) are the ideal representation for "bucket identifiers" would strengthen the methodological grounding.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the design of the dynamic threshold used in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method? How did you arrive at the formula `AWES − AWPS/AWES`? Were other formulations considered, and if so, how did they perform? Providing this justification seems crucial for assessing the robustness of the method and the claim that it is parameter-free.
2.  The evaluation compares against the best result from Röder et al. (2015). Could you comment on how your method compares to more standard and simpler baselines like `C_v` (which also relies on an external corpus) or `C_uci` (NPMI-based coherence)? A direct comparison would help situate the contribution more clearly within the landscape of coherence metrics.
3.  The text classification experiment is compelling. However, you report the average of the 5 runs with the highest coherence and the 5 with the lowest. Given the stochastic nature of LDA, have you analyzed the variance? Is the difference between the "Highest 5" and "Lowest 5" groups statistically significant?
