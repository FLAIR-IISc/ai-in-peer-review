### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics generated by models like LDA. The core idea is to simulate a human-like process of grouping a topic's words into thematic "buckets." A topic is considered coherent if most of its words fall into a single, large bucket. The authors propose three methods to implement this bucketing: an agglomerative clustering-based approach (TBuckets-Clustering) and two more novel methods based on Singular Value Decomposition (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The authors evaluate their methods by correlating the generated coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, demonstrating that their best-performing method, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, achieves state-of-the-art results on two of the datasets. A key claimed advantage of the SVD-based methods is that they are parameter-free. Finally, the paper demonstrates the practical utility of the metric in a weakly-supervised text classification task, showing that selecting topic models with higher coherence scores can lead to improved classification performance.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A Novel SVD-based Topic Coherence Framework:** The use of SVD on a matrix of word embeddings to identify latent thematic dimensions (represented by eigenvectors) and then group words into buckets is a novel and conceptually interesting departure from traditional pairwise PMI or vector similarity measures. This provides a new way of thinking about sub-themes within a single topic.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that its best method, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, outperforms previously reported state-of-the-art results on the NYT and 20NG datasets. This is a significant achievement in the well-studied area of topic coherence evaluation.

3.  **Practical Application and Validation:** The inclusion of a downstream task (weakly-supervised classification) to demonstrate the metric's utility is a valuable contribution. It shows that the metric is not just an academic exercise in correlating with human scores but can be used to improve the performance of real-world NLP systems that rely on topic models.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The primary strength of this paper is its performance. Achieving a new state-of-the-art on established benchmarks is a clear and compelling argument for acceptance. The improvements over the best results from Röder et al. (2015) on the NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859) datasets are substantial.

2.  **Intuitive and Well-Motivated Core Idea:** The "bucketing" metaphor is intuitive and provides a clear conceptual model for what makes a topic coherent. The dry-run examples in Section 3 effectively illustrate this intuition, making the motivation for the entire approach easy to follow.

3.  **Largely Parameter-Free Approach:** While I have some reservations (see Weaknesses), the claim that the SVD-based methods are parameter-free is a major practical advantage. The state-of-the-art model they compare against (Röder et al., 2015) is a framework that spans a large configuration space, requiring significant effort to find the optimal setup. A method that works well out-of-the-box is highly valuable for practitioners who want to quickly assess the quality of their topic models without extensive tuning.

4.  **Novelty of Methodology:** The proposed SVD methodology is genuinely novel. While SVD and word embeddings are standard tools, their application in this specific manner—using eigenvectors of the embedding covariance matrix as proxies for thematic centroids—is creative. The reorganization step, while heuristic, shows a thoughtful attempt to correct for the initial SVD's limitations.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for Key Methodological Choices:** Several crucial design decisions in the SVD-based methods feel ad-hoc and lack theoretical or strong empirical justification.
    *   **The Reorganization Threshold:** The formula for the dynamic threshold in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, `AWES - AWPS/AWES`, is highly specific and appears arbitrary. There is no explanation for why this particular combination of terms is the correct one. This undermines the "parameter-free" claim, as it replaces a tunable parameter with a complex, unmotivated heuristic. An ablation study showing the effect of different components of this formula would have strengthened the paper.
    *   **Final Score Weighting:** The paper states that the final coherence scores are weighted by the "square of the highest singular value." Why the square? Why only the highest value? This feels like another post-hoc tuning step that is not well explained or justified. How does performance change without this weighting?
    *   **Choice of Eigenvectors:** The intuition behind using the eigenvectors of A^TA (the principal components of the embedding dimensions) as "bucket identifiers" is not fully fleshed out. While it captures the main axes of variation in the embedding space for the given words, it's not immediately obvious why these axes should correspond to coherent semantic sub-themes. A clearer explanation would be beneficial.

2.  **Clarity of the SVD Method Description:** The description in Section 3 is slightly imprecise. It says, "We use the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers," where `n` is the number of words. The `V` matrix in SVD is dxd, where d is the embedding dimension (300). The paper should clarify if it means the `n` eigenvectors corresponding to the `n` largest singular values. A more precise mathematical description of the process would improve reproducibility and clarity.

3.  **Inconsistent Results in Downstream Task:** The text classification experiment is a strong point, but the results in Table 3 contain an anomaly that weakens the paper's conclusion. For the MEDICAL vs SPACE dataset, the average F1 for the 5 topic model runs with the *lowest* coherence scores (95.37) is higher than that for the runs with the *highest* coherence scores (94.83). The authors' explanation that this dataset is "easy" is insufficient. This counter-example directly challenges the central hypothesis that higher coherence (as measured by TBuckets) reliably leads to better human annotation and thus better downstream performance. This requires a more thorough discussion.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the specific functional form of the reorganization threshold in Section 3.1 (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`)? Was this formula derived from first principles, or was it found through empirical exploration? How sensitive is the model's performance to variations of this heuristic?

2.  Similarly, what is the rationale behind weighting the final coherence score by the *square* of the highest singular value? What is the performance impact of removing this weighting or using a different function (e.g., linear weighting)?

3.  Regarding the text classification experiment (Section 4.3), how do you explain the contradictory result on the MEDICAL vs SPACE dataset, where lower-coherence topic sets led to slightly better classification performance? Does this suggest limitations to the utility of your metric or the experimental setup?
