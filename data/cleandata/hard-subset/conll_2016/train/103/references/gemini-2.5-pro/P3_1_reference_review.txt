### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics generated by probabilistic topic models. The central idea is that the words of a coherent topic should cluster into a single dominant thematic group, or "bucket." The paper operationalizes this intuition by proposing three methods for automatically creating these buckets from topic words represented by pre-trained word embeddings: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The final coherence score is derived from the size and composition of the largest bucket. The methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets and are also tested on a downstream text classification task.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel and Intuitive SVD-based Coherence Metric:** The use of SVD on a matrix of word embeddings to identify latent sub-themes ("buckets") within a topic is a creative and original approach. The subsequent reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) is a principled way to refine these buckets.
2.  **A State-of-the-Art Parameter-Free Method:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two of the three benchmark datasets, outperforming the complex and parameter-heavy framework of Röder et al. (2015). Its parameter-free nature is a significant practical advantage, making it easier to apply and more robust.
3.  **Extrinsic Validation on a Downstream Task:** The paper successfully demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed metric by showing that selecting topic models with higher coherence scores leads to improved performance in a weakly supervised text classification setting. This provides strong evidence that the metric captures a meaningful and useful aspect of topic quality.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this work lies in its results. The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique surpasses a strong, state-of-the-art baseline on the NYT and 20NG datasets. This is a compelling achievement, particularly since the baseline method itself represents an extensive exploration of a large configuration space of coherence measures. The fact that a single, fixed method can outperform a highly tuned one is a significant finding.

2.  **Methodological Novelty and Simplicity:** The core "bucketing" concept is intuitive and provides a fresh perspective on topic coherence that moves beyond pairwise word similarity scores (like PMI). The application of SVD to capture orthogonal "themes" is particularly innovative. While the reorganization step adds complexity, the core SVD approach is elegant, and its parameter-free design is a major advantage over competing methods that require tuning of window sizes, probability smoothing, or aggregation functions. This makes the proposed method much more accessible for practitioners.

3.  **Clear Practical Implications:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is a crucial and well-executed part of the paper. It grounds the abstract task of coherence measurement in a concrete application. By showing a tangible benefit—improved F1 scores—from using their metric to filter topic models, the authors make a convincing case for the real-world relevance of their work. This is often missing in papers on evaluation metrics and greatly strengthens the submission.

4.  **Well-Written and Structured:** The paper is clearly written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The motivation is well-established with clear examples, the methods are described in sufficient detail, and the experiments are well-designed and directly support the claims.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for SVD Methodology:** The conceptual leap from the mathematical properties of SVD to its application for thematic bucketing is not adequately explained. The paper states that the eigenvectors of V represent "orthogonal d dimensional directions" and serve as "distinct bucket identifiers," but the intuition for *why* this should work is missing. Why should an abstract direction in embedding space correspond to a semantic theme? And why is assigning a word to its most similar eigenvector an effective grouping strategy? A more detailed justification, perhaps with a geometric interpretation or a small, illustrative example, would make the SVD-based methods less of a "black box" and more convincing.

2.  **Ad-hoc Formulation of the Reorganization Step:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method, which delivers the best results, relies on a complex set of rules and a dynamically computed threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`). While this makes the method technically parameter-free, the formula for the threshold itself feels arbitrary and engineered. The paper provides no derivation or justification for this specific functional form. This lack of explanation makes the key component of the best-performing model seem like a heuristic, which undermines the elegance of the SVD approach and raises questions about its generalizability.

3.  **Limited Exploration of Embedding Sensitivity:** The experiments rely exclusively on a single set of 300-dimensional GloVe embeddings. The performance of any method based on word embeddings is highly dependent on the quality and properties of those embeddings. The significantly weaker performance on the Genomics dataset, where general-purpose embeddings may be suboptimal, highlights this dependency. The paper would be much stronger if it included an analysis of how the results change with different embedding models (e.g., word2vec, FastText) or dimensionalities. This would provide valuable insight into the robustness of the TBuckets approach.

4.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis of Failures:** The paper presents examples of good and bad topics (Table 2), but a more thorough analysis of the failure cases would be beneficial. For instance, on the Genomics dataset where the method underperforms, what kinds of topics is it misjudging? Are there specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., polysemy, technical jargon) that the bucketing approach struggles with? Such an analysis would provide a more nuanced understanding of the method's limitations.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed intuition or theoretical grounding for using the right singular vectors (eigenvectors of V) as thematic "bucket identifiers"? For instance, how does this compare to a more standard approach like performing k-means clustering on the word embeddings, where the number of clusters *k* could be determined by analyzing the spectrum of singular values?

2.  The formula for the dynamic threshold in the SVD-Reorg step is crucial for its performance but appears somewhat ad-hoc. Could you explain the rationale behind this specific formulation (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`)? Were alternative formulations for a dynamic threshold considered, and if so, how did they perform?

3.  Given the performance drop on the Genomics dataset, have you performed any analysis on the GloVe embeddings for the vocabulary in that domain? Does this result suggest that the TBuckets-SVD approach is particularly sensitive to the quality of the pre-trained embeddings when dealing with specialized corpora?
