### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel family of methods for measuring the coherence of topics from probabilistic topic models. The core idea is to group a topic's top words into "buckets" based on semantic similarity, with the intuition that a coherent topic will be dominated by a single large bucket. The authors propose three variants: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the topic words' embeddings. The primary contribution is the proposed TBuckets methodology, particularly the `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` variant, which is shown to achieve correlation with human judgments that is competitive with or slightly exceeds a state-of-the-art baseline on two of three benchmark datasets. A secondary contribution is a small-scale application study suggesting that this metric can be used to select better-quality topic models for a downstream text classification task.

### Strengths

1.  **Intuitive Premise:** The fundamental idea that a coherent topic should consist of words that fall into a single thematic group is simple and compelling. The "bucketing" approach is a reasonable operationalization of this intuition, and the use of pre-trained word embeddings to measure semantic similarity is a standard and appropriate choice.

2.  **Competitive Empirical Results:** The best-performing proposed method, `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`, achieves strong Pearson correlation scores with human judgments on the NYT and 20NG datasets, slightly surpassing the reported baseline from Röder et al. (2015). This demonstrates that the approach is empirically viable.

3.  **Attempt at a Parameter-Free Method:** The authors' goal of creating a parameter-free metric with `TBuckets-SVD` is commendable. Many existing coherence metrics require external corpora or careful parameter tuning, so a method that avoids this would be a valuable contribution to the field.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The experimental validation is not sufficiently rigorous. The authors compare their results to a single value reported as the "best performing settings" from Röder et al. (2015), a survey paper that explored a vast configuration space. This is not a direct or fair comparison. A robust evaluation would require re-implementing and running widely accepted, standard coherence metrics like C_v, C_uci, and C_npmi on the exact same data. The absence of a direct comparison to these established methods makes it difficult to assess the practical utility and advancement of the proposed technique. The reported improvements are marginal and, without stronger baselines, may not be meaningful.

2.  **Questionable Methodological Soundness of the SVD Approach:** The use of SVD, while technically interesting, lacks strong theoretical justification in this context. The paper claims the right-singular vectors represent "orthogonal d dimensional directions" that can act as "bucket identifiers," but this is more of an assertion than a proven principle for coherence measurement. It is unclear why this specific decomposition is superior to more straightforward clustering methods like k-means. The approach feels like an engineering heuristic rather than a well-grounded technique.

3.  **The Best-Performing Method Undermines the "Parameter-Free" Claim:** The strongest results are achieved not by the "parameter-free" `TBuckets-SVD` but by `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`, which introduces a complex, multi-step reorganization process governed by a "dynamic threshold." This threshold is calculated with a highly specific, unmotivated formula (`AWES − AWPS/AWES`). This appears to be a carefully engineered heuristic designed to patch deficiencies in the original SVD approach. This post-hoc complexity contradicts the elegance of a parameter-free method and introduces a form of hidden parameterization that is not rigorously justified or analyzed.

4.  **Weak Validation via Downstream Task:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is presented as evidence of the metric's utility, but the experiment is too small-scale to be conclusive. The comparison is based on an average of only 5 runs, and the differences in performance are small. The paper provides no statistical significance testing, making it impossible to know if the observed improvements are real or simply due to the inherent stochasticity of topic modeling. This section reads more as a preliminary proof-of-concept than a solid validation of the metric's practical value.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more principled justification for using the right-singular vectors of the word embedding matrix as thematic "bucket identifiers"? Why is this formulation theoretically preferable to more conventional clustering algorithms (e.g., k-means) applied to the word embeddings?

2.  The dynamic threshold in `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` is a crucial component of your best-performing model, yet its formulation appears ad-hoc. Could you elaborate on its derivation? Furthermore, have you conducted an ablation study to test the sensitivity of your results to this specific formula, or compared it against a simpler, fixed threshold?

3.  To establish the contribution of your work more clearly, could you provide results comparing TBuckets directly against established and widely-used coherence metrics such as C_v and C_npmi (as described by Lau et al., 2014, and Röder et al., 2015), implemented and run on the same datasets? This would provide a much more direct and convincing comparison than citing a single result from prior work.

4.  Regarding the text classification experiment, are the reported improvements in macro-F1 score statistically significant? Please provide p-values or confidence intervals to support the claim that selecting topic sets based on your coherence score leads to a reliable improvement in downstream task performance.
