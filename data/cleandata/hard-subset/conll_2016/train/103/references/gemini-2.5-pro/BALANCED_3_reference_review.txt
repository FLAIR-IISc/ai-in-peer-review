### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel family of methods for evaluating the coherence of topics produced by topic models. The central idea is that a coherent topic's words should belong to a single, unified theme. TBuckets operationalizes this by attempting to group a topic's top words into thematic "buckets" based on the semantic similarity of their pre-trained word embeddings. The paper proposes three specific methods for this bucketing: one using agglomerative clustering and two parameter-free variants using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The second SVD-based method adds a heuristic reorganization step to refine the buckets. A topic's final coherence score is calculated based on the properties of the largest resulting bucket, such as its size. The authors evaluate their methods by measuring the correlation of their scores with human judgments on three standard benchmark datasets, showing that their best method (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) outperforms the previous state-of-the-art on two of them. They further demonstrate the practical utility of their metric by using it to select high-quality topic models for improving a downstream weakly supervised text classification task.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A novel, intuitive, and parameter-free method for topic coherence evaluation.** The core idea of using SVD on word embeddings to discover and group words along a topic's primary semantic axes is a clever and original approach. Its parameter-free nature is a significant practical advantage over many competing methods that require extensive tuning.
2.  **State-of-the-art empirical results on standard benchmarks.** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves a higher correlation with human coherence judgments than previously reported best results on the widely-used NYT and 20NG datasets, providing strong evidence of its effectiveness.
3.  **A practical demonstration of the metric's utility in a downstream application.** By showing that the coherence metric can be used to select better topic models that improve performance on a text classification task, the paper effectively bridges the gap between intrinsic evaluation (coherence) and extrinsic, task-based evaluation.

### Strengths

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Method:** The "bucketing" metaphor is an intuitive and appealing way to conceptualize topic coherence. The implementation via SVD on word embeddings is particularly clever, framing the problem as one of identifying orthogonal semantic sub-themes within a topic. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based approaches is a major strength, making the method more robust and easier to apply than techniques requiring extensive parameter sweeps, such as the framework by Röder et al. (2015).
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper presents compelling results, clearly outperforming a strong state-of-the-art baseline on two of the three benchmark datasets (NYT: 0.837 vs. 0.806; 20NG: 0.869 vs. 0.859). This demonstrates that the proposed method is not just conceptually interesting but also highly effective in practice, capturing nuances of topic quality that align well with human perception.
3.  **Demonstrated Utility for Downstream Tasks:** The application of TBuckets to weakly supervised text classification (Section 4.3) is a significant and commendable part of the paper. Many studies on coherence metrics stop at intrinsic evaluation (i.e., correlation with human scores). By showing that selecting topic model runs based on their TBuckets score leads to better classification accuracy, the authors provide strong evidence for the metric's real-world utility. This makes the work more impactful and relevant.
4.  **Clarity and Thoroughness:** The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The authors do a good job of explaining the intuition behind their methods with concrete examples. The systematic exploration of three different bucketing techniques (Clustering, SVD, SVD-Reorg) and multiple scoring functions (size, WL, EVS) contributes to a thorough investigation of the proposed idea.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity and Justification of the Reorganization Step:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method provides the best results, but the description of the reorganization logic is dense and the justification for the specific formula for the dynamic threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) is not provided. This component feels somewhat heuristic and complex. While the authors claim it is "parameter free", it introduces a non-trivial, hard-coded formula whose properties are not explored. A more detailed explanation, an ablation study on the components of this formula, or a comparison to a simpler fixed threshold would significantly strengthen the paper's claims about this crucial step.
2.  **Limited Comparison to Individual Baselines:** The primary comparison is against the "best performing settings" reported by Röder et al. (2015), which represents the upper bound found by an extensive search over a large configuration space. While this is a strong point of comparison, the paper would benefit from direct comparisons against other well-established, individual coherence metrics like `C_v`, `C_uci` (NPMI-based), and `C_umass`. Including these prominent methods in Table 1 would provide a clearer, more comprehensive picture of where TBuckets fits within the broader landscape of coherence metrics.
3.  **Unexplored Dependence on Word Embeddings:** The method is fundamentally dependent on the quality of the input word embeddings. The experiments rely on a single set of pre-trained GloVe embeddings. The performance could vary significantly with different embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, FastText, BERT) or with embeddings trained specifically on the target domain corpora. This is particularly relevant for the Genomics dataset, where the method underperforms the SOTA, potentially because general-purpose embeddings fail to capture the specialized semantics of the domain. A discussion of this sensitivity would make the work more complete.
4.  **Lack of Computational Analysis:** The paper does not discuss the computational complexity of the proposed methods. While performing SVD on a 10x300 matrix for a single topic is trivial, applying this to evaluate a model with hundreds of topics, or potentially incorporating it into a model's training loop, could become a bottleneck. A brief analysis of the computational cost in comparison to prominent PMI-based baselines would be a valuable addition for practitioners considering adopting this method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the derivation and intuition behind the specific formula used for the dynamic threshold in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method? Have you experimented with alternative formulations, and how sensitive are the results to this specific choice?
2.  Your method underperforms the state-of-the-art on the Genomics dataset. Do you attribute this to the use of general-purpose GloVe embeddings being less suitable for this specialized domain? Have you considered experiments with domain-specific embeddings to test this hypothesis?
3.  In the text classification experiment (Section 4.3), you define the quality of a *set* of topics by summing the individual topic coherence scores. Could you comment on whether this simple summation is the most appropriate aggregation method? For instance, a model with a few excellent topics and many poor ones might receive a similar score to a model with consistently mediocre topics. Have you explored other aggregation schemes, such as the average or median score?
