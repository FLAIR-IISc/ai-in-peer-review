### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics produced by topic models. The core idea is that a coherent topic's words should cluster into a single large semantic group, or "bucket". The authors propose three methods to implement this: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), one of which includes a "reorganization" step. The methods are evaluated by correlating their coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets. The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  A new and intuitive framework (TBuckets) for topic coherence measurement based on partitioning a topic's words into semantic groups.
2.  An SVD-based implementation (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two of the three evaluated datasets.
3.  An application study demonstrating that selecting topic models based on the proposed coherence score can improve performance on a downstream weakly supervised text classification task.

### Strengths

1.  **Intuitive and Novel Approach:** The central idea of bucketing words based on semantic themes is conceptually simple and provides an intuitive explanation for why a topic is or is not coherent. This contrasts with more abstract pairwise-similarity aggregation methods and is a commendable direction of research.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art results on the NYT and 20NG datasets, surpassing the best-performing configurations reported in the comprehensive study by Röder et al. (2015). This is a strong empirical result.
3.  **Parameter-Free Method:** A significant advantage of the SVD-based methods is their claim to be parameter-free. This is highly desirable in practice, as many existing coherence metrics require access to an external corpus and involve several tunable parameters (e.g., window size, confirmation measure choice) which can be computationally expensive to optimize.
4.  **Demonstrated Utility:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 provides a practical use-case for the proposed metric. By showing that selecting topic models with higher coherence leads to better downstream performance, the authors effectively motivate the need for their work beyond simply correlating with human scores.

### Weaknesses

Despite the promising results, the paper suffers from a significant number of methodological ambiguities, presentation issues, and a lack of experimental rigor, which collectively undermine the confidence in its conclusions.

1.  **Insufficient Methodological Justification:** The technical details of the SVD-based methods are not well-motivated.
    *   The choice to use eigenvectors from the V matrix (from A = USV^T) as "bucket identifiers" is justified with the vague statement that they represent "some definite concept". The eigenvectors of A^T A capture the principal directions of variance in the feature (embedding dimension) space. It is not at all obvious why these directions should correspond to distinct semantic sub-themes or why a word should be assigned to the bucket of its most similar eigenvector. A more rigorous explanation is needed.
    *   The claim of the SVD-Reorg method being "completely parameter less" is questionable. The reorganization step introduces a "dynamic threshold" computed with the formula `AWES − AWPS/AWES`. This formula is presented without any theoretical or empirical justification, making it appear arbitrary and ad-hoc. The explanation that it "manages the gap in the ranges" is insufficient. This is effectively a data-dependent hyperparameter, and its formulation requires much stronger support.
    *   The final scoring mechanism is also poorly motivated. Weighting the scores by the "square of the highest singular value" is stated without any explanation. Why the square? Why only the highest value? This seems like a feature that was engineered to fit the data.

2.  **Lack of Experimental Rigor:**
    *   The comparison to the state-of-the-art is indirect. The authors compare their results to the best values *reported* in Röder et al. (2015). A direct comparison requires implementing and running key baselines (e.g., C_V, C_NPMI) within the authors' exact experimental setup to control for variables like the choice of word embeddings and preprocessing.
    *   The results for TBuckets-Clustering (Table 1) are reported with the optimal `Max Dist` parameter values shown in parentheses. This implies the parameter was tuned on the test set, which would inflate the performance. The procedure for selecting this parameter should be clarified; ideally, it should be tuned on a separate development set.
    *   The paper lacks any statistical significance testing. It is unclear whether the reported improvements over the baseline (e.g., 0.837 vs. 0.806 on NYT) are statistically significant. This is a critical omission for a paper focused on metric evaluation.

3.  **Serious Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper exhibits a concerning lack of attention to detail, which is unsuitable for a top-tier conference.
    *   **Citations:** The in-text citations and the reference list are extremely sloppy and inconsistent. For instance, citations appear as "(David M. Blei, 2007)", and the bibliography contains malformed entries like "Aletras and Stevenson.,?" and "Blei et al\.,?". Many details are missing or incorrectly formatted. This is a major flaw that suggests the paper was prepared in a rush.
    *   **Typos and Errors:** There are numerous grammatical errors and typos throughout the text (e.g., "parameter less", "a indication", "repeated for 5 times"). More critically, there appears to be a copy-paste error in the example dry run in Section 3 for the non-coherent topic, where "Bucket-1: {storm, weather}" is incorrectly listed.
    *   **Clarity:** Table 1 is cluttered and difficult to parse. The `WL*EVS` scoring metric is not defined. The placement of the Related Work section near the end of the paper is unconventional and hinders the reader's ability to contextualize the work early on.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Can you provide a more rigorous theoretical or geometric justification for using the right singular vectors (columns of V) as "bucket identifiers" and for assigning words based on cosine similarity to these vectors?
2.  Could you please elaborate on the derivation and justification for the specific formula used to compute the dynamic threshold in the SVD-Reorg method? Why is this formulation preferable to simpler alternatives, and how does it prevent the method from being considered parametric?
3.  What is the rationale for weighting the final coherence scores by the square of the largest singular value? How was this specific weighting function chosen?
4.  For the text classification experiment, how do you ensure that the difference in performance between the "Highest 5" and "Lowest 5" topic sets is due to coherence and not simply due to the LDA inference algorithm converging to a poorer local optimum (which might result in both lower coherence and lower classification accuracy)?
