**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**
This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics from topic models. The core idea is that a coherent topic consists of words that can be grouped into a single dominant thematic "bucket". The authors propose three methods to implement this bucketing: one based on agglomerative clustering of word embeddings, a second using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to identify orthogonal sub-themes, and a third that applies a reorganization step to the SVD-based buckets. The final coherence score is derived from properties of the largest resulting bucket. The methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets and in a downstream text classification task.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  A novel and intuitive framework for topic coherence based on grouping topic words via their embeddings, which contrasts with prevalent methods that aggregate pairwise word similarities.
2.  The application of SVD to decompose a topic's word set into orthogonal "sub-themes", offering a new way to analyze a topic's internal thematic structure.
3.  An extrinsic evaluation that attempts to demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed metric for selecting high-quality topic models to improve a weakly-supervised classification task.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novelty and Intuition:** The central "bucketing" concept is elegant and provides a fresh perspective on topic coherence. It moves beyond simply averaging pairwise similarities (like PMI-based metrics) and instead tries to discover the latent thematic structure of a topic. The SVD-based approach, in particular, is a clever way to operationalize the search for these sub-themes.
2.  **Strong Intrinsic Evaluation Results:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art Pearson correlation with human judgments on two widely used benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG). Outperforming the results from Röder et al. (2015), which involved an extensive search over a large configuration space, is a notable achievement.
3.  **Inclusion of an Extrinsic Evaluation:** The authors rightly recognize that intrinsic correlation is not the only measure of a metric's utility. By testing their metric in a model selection scenario for a downstream classification task (Section 4.3), they provide a practical demonstration of its potential impact. While the results are not uniformly positive, the inclusion of this experiment strengthens the paper.

**Weaknesses**
My arguments against acceptance are primarily rooted in a lack of methodological rigor, sloppy presentation, and overstated claims, which collectively undermine the credibility of the work despite its promising results.

1.  **Unjustified and Ad-hoc Methodological Choices:** The paper's core methods contain several critical design choices that are presented without sufficient justification, giving them an ad-hoc appearance.
    *   The reorganization step in the best-performing model, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, hinges on a "dynamic threshold" defined by the formula `AWES - AWPS/AWES`. The justification for this specific, complex formula is vague ("The ratio...denotes flexibility...The subtraction...manages the gap..."). This is not a principled derivation. The work would be much stronger if the authors had provided an ablation study or sensitivity analysis on this formula's structure.
    *   The final coherence scores for the SVD-based methods are weighted by the "square of the highest singular value." There is no justification provided for squaring the value or for using only the single highest value. This seems arbitrary and requires theoretical or empirical support.
    *   The TBuckets-SVD method uses the "first n eigenvectors" from the V matrix. The dimensions of V are d x d, so this choice needs clarification and justification. Is it the eigenvectors corresponding to the n largest singular values? Why `n` (the number of topic words)? A more detailed explanation is required.

2.  **Poor Presentation and Numerous Errors:** The paper is marred by a concerning number of typographical and logical errors, which suggests a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   The manual dry-run in Section 3, intended to build intuition, contains a significant copy-paste error. In the second example, the text reads "Bucket-1: {storm, weather}", which is nonsensically copied from the preceding example. Such a conspicuous error in a core explanatory section is a major flaw.
    *   The reference list is in a state of disarray. It is riddled with formatting errors, stray question marks, repeated author names, and inconsistent styles (e.g., the entries for "Aletras and Stevenson.,? 2013, Aletras and Stevenson.," and "Blei and Lafferty.,? 2006, Blei and Lafferty."). For a submission to a top-tier venue, this level of sloppiness is unacceptable and hinders the reader's ability to consult the cited works.

3.  **Overstated Claims Regarding Parameters:** The paper repeatedly emphasizes that its SVD-based methods are "completely parameter free". This claim is misleading. The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method relies on the highly specific threshold formula discussed in Weakness #1. While this formula is "dynamic" and not tuned on a development set, it is a hard-coded, complex modeling choice that functions as a non-tunable hyperparameter. The claim of being "parameter free" implies a simplicity and robustness that is not fully supported by the method's description.

4.  **Unconvincing Extrinsic Evaluation Results:** The results of the text classification experiment in Section 4.3 are ambiguous and do not provide unequivocal support for the metric's utility. For the MED-SPACE dataset, topic models ranked with the *lowest* coherence scores by the metric actually yield better classification accuracy than those ranked highest. The authors' post-hoc explanation that this dataset is "easy" is unsatisfying and fails to address this contradictory result, suggesting potential limitations of the metric.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide a more rigorous justification for the specific formula `AWES - AWPS/AWES` used as the dynamic threshold in the SVD-Reorg method? How sensitive are the final results to this exact formulation? For instance, what happens if a simpler threshold like `(AWES - AWPS)` or a constant factor is used?
2.  Please justify the decision to weight the final SVD-based coherence scores by the *square* of the highest singular value. What is the impact of this weighting on the final correlation scores?
3.  In Table 3, your metric appears to fail at identifying the best topic models for the MED-SPACE dataset, where the "Lowest Coherence" sets outperform the "Highest Coherence" sets. Could this indicate that your metric, which favors single-theme dominance, might be poorly suited for domains where high-quality topics are naturally composed of multiple, distinct-but-related sub-themes?
