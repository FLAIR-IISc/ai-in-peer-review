### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics produced by topic models. The central idea is to group the top words of a topic into thematic "buckets" and use the size and composition of the largest bucket to derive a coherence score. The authors propose three methods for this grouping: agglomerative clustering, Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), and an SVD-based method with a post-hoc reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg).

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  A new method for evaluating topic coherence based on grouping topic words using their word embeddings. The SVD-based variant is presented as the primary technical novelty.
2.  An empirical evaluation showing that the proposed methods, particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, achieve strong correlations with human coherence judgments on three standard datasets, outperforming the state-of-the-art benchmark from Röder et al. (2015) on two of them.
3.  A preliminary application study suggesting that the proposed coherence metric can be used to select higher-quality topic models for a downstream weakly-supervised text classification task, leading to improved performance.

### Strengths

1.  **Intuitive Premise:** The underlying intuition of the paper is simple and compelling. The idea that a coherent topic should consist of words that can be grouped into a single large thematic cluster is easy to grasp and aligns well with how humans often assess topic quality.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary claim of the paper is supported by the results in Table 1. The reported Pearson correlation coefficients on the NYT and 20NG datasets are high and demonstrate a performance improvement over the complex, highly-tuned framework of Röder et al. (2015). This suggests the proposed method is effective at capturing aspects of topic quality that align with human perception.
3.  **Demonstration of Utility:** The inclusion of the text classification experiment (Section 4.3) is a notable strength. While many papers on coherence metrics stop at correlation with human ratings, this work attempts to show that the metric has practical utility. Demonstrating that selecting topic models based on the TBuckets score can improve performance on a downstream task provides valuable evidence that the metric is capturing a meaningful signal.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Methodological Justification for SVD:** The core technical novelty, the TBuckets-SVD method, lacks sufficient methodological justification. The authors state that the V matrix from SVD "records the eigenvectors of ATA, which are in turn the orthogonal d dimensional directions we are interested in." This is a description of what SVD computes, not a justification for *why* these eigenvectors should serve as effective "bucket identifiers" for thematic grouping. It is not obvious why assigning a word to the principal component direction with which its embedding has the highest cosine similarity is a principled way to form semantic clusters. This approach seems unconventional and requires a much stronger theoretical or empirical argument to be convincing when compared to standard, well-understood clustering algorithms like K-Means or DBSCAN applied to the same word embeddings.
2.  **"Parameter-Free" Claim is Misleading:** The paper repeatedly emphasizes that the SVD-based methods are "completely parameter free," contrasting this with the state-of-the-art. However, the best-performing method, `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`, is built upon a complex set of reorganization rules and a "dynamic threshold" defined by the formula `AWES - AWPS/AWES`. This formula, and the rules governing the reorganization, appear entirely heuristic and lack clear motivation. This introduces a form of parameterization, albeit one that is derived from the data rather than set globally. This ad-hoc "reorganization" step feels like post-hoc engineering designed to correct the deficiencies of the core SVD method, undermining the central claim of a clean, parameter-free approach.
3.  **Insufficient Comparison with Baselines:** The experimental evaluation compares against a single number for the "best performing settings" reported in Röder et al. (2015). This is insufficient for a rigorous comparison. The work of Röder et al. is a framework that systematizes many well-established coherence measures, such as those based on PMI and NPMI (e.g., C_V, C_UMass, C_NPMI), which have become de facto standards in the field. A proper evaluation would require the authors to implement and run several of these established baselines within their exact experimental setup to ensure a fair, apples-to-apples comparison. Citing a single number from a prior publication leaves open the possibility that differences in preprocessing, corpus statistics, or other confounding factors could account for the observed performance differences.
4.  **Limited Scope of Application Study:** The text classification experiment, while a good idea, is too limited to serve as strong evidence. It is conducted on only four binary subsets of a single dataset. Furthermore, the comparison between strategy S1 (average of 5 runs) and S2 (average of the *best* 5 out of 10 runs) is not entirely straightforward. While the comparison with the "Lowest 5" sets is helpful, the overall experiment is not extensive enough to generalize the utility of the metric.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more rigorous justification for using the eigenvectors of the V matrix (from SVD of the word embedding matrix) as thematic "bucket identifiers"? Why should this be preferred over more standard vector clustering algorithms?
2.  The `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method relies on a specific, non-obvious formula for its dynamic threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`). Could you please elaborate on the derivation or intuition behind this particular formulation? Have alternative formulations for this threshold been explored?
3.  Given that many established coherence metrics (like C_V or C_NPMI) are widely used and understood, would it be possible to provide direct performance comparisons against them, run within your own experimental framework, to supplement the comparison against the single value reported from Röder et al. (2015)?
