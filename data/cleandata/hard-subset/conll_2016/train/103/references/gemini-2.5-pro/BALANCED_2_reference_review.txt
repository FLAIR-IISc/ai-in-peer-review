### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel framework, TBuckets, for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core intuition is that words in a coherent topic should belong to a single, large thematic group, which the authors term a "bucket." The paper introduces three unsupervised techniques to partition the top words of a topic into these buckets using pre-trained word embeddings: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The final coherence score is derived from the properties of the largest resulting bucket. The authors evaluate their methods on three standard datasets, showing that their parameter-free SVD-based approach with a reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two datasets. They further demonstrate the practical utility of their metric by showing it can be used to select better topic models for a downstream weakly supervised text classification task.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A novel and parameter-free method for topic coherence:** The paper introduces a conceptually intuitive framework for measuring coherence based on grouping topic words. The SVD-based variants are particularly strong contributions, as they provide an elegant, parameter-free way to identify sub-themes within a topic and achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive results. The parameter-free nature is a significant practical advantage over prior work that requires extensive tuning.
2.  **Demonstration of downstream task improvement:** The paper validates the proposed metric not just through correlation with human scores, but also by showing its practical value. The experiments in Section 4.3 successfully demonstrate that using TBuckets to select higher-quality topic sets leads to tangible improvements in a weakly supervised classification task, strengthening the claim that the metric captures a useful aspect of topic quality.

### Strengths

1.  **Intuitive and Elegant Methodology:** The central idea of partitioning topic words into "buckets" is easy to grasp and well-motivated by the provided examples. The use of SVD on a matrix of word embeddings to identify orthogonal sub-themes is a particularly novel and elegant way to operationalize this intuition. It provides a principled mathematical foundation for the bucketing process.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance without Parameter Tuning:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg model outperforms the complex, tuned state-of-the-art from Roder et al. (2015) on two out of three benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG). Achieving this without requiring any hyperparameter tuning is a major strength. This makes the method robust, easy to implement, and readily applicable to new domains without a costly search for optimal parameters.
3.  **Thorough Experimental Validation:** The evaluation is sound. The authors use standard datasets and metrics, allowing for direct comparison with prior work. More importantly, the inclusion of the text classification application (Section 4.3) provides compelling evidence of the metric's utility beyond simply mimicking human coherence ratings. Showing that better coherence scores translate to better performance on a downstream task is a valuable and often overlooked form of evaluation.
4.  **Clear Presentation and Analysis:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The "dry run" examples in Section 3 are very effective in communicating the core idea. The authors also provide a good analysis of their results, including qualitative examples (Table 2) that give insight into how the method works in practice.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Complexity and Heuristics in the Reorganization Step:** While empirically effective, the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg step introduces significant complexity and relies on a series of heuristic rules. The justification for the specific formula used to calculate the dynamic threshold (`AWES − AWPS/AWES`) is not provided, making it seem arbitrary. This complexity detracts from the elegance and simplicity of the core SVD approach. A more detailed ablation study or justification for these design choices would be necessary to understand their importance and robustness.
2.  **Poor Performance on the Genomics Dataset:** The proposed SVD-based methods perform substantially worse than the state-of-the-art on the Genomics dataset. The paper acknowledges this result but does not provide a satisfying analysis of why this might be the case. This is a significant limitation, as it suggests the method may not be robust across different domains. The problem likely stems from the use of general-purpose GloVe embeddings, which may not capture the specific semantics of the genomics domain. The fact that the simpler clustering method performs better on this dataset warrants a deeper discussion.
3.  **Dependence on Word Embeddings:** The entire framework is critically dependent on the quality and domain-relevance of the pre-trained word embeddings. All experiments are conducted with a single set of embeddings (GloVe). The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of this sensitivity. For example, how do the results change with different embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec, fastText) or with embeddings trained specifically on the target domain (which could potentially resolve the issue with the Genomics dataset)?
4.  **Limited Comparison to Other Modern Coherence Metrics:** The comparison to Roder et al. (2015) is appropriate, but the field has evolved since then. Metrics like `C_v` and `C_npmi` (which are related to the PMI-based methods discussed but have specific, popular implementations) have become de facto standards for topic model evaluation. Comparing against these widely used metrics would provide a more complete picture of where TBuckets stands in the current landscape.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the specific mathematical formulation of the dynamic threshold used in the SVD-Reorg step? Was this formula derived from first principles, or was it found to be empirically effective? How sensitive is the model's performance to variations in this formula?
2.  Regarding the performance gap on the Genomics dataset: Do you believe this is primarily due to the choice of general-domain word embeddings? Have you experimented with or can you speculate on how domain-specific embeddings might impact the performance of your SVD-based methods on this particular dataset?
3.  The SVD approach inherently seeks orthogonal bases to represent the sub-themes in a topic. Does this orthogonality assumption pose a limitation for topics that may contain multiple, closely related (i.e., non-orthogonal) but distinct sub-themes?
