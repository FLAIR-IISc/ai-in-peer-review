### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper introduces `TBuckets`, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core idea is that a coherent topic should consist of words that can be grouped into a single, large thematic "bucket," while an incoherent topic will fragment into multiple distinct buckets. The authors propose three methods to implement this bucketing: an agglomerative clustering baseline (`TBuckets-Clustering`), a novel method using Singular Value Decomposition (`TBuckets-SVD`) on the topic words' embeddings, and an extension that reorganizes the SVD-generated buckets (`TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`). The coherence score is then derived from the properties of the largest bucket formed.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel, Parameter-Free Method for Topic Coherence:** The central contribution is the `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method. It introduces a unique way of identifying sub-themes within a topic by using the eigenvectors of the word embedding matrix as "bucket identifiers." The reorganization step, which uses a dynamically calculated, non-tunable threshold, makes the final proposed method effectively parameter-free, a significant advantage over many existing coherence measures.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates that `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` achieves a higher correlation with human coherence judgments than previously reported state-of-the-art results on two out of three standard benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG). This provides strong empirical validation for the proposed approach.

3.  **Demonstration of Practical Utility:** The authors go beyond intrinsic evaluation by applying their coherence measure to a downstream task. They show that selecting topic sets with higher `TBuckets` scores leads to improved performance in a weakly supervised text classification setting, grounding the metric's usefulness in a practical application.

### Strengths

1.  **Intuitive and Well-Motivated Core Idea:** The "bucketing" metaphor is highly intuitive and aligns well with how humans often assess topic quality—by looking for a single, dominant theme. The paper effectively explains this intuition with clear examples, making the motivation for the work compelling and easy to follow.

2.  **Novelty of the SVD-based Approach:** Using SVD on a matrix of word embeddings to identify sub-themes is a novel and clever technical contribution. The idea of treating the right singular vectors (eigenvectors of A^T A) as representations of latent semantic axes or "sub-themes" within the small set of topic words is creative and appears to be effective.

3.  **Strong and Direct Empirical Comparison:** The experimental setup is sound. The authors evaluate their method on standard, publicly available datasets and use the established metric of Pearson correlation with human scores. Crucially, they compare directly against the comprehensive results from Röder et al. (2015), allowing for a clear assessment of their method's performance relative to the state of the art. Achieving superior results on two of the three datasets is a significant strength.

4.  **Emphasis on Parameter-Free Methods:** The development of a high-performing, parameter-free coherence metric (`TBuckets-SVD` and `SVD-Reorg`) is a major practical advantage. Many existing coherence measures (including the large configuration space in Röder et al.) require tuning parameters like window sizes or confirmation measure choices, which can be data-dependent and cumbersome. A parameter-free method is much more readily applicable by practitioners.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for the Reorganization Threshold:** The formula for the dynamic threshold in `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) feels heuristic and is not rigorously justified. The explanation—that the ratio "denotes flexibility" and the subtraction "manages the gap"—is vague. While it demonstrably improves results, the paper would be much stronger if it provided a more principled explanation or ablation study for why this specific functional form was chosen over other potential dynamic thresholds. Without this, it appears to be a piece of carefully engineered but poorly explained machinery.

2.  **Evaluation is Limited to a Fixed Number of Topic Words (N=10):** All experiments are conducted on topics represented by their top 10 words. It is unclear how the proposed methods, particularly SVD-based ones, would perform or behave as N increases. For N=20 or N=30, the number of potential sub-themes and the resulting bucket distributions could change significantly. A discussion or experiment on the sensitivity of the method to the number of topic words would have strengthened the paper's claims of robustness.

3.  **Lack of Discussion on Computational Complexity:** The paper does not mention the computational cost of the proposed methods. While SVD on a 10 x 300 matrix is trivial, a formal analysis would be beneficial. More importantly, for a full topic model with K topics, this procedure must be run K times. While likely not prohibitive, a comparison of its computational footprint against classic PMI-based measures, which rely on pre-computed corpus statistics, would provide a more complete picture for practitioners.

4.  **Clarity on SVD Component Choice:** The paper states it focuses on the V matrix from SVD because it captures feature-feature interactions. While this is reasonable, the intuition could be elaborated. The columns of U would represent "eigenwords" (principal components in the word space), while the columns of V represent principal components in the embedding dimension space. A slightly more detailed explanation of why the latter is a better choice for defining "sub-themes" would improve the methodological clarity.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed theoretical or empirical justification for the specific formula used to calculate the dynamic threshold in the `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method? For instance, were other functional forms considered, and what makes `AWES - AWPS/AWES` a particularly effective choice?

2.  How sensitive is your method, especially `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`, to the number of top words (N) used to represent a topic? Do the correlation scores with human judgments remain high for N=15 or N=20, and how does the distribution of words into buckets change as N increases?

3.  In the text classification experiment (Section 4.3), you compare the average of the top 5 coherence runs to the average of all 5 runs (S1) and the lowest 5 runs. This is a good demonstration. However, is there a direct correlation between the topic set's coherence score and the final classification F1-score for each of the 10 individual runs? A scatter plot showing this relationship would provide stronger evidence for the utility of the metric.
