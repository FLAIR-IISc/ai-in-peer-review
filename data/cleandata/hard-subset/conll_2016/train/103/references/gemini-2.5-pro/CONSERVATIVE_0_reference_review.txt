### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a new method, TBuckets, for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models. The core intuition is that the words in a coherent topic should belong to a single, dominant semantic theme. The method operationalizes this by grouping topic words into "buckets" and scoring coherence based on the size and composition of the largest bucket. The authors present three techniques for creating these buckets: one based on agglomerative clustering of word embeddings (TBuckets-Clustering), and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the topic's word embedding matrix. The primary SVD method (TBuckets-SVD) uses eigenvectors as proxies for sub-themes, while an enhanced version (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) applies a complex set of heuristic rules to move words between buckets post-SVD. The methods are evaluated by calculating the Pearson correlation between their scores and human coherence judgments on three standard datasets.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel coherence metric based on SVD:** The use of SVD on a topic's word embedding matrix, treating the resulting eigenvectors as "bucket identifiers" for grouping words, is a novel approach to quantifying topic coherence.
2.  **Competitive empirical results:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method is shown to achieve state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two out of three benchmark datasets, surpassing the best-performing configurations reported in the comprehensive study by Röder et al. (2015).
3.  **Demonstration of downstream utility:** The paper includes an experiment showing that using the proposed metric to select higher-quality topic models can improve performance on a weakly-supervised text classification task, providing evidence of the metric's practical value.

### Strengths

1.  **Intuitive Core Idea:** The fundamental concept of the paper—that a coherent topic is one where most words can be grouped into a single thematic "bucket"—is simple, interpretable, and aligns well with how humans often assess topic quality. This provides a solid conceptual foundation for the work.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary claim of the paper is supported by strong results on standard benchmarks. Outperforming the best metrics from the extensive search performed by Röder et al. (2015) on the NYT and 20NG datasets is a significant result and suggests the proposed approach is effective.
3.  **Application-Oriented Evaluation:** The inclusion of the text classification experiment (Section 4.3) is a commendable strength. Much of the work on coherence metrics stops at correlation with human ratings. Demonstrating that the metric can be used to improve a downstream task provides more convincing evidence of its utility and helps ground the work in a practical application scenario.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Justification for SVD Mechanism:** The central technical novelty, using SVD eigenvectors as "bucket identifiers" or "sub-themes," is not well-justified. The paper states this is "intuitive" because the V matrix captures "feature-feature interactions," but this is a hand-wavy explanation. There is no theoretical or empirical evidence provided to support the claim that the orthogonal bases of a matrix of word embeddings for a small set of 10 words should correspond to meaningful semantic sub-topics. This core mechanism feels arbitrary and lacks the rigorous grounding expected for a new method.
2.  **"Parameter-Free" Claim is Misleading:** The authors repeatedly emphasize that their SVD-based methods are "parameter-free," which is a significant overstatement. The best-performing method, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, introduces a highly engineered "dynamic threshold" (`AWES − AWPS/AWES`). While not a manually-tuned hyperparameter in the traditional sense, this formula is a complex design choice that functions as an implicit, data-dependent parameter. The paper offers no justification for this specific formulation over any other. Furthermore, the entire approach is critically dependent on the choice of pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe, 300d), which introduces a massive set of external parameters and a significant dependency that is not explored.
3.  **Weak Comparison to Baselines:** The evaluation compares the proposed method's scores against a *single number* reported in a prior paper (Röder et al., 2015). This is not a rigorous comparison. Established and widely used coherence metrics like C_V (NPMI-based) and C_UMASS are the de facto standards in the field. A proper evaluation would require the authors to implement or use existing implementations of these baselines and run them within their exact experimental framework to ensure a fair, direct comparison. Without this, it is difficult to confidently assess the claimed superiority of the proposed method.
4.  **Methodological Complexity and Lack of Ablation:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method, which provides the best results, is convoluted. It involves a complex set of conditions for moving words into or out of the largest bucket, governed by four different similarity pulls (NBPW, NBPE, LBPW, OBPW) and the aforementioned dynamic threshold. This complexity feels like ad-hoc engineering designed to fit the evaluation data. A thorough ablation study is needed to understand the contribution of each component (e.g., the "into" step, the "from" step, the specific threshold formula), but this is absent from the paper. The significant performance jump from the simpler TBuckets-SVD to the complex Reorg variant suggests that these heuristics are doing the heavy lifting, which raises concerns about the method's generalizability and robustness.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Can you provide a more formal justification, perhaps grounded in linear algebra or distributional semantics, for why the right singular vectors of a topic's word embedding matrix should be interpreted as coherent "sub-themes"? The current explanation is based on intuition alone.
2.  The claim that your best method is "parameter-free" is a key selling point, yet the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg variant introduces a complex, heuristic formula for its reorganization threshold. Could you explain the rationale behind this specific formula? Have you tested simpler alternatives, and if so, how did they perform? This would help clarify whether this is a robust mechanism or a carefully engineered component.
3.  For a more direct and convincing comparison, could you provide results from running standard baselines like C_V (the metric used in Palmetto) or C_UMASS on the exact same topic sets used in your experiments? Comparing against numbers reported in another paper is indirect and may not be an apples-to-apples comparison.
