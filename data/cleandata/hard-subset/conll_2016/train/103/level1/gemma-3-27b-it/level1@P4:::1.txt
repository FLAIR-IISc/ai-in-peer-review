This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measure topic quality based on grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core intuition is that a coherent topic will result in a single large bucket, while incoherent topics will be fragmented into multiple smaller buckets. Three techniques are explored for bucket creation: clustering-based, SVD-based, and SVD with reorganization. The authors demonstrate that TBuckets outperforms state-of-the-art methods on three public datasets and showcases its usefulness for weakly supervised text classification.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **The TBuckets approach to topic coherence evaluation:** This is the primary contribution. The idea of representing a topic's coherence based on the formation of word buckets, derived from word embeddings, offers a new perspective on evaluating topic quality beyond existing methods relying on PMI, distributional semantics, or human judgment proxies.
2. **The SVD-based bucket creation techniques (TBuckets-SVD & TBuckets-SVD-Reorg):** While the overall TBuckets concept is the core contribution, the parameter-free SVD-based methods are a noteworthy addition. The reorganization step appears to be a refinement that addresses practical limitations observed in the initial SVD implementation.
3. **Empirical validation and application to weakly supervised classification:** The comprehensive evaluation across three datasets and demonstration of utility in a downstream task (weakly supervised text classification) provide evidence supporting the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the approach:** The concept of TBuckets provides a different angle to assess topic coherence, moving away from traditional statistical or semantic measures. It implicitly captures the human intuition of grouping related words under a common theme.
2. **Parameter-free SVD methods:** The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg techniques are particularly strong due to their lack of parameter tuning, a common challenge in existing topic coherence measures. This simplifies the application of the method.
3. **Strong empirical results:** The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over state-of-the-art methods on the chosen datasets, indicating the effectiveness of the TBuckets approach.
4. **Demonstrated utility in a downstream task:**  The successful application to weakly supervised text classification further validates the practical relevance of the proposed topic coherence measure.  Showing that higher quality topics, as assessed by TBuckets, lead to improved classification performance is a significant strength.
5. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The methodology is explained clearly, and the experimental setup is detailed.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited explanation of bucket reorganization:** While the paper mentions the "reorganization" step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, the detailed criteria for shifting words between buckets (NBPW, NBPE, LBPW, OBPW, thresholds) is complex and could benefit from a more intuitive explanation. The dynamic threshold calculation seems somewhat arbitrary and its impact on results isn't fully explored.
2. **Dependence on word embedding quality:** The TBuckets approach heavily relies on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings.  The paper does not investigate the sensitivity of the results to different word embedding models or the potential impact of domain mismatch between the embeddings and the datasets used.
3. **Limited comparison to recent coherence measures:** The primary comparison is to Roder et al. (2015). While a strong baseline, the field of topic coherence evaluation has continued to evolve.  A more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods would strengthen the claims of superiority.
4. **Computational complexity not discussed:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the TBuckets approach, particularly the SVD-based methods.  This information would be important for assessing the scalability of the method to larger datasets.
5. **Lack of qualitative analysis:** While quantitative results are presented, the paper lacks a more in-depth qualitative analysis of the formed buckets for specific topics. Showcasing examples of successful and unsuccessful bucket formations would provide more insight into the strengths and limitations of the approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the bucket reorganization step, perhaps with a visual illustration?  How sensitive are the results to the dynamic threshold calculation?
2. Have you investigated the impact of using different pre-trained word embeddings on the performance of TBuckets?
3. How does the computational complexity of TBuckets scale with the number of topics and the size of the corpus? Can you provide empirical runtime data for different dataset sizes?
4. Could you present some examples of topics where TBuckets successfully identifies coherent buckets, as well as examples where it struggles? This would help to illustrate the strengths and limitations of the method.
