This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for measuring topic quality in Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) models. The core idea is to group topic words into "buckets" representing coherent themes, with a single large bucket indicating high topic coherence. Three techniques are explored for bucket creation: clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization. The authors demonstrate that TBuckets outperforms state-of-the-art techniques on three public datasets and show its utility in weakly supervised text classification.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **TBuckets Framework:** The introduction of the TBuckets framework for topic coherence evaluation, based on grouping topic words into thematic buckets, is the primary contribution. This provides a different perspective on assessing topic quality beyond existing methods focused on word co-occurrence or distributional semantics.
2. **SVD-based TBuckets with Reorganization:** The SVD-based bucket creation techniques (both basic SVD and the reorganized version) represent a notable contribution, particularly their parameter-free nature. The reorganization step, designed to address limitations of the initial SVD approach, shows a tangible improvement in performance.
3. **Empirical Validation and Application to Text Classification:** The thorough experimental evaluation on three datasets, coupled with the demonstration of TBuckets’ effectiveness in improving weakly supervised text classification, provides strong empirical support for the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** TBuckets offers a conceptually different approach to topic coherence evaluation. It shifts the focus from statistical relationships between words to grouping them based on semantic similarity as reflected by word embeddings, mirroring a human-like assessment process.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates that TBuckets, and particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods in terms of correlation with human judgment across multiple datasets. This provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method.
3. **Parameter-Free SVD Techniques:** The SVD-based methods offer a significant advantage in terms of simplicity and ease of use, requiring no parameter tuning, unlike many other coherence measures. This makes them more practical for wider adoption.
4. **Demonstrated Utility in Downstream Task:** Showing the applicability of TBuckets to a real-world task, weakly supervised text classification, reinforces the practical relevance and value of the proposed research. The observed improvement in classification accuracy further supports the benefit of using TBuckets to select higher-quality topics.
5. **Clear and Detailed Explanation:** The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the TBuckets framework, the different bucket creation techniques, and the coherence scoring method. The example walkthrough of the bucket creation process greatly aids understanding.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Reliance on Word Embeddings:** The performance of TBuckets heavily relies on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. The paper uses GloVe embeddings, which are generally good, but the framework's robustness to different embedding models is not explored. A sensitivity analysis to different embedding sources would be valuable.
2. **Reorganization Step Heuristics:** The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg introduces parameters (AWPS and AWES) that, while not directly tuned, are used to determine thresholds for word movement between buckets. The justification for these parameters, based on “flexibility among words,” feels somewhat heuristic and lacks rigorous theoretical grounding. A more principled approach to defining these thresholds could strengthen the method.
3. **Limited Scope of Downstream Task:** The text classification experiment, while positive, is limited to a specific setting (weakly supervised learning with LPA-TD). Exploring the benefits of TBuckets in other downstream tasks, or with different classification algorithms, could further demonstrate its broader applicability.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The paper doesn’t provide an ablation study to understand the contribution of each component within TBuckets-SVD-Reorg. Specifically, it is not clear how much of the improvement is due to the SVD approach itself versus the reorganization step.
5. **Presentation of Results:** While the table presenting the experimental results is informative, it could be improved by including standard deviations or confidence intervals to provide a better sense of the statistical significance of the observed differences.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of TBuckets to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with other embedding models (e.g., Word2Vec, fastText)?
2.  Could you provide more justification for the formulation of the dynamic threshold used in the reorganization step (AWES − AWPS/AWES)? What is the intuition behind this specific formula?
3.  Have you considered other methods for determining the optimal number of buckets beyond the implicit determination through the SVD process?
4.  What are the computational costs associated with each of the TBuckets techniques, particularly for large topic models?

*  The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand.
*  The motivation for the research is clearly articulated.
