This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for measuring topic quality in Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) models. The core idea is to group topic words into “buckets” based on semantic similarity using word embeddings, with the assumption that coherent topics will result in a single large bucket. Three techniques are explored for bucket creation: clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization. The authors demonstrate improved performance compared to state-of-the-art methods on three public datasets and showcase the utility of TBuckets for weakly supervised text classification.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Topic Coherence Measure (TBuckets):** The central contribution is the TBuckets approach itself. While the idea of evaluating topic coherence via semantic relationships isn't entirely new, framing it as a "bucketing" problem with word embeddings offers a fresh perspective and a different mechanism for quantifying coherence. This is particularly valuable as existing methods often rely on statistical co-occurrence which may not capture nuanced semantic relationships.
2. **Parameter-Free SVD-based Techniques:** The SVD-based TBuckets methods (both with and without reorganization) are a significant strength. The authors rightly emphasize their advantage of requiring no parameter tuning, a notable benefit compared to many existing coherence measures that demand careful parameter optimization. This practical advantage makes them immediately usable and potentially more robust.
3. **Demonstration of Utility in Weakly Supervised Classification:**  The application of TBuckets to a weakly supervised text classification task provides empirical validation of its effectiveness.  Showing how TBuckets can improve topic quality, which then translates to better classification performance, strengthens the practical relevance of the work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of topic quality assessment and provides a logical motivation for the TBuckets approach. The connection between coherent topics and having a small number of large, semantically related buckets is intuitive and well-explained.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors evaluate TBuckets on three publicly available datasets, comparing it against existing state-of-the-art methods. The use of Pearson correlation to assess the alignment between TBuckets scores and human judgments is a standard and appropriate evaluation metric.
3. **Thorough Description of Techniques:** The paper provides a detailed explanation of each TBuckets technique (clustering, SVD, SVD with reorganization), including the mathematical foundations (SVD) and the rationale behind the reorganization step. This level of detail allows for reproducibility and facilitates understanding.
4. **Practical Benefit of Parameter-Free Methods:**  Highlighting the parameter-free nature of the SVD-based approaches is a strong selling point. It positions TBuckets as a potentially more convenient and reliable option for researchers and practitioners.
5. **Demonstrated Improvement in Downstream Task:** The application to weakly supervised text classification demonstrates the usefulness of the proposed method beyond simply measuring topic quality; it shows how it can directly impact performance in a relevant NLP task.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Underlying Concepts:** While the TBuckets approach is a novel *combination* of existing techniques (word embeddings, SVD, clustering), the individual components are well-established. The paper could benefit from more explicitly acknowledging the existing literature on semantic coherence measures and positioning TBuckets as an incremental improvement rather than a radical departure.
2. **Reliance on Pre-trained Word Embeddings:** The performance of TBuckets is directly tied to the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings used. The paper doesn't explore the sensitivity of the approach to different embedding models or the potential for training embeddings specifically tailored to the target domain. This is a potential limitation.
3. **Reorganization Step Complexity:**  The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique, while improving performance, adds complexity with the dynamic threshold calculation. The explanation of the threshold mechanism, while detailed, could be simplified and made more intuitive.  The authors should also discuss the potential limitations or edge cases where this reorganization might lead to suboptimal bucket assignments.
4. **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper focuses on overall performance gains.  A more insightful analysis would include examples of topics where TBuckets *fails* to accurately reflect human judgments, and a discussion of the reasons for these failures.  This could help identify areas for future improvement.
5. **Presentation could be more concise:** The paper is somewhat verbose, especially in the background and related work sections. Streamlining these sections could improve readability.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive is the performance of TBuckets to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings?  Have you experimented with different embedding models or considered training embeddings on the target corpus?
2. Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of the dynamic threshold calculation in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique? Perhaps with a simplified example?
3. Can you provide examples of topics where TBuckets performs poorly, and discuss the potential reasons for these failures?
4.  How does the computational cost of TBuckets (particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) scale with the size of the topic model and the vocabulary?



**Additional Comments:**

- The reference list is comprehensive.
- The experimental results are presented clearly in tables.
- The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
