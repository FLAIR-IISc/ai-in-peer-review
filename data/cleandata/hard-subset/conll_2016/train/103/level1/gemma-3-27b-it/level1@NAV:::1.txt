## Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for measuring topic quality in Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) models. The core idea is to group topic words into “buckets” based on semantic similarity (using word embeddings), with the assumption that a coherent topic will result in a single large bucket representing a unified theme. The authors explore three techniques for bucket creation: clustering, SVD, and SVD with reorganization, and demonstrate improved performance compared to state-of-the-art methods on three publicly available datasets, alongside an application to weakly supervised text classification.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the TBuckets concept itself.  The intuitive notion of representing topic coherence through the structure of word groupings within a topic is valuable.  While not entirely novel (existing work leverages semantic similarity between words within a topic), the specific framing of creating "buckets" and the quantitative measures derived from them offer a distinct approach. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of SVD-based techniques that require no parameter tuning, a practical advantage over methods needing careful optimization. Finally, the application to weakly supervised text classification adds empirical support for the utility of the proposed coherence measure.

**Strengths:**

1. **Intuitive and Well-Motivated Approach:** The core idea of TBuckets aligns with how humans assess topic coherence – looking for a central theme represented by closely related words. This human-interpretability focus is commendable.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods in topic coherence measurement, as demonstrated by Pearson correlation coefficients on three datasets. This provides strong evidence of TBuckets' effectiveness.
3. **Parameter-Free Methods:** The SVD-based TBuckets techniques avoid the need for parameter tuning, making them more readily applicable in practical scenarios. This is a significant advantage over methods requiring extensive parameter optimization.
4. **Practical Application:**  Demonstrating the benefit of TBuckets in improving text classification performance provides a concrete application and highlights the value of accurate topic coherence assessment.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Underlying Techniques:** The individual components (word embeddings, SVD, clustering) are well-established techniques. The novelty resides in their combination and application to this specific “bucketing” framework, which feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.
2. **Reorganization Step’s Heuristics:** The method for reorganizing buckets (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) relies on several heuristics and parameters (AWPS, AWES) which, while seemingly reasonable, lack rigorous justification and could be sensitive to the dataset. The explanation of the dynamic threshold feels complex and difficult to replicate without thorough understanding.
3. **Limited Analysis of Bucket Structure:** While the paper demonstrates *that* TBuckets work, it offers limited insight into *why* certain bucket structures correlate with higher coherence.  A deeper analysis of the characteristics of good vs. bad bucket arrangements would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of the reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) to the parameters AWPS and AWES?
2.  Could you elaborate on how the choice of word embeddings (GloVe) might influence the performance of TBuckets? Have you experimented with other embedding models?
3.  What is the computational complexity of each of the TBuckets techniques, and how does it scale with the number of topic words and the size of the vocabulary?
