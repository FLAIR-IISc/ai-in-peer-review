This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measuring topic quality based on grouping topic words into thematic buckets, leveraging word embeddings and three distinct bucketing techniques: clustering, SVD, and SVD with reorganization. The core idea – that coherent topics should yield a single large bucket – is intuitive and potentially valuable. The paper demonstrates TBuckets outperforms state-of-the-art techniques on three publicly available datasets and showcases its utility in weakly supervised text classification.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Topic Coherence Measure:** The TBuckets approach itself is the most significant contribution. Framing topic coherence as a bucketing problem, and particularly leveraging the inherent semantic structure captured in word embeddings to form these buckets, is a fresh perspective in topic evaluation.
2. **SVD-based Bucketing Techniques:** The introduction of SVD-based bucketing (both basic and reorganized) is a valuable addition. The complete parameter-free nature of these methods is a practical advantage over existing approaches requiring extensive tuning. The reorganization step adds sophistication and improves performance.
3. **Empirical Validation & Application:** The thorough experimental evaluation on three datasets, coupled with a demonstration of TBuckets’ effectiveness in a downstream task (weakly supervised text classification), strengthens the paper’s claims and demonstrates practical relevance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods in topic coherence measurement as measured by Pearson correlation with human judgements. This is a crucial indicator of the approach’s validity and practical benefit. The consistent outperformance across datasets enhances confidence in the findings.
2. **Parameter-Free Methods:** The SVD-based techniques’ independence from parameter tuning is a considerable strength. Many existing topic coherence measures are sensitive to parameter settings, requiring substantial effort for optimization. TBuckets-SVD provides a simpler, more readily applicable solution.
3. **Clear and Intuitive Methodology:** The concept of grouping words into thematic buckets to assess coherence is easily understood. The paper clearly articulates the intuition behind the method and presents the technical details in a logically structured manner. The example walkthroughs illustrating the bucketing process are particularly helpful.
4. **Demonstrated Practical Application:** Showing that improved topic coherence, as measured by TBuckets, translates to better performance in a downstream task (weakly supervised text classification) is a significant plus. It highlights the real-world value of the proposed method.
5. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a thorough overview of existing topic coherence measures and related techniques, placing TBuckets within the broader research landscape and clearly differentiating it from prior work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Bucket Characteristics:** While the paper states that a “single large bucket signifies a single coherent theme,” it lacks deeper analysis of *why* this is the case. What specific properties of the largest bucket (e.g., average pairwise semantic similarity, variance in word embeddings) correlate most strongly with human judgments of coherence? A more detailed analysis of bucket characteristics could provide further insights and potentially lead to improved coherence scores.
2. **Dependence on Word Embedding Quality:** TBuckets relies heavily on the quality of pre-trained word embeddings. The performance is therefore susceptible to biases and limitations present in these embeddings. The paper acknowledges using GloVe embeddings but doesn't explore the impact of using different embeddings or fine-tuning them for specific domains.
3. **Reorganization Heuristic:** The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, while improving performance, relies on somewhat arbitrarily defined thresholds (AWES - AWPS). While the authors attempt to dynamically adjust this based on the data, the rationale behind this specific formula is not fully explained and could potentially be optimized. The effect of different thresholding strategies isn’t investigated.
4. **Limited Scalability Discussion:** The paper doesn’t explicitly address the computational cost of TBuckets, particularly for very large vocabularies or topics with numerous words. The SVD step, in particular, can be computationally expensive. A discussion of scalability limitations and potential optimizations would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more in-depth analysis of the properties of the largest bucket that correlate with human judgements of topic coherence? (e.g., average pairwise semantic similarity, variance of word embeddings).
2.  Have you experimented with different pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, fastText) or considered fine-tuning embeddings on the specific datasets used in your evaluation? If so, what impact did this have on performance?
3.  What is the computational complexity of the TBuckets approach, particularly the SVD step? How does this scale with the number of words in a topic and the size of the vocabulary?
4.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind the formula used to calculate the dynamic threshold for the reorganization step (AWES - AWPS)? Was this determined empirically, and have you explored alternative thresholding strategies?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "completely parameter free" regarding SVD-based techniques should be nuanced. While it doesn’t require *tuning*, the dimensionality of the word embeddings (d=300) is a parameter.
*   A visualization of the formed buckets for a few example topics would greatly enhance understanding.
*   The section on related work is excellent, but it could benefit from a brief discussion of recent advances in neural topic models and their associated coherence measures.
