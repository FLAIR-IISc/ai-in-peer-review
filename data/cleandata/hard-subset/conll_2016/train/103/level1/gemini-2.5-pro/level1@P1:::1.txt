### Review for CONLL 2016

**Summary of the paper**

This paper proposes a novel method, called TBuckets, for automatically measuring the coherence of topics generated by models like LDA. The core intuition is that a coherent topic should consist of words centered around a single theme. The method operationalizes this by grouping a topic's top words into semantic "buckets". A topic is considered coherent if most of its words fall into a single large bucket. The authors use pre-trained word embeddings to represent words and propose three techniques for this bucketing process: (1) an agglomerative clustering-based approach, (2) a method based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) where eigenvectors represent bucket themes, and (3) an extension of the SVD method that includes a parameter-free reorganization step to refine the buckets. The proposed methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets. Additionally, the paper demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed coherence measure by using it to select high-quality topic models for a weakly supervised text classification task, showing improved downstream performance.

**Main Contributions**

1.  The primary contribution is the novel, parameter-free SVD-based method (TBuckets-SVD and its refinement, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) for measuring topic coherence. This approach cleverly uses SVD on a matrix of word embeddings to identify latent sub-themes within a topic and group words accordingly. Its effectiveness, combined with the significant practical advantage of being parameter-free, makes it a strong contribution to the field.
2.  A secondary but significant contribution is the demonstration of the proposed measure's practical utility in a downstream task. By showing that selecting topic models based on the TBuckets score improves performance in weakly supervised text classification, the authors provide compelling evidence that their coherence measure captures a meaningful aspect of model quality that has tangible benefits beyond simply correlating with human annotations.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novelty and Strong Intuition:** The "bucketing" metaphor is intuitive and provides a fresh perspective on topic coherence. While distributional semantics has been used before, the application of SVD to find orthogonal "sub-themes" (represented by eigenvectors) and assign words to them is a novel and elegant formulation. This moves beyond simple pairwise similarity calculations (common in PMI-based measures) to a more holistic, group-based assessment of a topic's thematic consistency.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art results on two of the three benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG), outperforming the best results from the comprehensive framework of Röder et al. (2015). This is a strong empirical result that validates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3.  **Parameter-Free Nature of SVD Methods:** A key advantage of TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is that they are parameter-free. Many existing coherence measures require tuning multiple parameters (e.g., reference corpus, window size for co-occurrence counts, PMI variants), making them difficult to apply robustly across different datasets. The proposed method's lack of hyperparameters makes it more general, easier to implement, and more practical for widespread use. The dynamic, data-driven threshold in the reorganization step is a particularly nice touch.
4.  **Excellent Justification via Downstream Task:** The weakly supervised text classification experiment (Section 4.3) is a major strength. It grounds the abstract task of measuring coherence in a concrete application. Showing that filtering topic models by the proposed coherence score leads to better classification accuracy provides strong evidence that the measure is not just mimicking human labels but is capturing a property of topics that is functionally important.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Clarity and Justification of the Reorganization Step:** The description of the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method is difficult to follow. While the conditions for moving words are stated, the formulation of the dynamic threshold (`AWES − AWPS/AWES`) lacks a clear justification. The paper describes it in vague terms like the ratio "denotes flexibility" and the subtraction "manages the gap." This feels ad-hoc and unprincipled. A more detailed explanation, an ablation study on the components of this threshold, or a simpler alternative would significantly strengthen the paper's technical contribution.
2.  **Dependence on External Resources:** The method is entirely dependent on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. The paper uses a single set of GloVe embeddings trained on Wikipedia/Gigaword. Performance on domain-specific corpora, like the Genomics dataset, could be suboptimal with such generic embeddings. The paper would be stronger if it included a discussion of this limitation or an experiment showing how performance changes with different embeddings (e.g., word2vec, or embeddings trained on the target domain).
3.  **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is conducted using only the top 10 words for each topic. The performance of coherence measures can be sensitive to this number (N). It is unclear how TBuckets would perform with N=15 or N=20. Since this is a common parameter in topic model analysis, an analysis of the method's sensitivity to N would make the evaluation more comprehensive.
4.  **Insufficient Detail in Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The paper compares its results against the "Best performing settings" from Röder et al. (2015) but provides no detail on what those settings were. Röder et al. present a large configuration space of measures. For a reader to fully appreciate the novelty and advantage of TBuckets, it would be useful to know what kind of measure it is outperforming. Is it another distributional method, or a PMI-based one? A brief conceptual comparison would better position the paper's contribution.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a more detailed intuition or theoretical justification for the specific formula used for the dynamic threshold in the SVD-Reorg method? Have you experimented with alternative formulations, and if so, how did they perform?
2.  In the text classification experiment (Table 3), the result for the POLITICS vs RELIGION dataset shows that S1 (6 runs) has a slightly better average F1 score (85.61) than S2's "Highest 5 Coh. sets" (85.23). While the difference is small, it seems to contradict the central claim of that section. Could you comment on this result?
3.  Similarly, in Table 3, for the PC vs MAC dataset, the average accuracy of the "Lowest 5 Coh. sets" (67.96) is higher than the S1 baseline (66.98). This is counter-intuitive. Is there a reason why selecting for the *worst* topics might lead to better performance than a random selection in this case?
