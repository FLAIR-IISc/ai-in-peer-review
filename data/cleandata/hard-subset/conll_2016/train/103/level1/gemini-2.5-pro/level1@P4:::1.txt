### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a novel method, TBuckets, for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The central idea is to group a topic's most probable words into thematic "buckets" based on the semantic similarity of their word embeddings. The authors reason that a coherent topic will yield a single large bucket, while an incoherent topic will result in multiple smaller buckets. Three bucketing techniques are presented: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), with the final one including a bucket reorganization step. The topic's coherence score is then calculated based on the properties of the largest resulting bucket. The proposed methods are evaluated by measuring the Pearson correlation of their scores with human coherence judgments on three benchmark datasets. The paper also demonstrates the practical utility of the measure by using it to select higher-quality topic sets for a weakly supervised text classification task, which in turn improves classification performance.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Novel Framework for Topic Coherence:** The paper introduces an intuitive and novel framework (TBuckets) for topic coherence evaluation. Instead of relying on pairwise word co-occurrence statistics like many prior methods, it operationalizes the human-like process of grouping words into semantic themes. The use of SVD on word embedding matrices to identify orthogonal sub-themes within a topic is a technically interesting contribution.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art results on two of the three standard benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG), outperforming previously published results. This is a significant finding, particularly given that the SVD-based methods are presented as being parameter-free, which contrasts with many competing approaches.
3.  **Demonstration of Downstream Task Utility:** The authors provide a practical application of their coherence measure in a weakly supervised text classification scenario. They show that by pre-screening topic models and selecting only the most coherent ones for human annotation, they can achieve better classification accuracy. This provides compelling evidence for the measure's real-world usefulness beyond correlation with human ratings.

### Strengths
1.  **High Novelty and Strong Intuition:** The core concept of partitioning topic words into thematic buckets is well-motivated, intuitive, and clearly explained. It represents a departure from standard PMI or co-occurrence-based metrics and aligns well with how a human might assess a topic's interpretability. The technical implementation via SVD, which identifies orthogonal semantic dimensions (eigenvectors) to serve as bucket prototypes, is a clever and effective approach.
2.  **Strong Quantitative Results:** The experimental results in Table 1 are compelling. The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves Pearson's r correlations of 0.837 on NYT and 0.869 on 20NG, surpassing the state-of-the-art baselines reported from Roder et al. (2015). The performance is competitive across all tested datasets, providing strong empirical validation for the proposed approach.
3.  **Parameter-Free Method:** The SVD-based methods are presented as parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage. Many state-of-the-art coherence measures require tuning multiple parameters (e.g., window size, confirmation measure, probability estimation method), making them complex and potentially brittle. A robust, parameter-free method like TBuckets-SVD is more easily applicable in a wide range of scenarios without requiring a labeled validation set for tuning.
4.  **Clear Application to a Downstream Task:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is a key strength. It demonstrates that the proposed coherence score is not just an academic metric but has tangible benefits. Showing that selecting topic models based on the TBuckets score improves classification F1-score provides a strong argument for the measure's practical value and its ability to capture aspects of topic quality that are relevant for downstream applications.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Clarity and Justification of Technical Details:** Several key technical details lack sufficient explanation, which impacts reproducibility and technical soundness.
    *   The "dynamic threshold" for the reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is based on the formula `AWES - AWPS/AWES`. This formula appears heuristic, and its derivation is not explained. This weakens the "parameter-free" claim, as it is a fixed but complex heuristic choice that functions as a parameter. A justification for this specific formula or an ablation study on its components is needed.
    *   The motivation for weighting the final coherence score by the "square of the highest singular value" is not provided. It is unclear what this weighting represents and how it contributes to the final performance.
    *   In the description of TBuckets-SVD, the V matrix eigenvectors are used as bucket identifiers, but the process of selecting and using these is not fully clear. For example, it is stated that the "first n eigenvectors" are used, but the value of `n` is not explicitly defined (e.g., is it the number of words in the topic?).
2.  **Ambiguity in the Reorganization Algorithm:** The description of the reorganization step (Section 3.1) is ambiguous. The condition for moving a word *from* the largest bucket involves `OBPW > max(NBPW, NBPE)`. `OBPW` is defined as "Maximum semantic similarity of the word with all words in a bucket other than the native bucket." It is unclear if this means the maximum similarity to any word in any other bucket, or if it is calculated on a per-bucket basis. This lack of precision makes the algorithm difficult to reimplement correctly.
3.  **Potential Over-reliance on Word Embeddings:** The method's success is fundamentally tied to the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. The paper uses a single set of general-purpose GloVe embeddings. The performance on the domain-specific Genomics dataset is notably lower than on the news-based datasets, which may be due to a mismatch between the embedding corpus and the dataset's vocabulary. The paper does not discuss this limitation or explore the sensitivity of the method to different embedding models (e.g., word2vec) or embeddings trained on domain-specific corpora.
4.  **Limited Scope of Experimental Parameters:** The evaluation is performed only on topics composed of the top 10 words. The behavior of coherence measures can change significantly with the number of words considered (N). An analysis of how TBuckets' performance varies with different values of N would provide a more complete picture of its robustness and generalizability.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the derivation of the dynamic threshold `AWES - AWPS/AWES` used in the reorganization step? What is the intuition behind this specific formulation, and have you considered simpler or alternative formulas?
2.  What is the justification for weighting the SVD-based coherence scores by the square of the highest singular value? Could you report the performance of the SVD-based methods without this weighting to help understand its contribution?
3.  The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 selects the best 5 out of 10 topic model runs in strategy S2, whereas S1 uses an average of 5 runs. Could the improved performance in S2 be partly attributed to having a larger pool of models to select from? Would a fairer comparison involve generating 10 models for S1 and averaging the performance of 5 randomly selected sets?
