This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel method for automatically measuring the coherence of topics learned by models like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core intuition is that a coherent topic’s constituent words belong to a single, unified theme. The method operationalizes this by representing topic words as embeddings and grouping them into "buckets." A topic is deemed coherent if most of its words fall into one large bucket. The authors present three techniques for this bucketing process: one using agglomerative clustering and two parameter-free methods based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), with the final version including a reorganization step. The proposed methods are evaluated against human judgments on three standard datasets and applied to improve a downstream text classification task.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and intuitive SVD-based framework for measuring topic coherence (TBuckets-SVD and SVD-Reorg). These methods achieve state-of-the-art performance while being parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage over existing coherence metrics that often require extensive parameter tuning.
2.  A practical demonstration of the proposed metric's utility. The authors show that selecting topic sets based on their TBuckets score improves performance in a weakly supervised text classification task, providing a tangible use case beyond simple topic evaluation.

**Strengths:**
1.  The empirical results are strong. The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method surpasses the state-of-the-art on two out of three widely used benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG), providing clear evidence of its effectiveness.
2.  The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based methods is a significant strength. It removes the need for costly and dataset-dependent parameter tuning, making the approach more robust, generalizable, and easier for practitioners to adopt compared to many competing methods.
3.  The paper validates the metric’s usefulness in a downstream application. By showing that filtering topics for human annotation using TBuckets leads to better classification accuracy, the authors demonstrate the real-world value of their approach.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The justification for the dynamic threshold formula used in the SVD-Reorg method is underdeveloped. The formula is presented without a clear derivation or analysis, making this crucial reorganization step feel ad-hoc rather than principled. A more thorough explanation or ablation study is needed.
2.  The method is highly dependent on the quality of external, pre-trained word embeddings. The study does not investigate the impact of using different embeddings or embeddings trained on the target corpora. This is a notable omission, especially for the specialized Genomics dataset where generic embeddings may be suboptimal and where the method's performance was weakest.
3.  The evaluation is limited to topics comprising their top 10 most probable words. The paper does not analyze how performance changes with a different number of topic words (e.g., 15 or 20), which is an important parameter in topic model analysis.
