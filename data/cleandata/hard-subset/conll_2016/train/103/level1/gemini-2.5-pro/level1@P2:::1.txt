**Summary**
This paper proposes a novel method, TBuckets, for automatically measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models like LDA. The core intuition is that a coherent topic should consist of words that belong to a single, dominant theme. The method operationalizes this by grouping a topic's top N words into "buckets" based on their semantic similarity, derived from pre-trained word embeddings. A topic is deemed coherent if most of its words fall into a single large bucket. The authors present three techniques for this bucketing process: one using standard agglomerative clustering, and two more novel methods based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The most advanced method, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, uses SVD to identify orthogonal sub-themes within the topic words and then applies a parameter-free reorganization step to refine the buckets. The proposed methods are evaluated by measuring the Pearson correlation of their coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets. The results show that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art. The paper also demonstrates the practical utility of the metric in a weakly supervised text classification task.

**Contributions**
1.  **A Novel SVD-based Topic Coherence Metric:** The primary contribution is the formulation of topic coherence as a problem of identifying dominant sub-themes using SVD on the matrix of topic word embeddings. The `TBuckets-SVD` and `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` methods are elegant and conceptually different from the prevalent PMI-based or centroid-based coherence measures. The reorganization step, which uses a dynamically calculated threshold to avoid introducing new hyperparameters, is a particularly clever refinement.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Performance with a Parameter-Free Method:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that its best-performing method, `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`, achieves state-of-the-art results on two out of three widely used benchmark datasets. A significant advantage highlighted by the authors is that this method is parameter-free, making it more robust and easier to apply in practice compared to existing methods that often require extensive tuning.
3.  **Extrinsic Evaluation Demonstrating Practical Utility:** The authors go beyond intrinsic correlation-based evaluation by applying their coherence metric to a downstream task. They show that using TBuckets to select higher-quality topic models for human annotation in a weakly supervised text classification framework leads to improved classification performance. This demonstrates that the proposed metric has tangible benefits for real-world applications.

**Strengths**
1.  **Methodological Novelty and Strong Intuition:** The core idea of using SVD to find orthogonal bases (sub-themes) within the semantic space of a topic's words is novel and well-motivated. It moves beyond pairwise word similarity measures and attempts to capture the underlying thematic structure of a topic. The "one big bucket" hypothesis is intuitive and easy to understand, providing a solid conceptual foundation for the work.
2.  **Excellent Empirical Results:** The paper reports very strong results. As shown in Table 1, `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` achieves a Pearson's r of 0.837 on NYT and 0.869 on 20NG, surpassing the reported state-of-the-art scores of 0.806 and 0.859, respectively. Achieving this on standard benchmarks against a strong baseline from Röder et al. (2015) is a significant accomplishment and a compelling argument for acceptance.
3.  **Parameter-Free Approach:** The main proposed methods, `TBuckets-SVD` and `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`, are presented as parameter-free. This is a major practical advantage. Many existing coherence metrics depend on parameters like window size for co-occurrence counts or require tuning within a large configuration space. A robust, parameter-free method is highly desirable for practitioners who want to reliably evaluate topic models without a lengthy and dataset-specific tuning process.
4.  **Demonstrated Usefulness in a Downstream Task:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 provides valuable extrinsic validation. It shows that the metric is not just better at mimicking human judgments in isolation, but that these improved judgments can lead to better outcomes in a practical application. The large performance gap on the POLITICS vs SCIENCE dataset (96.19% for high-coherence topic sets vs. 91.09% for low-coherence sets) is particularly convincing evidence of the metric's utility.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **The "Parameter-Free" Claim is Overstated:** While the SVD and reorganization steps themselves do not introduce tunable parameters, the entire method is critically dependent on several external choices that function as parameters. The most significant are: the choice of pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe vs. word2vec, dimensionality, training corpus), and the number of top words (N=10) chosen to represent the topic. The performance of the method is likely highly sensitive to the quality and properties of the chosen embeddings. The paper does not analyze this sensitivity, which limits the claim of being truly "parameter-free."
2.  **Opacity of the Reorganization Threshold:** The formula for the dynamic threshold in the reorganization step (`AWES − AWPS/AWES`) is presented without sufficient justification. The description—"The ratio...denotes flexibility" and "The subtraction...manages the gap"—is vague and feels heuristic. A more principled explanation or an ablation study comparing this formula to simpler alternatives (e.g., a fixed offset, or just using LBPW > NBPE) would be necessary to understand why this specific formulation was chosen and to build confidence in its robustness.
3.  **Insufficient Detail in Comparison to Baselines:** The paper compares its results to the "Best performing settings" from Röder et al. (2015). However, Röder et al. explore a massive configuration space. It would be more transparent and scientifically rigorous to specify *which* SOTA metric (e.g., C_V, C_NPMI) is being compared against. Furthermore, for a more direct comparison, re-implementing and running at least one or two canonical baselines (e.g., from Newman et al. or Mimno et al.) within the same experimental framework would strengthen the claims.
4.  **Limited Analysis of Failure Cases:** The paper presents examples of good and bad topics and their resulting bucketings (Table 2), which is helpful. However, it would benefit from a deeper analysis of failure cases. When does `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` fail to correlate with human judgment? For example, are there coherent topics with distinct but closely related sub-themes (e.g., a topic about "courts" containing words for judges, lawyers, and legal procedures) that are incorrectly penalized for having multiple buckets? Understanding the limitations of the method is as important as understanding its strengths.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please elaborate on the "parameter-free" nature of your SVD-based methods? How sensitive is performance to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., GloVe vs. word2vec, different dimensionalities) and the number of top words (N) selected from each topic?
2.  The formula for the dynamic threshold in the reorganization step appears somewhat ad-hoc. Could you provide a more detailed justification for its specific form? Have you experimented with alternative, simpler thresholding mechanisms, and if so, how did they perform?
3.  In the text classification experiment, are the observed differences in macro-F1 scores (e.g., 96.19% vs. 91.09% for the highest vs. lowest coherence sets) statistically significant? This would add considerable weight to the claim of practical utility.
