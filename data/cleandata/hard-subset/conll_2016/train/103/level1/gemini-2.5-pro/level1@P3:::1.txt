### **Summary**
This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for automatically measuring the coherence of topics generated by models like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core intuition is that words in a coherent topic should belong to a single semantic "bucket". The authors operationalize this by representing topic words with pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe) and then grouping them using one of three techniques: agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering), Singular Value Decomposition (TBuckets-SVD) to find orthogonal sub-themes, and a refinement of the SVD approach that reorganizes words into or out of the largest bucket (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The final coherence score is derived from the size and composition of the largest bucket. The methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, demonstrating that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art method from Röder et al. (2015) on two of the datasets. Additionally, the paper shows the practical utility of this metric by using it to select higher-quality topic models for a weakly supervised text classification task, leading to improved performance.

### **Contributions**
1.  **A novel, parameter-free method for topic coherence based on SVD of word embeddings.** The core contribution is the use of SVD on a matrix of topic word embeddings. The insight to use the right singular vectors (V matrix) as representations of orthogonal sub-themes within a topic is elegant and principled. This approach (TBuckets-SVD and its reorganized variant) requires no parameter tuning, which is a significant practical advantage over existing state-of-the-art methods like those surveyed by Röder et al. (2015), which involve a complex configuration space of choices (confirmation measures, aggregation methods, etc.).

2.  **An extrinsic evaluation demonstrating the utility of the coherence metric for a downstream task.** The paper goes beyond simple correlation with human scores by showing that their metric can be used to improve a real-world application. By selecting topic models that score highly on their coherence metric, they achieve better results in a weakly supervised text classification setup. This provides strong evidence that the metric captures a meaningful aspect of model quality that has tangible benefits.

### **Strengths**
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance and State-of-the-Art Comparison:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on the 20NG and NYT datasets, surpassing the best-performing configurations from the comprehensive study by Röder et al. (2015). The evaluation is conducted on standard, publicly available datasets, making the results directly comparable and impactful. The fact that this performance is achieved without any parameter tuning is a major strength.

2.  **Novelty and Intuitive Appeal of the Method:** The central idea of "bucketing" topic words is intuitive and well-motivated. The implementation via SVD is novel in this context. It provides a more global view of the topic's semantic space compared to pairwise PMI/NPMI-based measures, which can be brittle. SVD naturally identifies the principal axes of semantic variation among the topic words, providing a robust way to partition them into sub-themes.

3.  **Demonstration of Practical Utility:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is a significant strength. Many papers on evaluation metrics fail to close the loop and show *why* a better metric matters. By demonstrating that selecting topic models based on the proposed coherence score improves classification F1-score, the authors make a compelling case for the practical relevance of their work. The experimental design, which compares the performance on the top 5 vs. bottom 5 coherence models, effectively isolates the impact of topic quality.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly laid out with illustrative examples in Section 1 and Section 3. The step-by-step "dry run" of the bucketing procedure effectively communicates the core intuition to the reader before diving into the technical implementations.

### **Weaknesses**
1.  **Complexity and Justification of the Reorganization Heuristic:** While the base TBuckets-SVD method is elegant, the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg extension introduces significant complexity. The reorganization logic relies on four different similarity "pulls" (NBPW, NBPE, LBPW, OBPW) and a dynamically computed threshold based on `AWES − AWPS/AWES`. This formula feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks a clear theoretical or empirical justification beyond the brief intuition provided. It replaces the parameter-free nature of the base SVD method with a complex, non-tunable heuristic that is hard to analyze. The performance gain from this step, while notable (e.g., 0.772 to 0.837 on NYT for WL score), comes at the cost of the model's simplicity and interpretability.

2.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison to State-of-the-Art:** The comparison to Röder et al. (2015) may be confounded by the choice of word representations. The proposed method relies on pre-trained 300-dimensional GloVe vectors, while many of the classic coherence measures (like C_V, C_NPMI) evaluated by Röder et al. are based on co-occurrence statistics computed from a specific corpus (e.g., Wikipedia). The observed performance improvement might be partially due to the power of the GloVe embeddings rather than solely the superiority of the TBuckets method. A fairer comparison would involve re-implementing a strong baseline (e.g., C_V) using word similarities derived from the same GloVe vectors.

3.  **Ambiguity in Parameter Selection for the Clustering Baseline:** For the TBuckets-Clustering method, results are reported for specific `Max Dist` values in Table 1 (e.g., 0.63 for NYT, 0.7 for 20NG). However, the paper does not specify how these values were chosen. Figure 1 shows performance variation across a range of values, but it appears to be plotted on the test data itself. If the `Max Dist` value was selected post-hoc as the one that gives the best correlation on the test set, this would be an invalid evaluation protocol. This needs to be clarified.

### **Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide more justification or an ablation study for the specific formulation of the dynamic threshold in the SVD-Reorg method? How sensitive is performance to this formulation? For instance, how does it compare against using a simpler rule (e.g., `LBPW > NBPE`) or a fixed threshold tuned on a development set?

2.  Regarding the TBuckets-Clustering results in Table 1, how were the optimal `Max Dist` values selected for each dataset and linkage configuration? Was a separate development set used for this tuning, or were these values selected based on peak performance on the test sets shown in Figure 1?

3.  The performance gains over Röder et al. (2015) are impressive. However, to what extent do you believe these gains are attributable to the TBuckets method versus the quality of the pre-trained GloVe embeddings? Have you considered an experiment where you compute a strong baseline coherence measure, like C_V or C_NPMI, but replace the original co-occurrence counts with cosine similarity from your GloVe vectors to provide a more direct comparison of the algorithmic frameworks?
