

Summary: This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic quality in LDA-based topic models by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The core intuition is that a single large bucket indicates a coherent topic. The authors present three techniques: TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (SVD-based bucket creation), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (reorganization of buckets from SVD). The approach is evaluated on three datasets against human-annotated coherence scores and applied to weakly supervised text classification.

Main contributions as assessed:
1. The TBuckets framework for topic coherence measurement based on thematic bucketing of topic words.
2. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg), a significant practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods requiring parameter tuning.
3. Demonstration of TBuckets' utility in weakly supervised text classification, showing improved performance when using high-quality topics identified by the method.

Strengths:
1. Technical soundness of the coherence measurement approach: The paper presents a well-motivated method that aligns with human interpretation of topic coherence. The bucketing process effectively captures the intuitive notion that coherent topics should form a single thematic group. The evaluation methodology using Pearson correlation with human scores is standard and appropriate. The results show clear improvements over state-of-the-art on two of three datasets (NYT and 20NG), with TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieving the highest correlations.

2. Parameter-free nature of SVD-based techniques: This is a strong practical contribution. The paper demonstrates that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning, unlike state-of-the-art methods that typically require careful tuning of multiple parameters. This significantly reduces the practical barrier to adopting the method. The authors provide evidence that their SVD-based techniques outperform tuned state-of-the-art methods, validating the value of this parameter-free approach.

3. Practical application to text classification: The weakly supervised text classification experiments effectively demonstrate the real-world utility of the coherence measurement. The results show that selecting high-quality topics identified by TBuckets leads to better classification performance in three out of four datasets, with substantial improvements on the most challenging dataset (PC vs MAC). This application provides strong evidence of the method's practical value beyond just coherence measurement.

Weaknesses:
1. Limited novelty of the core idea: While the TBuckets approach is well-executed, the fundamental concept of using semantic similarity to group topic words for coherence measurement has been explored in prior work (e.g., Aletras and Stevenson, 2013; Newman et al., 2010b). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its approach from these existing methods or clearly articulate what makes TBuckets fundamentally different. The SVD-based bucketing is presented as novel, but the intuition is similar to existing distributional semantic approaches.

2. Insufficient analysis of the reorganization step: The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms TBuckets-SVD but doesn't provide a clear ablation study to isolate the contribution of the reorganization step. It's unclear how much of the improvement comes from the SVD approach versus the reorganization. A detailed analysis of which topics benefit most from reorganization would strengthen the claims.

3. Questionable "parameter-free" claim: The paper states TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold calculated from topic-specific values (AWPS and AWES). This threshold calculation involves a formula that, while topic-dependent, still requires understanding the underlying parameters. The claim of being "parameter-free" needs further clarification to be fully convincing.

4. Limited comparison with recent methods: The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015), but doesn't directly compare with more recent coherence measures that might have been published after their work. A more comprehensive comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art would strengthen the novelty claims.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the TBuckets approach fundamentally differ from existing semantic similarity-based coherence measures (e.g., Aletras and Stevenson, 2013)? What specific improvements does it offer beyond these methods?

2. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a threshold calculated from AWPS and AWES. Can you clarify how this threshold calculation avoids parameter tuning, and whether it's truly topic-specific without any fixed parameters?

3. In the text classification experiments, the performance difference between high and low coherence topics was most pronounced on the PC vs MAC dataset. Could you provide more detailed analysis of which specific topic characteristics lead to the largest improvements when using high-quality topics identified by TBuckets?
