

# Peer Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic coherence in LDA-based topic models by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The authors propose three techniques: TBuckets-Clustering (using agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (using singular value decomposition to identify sub-themes), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (adding a reorganization step to the SVD approach). The primary contributions, as I see them, are:

1. **A practical and intuitive method for topic coherence measurement** that aligns with human perception of topic quality through bucketing topic words into thematic groups, with the size of the largest bucket serving as the coherence indicator.

2. **Parameter-free SVD-based techniques** (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that eliminate the need for the multiple parameter tunings required by state-of-the-art methods, offering a significant practical advantage.

3. **Demonstrated utility in weakly supervised text classification** where using TBuckets to select high-quality topics for human annotation leads to measurable improvements in classification performance, particularly for challenging datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Strong practical motivation and intuition:** The paper successfully grounds its approach in human understanding of topic coherence, making the TBuckets concept immediately intuitive. The examples of coherent and non-coherent topics clearly illustrate how the bucketing mechanism captures human judgment. This intuitive foundation is a significant contribution to the field, as most existing coherence measures rely on statistical properties rather than semantic coherence.

2. **Parameter-free nature of SVD techniques:** The claim that TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no parameter tuning is well-supported by the results. The authors' demonstration that these techniques outperform state-of-the-art methods (which require careful parameter tuning) on two out of three datasets is compelling. This is a major practical advantage for practitioners who often struggle with the parameter sensitivity of existing methods.

3. **Demonstrated application value:** The text classification experiment (Table 3) provides strong evidence of the real-world utility of the approach. The consistent improvement in classification accuracy when using TBuckets to select high-quality topics for human annotation—particularly the significant 3.1% gain for the difficult PC vs MAC dataset—shows clear practical value beyond just topic coherence measurement.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core approach:** While the bucketing intuition is sound, the actual techniques (clustering, SVD, reorganization) are not particularly novel. The clustering approach is a standard technique applied to word embeddings, and the SVD approach is conceptually similar to previous work using vector space methods for topic coherence (e.g., Aletras and Stevenson, 2013). The paper does not sufficiently distinguish its approach from these prior works, making the novelty claim somewhat overstated.

2. **Lack of theoretical justification:** The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation for why the largest bucket size should correlate with human perception of coherence. The examples provided are illustrative but not rigorous. Without a theoretical explanation for this correlation, it's unclear whether this approach would generalize well to different topic models, datasets, or domains.

3. **Inadequate comparison with recent work:** The comparison is limited to Roder et al. (2015), but the paper doesn't address whether their method outperforms more recent approaches to topic coherence measurement. The authors should compare with methods published after 2015 to establish their true position in the current state of the art.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter free," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on AWPS and AWES. Please clarify how this threshold is computed and why it doesn't require any tuning. The authors should provide a formal definition of the threshold calculation.

2. The paper mentions that TBuckets-Clustering requires only one parameter (Max Dist), but doesn't explain how the optimal value was determined. In Figure 1, the performance varies with Max Dist—how did the authors choose the specific values used in their experiments?

3. The authors use GloVe embeddings but don't discuss how the choice of embedding model might affect the results. Would using different embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, fastText) change the coherence scores significantly? The authors should address this to demonstrate the robustness of their approach.

## Additional Comments

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the TBuckets approach differs from prior work using distributional semantics for topic coherence. While the authors cite Aletras and Stevenson (2013), they don't clearly articulate the conceptual and methodological distinctions. A more thorough comparison would strengthen the novelty claim.

The text classification experiment is well-executed and shows practical value, but the authors should clarify whether the improvement comes from the coherence measure itself or from the specific reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg. It would be valuable to see a comparison with using other coherence measures (e.g., CUCI, CUMASS) for topic selection in the same text classification task.

Overall, the paper presents a practical and intuitive approach to topic coherence measurement with clear advantages in terms of parameter-free operation and demonstrated utility in text classification. However, the novelty of the core approach is limited, and the paper would be stronger with more rigorous comparison to recent work and theoretical justification for the bucketing mechanism.
