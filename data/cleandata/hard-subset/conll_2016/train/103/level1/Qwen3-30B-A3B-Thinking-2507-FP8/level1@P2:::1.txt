

# Review for "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measure topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg: A parameter-free technique that demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on two of three datasets, outperforming existing methods without requiring parameter tuning.
2. A new conceptual framework for topic coherence measurement based on semantic grouping of words rather than statistical word co-occurrence or distributional similarity.
3. Practical demonstration of TBuckets' utility for weakly supervised text classification, showing improved classification performance when using coherence scores to select high-quality topics.

## Strengths

1. **Parameter-free coherence measurement**: The most significant contribution is TBuckets-SVD-Reorg's ability to achieve state-of-the-art results without any parameter tuning. Unlike the state-of-the-art methods compared (which require careful tuning of multiple parameters), TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are completely parameter-free. This is a substantial practical advantage for real-world applications where parameter tuning is time-consuming and requires expertise. The paper demonstrates this clearly in Table 1, where TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the best results reported by Roder et al. (2015) on two datasets (NYT and 20NG) while requiring no parameter adjustments.

2. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper provides thorough empirical validation across three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) using Pearson's correlation with human-annotated coherence scores, the standard evaluation metric in this field. The results show consistent improvement over state-of-the-art methods on two datasets, with TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieving 0.837 (NYT) and 0.863 (20NG) compared to Roder et al.'s best of 0.806 and 0.859, respectively. This demonstrates the robustness of the approach.

3. **Practical application to text classification**: The paper successfully demonstrates the real-world utility of TBuckets by applying it to weakly supervised text classification. The results in Table 3 show that using TBuckets to select high-quality topics for human annotation leads to consistently better classification performance across most datasets (3 out of 4), with significant improvements in the most challenging datasets (PC vs MAC and POLITICS vs SCIENCE). This shows the method has tangible value beyond just measuring coherence.

4. **Conceptual novelty**: The paper offers a fundamentally different perspective on topic coherence measurement compared to existing approaches. Rather than relying on word co-occurrence statistics or vector similarities, it introduces the intuitive concept of grouping words into thematic buckets, which directly aligns with human perception of topic coherence. The examples in Section 3 (e.g., the weather topic vs. the Afghanistan/Wikileaks topic) effectively illustrate this novel perspective.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of techniques**: While the concept of grouping words into buckets is novel, the paper's core techniques (clustering, SVD) are well-established in NLP. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how the application of these techniques to topic coherence is novel compared to previous work. For example, the SVD-based technique borrows heavily from standard linear algebra applications in NLP, and the clustering approach is similar to other topic coherence methods. The paper could have better articulated the specific innovation in applying these established techniques to this particular problem.

2. **Insufficient ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide thorough ablation studies to determine the contribution of each component in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg. It's unclear how much of the improvement comes from the reorganization step versus the SVD approach itself. For instance, TBuckets-SVD (without reorganization) already achieves good results (0.772 for NYT with WL scoring), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg only marginally improves this (0.837 for NYT). Without ablation studies, it's difficult to assess which aspect of the method is truly driving the performance gains.

3. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The evaluation is restricted to three relatively small datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) with only 100 topics each. This is insufficient to establish the generalizability of the approach across different domain types and topic distributions. The paper doesn't address whether the method would perform equally well on larger, more diverse datasets or on topics with different characteristics (e.g., very short topics, topics with high semantic diversity).

4. **Incomplete comparison with recent work**: The paper compares with Roder et al. (2015), but doesn't address more recent work on topic coherence measures that might have emerged since then. For example, methods like those proposed by Liu et al. (2015) or Zhao et al. (2015) might provide stronger baselines, and the paper should have compared against them to fully establish its state-of-the-art status.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is parameter-free, but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on AWPS and AWES. How does this dynamic threshold differ from parameter tuning, and why is it considered parameter-free?

2. The paper shows that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves better results than TBuckets-SVD, but the improvement is marginal (e.g., 0.065 for NYT). What specific aspects of the reorganization step are responsible for this improvement, and how does this relate to the limitations of the SVD approach alone?

3. The paper demonstrates application to weakly supervised text classification but doesn't provide comparison with other coherence-based selection methods. How does TBuckets compare to approaches that might use other coherence measures for topic selection in text classification tasks?
