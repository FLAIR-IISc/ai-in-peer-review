

# Peer Review: "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

**Summary and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes "TBuckets," a novel approach for measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The authors claim three contributions: (1) a novel bucketing approach for topic coherence measurement, (2) parameter-free SVD-based techniques, and (3) demonstrated utility for weakly supervised text classification. As I see them, the most significant contribution is the parameter-free nature of TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, which represents a meaningful practical advantage over existing methods. The second most significant contribution is the demonstrated improvement in weakly supervised text classification when using TBuckets to select high-quality topics. The core bucketing idea itself has limited novelty compared to existing work.

**Strengths**

1. **Parameter-free SVD-based techniques**: The paper's most compelling contribution is the development of TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, which require no parameter tuning. This is a significant practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Röder et al., 2015) that require extensive parameter tuning. The authors convincingly demonstrate that these parameter-free methods outperform tuned baselines on two out of three datasets (NYT and 20NG), validating the practical value of this contribution.

2. **Real-world application to text classification**: The paper makes a strong case for the utility of topic coherence measurement by showing that selecting high-quality topics using TBuckets-SVD-Reorg leads to better classification performance in weakly supervised text classification. The results in Table 3 (e.g., 96.19% vs 95.95% macroF1 for POLITICS vs SCIENCE) provide compelling evidence that coherence measurement directly impacts downstream application performance.

3. **Rigorous experimental evaluation**: The evaluation across three diverse datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) with comprehensive comparison to state-of-the-art methods demonstrates thoroughness. The inclusion of both Pearson's r correlation with human judgments and application to text classification provides a well-rounded evaluation that addresses both theoretical and practical aspects.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of novelty in core concept**: The bucketing approach bears significant resemblance to existing topic coherence measures based on semantic grouping (e.g., Newman et al., 2010; Aletras and Stevenson, 2013). The authors fail to adequately distinguish their method from these prior works. The core idea of grouping topic words based on semantic similarity is not novel, and the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate how TBuckets advances beyond this established concept.

2. **Insufficient justification for reorganization step**: The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique introduces a reorganization step with a dynamic threshold that is not well-justified. The authors claim the threshold "removes possibility of any parameter tuning," but the calculation (AWES - AWPS*AWES) appears arbitrary and ad hoc. The paper doesn't provide evidence that this specific threshold calculation is superior to simpler alternatives, making the contribution of this step questionable.

3. **Computational complexity not addressed**: The paper claims a practical advantage in being parameter-free, but doesn't discuss computational complexity compared to existing methods. The SVD-based techniques could be significantly more expensive than simpler approaches like CUCI or CUMASS, which would be a major drawback for large-scale applications. This omission weakens the practical claim of the parameter-free advantage.

4. **Limited discussion of limitations**: The paper fails to address important limitations. For instance, it doesn't discuss how the method would perform on topics that are inherently multimodal (e.g., topics covering multiple distinct concepts), which would challenge the single-large-bucket coherence metric. The evaluation on Genomics data (which the authors mention as a domain requiring specialized word similarity) doesn't demonstrate any domain adaptation, suggesting the method may not generalize well.

**Questions to Authors**

1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg are "completely parameter free." However, the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold based on dataset-specific values (AWPS and AWES). How exactly does this avoid parameter tuning, and why can't the same threshold calculation be applied to other coherence measures?

2. The paper demonstrates that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on NYT and 20NG but performs worse than the best baselines on Genomics. Given that the authors mention Genomics as a domain requiring specialized word similarity, why doesn't the method adapt to domain-specific word representations, and how would they propose to address this limitation?

3. How does the computational complexity of TBuckets-SVD (O(n^3) for SVD on n words) compare to existing methods like CUCI (O(n^2))? For large topic word sets (e.g., n=100), would this make the method impractical for real-world applications?
