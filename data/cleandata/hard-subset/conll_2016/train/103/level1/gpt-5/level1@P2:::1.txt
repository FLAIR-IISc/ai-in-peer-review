Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of embedding-based methods to assess the coherence of LDA topics by grouping top-N topic words into “buckets” that represent sub-themes. The intuition is that a highly coherent topic should yield one dominant bucket. Three instantiations are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering over word embeddings with a distance threshold; (ii) an SVD-based, parameter-free method that assigns words to the most similar eigenvector of ATA; and (iii) an SVD method with an automatic “reorganization” step that moves words into/from the largest bucket via a dynamic threshold derived from word–word and word–eigenvector similarities. Topic coherence is scored from bucket properties (size, LDA-order weighting, eigenvector similarity), with SVD-based scores weighted by the largest singular value. The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation with human topic coherence annotations on NYT, 20NG, and Genomics (from Röder et al., 2015), and applied to weakly supervised topic-labeling classification by selecting topic models with higher TBuckets scores.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, intuitive bucketization framework for topic coherence based on word embeddings, including a parameter-free SVD variant and a reorganization heuristic.
2) Empirical evidence that the SVD-Reorg method matches or outperforms strong baselines on 2/3 datasets (NYT, 20NG) and performs competitively otherwise.
3) A practical application showing that selecting topic models by TBuckets coherence can improve weakly supervised topic-labeling classification.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive coherence signal via “largest bucket”: The main idea aligns with how humans judge topics—grouping words into themes and checking if a single theme dominates. This transparency is an advantage over more opaque measures, and the paper provides illustrative examples that make the intuition easy to grasp.

- Competitive to state of the art with minimal tuning: On NYT and 20NG, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg slightly exceeds the best settings from Röder et al. (2015) while avoiding their extensive configuration search. Even the clustering variant requires only a single parameter. This is a real usability gain for practitioners.

- Sensible use of embeddings and orthogonal sub-themes: Using word embeddings to capture semantic proximity is standard, but exploiting the eigenvectors of ATA as orthogonal sub-theme directions is a neat way to induce candidate “bucket identifiers.” The reorganization step addresses practical assignment errors and appears to improve performance consistently.

- Practical downstream utility: The topic-labeling classification experiment, although limited in scope, shows that selecting higher-coherence topic models correlates with higher classification accuracy in 3/4 settings and that low-coherence selections can be harmful. This helps motivate TBuckets beyond correlation numbers.

- Parameter-free reorganization threshold: The dynamic threshold built from average word–word vs. word–eigenvector similarities is a reasonable attempt to avoid hand-tuning and to adapt to topic difficulty.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior embedding-based coherence: Prior work (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson, 2013) already employs distributional semantics to evaluate topic coherence, and bucketing/cluster-based interpretations are a natural extension. The SVD-based bucket identifiers and the specific reorg heuristic are new, but the conceptual advance over existing embedding-based or clustering/graph coherence measures feels incremental.

- Several heuristic choices lack justification or ablations: Key design decisions—using the size of the largest bucket as the core signal, the LDA-order weighting (1/log(i+1)), the EVS term, and the final weighting by the square of the largest singular value—are not theoretically motivated nor ablated. It is unclear which components drive gains; e.g., how does “largest bucket size only” compare to pairwise-average cosine baselines? The “parameter-free” claim is weakened by these embedded choices.

- Evaluation scope and statistical rigor: Results are only Pearson correlations on three 100-topic datasets. No confidence intervals or statistical significance tests are reported; several improvements (e.g., 0.869 vs. 0.859) may not be significant. Spearman rank correlations (often used for coherence) and robustness to the choice of N (top words per topic) are missing. The Genomics results are notably worse, with no systematic attempt to mitigate domain mismatch via in-domain embeddings.

- Reproducibility and missing details: Handling of OOV words, preprocessing, the exact similarity metrics, and implementation specifics (e.g., how many eigenvectors when n<d; tie-breaking; clustering linkage defaults; cosine vs. dot-product; normalization) are not fully specified. No code release is mentioned. The dynamic threshold formula AWES − AWPS/AWES is ambiguous without parentheses and units; a clearer definition is needed.

- Clarity issues and minor errors: There are typographical slips (e.g., an example bucket sequence appears to include “storm, weather” in the Afghanistan example) and some notation is introduced late or informally. The explanation of why V (features) rather than U (entities) is preferred could be strengthened further.

Questions to authors
1) Please report statistical significance (e.g., Fisher r-to-z) and confidence intervals for Pearson, and add Spearman correlations. Are gains over Röder et al. significant on NYT/20NG?

2) Provide ablations: (a) largest-bucket size only; (b) without EVS and/or WL; (c) without the singular-value weighting; (d) SVD without reorg. Also compare to a simple baseline: mean pairwise cosine among top-N words.

3) Clarify OOV handling and the exact similarity function (cosine vs. dot product, normalization). What fraction of topic words lacked embeddings per dataset?

4) For Genomics, can you show results with in-domain embeddings? If not available, can you analyze error cases to explain the drop and whether reorg helps/hurts?

5) Specify the SVD assignment details: number of eigenvectors used (n, d, or rank), behavior when n<d, and whether eigenvectors are signed (handling sign ambiguity). How sensitive are results to these choices?

6) In the reorganization threshold, is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES) or (AWES − AWPS)/AWES? Please justify this form and show sensitivity (or a proof it is scale-invariant and well-behaved).

7) Runtime and scalability: What is the average time per topic for SVD and reorg (N=10 words, d=300)? Any issues when N increases (e.g., N=20)?

Overall assessment
The paper presents a clear and practical idea with competitive results and a useful downstream demonstration. However, the incremental novelty, heuristic choices without ablations, and limited statistical rigor weaken the case for acceptance. With added analyses (significance, ablations, clarity on the threshold and SVD choices, OOV handling) and stronger treatment of the Genomics domain mismatch, this could become a solid contribution. As it stands, I lean toward a borderline reject, but I see promise if the above concerns are addressed.
