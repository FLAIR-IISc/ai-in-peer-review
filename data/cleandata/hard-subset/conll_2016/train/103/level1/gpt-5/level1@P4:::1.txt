Summary
The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of an LDA topic into “buckets” based on semantic similarity derived from word embeddings. The core intuition is that a coherent topic should largely occupy a single bucket (i.e., a single dominant theme). Three variants are presented: (i) TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering with a distance threshold), (ii) TBuckets-SVD (assign each word to the eigenvector of ATA it is most similar to), and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (a parameter-free reorganization heuristic that moves words into/out of the largest bucket based on word–word and word–eigenvector similarities with a dynamic threshold). Topic coherence is computed from the largest bucket via size, LDA word-order weighting, and (for SVD variants) word–eigenvector similarity, optionally weighted by the top singular value squared. The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation with human judgments on 20NG, NYT, and Genomics datasets; SVD-Reorg outperforms the best configurations in Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets and is competitive on the third. The paper also shows a utility case in weakly supervised text classification: selecting topic runs with higher TBuckets coherence leads to improved macro-F1 on several 20NG binary subsets.

Main contributions (as assessed)
- A simple, embedding-based topic coherence framework that operationalizes “single-theme dominance” via largest-bucket properties, including a parameter-free SVD-based variant and a reorganization heuristic.
- Empirical results showing competitive or better correlations with human judgments than a strong baseline (Röder et al., 2015) on two datasets, and a practical application to selecting topic runs for weakly supervised classification.
- An interpretable bucketing procedure that yields human-readable groupings of topic words.

Strengths
- Clear intuition and interpretability: The “largest bucket = single coherent theme” principle is easy to grasp and inspect post hoc. The paper includes step-by-step examples and bucket outputs, aiding transparency.
- Competitive performance with minimal tuning: TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require no hyperparameter tuning, yet match or surpass the best reported Röder configurations on NYT (0.837 vs. 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs. 0.859). This is notable given Röder’s framework requires exploring a large configuration space.
- Practical relevance demonstrated: Using TBuckets to select higher-coherence topic runs improves weakly supervised classification performance (e.g., POLITICS vs SCIENCE improves from 95.95 to 96.19 macro-F1 using the top-5 runs, and shows a large gap vs. the lowest-coherence runs, 91.09), supporting downstream utility.
- Simplicity and efficiency: The methods operate on the top 10 topic words and pre-trained embeddings; computational cost (especially for SVD on small matrices) is negligible, making the approach practical.
- Comprehensive variants and ablations: The paper explores clustering vs. SVD, several scoring variants (size, WL, EVS, and combinations), and provides sensitivity analysis for the clustering threshold.

Weaknesses
- Ambiguity and minor errors in exposition: There are typos and inconsistencies in the dry-run examples (e.g., an “official” step referencing an unrelated bucket “storm, weather”), and the reorganization threshold formula “AWES − AWPSAWES” is ambiguous in notation. The rationale for weighting with the square of the top singular value is not well justified.
- Fairness and statistical rigor of comparisons: For TBuckets-Clustering, the Max Dist parameter appears to be tuned per dataset (values in parentheses), potentially on test data; this could inflate correlation. No statistical significance testing is reported for improvements over Röder et al.; some gains are modest (e.g., +0.01 on 20NG). Baseline numbers are taken from the literature rather than re-run under identical conditions.
- Domain dependence of embeddings: Performance drops on the Genomics dataset for SVD-based variants (≈0.69 vs. Röder 0.773), likely due to domain mismatch of GloVe embeddings. The paper acknowledges this but does not quantify OOV rates or test domain-specific embeddings, limiting generality claims.
- Methodological choices need stronger justification: The use of the first n eigenvectors of ATA as “bucket identifiers,” the specific WL definition (1/log(i+1)), and the singular-value weighting are not theoretically motivated or validated via ablations. The SVD-Reorg conditions rely on multiple similarity quantities; while pitched as parameter-free, their behavior and stability are not thoroughly analyzed.
- Limited evaluation scope: Only Pearson’s r is reported; rank-based correlations (e.g., Spearman) would be informative given typical use for ranking topics. Sensitivity to the number of top words per topic (N=10 fixed here) is not studied. Reproducibility details (code, exact preprocessing, OOV handling) are missing.

Questions to Authors
- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the exact formula and notation for the dynamic threshold (is it AWES − AWPS/AWES?). Provide an ablation showing how this threshold affects performance, and whether a constant threshold performs similarly.
- SVD bucket identifiers: Why use the first n eigenvectors of ATA as bucket IDs instead of using U (word-space) or a reduced number k determined by explained variance? How sensitive are results to the number of eigenvectors considered?
- Singular-value weighting: What is the motivation for multiplying coherence scores by the square of the highest singular value? Do results hold without this weighting?
- Clustering parameter selection: How were Max Dist values chosen for Table 1? Were they tuned on the test sets? Can you report results with a fixed threshold chosen on a validation set to ensure fair comparison?
- Statistical significance: Are the improvements over Röder et al. statistically significant? Please report confidence intervals or p-values for correlations.
- Embeddings and OOV: What fraction of topic words were OOV for each dataset? Do domain-specific embeddings (e.g., biomedical) improve Genomics results? Any experiments with alternative embeddings (word2vec, fastText) or contextual embeddings?
- N sensitivity: How does performance vary with the number of top words per topic (e.g., N=5, 15, 20)?

Additional comments
- Consider reporting Spearman’s ρ in addition to Pearson’s r.
- Providing code and exact preprocessing details would improve reproducibility.
- Correct the typographical errors in the bucket examples and standardize notation.
