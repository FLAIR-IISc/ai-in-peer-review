Summary: The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of an LDA topic into “buckets” based on semantic similarity in embedding space. The key intuition is that a coherent topic should yield one dominant bucket. Three implementations are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering (one parameter), (ii) SVD-based assignment of words to eigenvector-defined directions (parameter-free), and (iii) SVD with a data-driven reorganization heuristic. Coherence is computed from properties of the largest bucket (size, LDA-order weight, eigenvector similarity), optionally weighted by the top singular value. On three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), TBuckets correlates with human judgments competitively or better than the best configurations reported by Röder et al. (2015). The authors also show that selecting topic runs by TBuckets improves weakly supervised text classification.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, parameter-free SVD-based coherence estimator with a reorganization heuristic that operationalizes the “one large theme” notion of coherence and yields strong correlations with human scores on NYT and 20NG.
2) An empirical demonstration that coherence-based run selection yields better downstream weakly supervised classification on several 20NG subsets.

Strengths
- Conceptual simplicity and interpretability: bucketing reflects human inspection of topic word lists; the “largest bucket” proxy maps cleanly to coherence and is easy to explain.
- Parameter-free SVD variants: avoiding external corpora and hyperparameter sweeps is attractive; the reorg threshold is computed from topic statistics, reducing tuning burden.
- Competitive empirical results: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg matches or exceeds Röder et al.’s best settings on 20NG (r=0.869 vs. 0.859) and NYT (0.837 vs. 0.806), using only pre-trained GloVe embeddings.
- Downstream utility: selecting the top-5 topic runs by TBuckets improves macro-F1 in 3/4 settings, with large gaps between best and worst runs (e.g., POLITICS vs SCIENCE: 96.19 vs 91.09).

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical grounding: assigning words to the most similar eigenvector of ATA is heuristic; why the first n eigenvectors and weighting by the largest singular value squared are not justified.
- Reorganization heuristic is ad hoc despite being “parameter-free”; no ablation quantifies its specific contribution beyond Table 1 variants.
- Domain sensitivity: performance degrades on Genomics (≤0.691 vs. 0.773), yet no analysis of OOV rates, embedding suitability, or domain-specific similarity.
- Evaluation gaps: no significance testing for r differences; comparison is only to Röder et al. configurations, omitting other embedding-based coherence measures; details of LDA training and preprocessing are sparse.
- Coherence depends only on top-10 words; robustness to N and to vocabulary coverage is unreported.

Questions to authors
- Why use the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- What is the ablation impact of the reorganization step on each dataset?
- How do OOV handling and alternative embeddings (e.g., domain-tuned) affect performance, especially on Genomics?
- Are r improvements statistically significant?
