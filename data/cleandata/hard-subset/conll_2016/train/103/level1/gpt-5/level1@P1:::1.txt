Summary and contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, an automated topic coherence measure that groups the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using pre-trained word embeddings. The central intuition is that a coherent topic should yield one large thematic bucket. Three instantiations are introduced: (i) agglomerative clustering over embeddings, (ii) SVD-based bucketing by assigning words to principal directions (eigenvectors), and (iii) a parameter-free reorganization step that moves words into or out of the largest bucket using dynamic thresholds. Topic coherence is scored primarily by the size (and optionally LDA rank-weighted size and eigenvector similarity) of the largest bucket, sometimes scaled by the top singular value. Evaluation on three standard datasets reports Pearson correlation with human judgments, and an application demonstrates that selecting topic sets by TBuckets improves weakly supervised text classification.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A simple, largely parameter-free, embedding-based coherence estimator (TBuckets-SVD and -SVD-Reorg) that correlates strongly with human judgments and outperforms the best settings reported by Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets.
2) A pragmatic “largest-bucket” coherence paradigm that operationalizes human intuition about single-theme topics, with a lightweight SVD-driven implementation and a dynamic, corpus-adaptive reorganization heuristic.
3) A demonstration that selecting topic sets with higher TBuckets coherence yields better weakly supervised classification performance in 20NG subsets.

Strengths
- Strong empirical correlations with human judgments, with minimal tuning: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves r=0.837 (NYT) and 0.869 (20NG), surpassing Röder et al.’s best (0.806 and 0.859). That these results arise from a parameter-free SVD pipeline (aside from the choice of embeddings) is appealing for practitioners who wish to avoid the combinatorial configuration space in prior work.

- Clear, intuitive rationale linked to human interpretability: The largest-bucket criterion matches how annotators assess whether a topic expresses one dominant theme. The examples provided (e.g., TV news topic) show that the method captures obvious misplacements and benefits from the reorganization step.

- Computationally simple and scalable for typical topic sizes: SVD on a small n×d matrix (e.g., 10×300) and cosine operations are fast, making it easy to plug the measure into topic modeling pipelines or to evaluate multiple runs (as done in the classification application).

- Useful application evidence: In weakly supervised classification, selecting the best five topic sets by TBuckets yields equal or better macro-F1 than selecting five arbitrary runs in 3/4 tasks, and large gaps between best vs worst runs (e.g., POLITICS vs SCIENCE: 96.19 vs 91.09) underscore the utility of coherence filtering before annotation.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior embedding/distributional approaches: Using word vectors and clustering for topic coherence has precedents (e.g., distributional vector-based measures, centroid similarity). The SVD-based assignment to eigen-directions is an interesting twist, but its conceptual advantage over simpler pairwise/centroid measures or direct clustering is not deeply analyzed. An ablation comparing TBuckets to a centroid-based coherence or k-means cluster-size baseline would help.

- Methodological ambiguities and ad hoc elements: The SVD bucketing assigns each word to the most similar eigenvector of A^T A; the geometric justification is under-specified (e.g., relation to projections U S, use of absolute values, handling sign indeterminacy). The reorganization threshold formula is unclear (AWES − AWPSAWES lacks parentheses) and seems heuristic. The choice to weight scores by the squared top singular value is not motivated theoretically or empirically.

- Domain mismatch and limited robustness: Performance drops substantially on Genomics (r≈0.69 vs 0.773), plausibly due to using general-purpose GloVe. This exposes a key dependency: TBuckets’ quality hinges on embedding coverage and domain fit. While the paper acknowledges this and lists it as future work, a simple experiment with domain-trained embeddings (or at least an analysis of OOV and low-frequency biomedical terms) would strengthen claims.

- Incomplete comparative evaluation and significance testing: The comparison is mainly against the “best” Röder configurations reported in their paper; no direct reimplementation or significance tests are shown. It is unclear whether differences (e.g., 0.869 vs 0.859) are statistically significant. The paper also does not compare to recent embedding-based coherence scores beyond Aletras & Stevenson’s family, or to topic-internal vector-space cohesiveness measures such as average pairwise cosines.

- Application study design could be tightened: In the classification application, S2 evaluates 10 topic runs but annotates the best five; this doubles topic induction cost and leverages TBuckets to pick runs, which is fair, but the gains over S1 are small in two cases and negative in one (POLITICS vs RELIGION). More details on labeling consistency, number of topics per run, and whether annotators were blind to topic order would help interpret results.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the reorganization threshold formula: is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? How sensitive are results to this design? Did you try alternatives (e.g., fixed margin, z-normalized similarities)?

- In SVD bucketing, do you use cosine similarity between normalized word vectors and eigenvectors? Do you consider the sign ambiguity of eigenvectors (i.e., use absolute similarity)? Why not assign by the largest absolute projection (A V = U S)?

- Why weight coherence by the square of the top singular value? Did you compare no weighting vs linear vs squared scaling?

- How many topic words (N) were used (always 10)? How does performance change with N (e.g., 5, 20)? Does the largest-bucket metric degrade with noisier tails?

- For Genomics, what fraction of topic words lacked high-quality embeddings? Could domain-specific embeddings mitigate the drop?

Additional comments
- Please report statistical significance (e.g., Williams or Steiger tests for dependent correlations) for Table 1.

- Minor typos (e.g., section 3 examples: “Word Seen: official. Bucket-1: {storm, weather}”) should be corrected.

- Consider adding a simple centroid-similarity or average pairwise cosine baseline to contextualize the gains from SVD/Reorg.
