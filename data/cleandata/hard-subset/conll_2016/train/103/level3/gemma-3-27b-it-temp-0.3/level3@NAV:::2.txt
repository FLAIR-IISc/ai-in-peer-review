## Review for "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for evaluating topic quality by grouping topic words into clusters (“buckets”) based on cosine similarity of word embeddings. The core idea is that coherent topics will result in a single large bucket, while incoherent topics will have multiple smaller buckets. Three methods are introduced for bucket creation: a greedy clustering approach, an eigenvector-based method (SVD), and a reorganization step to refine the SVD results. The authors evaluate TBuckets on three standard datasets and demonstrate its effectiveness in a weakly supervised text classification setting, achieving competitive or superior performance compared to the state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015).

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the empirical demonstration that this relatively simple word-embedding-based bucketing approach can effectively correlate with human judgements of topic coherence and improve performance in a downstream task. While the idea itself isn’t groundbreaking, the thorough evaluation and consistent outperformance of existing methods are valuable. A secondary contribution is the introduction of the reorganization step for the SVD-based method, which appears to improve performance by addressing some limitations of the initial bucketing.

**Strengths:**

*   **Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates consistently strong results across multiple datasets, outperforming or matching the state-of-the-art. This is a significant finding and suggests the method is robust.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The motivation for developing a new topic coherence measure is well-articulated, highlighting the limitations of existing approaches and the importance of human interpretability.
*   **Parameter-Free Methods:** The SVD-based techniques require no parameter tuning, which is a practical advantage over methods requiring extensive optimization.
*   **Demonstrated Utility:** The application to weakly supervised text classification provides a concrete example of how TBuckets can be used in a real-world scenario.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper lacks a strong theoretical foundation for why word-word similarity, as captured by word embeddings, should directly correlate with topic coherence (which is inherently document-level). The connection feels somewhat ad hoc.
*   **Contextual Mismatch:** A key concern is the potential mismatch between the context used to generate word embeddings (sequential context) and the context of topics (document-level). This could lead to spurious correlations.
*   **Clarity of Section 3:** Section 3, describing the TBuckets approach, is difficult to follow. The passive voice and lack of clear structure obscure the design choices and make it hard to understand the flow of the methods.
*   **Missing Significance Tests:** The results in Table 1 lack statistical significance tests. It is unclear whether the observed improvements are statistically significant or due to chance.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings (e.g., word2vec vs. GloVe)? Did you experiment with other embedding models, and if so, how did the performance change?
2.  Could you elaborate on the potential impact of the contextual mismatch between word embeddings and topic context? Have you considered any methods to mitigate this issue?
3.  Please provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for the results presented in Table 1.



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more concise and structured presentation of Section 3. Consider dedicating a subsection to each method (clustering, SVD, reorganization) with clear explanations and pseudocode.
*   Removing some of the more verbose descriptions and examples would allow for more space to discuss the limitations and potential future directions of the work.
