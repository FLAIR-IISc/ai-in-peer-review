## Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for evaluating topic quality based on grouping topic words into thematic buckets using word embeddings. Three techniques are explored for bucket creation: clustering, SVD, and SVD with reorganization. The authors demonstrate that TBuckets outperforms state-of-the-art methods on three standard datasets and showcases its utility in weakly supervised text classification. 

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, lies in the exploration of embedding-based methods for topic coherence evaluation, and the comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple datasets. The investigation of different bucket creation techniques (clustering, SVD, and reorganization) provides a valuable comparative analysis. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of TBuckets’ effectiveness in a downstream task – weakly supervised text classification – providing practical relevance to the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper rigorously evaluates the proposed methods against existing state-of-the-art techniques on three publicly available datasets, providing a solid empirical foundation.
*   **Novel Approach:** The TBuckets concept offers a fresh perspective on topic coherence evaluation, moving beyond traditional statistical measures and leveraging the semantic information captured in word embeddings.
*   **Parameter-Free Methods:** The SVD-based techniques are highlighted as parameter-free, which is a significant advantage in terms of ease of use and applicability.
*   **Downstream Task Application:** Demonstrating the utility of TBuckets in weakly supervised text classification strengthens the practical relevance of the research.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Inconsistent Performance:** While the method shows promise, the results are inconsistent across datasets. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg only consistently outperforms the state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the approach.
*   **Genomics Dataset Performance:** The notably poor performance of SVD-based methods on the genomics dataset is a significant issue. The paper lacks a thorough investigation into the reasons for this discrepancy.
*   **OOV Term Handling:** The treatment of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms is unclear. The impact of OOV terms on the evaluation results is not discussed, and it is a potential source of the observed inconsistencies, particularly in the genomics dataset.
*   **Misleading "No Parameters" Claim:** The claim that the SVD-based methods have "no parameters" is misleading. Pre-trained word embeddings inherently carry a large number of parameters learned during their training.
*   **Related Work Omission:** The paper overlooks relevant work in lexical chaining, a field closely related to the concept of grouping semantically related words.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What specific steps were taken to handle OOV terms when using pre-trained word embeddings? How might the presence of a high proportion of OOV terms (as seen in the genomics dataset) affect the performance of the proposed methods?
2.  Can you elaborate on potential reasons for the poor performance of SVD-based methods on the genomics dataset? Have you considered alternative embedding spaces or methods specifically trained on genomic data?
3.  How would you advise a user to select the most appropriate TBuckets technique (clustering, SVD, or SVD-Reorg) for a new dataset, given the observed inconsistencies in performance?
