This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for evaluating the quality of topics learned via Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to group topic words into “buckets” based on semantic similarity (using word embeddings), with the rationale that coherent topics will exhibit a single large bucket representing a unifying theme. The authors explore three techniques for bucket creation: a clustering-based approach, a method leveraging Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), and an SVD-based method with a reorganization step. The approach is evaluated on three standard datasets, demonstrating competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art techniques, and its utility is further demonstrated in a weakly supervised text classification task.

The primary contribution of this work is the TBuckets framework itself – a conceptually simple and practical method for topic coherence evaluation. While the underlying principles of leveraging word embeddings for semantic analysis aren’t novel, the application to topic quality assessment via bucket formation offers a different perspective. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical demonstration of TBuckets' effectiveness, showing competitive results against established methods on standard benchmarks. Finally, the demonstration of TBuckets' utility in improving weakly supervised text classification provides a practical application of the proposed technique.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical and Intuitive Approach:** The TBuckets approach is remarkably intuitive. The idea of grouping topic words into thematic buckets directly mirrors how humans assess topic coherence. This intuitive appeal makes the method easy to understand and potentially adaptable to various downstream applications.
2. **Empirical Performance:** The paper demonstrates that TBuckets outperforms the state-of-the-art on two of the three datasets tested (NYT and 20NG), and matches performance on the remaining dataset (Genomics). This is a significant result, especially considering the parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques.
3. **Demonstrated Application:** The inclusion of a weakly supervised text classification experiment adds practical value to the work. Showing that TBuckets can improve classification performance by identifying higher-quality topics reinforces its utility beyond simply providing a coherence score. The observation that selecting topics based on TBuckets score leads to significant performance improvements (especially when comparing highest vs. lowest coherence topics) is noteworthy.
4. **Clear Presentation (Overall):** The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and outlines the proposed solution. The experimental setup is clearly described, and results are presented in a readily interpretable format.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** While the TBuckets approach is intuitive, the paper lacks a strong theoretical grounding. The authors do not offer a principled explanation for *why* partitioning topic words into buckets based on word embedding similarity should correlate with human judgements of topic coherence. It feels somewhat ad hoc.
2. **Contextual Mismatch:** A significant concern is the potential mismatch between the contexts used to learn the word embeddings (typically sequential context in large corpora) and the context of topic modelling (document-level co-occurrence). Word embeddings may not accurately capture the relationships between words *within the specific domain* of the topic model, potentially leading to inaccurate bucket formation.
3. **Section 3 Clarity:** Section 3, describing the TBuckets methods, is dense and difficult to follow. The explanations are often passive and lack concrete details about the design choices. Subdividing this section into separate subsections for each technique (TBuckets-Clustering, TBuckets-SVD, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) would significantly improve readability. Some of the detailed explanation of the reorganization step (Section 3.1) feels overly complex and could benefit from simplification.
4. **Statistical Significance:** The results in Table 1 present correlation coefficients, but do not provide information about the statistical significance of these results. It is crucial to include p-values or confidence intervals to determine whether the observed improvements are statistically significant or due to chance.
5. **Language and Conciseness:** While generally clear, the writing contains some awkward phrasing and redundancies. The paper could benefit from a careful editing pass to improve conciseness and clarity.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Did you perform any statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to determine whether the improvements reported in Table 1 are statistically significant? Please provide p-values or confidence intervals.
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Did you experiment with different pre-trained embeddings (e.g., word2vec, fastText) or train your own embeddings on the specific datasets? If so, how did the performance vary?
3. Could you provide more insight into the computational complexity of each TBuckets method, particularly TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg?
4. The reorganization step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg seems complex. Could you provide a simplified explanation of how it works, perhaps with a more illustrative example?

**Additional Comments:**

- Consider removing the detailed descriptions of related work within Section 5. This section is quite lengthy and could be shortened to focus on the most relevant papers.
- The paper could benefit from visualizations of the created buckets for example topics, to provide a more intuitive understanding of the approach.
