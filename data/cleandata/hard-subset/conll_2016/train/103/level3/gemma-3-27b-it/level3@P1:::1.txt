This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for evaluating topic coherence based on grouping topic words into thematic buckets using word embeddings. The core idea is that a coherent topic will result in a single large bucket, while incoherent topics will fragment into many smaller buckets. Three techniques are explored for bucket creation: clustering-based, Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), and SVD with reorganization. The authors demonstrate improved performance over existing state-of-the-art methods on three publicly available datasets and showcase the utility of TBuckets for weakly supervised text classification.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the exploration of embedding-based methods for assessing topic coherence. While the idea of grouping topic words isn't entirely novel, the application of word embeddings and dimensionality reduction techniques (SVD) to achieve this is a worthwhile investigation. The empirical evaluation on standard datasets provides a necessary benchmark, and the demonstration of TBuckets’ utility in a downstream task (weakly supervised classification) adds practical relevance. A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental analysis, even if the results are somewhat mixed, revealing sensitivities and potential limitations of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of Embeddings:** The use of word embeddings to represent and group topic words is a conceptually sound approach. Leveraging pre-trained embeddings allows the method to benefit from existing semantic knowledge, potentially capturing nuanced relationships between words that traditional methods (e.g., PMI-based approaches) might miss. This is a significant step towards more semantically informed topic coherence evaluation.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors evaluate TBuckets on three different datasets, using a standardized evaluation metric (Pearson’s r correlation with human judgments). This allows for a meaningful comparison with existing state-of-the-art methods. The inclusion of a downstream task (text classification) demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed method.
3. **Parameter-Free Variants:** The SVD-based techniques (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) are advertised as being parameter-free, which is an attractive feature. In a field often plagued by parameter tuning, a method requiring minimal configuration is a valuable asset.
4. **Improved Text Classification:** The application of TBuckets for topic labeling in weakly supervised text classification, demonstrating an improvement in performance (in most datasets) by only presenting high quality topics to annotators showcases practical implications. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Inconsistent Performance & Dataset Sensitivity:** The most significant weakness is the inconsistent performance of the proposed methods across datasets. While TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art on some datasets (NYT and 20NG), it performs less well on others (Genomics). This raises serious concerns about the generalizability of the approach. The authors do not adequately address why the SVD-based methods perform poorly on the Genomics dataset.
2. **Lack of Analysis of Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Terms:** A likely contributing factor to the performance discrepancies, particularly on the Genomics dataset, is the presence of a high proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms. The paper does not discuss how OOV terms are handled, nor does it analyze their potential impact on the results. This omission is a critical oversight.  The quality of word embeddings relies heavily on adequate coverage of the vocabulary.
3. **Misleading "Parameter-Free" Claim:** The claim that the SVD-based methods are “parameter-free” is misleading.  While the methods themselves might not have explicitly tunable parameters, they rely on pre-trained word embeddings (GloVe), which were trained with numerous hyperparameters and on a vast corpus of text. These embeddings inherently encode parameters learned during training.
4. **Missing Related Work:** The paper overlooks relevant literature in the field of lexical chaining. Lexical chaining aims to group related words based on semantic similarity, similar to the core idea behind TBuckets. A discussion of how TBuckets differs from and builds upon existing lexical chaining approaches is crucial.
5. **Presentation Issues & Technical Clarity:** The graphs in Figure 1 are too small and difficult to interpret. Furthermore, the description of word embeddings is oversimplified and potentially inaccurate. The authors assume the use of cosine similarity but do not explicitly state whether the embeddings are normalized to unit length. This detail is crucial for accurate interpretation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How are out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms handled when computing word embeddings and performing the bucket creation process? What is the proportion of OOV terms in each dataset?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of why TBuckets-SVD performs poorly on the Genomics dataset? Do you believe the high proportion of OOV terms is a major contributing factor?
3. Could you elaborate on the relationship between TBuckets and existing work in lexical chaining? How does TBuckets differ from and build upon these approaches?
4. What criteria would you recommend for selecting the most appropriate TBuckets technique (Clustering, SVD, or SVD-Reorg) for a given dataset? Is there a way to predict performance based on dataset characteristics (e.g., vocabulary overlap with GloVe)?



**Additional Comments:**

- The paper would benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity of each TBuckets technique.
- Consider including error analysis, showing examples of topics where TBuckets performs well and poorly, and providing insights into the reasons for these outcomes.
- A more rigorous statistical analysis of the results (e.g., significance testing) would strengthen the conclusions.
