## Review: Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for evaluating topic quality based on grouping topic words into thematic clusters using word embeddings and various dimensionality reduction techniques (clustering, SVD, and SVD with reorganization). The authors claim their method outperforms state-of-the-art techniques and is particularly advantageous due to the parameter-free nature of the SVD-based methods. They also demonstrate the utility of TBuckets for weakly supervised text classification.

**Contributions:**

1. **Exploration of Embedding-Based Topic Quality Evaluation:** The paper contributes to the growing body of work on automatic topic quality assessment by investigating the use of word embeddings and dimensionality reduction to identify coherent thematic groupings within topics. This approach offers a potentially valuable alternative to existing methods relying on statistical co-occurrence or external knowledge bases.
2. **Comprehensive Evaluation Across Datasets:** The evaluation of TBuckets across three publicly available datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) provides a reasonable basis for assessing the method’s generalizability.  The comparison to existing state-of-the-art techniques offers a concrete benchmark for performance.
3. **Demonstration of Practical Application:** The application of TBuckets for topic selection in a weakly supervised text classification task provides a compelling example of its potential real-world utility and highlights a practical benefit of improved topic quality assessment.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The TBuckets approach, while building on existing techniques like SVD and clustering, introduces a specific methodology for grouping topic words based on embedding similarity, aiming to directly assess thematic coherence. The idea of evaluating topic quality based on bucket formation is intuitively appealing.
2. **Strong Empirical Results (NYT & 20NG):** The reported results demonstrate that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art on the NYT and 20NG datasets, suggesting the method is effective for certain types of text data. This is a significant finding.
3. **Parameter-Free Variants:** The claim of parameter-free SVD-based methods is attractive.  Reduced reliance on parameter tuning simplifies application and reduces the risk of overfitting.
4. **Practical Application in Text Classification:** The successful application to weakly supervised text classification demonstrates a tangible benefit of improved topic quality scoring and provides a compelling motivation for the research.  Showing a downstream task improvement is a strong positive.
5. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The authors utilize a standard evaluation scheme and compare their method to relevant baselines, contributing to the reproducibility and comparability of their work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Inconsistent Performance & Lack of Generalizability:** The most significant weakness is the inconsistency in performance across datasets. While TBuckets performs well on NYT and 20NG, it performs poorly on the Genomics dataset. This raises serious concerns about the method’s robustness and generalizability to datasets with differing characteristics.
2. **Poor Performance on Genomics & OOV Issue:** The notably poor performance on the Genomics dataset, and the lack of discussion regarding this, is a major flaw. The authors fail to address the potential impact of a high proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms in the Genomics dataset on the embedding quality and subsequent clustering/SVD performance. The lack of analysis in this area significantly weakens the conclusions.
3. **Lack of Guidance for Method Selection:** There is no discussion or guidance provided on which TBuckets variant (clustering, SVD, SVD-Reorg) is likely to perform best under different conditions (e.g., based on vocabulary overlap with the pre-trained embeddings).  The authors do not provide insights into when one method might be preferred over another.
4. **Oversimplified Treatment of Word Embeddings:** The paper’s description of word embeddings is oversimplified. The assumption that vector length is unimportant is misleading, as embeddings are not necessarily unit length. Clarification regarding normalization (or lack thereof) and the use of cosine similarity is crucial.
5. **Misleading Claim of “No Parameters”:** The claim that the SVD-based methods have “no parameters” is inaccurate. Pre-trained word embeddings themselves encode a vast number of parameters learned from a large corpus. The choice of pre-trained embeddings constitutes a significant parameter that influences performance.
6. **Missing Related Work:** The paper overlooks relevant work in lexical chaining, a well-established area of research focused on identifying semantic relationships between words in text.  Lexical chaining techniques share similarities with TBuckets and should be cited and discussed.
7. **Presentation Issues:** Figure 1 is too small to be easily readable, hindering the ability to visually assess the impact of varying Max Dist for TBuckets-Clustering.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What is the treatment of OOV terms when using pre-trained word embeddings? Are they ignored, or is some other strategy employed? How does this impact the results, particularly on the Genomics dataset?
2. Can you elaborate on why the TBuckets method performs so poorly on the Genomics dataset? Do you believe the high proportion of OOV terms is the primary factor, and if so, what strategies could be employed to mitigate this issue?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the normalization (or lack thereof) used with the word embeddings?  Specifically, are the embeddings unit length, and if not, how does this affect the use of cosine similarity?
4. Can you discuss the relationship between the TBuckets approach and existing work in lexical chaining? How does TBuckets differ from or build upon these techniques?
5.  What are the computational costs associated with each TBuckets variant (clustering, SVD, SVD-Reorg)?



**Additional Comments:**

* The notation used in the equations for coherence measures (e.g., CUMASS, CUSheffield) is difficult to follow without prior knowledge of the formulas. A more explicit explanation of the variables would be helpful.
* It would be beneficial to include a more detailed analysis of the types of topics for which TBuckets performs well and those for which it struggles.
