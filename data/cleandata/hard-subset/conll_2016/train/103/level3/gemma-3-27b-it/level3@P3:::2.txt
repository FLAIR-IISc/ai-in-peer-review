## Review: Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for evaluating the quality of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topics by grouping topic words into clusters (“buckets”) based on cosine similarity of word embeddings. Three methods are introduced for bucket creation: a greedy word-by-word assignment, an eigenvector-based method (SVD), and a reorganization step applied to the SVD method. The approach is evaluated on three standard datasets and demonstrates competitive performance in a weakly supervised text classification setting, often outperforming or matching the state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015).

**Main Contributions:**

1. **A novel, intuitive approach to topic coherence evaluation:** The central idea of using word embedding similarity to group topic words into thematic buckets is conceptually sound and offers a different perspective on measuring topic quality. This provides a valuable alternative to existing coherence metrics.
2. **Empirically competitive performance:** TBuckets, particularly the SVD-Reorg variant, demonstrates strong performance on standard datasets, achieving comparable or superior results to established methods. This suggests its practical utility.
3. **Demonstration of utility in a downstream task:** Showing that TBuckets can improve weakly supervised text classification by filtering topic quality highlights its potential impact beyond intrinsic evaluation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear motivation and problem definition:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of topic quality evaluation and the limitations of existing approaches, motivating the development of TBuckets.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The experimental results are convincing, showing consistent improvements over baseline methods on multiple datasets. The outperformance of SVD-Reorg on NYT and 20NG is particularly noteworthy.
3. **Parameter-free SVD methods:**  The authors rightly emphasize the advantage of the SVD-based techniques requiring no parameter tuning, simplifying their application.
4. **Practical application demonstrated:** The inclusion of a weakly supervised text classification task helps to ground the evaluation in a practical setting and shows the potential benefit of using TBuckets to improve downstream performance.
5. **Identified limitations and future directions:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of their approach and suggest promising avenues for future research, demonstrating a thoughtful understanding of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of theoretical justification:** While intuitively appealing, the paper lacks a strong theoretical basis for why word-word similarity, as captured by word embeddings, should directly correspond to human judgments of topic coherence. The connection remains largely empirical.
2. **Context mismatch concern:** The authors raise a valid concern regarding the discrepancy between the contexts used to learn word embeddings (sequential context) and the document-level context of topics. This potential mismatch could limit the effectiveness of the approach.
3. **Clarity of Section 3:** Section 3, detailing the TBuckets approach, is difficult to follow. The passive voice and overly detailed explanations obscure the key design choices of each method. A more structured presentation (one subsection per method) would significantly improve readability.
4. **Missing significance tests:** The authors are correct to point out the need for statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to support the reported performance differences in Table 1. Without these tests, it is difficult to determine whether the observed improvements are statistically meaningful.
5. **Limited ablation study:** The effect of each component of the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method (size, WL, EVS) is not individually assessed, making it difficult to understand which aspects contribute most to the performance gains.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Sensitivity to word embeddings:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings (e.g., word2vec vs. GloVe)?  Have you experimented with different pre-trained embeddings or considered fine-tuning embeddings on the specific datasets?
2. **Threshold selection:** While you state the reorganization threshold is not empirically determined, it *is* based on computed values (AWPS, AWES). How robust is the performance to variations in these computed values? Could a fixed threshold, determined via cross-validation, be more reliable?
3. **Computational cost:** What is the computational complexity of the TBuckets methods, particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, compared to existing coherence metrics? Is it scalable to very large topic models?
4. **Qualitative analysis:** Could you provide a more in-depth qualitative analysis of the buckets created by TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, illustrating cases where the method successfully captures meaningful semantic groupings and cases where it fails?



**Additional Comments:**

* There are some minor language issues throughout the paper. A careful proofread would improve clarity.
* Table 2 would benefit from including coherence scores alongside the buckets to facilitate comparison with human judgments.
* The reference list is well-formatted and comprehensive.
