This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to evaluating the quality of topics learned via Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to group topic words into "buckets" based on semantic similarity, leveraging word embeddings. The intuition is that a coherent topic will manifest as a single, large bucket, while incoherent topics will result in numerous smaller buckets. Three techniques are explored for creating these buckets: a clustering-based method, one utilizing Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), and an SVD-based approach with a reorganization step. The authors evaluate TBuckets on three publicly available datasets and demonstrate its effectiveness in weakly supervised text classification, reporting performance comparable to or exceeding state-of-the-art methods like those presented in Röder et al. (2015).

The primary contribution of this work is the TBuckets approach itself, providing a new and intuitive metric for topic coherence. While existing metrics often rely on statistical co-occurrence or knowledge-based resources, TBuckets directly assesses the semantic relatedness of topic words using modern word embeddings. A second, related contribution is the SVD-based techniques (particularly with reorganization), which offer a parameter-free alternative to existing coherence measures, a significant advantage in practical applications. Finally, the empirical validation on multiple datasets, and demonstration of utility in a downstream task, provides evidence of the effectiveness of TBuckets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** TBuckets presents a fresh perspective on topic coherence evaluation, moving beyond traditional statistical measures and leveraging the power of distributional semantics. The idea of grouping topic words based on semantic similarity is intuitive and offers a potentially more human-aligned assessment of topic quality.
2. **Strong Empirical Performance:** The reported results demonstrate that TBuckets consistently performs competitively with, and often outperforms, existing state-of-the-art methods on standard datasets. This is a crucial indicator of the practical utility of the proposed approach.
3. **Parameter-Free SVD Methods:** The SVD-based techniques (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) are particularly noteworthy for their lack of parameter tuning, a significant benefit over methods requiring extensive hyperparameter optimization. This makes the approach more accessible and easier to deploy.
4. **Demonstrated Utility in Downstream Task:**  Successfully applying TBuckets to a weakly supervised text classification task highlights its relevance beyond a purely intrinsic evaluation metric and underscores its potential for improving topic modeling-based applications.
5. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of topic quality and provides a compelling motivation for developing new evaluation metrics. The comparison to human interpretability of topics is well-stated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** While the intuition behind TBuckets is sound, the paper lacks a strong theoretical grounding for *why* grouping words based on semantic similarity should accurately reflect topic coherence. It relies heavily on empirical validation without exploring the underlying principles.
2. **Contextual Mismatch:** The use of word embeddings, trained on large corpora with sequential context, may not perfectly align with the document-level context in which topics are defined. Semantic similarity based on these embeddings might not fully capture the nuanced relationships between words within a specific topic.
3. **Clarity and Structure of Section 3:** The explanation of the TBuckets techniques in Section 3 is convoluted and difficult to follow. The passive voice and lack of clear subsections hinder understanding of the design choices and implementation details.  A more structured presentation, with a dedicated subsection for each method (Clustering, SVD, SVD-Reorg), would significantly improve clarity.
4. **Repetitive Details:** The description of the reorganization step within TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is verbose and contains unnecessary details regarding the dynamic threshold calculation.  Streamlining this section and focusing on the key concepts would make it more concise and impactful.
5. **Missing Statistical Significance:** Table 1 presents correlation coefficients without reporting any statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values). This makes it difficult to assess the reliability of the observed performance differences. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Have you performed statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to determine if the performance differences reported in Table 1 are statistically significant? Please provide these results.
2.  How sensitive is the performance of TBuckets to the choice of word embeddings?  Have you experimented with different pre-trained embeddings (e.g., word2vec, FastText) and observed any significant variations in results?
3.  Could you elaborate on the potential limitations of using word embeddings trained on sequential context for evaluating document-level topics? Have you considered techniques to mitigate the contextual mismatch, such as incorporating document-specific context into the embedding space?
4.  Regarding the dynamic threshold in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, can you provide a more intuitive explanation of how this threshold adapts based on topic characteristics, and why the chosen formula is effective?



**Additional Comments:**

- The paper would benefit from a more concise and focused discussion of related work. Some of the descriptions are overly detailed and detract from the core contributions.
- The writing quality could be improved with a careful proofread to address minor grammatical and stylistic issues.
- Consider providing illustrative examples of how TBuckets identifies coherent versus incoherent topics, alongside the example provided in Section 3. This would further strengthen the intuition behind the approach.
