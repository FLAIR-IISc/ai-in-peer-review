## Review for “Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets”

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for evaluating topic quality based on embedding-based grouping of topic words. The core idea is that coherent topics will form large, consolidated “buckets” of related words when using word embeddings and clustering/SVD techniques. The authors evaluate TBuckets on three standard datasets, demonstrating competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods, and showcase its utility in weakly supervised text classification. The main contribution, as I see it, lies in exploring embedding-based methods for topic coherence evaluation as an alternative to traditional measures. A secondary contribution is the application of this approach to improve weakly supervised text classification by pre-selecting high-quality topics.

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty of Approach:** The exploration of embedding-based bucketing for topic quality assessment is a reasonable and potentially valuable direction. Leveraging semantic relationships captured in word embeddings offers a different perspective compared to methods relying on statistical co-occurrence or external knowledge bases.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper evaluates TBuckets on three datasets, including a genomics dataset, providing a reasonably broad assessment of its performance. The comparison against existing state-of-the-art methods is also appreciated.
*   **Demonstrated Utility:** The application to weakly supervised text classification demonstrates a practical benefit of identifying high-quality topics, suggesting potential real-world impact.
*   **Parameter-Free Variants:** The SVD-based methods, being parameter-free, are an attractive feature, particularly in scenarios where extensive parameter tuning is undesirable.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Inconsistent Results:** The most significant weakness is the inconsistency in performance across methods and datasets. While TBuckets performs well on some datasets, it doesn’t consistently outperform the state-of-the-art. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the approach.
*   **Poor Performance on Genomics Dataset:** The notably poor performance of SVD-based methods on the genomics dataset is a major red flag. The authors do not adequately address *why* this occurs, leading to doubts about the method’s robustness.
*   **Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Issues:** The authors completely fail to discuss the impact of OOV terms. The genomics dataset likely has a high proportion of OOV terms given the specialized vocabulary, which could explain the poor SVD performance. Ignoring this is a significant oversight.
*   **Misleading "No Parameters" Claim:** The claim that the SVD-based methods have "no parameters" is misleading. Pre-trained word embeddings *inherently* contain a vast number of learned parameters.
*   **Missing Related Work:** The paper overlooks relevant work in lexical chaining, which is closely related to the concept of grouping semantically related words. Citation and discussion of this area are crucial.
* **Presentation Issues:** The graphs in Figure 1 are far too small to be easily readable.

**Questions to Authors:**

*   What is the treatment of OOV terms when using pre-trained word embeddings? Are they ignored, or is some other strategy employed? How does this affect the results, especially on the genomics dataset?
*   Can you provide an analysis of the vocabulary overlap between the training corpus of the GloVe embeddings and the datasets used for evaluation? Does a lower vocabulary overlap correlate with poorer performance?
*   How does the choice of word embedding model (e.g., Word2Vec, GloVe) affect the performance of TBuckets?
*   Can you discuss the connection between TBuckets and lexical chaining techniques, and explain how your approach differs?
