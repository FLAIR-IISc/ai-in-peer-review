## Review of “Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets”

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for evaluating the quality of topics learned via Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to group topic words into “buckets” based on cosine similarity of their word embeddings, with fewer, larger buckets indicating higher topic coherence. Three methods for bucket creation are introduced: a simple greedy approach, an eigenvector-based method, and a refinement of the latter through reorganization. The method is evaluated on three standard datasets and demonstrates competitive performance in a weakly supervised text classification task, often outperforming or matching the state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015).

The primary contribution of this work, in my view, is the empirical demonstration that a relatively simple, embedding-based grouping of topic words correlates with human judgements of topic coherence and can improve downstream performance in a weakly supervised setting. While the core idea isn't groundbreaking, the implementation and evaluation provide a valuable addition to the toolkit for topic model assessment. A secondary contribution lies in the exploration of different bucket creation methods, particularly the SVD-based approaches which are parameter-free. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Empirical Performance:** The method consistently performs well compared to established techniques on multiple datasets, demonstrating its practical utility.
*   **Parameter-Free Methods:** The SVD-based approaches are a notable advantage, avoiding the tuning complexity of other coherence measures.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the rationale behind the TBuckets approach, connecting it to human intuition about topic coherence.
*   **Application to Downstream Task:** Demonstrating the value of improved topic quality in a text classification scenario strengthens the results.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The approach feels somewhat *ad hoc*. There’s no strong theoretical argument for why word-word similarity, as captured by embeddings, should directly translate to document-level topic coherence. 
*   **Contextual Mismatch:** The use of word embeddings, trained on sequential context, to evaluate topics which are derived from document-level co-occurrence raises a concern about potential mismatch in contextual representation.
*   **Clarity of Section 3:** The description of the TBuckets methods (Section 3) is convoluted and difficult to follow. The passive voice and intricate explanations obscure the design choices. A dedicated subsection for each method, with clearer language, would significantly improve readability.
*   **Missing Significance Tests:** The paper lacks statistical significance tests for the results presented in Table 1. Establishing the statistical significance of the improvements over the baseline would substantially bolster the claims.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Specifically, would using word2vec instead of GloVe significantly alter the performance?
2.  Could you provide more intuition regarding the "reorganization" step in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg? Perhaps an example illustrating when and why a word might be moved between buckets.
3.  Can you elaborate on the limitations of your approach? Are there specific types of topics or datasets where TBuckets might not perform well?
