Summary
The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic-coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into semantic “buckets” using word embeddings. The central intuition is that a topic is more coherent if most words fall into a single large bucket. Three realizations are presented: (1) agglomerative clustering over word embeddings; (2) an SVD-based assignment of words to eigenvector “directions” as bucket identifiers; and (3) SVD followed by a “reorganization” step that moves words into or out of the largest bucket based on similarity criteria with a dynamically computed threshold. The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation with human coherence judgments on three datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), and the SVD variants are largely parameter-free. The paper also demonstrates a practical use case in weakly supervised text classification: selecting topic runs with higher coherence improves downstream performance.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An embedding-based, bucket-size-oriented coherence metric with an SVD+reorganization variant that achieves state-of-the-art correlations on 2/3 datasets, while being largely parameter-free.
2) A systematic empirical evaluation across three standard datasets, including analysis of different scoring variants (size, WL, EVS), and comparison to Röder et al. (2015).
3) A practical application showing that prioritizing high-coherence topic sets can improve weakly supervised classification accuracy with the same annotation budget.

Strengths
- Empirical effectiveness and simplicity: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg matches or exceeds the best reported correlations in Röder et al. (2015) on NYT and 20NG, and clustering performs at least competitively on Genomics. The approach is easy to implement and understand, and the “single large bucket” intuition aligns with human inspection.
- Parameter-light design: The SVD-based variants require no tuned hyperparameters, a meaningful advantage over many coherence measures that need multiple corpus and smoothing choices. This improves reproducibility and reduces engineering overhead.
- Practical utility for weak supervision: The classification study is a compelling use case. Selecting the most coherent topic sets yields improved macro-F1 in 3/4 tasks, with pronounced gains on harder splits (e.g., PC vs MAC), supporting the claim that coherence is actionable for downstream labeling workflows.
- Sensible ablations within the framework: Reporting different scoring variants (size, WL, EVS, and their combinations) helps readers understand which components drive performance and shows that simple size-based signals, when guided by SVD structure, already go far.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and ad hoc design choices: The core idea—using word-level embedding similarities to judge coherence—is well explored. The bucketization and reorganization rules (e.g., using eigenvectors as bucket identifiers and the particular thresholding formula) feel heuristic, with little theoretical justification or formal analysis of when/why they should work.
- Missing statistical testing and uncertainty: Table 1 shows notable but sometimes small improvements. No significance tests, confidence intervals, or effect-size analyses are provided for either coherence correlations or classification outcomes, making it hard to judge the robustness of gains.
- Embedding mismatch and sensitivity: Pretrained GloVe embeddings are trained on sequential co-occurrence, while topics reflect document-level co-association. This mismatch could explain weaker Genomics results. The paper does not explore sensitivity to embedding choice (word2vec, fastText, domain-specific embeddings), dimensionality, or OOV handling, leaving open questions about stability and portability.
- Potential metric bias: The metric may over-reward topics with a single dominant theme and penalize valid multi-faceted topics (e.g., intentionally compound or hierarchical topics). There is no analysis of failure modes or whether human raters consider such topics coherent when split across two medium-sized buckets.
- Evaluation scope and fairness: The classification study compares annotating 5 runs vs selecting the best 5 of 10 runs. While annotation effort is matched, the compute budget is not, and the selection process risks cherry-picking. Also, the study lacks comparison to simple embedding-based baselines (e.g., average pairwise cosine) or to NPMI variants beyond Röder’s best settings.

Questions to Authors
- Significance: Are the improvements over Röder et al. (2015) significant (e.g., via permutation tests on correlations)? Please provide CIs for Pearson r and for macro-F1 in Table 3.
- Embedding sensitivity: How do results change with word2vec, fastText, or domain-specific embeddings (especially for Genomics)? What about different dimensionalities?
- Reorganization threshold: Can you justify the specific formulation AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? How sensitive are results to this formula, and have simpler thresholds performed similarly?
- Bucket identifiers: Why use the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers, and how does this relate to rank(A)? Would using only top-k (k < n) directions reduce noise?
- Multi-theme topics: How does TBuckets handle topics that are coherent but intentionally bimodal? Have you analyzed human ratings for such cases vs your scores?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and exact preprocessing details (tokenization, stopword handling, OOV treatment, cosine computation, clustering threshold selection)?

Additional Comments
- Writing and organization: Section 3 is difficult to follow. Consider one clear subsection per method (Clustering, SVD, SVD-Reorg) with concise pseudocode, explicit inputs/outputs, and complexity. Reduce narrative examples and move them to an appendix; focus on design choices and rationale.
- Baselines: Include a simple baseline such as average pairwise cosine among top-N words, and Sheffield-style distributional coherence, to better isolate the value of bucketing vs general embedding similarity.
- Reporting: Add Spearman correlations (rank-based robustness), per-dataset scatter plots, and error bars. Clarify top-N word selection and whether results hold for N ∈ {10, 20}.
- Application study: Control for compute budget by comparing top-5 out of 5 vs top-5 out of 10 under the same total runtime, or report the marginal cost-benefit.
