Summary
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of embedding-based measures for topic quality that group a topic’s top words into “buckets” intended to reflect coherent sub-themes. Three realizations are explored: agglomerative clustering over word embeddings, SVD-based bucketing by assigning words to eigenvector directions, and an SVD variant with a post-hoc reorganization step. Topic coherence is scored primarily by properties of the largest bucket. The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation with human judgments on NYT, 20NG, and Genomics datasets, and a small application demonstrates that selecting topic runs by TBuckets score can improve weakly supervised text classification.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An embedding-based bucketization framework (TBuckets) for topic coherence, including an SVD-based assignment and a reorganization heuristic driven by similarity “pulls.”
2) An empirical evaluation on three standard datasets, showing competitive or strong correlations on two datasets and a mixed picture overall, plus a downstream case study in weakly supervised classification.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive formulation grounded in word embeddings. The bucket metaphor provides interpretability: larger single buckets indicate a dominant theme; this maps well to human intuition for topical coherence.
- Competitive results on standard datasets. The SVD-Reorg variant achieves strong Pearson correlations on NYT and 20NG (≥ those reported by Röder et al.), and the clustering variant is competitive on Genomics, indicating that the general idea is viable.
- Practical, low-overhead implementation. The SVD variants avoid parameter tuning over complex configuration spaces (in contrast to Röder et al.), which is attractive for practitioners and simplifies deployment.
- Useful downstream signal. The text classification case study shows that selecting “high-quality” topic runs by TBuckets improves macro-F1 in 3/4 tasks, suggesting the scores have practical utility beyond intrinsic correlation.

Weaknesses
- Inconsistent performance across methods and datasets; no method consistently surpasses the SOTA. While SVD-Reorg is best on NYT/20NG, it underperforms substantially on Genomics (r≈0.68–0.69 vs 0.77 for Röder et al.), raising concerns about robustness. The clustering variant recovers competitive performance on Genomics, but overall the paper lacks a principled explanation for when each method should be preferred.
- Likely domain mismatch and OOV effects not analyzed. The methods rely on off-the-shelf GloVe trained on Wikipedia+Gigaword. The Genomics dataset plausibly contains many OOV or poorly represented terms, which can degrade cosine similarities and SVD structure. The paper does not report OOV rates, coverage, similarity distributions, or an OOV handling strategy (e.g., ignore, random init, UNK), making it difficult to interpret the failures on Genomics or generalize to new domains.
- “No parameters” claim is misleading. Although SVD/SVD-Reorg require no tuned hyperparameters at scoring time, the approach depends critically on pretrained embeddings that embody numerous implicit parameters and training choices. Further, the reorganization uses a hand-crafted thresholding formula (AWES − AWPS/AWES) and the choice to use the first n eigenvectors as buckets is itself a design parameter. The claim should be toned down and clarified.
- Embedding handling is underspecified or oversimplified. The paper assumes vector length is unimportant because cosine similarity is used, but it is unclear whether vectors are explicitly normalized before similarity and SVD. Using non-unit GloVe vectors can affect dot products and SVD directions; normalization choices can materially change bucket assignments and EVS scores. The method should specify normalization, similarity metric, and whether eigenvectors/word vectors are compared with cosine or dot product.
- Missing related work: lexical chaining. The core idea—grouping semantically related words into chains/clusters to assess coherence—has ties to lexical chaining and cohesion measures (e.g., Morris & Hirst, Barzilay & Elhadad, Galley & McKeown). A discussion and positioning relative to this literature is warranted.
- Limited guidance for practitioners. There is no mechanism to predict which TBuckets variant will work best for a new dataset. Simple diagnostics (e.g., vocabulary overlap with the embedding vocabulary, OOV rates, domain similarity) could inform method selection but are not discussed.
- Presentation and reporting issues. Figure 1 is too small to be readable. The paper would benefit from reporting OOV coverage, confidence intervals/significance tests for correlations, and ablations (e.g., with/without vector normalization; alternative embeddings; number of topic words). The handling of words not in the embedding space is unclear—are they dropped from bucket formation and scoring?

Questions to Authors
- OOV handling: How are topic words not present in GloVe treated? Are they ignored, mapped to UNK, or randomly initialized? What proportion of topic words are OOV in each dataset, particularly Genomics? How sensitive are correlations to OOV rate?
- Embedding preprocessing: Do you L2-normalize word vectors before computing similarities and before SVD? Are word–eigenvector similarities cosine or dot products? Are eigenvectors normalized?
- SVD design choices: Why use the first n eigenvectors (n = number of topic words) as bucket identifiers rather than selecting k < n based on explained variance or a threshold on singular values? Did you try dimensionality reduction prior to assignment?
- Reorganization threshold: Please justify the specific form AWES − AWPS/AWES. How robust is performance to this choice? Could a simpler margin on cosine suffice?
- Predictive guidance: Can you provide simple heuristics (e.g., vocabulary coverage with GloVe, domain similarity scores) to advise users when to choose clustering vs. SVD-based variants?
- Alternative embeddings: Did you try domain-specific embeddings (e.g., biomedical) for Genomics? If so, how did performance change?
- Classification case study: How much annotator effort was saved in S2 vs S1 (number of topics labeled)? Are gains statistically significant across runs?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Enlarge Figure 1 and ensure axes/legends are readable. Report exact settings for all clustering variants and include confidence intervals for reported correlations. Cite and discuss lexical chaining/cohesion literature to better situate the contribution. Clarify reproducibility: embedding version, tokenization, stopword handling, lemmatization, and treatment of multiword expressions.
