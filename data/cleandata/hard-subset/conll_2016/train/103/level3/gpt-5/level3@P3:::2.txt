Summary
The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic-coherence measures that groups the top-n words of a topic into “buckets” based on cosine similarity of pre-trained word embeddings. The intuition is that a coherent topic should yield one large bucket (single dominant theme). Three variants are presented: (i) an agglomerative clustering approach with a distance threshold, (ii) an SVD-based method that assigns words to eigenvector “directions,” and (iii) a reorganization heuristic that moves words into/out of the largest bucket using similarity-based criteria with a dynamic threshold. The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation with human coherence ratings on three standard datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics) and in a weakly supervised text classification setting where TBuckets is used to select the best topic runs for annotation. TBuckets matches or exceeds the best configurations reported by Röder et al. (2015) on two of the three datasets, and the SVD-based variants are claimed to be parameter-free.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple bucket-based topic coherence family that operationalizes “single-theme dominance” using word-embedding cosine similarity, including an SVD-based assignment and a reorganization heuristic.
2) An empirical comparison to Röder et al. (2015) on three datasets, showing competitive or better Pearson correlations, and a small demonstration of utility for weakly supervised classification via selecting topic runs.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive idea with competitive results: The bucketization criterion is easy to understand and aligns with human judgments of single-theme coherence. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the Röder et al. (2015) best settings on NYT and 20NG and is close on Genomics.
- Minimal tuning in SVD variants: Relative to the extensive configuration space in Röder et al., the SVD-based methods avoid per-dataset tuning, which is appealing for practical use.
- Sensible use of off-the-shelf resources: Leveraging publicly available GloVe embeddings makes the approach easy to reproduce and deploy.
- Utility beyond correlation benchmarks: The weakly supervised classification experiment suggests the scores can guide selection of topic runs to improve downstream performance, particularly on harder 20NG splits.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and ad hoc design: Using word–word similarity from distributional representations to assess topic coherence is established (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson, 2013). The primary novelty is the specific bucketization and reorganization heuristics, which lack theoretical justification. The reorganization threshold formula, in particular, appears heuristic without analysis or ablation.
- Potential context mismatch: Embeddings capture sequential/context-window semantics, whereas topics are document-level distributions. The paper does not address how this mismatch affects coherence measurement, especially for polysemous words or domain shift (visible in weaker Genomics performance).
- Missing statistical significance: Table 1 reports Pearson r values without confidence intervals or significance testing (e.g., Fisher r-to-z) for differences both across methods and against Röder et al. (2015). This is essential to substantiate “outperforms” claims.
- Methodological clarity issues in Section 3: The SVD approach uses “first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers,” but the rationale for coupling the number of buckets to the number of words is unclear; topics with k sub-themes could be better modeled with k < n buckets. The reorganization conditions (LBPW > NBPE; OBPW > max(NBPW, NBPE)) and the dynamic threshold AWES − AWPS/AWES need explicit justification, parentheses, and intuition; currently, the derivation feels opaque and ad hoc. Passive phrasing obscures design choices and ablations.
- Evaluation scope and fairness: Only the best settings from Röder et al. are quoted; it would be informative to compare to strong distributional baselines (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson variants, NPMI-based measures) under the same evaluation splits. The classification study briefly reports small improvements without variance estimates and compares “top 5” vs “bottom 5” chosen by the same metric (selection bias risk). It does not test whether simpler coherence (e.g., NPMI) could yield similar selection gains.

Questions to Authors
- Significance testing: Please report 95% CIs and statistical tests for correlations (e.g., Fisher r-to-z) in Table 1, including differences versus Röder et al. (2015). Are improvements on NYT and 20NG statistically significant?
- Embedding sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the choice of embeddings (word2vec, GloVe, FastText), dimensionality, training corpora, and in-domain vs generic embeddings (especially for Genomics)? Please include a sensitivity analysis.
- OOV handling and preprocessing: How are OOV topic words treated? Are stopwords included in top-n lists? Any stemming/casing normalization? Do results change if n varies (e.g., top-5, top-20)?
- SVD bucket count: Why use the first n eigenvectors (n = number of words) as bucket identifiers rather than selecting k < n directions (e.g., via singular value gaps or explained variance)? Did you try k-selection heuristics or projecting to the top-k subspace and then clustering?
- Reorganization threshold: Please clarify the exact formula with parentheses (AWES − (AWPS/AWES)?), provide intuition, and include an ablation measuring performance without reorg and with alternative thresholds (fixed margin, percentile-based).
- Downstream selection baseline: In the classification experiment, how does TBuckets-based selection compare to selecting runs by NPMI or Röder’s best coherence? Please add variance/confidence intervals and significance tests.

Additional comments and suggestions
- Exposition: Section 3 is hard to follow. Please restructure with one clear subsection per method (Clustering, SVD, SVD-Reorg) including a short algorithmic description, inputs/outputs, complexity, and hyperparameters. Replace passive voice with explicit design rationales, and prune redundant background so the core methods fit cleanly.
- Scoring choices: Justify weighting by the square of the largest singular value, and the focus on the largest bucket only. Consider reporting a complement penalty for dispersion across buckets or an entropy-based measure to capture multi-theme topics more gracefully.
- Robustness metrics: In addition to Pearson, consider Spearman rank correlation; report bootstrapped CIs.
- Reproducibility: Please release code, topic lists, and exact preprocessing to facilitate verification.
