Summary
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of measures for topic coherence that group a topic’s top-n words into “buckets” based on word-embedding similarity. The central intuition is that a topic with a single dominant bucket corresponds to a single coherent theme and thus higher interpretability. Three instantiations are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering over word embeddings with a distance threshold; (ii) an SVD-based variant that uses eigenvectors of AᵀA (A is the word-by-d matrix of embeddings) as bucket identifiers; and (iii) an SVD variant with a reorganization step that moves words into/out of the largest bucket based on similarity heuristics and a dynamic threshold. Topic coherence is scored primarily by the size/weighted size of the largest bucket, optionally weighted by eigenvector similarity and the top singular value. The methods are evaluated via Pearson correlation with human judgments on 20NG, NYT, and Genomics (top-10 words), and in a weakly supervised text classification setting where topic coherence is used to select better runs for annotation. TBuckets matches or outperforms the best settings reported by Röder et al. (2015) on two of three datasets and shows modest gains in the classification study.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, embedding-based bucketing framework for topic coherence, including SVD and reorganization variants that are largely parameter-free and easy to implement.
2) Empirical evidence that TBuckets achieves competitive or better correlation with human coherence judgments than a strong prior framework (Röder et al., 2015) on two standard datasets, and that coherence-based run selection can improve weakly supervised text classification in several 20NG subsets.
3) An interpretable operationalization of “single-theme dominance” via largest-bucket properties, offering a complementary perspective to PMI/NPMI-style measures.

Strengths
- Competitive to state-of-the-art correlations with minimal tuning: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg improves over Röder et al. on NYT and 20NG while avoiding extensive parameter search. This is valuable for practitioners who want a robust default without large configuration sweeps.
- Conceptual simplicity and interpretability: The “word bucket” intuition closely mirrors how humans assess topics. Buckets are easy to inspect and relate to sub-themes, which is a practical advantage for error analysis and tooling.
- Sensible use of distributional semantics: Leveraging word embeddings to group topic words is natural and often effective. The SVD view provides a clean way to derive orthogonal directions that can act as sub-theme identifiers.
- Utility beyond intrinsic evaluation: The text classification study illustrates how coherence can guide resource allocation in weak supervision (selecting runs to annotate). Even small gains matter when annotation budgets are fixed.
- Clarity of empirical setup: Datasets, top-10-word representation, and the evaluation protocol (Pearson r) align with common practice, facilitating comparison with prior work.

Weaknesses
- Limited theoretical grounding and somewhat ad hoc design: The choice to score only the largest bucket (and to weight by the top singular value) lacks justification beyond intuition. Topics with multiple coherent sub-themes may be unfairly penalized. Please discuss alternatives (e.g., entropy of bucket distribution, top-k bucket coverage) and provide ablations.
- Embedding–topic context mismatch and domain sensitivity: Global embeddings (GloVe trained on Wikipedia+Gigaword) may not capture document-level topical relations or domain-specific semantics (evidenced by weaker Genomics results). A sensitivity analysis using different embeddings (word2vec, fastText, in-domain embeddings) and OOV handling is important.
- Statistical significance and effect sizes missing: Improvements in Table 1 and the classification gains in Table 3 are not accompanied by significance tests or confidence intervals. Please report statistical tests (e.g., Williams/Steiger tests for dependent correlations; CIs or paired tests for macro-F1) and effect sizes.
- Ambiguities and reproducibility gaps: The SVD procedure uses “first n eigenvectors” as bucket identifiers; clarify dimensions when n > d or n < d. The reorganization threshold formula (AWES − AWPSAWES) is ambiguous and likely a typesetting error—please specify exact computation. Also detail OOV handling, similarity normalization, and runtime/complexity.
- Exposition and structure: Section 3 is hard to follow, with passive phrasing obscuring design choices and decision points (e.g., how ties are broken, how many buckets are allowed). The current narrative mixes intuition, algorithms, and examples. A clearer separation (one subsection per method with pseudo-code/step-by-step descriptions) would help.

Questions to Authors
- Embeddings and OOV:
  - How are topic words missing from the embedding vocabulary handled? Dropped, replaced, or assigned a default vector? What fraction of topic words is OOV per dataset?
  - How sensitive are results to the choice of embeddings (GloVe vs word2vec vs fastText) and to in-domain training (especially for Genomics)?
- SVD details:
  - V has dimensionality d; you use the “first n eigenvectors” as bucket identifiers. Is it min(n, d)? If n < d, why not restrict to the top-k directions selected by variance explained? If n > d (unlikely with top-10), how is this handled?
  - How is word–eigenvector similarity computed and normalized? Are eigenvectors re-scaled?
- Reorganization step:
  - Please clarify the exact threshold formula and its motivation. Is it AWES − (AWPS/AWES)? Why should this difference calibrate word–word vs word–eigenvector similarity ranges?
  - Provide ablations quantifying the contributions of into vs from moves separately.
- Evaluation:
  - Report Spearman correlation in addition to Pearson, and statistical tests for correlation differences vs baselines.
  - Vary the number of top words (e.g., 10/20/30). Do conclusions hold?
  - In the classification study, are macro-F1 differences significant? What is the human annotation cost difference between S1 and S2, if any?
- Clustering baseline:
  - How was Max Dist chosen? Was there a development split, or was it selected on test sets? Please report sensitivity curves and a principled selection protocol.

Additional comments
- Consider scoring functions that combine largest-bucket size with dispersion (e.g., Gini/entropy over bucket sizes) to avoid penalizing legitimately multi-faceted topics and to reduce brittleness.
- Minor language/typo issues: e.g., the reorganization threshold formula; “native bucket pull” terms could be defined more formally; a few examples contain mislabeled intermediate bucket states.
- Please include code and scripts to ensure reproducibility (including embedding versions, preprocessing, and exact hyperparameters).
