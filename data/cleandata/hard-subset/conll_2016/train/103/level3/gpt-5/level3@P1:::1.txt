Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes TBuckets, an embedding-based family of topic coherence measures that group a topic’s top words into “buckets” representing sub-themes. The intuition is that a topic with a single large bucket is more coherent. Three realizations are described: (i) agglomerative clustering over word embeddings (TBuckets-Clustering), (ii) SVD on the topic’s word-embedding matrix with bucket identifiers given by eigenvectors (TBuckets-SVD), and (iii) a reorganization step that moves words into/out of the largest bucket based on similarity criteria (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation with human coherence ratings on NYT, 20NG, and Genomics; SVD-Reorg exceeds the best configurations reported by Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets. The paper also demonstrates a use case for weakly supervised text classification, selecting high-coherence topic sets to annotate and obtaining improved macro-F1 in several binary subsets of 20NG.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A simple, embedding-based bucketing paradigm for topic coherence that is computationally light and conceptually intuitive, with two parameter-light SVD variants.
2) Empirical evidence that SVD-Reorg attains strong correlation with human judgments on common benchmarks (NYT, 20NG), and a practical demonstration that selecting topic sets by TBuckets scores can improve weakly supervised classification.

Strengths
- Clear, intuitive idea with low operational complexity: Representing topic words in an embedding space and rewarding a dominant bucket captures an interpretable notion of coherence without requiring external co-occurrence counts or heavy configuration. The SVD variants are efficient and avoid multiple hyperparameters typical of coherence frameworks.
- Competitive to SOTA on two benchmarks: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves r = 0.833 (NYT) and 0.869 (20NG), exceeding the best numbers reported by Röder et al., indicating the approach can match or beat carefully tuned PMI/NPMI-style measures in common news/forum domains.
- Practical utility beyond evaluation: The weakly supervised classification experiment shows that ranking topic sets by TBuckets scores can yield higher macro-F1 in three of four 20NG tasks, and large gaps between “highest-5” and “lowest-5” coherence runs on harder tasks (e.g., PC–MAC), underscoring real-world usefulness of the metric in annotation workflows.
- Thoughtful SVD-Reorg step: The reorganization heuristics address a recurrent failure mode where semantically related words are assigned to different eigenvector-aligned buckets. The dynamic thresholding (using AWPS and AWES) is a reasonable attempt to be parameter-agnostic.

Weaknesses
- Inconsistent cross-dataset performance; poor on Genomics: SVD-based methods underperform badly on Genomics (e.g., 0.691 vs 0.773 for Röder), raising concerns about robustness in specialized domains. While TBuckets-Clustering reaches 0.774 on Genomics, no single variant is consistently superior across datasets, complicating practical adoption.
- OOV handling and domain mismatch not analyzed: The likely cause of Genomics underperformance—high OOV rates and weak coverage of biomedical semantics in GloVe trained on Wikipedia/Gigaword—is not measured or discussed. The paper does not report OOV rates, the policy for OOV tokens (dropped? randomized? zero vectors?), or ablations with domain-specific embeddings (e.g., PubMed, fastText subword models). This is a critical gap for a method whose core signal is embedding similarity.
- “No parameters” claim is overstated: Although SVD variants avoid tuning thresholds, the approach relies heavily on pretrained embeddings whose training data, dimension, windowing, and normalization are de facto design choices that act as hyperparameters. TBuckets-Clustering also uses a distance threshold and linkage choice, and SVD-Reorg introduces derived thresholds and multiple similarity criteria. The paper should qualify and contextualize the parameter-free claim.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility issues: It is unclear whether vectors are unit-normalized and whether cosine or dot similarity is used throughout (important for SVD projections and eigenvector comparisons). The choice to use the “first n eigenvectors as buckets” (with n = number of words?) is unusual and not justified; typically, one would select k < d components. The reorganization threshold formula is ambiguously typeset and its motivation is heuristic. The final scoring (size, WL, EVS) weighted by the square of the top singular value lacks empirical justification or ablation. Significance testing and confidence intervals for correlations are missing.
- Related work coverage is incomplete: Prior embedding-based coherence measures (e.g., Aletras & Stevenson) are mentioned but not compared head-to-head. The paper also overlooks the lexical chaining literature (e.g., Morris & Hirst and successors) that clusters semantically related words within texts—a closely related conceptual lineage to “bucketing.” Positioning TBuckets relative to lexical chaining and modern neural variants would strengthen the contribution.
- Presentation issues: Figure 1 is too small to read; the normalization of TBuckets coherence to [0,1] versus human [1,3] in Table 2 is not fully explained, and some examples contain typographical inconsistencies that make inspection harder.

Questions to the authors
- What is the OOV rate per dataset (overall and per topic), and how are OOV words handled? Please report correlations after (a) dropping OOVs, (b) using a UNK vector, and (c) swapping in domain embeddings (e.g., PubMed, fastText with subwords) for Genomics.
- Why use the first n eigenvectors as bucket identifiers? Did you consider selecting k < d via explained variance or a heuristic tied to topic length? How sensitive are results to k?
- Are word vectors and eigenvectors unit-normalized before computing similarities? Is cosine used consistently? Please clarify the exact similarity computations and normalization steps.
- Can you provide criteria or meta-features (e.g., dataset OOV rate, embedding–vocabulary overlap, average pairwise similarity) to predict when SVD-Reorg vs Clustering should be preferred?
- How were the Röder et al. baselines obtained—reproduced or copied from reported best configurations? Can you include statistical significance tests (e.g., bootstrap CIs) for the correlation differences?
- Could you add ablations on the final scoring: size vs WL vs EVS, and with/without weighting by the top singular value?

Overall, the idea is promising and practically useful when the embedding space matches the domain, but the paper needs deeper analysis of failure modes (especially OOV and domain shift), clearer methodology, and broader related work to establish robustness and generality.
