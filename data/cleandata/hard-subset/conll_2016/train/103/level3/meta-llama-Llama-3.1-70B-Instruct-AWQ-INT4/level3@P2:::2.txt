**Summary**

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, to measure the quality of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) based topics. The approach groups topic words into thematic groups (buckets) using word embeddings and evaluates three techniques for creating these buckets: clustering-based, singular value decomposition (SVD)-based, and SVD-based with reorganization. The authors evaluate their techniques on three publicly available datasets and demonstrate better performance than the state-of-the-art results. Additionally, they highlight the utility of TBuckets for weakly supervised text classification.

**Contributions**

1. A novel approach to measure topic quality using word embeddings and bucketing techniques.
2. Evaluation of three techniques for creating buckets, including a parameter-free SVD-based method.
3. Demonstration of the approach's effectiveness on three standard datasets and a weakly supervised text classification setting.

**Strengths**

1. The paper proposes a simple and intuitive approach to measuring topic quality, which is easy to understand and implement.
2. The evaluation on three standard datasets provides a comprehensive comparison with the state of the art.
3. The application of TBuckets to weakly supervised text classification demonstrates its potential utility in real-world settings.
4. The authors provide a clear and detailed description of their approach and techniques.

**Weaknesses**

1. The idea of using word-word similarity to measure topic quality is not theoretically justified, and the authors do not provide a clear explanation for why this approach should work.
2. The paper raises concerns about the mismatch between the contexts for topics (document-level) and word embeddings (sequential context).
3. The authors do not provide significance tests for the reported results in Table 1, which makes it difficult to assess the statistical significance of their findings.
4. The writing style is clear, but there are some language issues and passive phrasing that obscure design choices.
5. The organization of Section 3 is hard to follow, and the authors could benefit from clearer exposition and removal of uninformative content.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a theoretical justification for using word-word similarity to measure topic quality?
2. How sensitive are your results to the choice of word embeddings (e.g., word2vec vs GloVe)?
3. Can you provide significance tests for the reported results in Table 1?
4. How do you plan to address the mismatch between the contexts for topics and word embeddings in future work?

**Additional Comments**

The authors should consider providing more details on the design choices and trade-offs involved in their approach. Additionally, they could benefit from clearer exposition and removal of uninformative content to make the paper more concise and easier to follow.
