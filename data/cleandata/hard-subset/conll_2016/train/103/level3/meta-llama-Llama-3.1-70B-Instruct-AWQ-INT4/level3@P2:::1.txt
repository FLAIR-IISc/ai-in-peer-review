**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach, called TBuckets, for measuring the quality of topics learned from text data using Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The approach groups topic words into thematic buckets, with the intuition that a single large bucket signifies a coherent theme. The authors explore three techniques for creating buckets: clustering-based, singular value decomposition (SVD)-based, and SVD-based with reorganization. They evaluate their techniques on three publicly available datasets and demonstrate better performance than the state-of-the-art results. Additionally, they highlight the utility of TBuckets for weakly supervised text classification.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel approach to topic quality evaluation**: The paper proposes a new method for evaluating topic quality using word embeddings and matrix factorization, which achieves strong results on standard datasets.
2. **Improved performance on some datasets**: The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms the state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets, specifically NYT and 20NG.
3. **Application to text classification**: The paper shows the usefulness of TBuckets for weakly supervised text classification, where high-quality topics are essential for accurate classification.

**Strengths**

1. **Strong results on some datasets**: The paper achieves state-of-the-art results on two datasets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Novelty of the approach**: The use of word embeddings and matrix factorization for topic quality evaluation is a new and interesting direction.
3. **Application to text classification**: The paper demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed approach in a real-world application.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Inconsistent results across methods and datasets**: The paper's results are inconsistent, with no proposed method consistently beating the state of the art across all datasets.
2. **Poor performance on genomics dataset**: The SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset, raising concerns about the approach's applicability to arbitrary datasets.
3. **Lack of discussion on out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms**: The paper lacks discussion or analysis of the impact of OOV terms on performance, which is a significant concern in many real-world applications.
4. **Misleading claim about parameter-free approach**: The paper claims that the method has "no parameters," which is misleading since pretrained word embeddings carry many implicit parameters.
5. **Unclear treatment of OOV terms**: The paper does not clearly explain how OOV terms are handled, which is essential for understanding the approach's limitations.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the inconsistent results across methods and datasets?
2. What is the impact of OOV terms on the performance of the proposed approach, and how do you plan to handle them?
3. Can you provide more details on the preprocessing of word embeddings, specifically regarding normalization and cosine similarity calculation?
4. How do you plan to predict which method will work best for a given dataset, and what features of the dataset would inform this decision?
