**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, to measure the quality of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) based topics. The approach groups topic words into thematic groups, called buckets, and computes a coherence score based on the properties of the largest bucket. The authors evaluate their approach on three publicly available datasets and demonstrate better performance than the state-of-the-art results. They also highlight the utility of TBuckets for the task of weakly supervised text classification.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel approach to topic quality evaluation**: The authors propose a new approach to evaluate topic quality by grouping topic words into thematic groups, called buckets.
2. **Three techniques for creating buckets**: The authors present three techniques for creating buckets: TBuckets-Clustering, TBuckets-SVD, and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg.
3. **Evaluation on standard datasets**: The authors evaluate their approach on three publicly available datasets and demonstrate better performance than the state-of-the-art results.

**Strengths**

1. **Strong results on standard datasets**: The authors demonstrate better performance than the state-of-the-art results on two out of three datasets.
2. **Novel approach to topic quality evaluation**: The authors propose a new approach to evaluate topic quality, which is different from existing approaches.
3. **Utility for weakly supervised text classification**: The authors highlight the utility of TBuckets for the task of weakly supervised text classification.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Inconsistent results across methods and datasets**: The authors' results are inconsistent across methods and datasets, which raises practical concerns about applying the method to arbitrary datasets.
2. **Poor performance on the genomics dataset**: The authors' SVD-based methods perform poorly on the genomics dataset, which may be due to the high proportion of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms.
3. **Lack of discussion on OOV terms**: The authors do not discuss the impact of OOV terms on their approach, which may affect the performance of their method.
4. **Misleading claim about parameter-free method**: The authors claim that their method has "no parameters", but this is misleading because pre-trained word embeddings carry many implicit parameters.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of inconsistent results across methods and datasets?
2. Can you provide more insights on why your SVD-based methods perform poorly on the genomics dataset?
3. How do you plan to handle OOV terms in your approach?
4. Can you clarify the role of pre-trained word embeddings in your method and how they affect the performance of your approach?
