**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach, called TBuckets, for measuring the quality of topics learned from text data using Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The approach groups topic words into thematic clusters, or "buckets", based on their word embeddings. Three techniques are introduced for creating these buckets: a simple greedy word-by-word assignment, an eigenvector-based method, and a reorganization method. The paper evaluates the proposed approach on three standard datasets and a weakly supervised text classification setting, demonstrating its effectiveness in measuring topic quality.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel approach to measuring topic quality**: The paper proposes a new method for evaluating topic models by partitioning top-n words into clusters using cosine similarity of word embeddings.
2. **Three techniques for creating buckets**: The paper introduces three methods for creating buckets of topic words, including a simple greedy approach, an eigenvector-based method, and a reorganization method.
3. **Empirical evaluation on standard datasets**: The paper evaluates the proposed approach on three standard datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in measuring topic quality.
4. **Application to weakly supervised text classification**: The paper shows the usefulness of the proposed approach in a weakly supervised text classification setting.

**Strengths**

1. **Simple and effective approach**: The proposed approach is easy to understand and implement, and demonstrates good performance in measuring topic quality.
2. **Robust evaluation**: The paper evaluates the proposed approach on multiple datasets and in a real-world application, demonstrating its effectiveness in different settings.
3. **Comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper compares the proposed approach to the state-of-the-art method (Röder et al., 2015), demonstrating its competitive performance.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper does not provide a theoretical justification for using word-word similarity to measure topic quality.
2. **Mismatch between contexts**: The paper raises concerns about the mismatch between the context in which topics are learned (document-level) and the context in which word embeddings are learned (sequential context).
3. **Language issues**: The paper contains some language issues, which can make it difficult to understand in some places.
4. **Organization and clarity**: The paper could benefit from clearer exposition and organization, particularly in Section 3, which is hard to follow.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide significance tests for the reported results in Table 1 to demonstrate the statistical significance of the differences between the proposed approach and the state-of-the-art method?
2. How sensitive is the proposed approach to the choice of word embeddings (e.g., word2vec vs GloVe)?
3. Can you provide more details on the design choices behind the three techniques for creating buckets, and why you chose to use cosine similarity of word embeddings?
4. How do you plan to address the mismatch between the context in which topics are learned and the context in which word embeddings are learned?
