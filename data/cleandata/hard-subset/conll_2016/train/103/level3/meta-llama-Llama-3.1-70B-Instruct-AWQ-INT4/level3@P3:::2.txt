This paper proposes a novel approach to evaluate topic models by partitioning top-n words into clusters ("buckets") using cosine similarity of word embeddings. The authors introduce three methods for creating these buckets: a simple greedy word-by-word bucket assignment, an eigenvector-based method, and a reorganisation method. The proposed approach, TBuckets, is evaluated on three standard datasets and a weakly supervised text classification setting, where it empirically outperforms or matches the state of the art.

**Summary of the Paper**

The authors propose a novel approach to measure topic quality using word embeddings. The idea is to group topic words into thematic groups (buckets) and evaluate the coherence of the topic based on the properties of the buckets. The authors introduce three techniques for creating buckets: TBuckets-Clustering, TBuckets-SVD, and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg. The approach is evaluated on three standard datasets and a weakly supervised text classification setting, where it outperforms or matches the state of the art.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel approach to evaluate topic models**: The authors propose a new approach to evaluate topic models by partitioning top-n words into clusters ("buckets") using cosine similarity of word embeddings.
2. **Three methods for creating buckets**: The authors introduce three methods for creating buckets: a simple greedy word-by-word bucket assignment, an eigenvector-based method, and a reorganisation method.
3. **Evaluation on standard datasets and text classification setting**: The authors evaluate the proposed approach on three standard datasets and a weakly supervised text classification setting, where it empirically outperforms or matches the state of the art.

**Strengths**

1. **Simple and intuitive idea**: The idea of grouping topic words into thematic groups (buckets) is simple and intuitive, and it provides a clear way to evaluate topic coherence.
2. **Good performance on standard datasets**: The proposed approach outperforms or matches the state of the art on three standard datasets.
3. **Useful for text classification**: The approach is useful for text classification, where it can help to select high-quality topics for human annotation.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The authors do not provide a theoretical justification for using word-word similarity to measure topic quality.
2. **Mismatch between contexts**: There is a mismatch between the contexts for topics (document-level) and word embeddings (sequential context), which may affect the performance of the approach.
3. **No significance tests**: The authors do not report significance tests for the results, which makes it difficult to determine the statistical significance of the differences between the proposed approach and the state of the art.
4. **Language issues**: The paper contains some language issues, which may make it difficult to understand for non-native English speakers.
5. **Section 3 is hard to follow**: Section 3 is hard to follow due to passive phrasing and unclear design choices.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a theoretical justification for using word-word similarity to measure topic quality?
2. How do you plan to address the mismatch between the contexts for topics and word embeddings?
3. Can you report significance tests for the results to determine the statistical significance of the differences between the proposed approach and the state of the art?
4. Can you clarify the design choices in Section 3 and provide a clearer exposition of the methods?
5. How sensitive is the approach to the choice of word embeddings (e.g., word2vec vs GloVe)?
