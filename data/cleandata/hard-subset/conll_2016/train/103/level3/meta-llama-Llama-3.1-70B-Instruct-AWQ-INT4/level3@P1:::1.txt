**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel approach, called TBuckets, for measuring the quality of topics learned from text using Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The approach groups topic words into thematic buckets using word embeddings and evaluates the coherence of the topics based on the properties of the buckets. The authors explore three techniques for creating buckets: clustering-based, singular value decomposition (SVD)-based, and SVD-based with reorganization. The paper evaluates the proposed approach on three publicly available datasets and demonstrates its effectiveness in measuring topic coherence. Additionally, the authors show the usefulness of the approach in weakly supervised text classification.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel approach to topic quality evaluation**: The paper proposes a new approach to evaluating topic quality using word embeddings and bucketing, which achieves strong results on standard datasets.
2. **Improved performance on certain datasets**: The SVD-based methods with reorganization outperform the state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets, namely NYT and 20NG.
3. **Application to text classification**: The paper demonstrates the usefulness of the approach in weakly supervised text classification, where it can help improve classification performance by selecting high-quality topics for human annotation.

**Strengths**

1. **Effective use of word embeddings**: The paper leverages the strengths of word embeddings in capturing semantic relationships between words to evaluate topic coherence.
2. **Improved performance on certain datasets**: The SVD-based methods with reorganization show promising results on certain datasets, indicating the potential of the approach.
3. **Useful application to text classification**: The paper demonstrates a practical application of the approach in weakly supervised text classification, which can be beneficial in real-world scenarios.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Inconsistent results across methods and datasets**: The paper reports inconsistent results across different methods and datasets, which raises concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the approach.
2. **Lack of analysis on out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms**: The paper does not discuss or analyze the impact of OOV terms on the performance of the approach, which can be a significant limitation.
3. **Misleading claim about parameter-free methods**: The paper claims that the SVD-based methods are parameter-free, which is misleading since pretrained word embeddings carry many implicit parameters.
4. **Unclear treatment of OOV terms**: The paper does not clearly explain how OOV terms are handled, which can affect the evaluation results.
5. **Presentation issues**: The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable, which can make it difficult to interpret the results.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the inconsistency in results across methods and datasets?
2. Can you provide more analysis on the impact of OOV terms on the performance of the approach?
3. How do you handle OOV terms in the evaluation, and what are the implications of ignoring them?
4. Can you clarify the normalization and cosine similarity used in the word embeddings, and how they affect the results?
5. How do you plan to extend the approach to handle other types of text data, such as short texts or texts with limited vocabulary?
