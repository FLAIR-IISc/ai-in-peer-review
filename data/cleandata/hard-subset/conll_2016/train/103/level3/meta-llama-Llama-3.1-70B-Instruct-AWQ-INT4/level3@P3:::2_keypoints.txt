- Novel evaluation idea: partition top-n topic words into clusters ("buckets") using cosine similarity of word embeddings to measure topic quality.
- Three bucket methods: greedy word-by-word assignment, eigenvector-based SVD method, and an SVD-based reorganisation variant (TBuckets-Clustering, TBuckets-SVD, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg).
- Empirical evaluation: tested on three standard datasets and a weakly supervised text-classification task, outperforming or matching state-of-the-art metrics.
- Intuitive and simple: grouping topic words into thematic buckets is a clear, easy-to-understand way to assess coherence.
- Practical utility: can help select high-quality topics for human annotation in text-classification settings.
- Strong empirical results: shows competitive performance across multiple datasets.
- Theoretical gap: no formal justification provided for using word–word similarity as a proxy for topic quality.
- Context mismatch: potential mismatch between document-level topic contexts and the sequential contexts captured by word embeddings.
- Statistical validity missing: no significance tests reported to confirm whether improvements are statistically meaningful.
- Language and clarity issues: manuscript contains language problems that may impede comprehension for non-native readers.
- Section 3 unclear: exposition is hard to follow due to passive phrasing and unclear design choices.
- Embedding sensitivity unclear: unclear how sensitive the approach is to choice of embeddings (e.g., word2vec vs. GloVe).
- Requests to authors: provide theoretical justification, address context mismatch, report significance tests, clarify Section 3 design choices, and evaluate embedding sensitivity.
