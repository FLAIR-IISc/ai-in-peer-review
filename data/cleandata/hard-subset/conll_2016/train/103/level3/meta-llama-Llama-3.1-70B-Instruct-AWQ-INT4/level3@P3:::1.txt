This paper proposes a novel approach to measuring topic quality using word embeddings and matrix factorization techniques. The authors introduce TBuckets, a method that groups topic words into thematic buckets, and evaluate its performance on three publicly available datasets. The paper presents three techniques for creating buckets: clustering-based, SVD-based, and SVD-based with reorganization. The results show that the proposed methods achieve strong results on standard datasets, outperforming state-of-the-art techniques in some cases.

However, the results are inconsistent across methods and datasets, and no proposed method consistently beats the state-of-the-art. The SVD-based methods perform notably poorly on the genomics dataset, raising concerns about the applicability of the method to arbitrary datasets. The paper lacks a thorough discussion and analysis of the impact of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms on the performance of the proposed methods, which could be a possible cause of the inconsistent results.

Moreover, the paper does not provide a mechanism or discussion for predicting which method will work best for a given dataset, such as based on vocabulary match with the pre-trained word embeddings. The authors claim that their method has "no parameters," which is misleading, as pre-trained word embeddings carry many implicit parameters.

The paper also overlooks related work in the lexical chaining literature, which should be cited and discussed. The treatment of OOV terms is unclear, and it is not specified whether they are ignored or how they affect the evaluation results. The description of word embeddings is oversimplified, assuming that vector length is unimportant when vectors may not be unit length. Clarification about normalization and cosine use is needed.

The presentation of the paper could be improved, particularly the graphs in Figure 1, which are too small to be readable.

**Contributions:**

1. The paper proposes a novel approach to measuring topic quality using word embeddings and matrix factorization techniques.
2. The authors introduce three techniques for creating buckets of words and evaluate their performance on three publicly available datasets.
3. The paper demonstrates the usefulness of the proposed method for weakly supervised text classification.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper presents a novel approach to measuring topic quality, which is a crucial task in topic modeling.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their methods on three publicly available datasets.
3. The paper demonstrates the potential of the proposed method for weakly supervised text classification.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The results are inconsistent across methods and datasets, and no proposed method consistently beats the state-of-the-art.
2. The paper lacks a thorough discussion and analysis of the impact of OOV terms on the performance of the proposed methods.
3. The authors do not provide a mechanism or discussion for predicting which method will work best for a given dataset.
4. The paper overlooks related work in the lexical chaining literature.
5. The treatment of OOV terms is unclear, and the description of word embeddings is oversimplified.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do you plan to address the inconsistent results across methods and datasets?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of OOV terms on the performance of the proposed methods?
3. How do you plan to predict which method will work best for a given dataset?
4. Can you discuss the related work in the lexical chaining literature and how it relates to your proposed method?
5. How do you plan to improve the treatment of OOV terms and provide a more accurate description of word embeddings?
