### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a new family of methods, called TBuckets, for automatically evaluating the coherence of topics learned by models like LDA. The core idea is to take the top N words of a topic and partition them into thematic clusters, or "buckets," based on the cosine similarity of their pre-trained word embeddings. The intuition is that a coherent topic will result in one large bucket containing most of the words, while an incoherent topic will be fragmented into multiple smaller buckets. The authors introduce three specific bucketing techniques: a simple greedy clustering method, a method based on the eigenvectors from an SVD of the word embedding matrix, and a final method that adds a reorganization step to the SVD approach. The methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, where the best-performing variant (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) is shown to outperform or match the state-of-the-art results from Röder et al. (2015). The utility of the metric is also demonstrated in a weakly supervised text classification task, where selecting topic models with higher coherence scores leads to improved classification performance.

### Contributions
1.  **A simple, parameter-free, and empirically effective method for topic coherence evaluation.** The primary contribution is the demonstration that a straightforward method based on partitioning words using pre-trained embeddings (specifically, the SVD-based variants) can achieve state-of-the-art performance in correlating with human coherence judgments. The fact that the SVD methods are parameter-free is a significant practical advantage over complex, highly-parameterized baselines.
2.  **Validation in a downstream application.** The paper provides a practical demonstration of the proposed metric's usefulness by showing that it can be used to select higher-quality topic models, which in turn leads to improved performance in a weakly supervised text classification setting. This goes beyond standard correlation analysis and strengthens the claim of the metric's utility.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on the NYT and 20NG datasets and is competitive on the Genomics dataset (Table 1). Outperforming a strong and comprehensive baseline like Röder et al. (2015) is a significant result and provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness.
2.  **Simplicity and Parameter-Free Nature:** Compared to the baseline, which involves exploring a large configuration space of different measures and parameters, the proposed SVD-based methods are conceptually simple and require no parameter tuning. This makes them highly practical, easy to implement, and more likely to be adopted by other researchers and practitioners.
3.  **Intuitive Core Idea:** The central concept of grouping semantically similar topic words into "buckets" is intuitive and easy to understand. The dry-run examples provided at the beginning of Section 3 effectively illustrate this intuition, making the motivation behind the approach clear.
4.  **Demonstrated Usefulness:** The text classification experiment (Section 4.3) is a valuable addition. It successfully shows that the proposed metric has practical utility beyond just mimicking human scores. By using TBuckets to filter for high-quality topic sets before human labeling, the authors demonstrate a tangible benefit, leading to better classification accuracy.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** The paper's main weakness is its reliance on intuition without providing a deeper theoretical justification. The approach is somewhat ad hoc. It is not explained *why* partitioning words based on pairwise similarity in an embedding space should be a principled way to measure the quality of a topic, which is fundamentally a probability distribution over a vocabulary derived from document-level word co-occurrence.
2.  **Potential Mismatch of Contexts:** There is a potential conceptual mismatch that is not addressed. Word embeddings like GloVe capture semantic relationships from local, sequential word contexts (e.g., a sliding window). In contrast, topic models capture "aboutness" from global, document-level co-occurrence statistics. It is plausible that these two types of contexts capture different kinds of semantic relationships, and a word set that is coherent in one context may not be in the other. The paper does not discuss or investigate this potential discrepancy.
3.  **Clarity of Exposition in Section 3:** The description of the methods, particularly the `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` algorithm, is difficult to follow. The use of passive phrasing (e.g., "Observation of buckets getting formed revealed...") obscures the design choices and makes the process seem less like a principled algorithm and more like a series of post-hoc fixes. The motivation and derivation of the dynamic threshold for reorganization are particularly opaque. The section would be much clearer if each of the three methods were presented in its own dedicated subsection with a clear, step-by-step algorithmic description.
4.  **Incomplete Experimental Analysis:** The empirical results are promising, but the analysis is incomplete.
    *   Table 1 reports performance improvements, but no significance tests are performed. It is unclear if the observed gains over the baseline are statistically significant.
    *   The entire evaluation relies on a single set of pre-trained word embeddings (300d GloVe). The method's sensitivity to the choice of embeddings (e.g., word2vec vs. GloVe, different dimensions, embeddings trained on different or domain-specific corpora) is a critical unanswered question.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please provide statistical significance tests for the results in Table 1 to confirm that your improvements over the Röder et al. (2015) baseline are statistically significant?
2.  How sensitive is your approach to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with other popular embeddings like word2vec or FastText, or with embeddings of different dimensionality? Performance on the Genomics dataset is notably lower than on the other two; would embeddings trained on a biomedical corpus (e.g., PubMed) improve results there?
3.  Could you provide a clearer motivation for the specific formula used to calculate the dynamic threshold in the `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method? How was this formula derived, and were alternative formulations considered?
4.  Could you comment on the potential conceptual mismatch between the local context used to train word embeddings and the document-level context from which topics are derived? Do you believe this poses a limitation to your approach?
