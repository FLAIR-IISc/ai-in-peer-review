### Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a novel method, TBuckets, for measuring the coherence of topics generated by models like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to group a topic's most probable words into semantic "buckets" using their pre-trained word embeddings. The intuition is that a coherent topic will result in one large bucket containing most of its words, while an incoherent topic will be split into multiple smaller buckets. The authors present three techniques for this bucketing process: agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering), Singular Value Decomposition (TBuckets-SVD), and an SVD-based method with a post-hoc reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The method is evaluated by correlating its scores with human judgments on three standard datasets and is also applied to a weakly supervised text classification task.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel, embedding-centric framework for topic coherence:** The paper introduces an intuitive and original approach to evaluating topic quality by operationalizing the idea of semantic grouping ("bucketing") via word embeddings. This moves beyond traditional pairwise co-occurrence measures (like PMI) towards a more holistic, group-based assessment of topic words.
2.  **Strong empirical results on general-domain datasets:** The proposed `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method achieves state-of-the-art performance on two of the three benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG), demonstrating that using dense vector representations of words is a powerful technique for this task.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Strong Intuition:** The central idea of forming semantic buckets is highly intuitive and provides a fresh perspective on topic coherence evaluation. Instead of aggregating pairwise word similarity scores, this method attempts to find a dominant semantic theme within a topic. The use of SVD to identify orthogonal "sub-themes" is a particularly interesting technical choice.

2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` technique demonstrates impressive performance, outperforming the best results reported by Röder et al. (2015) on the NYT and 20NG datasets. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of leveraging pre-trained word embeddings for this task, at least on general-domain text where embedding coverage is high.

3.  **Demonstrated Utility in a Downstream Task:** The authors validate their coherence metric by showing its utility in a weakly supervised text classification setting. By using TBuckets to select higher-quality topic sets for human annotation, they achieve improved classification performance on several datasets (Table 3). This is a valuable addition that grounds the work in a practical application and shows that the proposed metric has real-world benefits beyond simply correlating with human scores.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent Performance and Lack of Practical Guidance:** The paper's strongest weakness is the inconsistent performance of the proposed methods across datasets. While `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` is the best performer on NYT and 20NG, its performance drops dramatically on the Genomics dataset, where it is significantly outperformed by the baseline and the `TBuckets-Clustering` variant. The paper offers no analysis as to why this might be the case, nor does it provide any guidance to a practitioner on how to choose the best TBuckets variant for a new dataset. This lack of robustness and guidance severely limits the practical applicability of the proposed framework.

2.  **Critical Omission of Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) Word Handling:** The entire approach is predicated on the availability of word embeddings. However, the paper completely fails to discuss how Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) words are handled. This is a critical detail, especially given the poor performance on the Genomics dataset, which likely contains many domain-specific terms absent from the GloVe training corpus (Wikipedia/Gigaword). Are OOV words ignored? If so, a topic with many OOV words would receive an artificially low score based on a smaller subset of its words. This omission undermines the replicability and credibility of the experimental results.

3.  **Misleading "Parameter-Free" Claim:** The authors repeatedly claim the SVD-based methods are "completely parameter free," which is misleading. The choice of pre-trained word embeddings is a massive, implicit hyperparameter. The results are entirely dependent on the source corpus, dimensionality, context window, and training algorithm of the chosen embeddings. A different set of embeddings (e.g., word2vec, or GloVe trained on a different corpus) would almost certainly yield different results. This claim should be retracted or heavily qualified.

4.  **Missing Discussion of Related Work:** The idea of grouping semantically related words is the central concept in the literature on lexical chaining. This body of work is highly relevant to the "bucketing" idea and should have been cited and discussed to properly contextualize the paper's contribution.

5.  **Presentation Issues:** The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be legible, preventing the reader from properly interpreting the effect of the `Max Dist` parameter for the clustering-based method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify how Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) words were handled in your experiments? What was the OOV rate for the GloVe embeddings across the three datasets? An analysis correlating OOV rates with the performance of your SVD-based methods would be very insightful.

2.  Your proposed methods show highly variable performance across datasets. For instance, `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` excels on NYT/20NG but performs poorly on Genomics, where `TBuckets-Clustering` is superior. Can you offer any hypotheses for this behavior? Based on your findings, what characteristics of a dataset or its topics would lead you to recommend one TBuckets variant over another?

3.  Given that the choice of word embeddings (source corpus, dimensionality, etc.) is a critical parameter, how sensitive are your results to different embeddings? Have you experimented with other pre-trained vectors, such as word2vec?
