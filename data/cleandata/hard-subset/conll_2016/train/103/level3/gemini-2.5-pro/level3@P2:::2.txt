### Summary

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the quality of topics learned by models like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to partition the top N words of a topic into thematic groups, or "buckets," based on the semantic similarity of their word embeddings. The authors' intuition is that a coherent topic will result in a single large bucket, while an incoherent topic will be split into multiple smaller buckets. The paper proposes three methods for this partitioning: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), one of which includes a reorganization step. The proposed methods are evaluated against human judgments on three standard datasets, where they achieve results comparable or superior to the state-of-the-art. The paper also demonstrates the practical utility of the proposed metric in a weakly supervised text classification task, showing that selecting topic models with higher coherence scores leads to better classification performance.

### Contributions

1.  **A novel and effective set of methods for measuring topic coherence.** The primary contribution is the "TBuckets" framework, which leverages pre-trained word embeddings to group topic words. The SVD-based methods (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) are particularly notable for being parameter-free, offering a practical advantage over competing methods that require significant tuning.
2.  **Strong empirical results on standard benchmarks.** The paper provides a thorough evaluation on three public datasets, demonstrating that the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method outperforms the previous state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015) on two of the three datasets in terms of correlation with human judgments.
3.  **A demonstration of practical utility in a downstream application.** The authors show that their coherence metric can be used to select higher-quality topic models, which in turn improves performance on a weakly supervised text classification task. This application-oriented evaluation strengthens the paper's claims about the usefulness of the proposed metric.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance and State-of-the-Art Results:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves a higher Pearson correlation with human judgments than the best-performing systems reported in Röder et al. (2015) on the NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859) datasets. These strong quantitative results are a compelling argument for the method's effectiveness.
2.  **Simplicity and Practicality:** The core idea of grouping semantically similar words is intuitive. More importantly, the SVD-based methods are presented as parameter-free. This is a significant advantage over many existing coherence measures, including the framework of Röder et al. (2015) which explores a large configuration space, making the proposed method much easier to apply in practice.
3.  **Demonstrated Usefulness for Downstream Tasks:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 adds significant value to the paper. By showing that filtering topic models based on the proposed coherence score leads to improved classification accuracy, the authors provide concrete evidence that their metric captures a meaningful aspect of model quality that has tangible benefits for real-world applications. This moves the work beyond a simple correlation study.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of the Method Description:** Section 3, which describes the core methods, is difficult to follow. The writing uses passive phrasing that obscures the specific algorithms and design choices. For example, the description of TBuckets-SVD is abstract, and the motivation for focusing on the V matrix could be explained more clearly. The reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) introduces several new concepts (NBPW, NBPE, etc.) and a complex dynamic threshold, which would be much more understandable if presented as a step-by-step algorithm or with pseudocode. A clearer structure, perhaps with a dedicated subsection for each of the three methods, would greatly improve the paper's reproducibility and impact.
2.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis and Justification:** The approach is presented as intuitive but lacks a deeper theoretical justification. There is a potential conceptual mismatch between the source of information for word embeddings (local, sequential word context) and for topic models (global, document-level word co-occurrence). The paper does not discuss this potential issue or analyze why word-word similarity from a local context is a suitable proxy for coherence in a document-level topic. The method feels somewhat ad hoc, and the paper would be stronger if it provided more analysis on *why* this approach works so well.
3.  **Absence of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper claims state-of-the-art performance based on the correlation scores in Table 1. However, no statistical significance tests are reported. Without such tests, it is impossible to determine if the observed improvements over the baseline (Röder et al., 2015) are statistically significant or simply due to random variation. This is a critical omission for a paper whose main claims rest on empirical superiority.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please perform statistical significance tests on the results presented in Table 1? This would help clarify whether the improvements of your TBuckets methods over the state-of-the-art are statistically significant.
2.  Your experiments rely on 300-dimensional GloVe embeddings. How sensitive is the performance of TBuckets to the choice of word embeddings? For instance, have you investigated the effect of using different models (e.g., word2vec, FastText), different dimensionalities, or embeddings trained on corpora that are more domain-specific (especially for the Genomics dataset)?
