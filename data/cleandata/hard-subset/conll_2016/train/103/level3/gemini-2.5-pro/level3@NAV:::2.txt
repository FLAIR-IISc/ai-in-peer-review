**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**
This paper proposes a novel, intuitive approach named TBuckets for evaluating topic coherence. The core idea is to partition the top words of a topic into semantic clusters ("buckets") using pre-trained word embeddings. A topic is considered coherent if most of its words fall into a single large bucket. The authors introduce three bucketing methods: a greedy clustering approach, an eigenvector-based SVD method, and an SVD method with a reorganisation step. The primary contributions are: 1) An empirical demonstration that this simple clustering-based method can achieve state-of-the-art performance in correlating with human judgments of topic quality. 2) The proposal of parameter-free variants that offer a practical advantage over prior art requiring extensive tuning.

**Strengths:**
1.  The proposed method, particularly TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, demonstrates strong empirical performance, outperforming or matching the state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015) on two of three standard datasets. This suggests the approach is effective in practice.
2.  The SVD-based methods are parameter-free, which is a significant practical advantage. This removes the need for costly parameter sweeps common in other topic coherence measures, making the method more accessible and reliable.
3.  The paper successfully demonstrates the utility of the proposed metric in a downstream task of weakly supervised text classification, showing that selecting topic models with higher coherence scores can lead to better classification accuracy. This provides a clear practical motivation for the work.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The method, while effective, feels somewhat ad hoc and lacks a strong theoretical justification. The paper does not provide new insight into why partitioning words into discrete buckets should be superior to aggregating pairwise similarity scores, and the reorganisation step is based on heuristics that are not fully motivated.
2.  There is a potential conceptual mismatch between the signals used to generate topics (document-level co-occurrence) and those used to train word embeddings (local window-based context). The implications of this mismatch are not discussed.
3.  The exposition of the methods in Section 3 is difficult to follow. The use of passive voice and a narrative structure obscures the specific algorithmic steps and design choices. Restructuring this section with clear subsections for each method would significantly improve readability.
4.  The empirical claims in Table 1 are not supported by statistical significance tests. It is therefore unclear if the reported improvements over a strong baseline are statistically meaningful.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Can you provide significance tests for the correlation results in Table 1 to validate that the observed improvements over the state-of-the-art are statistically significant?
2.  The experiments use GloVe embeddings. How sensitive is your method to the choice of word embedding model (e.g., word2vec vs. GloVe) or the training corpus? Would using domain-specific embeddings further improve performance on datasets like Genomics?
