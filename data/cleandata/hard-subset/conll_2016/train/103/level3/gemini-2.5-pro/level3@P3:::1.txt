### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a novel framework, TBuckets, for evaluating the coherence of topics produced by models like LDA. The core idea is to group the top words of a topic into semantic "buckets" using pre-trained word embeddings. The intuition is that a coherent topic will result in one large, dominant bucket. The authors propose three methods to create these buckets: agglomerative clustering (TBuckets-Clustering), Singular Value Decomposition (TBuckets-SVD) to identify orthogonal sub-themes, and a reorganization step on top of SVD to refine the buckets (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The methods are evaluated by measuring the correlation of their coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets. The results show that the proposed methods, particularly SVD-Reorg, achieve state-of-the-art performance on two of the three datasets.

### Contributions

1.  **A Novel Embedding-Based Framework for Topic Coherence:** The primary contribution is a new, intuitive approach to measuring topic coherence. Instead of relying on pairwise word similarity scores aggregated across a topic, the "bucketing" method attempts to identify the number of distinct semantic themes within a topic. This conceptual shift from aggregation to partitioning is a valuable contribution to the field.
2.  **Use of Matrix Factorization for Sub-Theme Identification:** The paper introduces the use of SVD on a topic's word embedding matrix as a way to identify orthogonal sub-themes, which then serve as the basis for buckets. This is a novel application of matrix factorization in this specific context and the proposed reorganization step (SVD-Reorg) is a creative heuristic to refine this process.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed methods achieve very strong correlation with human judgments on the 20NG and NYT datasets, outperforming prior state-of-the-art results. This demonstrates the effectiveness of using dense vector representations for this task.

### Strengths

1.  **Intuitive and Novel Approach:** The central idea of partitioning topic words into thematic buckets is conceptually clear and compelling. It aligns well with the human process of interpreting a topic by identifying its core concept and seeing which words fit. This provides a fresh perspective compared to prevalent methods based on averaging pairwise PMI or cosine similarity scores.
2.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The experimental results are strong. On the NYT and 20NG datasets, the `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method achieves Pearson correlations of 0.837 and 0.869 respectively, surpassing the previous state-of-the-art baselines. This provides solid empirical validation for the proposed approach.
3.  **Simplicity and Practicality of SVD methods:** The SVD-based methods are presented as being free of tunable hyperparameters, which is a significant practical advantage over complex coherence frameworks like that of Röder et al. (2015), which require searching through a large configuration space. This simplicity makes the method attractive for practitioners.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Inconsistent Performance and Lack of Analysis:** The paper's most significant weakness is the inconsistent performance of its methods across datasets and the absence of any analysis to explain why. `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` is the best performer on NYT and 20NG, but it performs poorly on the Genomics dataset. Conversely, `TBuckets-Clustering` is the best of the proposed methods on Genomics. This inconsistency raises serious practical concerns: a user with a new dataset would have no principled way to choose which of the proposed methods to apply. The paper offers no discussion or mechanism for predicting which method will work best in a given scenario.
2.  **Failure to Address Out-of-Vocabulary (OOV) Words:** A likely cause for the inconsistent performance, particularly the failure of SVD methods on the specialized Genomics dataset, is the presence of OOV words. The method relies entirely on pre-trained GloVe embeddings trained on general domain text (Wikipedia/Gigaword). Domain-specific terms in the Genomics dataset are likely missing from the GloVe vocabulary. The paper completely fails to discuss how OOV words are handled (are they ignored? assigned a zero vector?) or to analyze their potential impact. This is a critical omission for any method built upon pre-trained embeddings.
3.  **Misleading Claims and Unclear Methodological Details:**
    *   The claim that the SVD-based methods are "completely parameter free" is misleading. The choice of pre-trained word embeddings is a massive, implicit parameter choice involving millions of parameters. The performance is fundamentally tied to these embeddings, but this dependency is downplayed.
    *   The description of how word embeddings are used is imprecise. The paper mentions cosine similarity but does not clarify if vectors are L2-normalized. This is important as vector length can carry semantic information, and assuming unit-length vectors without stating so is an oversimplification. The "reorganization" logic relies on comparing similarities (e.g., LBPW > NBPE), but the exact similarity metric is not explicitly defined.
4.  **Missing Related Work:** The idea of grouping semantically related words is central to the literature on lexical chaining. This body of work, which has been used for tasks like text summarization and coherence modeling, is highly relevant as it deals with identifying sequences of related words in text. This related work should have been cited and discussed to properly contextualize the paper's contribution.
5.  **Presentation Issues:** The graphs in Figure 1, which show the performance of `TBuckets-Clustering` under different distance thresholds, are too small to be legible. This prevents the reader from properly assessing the sensitivity of this method to its main parameter.

### Questions to Authors

1.  How are out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words handled by your methods? Could you please report the OOV rates for the GloVe embeddings on each of the three datasets? Do you believe the OOV rate is a primary cause for the poor performance of the SVD-based methods on the Genomics dataset?
2.  Given the inconsistent performance, what guidance can you offer practitioners for choosing among TBuckets-Clustering, SVD, and SVD-Reorg for a new dataset? Would a metric like vocabulary overlap with the pre-trained embeddings be a reliable predictor of performance?
3.  Could you clarify the claim that the SVD-based methods are "completely parameter free"? While they lack user-tuned hyperparameters for the algorithm itself, the choice of pre-trained embeddings is a critical dependency. Have you experimented with other embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, or embeddings trained on a domain-specific corpus) to analyze this sensitivity?
4.  Could you please clarify the specific similarity metric used for "semantic similarity" and "similarity with the eigenvector" in Sections 3.1 and 3.2? Is it cosine similarity, and if so, are the word and eigenvector vectors L2-normalized before the calculation?
