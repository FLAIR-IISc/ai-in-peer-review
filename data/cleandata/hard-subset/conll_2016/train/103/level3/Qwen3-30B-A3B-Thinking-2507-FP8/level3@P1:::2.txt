

Summary: This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach to measuring topic quality in LDA-based topic models by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The authors introduce three techniques for creating these buckets: clustering-based (TBuckets-Clustering), SVD-based (TBuckets-SVD), and SVD with reorganization (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). The approach is evaluated on three standard datasets and demonstrated for weakly supervised text classification.

Main Contributions: The primary contribution is the TBuckets framework, which offers a simple yet effective way to measure topic coherence through bucket formation. The secondary contribution is the parameter-free nature of TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, which outperforms state-of-the-art methods on two of three datasets without requiring parameter tuning. The tertiary contribution is the practical demonstration of TBuckets' utility in improving weakly supervised text classification by selecting high-quality topics.

Strengths:
1. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves state-of-the-art results on two out of three datasets (NYT and 20NG) while being completely parameter-free, representing a significant practical advantage over existing methods that require extensive parameter tuning. This is particularly noteworthy given that the authors' method outperforms the best results reported by Roder et al. (2015) without any hyperparameter optimization.

2. The application to weakly supervised text classification provides compelling evidence of the method's utility beyond topic quality measurement. The results in Table 3 demonstrate that using TBuckets to select high-quality topics leads to better classification performance (68.47% vs. 66.98% for PC vs MAC) and that the difference between high and low coherence topic sets is substantial (96.19% vs. 91.09% for POLITICS vs SCIENCE), validating the practical importance of topic quality measurement.

3. The intuitive connection between the bucket formation process and human interpretation of topic coherence is well-justified through the examples provided in Section 3. The paper effectively demonstrates how a single large bucket corresponds to coherent topics while multiple medium-sized buckets indicate lower coherence, aligning with human judgments.

Weaknesses:
1. The paper lacks theoretical justification for why grouping topic words into buckets using word embeddings would correlate with human judgments of topic coherence. The connection between the method and the underlying concept of topic quality appears ad hoc, with no clear explanation for why word-word similarity (as measured by embeddings) should serve as a proxy for topic coherence.

2. The paper doesn't address a fundamental mismatch between the contexts used for training word embeddings (which typically rely on sequential context in large corpora) and the document-level context of topics. This could lead to a disconnect between the word similarity measures used and the actual semantic relationships relevant to topic coherence.

3. The paper fails to provide statistical significance tests for the results in Table 1. Without significance testing, it's difficult to assess whether the reported improvements over the state-of-the-art are statistically meaningful or merely due to random variation in the evaluation.

4. The exposition in Section 3 is unclear and difficult to follow due to passive phrasing and lack of clear organization. The description of the reorganization procedure (3.1) is particularly convoluted, obscuring the key design choices. The paper would benefit from clearer subsections for each method and more direct explanations of the algorithmic choices.

Questions to Authors:
1. Could you provide statistical significance tests for the results in Table 1 to demonstrate whether the performance improvements over state-of-the-art are statistically significant?
2. How sensitive is your approach to the choice of word embeddings (e.g., GloVe vs. word2vec)? Have you tested different embedding models?
3. Could you clarify how the dynamic threshold for reorganization (AWES - AWPS/AWES) was derived and why it's preferable to a fixed threshold?
4. Why does the paper claim that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is "completely parameter-free" when it uses a dynamic threshold that depends on topic-specific properties?
