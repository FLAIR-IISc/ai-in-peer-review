

**Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"**

This paper proposes TBuckets, a method for evaluating topic model quality by grouping topic words into thematic clusters ("buckets") based on cosine similarity of word embeddings. The authors introduce three techniques: a greedy word-by-word assignment, an eigenvector-based method using SVD, and a reorganization refinement. The approach is evaluated on three standard datasets and demonstrates improved or matching performance to Röder et al. (2015) in topic coherence evaluation, with additional application to weakly supervised text classification.

**Main Contributions:**
1. A practical method for topic quality evaluation using word embeddings and bucket formation.
2. Parameter-free approaches (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that outperform tuned state-of-the-art methods.
3. Demonstration of utility in weakly supervised text classification by filtering high-quality topics.

**Strengths:**
1. The empirical results are compelling, showing TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on two out of three datasets with no parameter tuning.
2. The application to text classification (Table 3) demonstrates clear practical utility, particularly for challenging datasets like PC vs MAC.
3. The parameter-free nature of two methods represents a significant practical advantage over existing approaches requiring extensive tuning.

**Weaknesses:**
1. The core idea lacks theoretical justification; the paper fails to explain why word embedding similarity (sequential context) aligns with document-level topic coherence.
2. Section 3 is poorly structured with passive voice obscuring methodological choices, making it difficult to follow the algorithmic details.
3. Critical omissions include missing significance tests for Table 1 results and no analysis of sensitivity to word embedding choices (e.g., GloVe vs. word2vec).
4. The paper does not address the fundamental mismatch between word embeddings trained on sequential context and topics defined at document level.

**Questions to Authors:**
1. Could you provide significance tests for the results in Table 1?
2. How would your method perform with different word embedding sources (e.g., word2vec vs. GloVe)?
3. Can you clarify the theoretical rationale for using word embeddings to measure topic coherence?

The paper presents a practical approach with strong empirical results but suffers from theoretical gaps and insufficient methodological clarity. While the performance improvements are noteworthy, the lack of theoretical foundation and missing experimental analyses significantly limit the contribution. The writing quality could also be improved to better convey the technical content.
