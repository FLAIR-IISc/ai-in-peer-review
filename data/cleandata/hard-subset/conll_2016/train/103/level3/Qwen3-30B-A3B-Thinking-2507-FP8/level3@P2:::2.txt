

# Review for "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper introduces TBuckets, a method for measuring topic coherence in LDA-based topic models by grouping topic words into thematic buckets using word embeddings. The main contributions are:
1. A novel approach to topic coherence measurement based on word embedding similarity and bucket formation, which demonstrates strong empirical performance.
2. Three specific implementations of the bucketing approach: TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering), TBuckets-SVD (eigenvector-based bucket identification), and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg (reorganization of buckets for improved coherence).
3. Demonstration of practical utility in weakly supervised text classification, where selecting high-quality topics using TBuckets leads to improved classification performance.

## Strengths
1. **Strong empirical performance without parameter tuning**: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art (Röder et al., 2015) on two of three datasets (NYT and 20NG) while requiring no parameter tuning. This is particularly valuable as most previous approaches require careful parameter optimization, making the method more practical for real-world applications.

2. **Practical application to text classification**: The paper demonstrates the utility of TBuckets in weakly supervised text classification by showing that selecting high-quality topics identified through TBuckets leads to significantly better classification performance (e.g., 96.19% vs 95.95% for POLITICS vs SCIENCE). This practical validation strengthens the paper's contribution beyond theoretical coherence measurement.

3. **Parameter-free SVD-based approaches**: The TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg techniques are completely parameter-free, which is a notable advantage over previous methods. The paper correctly emphasizes this as a significant contribution, as parameter tuning is often a major hurdle in applying topic coherence measures.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper provides no clear theoretical rationale for why word embedding similarity should correlate with human-annotated topic coherence. There's a fundamental mismatch between document-level topic contexts (where topics are defined) and the sequential contexts used to train word embeddings. The authors should address this critical gap.

2. **Insufficient statistical validation**: The paper reports performance improvements without statistical significance testing (Table 1). For example, the 0.031 increase in Pearson's r for NYT with TBuckets-SVD-Reorg compared to the previous best should be tested for statistical significance to determine if it's meaningful.

3. **Poorly structured methodology section**: Section 3 is difficult to follow due to passive phrasing and lack of clear design choices. The paper would benefit from restructuring with one subsection per method, clearly explaining the rationale and implementation details for each technique.

4. **Lack of embedding sensitivity analysis**: The paper uses GloVe embeddings exclusively without addressing how results might vary with different embedding models (e.g., word2vec vs GloVe). This omission is significant as embedding quality directly impacts the method's performance.

## Questions to Authors
1. Please provide statistical significance tests for the performance improvements reported in Table 1 to validate whether the observed differences are meaningful.

2. How sensitive are your results to the choice of word embeddings? Please compare results using different embedding models (e.g., GloVe vs word2vec) to assess the robustness of your approach.

3. Can you provide a clearer theoretical justification for why word embeddings trained on sequential text should correlate with human-annotated topic coherence, given the mismatch between document-level topic contexts and the sequential contexts used to train embeddings?
