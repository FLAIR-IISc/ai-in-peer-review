

# Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary
The paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic coherence in LDA-based topic models by grouping topic words into thematic clusters ("buckets") based on semantic similarity (using cosine similarity of word embeddings). The authors introduce three methods for bucket formation: a simple greedy word-by-word assignment, an eigenvector-based method using SVD, and a reorganization method that improves upon the SVD-based approach. The approach is evaluated on three standard datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) and a weakly supervised text classification task, showing competitive or superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

## Main Contributions
1. A simple yet effective method for topic coherence measurement using word embeddings and bucket formation that outperforms state-of-the-art on two of three standard datasets.
2. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques, eliminating the need for extensive hyperparameter tuning common in existing methods.
3. Practical demonstration of the approach's utility through improved weakly supervised text classification performance when using coherence scores to select high-quality topics for human annotation.

## Strengths
1. The core idea of topic coherence measurement through semantic bucketing is intuitive and well-motivated. The examples in Section 3 effectively illustrate how bucket formation reflects topic coherence, making the approach accessible to readers. The parameter-free nature of TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is particularly valuable compared to the state-of-the-art which requires multiple parameter tunings.

2. The application to weakly supervised text classification is well-executed and demonstrates clear practical value. Table 3 shows significant improvements (e.g., 0.5% to 5% absolute gain in classification accuracy) when using coherence scores to select high-quality topics for human annotation, providing compelling evidence of the approach's utility beyond just topic evaluation.

3. The paper is generally well-structured and clearly written, with a logical progression from problem statement to methodology to evaluation. The experimental setup is standard and appropriate for the task, and the comparison with Röder et al. (2015) is thorough.

## Weaknesses
1. The theoretical justification for using word embeddings to measure topic coherence is weak. The paper fails to address the fundamental mismatch between the context used to train word embeddings (sequential context) and the context of topics (document-level). This is a significant conceptual concern that requires stronger justification.

2. The paper lacks statistical significance tests for the results in Table 1. The reported improvements over Röder et al. (2015) are not accompanied by significance tests, making it difficult to determine whether the differences are statistically meaningful or could be due to chance.

3. Section 3 is poorly written and difficult to follow. The passive phrasing obscures the design choices, and the presentation of the three methods is not clearly separated. The description of the reorganization step in 3.1 is particularly convoluted with multiple technical terms without clear explanation.

4. The paper does not address sensitivity to the choice of word embeddings (e.g., GloVe vs. word2vec). Given that the approach relies entirely on word embeddings, this is a critical gap that should be addressed.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does the choice of word embedding model (e.g., GloVe vs. word2vec) affect your results, and have you conducted experiments to compare different embedding models?

2. Can you provide statistical significance tests for the results in Table 1 to confirm that the improvements over Röder et al. (2015) are statistically significant?

3. Could you clarify why the bucket formation process using word embeddings is appropriate for evaluating topic coherence, given that word embeddings are trained on sequential context while topics are document-level constructs?

4. Would you be able to provide a clearer exposition of the three methods, perhaps with one subsection per method, to improve the readability of Section 3?

Overall, the paper presents a simple, effective approach to topic coherence measurement with clear practical utility. While the core idea is strong, the lack of theoretical justification for the embedding approach and the absence of significance testing for the main results are significant weaknesses that should be addressed. With these improvements, the paper would make a valuable contribution to the field.
