- Summary: Proposes TBuckets to measure topic coherence by clustering topic words into semantic "buckets" using word-embedding cosine similarity, with three bucket-formation methods and evaluation on three datasets plus a weakly supervised classification task.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a simple, effective bucket-based coherence metric that outperforms prior methods on two of three datasets.

- Contribution 2: Presents parameter-free SVD-based TBuckets variants, reducing hyperparameter tuning.

- Contribution 3: Demonstrates practical utility by improving weakly supervised text classification via topic selection for annotation.

- Strength  1: Intuitive, well-motivated idea with clear illustrative examples and accessible presentation of bucket-based coherence.

- Strength  2: Strong empirical application to weakly supervised classification with notable accuracy gains when using coherence for topic selection.

- Strength  3: Well-structured paper and appropriate experimental setup with thorough comparison to prior work.

- Weakness 1: Weak theoretical justification for using sequential-context-trained word embeddings to assess document-level topic coherence.

- Weakness 2: Missing statistical significance tests for the reported improvements in Table 1.

- Weakness 3: Section 3 is poorly written and confusing; the three methods and the reorganization step lack clear, separate exposition.

- Weakness 4: No analysis of sensitivity to different embedding choices (e.g., GloVe vs. word2vec), despite reliance on embeddings.

- Question 1: How do different word embedding models (GloVe vs. word2vec) affect results, and were comparisons run?

- Question 2: Can the authors provide statistical significance tests for Table 1 results?

- Question 3: Why is embedding-based bucket formation appropriate for document-level topic coherence given embeddings’ sequential training context?

- Question 4: Can Section 3 be clarified with one subsection per method to improve readability?

- Overall: Solid, practical approach with strong empirical signals but needs stronger theoretical justification, significance testing, clarity in method presentation, and embedding-sensitivity analysis.
