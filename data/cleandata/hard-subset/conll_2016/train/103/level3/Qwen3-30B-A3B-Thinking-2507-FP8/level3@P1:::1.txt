

This paper proposes TBuckets, a method for measuring topic quality in LDA models by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The main contributions as I see them are: (1) a novel intuition-driven approach to topic quality evaluation based on bucketing topic words by semantic similarity, (2) three specific implementations of this intuition (clustering-based, SVD-based, and SVD with reorganization), and (3) an application demonstrating improved weakly supervised text classification when using TBuckets to select high-quality topics.

Strengths: The most compelling strength is the practical application to weakly supervised text classification, where TBuckets significantly improves classification accuracy (e.g., 96.19% vs. 95.95% for POLITICS vs SCIENCE) by enabling selection of high-quality topics for human annotation. This demonstrates real-world utility beyond just topic evaluation. Second, the intuitive bucketing approach aligns well with human topic assessment, making the method conceptually clear. Third, the SVD-based techniques (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) offer parameter-free topic quality measurement, which is a practical advantage over state-of-the-art methods requiring multiple parameter tunings.

Weaknesses: The most significant weakness is inconsistent performance across datasets. While TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms the state-of-the-art on NYT and 20NG, it performs notably worse on the Genomics dataset (0.68 vs. 0.773), with the SVD techniques scoring 0.698, 0.694, and 0.691 compared to the best reported 0.773. This inconsistency raises serious practical concerns about the method's reliability across different domains. Second, the claim that "TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg require absolutely no parameter tuning" is misleading, as the method relies on pretrained GloVe word embeddings which contain thousands of parameters and are trained on specific corpora. Third, the paper lacks any analysis of out-of-vocabulary (OOV) terms, which are particularly prevalent in the Genomics dataset (e.g., technical terms like "gene" or "chromosome" might not be in GloVe's vocabulary), and this omission directly explains the poor performance on Genomics. Fourth, the paper overlooks relevant work in lexical chaining literature, which also groups words into thematic clusters based on semantic relationships. Fifth, the description of word embeddings is incorrect: it claims vector length is unimportant but fails to address that cosine similarity requires normalized vectors, and the paper doesn't clarify whether GloVe vectors were normalized before use.

Questions to Authors: 
1. The paper claims the SVD-based methods require "no parameter tuning," but the GloVe embeddings themselves contain thousands of parameters. How would you respond to the criticism that the method is not truly parameter-free?
2. Given the significant performance drop on Genomics (0.68 vs. 0.773), what specific factors in the Genomics domain (e.g., OOV terms, domain-specific vocabulary) caused this poor performance, and how could the method be adapted to handle such domains?
3. How would you determine which TBuckets technique (clustering, SVD, or SVD-Reorg) would work best for a new dataset without having to test all three? Is there a way to predict which method would perform well based on dataset characteristics?
4. The paper states that "word embeddings of a large set of words, trained on various corpora, are now available publicly," but it doesn't specify how they handled OOV terms. Were OOV terms simply ignored, and if so, how did this affect the bucketing process and evaluation results?

Additional Comments: The description of word embeddings in Section 2.1 is oversimplified and technically incorrect; the paper should clarify whether vectors were normalized for cosine similarity calculations. The graphs in Figure 1 are too small to be readable, which hinders interpretation of the parameter sensitivity analysis for TBuckets-Clustering. The paper would benefit from a discussion of how the method would handle datasets with high OOV rates, which is particularly relevant for specialized domains like Genomics.
