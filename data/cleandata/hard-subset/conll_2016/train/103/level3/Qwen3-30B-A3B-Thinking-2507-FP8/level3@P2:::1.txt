

# Review for "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic quality by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The authors argue that a single large bucket indicates high topic coherence. The approach leverages word embeddings and three techniques for bucket formation: clustering-based, SVD-based, and SVD with reorganization. The main contributions are:

1. A novel topic quality evaluation framework that groups words into semantic buckets, conceptually aligning with human assessment of coherence.
2. Demonstrated superior performance on two out of three standard datasets (NYT and 20NG) compared to state-of-the-art techniques, with SVD-based methods being completely parameter-free.
3. Practical application for weakly supervised text classification, showing improved performance when using topics filtered by TBuckets.

## Strengths
1. The core intuition behind TBuckets (that coherent topics form single large buckets) is well-motivated and aligns with human assessment of coherence. The examples provided effectively illustrate how bucket formation correlates with coherence.

2. The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based techniques is a significant advantage over state-of-the-art methods that require extensive parameter tuning. This makes the approach more practical for real-world applications where parameter optimization can be challenging.

3. The application to weakly supervised text classification demonstrates clear practical utility. The results show consistent improvement in classification accuracy (3 out of 4 datasets) when using TBuckets to filter high-quality topics before human annotation, which is a compelling real-world use case.

## Weaknesses
1. The inconsistent performance across datasets is a major concern. While TBuckets-SVD-Reorg outperforms state-of-the-art on NYT and 20NG, it performs notably worse than competitors on the Genomics dataset (0.68 vs. 0.773). The paper fails to analyze this inconsistency, particularly given Genomics' high proportion of domain-specific terms that are likely OOV for standard GloVe embeddings. Without addressing this, the method's practical utility for arbitrary datasets is questionable.

2. The claim that the method is "completely parameter-free" is misleading. The approach relies on pretrained word embeddings (GloVe), which carry many implicit parameters. The paper does not acknowledge this limitation, creating an inaccurate impression of the method's simplicity.

3. The paper overlooks relevant related work in lexical chaining literature (e.g., Hearst, 1992; Radev et al.), which explores similar approaches to grouping words by semantic similarity. This omission weakens the novelty claim and leaves the contribution's relationship to existing work unclear.

4. The treatment of OOV terms is unclear and unanalyzed, which is critical for the Genomics dataset. The paper does not explain how OOV terms are handled (ignored? replaced?), nor does it provide data on the proportion of OOV terms in each dataset or their impact on results.

5. The description of word embeddings is incorrect. The paper assumes vector length is unimportant, when in fact cosine similarity requires normalized vectors. The authors fail to clarify whether vectors are normalized before computing similarities, which is a fundamental aspect of the method.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims SVD-based techniques are "completely parameter free." Given that the approach relies on pretrained word embeddings (which have many implicit parameters), could you clarify how this claim holds and acknowledge the implicit parameters in your response?

2. For the Genomics dataset, which contains many domain-specific terms likely to be OOV for GloVe embeddings, how are OOV terms handled? What is the proportion of OOV terms in this dataset, and how does this affect the results?

3. The paper overlooks related work in lexical chaining. Could you clarify how your approach differs from or relates to this literature, and explain why this work was not cited in your related work section?
