This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for measuring topic quality in Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) models. The core idea is to group topic words into “buckets” representing coherent themes; a single large bucket indicates high coherence. The authors explore three techniques for bucket creation: clustering, SVD, and SVD with reorganization, and demonstrate improved performance compared to state-of-the-art methods on three public datasets, further showcasing its utility in weakly supervised text classification.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Topic Coherence Measure:** The TBuckets approach presents a new way to assess topic coherence by focusing on the thematic grouping of words within a topic, rather than relying on traditional statistical measures like PMI. This offers a different perspective on evaluating topic quality.
2. **Parameter-Free SVD Techniques:**  The SVD-based methods (TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) are highlighted as being completely parameter-free, which is a significant advantage over existing methods that often require substantial parameter tuning.
3. **Demonstration of Practical Utility:**  The authors successfully demonstrate the usefulness of TBuckets beyond just a coherence metric by applying it to a weakly supervised text classification task, indicating its potential in downstream applications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of topic quality assessment and the limitations of existing methods. The motivation for exploring a new approach is well-defined.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup is well-designed, employing three publicly available datasets and comparing the proposed methods against a strong baseline (Roder et al., 2015). The use of Pearson correlation to assess the alignment with human judgments is appropriate.
3. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results demonstrate that TBuckets-SVD-Reorg consistently outperforms the state-of-the-art on two of the three datasets, and performs competitively on the remaining one. This provides strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
4. **Practical Application:**  The successful application of TBuckets to weakly supervised text classification adds practical relevance and demonstrates the potential impact of the research.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the methodology and understand the results. The inclusion of an example illustrating the bucket creation process is particularly helpful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of word embeddings, SVD, and bucket creation is novel, the individual components (word embeddings, SVD) are well-established techniques. The core innovation lies in *how* these are combined, and the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the novelty relative to existing approaches that also leverage these techniques.
2. **Reorganization Step Complexity:** The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg technique, while achieving the best performance, introduces a dynamic threshold for reorganization that seems complex and relies on intermediate calculations (AWPS, AWES).  The justification for this specific threshold calculation isn't fully convincing, and a more detailed analysis of its sensitivity and impact on results would be valuable. A simpler, potentially more robust alternative should be considered.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper shows improved performance, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Investigating *why* TBuckets succeeds or fails in certain cases could provide valuable insights and guide future improvements. Specifically, analyzing the types of topics for which TBuckets performs well or poorly would be beneficial.
4. **Dataset Specificity:** The evaluation relies on datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) that may not be representative of all topic modeling applications. Exploring the performance of TBuckets on a more diverse set of datasets would strengthen the generalizability of the findings.
5. **Discussion of Parameter Sensitivity (Clustering):** While the authors mention that SVD methods are parameter free, the clustering based approach requires tuning of the “Max Dist” parameter. The impact of this parameter is briefly discussed in Figure 1, but a more comprehensive analysis of its influence on performance across different datasets would be useful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the novelty of TBuckets compared to existing approaches that also combine word embeddings and dimensionality reduction techniques (like SVD) for topic analysis?
2.  The dynamic threshold in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg seems complex. What is the rationale behind using AWPS and AWES in this specific way? Have you explored simpler thresholding strategies?
3.  Can you provide examples of topics where TBuckets performs poorly and discuss potential reasons for these failures?
4.  Have you considered evaluating TBuckets on additional datasets representing different domains or types of text?
5.  Regarding the clustering-based approach, can you provide a more detailed analysis of how the “Max Dist” parameter impacts performance on the datasets used, including best practices for setting this parameter?



**Additional Comments:**

*   In the introduction, the authors mention several variations of topic models. It might be useful to briefly discuss how TBuckets could potentially be applied to evaluate the quality of these different models.
*   The references are generally well-cited, but a few could benefit from more context or explanation regarding their relevance to the paper.
*   Minor: Check for consistency in terminology (e.g., "coherence score" vs. "coherence").
