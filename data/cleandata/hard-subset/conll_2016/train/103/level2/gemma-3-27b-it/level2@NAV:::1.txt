## Review of "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for evaluating the quality of topics learned via Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core idea is to group topic words into “buckets” representing coherent themes, with fewer, larger buckets indicating higher quality.  Three techniques are explored for bucket creation: clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization. The authors claim to outperform state-of-the-art methods on three public datasets and demonstrate usefulness in a weakly supervised text classification task.

The most significant contribution of this work is the TBuckets approach itself—a conceptually intuitive way to assess topic coherence by focusing on the thematic grouping of words. The SVD-based methods, particularly with reorganization, are a strong point, demonstrating competitive performance *without* requiring parameter tuning, a notable advantage over existing approaches. Further, the application to weakly supervised text classification provides a practical demonstration of the method’s utility.

A key strength lies in the thorough experimental evaluation.  The use of established datasets and comparison to a recent state-of-the-art method (Roder et al., 2015) provides a strong basis for comparison.  The demonstration of improved classification performance is also compelling. The detailed explanation of the TBuckets techniques, including the rationale for the reorganization step, is well presented. 

However, the paper suffers from a few weaknesses. The motivation behind using word embeddings specifically isn’t deeply explored. While standard, a discussion of alternative semantic representations would strengthen the work. Furthermore, the explanation of the reorganization step, while detailed, feels somewhat *ad hoc*. The dynamic threshold calculation, while attempting to address the issue of disparate scales, lacks a more rigorous theoretical justification. Finally, the improvements in text classification accuracy are modest, and a more comprehensive benchmark against other weakly supervised methods would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why word embeddings were chosen over other semantic representations (e.g., WordNet-based measures) and discuss potential limitations introduced by using embeddings?
2.  The dynamic threshold for reorganization seems empirically motivated. Can you provide a more theoretical justification or sensitivity analysis for this approach?
3.  Regarding the text classification experiment, how does the performance compare to other, non-topic-model based weakly supervised classification methods?
