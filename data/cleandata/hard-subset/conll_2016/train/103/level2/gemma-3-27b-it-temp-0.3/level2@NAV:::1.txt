## Review for "Measuring Topic Quality using Word Buckets"

This paper proposes a novel approach, TBuckets, for measuring the quality of Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topics. The core idea is to group topic words into “buckets” based on semantic similarity using word embeddings, with the assumption that coherent topics will result in a single large bucket. The authors explore three techniques for bucket creation: clustering-based, SVD, and SVD with reorganization. They demonstrate that TBuckets outperforms state-of-the-art methods on three public datasets and showcases its utility in weakly supervised text classification.

The primary contribution of this work is the TBuckets approach itself, offering a new perspective on topic coherence assessment. The SVD-based techniques, particularly with reorganization, are a strong secondary contribution, as they achieve competitive performance *without* parameter tuning, a notable advantage. Finally, the demonstration of TBuckets’ usefulness in improving weakly supervised text classification provides practical validation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** TBuckets presents a conceptually fresh approach to topic coherence, moving beyond traditional measures like PMI and leveraging word embeddings for semantic grouping.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates statistically significant improvements over existing methods on multiple datasets, lending credibility to the proposed approach.
3. **Parameter-Free Variants:** The SVD-based techniques are a significant strength, offering a practical advantage by eliminating the need for parameter optimization.
4. **Practical Application:** The successful application to weakly supervised text classification highlights the real-world utility of TBuckets.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the TBuckets methodology, including the rationale behind the reorganization step.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** While the intuition behind TBuckets is reasonable, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical grounding for why bucket formation should correlate with human judgment of topic coherence.
2. **Sensitivity to Word Embeddings:** The performance of TBuckets is inherently tied to the quality of the underlying word embeddings. The paper doesn’t explore the impact of different embedding models.
3. **Reorganization Step Complexity:** The reorganization step, while effective, introduces additional complexity and relies on somewhat arbitrary thresholds (AWES - AWPS). A more systematic exploration of this step would be beneficial.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of *why* TBuckets succeeds or fails in specific cases. Examining examples where the method performs poorly could provide valuable insights.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with different pre-trained embeddings or fine-tuned embeddings on the specific datasets?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the rationale behind the AWES - AWPS threshold in the reorganization step? Is there a theoretical basis for this choice, or is it purely empirical?
3.  Can you provide examples of topics where TBuckets performs poorly and discuss the potential reasons for the failure?
