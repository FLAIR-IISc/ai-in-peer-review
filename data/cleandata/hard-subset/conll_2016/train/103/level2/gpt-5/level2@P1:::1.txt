Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using word embeddings. The central intuition is that a coherent topic yields a dominant, large bucket. Three concrete variants are presented: (i) TBuckets-Clustering (agglomerative clustering with a distance threshold), (ii) TBuckets-SVD (assigning words to orthogonal directions obtained from SVD of the word-embedding matrix), and (iii) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, which adds a data-driven reorganization step moving words into or out of the largest bucket based on native-bucket vs largest-bucket pull. A topic-level coherence score is computed from properties of the largest bucket (size, LDA word-order weighting, and eigenvector similarity, optionally weighted by the top singular value). Evaluation on 20NG, NYT, and Genomics reports Pearson correlations with human judgments; SVD-Reorg outperforms the best settings from Röder et al. (2015) on 2/3 datasets. As an application, selecting topic runs with higher TBuckets scores improves weakly supervised text classification in most tested 20NG binary subsets.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A new embedding-based topic coherence family operationalizing the “single large bucket” intuition, with a parameter-free SVD-based variant and a novel reorganization mechanism.
2) An empirical evaluation showing competitive or superior correlation with human judgments on standard datasets, with favorable comparisons to Röder et al. (2015).
3) A practical use case demonstrating that selecting high-coherence topic sets can improve downstream weakly supervised classification.

Strengths
- Clear and intuitive coherence formulation grounded in human inspection behavior. The paper translates a natural “group similar words while reading down the list” heuristic into computable procedures, which is appealing and easy to reason about.
- Competitive empirical performance with minimal tuning. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves higher Pearson r than Röder et al. on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859), while being largely parameter-free. This is a strong practical advantage compared to coherence families that require searching over multiple configuration dimensions.
- Sensible use of word embeddings. Representing topic words in a distributional space removes the dependence on corpus co-occurrence statistics used by many coherence measures, enabling portability across domains (subject to embedding coverage).
- Reorganization step addresses a real failure mode. The diagnostic examples (e.g., television topic where words like “news” and “fox” are pulled into the main bucket) are convincing, and the push/pull criteria (NBPW/NBPE vs LBPW/OBPW) provide a clear mechanism.
- Demonstrated downstream value. Selecting topic sets by TBuckets coherence improves or maintains macro-F1 in 3/4 binary tasks, and the large gap between highest vs lowest coherence sets (e.g., POLITICS vs SCIENCE: 96.19 vs 91.09) underscores utility for topic-labelling workflows.

Weaknesses
- Statistical significance and robustness of correlations are not assessed. The reported gains over Röder et al. are modest (e.g., +0.01 on 20NG), and with only 100 topics per dataset, it is important to report confidence intervals or significance tests for Pearson r. Without them, it is unclear whether improvements are reliable.
- Sensitivity to design choices is underexplored. Key choices—number of top words N (fixed at 10), embedding model/domain (GloVe Wiki+Gigaword), and similarity metric—may materially affect bucket formation. An ablation across N and across embeddings (e.g., word2vec, fastText, domain-specific embeddings for Genomics) would clarify generality and may explain the Genomics gap.
- The SVD bucket construction is somewhat ad hoc. Using the first n eigenvectors of ATA as bucket identifiers deserves more justification: why n (topic size) rather than intrinsic dimensionality; how often are multiple directions redundant; what is the computational cost per topic; how are ties handled. An analysis of bucket counts and sizes across topics would help.
- The reorganization threshold formula is heuristic and not well motivated. The threshold AWES − (AWPS/AWES) is unusual dimensionally and intuitively; alternatives (e.g., a margin on cosine similarities, percentile-based thresholds) and a sensitivity check would strengthen the case. A small ablation isolating the reorg’s contribution (beyond the per-metric table) would be useful.
- Evaluation scope and fairness details are limited. Röder et al.’s best settings can depend on external corpora and parameters; please clarify whether their numbers were reproduced or copied from the paper, and ensure comparable resources. Also, Spearman correlation (rank-based) is commonly reported for coherence; adding it would provide a fuller picture.

Questions to authors
- Significance: Are the improvements over Röder et al. statistically significant? Please report CIs or permutation tests for Pearson r across datasets.
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to N (top words per topic)? Does performance improve with N=15 or 20, and how does the largest-bucket heuristic scale?
- Embeddings: How are OOV topic words handled? Did you try domain-specific embeddings for Genomics, and if so, how did correlations change?
- SVD details: Why choose the first n eigenvectors as bucket IDs? Did you consider selecting k by explained variance or via clustering in V-space? What is the runtime per topic?
- Reorganization: Could you provide an ablation quantifying the incremental gain from reorg over SVD across datasets and metrics, and a brief sensitivity analysis of the dynamic threshold?
- Scoring: What is the rationale for weighting by the square of the top singular value? Did you compare linear vs quadratic weighting or no weighting?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Consider reporting Spearman correlations in addition to Pearson to assess rank consistency with human judgments.
- Provide error analysis where TBuckets underperforms, particularly on the Genomics dataset, to guide future domain-specific similarity measures.
- Clarify the clustering distance threshold selection (for TBuckets-Clustering) and report its stability across datasets; if it matches or exceeds Röder et al. on Genomics (0.774 vs 0.773), this is noteworthy but depends on threshold tuning.
- For the classification application, please include variance across runs and significance tests; the gains are small in some cases and reversed for MEDICAL vs SPACE.
- Releasing code and scripts (or at least pseudocode for TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) would substantially aid reproducibility and adoption.

Overall, this is a thoughtful, well-motivated approach with promising empirical results and practical relevance. Addressing the points above—especially significance, sensitivity, and clearer justification of SVD/Reorg design choices—would strengthen the contribution.
