Summary and contributions
This paper proposes TBuckets, a family of topic coherence measures that group the top-N words of a topic into “buckets” using word embeddings. The central hypothesis is that topics with one large bucket (single theme) are more coherent. Three instantiations are presented: (i) agglomerative clustering with a distance threshold (TBuckets-Clustering), (ii) SVD-based bucketing using principal directions of the word-embedding matrix (TBuckets-SVD), and (iii) SVD plus a parameter-free reorganization step that moves words into/from the largest bucket using a dynamic threshold (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). Coherence scoring uses properties of the largest bucket (size, LDA word-order weights, eigenvector similarity) optionally weighted by the top singular value. The methods are evaluated by Pearson correlation with human coherence judgements on three datasets (NYT, 20NG, Genomics), and a downstream weakly supervised text classification setting where runs with higher coherence are prioritized for annotation.

As I see it, the key contributions are:
1) A bucket-based coherence framework that leverages word embeddings, including a novel SVD-based bucketing and a parameter-free reorganization heuristic.
2) Empirical evaluation showing competitive or improved correlation with human judgements on 2 of 3 datasets compared to Röder et al. (2015), with an analysis of parameter sensitivity for clustering.
3) An application showing that selecting topic models by TBuckets coherence can improve weakly supervised classification in several 20NG subsets.

Strengths
- Clear, human-aligned intuition and simple scoring: Focusing on how many top words fall into a dominant semantic cluster is easy to interpret and aligns with human inspection workflows. Examples illustrate the intuition well.
- Competitive empirical results with minimal tuning: TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves higher Pearson r than the best configurations in Röder et al. (2015) on NYT (0.837 vs 0.806) and 20NG (0.869 vs 0.859). The SVD variants are “parameter-free,” which is valuable given the extensive tuning often required by PMI/NPMI-based measures.
- Practical downstream utility: The text classification experiment demonstrates that selecting the most coherent runs for annotation generally improves macro-F1 (3/4 datasets) and can avoid notably bad runs (e.g., POLITICS vs SCIENCE: 96.19 vs 91.09 for highest vs lowest coherence sets).
- Reasonable coverage of related work and positioning: The paper situates TBuckets among PMI/NPMI and distributional measures, referencing Aletras & Stevenson (2013) and Röder et al. (2015), helping readers understand how TBuckets differs.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior distributional approaches: Using embeddings to assess semantic relatedness of topic words is established; TBuckets’ primary novelty is operationalizing coherence via “largest bucket size.” This is a reasonable heuristic but conceptually close to existing clustering/graph-connectivity notions. The SVD-based bucketing (assign words to principal directions) is unconventional but lacks theoretical justification for why eigenvectors should serve as “bucket prototypes.”
- Insufficient methodological clarity and ablation: The reorganization threshold formula is ambiguously written (AWES − AWPSAWES) and not analyzed. It is unclear how sensitive results are to the top singular value weighting; no ablation is provided to show the contribution of size vs WL vs EVS vs σ1^2 weighting. The choice to use the first n eigenvectors (n = number of words) as buckets lacks justification and may confound “number of buckets” with topic length rather than intrinsic sub-themes.
- Domain robustness and embedding dependence: Performance drops substantially on Genomics (0.691 vs 0.773 SOTA for SVD-Reorg). Given reliance on general GloVe, this suggests strong domain-dependence. No experiments with domain-specific embeddings, OOV handling, or multiple embedding sources are provided, which limits the generality of the approach.
- Evaluation breadth and statistical analysis: Only Pearson correlation is reported; Spearman rank correlation (often used for comparing coherence rankings) and significance testing are absent. The comparison is to the single best configuration reported by Röder et al., but no direct reimplementation or paired tests are shown. Variance or confidence intervals for TBuckets (e.g., across bootstrap samples of topics) are not reported.
- Mixed downstream gains and small effects: In the classification application, gains are small or inconsistent (e.g., POLITICS vs RELIGION: best-5 coherence set performs slightly worse than S1; MEDICAL vs SPACE “lowest-5” outperforms “highest-5”). This undercuts the claim that TBuckets consistently boosts downstream utility.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the reorganization threshold formula (parentheses and exact computation). What are the empirical ranges of AWPS and AWES? Can the threshold become negative? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Why should principal directions (columns of V) serve as bucket prototypes? Did you try simpler, parameter-free alternatives (e.g., spherical k-means with k chosen by maximizing silhouette, or community detection on a word-similarity graph)?
- What is the impact of removing the σ1^2 weighting in scoring? Please provide ablations for size vs WL vs EVS and with/without σ1^2.
- How do you handle OOV topic words w.r.t. GloVe? What fraction of words are OOV per dataset?
- Did you test with multiple embedding sources (e.g., word2vec, fastText) or domain-specific embeddings for Genomics? If so, how do results change?
- Why report only Pearson r? Please add Spearman ρ and statistical significance (e.g., Williams test for dependent correlations).

Additional comments and suggestions
- Provide implementation details (cosine similarity normalization, preprocessing, tie-breaking in assignments), and release code to ensure reproducibility.
- Consider evaluating bucket quality intrinsically (e.g., purity/entropy w.r.t. human-assigned labels for topic words where available) and reporting the distribution over bucket sizes, not only the largest.
- The non-coherent topic example contains a likely typo (“Bucket-1: {storm, weather}”). Please proofread; several references and tables have formatting issues.
- Discuss limitations: Topics with multiple legitimate sub-themes (e.g., multi-faceted entities) may be unfairly penalized by a largest-bucket-only criterion.
- Consider extending evaluation to topic intrusion tasks or human judgments collected anew for your methods to complement correlation with existing datasets.

Overall, the paper presents a clear, interpretable idea with competitive results on two benchmarks and promising downstream utility. Strengthening methodological clarity, ablations, and robustness analyses would significantly improve the contribution.
