

Summary: This paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic quality in LDA-based topic models by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The main contributions are: 1) a coherent theme-based intuition for topic quality measurement (single large bucket = high coherence), 2) three specific techniques for creating buckets (clustering-based, SVD-based, and SVD with reorganization), and 3) demonstration that their SVD-based techniques require no parameter tuning while outperforming state-of-the-art on two of three datasets.

Strengths:
1. The TBuckets concept is intuitively compelling and well-illustrated with clear examples showing how coherent topics form single large buckets while non-coherent topics form multiple smaller buckets. This human-centered perspective provides a valuable alternative to purely statistical coherence measures.

2. TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves state-of-the-art results on two out of three datasets (NYT and 20NG), with meaningful improvements (0.837 vs. 0.806 Pearson's r on NYT). The fact that it outperforms previous methods without requiring parameter tuning is a significant practical advantage over existing approaches.

3. The paper effectively demonstrates the practical utility of TBuckets for weakly supervised text classification, showing that filtering topics by coherence score before human annotation improves classification performance by 1-5% on most datasets, with a substantial 4.86% improvement on the challenging PC vs MAC dataset.

4. The SVD-based techniques are well-motivated and properly contextualized within the literature. The reorganization step addresses a clear limitation of the basic SVD approach, and the dynamic threshold calculation (while needing more explanation) is an elegant solution to the problem.

Weaknesses:
1. The performance gains, while statistically significant, are modest (0.031 on NYT, 0.006 on 20NG), and the paper doesn't sufficiently justify why these small improvements are practically meaningful for topic modeling applications. The paper should clarify how these differences would impact real-world topic model selection.

2. The evaluation is limited to just three datasets, all drawn from the same source (Röder et al., 2015). The paper would be stronger with additional experiments on different domain collections (e.g., biomedical, legal) to demonstrate broader applicability.

3. The reorganization threshold calculation (AWES - AWPS*AWES) is not well-explained. While the paper claims it's "parameter-free," the formula's components (AWES, AWPS) require detailed explanation to understand how it's computed in practice. A concrete example would help clarify this important aspect.

4. The paper doesn't adequately compare with the most recent work beyond Röder et al. (2015). The 2015 framework spans a large configuration space, and it's unclear how TBuckets fits into this landscape or whether it could be integrated with other coherence measures.

5. The computational complexity of the methods isn't discussed. For large topic models (e.g., thousands of topics), the SVD approach could become computationally expensive compared to simpler methods like PMI-based coherence.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper claims TBuckets-SVD-Reorg requires "no parameter tuning," but the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold. Could you provide a concrete example showing how this threshold is calculated for a specific topic, and explain why this is considered parameter-free?

2. The evaluation is limited to three datasets. Would you expect similar performance improvements on topics with fewer than 10 words (e.g., from LDA runs with different hyperparameters)?

3. In Table 3, the performance difference between high and low coherence topic sets is most pronounced for PC vs MAC (68.47 vs 67.96). Could you provide a qualitative analysis of the topics that led to this difference, showing how the bucketing approach identified problematic topics?
