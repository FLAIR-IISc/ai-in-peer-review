

Summary: The paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The main contributions are: 1) a parameter-free coherence measure (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) that outperforms state-of-the-art on two datasets, 2) an intuitive human-aligned method for topic coherence assessment, and 3) demonstrated utility for weakly supervised text classification.

Strengths:
1. The parameter-free nature of TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg is a significant practical contribution. Unlike state-of-the-art methods requiring extensive hyperparameter tuning (e.g., Röder et al., 2015), these techniques operate without parameters, addressing a major pain point in topic coherence evaluation. This is clearly demonstrated in Table 1 where TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves the highest correlation on NYT (0.837) and 20NG (0.869) without any tuning.

2. The human-aligned bucketing mechanism provides compelling intuitive justification for the approach. The examples in Section 3 (e.g., weather vs. Afghanistan/Wikileaks topics) effectively illustrate how bucket distribution reflects human coherence judgment, making the method conceptually sound and interpretable.

3. The application to text classification (Section 4.3) demonstrates tangible utility beyond coherence measurement. The 1.5% accuracy improvement in the challenging PC vs MAC task (68.47% vs. 66.98%) when using high-coherence topics shows clear practical value, especially given the minimal additional computational cost.

Weaknesses:
1. The statistical significance of the results is inadequately addressed. The paper reports Pearson's r correlations (Table 1) but lacks p-values, confidence intervals, or statistical tests comparing TBuckets-SVD-Reorg against the best Röder et al. (2015) results. Without this, it's impossible to determine if the observed improvements (e.g., +0.031 on NYT) are meaningful or due to random variation, weakening the primary claim of "outperforming state-of-the-art."

2. The comparison with state-of-the-art is incomplete and potentially misleading. Röder et al. (2015) present a framework with multiple configurations, but the paper only compares against the best-reported values from their work (0.806, 0.859, 0.773), not against the specific configurations that achieved these results. The paper should have evaluated against the best-performing configurations within Röder's framework, not just the top numbers.

3. The reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) lacks rigorous justification. The paper claims reorganization addresses "words being put to another bucket due to high similarity with that eigenvector" (Section 3.1), but provides no systematic analysis of why SVD fails (e.g., no error analysis showing how often reorganization fixes misgroupings). The dynamic threshold mechanism (AWES - AWPS*AWES) is presented as parameter-free, but its derivation is unclear and potentially introduces hidden parameters dependent on topic characteristics.

Questions to Authors:
1. How does the dynamic threshold in TBuckets-SVD-Reorg avoid parameter tuning? The description suggests it's computed from topic-specific statistics (AWPS, AWES), but this approach may implicitly depend on the dataset's properties, contradicting the "parameter-free" claim.

2. Why does TBuckets-SVD-Reorg underperform on Genomics (0.68 vs. 0.773 in Röder et al.) despite outperforming on other datasets? The paper does not discuss dataset-specific challenges or potential limitations of the approach for certain topic distributions.
