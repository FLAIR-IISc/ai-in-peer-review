

Summary: The paper proposes TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring topic coherence by grouping topic words into thematic buckets. The authors present three techniques: clustering-based, SVD-based, and SVD with reorganization. TBuckets demonstrates improved performance over state-of-the-art methods on three datasets (20NG, NYT, Genomics) and shows utility for weakly supervised text classification.

Main Contributions: 1) A novel framework for topic coherence measurement based on word bucketing, 2) Parameter-free SVD-based techniques that outperform tuned state-of-the-art methods, 3) Demonstration of practical utility in improving weakly supervised text classification.

Strengths: 
1) TBuckets-SVD-Reorg achieves superior performance on 2 out of 3 datasets compared to state-of-the-art, with the significant advantage of being completely parameter-free, eliminating a major barrier to practical adoption.
2) The application to text classification demonstrates clear practical value, showing that filtering for high-coherence topics significantly improves classification performance in 3 of 4 tested scenarios.
3) The paper provides a thorough and well-structured comparison with previous methods, including detailed examples of bucket formation that effectively illustrate the core concept.

Weaknesses:
1) The paper lacks discussion of limitations, particularly regarding scalability to larger topic sets or performance on topics with fewer words. This omission weakens the practical assessment of the method's boundaries.
2) The claim that SVD-based techniques are "completely parameter-free" is misleading, as the reorganization step uses a dynamic threshold dependent on topic-specific calculations, which requires explanation.
3) The evaluation relies solely on datasets from Roder et al. (2015), without testing on additional datasets or with different topic modeling approaches, limiting the generalizability of the findings.

Questions to Authors:
1) How does the method perform on topics with fewer than 5 words, which may occur in some topic modeling applications?
2) Could the authors provide more insight into why the method performs better on some datasets (NYT, 20NG) than others (Genomics)?
