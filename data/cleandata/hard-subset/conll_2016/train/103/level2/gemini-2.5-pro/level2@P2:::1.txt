### Summary of the paper and main contributions

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models like LDA. The core idea is to group a topic's top words into thematic "buckets" based on the semantic similarity of their pre-trained word embeddings. The distribution of words across these buckets, particularly the size and composition of the largest bucket, is used to derive a coherence score, with the intuition that a highly coherent topic should yield one single, large bucket. The authors propose three methods for this bucketing process: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), with the second SVD variant including a "reorganization" step to refine the buckets.

The proposed methods are evaluated by correlating their scores with human judgments on three standard datasets. The results show that the proposed `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg` method achieves state-of-the-art performance on two of the three datasets. The paper also demonstrates the practical utility of the metric by showing that it can be used to select higher-quality sets of topics to improve performance in a weakly supervised text classification task.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and intuitive framework for topic coherence evaluation.** The "bucketing" concept provides a fresh perspective on measuring topic quality that moves beyond the standard approach of aggregating pairwise word similarities. The use of SVD to identify orthogonal sub-themes as the basis for buckets is a particularly creative and well-motivated instantiation of this idea.
2.  **A parameter-free method that achieves state-of-the-art performance.** The best-performing model, `TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`, outperforms prior work on two widely used benchmarks without requiring any hyperparameter tuning. This is a significant practical advantage over many competing methods that rely on a complex and computationally expensive search over a large configuration space.
3.  **A compelling demonstration of the metric's utility in a downstream application.** The authors show that using the TBuckets score to filter topic models for a weakly supervised classification task leads to improved accuracy. This provides strong evidence that the metric captures a meaningful and useful dimension of topic quality.

### Strengths

1.  **High degree of novelty and strong intuition.** The core idea of grouping topic words into buckets is both novel and highly intuitive, closely mimicking how a human might assess a topic's thematic consistency. The implementation via SVD, which frames sub-themes as orthogonal dimensions in the embedding space, is a principled and elegant approach. This represents a valuable conceptual contribution to the field of topic model evaluation.
2.  **Excellent empirical results.** The paper demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the NYT and 20NG datasets, which are standard benchmarks for this task. The fact that this performance is achieved by a parameter-free method (`TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`) is a major strength, making the results even more impressive and the method highly attractive for practical use.
3.  **The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based methods.** A significant weakness of many topic coherence metrics is their reliance on hyperparameters (e.g., window sizes, confirmation measures, aggregation functions) that must be tuned. The proposed SVD methods obviate this need, offering a robust, off-the-shelf solution. This substantially lowers the barrier to adoption and increases the likelihood of the method being used in practice.
4.  **Strong validation through a downstream task.** The weakly supervised text classification experiment is a highlight of the paper. It moves beyond simply correlating with human scores and shows that the metric has tangible value for improving application performance. This provides convincing evidence that the metric captures an aspect of quality that matters for real-world tasks.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of the SVD-based methodology.** The technical description of the SVD-based methods lacks some crucial details and justification, which could impede understanding and reproducibility.
    *   It is stated that the first `n` eigenvectors from the `V` matrix are used as bucket identifiers for the `n` words in a topic. The intuition for choosing exactly `n` eigenvectors is not explained. A more detailed justification for why these specific vectors correspond to "sub-themes" would strengthen the paper.
    *   The reorganization step (`TBuckets-SVD-Reorg`) relies on a complex, dynamically computed threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`). The motivation for this specific formula is not clearly explained beyond a brief, high-level statement. The introduction of such a complex heuristic, while claimed to be parameter-free, needs a more rigorous justification or an ablation study to show the contribution of its different components.
2.  **Insufficient analysis of results and limitations.** While the overall results are strong, the paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of its performance.
    *   Performance on the Genomics dataset is significantly lower than the state-of-the-art. The paper does not offer any hypothesis for this discrepancy. An analysis of whether this is due to the use of general-domain word embeddings (GloVe) or a fundamental limitation of the bucketing approach for specialized domains would be insightful.
    *   The paper lacks a qualitative error analysis. Presenting examples where the TBuckets score differs significantly from human judgments (both false positives and false negatives) would provide valuable insight into the method's failure modes.
3.  **Unclear motivation for scoring function details.** Several scoring variations are presented (size, WL, EVS), and for the SVD methods, these are weighted by the "square of the highest singular value". The paper does not provide a clear motivation for this weighting scheme or offer guidance on which scoring function is preferable and why. A more thorough discussion of these choices is needed.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the `TBuckets-SVD` method, could you elaborate on the choice to use exactly `n` eigenvectors (where `n` is the number of topic words) as bucket identifiers? What is the intuition behind this 1-to-1 mapping, and have you experimented with using a different number of eigenvectors (e.g., determined by the magnitude of singular values)?
2.  The dynamic threshold used in the reorganization step is a critical component of your best-performing model. Could you provide a more detailed derivation or justification for this specific formula (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`)? An ablation study showing how performance changes with simpler thresholds or variations of this formula would be very helpful.
3.  Your method's correlation with human judgments on the Genomics dataset is substantially lower than on the other two datasets and also lower than the prior state-of-the-art. What is your hypothesis for this performance gap? Do you believe it is primarily due to the domain mismatch of the pre-trained GloVe embeddings, or do you suspect a limitation in the bucketing approach itself when applied to this type of data?
