### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel approach for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The central idea is that words within a coherent topic should belong to a single semantic group, or "bucket." The approach leverages pre-trained word embeddings to represent topic words and proposes three distinct methods for grouping them: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The SVD-based methods are notable for being parameter-free. The work evaluates these methods by correlating their coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on two of them. Furthermore, it shows the practical utility of the proposed metric in a weakly supervised text classification task, where selecting topic models with higher coherence leads to better classification accuracy.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  The introduction of a novel and intuitive SVD-based, parameter-free method (TBuckets-SVD and its variant, TBuckets-SVD-Reorg) for topic coherence evaluation. This method uses the eigenvectors of the topic words' embedding matrix to identify and group words into semantic sub-themes, achieving state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments.
2.  A practical demonstration of the metric's value in a downstream application, showing that it can be used to select higher-quality topic models that improve performance in weakly supervised text classification.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Conceptual Soundness:** The core idea of grouping topic words into semantic "buckets" is intuitive and well-motivated. The use of SVD on a matrix of word embeddings to identify orthogonal sub-themes within a topic is an elegant and novel method to automate this bucketing process. It provides a principled way to decompose a topic into its constituent semantic components.
2.  **Parameter-Free Approach:** A significant strength is that the main proposed methods, TBuckets-SVD and TBuckets-SVD-Reorg, are parameter-free. This contrasts favorably with many existing coherence measures which may depend on tunable parameters or statistics from external reference corpora (e.g., sliding window sizes for PMI calculation). This makes the method more robust, easier to apply, and less prone to overfitting on specific datasets.
3.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art results on the NYT and 20NG datasets, outperforming the best configurations from the comprehensive study by Roder et al. (2015). The results are directly comparable, as the evaluation uses the same datasets and metric (Pearson's correlation with human scores), providing strong evidence for the method's effectiveness.
4.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The paper goes beyond simple correlation with human scores by including a text classification experiment. This experiment effectively demonstrates that the proposed coherence metric has practical value. By using TBuckets to filter for higher-quality sets of topics, the work shows a tangible improvement in the performance of a downstream task, which strengthens the overall contribution significantly.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Clarity of the Reorganization Heuristic:** The description of the reorganization step in Section 3.1 is difficult to follow. While the motivation to correct mis-assignments from the initial SVD step is clear, the justification for the specific formula used for the dynamic threshold (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`) is not fully explained. The formula feels heuristic, and the paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of its derivation or an ablation study showing its importance compared to simpler alternatives (e.g., a fixed threshold or a different formulation). This would help readers better understand why this specific design choice was made.
2.  **Limited Analysis of Scoring Function:** The coherence score is derived entirely from the properties of the single largest bucket formed. While this aligns with the core intuition, it ignores any information contained in the distribution and composition of the smaller buckets. For instance, a topic that splits into two moderately sized, coherent buckets is likely less coherent than one with one large bucket and a few single-word outlier buckets. A discussion of how information from the smaller buckets could be incorporated, perhaps as a penalty term, would make the analysis more complete.
3.  **Inconsistent Performance on Genomics Dataset:** The SVD-based methods perform noticeably worse on the Genomics dataset, where they are outperformed by both the state-of-the-art and the paper's own simpler clustering-based method. The paper reports this result but does not offer an analysis or hypothesis for this performance gap. It is possible that the general-purpose GloVe embeddings (trained on Wikipedia/Gigaword) are not well-suited for this highly specialized domain. A discussion of this potential limitation would strengthen the paper's experimental analysis and provide valuable context for future work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The dynamic threshold used in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method is a key component for improving upon the base SVD method. Could the paper provide more intuition behind the specific formulation (`AWES - AWPS/AWES`)? Was this derived from first principles, or was it the result of empirical exploration?
2.  Regarding the results on the Genomics dataset, what is the leading hypothesis for why the SVD-based methods underperform in this domain? Is it suspected that the pre-trained word embeddings lack the domain-specific semantic knowledge required for the method to function effectively? Have any preliminary experiments with domain-specific embeddings been considered?
