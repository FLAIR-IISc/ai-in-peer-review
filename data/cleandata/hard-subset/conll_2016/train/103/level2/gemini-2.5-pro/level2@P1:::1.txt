**Summary of the paper**
This paper introduces a novel approach, TBuckets, for measuring the coherence of topics generated by topic models like LDA. The core idea is to simulate the human process of identifying themes by automatically grouping the top words of a topic into semantic "buckets". A topic is considered coherent if most of its words fall into a single large bucket. The authors propose three methods to create these buckets using pre-trained word embeddings: one based on agglomerative clustering and two based on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). The SVD-based approach treats the right-singular vectors as representations of latent sub-themes within the topic, assigning each word to its most similar sub-theme. A refinement, SVD-Reorg, is proposed to further improve the bucketing by moving words into or out of the largest bucket based on a dynamic similarity threshold. The authors evaluate their methods by correlating the generated coherence scores with human judgments on three standard datasets and demonstrate that their best method outperforms the state-of-the-art. They also show the practical utility of their metric in a weakly-supervised text classification task, where selecting more coherent topic sets for annotation leads to improved performance.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is a novel and intuitive SVD-based method (TBuckets-SVD and its SVD-Reorg variant) for measuring topic coherence. This approach moves beyond pairwise word similarity scores (common in previous work) to a more holistic method of identifying latent sub-themes within a topic. A key advantage of this method is that it is parameter-free, unlike many competing approaches.
2.  A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that the proposed coherence metric has practical value in a downstream application. By using TBuckets to filter topic models before human annotation in a weakly-supervised classification setting, the authors show an improvement in final classification accuracy, grounding the work in a real-world use case.

**Strengths**
1.  **Novelty and Strong Intuition:** The central idea of creating "buckets" of thematically related words is highly intuitive and provides a clear conceptual model for topic coherence. The application of SVD to automatically discover these sub-themes (buckets) is a novel and clever way to formalize this intuition. This represents a fresh perspective in a field where most metrics rely on aggregating pairwise PMI or cosine similarity scores.
2.  **Excellent Empirical Performance:** The proposed TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method achieves state-of-the-art results on two of the three benchmark datasets (NYT and 20NG), and the clustering-based method is competitive on the third (Genomics). This strong performance against a comprehensive benchmark from Röder et al. (2015) provides convincing evidence of the method's effectiveness.
3.  **Parameter-Free Method:** The SVD-based methods are presented as being parameter-free. This is a significant practical advantage over the state-of-the-art method from Röder et al. (2015), which involves searching through a large configuration space, and even the authors' own clustering-based method, which requires tuning a distance threshold. A robust, parameter-free metric is highly valuable for practitioners.
4.  **Demonstrated Practical Utility:** The text classification experiment in Section 4.3 is a strong point of the paper. It successfully connects the abstract goal of correlating with human judgments to a tangible benefit in a downstream task. Showing that pre-selecting topic sets based on the proposed coherence score improves classification accuracy makes a compelling case for the metric's usefulness beyond pure evaluation.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Clarity and Justification of the Reorganization Step:** The SVD-Reorg method is key to achieving the best results, but its description lacks some clarity and justification. The formula for the dynamic threshold, `AWES - AWPS/AWES`, feels somewhat ad-hoc. The paper would be stronger if it provided more intuition or empirical justification for this specific formulation over other possibilities. The claim of the method being "completely parameter free" is slightly weakened by the introduction of such a complex, unmotivated heuristic.
2.  **Insufficient Ablation of Scoring Components:** The final coherence score is derived from several components (size of the largest bucket, LDA word order weight `WL`, eigenvector similarity `EVS`) and, for SVD methods, is weighted by the "square of the highest singular value". The motivation for this specific weighting scheme is not explained. An ablation study would be very helpful to understand the contribution of each component (especially the singular value weighting) to the final performance. Without this, it is difficult to assess which parts of the proposed scoring function are most critical.
3.  **Lack of Detail on SVD-Reorg Logic:** The logic for moving words *from* the largest bucket (`OBPW > max(NBPW, NBPE)`) seems potentially problematic. It isn't clear if the dynamic threshold is also applied here. This condition could cause a word to be evicted even if its association with another bucket is only marginally stronger than its association with its native (largest) bucket. A more detailed explanation or a worked example for this "from" step would be beneficial.
4.  **Minor Gaps in Experimental Reporting:** In the text classification experiment (Table 3), the results for the MEDICAL vs SPACE dataset show that the 5 topic sets with the lowest coherence scores perform slightly better than the 5 with the highest scores. While the authors correctly note this is an "easy" dataset, this counter-intuitive result warrants more discussion as it seems to challenge the central hypothesis of the experiment. Similarly, performance on POLITICS vs RELIGION is slightly worse for the higher-coherence sets. Addressing these anomalies would strengthen the paper's conclusions.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you elaborate on the design of the dynamic threshold used in the TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method? Was this formula derived from theoretical principles, or was it one of several heuristics you experimented with?
2.  What is the motivation for weighting the SVD-based coherence scores by the square of the largest singular value? Could you provide results without this weighting to show its impact on the final correlation scores?
3.  Regarding the text classification experiment in Table 3: do you have any further insights into why the sets of topics with the lowest coherence scores outperformed the highest-coherence sets on the MEDICAL vs SPACE dataset? Does this suggest limitations on when your coherence metric is a useful predictor of downstream performance?
