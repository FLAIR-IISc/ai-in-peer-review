**Summary and Main Contributions:**
This paper introduces TBuckets, a novel framework for measuring the coherence of topics from models like Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA). The core intuition is that a coherent topic should have its constituent words group into a single large semantic "bucket". The authors use word embeddings to represent topic words and propose three techniques to create these buckets: one based on agglomerative clustering and two using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), with the final method including a data-driven reorganization step (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg).

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel bucketing methodology for evaluating topic coherence. This is an intuitive approach that mimics a human process of identifying a central theme among a set of words.
2.  The development of an effective and largely parameter-free coherence metric (TBuckets-SVD-Reorg). This method achieves state-of-the-art correlation with human judgments on two out of three standard benchmark datasets.
3.  A compelling demonstration of the metric's practical utility. The authors show that using TBuckets to select higher-quality topic sets leads to improved performance in a downstream weakly supervised text classification task.

**Strengths:**
1.  The proposed bucketing method is conceptually elegant and provides a fresh perspective on topic coherence evaluation, moving beyond the standard paradigm of aggregating pairwise word similarity scores.
2.  The empirical results are strong. The TBuckets-SVD-Reorg method shows a clear improvement over the reported state-of-the-art on the New York Times and 20 Newsgroups datasets, providing compelling evidence for its effectiveness.
3.  The parameter-free nature of the SVD-based methods is a significant practical advantage over competing coherence metrics that often require extensive parameter tuning. This makes the proposed method more robust, accessible, and easily applicable to new domains.
4.  The text classification experiment successfully validates the metric's real-world value, demonstrating that higher coherence scores as measured by TBuckets correlate with better performance on a downstream task.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The claim that the SVD-Reorg method is "completely parameter less" could be refined. The dynamic threshold for reorganization is based on a specific formula. While not manually tuned, this formula is a crucial design choice, and an ablation study or justification for this specific formulation would strengthen the paper.
2.  The performance of the proposed SVD-based methods is notably weaker on the Genomics dataset, where it is outperformed by both the baseline and the authors' clustering-based approach. The paper lacks a discussion explaining this discrepancy, which may be due to a mismatch between the general-domain word embeddings and the specialized vocabulary.
3.  The evaluation compares against a strong but dated state-of-the-art result from 2015. While the comparison is direct and fair, positioning the work against more recent advancements in topic coherence evaluation would provide a more comprehensive picture of its contributions.
