This paper proposes a method for exploring correspondences between languages based on an MDL. The authors develop a model for correspondences between words with similar meanings in several Slavic languages. They develop code for rules matching substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that balances the model's description and the data given to the model.

The model is trained with EM and tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are shown by several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of the correspondences found.

The motivation and construction of this approach are understandable. MDL seems like a reasonable tool for attacking the problem, and the motivation for employing EM is well-presented. However, I must admit that some derivations were not entirely clear to me.

The authors point to the similarity of the MDL objective to Bayesian estimation and consider the application of Bayesian estimation in (biological) phylogenetic estimation, for example, using the MrBayes tool. An empirical comparison here could be insightful.

Related Works:
- Lack of comparison methods for borrowing and identifying cognates or other computational methods for historical linguistics. For example, studies by Alexander Bouchard-Kot, Tandy Warnow, Lew Nachle, and Andrew Kitchen. Some may not have tools available to apply in the given datasets, but one can refer to List and Moran (2013). There are also relevant tools for biological phylogenetic estimation that can be applied (Pop, MrBayes, etc.).

Approach and Methodology
- Alignment process: Memory/runtime constraint seems to be a major drawback, allowing comparison of a maximum of 5 languages. It would be interesting to see if more languages would appear as long as many languages are involved, and there are phylogenetic trees. I am curious what ideas the authors have to deal with this issue.
- Phylogenetic tree: The disadvantages of adding neighbors to create a phylogenetic tree (such as manually specifying the root) are known. What about more sophisticated methods?
- Do you run EM for convergence or is there any other stopping criterion?

Data
- Two datasets are mixed, one homogeneous and one non-homogeneous (original list). Have you considered how this might affect the results?
- The data is in orthographic form, which can hide many correspondences. This is especially evident in languages with different scripts. Therefore, the learned rules may indicate script changes rather than actual linguistic correspondences. This seems like a drawback that could be avoided by working at the level of phonetic scripts.

Unclear Points
- "What is the optimal unigram for symbol uses in all rules"? (line 286)
- The merging done in the maximization step was not entirely clear to me.

Minor Issue
- "Focus in on" -> "Focus on" (line 440)
