### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a method for the unsupervised discovery of string-level correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors formulate an objective function that balances the complexity of a model (a set of correspondence rules) with the complexity of encoding the data (cognate lists) using that model. They propose an efficient, deterministic, EM-like algorithm to infer a good set of correspondence rules. The method is evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, with results presented through pairwise distance measures, a derived phylogenetic tree, and a detailed four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A principled, multi-language framework for correspondence discovery.** The primary contribution is the application of MDL to simultaneously discover correspondence rules across more than two languages. This is a significant step beyond common pairwise approaches. The framework is theoretically sound, data-driven, and avoids making strong prior assumptions about the distribution or shape of correspondence rules. The proposed "Shared Description Length" metric is a novel and intuitive way to quantify the structural similarity between any subset of the languages under analysis.
2.  **An experimental analysis demonstrating the method's utility.** The authors provide a compelling application of their method to a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results of the pairwise analysis and the generated phylogenetic tree align well with established linguistic classifications, serving as a strong proof of concept. The more detailed four-way analysis effectively showcases the unique strengths of the multi-language approach by identifying and quantifying similarities between various subgroups (e.g., CS-PL, RU-BG) within the larger context.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Unbiased Approach:** The use of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle is a key strength. It provides a formal, information-theoretic foundation for model selection that naturally balances model complexity with data fit, thereby guarding against overfitting. This approach is data-driven and does not require arbitrary assumptions about the underlying distribution or form of the correspondence rules, leading to a more objective discovery process.
2.  **Capability for Simultaneous Multi-Language Analysis:** A significant advantage of this method over many existing techniques is its ability to analyze more than two languages at once. This is not merely an extension of a pairwise method; the model is designed to find rules that span any subset of the input languages. The four-way analysis of Slavic languages (Section 5.3) and the resulting Shared Description Lengths (Figure 3) are excellent demonstrations of this power, offering a much more granular and nuanced view of linguistic relationships than a simple phylogenetic tree can provide.
3.  **Clear Motivation and Intuitive Algorithm:** The paper does a very good job of motivating the problem and explaining the high-level intuition behind the proposed solution. The reasoning for adopting an EM-like procedure—where knowing the rules helps find the best alignment, and knowing the alignment helps find the best rules—is presented clearly. The optimization strategy of starting with singleton rules and iteratively merging them based on compression gain is both sensible and easy to understand.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Comparison to Existing Work:** The primary weakness of this paper is the lack of empirical comparison to established methods in computational historical linguistics and phylogenetics. While the MDL framework is novel, the task of inferring language relationships is not. The authors could have strengthened their evaluation by comparing their NCD-based phylogenetic tree to trees generated by standard methods used in both linguistics (e.g., using toolkits like that of List and Moran, 2013) and biology (e.g., Bayesian inference methods like MrBayes, or even simpler distance-based methods like UPGMA). Without this context, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed method offers a significant advantage over the state of the art.
2.  **Significant Scalability Limitations:** The paper notes a computational bottleneck in the alignment procedure that currently limits the analysis to a maximum of five languages. For a method whose main advantage is its multi-language capability, this is a major practical drawback. The ability to generate large-scale phylogenies is a key application area for this type of research, and the current limitation severely curtails the method's potential impact and applicability to larger language families.
3.  **Reliance on Orthography over Phonetic Transcription:** The experiments are conducted on raw orthographic data. This is a potential weakness, as orthography can obscure or misrepresent true linguistic correspondences, especially when dealing with languages that use different writing systems (e.g., Latin vs. Cyrillic). The analysis risks capturing superficial script-level regularities rather than deeper phonological or morphological ones. For instance, the authors note that the Cyrillic version of Serbo-Croatian clusters slightly closer to other Cyrillic-based languages. This suggests the model is influenced by the script, which may confound the linguistic interpretation of the discovered rules and distance measures. Using phonetic transcriptions (like IPA) would provide a more standardized and linguistically meaningful basis for comparison.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The paper states a computational bottleneck limits the method to ~5 languages. Could you elaborate on the source of this complexity? Do you have plans or initial ideas for improving the efficiency of the alignment procedure to make the method scalable to a larger number of languages?
2.  Could you please clarify the merge operation in the Maximization step (Section 4.4)? The text says you merge "the two patterns which lead to the highest decrease in overall description length." How are candidate pairs of patterns for merging identified?
3.  In Section 4.1 (Encoding a Correspondence Rule), you mention using "the optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules." Could you elaborate on how this unigram distribution is calculated? Is it a global distribution over all characters in all alphabets that appear in the rule table Π?
4.  What is the stopping criterion for the EM training procedure? Do you run for a fixed number of iterations, or until the total description length converges?
