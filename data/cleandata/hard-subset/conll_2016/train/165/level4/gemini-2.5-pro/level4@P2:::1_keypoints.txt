- Presents an MDL-based unsupervised method to discover string-level correspondences across multiple languages.  
- Balances model complexity (correspondence rules) and data encoding (cognate lists) in an objective function.  
- Proposes an efficient, deterministic EM-like algorithm to infer correspondence rules.  
- Evaluated on 13 Slavic languages with pairwise distances, a phylogenetic tree, and a detailed Czech–Polish–Russian–Bulgarian four-way analysis.  
- Contribution: principled multi-language framework applying MDL to discover correspondences beyond pairwise methods.  
- Contribution: introduces Shared Description Length as a metric for structural similarity among language subsets.  
- Contribution: experimental results align with established linguistic classifications, demonstrating method utility.  
- Strength: MDL provides a formal, unbiased, data-driven foundation that mitigates overfitting.  
- Strength: method can analyze more than two languages simultaneously and find rules spanning any subset.  
- Strength: clear motivation and intuitive EM-like algorithm with a sensible merge-by-compression strategy.  
- Weakness: lacks empirical comparison to established computational-historical and phylogenetic methods.  
- Weakness: scalability limited by an alignment bottleneck, currently capping analysis at ~5 languages.  
- Weakness: experiments use orthography rather than phonetic transcription, risking script-driven artifacts.  
- Questions: clarify source of the computational bottleneck and plans for scalability improvements.  
- Questions: explain how candidate pattern pairs for merging are identified in the M-step.  
- Questions: detail how the “optimal unigram” for symbol usages is computed and whether it’s global.  
- Questions: state the stopping criterion used for the EM training procedure (fixed iterations vs. convergence).
