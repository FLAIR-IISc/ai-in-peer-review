- Summary: Proposes an MDL-based EM algorithm to automatically discover string-level correspondences across multiple languages; evaluated on 13 Slavic languages with pairwise distances, a phylogenetic tree, qualitative rules, and a data over-weighting mechanism.

- Contribution: Introduces a principled, non-parametric MDL framework that can infer correspondences involving any subset of languages.

- Contribution: Proposes the Shared Description Length (sDL) metric to quantify multi-way linguistic similarity beyond pairwise measures.

- Strength: Strong theoretical foundation and clear motivation—MDL is well-justified and the EM training derivation is clear.

- Strength: Compelling quantitative and qualitative results—NCD-based tree matches known Slavic groups and discovered rules capture orthographic, morphological, and phonological phenomena.

- Strength: Novel multi-language analysis—sDL reveals nuanced subgroup and subset shared structures not visible in pairwise analyses.

- Weakness: Insufficient engagement with related work in computational historical linguistics and phylogenetics (e.g., Bayesian methods, List & Moran) and lack of comparative discussion.

- Weakness: Major scalability limitations—memory/runtime bottleneck restricts current implementation to at most five languages, undermining multi-language claims.

- Weakness: Reliance on orthography instead of phonetic transcription conflates script differences with linguistic correspondence and is under-discussed.

- Weakness: Missing methodological detail—EM stopping criterion is not specified.

- Weakness: Missing methodological detail—the Maximization-step “merging” procedure is described only at a high level; generation and evaluation of candidate merges are unclear.

- Weakness: No justification for using neighbor-joining for tree construction when more sophisticated, standard phylogenetic methods exist.

- Question: What concrete plans or ideas do you have to improve scalability of the alignment procedure for larger N?

- Question: How does your approach conceptually and empirically compare to established phylogenetic methods like Bayesian inference (e.g., MrBayes)?

- Question: Have you considered applying the method to phonetically transcribed data (e.g., IPA), and how would you expect results to differ from orthography-based findings?

- Question: Can you provide a detailed, concrete example of the Maximization-step merging process showing how two patterns are selected and how the merge reduces overall description length?
