- Proposes an MDL-based method to learn phonological correspondence rules from cognate substrings across 13 Slavic languages, optimized with EM and evaluated via distance measures, phylogenetic tree, and qualitative examples.

- Well-motivated application of MDL and clear justification for using EM, though some methodological derivations need clearer explanation.

- Limited engagement with related work in computational historical linguistics; should compare to established cognate/borrrowing/phylogenetic methods and cite researchers like Bouchard-Cote, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen, and tools such as List & Moran (2013).

- The MDL objective’s similarity to Bayesian inference deserves deeper discussion and (if possible) empirical comparison to Bayesian phylogenetic tools (e.g., MrBayes).

- Major scalability concern: alignment memory/runtime bottleneck restricts experiments to at most five languages; authors should analyze the bottleneck and propose scaling strategies.

- Phylogenetic evaluation relies on neighbor-joining, which has limitations (e.g., root specification); more sophisticated phylogenetic methods should be considered or discussed.

- Data representation issue: using orthography rather than phonetic transcriptions risks capturing script-level artifacts instead of true phonological correspondences; authors should address this and consider phonetic data.

- Dataset mixing concern: combining a cognate dataset with Swadesh lists (not exclusively cognate-based) may bias learning; clarify how this mixture affects results.

- Clarify EM convergence/stopping criterion (iterations, convergence threshold, or other heuristic).

- Explain the maximization step’s rule-merging procedure more clearly, ideally with a small illustrative example.

- Clarify the meaning of “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286).

- Minor copyedit: change “focus in on” to “focus on” (line 440).
