This paper introduces a method based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to discover phonological correspondences across multiple languages. The authors model these correspondences as rules that match substrings in cognates from a set of 13 Slavic languages. An MDL objective function is formulated to balance the complexity of the model (the rules) against the description of the data given the model, and this objective is optimized using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The method's performance is evaluated through several distance measures, the generation of a phylogenetic tree, and qualitative examples of the discovered correspondences.

The paper is well-motivated, and the application of the MDL principle to this problem is sound and intuitive. The justification for using the EM algorithm for optimization is also clearly presented. However, some derivations in the methodology could be explained in more detail for better clarity.

The following sections outline major and minor points for improvement.

**Major Comments**

**1. Engagement with Related Work**
A significant weakness of the paper is its limited engagement with related work in computational historical linguistics. The paper would be substantially strengthened by discussing and comparing its approach to established methods for cognate detection, borrowing detection, and phylogenetic inference.

*   This includes work by researchers such as Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen.
*   While some tools from this community may not be directly applicable to the dataset, the authors should at least acknowledge relevant software packages like the toolkit described by List and Moran (2013).
*   The authors correctly note the resemblance of their MDL objective to Bayesian inference. This connection warrants a deeper discussion, particularly in relation to Bayesian methods used in biological and linguistic phylogenetic inference (e.g., using tools like MrBayes). An empirical comparison, even on a small scale, could provide valuable insight into the relative strengths of the proposed method.

**2. Methodological and Scalability Concerns**
*   **Alignment Bottleneck:** The reported memory and runtime bottleneck, which limits the comparison to a maximum of five languages, appears to be a major drawback. This is particularly restrictive for a method intended for multilingual comparison and phylogenetic tree construction. The authors should discuss the nature of this bottleneck in more detail and propose potential strategies for scaling the approach to a larger number of languages.
*   **Phylogenetic Tree Construction:** The use of neighbor-joining for constructing the phylogenetic tree is a reasonable baseline, but it has known limitations, such as requiring the root to be specified manually. The analysis would be more robust if the authors employed or at least discussed more sophisticated phylogenetic inference methods.

**3. Data Representation and Handling**
*   **Orthography vs. Phonetics:** The reliance on orthographic data, rather than phonetic transcriptions, is a significant shortcoming. Orthography can obscure true phonological correspondences, especially when dealing with languages that use different scripts (as is the case with some Slavic languages). This raises the concern that the learned rules may be capturing superficial, script-level changes rather than genuine linguistic sound changes. The authors should address this limitation and consider how using phonetic data would impact their results.
*   **Mixed Datasets:** The paper mentions using a mix of two datasets: one with known cognates and another (Swadesh lists) which is not exclusively cognate-based. The authors should clarify how this data mixture might influence the learning process and the validity of the discovered correspondences.

**Questions and Points for Clarification**

1.  **EM Convergence:** The stopping criterion for the EM algorithm is not specified. Do the authors run the algorithm until convergence, for a fixed number of iterations, or based on another heuristic?
2.  **Maximization Step:** The procedure for merging rules during the maximization step was not entirely clear. A more detailed explanation or a small, illustrative example would be beneficial.
3.  **Optimal Unigram:** Could the authors please clarify the meaning of the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286)?

**Minor Comment**

*   Line 440: "focus in on" should be "focus on".
