This paper proposes a method based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle for discovering linguistic correspondences between related languages. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning across a set of Slavic languages. They develop a formal system of codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective function that balances the complexity of the model (the description length of the rules) against the description length of the data given the model. The model is trained using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The results are presented through several distance measures, a derived phylogenetic tree, and illustrative examples of the discovered correspondences.

The paper is well-motivated, and the overall formulation of the approach is sound. The application of the MDL principle provides a principled framework for this task, and the motivation for employing the EM algorithm is presented clearly. The connection the authors draw between the MDL objective and Bayesian inference is interesting. In this context, it would be insightful to include an empirical comparison with established Bayesian methods used in biological phylogenetic inference (e.g., MrBayes), which could provide a valuable benchmark for the proposed method.

While the core idea is promising, the paper has several areas that could be significantly improved.

**Related Work**
The paper would be substantially strengthened by a more thorough discussion of and comparison to related work in computational historical linguistics. The current version lacks context regarding existing methods for tasks like borrowing detection, cognate detection, and phylogenetic inference. A discussion of work by researchers such as Alexandre Bouchard-Côté, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen would be highly relevant. While some of their tools may not be directly applicable to the dataset used, their approaches should be acknowledged. Furthermore, readily available toolkits like that of List and Moran (2013) and standard tools from biological phylogenetics (e.g., PAUP, MrBayes) could serve as important baselines.

**Approach and Methodology**
*   **Scalability:** A major concern is the scalability of the proposed alignment procedure. The authors note a memory/runtime bottleneck that limits the comparison to a maximum of five languages at a time. For a study that aims to reconstruct phylogenetic relationships, this is a significant drawback. It would be valuable if the authors discussed their ideas for addressing this limitation to enable analysis of larger language groups simultaneously.
*   **Phylogenetic Tree Construction:** The choice of the neighbor-joining algorithm for creating the phylogenetic tree is somewhat basic and has known disadvantages, such as requiring the root to be specified manually. The analysis would be more robust if it employed more sophisticated and standard methods from phylogenetics.
*   **EM Stopping Criterion:** The paper does not specify the stopping criterion for the EM algorithm. Do the authors run it until convergence, for a fixed number of iterations, or based on another metric? This detail is important for reproducibility.

**Data**
*   **Data Representation:** A significant methodological limitation is the reliance on orthographic data rather than phonetic transcriptions. This is particularly problematic for Slavic languages, which use a mix of Latin and Cyrillic scripts. The current approach risks learning superficial rules that reflect differences in writing systems rather than genuine linguistic sound correspondences. This shortcoming could be largely mitigated by working with phonetically transcribed data.
*   **Dataset Composition:** The paper mentions that two datasets are combined: one containing known cognates and another based on Swadesh lists, which are not necessarily cognate sets. The authors should consider and discuss how mixing these different types of data might impact the validity and interpretation of the learned rules and the resulting phylogenetic tree.

**Points Requiring Clarification**
*   The merging procedure performed in the maximization step of the EM algorithm was not entirely clear. A more detailed explanation or a small worked example would greatly improve clarity.
*   Could the authors please clarify the meaning of the phrase "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286)?

**Minor Issue**
*   Line 440: "focus in on" should be "focus on".
