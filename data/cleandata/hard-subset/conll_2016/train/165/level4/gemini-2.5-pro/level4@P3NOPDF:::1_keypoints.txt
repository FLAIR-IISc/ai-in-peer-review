- Proposes an MDL-based method to discover substring correspondence rules across 13 Slavic languages, trained with EM and evaluated via distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples.  
- Approach is well-motivated and formally sound; MDL framework and EM motivation are clear, with an interesting MDL–Bayesian connection.  
- Recommend empirical comparison to established Bayesian phylogenetic methods (e.g., MrBayes) as a benchmark.  
- Related-work coverage is insufficient; missing discussion of computational historical linguistics (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen) and toolkits like List & Moran and standard phylogenetic tools (PAUP, MrBayes).  
- Scalability concern: alignment procedure has a memory/runtime bottleneck limiting comparisons to at most five languages, which undermines phylogenetic aims; authors should discuss fixes.  
- Phylogenetic-tree construction uses neighbor-joining, a basic method with known drawbacks; more robust, standard phylogenetic methods should be used.  
- EM stopping criterion is unspecified—need to state convergence condition or iteration limit for reproducibility.  
- Data representation is problematic: reliance on orthography (mix of Latin/Cyrillic) risks learning script differences instead of true phonetic correspondences; phonetic transcriptions are preferred.  
- Dataset composition mixes known cognates with Swadesh lists (not necessarily cognate), which may bias learned rules and tree interpretation—this should be discussed.  
- Clarify the maximization-step merging procedure in EM, ideally with a detailed explanation or worked example.  
- Clarify the meaning of “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286).  
- Minor typo: change “focus in on” to “focus on” (line 440).
