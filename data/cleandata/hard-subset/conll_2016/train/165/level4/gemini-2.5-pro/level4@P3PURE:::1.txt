This paper proposes a method based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to discover correspondences between words with shared meanings across multiple Slavic languages. The authors develop codes for rules that match substrings and formulate an MDL objective that balances the complexity of the model with the description of the data given the model. The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and evaluated on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are presented through several distance measures, a derived phylogenetic tree, and examples of the discovered correspondences.

The motivation for the approach and its formulation are sound. MDL is a reasonable framework for this problem, and the justification for employing the EM algorithm is presented clearly. The overall approach is sensible, although some of the mathematical derivations were difficult to follow.

**Critique and Suggestions for Improvement**

**1. Comparison to Related Work**
The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison to existing computational methods in historical linguistics.
*   The discussion lacks a comparison to methods for borrowing and cognate detection. The authors should consider studies by researchers such as Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen. While some of these methods may not have publicly available tools, the toolkit by List and Moran (2013) is a relevant example that could be mentioned.
*   The authors correctly point out the resemblance of the MDL objective to Bayesian inference. Given this connection, an empirical comparison to established tools from biological phylogenetic inference, which are often applied to linguistic data (e.g., PAUP*, MrBayes), could provide valuable insight into the method's performance.

**2. Approach and Methodology**
*   **Scalability:** The alignment procedure is noted as a memory and runtime bottleneck, limiting experiments to a maximum of five languages at once. This appears to be a major drawback for a method intended for phylogenetic analysis across multiple languages. It would be valuable if the authors discussed potential ideas for overcoming this limitation.
*   **Phylogenetic Tree Construction:** While neighbor-joining is a common baseline, it has known disadvantages, such as requiring the root to be specified manually. The analysis would be more robust if the authors employed or at least discussed more sophisticated phylogenetic inference methods.
*   **EM Stopping Criterion:** What stopping criterion is used for the EM algorithm? Is it run until convergence or for a fixed number of iterations?

**3. Data**
*   **Orthography vs. Phonetics:** The model operates on orthographic data, which may hide genuine linguistic correspondences, especially when dealing with languages that use different scripts (e.g., Cyrillic and Latin). Consequently, the learned rules might reflect changes in orthographic conventions more than actual phonological changes. This seems like a significant shortcoming that could be avoided by working with phonetic transcriptions.
*   **Dataset Composition:** The experiments mix two datasets: one containing known cognates and another (the Swadesh lists) where cognacy is not guaranteed. Have the authors considered how this mixing might impact the results?

**4. Unclear Points**
Certain aspects of the paper would benefit from additional clarification:
*   What is the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286)?
*   The merging procedure performed during the maximization step was not entirely clear and could be explained in more detail.

**Minor Issue**
*   Line 440: "focus in on" should be "focus on".
