- Paper proposes an MDL-based method for discovering cross-language word correspondences using substring-matching rules and an EM-trained model; motivation and MDL choice are sound.
- Authors clearly justify using EM, but some mathematical derivations are difficult to follow and need clarification.
- Recommend empirical comparison to Bayesian phylogenetic methods (e.g., MrBayes) to strengthen claims and connect to established biological/linguistic practice.
- Related work discussion is weak; cite and compare to computational historical-linguistics methods (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen) and toolkits like List & Moran (2013).
- Include comparisons of phylogenetic results to standard tools from computational biology (PAUP*, MrBayes) commonly applied to linguistic data.
- Alignment procedure has a major memory/runtime bottleneck limiting comparisons to five languages at a time; discuss scalability strategies.
- Neighbor-joining has known disadvantages (e.g., manual rooting); consider or discuss more sophisticated tree-building methods.
- Unclear what stopping criterion is used for EM (convergence threshold, fixed iterations, or other); specify this.
- Data mixes confirmed cognates and Swadesh lists (shared meaning but not necessarily cognate); discuss the impact of mixing these sources.
- Using orthographic data risks learning script-level transformations rather than true phonetic/morphological correspondences; recommend phonetic transcriptions where possible.
- Clarify the phrase "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) — its meaning is unclear.
- Provide a clearer description of the rule-merging procedure during the M-step of EM; current description is insufficiently detailed.
- Minor typo: line 440 should read "focus on" instead of "focus in on."
- Suggested references to add: List & Moran (2013) toolkit and Bayesian phylogenetic work such as Kitchen et al. (2009).
