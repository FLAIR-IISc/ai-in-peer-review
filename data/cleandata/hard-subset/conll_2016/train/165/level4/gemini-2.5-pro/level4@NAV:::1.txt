This paper introduces a method for identifying correspondences between languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model relationships between semantically equivalent words across multiple Slavic languages. They formulate a coding scheme for rules that capture substring matches in two or more languages and define an MDL objective that balances the complexity of the model against the cost of encoding the data with that model. The model's parameters are learned using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The performance is demonstrated through various distance metrics, the generation of a phylogenetic tree, and qualitative examples of discovered correspondences.

The core motivation and the formulation of the problem are sound. The choice of the MDL principle is well-suited for this task, and the justification for using the EM algorithm is clearly articulated. However, I found some of the mathematical derivations difficult to follow.

The paper correctly notes the connection between the MDL objective and Bayesian inference. This parallel brings to mind the widespread use of Bayesian methods in biological phylogenetic inference, exemplified by tools such as MrBayes. A direct empirical comparison with such methods could provide valuable insights into the performance and positioning of the proposed approach.

**Related Work:**
*   The discussion of related work could be strengthened by including a comparison to existing methods in computational historical linguistics, particularly those focused on borrowing and cognate detection. For instance, the work of researchers like Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen is highly relevant. While readily available implementations may not exist for all these methods, a discussion is warranted. Furthermore, established toolkits like that of List and Moran (2013) should be acknowledged.
*   Similarly, it would be beneficial to discuss or compare against established tools from biological phylogenetics that are applicable to linguistic data, such as PAUP* or MrBayes.

**Approach and Methodology:**
*   **Alignment Procedure:** The alignment procedure's computational complexity, which limits comparisons to a maximum of five languages, appears to be a significant bottleneck. Given the paper's focus on multi-language analysis and phylogeny, this scalability limitation is a notable constraint. I would be interested to learn about the authors' strategies for addressing this challenge in future work.
*   **Phylogenetic Tree:** The choice of neighbor-joining for phylogenetic tree construction has known limitations, such as the need to specify the root a priori. Have the authors considered employing more sophisticated phylogenetic inference methods?
*   Could the authors clarify the stopping criterion for the EM algorithm? Is it run until convergence, or is another threshold used?

**Data:**
*   The dataset appears to combine a cognate set with Swadesh lists, which are not strictly cognate sets. It would be useful for the authors to discuss the potential impact of mixing these data sources on the final results.
*   The use of orthographic data, especially with languages employing different scripts, is a potential weakness. This representation may obscure true phonetic correspondences, leading the model to learn rules that reflect script conversions rather than genuine linguistic sound changes. This limitation could be addressed by operating on phonetic transcriptions instead.

**Unclear Points:**
*   Could the authors please clarify the meaning of the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" mentioned on line 286?
*   The description of the merging process within the maximization step was difficult to follow and could benefit from further explanation.

**Minor Issue:**
*   A minor correction: "focus in on" (line 440) should be "focus on".
