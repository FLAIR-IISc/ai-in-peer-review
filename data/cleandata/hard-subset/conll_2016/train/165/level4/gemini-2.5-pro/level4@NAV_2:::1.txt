This paper presents a method for discovering string-level correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors formulate a model consisting of correspondence rules that match substrings across cognate words. An MDL objective function is defined to balance the complexity of the rule set (the model) with the cost of describing the data using those rules. The model is optimized using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The primary contribution is the novel MDL-based framework for simultaneously discovering correspondences among more than two languages. A secondary contribution is the quantitative analysis of multi-language similarity through shared description lengths, which offers a more detailed perspective than traditional pairwise comparisons.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Well-Motivated Framework:** The application of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle is a strong choice for this problem. It provides a formal, data-driven way to balance model complexity against data fit, naturally preventing overfitting and avoiding the need for strong prior assumptions about the distribution or shape of correspondence rules. The authors provide a clear motivation for this choice and for the use of an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to optimize the objective.
2.  **Validation through Sensible Results:** The empirical results are compelling. The pairwise analysis on 13 Slavic languages produces a phylogenetic tree (Figure 1) that largely aligns with established linguistic classifications. This serves as an effective external validation, demonstrating that the learned model captures genuine linguistic similarities.
3.  **Novel Multi-Way Language Analysis:** The four-way analysis (Section 5.3) is a key strength. By moving beyond pairwise comparisons, the authors demonstrate the ability to quantify similarities between arbitrary subsets of languages. The "shared Description Length" (sDL) visualization (Figure 3) provides a nuanced and insightful quantification of the relationships between Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian, identifying not only the known West/East Slavic groupings but also more subtle cross-group similarities.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** The paper positions itself within computational and historical linguistics but fails to compare its approach, either conceptually or empirically, to established methods in the field. There is no discussion of or comparison to statistical methods for cognate detection, borrowing analysis, or phylogenetic inference. Tools like MrBayes or PAUP for biological phylogeny, or computational linguistics toolkits (e.g., List and Moran, 2013), are highly relevant but unmentioned. An empirical comparison would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.
2.  **Significant Scalability Issues:** The paper states that the current implementation is limited to "up to five languages" due to memory and runtime bottlenecks in the alignment procedure. This is a major drawback for a method whose primary contribution is the analysis of *multiple* languages. This limitation severely curtails the practical utility of the approach for larger-scale phylogenetic or comparative studies.
3.  **Reliance on Orthography:** The experiments are conducted on raw orthographic data rather than phonetic transcriptions. This is a significant methodological shortcoming. Orthography can obscure or misrepresent true linguistic correspondences, especially when comparing languages with different writing systems (e.g., Latin vs. Cyrillic) or different orthographic conventions. Many of the learned "correspondences" may simply reflect differences in spelling conventions rather than deeper phonological or morphological relationships.
4.  **Mixing of Data Sources:** The methodology combines Swadesh lists, which contain words of shared meaning but not necessarily shared origin (cognates), with a dataset of known cognates. The potential impact of mixing these distinct data types on the discovered correspondence rules is not discussed or analyzed, which raises questions about the interpretation of the results.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation or a concrete example of the merging process performed during the Maximization step of your training procedure? The current description is somewhat abstract.
2.  The paper notes a significant computational bottleneck in the alignment procedure that limits the method to a small number of languages. Do you have plans or concrete ideas for improving the scalability of this step to make the analysis of a larger number of languages feasible?
3.  What stopping criterion is used for the EM algorithm? Is it run for a fixed number of iterations or until the description length converges?
4.  In Section 4.1, you refer to using the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" to encode the character sequences within a rule. Could you please clarify how this unigram distribution is defined and calculated?
5.  Regarding the phylogenetic tree in Figure 1, the use of the neighbor-joining method requires the root to be placed manually. How would the use of more sophisticated phylogenetic inference methods that can identify the root automatically potentially alter or enhance your analysis?
