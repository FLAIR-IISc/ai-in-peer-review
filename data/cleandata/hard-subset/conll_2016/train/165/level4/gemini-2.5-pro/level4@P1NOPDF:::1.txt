This paper proposes a method based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle for discovering correspondences between languages. The authors model correspondences between words with shared meanings across several Slavic languages by developing codes for rules that match substrings. They formulate an MDL objective that balances the complexity of the model (the description length of the rules) with the complexity of the data given the model.

The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The authors present their results using several distance measures, a generated phylogenetic tree, and examples of the discovered correspondences.

The motivation and formulation of the approach are sound. The choice of the MDL principle is well-suited for this problem, and the justification for using the EM algorithm is presented clearly. However, I found some of the mathematical derivations difficult to follow.

The authors correctly note the connection between their MDL objective and Bayesian inference. This connection immediately brings to mind the widespread use of Bayesian methods in biological phylogenetic inference (e.g., with tools like MrBayes). An empirical comparison against such methods could provide valuable insights and strengthen the paper's contribution.

### Related Work
The discussion of related work could be strengthened by including a comparison to other computational methods in historical linguistics, particularly those for borrowing and cognate detection. For instance, the authors could discuss the work of researchers like Alexandre Bouchard-Côté, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen. While tools for some of these methods may not be readily available for this specific dataset, the paper would benefit from acknowledging them. Furthermore, established toolkits like that of List and Moran (2013) are highly relevant. The paper could also compare its phylogenetic results to those produced by standard tools from computational biology, such as PAUP* or MrBayes, which are often applied to linguistic data.

### Approach and Methodology
*   **Alignment procedure:** The memory and runtime bottleneck, which limits comparisons to a maximum of five languages at a time, appears to be a significant drawback. Since a key contribution is the generation of phylogenetic trees, the ability to include more languages simultaneously would be highly desirable. It would be helpful if the authors could discuss potential strategies for scaling their approach to larger sets of languages.
*   **Phylogenetic tree:** The neighbor-joining algorithm used to construct the phylogenetic trees has known disadvantages, such as requiring the root to be specified manually. The authors should consider or at least discuss the use of more sophisticated tree-building methods.
*   It is unclear what stopping criterion is used for the EM algorithm. Do the authors run it until convergence, for a fixed number of iterations, or based on some other measure?

### Data
*   The data consists of a mix of two datasets: one containing confirmed cognates and another (the Swadesh lists) that includes words with shared meaning but which are not necessarily cognate. The authors should discuss how mixing these data sources might impact the results.
*   The use of orthographic data may obscure many true linguistic correspondences, a problem that is particularly acute when dealing with languages that use different scripts. Consequently, the learned rules may be capturing script-level transformations rather than genuine phonetic or morphological changes. This is a notable limitation that could potentially be addressed by working with phonetic transcriptions instead of orthography.

### Unclear Points
*   The meaning of the phrase "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) is unclear. Could the authors please clarify this concept?
*   The procedure for merging rules during the maximization step of the EM algorithm is not described with sufficient clarity. Further detail on this part of the process would be beneficial.

### Minor Issue
*   Line 440: "focus in on" should be "focus on".
