### Summary

This paper introduces a novel method for automatically discovering string-level correspondences across multiple languages. It formulates the problem within the Minimum Description Length (MDL) framework, which seeks the set of correspondence rules that provides the most succinct description of the data (a corpus of cognate tuples). The authors propose an efficient, deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) to infer the model of correspondence rules. The method is evaluated on a set of 13 Slavic languages, with results presented through pairwise distance measures, a phylogenetic tree that aligns with linguistic classifications, and qualitative examples of the discovered multi-language rules. The paper also introduces a technique for "data over-weighting" to allow users to control the granularity of the discovered rules.

### Contributions

1.  **A principled, non-parametric framework for multi-language correspondence discovery.** The primary contribution is the application of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to this task. This provides a theoretically sound and objective way to identify statistically significant string correspondences without making strong prior assumptions about their shape or distribution. The framework naturally handles correspondences involving any subset of the languages being analyzed, which is a significant advantage over many pairwise methods.

2.  **A novel information-theoretic measure for quantifying multi-way linguistic similarity.** The "Shared Description Length" (sDL) metric, derived directly from the MDL model, offers a fine-grained method for quantifying the amount of shared structure between arbitrary subsets of languages. This is demonstrated effectively in the four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian, providing insights that would be obscured by purely pairwise comparisons.

### Strengths

1.  **Sound Theoretical Foundation and Clear Motivation.** The paper's core strength lies in its well-motivated and theoretically grounded approach. MDL is a natural and powerful choice for this problem, as it formalizes the preference for simpler models and avoids the need for arbitrary assumptions about rule distributions, which is a limitation the authors correctly identify in prior work. The derivation of the EM-based training procedure from this principle is intuitive and clearly explained.

2.  **Compelling Quantitative and Qualitative Results.** The experimental results provide strong evidence for the method's efficacy. The quantitative analysis, particularly the Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) based phylogenetic tree for 13 Slavic languages, convincingly aligns with established linguistic classifications (West, East, South Slavic groups). Furthermore, the qualitative examples of learned correspondences are insightful, demonstrating the model's ability to capture a range of phenomena from orthographic conventions (e.g., `cz` vs. `č`) to morphological patterns (e.g., verb endings) and phonological shifts (e.g., liquid metathesis).

3.  **Novel and Insightful Multi-Language Analysis.** The four-way analysis showcases the unique strength of the proposed approach. By moving beyond pairwise comparisons, the paper demonstrates how the sDL metric can quantify complex relationships, such as identifying the CS-PL and RU-BG subgroups while also measuring the structure shared by all four languages, or by specific three-language subsets. This represents a more nuanced form of linguistic similarity analysis than is typically presented.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison to and Discussion of Related Work.** The paper's most significant weakness is the lack of engagement with a substantial body of related work in computational historical linguistics and phylogenetics. While the introduction briefly mentions the comparative method, it overlooks decades of computational research on cognate detection, borrowing detection, and phylogenetic inference. A comparison, even if only through discussion, with established phylogenetic methods (e.g., Bayesian inference models implemented in tools like MrBayes, or even character-based methods in PAUP) is warranted, especially since the authors note the similarity of their MDL objective to Bayesian inference. Tools from the historical linguistics community, such as those discussed in List and Moran (2013), are also highly relevant.

2.  **Significant Scalability Limitations.** The paper claims to address correspondence discovery in *multiple* languages, but the reported memory and runtime bottleneck, which limits the current implementation to a maximum of five languages, severely undermines this claim. For a method whose primary advantage is its ability to handle multi-language data, this is a critical drawback. The promise of phylogenetic analysis is most compelling with a larger number of languages, and the current limitation makes this impractical.

3.  **Reliance on Orthography over Phonetic Transcription.** The decision to work directly with orthographic forms is a major limitation that is not sufficiently discussed. This approach conflates linguistic correspondence with script similarity. As the authors' own results on Latin vs. Cyrillic Serbo-Croatian show, the model is sensitive to superficial script differences. This can mask deeper phonetic correspondences (e.g., between languages using different alphabets) or falsely identify patterns that are purely orthographic. Using a standardized phonetic transcription like IPA would provide a more linguistically sound basis for discovering true correspondences.

4.  **Lack of Methodological Detail.** Several key algorithmic details are underspecified, making the work harder to reproduce and evaluate. Specifically:
    *   The stopping criterion for the EM algorithm is not mentioned.
    *   The "merging" process in the Maximization step is central to the algorithm but is described only at a high level. A more concrete explanation of how candidate pairs of patterns are generated and evaluated would be beneficial.
    *   The justification for using neighbor-joining—a relatively simple method that requires manual rooting—for tree construction is absent, when more sophisticated methods are standard in the field.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The reported memory/runtime bottleneck for N>5 seems to be a critical limitation for a method intended for multi-language analysis. Do you have concrete plans or ideas for improving the scalability of the alignment procedure to handle a larger number of languages?

2.  Could you elaborate on how your approach conceptually compares to established methods in computational phylogenetics, such as Bayesian inference models (e.g., as used in MrBayes)? Given the noted similarity between MDL and Bayesian approaches, an empirical comparison on a shared task could be very insightful.

3.  Have you considered applying your method to phonetically transcribed data (e.g., in IPA)? How would you expect the results to differ, and to what extent do you believe the current orthography-based findings reflect genuine linguistic correspondence versus superficial script similarity?

4.  The merging process in the Maximization step is crucial but was not entirely clear. Could you provide a more detailed explanation or a small, concrete example of how two patterns are selected for a merge and how this leads to the highest decrease in the overall description length?
