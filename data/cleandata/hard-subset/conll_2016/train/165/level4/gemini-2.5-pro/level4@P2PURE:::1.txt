This paper proposes a method based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to discover correspondences between related languages. The authors model correspondences between words with shared meanings across several Slavic languages. They develop a coding scheme for rules that capture substring matches and formulate an MDL objective that balances model complexity with the cost of describing the data given the model. The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. Results are presented through several distance metrics, a derived phylogenetic tree, and illustrative examples of the discovered correspondences.

The motivation for the approach is well-founded, and its formulation is logical. The choice of MDL is appropriate for this task, and the justification for using the EM algorithm is clearly explained. However, I found some of the mathematical derivations difficult to follow.

The authors correctly note the connection between their MDL objective and Bayesian inference. Given the successful application of Bayesian methods in biological phylogenetic inference (e.g., with tools like MrBayes), an empirical comparison between the proposed MDL approach and an established Bayesian phylogenetic method could provide valuable insights.

**Related Work**
*   The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough comparison to related work in computational historical linguistics, particularly methods for borrowing and cognate detection. While the authors might not have access to executable tools for all relevant prior work (e.g., studies by Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen), a discussion of these approaches and their relationship to the proposed method would be beneficial. Furthermore, existing toolkits like that of List and Moran (2013) could be mentioned.
*   As noted above, established tools from biological phylogenetics (e.g., PAUP*, MrBayes) could serve as relevant baselines for the tree inference component.

**Approach and Methodology**
*   **Alignment Procedure:** A significant drawback appears to be the memory and runtime complexity of the alignment procedure, which limits comparisons to a maximum of five languages. Since the work involves multi-language comparison and phylogenetic tree inference, the ability to scale to more languages would be highly desirable. It would be helpful if the authors could discuss potential strategies for overcoming this scalability bottleneck.
*   **Phylogenetic Tree:** The use of Neighbor-Joining for tree construction has known limitations, such as requiring the root to be specified manually. The authors should consider or discuss the use of more sophisticated phylogenetic inference methods.
*   **EM Stopping Criterion:** The paper should clarify the stopping criterion used for the EM algorithm (e.g., convergence to a threshold, a fixed number of iterations).

**Data**
*   The paper combines two types of datasets: one containing known cognates and another (Swadesh lists) that does not guarantee cognacy. The authors should discuss how mixing these data sources might impact the results.
*   The use of orthographic representations, especially for languages with different scripts, is a potential limitation. This choice may obscure genuine phonetic correspondences and could lead the model to learn rules that reflect orthographic conventions rather than true linguistic sound changes. This shortcoming could be addressed by operating on phonetic transcriptions instead.

**Unclear Points**
*   Could the authors please clarify the meaning of the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules"? (line 286)
*   The procedure for merging rules during the maximization step was not entirely clear.

**Minor Issue**
*   "focus in on" -> "focus on" (line 440)
