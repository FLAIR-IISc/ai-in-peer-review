- Proposes an MDL-based method to learn substring-matching rules for cross-linguistic correspondences, trained with EM and evaluated on 13 Slavic languages via distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and example correspondences.  
- Motivation is sound and MDL is a reasonable choice; EM justification is acceptable.  
- Several derivations are not fully transparent and need clearer exposition.  
- Authors note similarity between MDL and Bayesian inference; an empirical comparison to Bayesian phylogenetic methods (e.g., MrBayes) is suggested.  
- Related work coverage is incomplete—missing comparisons to borrowing/cognate-detection and computational historical-linguistics work (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen, List & Moran 2013).  
- Consideration of biological phylogeny tools (paup, MrBayes) as baselines is warranted.  
- Alignment procedure is computationally expensive, currently limiting comparisons to at most five languages.  
- Use of neighbor-joining for tree construction has drawbacks (e.g., manual rooting); more sophisticated phylogenetic methods should be considered.  
- It is unclear whether EM was run to convergence or stopped by another criterion.  
- The dataset is a mixture of cognate and non-cognate (Swadesh) lists; the impact of this mixture on results should be examined.  
- Working with orthographic data may conflate script changes with linguistic correspondences; phonetic transcriptions are recommended.  
- Specific unclear methodological points: definition of the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286) and the merging process in the M-step.  
- Minor editorial issue: use “focus on” instead of “focus in on” (line 440).
