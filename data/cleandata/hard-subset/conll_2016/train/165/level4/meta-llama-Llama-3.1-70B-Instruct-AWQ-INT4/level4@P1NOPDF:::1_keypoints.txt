- Proposes an MDL-based method to discover cross-language correspondences via substring-matching rules and an MDL objective balancing model and data.  
- Models correspondences among words with the same meaning across multiple Slavic languages.  
- Trains the model with Expectation-Maximization and evaluates on 13 Slavic languages using distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and example correspondences.  
- Motivation and formulation are sound; MDL and EM are reasonable choices, but some derivations are unclear.  
- Suggests empirical comparison to Bayesian phylogenetic inference (e.g., MrBayes) because of resemblance between MDL and Bayesian objectives.  
- Lacks comparison to borrowing/cognate-detection and historical-linguistics methods (e.g., work by Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen; List & Moran 2013).  
- Alignment procedure has a major memory/runtime bottleneck, currently allowing comparisons of only up to ~5 languages—scalability concern.  
- Using neighbor-joining for tree construction has known drawbacks (e.g., manual rooting); more advanced phylogenetic methods should be considered.  
- Unclear whether EM is run to convergence or stopped by another criterion; stopping criterion should be specified.  
- Combining cognate and Swadesh (not-necessarily-cognate) datasets may bias results and should be discussed.  
- Working in orthographic form can hide true correspondences and may capture script differences rather than phonological ones; phonetic transcriptions are preferable.  
- The phrase “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286) is unclear and needs clarification.  
- The merging operation in the EM maximization step is not clearly explained; provide more detail.  
- Minor wording: change “focus in on” to “focus on” (line 440).  
- Cite relevant references and tools (List & Moran 2013; Kitchen et al. 2009; PAUP, MrBayes) and discuss applicability.
