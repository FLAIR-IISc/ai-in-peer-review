This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning in a number of Slavic languages. They develop codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that balances the description of the model and the data given the model.

The model is trained with Expectation-Maximization (EM) and tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are shown by several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of found correspondences. The motivation and formulation of the approach make sense, and MDL seems like a reasonable tool to attack the problem. The authors' explanation for employing EM is also clear.

However, some of the derivations were not entirely clear, and the authors could benefit from providing more detailed explanations or examples to facilitate understanding.

**Contributions:**

1. The authors propose a novel approach to discovering correspondences between languages using the MDL principle.
2. They develop an efficient algorithm for inferring good sets of correspondence rules directly from data.
3. The experiments demonstrate the efficacy of the approach in discovering non-trivial associations between languages.

**Strengths:**

1. The approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the MDL principle and its application to the problem.
2. The experimental results are promising, and the authors demonstrate the ability of the approach to discover meaningful correspondences between languages.
3. The use of EM for training the model is well-justified, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper lacks a comparison to other methods for borrowing and cognate detection or other computational methods for historical linguistics.
2. The alignment procedure has a significant memory/runtime bottleneck, which limits the number of languages that can be compared.
3. The use of neighbor joining for creating phylogenetic trees is known to have disadvantages, and the authors could consider more sophisticated methods.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do the authors plan to address the memory/runtime bottleneck in the alignment procedure to enable the comparison of more languages?
2. Have the authors considered using more sophisticated methods for creating phylogenetic trees, such as Bayesian inference or maximum likelihood methods?
3. What is the stopping criterion for the EM algorithm, and how do the authors ensure convergence?

**Minor Issues:**

1. The phrase "focus in on" should be replaced with "focus on" (line 440).
2. The authors could benefit from providing more detailed explanations or examples to facilitate understanding of some of the derivations.
