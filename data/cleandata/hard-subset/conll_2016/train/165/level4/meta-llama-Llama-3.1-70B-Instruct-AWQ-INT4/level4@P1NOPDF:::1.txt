This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning in a number of Slavic languages. They develop codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that balances the description of the model and the data given the model. 

The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are presented using several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of found correspondences. 

The motivation and formulation of the approach are sound. MDL seems like a reasonable tool to tackle the problem, and the motivation for employing EM is presented clearly. However, some of the derivations were not entirely clear to me.

The authors point out the resemblance of the MDL objective to Bayesian inference, which is reminiscent of the application of Bayesian inference in biological phylogenetic inference, e.g., using the MrBayes tool. An empirical comparison here could be insightful.

Related work:
The paper lacks a comparison to methods for borrowing and cognate detection or other computational methods for historical linguistics. For example, the studies by Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen would be relevant. Although some of these methods may not have available tools to apply to the given dataset, the authors could mention List and Moran (2013). Additionally, there are relevant tools for biological phylogeny inference that can be applied (e.g., paup, MrBayes).

Approach and methodology:
The alignment procedure appears to have a major drawback, namely, the memory/runtime bottleneck, which only allows the comparison of 5 languages at most. Given that multiple languages are involved, and phylogenetic trees are constructed, it would be interesting to see more languages. I am curious to know what ideas the authors have for dealing with this issue.

The use of neighbor joining for creating phylogenetic trees is known to have disadvantages, such as having to specify the root manually. More sophisticated methods could be explored.

It is also unclear whether the EM algorithm is run until convergence or if some other stopping criterion is used.

Data:
The paper combines two datasets, one of cognates and one not necessarily (the Swadesh lists). It would be worth considering how this might impact the results.

The data is in orthographic form, which may hide many correspondences, particularly in languages with different scripts. Therefore, the learned rules might indicate changes in script more than real linguistic correspondences. This seems like a shortcoming that could be avoided by working on the level of phonetic transcriptions.

Unclear points:
The concept of the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) is unclear.

The merging done in the maximization step was not entirely clear to me.

Minor issue:
"focus in on" should be replaced with "focus on" (line 440).
