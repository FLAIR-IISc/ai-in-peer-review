- Proposes a novel MDL-based method to identify word correspondences across languages via rules encoding matching substrings.
- Trains the model with EM and evaluates on 13 Slavic languages, reporting distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and example correspondences.
- Motivation is sound and MDL is a suitable framework; EM justification is well presented.
- Some derivations in the paper are not transparent and need clearer explanation.
- Notes resemblance between MDL objective and Bayesian inference and recommends empirical comparison with Bayesian phylogenetic tools (e.g., MrBayes).
- Suggests comparing the method to existing borrowing and cognate-detection approaches and computational historical-linguistics work (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen).
- Recommends a direct comparison with List and Moran (2013) and exploration of phylogeny tools like paup and MrBayes.
- Alignment procedure is constrained by memory and runtime, limiting comparisons to five languages at a time; author should address scaling to more languages.
- Using neighbor joining for trees has known drawbacks (e.g., manual root choice); more sophisticated phylogenetic methods should be considered.
- Clarify whether EM is run to convergence or stopped by another criterion.
- Combining a cognate dataset with Swadesh lists may bias results; the authors should consider this effect.
- Working in orthographic form can mask true correspondences and confound script differences; phonetic transcription would be preferable.
- Clarify the definition of the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286) and the merging process in the maximization step.
- Minor editorial fix: replace “focus in on” with “focus on” (line 440).
