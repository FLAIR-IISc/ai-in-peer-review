This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning in a number of Slavic languages by developing codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulating an MDL objective that balances the description of the model and the data given the model. The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are presented through several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of found correspondences.

The motivation and formulation of the approach are sound, and MDL seems like a reasonable tool to tackle the problem. The authors nicely present the motivation for employing EM. However, some of the derivations were not entirely clear to me, and I would have appreciated more detailed explanations.

The authors point out the resemblance of the MDL objective to Bayesian inference, which is reminiscent of the application of Bayesian inference in biological phylogenetic inference, such as using the MrBayes tool. An empirical comparison with these methods could be insightful and provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed approach.

One major limitation of the paper is the lack of comparison to existing methods for borrowing and cognate detection or other computational methods for historical linguistics. For example, the studies by Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen could be relevant. Although some of these methods may not have available tools to apply to the given dataset, the authors could have mentioned List and Moran (2013) and other relevant tools for biological phylogeny inference, such as PAUP and MrBayes.

Regarding the approach and methodology, the alignment procedure appears to be a major drawback, as it allows for the comparison of only five languages at most due to memory and runtime bottlenecks. As the approach involves multiple languages and phylogenetic trees, it would be interesting to see more languages included. I am curious to know what ideas the authors have for dealing with this issue.

Furthermore, the use of neighbor joining for creating phylogenetic trees is known to have disadvantages, such as requiring manual specification of the root. The authors could consider using more sophisticated methods to improve the accuracy of the phylogenetic trees.

Additionally, it is unclear whether the EM algorithm is run until convergence or if there is another stopping criterion. Providing more details on the training process would be helpful.

The data used in the paper consists of two mixed datasets, one of cognates and one not necessarily (the Swadesh lists). The authors should consider how this might impact the results and discuss the potential implications.

Moreover, the data is in orthographic form, which might hide many correspondences, especially in languages with different scripts. Therefore, the learned rules might indicate changes in script more than real linguistic correspondences. This seems like a shortcoming that could be avoided by working on the level of phonetic transcriptions.

There are a few unclear points in the paper. For instance, the concept of the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) is not well explained. Additionally, the merging done in the maximization step was not entirely clear to me.

On a minor note, there is a grammatical error in line 440, where "focus in on" should be replaced with "focus on".

In conclusion, while the paper proposes an interesting approach to discovering correspondences between languages, there are several limitations and unclear points that need to be addressed. With some revisions to the methodology and additional comparisons to existing methods, the paper could be significantly improved.
