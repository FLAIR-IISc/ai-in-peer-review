This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning in a number of Slavic languages. They develop codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that balances the description of the model and the data given the model.

The model is trained with Expectation-Maximization (EM) and tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are shown by several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of found correspondences. The motivation and formulation of the approach make sense. MDL seems like a reasonable tool to attack the problem, and the motivation for employing EM is presented nicely.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The authors propose an MDL-based approach for discovering correspondences between languages, which is a novel application of the MDL principle in the field of historical linguistics.
2. They develop an efficient algorithm for finding correspondences using EM, which is a significant contribution to the field.
3. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach by testing it on a set of 13 Slavic languages and showing promising results.

**Strengths:**

1. The authors provide a clear and well-motivated introduction to the problem of discovering correspondences between languages and the MDL principle.
2. The paper is well-structured, and the authors provide a detailed explanation of their approach and methodology.
3. The results are promising, and the authors provide a good discussion of the implications of their findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper lacks a comparison to other methods for borrowing and cognate detection or other computational methods for historical linguistics.
2. The alignment procedure has a major drawback, allowing the comparison of only 5 languages at most.
3. The authors use a simple method for creating phylogenetic trees, which may not be the most sophisticated approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Have you considered comparing your approach to other methods for borrowing and cognate detection or other computational methods for historical linguistics?
2. How do you plan to deal with the memory/runtime bottleneck in the alignment procedure to allow for the comparison of more languages?
3. Can you provide more details on the merging done in the maximization step of the EM algorithm?
4. Have you considered working on the level of phonetic transcriptions instead of orthographic form to avoid hiding correspondences?
