This paper presents a novel approach for identifying correspondences between languages, leveraging the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model word correspondences across multiple Slavic languages by developing codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages. An MDL objective function is formulated to balance model description and data given the model.

The proposed model is trained using Expectation-Maximization (EM) and evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The results are presented through various distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of discovered correspondences.

The motivation behind the approach is sound, and MDL appears to be a suitable tool for tackling this problem. The justification for employing EM is well-presented. However, some of the derivations were not entirely transparent to me.

The resemblance between the MDL objective and Bayesian inference is noted, which prompts consideration of the application of Bayesian inference in phylogenetic inference, such as with the MrBayes tool. An empirical comparison would provide valuable insights.

Related work:
- The authors would benefit from comparing their method to existing approaches for borrowing and cognate detection, as well as other computational methods for historical linguistics, including the works of Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen. Although some of these methods may not be directly applicable to the given dataset, a comparison with List and Moran (2013) would be informative. Additionally, tools for biological phylogeny inference, such as paup and MrBayes, could be explored.

Approach and methodology:
- The alignment procedure is limited by memory and runtime constraints, allowing for the comparison of only five languages at a time. As multiple languages and phylogenetic trees are involved, it would be interesting to see the inclusion of more languages. I am curious about the authors' potential solutions to this issue.
- The use of neighbor joining for creating phylogenetic trees is known to have drawbacks, such as requiring manual root specification. More sophisticated methods could be explored.
- Could the authors clarify whether EM is run until convergence or if another stopping criterion is employed?

Data:
- The combination of two datasets, one of cognates and one not necessarily (the Swadesh lists), may impact the results. Have the authors considered this potential effect?
- The data is in orthographic form, which may conceal many correspondences, particularly in languages with different scripts. This could lead to learned rules indicating script changes rather than genuine linguistic correspondences. Working with phonetic transcriptions might alleviate this shortcoming.

Unclear points:
- The definition of the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) is unclear.
- The merging process in the maximization step requires further clarification.

Minor issue:
- "focus in on" should be replaced with "focus on" (line 440).
