This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning in multiple Slavic languages by developing codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages. They formulate an MDL objective that balances the description of the model and the data given the model. The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are presented through various distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of found correspondences.

The motivation and formulation of the approach are well-reasoned, and the use of MDL seems like a reasonable tool to tackle the problem. The authors also present a clear motivation for employing EM. However, some of the derivations were not entirely clear to me, and further clarification would be beneficial.

The authors note the resemblance of the MDL objective to Bayesian inference, which is reminiscent of applications in biological phylogenetic inference, such as using the MrBayes tool. An empirical comparison with these methods could provide valuable insights.

In terms of related work, the paper lacks a comparison to methods for borrowing and cognate detection or other computational methods for historical linguistics. For example, studies by Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen could be relevant. Although some of these methods may not have available tools to apply to the given dataset, mentioning List and Moran (2013) would be a good starting point. Additionally, relevant tools for biological phylogeny inference, such as paup and MrBayes, could also be applied.

Regarding the approach and methodology, the alignment procedure appears to be a major drawback, allowing for the comparison of only 5 languages at most. As multiple languages and phylogenetic trees are involved, it would be interesting to see more languages included. I am curious to know what ideas the authors have for addressing this issue. Furthermore, using neighbor joining for creating phylogenetic trees is known to have disadvantages, such as requiring manual specification of the root. Considering more sophisticated methods could improve the results. It is also unclear whether EM is run until convergence or if there is another stopping criterion.

The data used in the study consists of two mixed datasets, one of cognates and one not necessarily (the Swadesh lists). It would be beneficial to consider how this might impact the results. Moreover, the data is in orthographic form, which may hide many correspondences, particularly in languages with different scripts. This could lead to learned rules indicating changes in script rather than real linguistic correspondences. Working on the level of phonetic transcriptions could help avoid this shortcoming.

There are a few unclear points in the paper. For instance, the concept of the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) requires further explanation. Additionally, the merging done in the maximization step was not entirely clear to me.

On a minor note, "focus in on" should be replaced with "focus on" (line 440).
