This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning in a number of Slavic languages by developing codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulating an MDL objective that balances the description of the model and the data given the model.

The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are presented using several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of found correspondences. The motivation and formulation of the approach are sound, and MDL seems like a reasonable tool to tackle the problem. The motivation for employing EM is also well-presented. However, some of the derivations were not entirely clear to me.

The authors note the resemblance of the MDL objective to Bayesian inference, which is reminiscent of the application of Bayesian inference in biological phylogenetic inference, such as using the MrBayes tool. An empirical comparison with these methods could provide valuable insights.

Related work:
The paper lacks a comparison to methods for borrowing and cognate detection or other computational methods for historical linguistics. For instance, the studies by Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen could be relevant. Although some of these methods may not have available tools to apply to the given dataset, the work by List and Moran (2013) could be mentioned. Additionally, there are relevant tools for biological phylogeny inference that can be applied, such as PAUP and MrBayes.

Approach and methodology:
The alignment procedure has a major drawback, which is the memory/runtime bottleneck, allowing the comparison of only 5 languages at most. As the approach involves multiple languages and phylogenetic trees, it would be interesting to see more languages included. I am curious to know what ideas the authors have for addressing this issue.
The use of neighbor joining for creating phylogenetic trees is known to have disadvantages, such as requiring manual specification of the root. More sophisticated methods could be explored.
It is unclear whether the EM algorithm is run until convergence or if there is another stopping criterion.

Data:
The paper combines two datasets, one of cognates and one not necessarily (the Swadesh lists). It would be interesting to know how this might impact the results.
The data is in orthographic form, which may hide many correspondences, especially in languages with different scripts. Therefore, the learned rules might indicate changes in script rather than real linguistic correspondences. This seems like a shortcoming that could be avoided by working on the level of phonetic transcriptions.

Unclear points:
The concept of the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) is unclear.
The merging done in the maximization step was not entirely clear to me.

Minor issue:
"focus in on" should be replaced with "focus on" (line 440).
