- Proposes MDL-based method to discover cross-language word correspondences by coding substring-matching rules across 13 Slavic languages.
- Models trade-off between model complexity and data fit via an MDL objective and trains with Expectation-Maximization (EM).
- Presents results using distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of discovered correspondences.
- Motivation and use of MDL and EM are well-reasoned and appropriate for the problem.
- Some derivations and the EM maximization/merging steps are unclear and need further clarification.
- Paper lacks empirical comparison to Bayesian phylogenetic methods (e.g., MrBayes, paup) despite noting MDL–Bayes resemblance.
- Related-work coverage is incomplete: missing comparisons to borrowing/cognate detection and computational historical-linguistics methods (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen; List & Moran 2013).
- Alignment procedure is a major drawback, apparently limiting comparisons to at most five languages and reducing scalability.
- Using neighbor-joining for phylogeny has known disadvantages (e.g., manual rooting); more sophisticated phylogenetic methods should be considered.
- Unclear whether EM is run to convergence or stopped by another criterion—stopping details should be specified.
- Dataset issues: mixes cognate and non-cognate (Swadesh) lists, which may affect results and interpretations.
- Using orthographic representations can obscure true phonological correspondences and may capture script differences; phonetic transcriptions are recommended.
- Specific unclear points: the notion of the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” and details of the merging in the maximization step need explanation.
- Minor editorial fix: replace “focus in on” with “focus on” (line 440).
