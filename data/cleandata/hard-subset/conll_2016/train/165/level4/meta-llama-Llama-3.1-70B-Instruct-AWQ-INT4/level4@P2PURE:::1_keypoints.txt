- Proposes an MDL-based method to discover cross-language word correspondences by coding substring-matching rules and balancing model vs. data description.
- Models correspondences across Slavic languages and trains via the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm.
- Evaluated on 13 Slavic languages with distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and example correspondences.
- Motivation and formalization are sound; MDL is a reasonable framework and EM use is well-motivated.
- Some derivations in the paper are unclear and need clarification.
- Suggests empirical comparison to Bayesian phylogenetic methods (e.g., MrBayes) due to resemblance between MDL and Bayesian objectives.
- Missing comparisons to related work in borrowing/cognate detection and computational historical linguistics (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen, List & Moran).
- Notes available biological phylogeny tools (PAUP, MrBayes) that could be applied or compared.
- Alignment procedure suffers from memory/runtime bottleneck, limiting comparisons to at most five languages and hindering scalability.
- Recommends including more languages and exploring fixes for the scalability bottleneck.
- Using neighbor-joining for phylogenies has known disadvantages (e.g., manual rooting); more sophisticated tree methods should be considered.
- Unclear whether EM is run to convergence or uses another stopping criterion; needs specification.
- Combining cognate datasets with Swadesh lists may affect results and should be analyzed.
- Using orthographic data may obscure true correspondences and conflate script changes with linguistic change; phonetic transcriptions are preferable.
- Specific unclear technical points: the meaning of the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" and the merging step in the maximization are not well explained.
- Minor editorial issue: change "focus in on" to "focus on" (line 440).
- References to add/consider: List & Moran (2013) toolkit and Kitchen et al. (2009) Bayesian phylogenetic analysis.
