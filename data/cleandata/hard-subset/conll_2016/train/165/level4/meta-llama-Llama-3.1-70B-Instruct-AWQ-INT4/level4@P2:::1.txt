This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning in a number of Slavic languages. They develop codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that balances the description of the model and the data given the model.

The authors' motivation and formulation of the approach make sense. MDL seems like a reasonable tool to attack the problem, and the motivation for employing Expectation-Maximization (EM) is presented nicely. However, some of the derivations were not entirely clear.

**Main Contributions**

1. The authors propose an MDL-based approach for discovering correspondences between languages, which is a novel application of the MDL principle in the field of historical linguistics.
2. They develop a code for rules that match substrings in two or more languages, which allows for the efficient computation of description lengths.
3. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach on a set of 13 Slavic languages, showing that it can recover meaningful correspondences and phylogenetic relationships.

**Strengths**

1. The authors provide a clear and well-motivated introduction to the problem of discovering correspondences between languages.
2. The MDL-based approach is well-suited to the problem, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how it works.
3. The experimental results are impressive, and the authors provide a good analysis of the recovered correspondences and phylogenetic relationships.

**Weaknesses**

1. The authors do not provide a thorough comparison to other methods for borrowing and cognate detection or other computational methods for historical linguistics.
2. The alignment procedure has a major drawback, allowing the comparison of only 5 languages at most, which limits the scalability of the approach.
3. The authors use a simple neighbor-joining method for creating phylogenetic trees, which may not be the most sophisticated method available.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how you plan to deal with the memory/runtime bottleneck in the alignment procedure?
2. Have you considered using more sophisticated methods for creating phylogenetic trees, such as Bayesian inference or maximum likelihood estimation?
3. Can you clarify the stopping criterion for the EM algorithm?
4. How do you think the mixing of two datasets (cognates and non-cognates) might impact the results?
5. Have you considered working on the level of phonetic transcriptions instead of orthographic form to avoid hiding correspondences?
