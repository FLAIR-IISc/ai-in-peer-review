This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning in a number of Slavic languages, developing codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulating an MDL objective that balances the description of the model and the data given the model.

The model is trained with Expectation-Maximization (EM) and tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are presented through several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of found correspondences. The motivation and formulation of the approach make sense, and MDL appears to be a reasonable tool for tackling the problem. The authors' explanation for employing EM is also clear.

However, some of the derivations were not entirely clear, and a more detailed explanation would be beneficial. Additionally, the authors point out the resemblance of the MDL objective to Bayesian inference, which is reminiscent of the application of Bayesian inference in biological phylogenetic inference, such as using the MrBayes tool. An empirical comparison with these methods could be insightful.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The authors propose a novel method for discovering correspondences between languages using the MDL principle.
2. They develop an efficient algorithm for training the model using EM.
3. The approach is tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages, and the results are presented through various distance measures and a phylogenetic tree.

**Strengths:**

1. The approach is well-motivated, and the use of MDL is a reasonable choice for the problem.
2. The authors provide a clear explanation of the EM algorithm and its application to the problem.
3. The results are presented in a clear and concise manner, and the phylogenetic tree provides a useful visualization of the language relationships.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper lacks a comparison to other methods for borrowing and cognate detection or computational methods for historical linguistics.
2. The alignment procedure has a significant memory/runtime bottleneck, allowing for the comparison of only 5 languages at most.
3. The authors use a simple neighbor-joining method for creating the phylogenetic tree, which has known disadvantages.
4. The data is in orthographic form, which may hide many correspondences, especially in languages with different scripts.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How do you plan to address the memory/runtime bottleneck in the alignment procedure to allow for the comparison of more languages?
2. Have you considered using more sophisticated methods for creating the phylogenetic tree, such as Bayesian inference or maximum likelihood estimation?
3. Do you run EM until convergence or have some other stopping criterion?
4. How do you think the results would be impacted if the data were in phonetic transcription form instead of orthographic form?
5. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" and the merging done in the maximization step?
