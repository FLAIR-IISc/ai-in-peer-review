- Proposes an MDL-based method to discover correspondences between words with the same meaning across languages, applied to Slavic languages.  
- Models correspondences via codes for substring-matching rules and formulates an MDL objective balancing model complexity and data fit.  
- Trains the model with Expectation-Maximization (EM) and evaluates on 13 Slavic languages using distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and example correspondences.  
- Motivation and formulation are clear; using MDL and EM for this problem is well justified.  

Main contributions:
- Novel application of the MDL principle to historical linguistics for correspondence discovery.  
- Development of an efficient EM-based algorithm to find correspondences.  
- Empirical demonstration on 13 Slavic languages showing promising results.  

Strengths:
- Clear, well-motivated introduction to the problem and MDL approach.  
- Well-structured paper with detailed methodological exposition.  
- Promising results with a thoughtful discussion of implications.  

Weaknesses:
- No comparison to existing methods for borrowing, cognate detection, or other computational historical-linguistics approaches.  
- Alignment procedure is limited by memory/runtime, allowing comparison of at most 5 languages simultaneously.  
- Phylogenetic tree construction uses a simple method that may lack sophistication.  

Questions to authors:
- Have you compared your method to other borrowing/cognate-detection or computational historical-linguistics approaches?  
- How will you address the memory/runtime bottleneck in the alignment procedure to scale to more languages?  
- Can you provide more details on the merging operation in the EM maximization step?  
- Have you considered using phonetic transcriptions instead of orthographic forms to reveal hidden correspondences?
