This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning in a number of Slavic languages. They develop codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that balances the description of the model and the data given the model. The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are presented through several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of found correspondences.

The motivation and formulation of the approach are sound. MDL appears to be a reasonable tool to tackle the problem, and the motivation for employing EM is clearly presented. However, some of the derivations were not entirely clear to me.

The authors point out the resemblance of the MDL objective to Bayesian inference, which brings to mind the application of Bayesian inference in biological phylogenetic inference, such as using the MrBayes tool. An empirical comparison with these methods could be insightful.

Regarding related work, the authors could benefit from comparing their method to other approaches for borrowing and cognate detection, or other computational methods for historical linguistics. For example, the studies by Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen could be relevant. Although some of these methods may not have available tools to apply to the given dataset, mentioning List and Moran (2013) would be a good starting point. Additionally, relevant tools for biological phylogeny inference, such as PAUP and MrBayes, could also be explored.

The approach and methodology have some drawbacks. The alignment procedure, which allows for the comparison of only 5 languages at most, appears to be a major bottleneck due to memory and runtime constraints. It would be interesting to see how the authors plan to address this issue, especially when dealing with multiple languages and phylogenetic trees. Furthermore, using neighbor joining for creating phylogenetic trees is known to have disadvantages, such as requiring manual specification of the root. More sophisticated methods could be explored. Additionally, it is unclear whether the EM algorithm is run until convergence or if there is another stopping criterion.

The data used in the study also raises some concerns. The authors combine two datasets, one of cognates and one not necessarily (the Swadesh lists), which may impact the results. The data is in orthographic form, which might hide many correspondences, especially in languages with different scripts. This could lead to learned rules indicating changes in script rather than real linguistic correspondences. This shortcoming could be avoided by working on the level of phonetic transcriptions.

There are some unclear points in the paper. The concept of the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) is not well-explained. Additionally, the merging done in the maximization step was not entirely clear to me.

On a minor note, the phrase "focus in on" (line 440) should be replaced with "focus on".
