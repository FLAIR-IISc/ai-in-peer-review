

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering cross-linguistic correspondences among Slavic languages, modeling word-level correspondences through substring-matching rules. The authors formulate an MDL objective balancing model complexity and data fit, train via EM, and evaluate on 13 Slavic languages using distance measures, phylogenetic trees, and example correspondences. The core motivation and MDL formulation are sound, and the rationale for EM is well-justified. However, several technical and methodological concerns require clarification.

**Strengths:** The MDL framework is appropriately applied to model linguistic correspondences, and the connection to Bayesian inference (e.g., parallels with phylogenetic tools like MrBayes) is a thoughtful observation. The empirical validation via phylogenetic trees and concrete examples demonstrates practical utility.

**Major Concerns:**

1.  **Methodological Clarity & Scope:** The derivation of the MDL objective and the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) are unclear. A more precise definition is needed. The alignment procedure suffers from a severe memory/runtime bottleneck, limiting comparisons to only 5 languages. Given the paper's focus on phylogenetic trees (which inherently benefit from larger language sets), this constraint significantly limits the scope and impact. The authors should explicitly address how they plan to scale beyond 5 languages.

2.  **Phylogenetic Methodology:** Using neighbor joining for tree construction requires manual root specification, a known limitation. More robust methods (e.g., maximum likelihood with model selection, Bayesian inference) would strengthen the phylogenetic analysis and align better with the paper's Bayesian analogy. The paper should justify the choice of neighbor joining or adopt a more sophisticated alternative.

3.  **Data Handling & Potential Bias:** Mixing cognate data (Swadesh lists) with non-cognate data introduces ambiguity. The authors should discuss how this mixture might affect the learned rules and results. Crucially, using orthographic data risks conflating script changes (e.g., Cyrillic vs. Latin) with genuine linguistic correspondences. For instance, a rule matching "c" in one language and "s" in another might reflect script adaptation rather than phonological change. Working with phonetic transcriptions (e.g., IPA) would avoid this critical shortcoming and is standard practice in historical linguistics.

4.  **EM Implementation:** The stopping criterion for EM (e.g., convergence threshold, max iterations) is unspecified. Clarifying this is essential for reproducibility.

**Related Work:** The paper lacks comparison to key computational historical linguistics methods. Relevant works include:
*   Cognate detection/borrowing methods by Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen (e.g., Bayesian approaches for linguistic phylogenies).
*   Open-source toolkits like List & Moran (2013) for quantitative historical linguistics.
*   Biological phylogeny tools (e.g., PAUP*, MrBayes) which could be adapted or compared against, given the stated Bayesian analogy. A brief discussion of how this work relates to or differs from these established approaches is needed.

**Minor Issues:**
*   "focus in on" (line 440) → "focus on".
*   The rationale for excluding non-Slavic languages (beyond the scope of the current dataset) should be briefly clarified.

**Conclusion:** The paper presents a conceptually interesting MDL-based approach for cross-linguistic correspondence discovery. The core idea has merit, but significant methodological gaps and data limitations weaken the contribution. The orthographic data handling is a major flaw requiring correction, and the scalability bottleneck severely restricts the empirical validation. Addressing the clarity issues (MDL derivation, unigram definition), adopting more robust phylogenetic methods, and utilizing phonetic transcriptions are essential for a stronger paper. Comparison to the cited related work would also significantly strengthen the positioning. As currently presented, the results are limited in scope and potentially confounded by data artifacts. The authors should resolve these points before publication.
