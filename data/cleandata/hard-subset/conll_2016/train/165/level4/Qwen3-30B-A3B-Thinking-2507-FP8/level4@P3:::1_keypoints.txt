- MDL-based framework for discovering linguistic correspondences without distributional assumptions, enabling unbiased string-level similarity measures.
- EM-based algorithm that efficiently infers correspondences across multiple languages and quantifies similarities between all language subsets.
- Demonstrated on Slavic languages, recovering expected subgroups and providing detailed information-theoretic similarity analyses.

- Addresses a gap in historical linguistics by avoiding arbitrary assumptions and offering objective measures of similarity.
- Strong theoretical foundation with a clear MDL encoding scheme and transparent EM training procedure.
- Comprehensive experiments (pairwise and four-way) using NCD and phylogenetic trees to corroborate findings.

- Scalability is limited to about five languages due to memory and runtime bottlenecks, limiting applicability to larger families.
- Reliance on raw orthography (no phonetic transcription) risks conflating script differences (e.g., Latin vs Cyrillic) with linguistic correspondences.
- Lacks comparison to state-of-the-art computational methods (e.g., List, Nakhleh, Kitchen), making relative effectiveness unclear.
- Mixing Swadesh lists with non-cognate data is not adequately addressed and may confound results.

- How would the method handle languages with very different scripts (e.g., Arabic vs Latin)—are phonetic transcriptions required?
- What specific scaling challenges prevent going beyond five languages, and what strategies could address them?
- How was the mix of Swadesh and other data controlled, and how might mixed data types affect discovered correspondences?

- Title and abstract should be clearer about identifying statistically significant correspondences versus linguistically “important” ones.
- The approach is promising but needs fixes for computational and data-handling issues to be broadly useful.
