

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper proposes a Minimum Description Length (MDL)-based approach for discovering statistically significant linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without assuming a prior distribution of these correspondences. The authors develop a principled framework for modeling correspondences as associated character strings, avoiding the arbitrary assumptions common in existing methods. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are: (1) a novel MDL-based framework that provides an objective, distribution-free method for discovering linguistic correspondences; (2) an efficient algorithm enabling simultaneous analysis of multiple languages (not limited to pairwise comparisons), allowing for fine-grained quantification of linguistic similarities across language subsets; and (3) empirical validation on Slavic languages demonstrating the method's ability to recover meaningful linguistic patterns and produce linguistically interpretable results.

**Strengths**  
1. **Principled theoretical foundation**: The paper's use of MDL provides a statistically sound and objective approach to correspondence discovery, avoiding the arbitrary assumptions that plague many existing methods. The authors clearly articulate how their framework aligns with information-theoretic principles and avoids the pitfalls of prior assumptions about correspondence distributions. This theoretical grounding is a significant strength that sets the work apart from previous approaches.

2. **Multi-language analysis capability**: The method's ability to simultaneously analyze multiple languages (not just pairs) represents a substantial advancement. The paper demonstrates this by providing detailed information-theoretic quantification of similarities across all language subsets in a four-language analysis (Czech, Polish, Russian, Bulgarian), revealing subtle linguistic patterns that pairwise methods would miss. This capability significantly expands the scope of what can be achieved in computational historical linguistics.

3. **Empirical validation**: The experiments on Slavic languages provide compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness. The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values aligns well with established linguistic classifications, and the discovered correspondences (e.g., phonological rules like (g,h), orthographic patterns like (cz,č), and verb endings like (ć,t)) demonstrate the method's ability to uncover linguistically meaningful patterns. The paper also effectively shows how data over-weighting can adjust the level of detail in discovered correspondences.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of comparison to relevant computational methods**: The paper fails to compare its approach to existing computational methods for historical linguistics, such as cognate detection (e.g., List and Moran 2013, Bouchard-Côté et al., Kitchen et al.). Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the method offers meaningful improvements over current state-of-the-art approaches. The authors should address this gap to establish their method's relative contribution.

2. **Significant computational limitations**: The alignment procedure has severe memory and runtime bottlenecks, limiting the method to processing only up to five languages on standard hardware. Given the paper's focus on multi-language analysis, this constraint is a major drawback. The authors should discuss potential strategies for scaling to more languages (e.g., approximations, parallelization, or more efficient algorithms) and provide a clear path forward for handling larger language sets.

3. **Orthographic data limitations**: Using raw orthographic data without phonetic transcription risks conflating script differences with linguistic correspondences. For example, the Serbo-Croatian analysis (Latin vs. Cyrillic scripts) shows the method may be detecting script-related patterns rather than genuine linguistic ones. The authors should address how their method handles script differences and whether phonetic transcription would improve results.

4. **Methodological clarity**: Several key aspects of the algorithm remain unclear, particularly the merging process in the maximization step and the definition of "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules." These ambiguities make it difficult to fully evaluate the method's technical merits and could hinder reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How do you plan to address the computational bottleneck that limits the method to five languages? What specific strategies (e.g., algorithmic improvements, hardware requirements) would you propose to handle larger language sets?

2. Given that the method uses raw orthographic data, how would you handle languages with different scripts (e.g., Latin vs. Cyrillic) to ensure discovered correspondences reflect linguistic patterns rather than script differences?

3. In the four-way analysis, the paper identifies that Russian and Bulgarian share significant similarity. How does this align with established linguistic classifications of Slavic languages, and what additional evidence would support this finding?
