

This paper introduces a Minimum Description Length (MDL) approach for discovering cross-lingual correspondences among Slavic languages, modeling word-level correspondences based on shared meanings. The authors develop substring-matching rules across languages and formulate an MDL objective balancing model complexity and data fit. The method employs Expectation-Maximization (EM) for training and is evaluated on 13 Slavic languages using distance measures, phylogenetic trees, and example correspondences.  

The motivation for using MDL and EM is well-justified, and the connection to Bayesian inference (e.g., parallels with phylogenetic tools like MrBayes) is thought-provoking. However, the derivation of the MDL objective lacks clarity in several key steps, hindering full assessment of the method’s theoretical foundation. An empirical comparison to Bayesian phylogenetic frameworks would strengthen this analogy.  

**Related Work:** The paper omits critical comparisons to established methods in historical linguistics. Relevant works include Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen’s approaches to cognate detection and borrowing, as well as List and Moran’s (2013) toolkit for quantitative historical linguistics. While some methods may not directly apply to the dataset, acknowledging these tools would contextualize the contribution. Similarly, leveraging existing phylogenetic software (e.g., PAUP*, MrBayes) for comparative analysis would enhance rigor.  

**Methodology:**  
- **Scalability:** The alignment procedure’s computational bottleneck limits comparisons to ≤5 languages, contradicting the paper’s focus on phylogenetic analysis across 13 languages. The authors should address how to scale to larger language sets.  
- **Phylogenetic Methods:** Neighbor-joining for tree construction requires manual root specification, a known limitation. Adopting more robust methods (e.g., maximum likelihood or Bayesian inference) would improve validity.  
- **EM Implementation:** The stopping criterion for EM is unspecified—convergence or fixed iterations? Clarification is needed.  

**Data and Analysis:**  
- The mixed dataset (cognate-based and non-cognate Swadesh lists) risks conflating results. The authors should analyze whether this heterogeneity impacts correspondence accuracy.  
- Orthographic data obscures phonetic correspondences, particularly across scripts (e.g., Cyrillic vs. Latin). Rules learned may reflect script changes rather than linguistic patterns. Using phonetic transcriptions would mitigate this flaw.  

**Unclear Points:**  
- The "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) requires explicit definition.  
- The merging step in the EM maximization phase is ambiguous and needs elaboration.  

**Minor Issues:**  
- "focus in on" → "focus on" (line 440).  
- Ensure consistent terminology (e.g., "cognate" vs. "correspondence").  

**Overall:** The core idea is promising, but critical gaps in methodology, evaluation, and related work weaken the contribution. Addressing the scalability bottleneck, clarifying derivations, adopting phonetic data, and benchmarking against established tools would substantially strengthen the paper. The current work offers a novel MDL application but fails to fully integrate with the broader historical linguistics and phylogenetics communities.
