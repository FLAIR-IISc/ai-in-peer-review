

This paper proposes a method for discovering cross-lingual correspondences using Minimum Description Length (MDL). The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning across multiple Slavic languages, developing codes for rules matching substrings in two or more languages. They formulate an MDL objective that balances model description length and data description given the model. The model is trained via Expectation-Maximization (EM) and evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, with results presented through distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and example correspondences.

The motivation and formulation are sound. MDL is a reasonable approach for this problem, and the justification for using EM is well-presented. However, some derivations remain unclear.

The authors note the resemblance between the MDL objective and Bayesian inference, prompting a comparison to Bayesian methods in phylogenetic inference (e.g., using MrBayes). An empirical comparison with such methods would be insightful.

**Related Work:**  
- The paper lacks comparison to established methods for borrowing detection, cognate identification, or other computational historical linguistics approaches. Relevant studies include those by Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen. While some tools (e.g., List & Moran, 2013) may not directly apply to the dataset, their framework should be acknowledged. Additionally, tools from biological phylogenetics (e.g., PAUP*, MrBayes) could offer relevant comparisons.

**Approach and Methodology:**  
- **Alignment procedure:** The memory/runtime bottleneck severely limits the method to comparing at most five languages. Given the focus on phylogenetic trees with multiple languages, it would be valuable to see results with more languages. The authors should address how they plan to overcome this constraint.  
- **Phylogenetic tree:** Using neighbor-joining for tree construction has known limitations (e.g., requiring manual root specification). More sophisticated methods (e.g., maximum likelihood, Bayesian inference) should be considered.  
- **EM convergence:** The stopping criterion for EM (e.g., convergence vs. fixed iterations) is unspecified.

**Data:**  
- The mixed dataset combines cognate and non-cognate data (Swadesh lists). The impact of this mixture on results requires clarification.  
- Orthographic data may obscure linguistic correspondences, especially across languages with different scripts. Learned rules might reflect script changes rather than genuine linguistic patterns. This limitation could be mitigated by using phonetic transcriptions.

**Unclear Points:**  
- The phrase "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) requires clarification.  
- The merging step in the maximization phase is not fully clear.

**Minor Issue:**  
- "focus in on" (line 440) should be "focus on."
