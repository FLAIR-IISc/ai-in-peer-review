

Summary: This paper introduces an MDL-based approach for discovering objective, statistically significant correspondences between words across multiple languages without assuming predefined distributional patterns. The primary contribution is an efficient algorithm that infers correspondence rules directly from data, enabling analysis beyond pairwise language comparisons. Secondary contributions include a novel four-way linguistic similarity quantification method and a practical framework for incorporating linguistic knowledge via data weighting.

Strengths:  
1. **Novel MDL formulation for multi-language correspondences**: The authors effectively leverage MDL to avoid arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions, a key limitation in prior work. Their two-part code design (model + data-to-model) provides a statistically rigorous foundation, and the deterministic EM training ensures reproducibility—critical for linguistic analysis.  
2. **Demonstrated multi-language analysis**: The paper successfully quantifies similarities across all language subsets (not just pairs), revealing nuanced groupings (e.g., Russian-Bulgarian affinity in the four-way analysis). The phylogenetic tree based on NCDs aligns with established linguistic classifications, validating the method’s practical utility.  
3. **Practical flexibility via data weighting**: The proposed data over-weighting technique (α ≠ 1) allows linguists to control granularity without compromising statistical ordering. This bridges the gap between objective MDL results and expert-driven analysis needs, as shown in the CS-PL-RU-BG examples where over-weighting reveals finer phonological patterns.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Orthographic data obscures phonological patterns**: Using raw orthography (e.g., Cyrillic/Latin scripts) risks conflating script changes with linguistic correspondences. For instance, the algorithm may learn "c→č" as a rule where phonological reality involves vowel shifts obscured by orthographic conventions. This undermines the claim of "string-level linguistic similarity" since transcription-level artifacts dominate.  
2. **Scalability limitations**: The alignment procedure’s combinatorial complexity restricts analysis to ≤5 languages (Section 4.3), a significant bottleneck for large-scale linguistic studies. The authors note this as a future direction but provide no concrete mitigation strategy (e.g., approximate inference or parallelization), weakening the method’s applicability.  
3. **Insufficient comparison to historical linguistics tools**: The review lacks benchmarking against established computational methods (e.g., PhyloBayes for phylogenetics, List & Moran’s toolkit for cognate detection). Without such comparisons, it’s unclear whether the approach advances beyond existing tools or merely rephrases known techniques. The Bayesian analogy (Section 3) is underdeveloped without empirical validation.

Questions to Authors:  
1. How would phonetic transcriptions (e.g., IPA) alter the discovered correspondences, particularly for script-divergent pairs like Serbo-Croatian Latin/Cyrillic?  
2. What specific computational optimizations (beyond "future work") could extend the method to ≥6 languages without prohibitive runtime?  
3. Could the data weighting technique (α > 1) be formalized to incorporate linguistic priors (e.g., known sound laws) while retaining MDL’s objectivity?
