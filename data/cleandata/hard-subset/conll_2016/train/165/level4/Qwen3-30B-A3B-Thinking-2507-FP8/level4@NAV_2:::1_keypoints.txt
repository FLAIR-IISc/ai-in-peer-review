- Summary: Proposes an MDL-based framework to discover string-level correspondences across multiple languages, with an MDL formulation, a deterministic EM + shortest-path alignment algorithm, and empirical validation for multi-language similarity and phylogenetic/grouping analyses.

- Strength: MDL formulation removes arbitrary distributional assumptions, producing objectively grounded correspondence rules with linguistically plausible patterns (e.g., Slavic phonological/orthographic regularities).

- Strength: Method quantifies similarities for all subsets of languages (not just pairs), enabling fine-grained groupings (e.g., RU–BG vs CS–PL nuances in the four-language case study).

- Strength: Implementation is efficient for up to five languages on standard hardware and includes a principled data over-weighting mechanism to control detail and incorporate domain knowledge.

- Weakness: No comparisons to established baselines (e.g., List & Moran toolkit, Bouchard-Cote’s Bayesian models, MrBayes), leaving relative novelty and effectiveness unassessed.

- Weakness: Reliance on raw orthographic data rather than phonetic transcription risks conflating script differences (Latin vs Cyrillic) with genuine linguistic correspondences.

- Weakness: Alignment’s combinatorial bottleneck (≤5 languages) and manual root placement in neighbor-joining trees limit scalability and practical applicability; no scalability remedies discussed.

- Question: How do you distinguish orthographic/script conventions from true phonological/lexical evolution given script diversity in the data?

- Question: What is the rationale for using the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286) in the data code, and how does that choice affect the statistical interpretation of learned correspondences?
