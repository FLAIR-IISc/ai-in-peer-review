

This paper proposes a method for discovering cross-lingual correspondences using Minimum Description Length (MDL). The authors model correspondences between words sharing meaning across several Slavic languages, defining rules that match substrings across two or more languages. They formulate an MDL objective balancing model complexity and data fit under the model. The approach is trained via Expectation-Maximization (EM) and evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, with results presented through distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and illustrative correspondence examples.

The motivation and approach are well-motivated. MDL is a suitable framework for this problem, and the rationale for using EM is clearly articulated. However, some derivations remain unclear. The authors draw a parallel between their MDL objective and Bayesian inference, reminiscent of methods like MrBayes in phylogenetic analysis. An empirical comparison with such approaches would strengthen the evaluation.

**Related Work**  
The paper lacks comparison to established methods in historical linguistics, particularly for cognate detection and borrowing analysis. Relevant studies include those by Bouchard-Cote, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen (e.g., Kitchen et al., 2009). While some tools may not directly apply to the dataset, the framework of List and Moran (2013) provides a valuable benchmark. Additionally, tools from biological phylogeny (e.g., PAUP*, MrBayes) could offer comparative insights.

**Approach and Methodology**  
- **Alignment Procedure**: A significant limitation is the memory/runtime bottleneck, restricting comparisons to ≤5 languages. Given the focus on phylogenetic trees, extending this to more languages would be valuable. The authors should address potential strategies to overcome this constraint.  
- **Phylogenetic Tree Construction**: Using neighbor joining for tree inference requires manual root specification, a known limitation. More robust methods (e.g., maximum likelihood) could be considered.  
- **EM Convergence**: It is unclear whether EM runs to full convergence or employs an alternative stopping criterion.  

**Data**  
- **Mixed Datasets**: The evaluation combines cognate data (Swadesh lists) with non-cognate data. This conflation may affect results; the authors should clarify its impact.  
- **Orthographic Representation**: Using orthographic forms risks obscuring linguistic correspondences, particularly across languages with different scripts (e.g., Cyrillic vs. Latin). Learned rules may capture script variations rather than genuine linguistic patterns. This limitation could be mitigated by working with phonetic transcriptions.  

**Unclear Points**  
- Line 286: What constitutes the "optimal unigram for symbol usage across all rules"?  
- The merging process in the maximization step is not fully explained.  

**Minor Issue**  
- Line 440: "focus in on" → "focus on."
