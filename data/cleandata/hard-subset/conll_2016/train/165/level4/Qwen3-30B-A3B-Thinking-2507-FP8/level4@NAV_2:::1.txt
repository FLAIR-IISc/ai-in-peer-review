

Summary: This paper introduces an MDL-based framework for objectively discovering string-level correspondences across multiple languages, with contributions ordered by strength: (1) a statistically grounded MDL formulation that avoids distributional assumptions and enables unbiased linguistic similarity quantification; (2) a deterministic EM algorithm for learning correspondence rules directly from data, with efficient alignment via shortest-path computation; (3) empirical validation demonstrating multi-language similarity quantification beyond pairwise analysis, including phylogenetic reconstruction and subset-level linguistic grouping.  

Strengths:  
1. The MDL approach provides a theoretically sound alternative to existing methods by eliminating arbitrary distributional assumptions, yielding objective correspondence rules verified through linguistic plausibility (e.g., phonological/orthographic patterns in Slavic languages).  
2. The algorithm’s ability to quantify similarities across *all subsets* of languages (not just pairs) enables unprecedented granularity in linguistic analysis, as evidenced by the four-language CS-PL-RU-BG case study revealing nuanced groupings (e.g., RU-BG similarity exceeding CS-PL in some contexts).  
3. The implementation’s efficiency for up to five languages (with runtime feasibility on standard hardware) and the principled data over-weighting technique for controlling detail level offer practical utility for linguists, particularly when integrating domain knowledge.  

Weaknesses:  
1. Critical omission of comparative baselines against established computational historical linguistics methods (e.g., List & Moran’s toolkit, Bouchard-Cote’s Bayesian models, or phylogenetic tools like MrBayes), hindering assessment of the method’s relative novelty and efficacy.  
2. Use of raw orthographic data (without phonetic transcription) risks conflating script-related changes (e.g., Latin vs. Cyrillic) with genuine linguistic correspondences, as noted in the paper’s own example (Serbo-Croatian script effects in Table 2).  
3. The alignment procedure’s combinatorial bottleneck (limiting to ≤5 languages) and manual root placement in neighbor-joining trees present significant practical barriers for broader linguistic applications, with no discussion of scalability solutions.  

Questions to Authors:  
1. How do you address the risk that orthographic rules (e.g., (z,š) in Table 3) may reflect script conventions rather than phonological/lexical evolution, especially given Slavic languages’ script diversity?  
2. What is the exact rationale for using the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) in the data code, and how does this choice impact the statistical interpretation of learned correspondences?
