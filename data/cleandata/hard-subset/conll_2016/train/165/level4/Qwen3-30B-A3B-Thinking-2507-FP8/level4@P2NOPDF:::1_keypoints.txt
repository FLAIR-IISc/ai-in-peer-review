- Proposes an MDL-based method to discover word-level cross-lingual correspondences among 13 Slavic languages using substring-matching rules and an EM training procedure.  
- Motivation for using MDL and EM is well-justified and interestingly related to Bayesian phylogenetic tools.  
- Derivation of the MDL objective is unclear in key steps, preventing full assessment of the theoretical foundation.  
- Empirical comparison to Bayesian phylogenetic frameworks (e.g., MrBayes) is lacking and would strengthen the claimed analogy.  
- Paper omits critical related work from historical linguistics (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen, List & Moran) and should acknowledge/compare to these methods.  
- Fails to leverage or compare against existing phylogenetic software (PAUP*, MrBayes), reducing methodological rigor.  
- Alignment procedure has a computational bottleneck limiting comparisons to ≤5 languages, contradicting evaluation on 13 languages—scalability must be addressed.  
- Use of neighbor-joining for tree construction is limited (requires manual rooting); maximum likelihood or Bayesian methods would be more robust.  
- EM implementation details are incomplete—stopping criterion (convergence vs fixed iterations) is unspecified.  
- Mixed dataset combining cognate-based and non-cognate Swadesh lists may conflate results; impact of heterogeneity should be analyzed.  
- Using orthographic forms (including mixed scripts) obscures phonetic correspondences and may reflect script differences rather than true linguistic patterns; phonetic transcriptions are recommended.  
- The term "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" is undefined and needs explicit clarification.  
- The merging step in the EM maximization phase is ambiguous and requires elaboration.  
- Minor issues: wording error ("focus in on" → "focus on") and inconsistent terminology ("cognate" vs "correspondence").  
- Overall: promising novel MDL application but weakened by gaps in methodology, evaluation, scalability, and integration with historical linguistics and phylogenetics; addressing these would substantially strengthen the contribution.
