

# Review for "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between words across multiple languages. The authors model correspondences as associated character strings without assuming a specific distribution, and develop an efficient algorithm for inferring these correspondences directly from data. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. A principled MDL framework for discovering linguistic correspondences without imposing arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions, enabling objective, unbiased string-level measures of linguistic similarity.
2. An efficient EM-based algorithm that can handle multiple languages simultaneously, allowing for the quantification of similarities between all subsets of analyzed languages.
3. A thorough demonstration of the approach on Slavic languages, showing it successfully identifies linguistic subgroups and quantifies similarities in a detailed, information-theoretic manner.

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a significant gap in historical linguistics research: discovering correspondences across multiple languages without arbitrary assumptions. The MDL approach is well-suited for this task, as it avoids making prior assumptions about the nature of linguistic correspondences, which is a major limitation of existing statistical methods. The authors convincingly argue that their approach provides a more objective measure of linguistic similarity.

2. The theoretical foundation is strong, with a well-structured MDL-based encoding scheme that carefully considers both the model description length and the data description length. The paper provides clear explanations of the encoding procedures and the EM training process, making the approach accessible to researchers in computational linguistics.

3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-executed. The pairwise analysis successfully recovers expected linguistic groupings (e.g., west, east, and south Slavic languages), and the four-way analysis demonstrates the unique capability of the approach to quantify similarities across all language subsets simultaneously. The use of NCD and phylogenetic trees provides multiple complementary perspectives on language similarity.

## Weaknesses
1. The computational limitations are a major concern. The approach can only handle up to five languages due to memory and runtime bottlenecks, as stated in Section 4.3. This severely restricts the applicability of the method to larger language families. The authors do not adequately address how to overcome this limitation, which is a significant drawback for practical applications in historical linguistics where larger language sets are often of interest.

2. The use of raw orthographic data without phonetic transcriptions is problematic, especially for Slavic languages that use both Latin and Cyrillic scripts. As noted in Section 5.1, the paper's data includes Serbo-Croatian in both Latin and Cyrillic script, yet the approach treats these as separate languages. This may lead to rules that reflect script differences rather than true linguistic correspondences, undermining the validity of the results. A more robust approach would require phonetic transcriptions to capture the underlying linguistic patterns.

3. The paper lacks meaningful comparison to existing computational methods in historical linguistics. While the authors cite some related work, they do not compare their approach to state-of-the-art methods for cognate detection or linguistic correspondence discovery (e.g., methods by List, Nakhleh, or Kitchen). Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess the relative strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

4. The data mixing issue is not adequately addressed. The paper uses both Swadesh lists (designed for linguistic comparison) and non-cognate data, which may confound the results. The authors should clarify how they ensure the data is appropriate for the task of discovering linguistic correspondences, as mixing different types of data could lead to misleading patterns.

## Questions to Authors
1. How would your approach handle languages with significantly different writing systems (e.g., Arabic vs. Latin script)? Would phonetic transcriptions be necessary for meaningful results?

2. The paper states that the approach can handle "up to five languages" due to computational constraints. What specific scaling challenges do you anticipate when extending to larger language sets, and what strategies might address these challenges?

3. The paper uses both Swadesh lists and non-cognate data. How did you ensure that the data was appropriate for discovering linguistic correspondences, and how might mixing different data types affect the results?

## Additional Comments
The paper's title and abstract could be more precise in describing the scope of the work. The phrase "automatically discover the most important correspondences" could be clarified to specify that the approach identifies correspondences based on statistical significance rather than "importance" as defined by linguistic expertise.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational historical linguistics by providing a principled method for discovering linguistic correspondences across multiple languages. While the computational limitations and data handling issues are significant concerns, the theoretical framework and experimental results demonstrate the potential of the approach. With addressing these weaknesses, this work could become a valuable resource for historical linguists.
