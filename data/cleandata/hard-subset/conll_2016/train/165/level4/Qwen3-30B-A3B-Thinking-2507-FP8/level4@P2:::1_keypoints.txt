- Proposes an MDL-based framework to discover linguistic correspondences without assuming a prior distribution, modeling correspondences as associated character strings.

- Contribution: novel, objective MDL framework for distribution-free correspondence discovery.

- Contribution: algorithm enabling simultaneous multi-language analysis (beyond pairwise), quantifying similarities across language subsets.

- Contribution: empirical validation on Slavic languages showing recovery of meaningful, linguistically interpretable patterns.

- Strength: principled theoretical foundation—MDL gives a statistically sound, assumption-light approach.

- Strength: multi-language analysis capability uncovers subtle subset similarities that pairwise methods miss.

- Strength: empirical results (phylogenetic alignment, phonological/orthographic/inflectional correspondences) support method effectiveness.

- Weakness: lacks comparisons to existing computational historical-linguistic methods (e.g., cognate detection), so relative improvement is unclear.

- Weakness: significant computational/memory bottlenecks limit analyses to about five languages on standard hardware; scalability is a major concern.

- Weakness: reliance on raw orthography risks conflating script differences with true linguistic correspondences (e.g., Latin vs. Cyrillic).

- Weakness: methodological details (merging in maximization, definition of “optimal unigram for symbol usages”) are unclear and hinder reproducibility.

- Question: how will you address the computational bottleneck to scale beyond five languages (algorithmic, approximation, parallelization, hardware)?

- Question: how will you handle different scripts to ensure correspondences reflect linguistic, not orthographic, patterns—would phonetic transcription be used?

- Question: how does the Russian–Bulgarian similarity found align with established Slavic classifications and what additional evidence could support this result?
