

This paper proposes a method for discovering cross-linguistic correspondences using Minimum Description Length (MDL). The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning across multiple Slavic languages, developing codes for rules that match substrings across two or more languages. They formulate an MDL objective that balances the description length of the model and the data under the model. The model is trained via the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, with results presented through distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of discovered correspondences.  

The motivation and formulation of the approach are well-motivated. MDL is a reasonable choice for this problem, and the rationale for using EM is clearly articulated. I note, however, that some derivations remain unclear. The authors draw a parallel between the MDL objective and Bayesian inference, which recalls applications in biological phylogenetic inference (e.g., using MrBayes). An empirical comparison with such methods could provide valuable insights.  

**Related Work**  
The paper lacks comparison to established methods for borrowing detection, cognate identification, or other computational approaches in historical linguistics. Relevant studies include those by Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen (e.g., Kitchen et al., 2009). While some tools may not directly apply to the dataset, the work of List and Moran (2013) should be cited as a relevant open-source toolkit. Additionally, methods from biological phylogeny inference (e.g., PAUP, MrBayes) could be compared.  

**Approach and Methodology**  
- **Alignment procedure**: The memory/runtime bottleneck restricts comparisons to at most five languages. Given the focus on phylogenetic trees and multiple languages, it would be valuable to include more languages. The authors should address how they plan to overcome this limitation.  
- **Phylogenetic tree**: Using neighbor-joining for tree construction is known to have disadvantages (e.g., requiring manual root specification). More sophisticated methods (e.g., maximum likelihood or Bayesian approaches) should be considered.  
- **EM convergence**: The paper does not specify whether EM runs to convergence or employs an alternative stopping criterion.  

**Data**  
- The dataset combines cognate and non-cognate lists (Swadesh lists), raising questions about potential bias in results. The authors should address how this mixture might affect evaluation.  
- Data is presented in orthographic form, which may obscure linguistic correspondences—particularly in languages with divergent scripts. Learned rules could conflate script changes with genuine linguistic patterns. Using phonetic transcriptions would mitigate this limitation.  

**Unclear Points**  
- The phrase "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286) requires clarification.  
- The merging procedure in the maximization step is not fully explained.  

**Minor Issues**  
- "focus in on" (line 440) → "focus on".
