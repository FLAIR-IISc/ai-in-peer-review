- Proposes an MDL-based method to discover word-level substring-matching correspondences among Slavic languages, trained via EM and evaluated with distance measures, phylogenetic trees, and example rules.
- MDL formulation and EM rationale are conceptually sound, and the connection to Bayesian inference is a useful observation.
- Empirical validation shows practical utility through phylogenetic trees and example correspondences.
- Derivation of the MDL objective and the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” is unclear and needs a precise definition.
- Alignment algorithm has severe memory/runtime bottlenecks, limiting experiments to 5 languages and restricting the study’s scope and impact.
- Phylogenetic analysis uses neighbor joining with manual rooting; more robust methods (e.g., maximum likelihood or Bayesian inference) should be justified or adopted.
- Mixing cognate (Swadesh) and non-cognate data may bias learned rules and should be discussed.
- Using orthographic data risks conflating script differences (Cyrillic vs Latin) with genuine linguistic correspondences; phonetic (IPA) transcriptions are recommended.
- EM implementation details, particularly the stopping criterion (convergence threshold, max iterations), are unspecified and hurt reproducibility.
- The paper lacks comparison to key related work in computational historical linguistics and phylogenetics (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen; List & Moran; PAUP*, MrBayes).
- Minor issues: typo “focus in on” → “focus on,” and the rationale for excluding non-Slavic languages should be briefly clarified.
- Conclusion: conceptually interesting MDL approach but significant methodological gaps (MDL clarity, orthographic data, scalability, phylogenetic methods, related-work comparison) must be addressed before publication.
