This paper introduces a Minimum Description Length (MDL)-based method for identifying cross-linguistic correspondences. The authors model relationships between words with shared meaning across a number of Slavic languages, developing codes to represent substring matches spanning two or more languages and formulating an MDL objective to balance model complexity and data fit. The model is trained using Expectation-Maximization (EM) and evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, with results presented through distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and illustrative correspondence examples.

The motivation behind the approach is sound and the application of MDL appears well-justified for this problem. The rationale for employing EM is also clearly presented. However, some of the mathematical derivations lacked complete clarity.

The authors note a connection between their MDL objective and Bayesian inference, naturally prompting consideration of established Bayesian phylogenetic methods – such as those implemented in tools like MrBayes – and an empirical comparison to such approaches could offer valuable insights.

Regarding related work, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of existing methods for cognate detection, borrowing identification, and other computational techniques used in historical linguistics. While acknowledging practical limitations, referencing studies by Bouchard-Cote, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen, as well as List & Moran (2013), would strengthen the context. Furthermore, acknowledging relevant tools from biological phylogeny (e.g., PAUP, MrBayes) would be beneficial.

Concerning the approach and methodology:

*   **Alignment Procedure:** The computational limitations of the alignment procedure, allowing comparisons of only up to 5 languages, present a significant drawback, particularly given the phylogenetic application which benefits from inclusion of more languages. Exploring potential solutions to this bottleneck would be of interest.
*   **Phylogenetic Tree:** The use of neighbor-joining for phylogenetic tree construction is known to have limitations, including the need for manual root specification. Investigating more sophisticated methods could improve the robustness of the resulting trees.
*   **EM Convergence:** Clarification of the stopping criterion for the EM algorithm (convergence or another metric) is needed.

Regarding the data:

*   **Dataset Composition:** The combination of a cognate dataset and a Swadesh list (which may not strictly represent cognates) raises concerns about potential impact on the results. Investigating this effect would be valuable.
*   **Orthographic Form:** The use of orthographic data, rather than phonetic transcriptions, may obscure underlying linguistic correspondences, especially across languages with different writing systems. The learned rules might reflect script changes rather than genuine linguistic relationships. Working with phonetic transcriptions could mitigate this issue. 

Finally, there are a few points requiring clarification:

*   The meaning of “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286) is unclear.
*   The merging process in the maximization step requires further explanation.

A minor editorial note: “focus in on” should be “focus on” (line 440).
