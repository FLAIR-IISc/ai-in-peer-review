This paper introduces a method for cross-lingual correspondence discovery based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words with shared meaning across a number of Slavic languages, developing codes for rules that identify matching substrings in two or more languages and formulating an MDL objective function to balance model complexity and data description. The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and evaluated on a dataset comprising 13 Slavic languages, with results presented through various distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of discovered correspondences. 

The motivation for this approach is sound, and the use of MDL appears to be a suitable strategy for this problem. The justification for employing EM is also well presented. However, some of the mathematical derivations lacked complete clarity.

The authors note the similarity between their MDL objective and Bayesian inference, prompting a natural connection to Bayesian phylogenetic inference methods used in biology – for example, with tools like MrBayes. An empirical comparison against such methods would be potentially very insightful.

Related Work:
The paper lacks comparison to existing methods for borrowing detection, cognate identification, or other computational techniques within historical linguistics. Relevant studies by Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen should be discussed. While the practical applicability of some tools to this specific dataset may be limited, the work of List and Moran (2013) is readily comparable and should be acknowledged. Furthermore, relevant tools from biological phylogeny inference – such as paup and MrBayes – could also be considered.

Approach and Methodology:
The alignment procedure appears to be a significant computational bottleneck, currently limiting comparisons to a maximum of 5 languages. Given the ambition of constructing phylogenetic trees incorporating multiple languages, exploring solutions to this scalability issue would be valuable. I am interested in the authors’ thoughts on this challenge. 
The use of neighbor joining for phylogenetic tree construction is known to have limitations, including the need for manual root specification. Investigating more sophisticated phylogenetic methods would be beneficial.
It is unclear whether the EM algorithm is run until convergence or if a different stopping criterion is employed.

Data:
The datasets combine cognates with potentially non-cognate data (Swadesh lists). The potential impact of this mixture on the results should be addressed.
The use of orthographic data may obscure underlying correspondences, especially in languages with different writing systems. The learned rules may reflect script changes rather than true linguistic relationships. Performing the analysis on phonetic transcriptions could mitigate this issue and lead to more accurate linguistic correspondence discovery.

Unclear Points:
The meaning of “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286) is unclear.
The merging step within the maximization step was not fully understood.

Minor Issue:
“focus in on” should be “focus on” (line 440).
