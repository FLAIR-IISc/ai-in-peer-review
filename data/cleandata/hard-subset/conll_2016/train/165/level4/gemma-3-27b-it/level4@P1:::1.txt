This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on MDL. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning in a number of Slavic languages. They develop codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that balances the description of the model and the data given the model. The model is trained with EM and tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are shown using several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of found correspondences.

The motivation and formulation of the approach are sound. MDL seems like a reasonable tool to tackle this problem, and the motivation for employing EM is presented clearly. While the derivations were not entirely clear to me in all details, the core ideas are well-explained.

The authors point out the resemblance of the MDL objective to Bayesian inference, which naturally leads one to consider comparisons with Bayesian approaches in (biological) phylogenetic inference, such as those implemented in tools like MrBayes. An empirical comparison could provide valuable insights.

**Contributions:** The primary contribution of this work is the application of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to the problem of discovering multi-lingual correspondences without strong prior assumptions. This approach allows for the identification of relationships between any subset of languages, providing a more flexible and potentially insightful analysis than methods restricted to pairwise comparisons. A second, notable contribution lies in the efficient deterministic algorithm developed for inferring these correspondences directly from data. Finally, the experimental evaluation on a set of Slavic languages demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the method, revealing non-trivial associations and enabling both quantitative and qualitative analysis of linguistic similarities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of MDL:** Applying the MDL principle to multi-lingual correspondence discovery is a novel and promising approach. Unlike methods reliant on predefined assumptions about correspondence shapes or distributions, this work allows the data to guide the discovery process, potentially uncovering unexpected relationships.
2. **Efficient Algorithm:** The authors have developed an efficient, deterministic algorithm capable of handling multiple languages simultaneously. This is a significant advantage over approaches limited to pairwise comparisons, which become computationally intractable as the number of languages increases. The described EM approach and optimization strategies for alignment are well-motivated.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation using NCD, phylogenetic trees, and detailed example correspondences provides a thorough assessment of the method’s performance. The observation that the method captures expected linguistic groupings (West Slavic, East Slavic, South Slavic) strengthens the validity of the results.
4. **Detailed Analysis & Quantifiable Results:** The method goes beyond simply identifying correspondences; it provides a quantifiable measure of linguistic similarity through shared description length. This allows for a more nuanced understanding of the relationships between languages, going beyond simple phylogenetic trees.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:** The reported memory/runtime bottleneck limiting comparisons to a maximum of five languages is a significant drawback, especially given the stated goal of analyzing relationships across multiple languages. This limits the applicability of the method to larger language families. Further optimization or alternative algorithmic strategies are needed to address this issue.
2. **Methodological Choices:** The use of neighbor joining for constructing the phylogenetic tree is a potentially suboptimal choice, as this method has known limitations and requires manual root specification. Exploring more sophisticated phylogenetic methods might yield more robust and interpretable results. The stopping criterion for the EM algorithm could be further clarified.
3. **Data Considerations:** The combination of Swadesh lists and other cognates in the dataset could introduce inconsistencies and potentially bias the results. A clearer justification for this data combination, or a separate analysis using each dataset individually, would be beneficial. Furthermore, reliance on orthographic form alone may obscure underlying phonetic correspondences, particularly across languages with different scripts. Transcribing the data phonetically could improve accuracy and reveal more nuanced relationships.
4. **Limited Comparison to Existing Work:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to established methods in computational historical linguistics, such as those developed by Bouchard-Cote, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen, or tools like List and Moran (2013). While the authors acknowledge these methods, a more detailed discussion of their strengths and weaknesses in relation to the proposed approach would strengthen the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What strategies are being considered to address the scalability limitations of the alignment procedure, allowing for the analysis of a larger number of languages?
2. Could you elaborate on the stopping criterion for the EM algorithm? Is it based on a convergence threshold or a fixed number of iterations?
3. How sensitive are the results to the inclusion of both Swadesh lists and other cognates in the dataset? Have you considered performing the analysis separately for each data source?
4. Have you explored the use of phonetic transcriptions instead of orthographic forms as input to the algorithm? If so, what impact did this have on the results?
5. Could you clarify what is meant by the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286)?

**Additional Comments:**

- The phrase “focus in on” should be corrected to “focus on” (line 440).
- The reference to List and Moran (2013) is helpful and should be included in the full version of the paper.
- It would be valuable to include a discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly in relation to languages with complex morphological systems or significant sound changes.
