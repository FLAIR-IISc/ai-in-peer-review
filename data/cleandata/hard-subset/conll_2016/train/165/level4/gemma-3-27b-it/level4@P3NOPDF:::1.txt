This paper presents a novel approach to discovering cross-linguistic correspondences between Slavic languages, leveraging Minimum Description Length (MDL) principles. The authors model correspondences between words sharing common meanings, developing a rule-based system to match substrings across languages and formulating an MDL objective to balance model complexity and data fit. The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and evaluated on a dataset encompassing 13 Slavic languages, with results presented through various distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and illustrative examples of discovered correspondences.

The motivation for this work is clear and the chosen approach – employing MDL to identify linguistic relationships – is well-justified. The rationale for utilizing EM for model training is also presented convincingly. However, certain derivations within the paper could benefit from further clarification to enhance understanding. 

The authors rightly note the connection between their MDL objective and Bayesian inference. Given this parallel, and the successful application of Bayesian phylogenetic methods in biological contexts (e.g., using tools like MrBayes), an empirical comparison to such methods would be a valuable addition. This would allow for a more robust assessment of the proposed MDL-based approach and highlight potential strengths and weaknesses relative to established techniques.

The related work section would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of existing methods for cognate detection, borrowing identification, and other computational approaches in historical linguistics. While the authors acknowledge challenges in applying certain tools to their specific dataset, mentioning relevant work by researchers such as Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen would provide essential context. Furthermore, inclusion of List and Moran (2013) and consideration of biological phylogeny inference tools (e.g., PAUP*, MrBayes) would demonstrate a broader awareness of the existing landscape. 

Regarding the approach and methodology, the computational limitations of the alignment procedure represent a significant constraint. The current implementation appears to restrict comparisons to a maximum of five languages, which limits the scalability of the method, particularly given the intended application to phylogenetic reconstruction involving multiple languages. Exploring strategies to overcome this memory/runtime bottleneck would be a crucial next step.

The use of neighbor joining for constructing the phylogenetic tree is a point of concern. Neighbor joining is known to have limitations, notably the requirement for manual root specification. Investigating more sophisticated phylogenetic methods, which address these drawbacks, would likely yield more reliable and informative results. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a clarification of the EM algorithm’s stopping criterion – is it run until convergence, or is a different criterion employed?

The composition of the dataset, which combines cognates and potentially non-cognate Swadesh lists, raises questions regarding potential biases in the results. The authors should discuss how this mixture might influence their findings. Furthermore, the reliance on orthographic forms, especially across languages with diverse scripts, may obscure underlying linguistic correspondences. The observed rules might reflect script variation rather than true linguistic changes. Addressing this limitation through the use of phonetic transcriptions would strengthen the validity and generalizability of the learned correspondences.

Several points require further elaboration. The meaning of “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286) is unclear and requires clarification. Similarly, the merging process in the maximization step warrants a more detailed explanation.

Finally, a minor stylistic note: “focus in on” should be corrected to “focus on” (line 440).

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to the field of computational historical linguistics. While the core ideas are sound and the motivation is clear, addressing the concerns raised regarding scalability, phylogenetic methodology, dataset composition, and clarity of explanations will significantly enhance the impact and robustness of this work.
