- Introduces an MDL-based method for discovering multi-language correspondences, applied to Slavic languages.  
- Models correspondences as matching substrings and formulates an MDL objective balancing model complexity and data fit.  
- Trains the model with an EM algorithm and evaluates via distance measures, phylogenetic trees, and inspection of learned correspondences.  
- Motivation for MDL and rationale for EM are clear, but several derivations and implementation details need clarification.  
- Main contribution: applying MDL to discover multi-language correspondences without strong prior assumptions.  
- Secondary contribution: demonstration on Slavic languages with quantitative/qualitative insights and released code.  

Strengths:
- Novel application of MDL offers a statistically grounded, succinctness-based criterion for correspondences.  
- Multi-language capability enables analyses beyond pairwise comparisons (demonstrated four-way analysis).  
- Results generally align with established linguistic knowledge (e.g., Czech–Polish, Russian–Bulgarian patterns).  

Weaknesses:
- Computational scalability: memory and runtime bottlenecks limit analysis to ~5 languages.  
- Methodological choices: neighbor joining for phylogenetics is suboptimal; no comparison to Bayesian/ML methods; EM stopping criterion unclear.  
- Data concerns: mixing Swadesh lists with other cognates may bias results; using orthographic forms without phonetic transcription risks capturing script differences rather than true correspondences.  
- Limited related-work discussion: omits key computational-historical-linguistics and Bayesian phylogenetics literature and tools.  

Key questions for authors:
- What strategies will address the computational bottleneck to scale to more languages?  
- Why was neighbor joining chosen, and will you compare results to Bayesian or ML phylogenetic methods?  
- What is the EM stopping criterion?  
- How does combining Swadesh lists with other cognate data affect results, and have alternative preprocessing strategies been explored?  
- What does “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” mean and how is it determined?  
- Please clarify the merging step in the M-step and how it improves the model.  

Additional comments:
- Paper needs a more detailed discussion of limitations and future research directions.  
- Minor grammatical fix: change “focus in on” to “focus on.”  
- Include more references to relevant tools and approaches (e.g., List & Moran, MrBayes, paup) to strengthen contextualization.
