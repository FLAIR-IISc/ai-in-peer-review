This paper presents a novel method for discovering cross-linguistic correspondences using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words with shared meanings across a range of Slavic languages. They define codes representing rules that identify matching substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective function to balance model complexity and data description. The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and evaluated on a dataset comprising 13 Slavic languages, with results presented via distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and illustrative examples of identified correspondences.

The motivation behind the approach is sound, and the application of MDL appears to be a reasonable strategy for tackling this problem. The justification for employing the EM algorithm is also well-articulated. However, some of the mathematical derivations lacked complete clarity.

A particularly interesting avenue for future work would be an empirical comparison with established Bayesian inference methods, especially given the authors’ observation of a connection between the MDL objective and Bayesian principles. This is a standard approach in (biological) phylogenetic inference – tools like MrBayes could provide a valuable benchmark.

**Related Work:** The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work. It currently lacks comparison to existing methods for cognate detection, borrowing identification, and other computational techniques used in historical linguistics. While acknowledging the potential unavailability of tools for direct application to the given dataset, the authors should at least mention relevant studies such as those by Bouchard-Cote, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen. List and Moran (2013) offer an accessible and openly available toolkit for quantitative historical linguistics that warrants discussion. Furthermore, mentioning relevant tools from biological phylogeny (e.g., PAUP*, MrBayes) would demonstrate a broader awareness of existing methodologies.

**Approach and Methodology:** A significant limitation of the current approach appears to be the computational cost, specifically the memory and runtime bottleneck that restricts comparisons to a maximum of five languages at a time. Given the goal of constructing phylogenetic trees encompassing multiple languages, addressing this limitation is crucial. It would be valuable to understand the authors’ plans for scaling the method to accommodate larger language groups.

The choice of neighbor-joining for phylogenetic tree construction is also worth reconsidering. Neighbor-joining is known to have drawbacks, including the need for manual root specification. Investigating more sophisticated phylogenetic methods might yield more robust and accurate results. 

Further clarification is needed regarding the EM algorithm’s termination condition. Is it run until convergence, or is a different stopping criterion employed?

**Data:** The combination of cognate datasets and Swadesh lists raises a potential concern. The authors should discuss how this mixture might influence the results and whether the differing data qualities have been considered. 

The use of orthographic data, while practical, may obscure genuine linguistic correspondences, particularly in languages with different writing systems. The learned rules could potentially reflect script variations rather than true linguistic relationships. A potential improvement would be to perform the analysis on phonetic transcriptions to mitigate this issue.

**Unclear Points:** 

*   Could the authors elaborate on the meaning of "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286)?
*   The merging process described in the maximization step requires further clarification.

**Minor Issue:**

*   Replace "focus in on" with "focus on" (line 440).
