This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on MDL. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning in a number of Slavic languages. They develop codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that balances the description of the model and the data given the model. The model is trained with EM and tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are shown by several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of found correspondences. 

The primary contribution of this work is the application of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to the problem of discovering multi-lingual correspondences, coupled with an efficient algorithm for doing so. While statistical methods have been applied to cognate identification and reconstruction, this work tackles the broader problem of identifying correspondences across multiple languages without imposing strong distributional assumptions. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating the practical applicability of this approach to Slavic languages, providing both quantitative (NCD-based phylogenetic trees) and qualitative (example correspondences) analyses. Finally, the exploration of data over-weighting as a mechanism for controlling the granularity of discovered correspondences is a valuable addition.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novel Application of MDL:** The use of MDL for discovering correspondences is a conceptually sound and novel approach. MDL provides a statistically grounded framework for balancing model complexity and data fit, addressing a key challenge in this area. This is particularly valuable as it avoids the arbitrary assumptions often imposed by other methods.
2.  **Multi-lingual Capability:** The method's ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a significant strength. Existing approaches frequently focus on pairwise comparisons, limiting their ability to capture broader patterns of linguistic similarity. The presented four-way analysis exemplifies this benefit, offering a richer understanding of relationships among languages.
3.  **Clear Presentation of the Algorithm:** The paper provides a detailed explanation of the algorithm, including the Expectation-Maximization (EM) steps and the design of the MDL code. This clarity allows for a comprehensive understanding of the method's inner workings and facilitates potential implementation and extension.
4. **Demonstrated Effectiveness:** The experiments on Slavic languages demonstrate the practical effectiveness of the approach. The generated phylogenetic tree aligns with known linguistic classifications, and the example correspondences reveal linguistically plausible patterns. The exploration of data over-weighting provides a useful control mechanism for the level of detail in the discovered correspondences.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Limitation:** The reported memory and runtime bottlenecks limit the application of the method to a maximum of five languages. This is a significant drawback, particularly given the potential benefits of analyzing larger language groups. Addressing this limitation is crucial for broader applicability.
2. **Methodological Choices:** The use of neighbor-joining for phylogenetic tree construction is a potential weakness. Neighbor-joining is known to be sensitive to long-branch attraction and requires manual root specification. More sophisticated phylogenetic methods (e.g., Bayesian inference) might yield more robust and reliable results. 
3. **Data Considerations:** The combination of Swadesh lists and other cognates in the dataset raises concerns about potential biases. The differing sources and selection criteria of these data types could influence the discovered correspondences. The use of orthographic form, rather than phonetic transcriptions, is also a limitation, potentially masking underlying linguistic relationships.
4. **Limited Comparison to Existing Work:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison to other methods for cognate detection, borrowing identification, or historical linguistic reconstruction (e.g., methods by Bouchard-Cote, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen; List and Moran). A more comprehensive review of the relevant literature would strengthen the paper's contribution.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What specific strategies are being considered to address the memory and runtime bottlenecks of the alignment procedure, and what is the expected improvement in scalability?
2.  Have you considered employing more sophisticated phylogenetic methods (e.g., Bayesian inference) for tree construction, and if so, what were the reasons for choosing neighbor-joining?
3.  Did you explore the impact of using only one type of data source (either Swadesh lists or the other cognates) on the discovered correspondences?
4.  Could you clarify the meaning of "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (line 286)? 
5. Could you provide further detail on the merging process within the maximization step of the EM algorithm?
