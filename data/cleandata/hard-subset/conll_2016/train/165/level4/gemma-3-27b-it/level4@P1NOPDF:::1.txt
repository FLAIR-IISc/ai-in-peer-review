This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on Minimum Description Length (MDL). The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning across a number of Slavic languages. They develop codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that balances the description length of the model and the data given the model. The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and tested on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The results are presented using several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of identified correspondences.

The motivation and formulation of the approach are sound. MDL appears to be a reasonable tool for tackling this problem, and the justification for employing EM is presented clearly. However, some of the derivations were not entirely clear to me.

The authors note the resemblance of the MDL objective to Bayesian inference, which naturally leads one to consider applications of Bayesian inference in (biological) phylogenetic inference, such as those employing tools like MrBayes. An empirical comparison with these methods could be insightful.

**Related Work:**

A key limitation is the lack of comparison to existing methods for borrowing and cognate detection, or other computational approaches in historical linguistics. For example, the work of Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen is relevant. While some of these methods may not have readily available tools applicable to the given dataset, List and Moran (2013) should be mentioned. Furthermore, relevant tools used for biological phylogeny inference (e.g., paup, MrBayes) could also be considered. 

**Approach and Methodology**

- **Alignment Procedure:** The memory and runtime requirements appear to be a major drawback, limiting comparisons to a maximum of five languages. Given the involvement of multiple languages and the construction of phylogenetic trees, it would be valuable to see the method scaled to include more languages. I am curious about the authors’ plans for addressing this computational limitation.
- **Phylogenetic Tree:** Using neighbor joining to create phylogenetic trees is known to have disadvantages, such as the need for manual root specification. Exploring more sophisticated methods would be beneficial.
- **EM Convergence:** Could the authors clarify whether EM is run until convergence, or if another stopping criterion is used?

**Data**

- **Dataset Composition:** The combination of a dataset of known cognates with a dataset that does not necessarily contain only cognates (the Swadesh lists) raises concerns about potential impacts on the results. Have the authors considered this aspect?
- **Orthographic Form:** The use of orthographic form for the data may obscure many underlying correspondences, particularly in languages with different scripts. The learned rules might therefore reflect script changes more than genuine linguistic correspondences. Working with phonetic transcriptions could mitigate this shortcoming.

**Unclear Points**

- What is meant by the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules”? (line 286)
- The merging step performed during the maximization step was not entirely clear. 

**Minor Issue**

- “focus in on” should be “focus on” (line 440).
