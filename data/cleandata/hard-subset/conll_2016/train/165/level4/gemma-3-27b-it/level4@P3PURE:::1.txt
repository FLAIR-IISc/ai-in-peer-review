This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning across a number of Slavic languages. They develop codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that balances the description length of the model and the data given the model. The model is trained using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm and tested on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. Results are presented using several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of discovered correspondences.

The motivation and formulation of the approach are sound. MDL appears to be a reasonable tool for tackling this problem, and the justification for employing EM is presented clearly. However, some of the derivations were not entirely clear to me.

The authors note the resemblance between their MDL objective and Bayesian inference. This naturally leads one to consider the application of Bayesian inference in (biological) phylogenetic inference, for example, using tools like MrBayes. An empirical comparison with such methods could be insightful.

Regarding related work, the paper lacks a comparison to methods for borrowing and cognate detection, or other computational methods in historical linguistics. For example, studies by Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen are relevant. While some of these may not have readily available tools for application to the given dataset, List and Moran (2013) could be mentioned. Furthermore, relevant tools from biological phylogeny inference (e.g., PAUP*, MrBayes) could also be considered.

Concerning the approach and methodology:

*   **Alignment procedure:** The memory and runtime requirements appear to be a major drawback, currently limiting comparison to a maximum of 5 languages. Given the goal of constructing phylogenetic trees involving multiple languages, it would be valuable to see consideration of strategies to scale to larger datasets. I am curious about the authors’ ideas for addressing this limitation.
*   **Phylogenetic tree:** The use of Neighbor Joining for creating phylogenetic trees is known to have disadvantages, such as the need for manual root specification. Exploring more sophisticated methods might be beneficial.
*   **EM Algorithm:** Could the authors clarify the stopping criterion for the EM algorithm – is it run until convergence, or is another criterion employed?

Regarding the data:

*   **Dataset Composition:** The combination of a cognate dataset with a Swadesh list (which does not guarantee cognates) raises a question about potential impact on the results. Have the authors considered this aspect?
*   **Orthographic Form:** The data is in orthographic form, which may obscure underlying correspondences, particularly in languages with different scripts. The learned rules might thus reflect script changes rather than true linguistic correspondences. This appears to be a potential shortcoming that could be mitigated by working with phonetic transcriptions.

Several points remain unclear:

*   What is the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286)?
*   The merging process during the maximization step was not entirely clear.

Finally, a minor issue:

*   “focus in on” should be “focus on” (line 440).
