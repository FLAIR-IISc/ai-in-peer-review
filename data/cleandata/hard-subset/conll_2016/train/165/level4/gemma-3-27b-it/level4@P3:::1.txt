This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning across a number of Slavic languages, developing codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulating an MDL objective to balance model and data description length. The model is trained with Expectation-Maximization (EM) and evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, presenting results through distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and example correspondences.

The motivation and formulation of the approach are sound. MDL appears a reasonable framework for tackling this problem, and the justification for employing EM is well-presented. However, some of the derivations within the paper lack clarity. The authors correctly point out the resemblance between the MDL objective and Bayesian inference, prompting consideration of parallels with Bayesian phylogenetic inference methods (e.g., MrBayes). An empirical comparison along these lines could be insightful.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **MDL Framework for Multi-lingual Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is the application of the MDL principle to the problem of discovering correspondences between multiple languages simultaneously. This approach offers a principled way to balance model complexity and data fit, avoiding the arbitrary assumptions often imposed by other statistical methods.
2. **Efficient Algorithm for Correspondence Inference:** The authors provide a deterministic algorithm based on EM to infer correspondence rules from data. While scalability is a limitation (discussed below), the algorithm itself represents a concrete contribution to the field, enabling the exploration of correspondences beyond pairwise language comparisons.
3. **Demonstration on Slavic Languages:** The empirical evaluation on a diverse set of Slavic languages demonstrates the practical applicability of the proposed method and highlights its ability to uncover both expected and potentially novel linguistic relationships. The provided examples and analyses provide valuable insights into the algorithm’s behavior.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** Applying MDL to the multi-lingual correspondence problem is a novel idea. Existing methods often rely on strong distributional assumptions or are limited to pairwise comparisons. This work avoids those limitations.
2. **Principled Model Selection:** The use of MDL provides a statistically sound foundation for model selection, automatically balancing complexity and goodness of fit. This is crucial for uncovering the "true" underlying correspondences without overfitting to noise.
3. **Detailed Analysis & Visualization:** The inclusion of both quantitative measures (NCD) and qualitative examples (phylogenetic tree, example correspondences) offers a comprehensive understanding of the method’s performance. The visualization of results aids in interpretation and validation.
4. **Clear Motivation and Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the motivation behind the proposed approach. The connection to related work is adequately explained, although with room for expansion (see Weaknesses).
5. **Demonstration of Scalability Potential:** While limited by current computational resources, the paper demonstrates the feasibility of applying the method to more than two languages, a significant advantage over many existing approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scalability:** The reported memory and runtime bottleneck, restricting analysis to a maximum of five languages, is a significant limitation. This hinders the application of the method to larger language families or more complex datasets. The authors should discuss potential strategies for addressing this issue, such as algorithmic optimizations, parallelization, or approximation techniques.
2. **Insufficient Comparison to Existing Work:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to relevant existing methods in computational historical linguistics. The omission of studies by Bouchard-Cote, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen, and the mention of only List and Moran (2013), represents a gap in the related work section. Furthermore, while acknowledging the similarity to Bayesian phylogenetic inference, a more detailed discussion and potential empirical comparison with methods like MrBayes is warranted.
3. **Data Quality and Representation:** The use of both cognate lists and Swadesh lists raises concerns about data consistency and potential biases. The impact of this data mix on the results should be investigated. Furthermore, working directly with orthographic forms, while practical, may obscure underlying phonetic correspondences. Considering phonetic transcriptions could potentially improve the accuracy and robustness of the method.
4. **Methodological Choices:** The use of neighbor-joining for constructing the phylogenetic tree is a questionable choice, given its known limitations (manual root specification, potential for inaccurate tree topologies). Exploring more sophisticated phylogenetic methods would be beneficial. The criteria for stopping the EM algorithm are also unclear. Specifying a convergence threshold or maximum iteration limit would enhance reproducibility.
5. **Unclear Technical Details:** Certain technical details, such as the definition of the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286) and the precise mechanics of the rule merging step in the maximization step, are not fully explained. Clarifying these aspects would improve the paper's clarity and accessibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What strategies are being considered to address the scalability limitations of the algorithm?
2. How sensitive are the results to the inclusion of both cognate lists and Swadesh lists in the dataset? Have you performed any analyses using only one type of data?
3. Have you considered incorporating phonetic transcriptions into the analysis? If so, what impact did this have on the results?
4. What stopping criterion is used for the EM algorithm?
5. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the rule merging step in the maximization step, perhaps with an illustrative example?

**Additional Comments:**

*   “focus in on” should be “focus on” (line 440).
*   It would be helpful to provide a more detailed discussion of the implications of the data over-weighting technique introduced in Section 4.5. What specific linguistic insights can be gained by adjusting the data weight?
*   The reference to List and Moran (2013) is relevant, but a broader survey of computational historical linguistics tools would strengthen the paper.
