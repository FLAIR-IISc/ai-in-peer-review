- Proposes an MDL-based method to discover correspondences between words across multiple languages, trained with EM and evaluated on 13 Slavic languages.  
- Main contribution: applies MDL to multi-lingual correspondence discovery, balancing model complexity and data fit.  
- Main contribution: provides a deterministic EM-based algorithm to infer multi-language correspondence rules beyond pairwise comparisons.  
- Main contribution: demonstrates the method on diverse Slavic languages, producing distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and example correspondences.  
- Strength: novel application of MDL to multilingual correspondence, avoiding strong distributional assumptions and pairwise limitations.  
- Strength: MDL offers principled model selection that balances complexity and goodness of fit to reduce overfitting.  
- Strength: includes quantitative (NCD) and qualitative (tree, examples) analyses with helpful visualizations.  
- Strength: paper is generally well-written with clear motivation and connections to related work.  
- Strength: demonstrates feasibility of multi-language analysis, indicating scalability potential despite current limits.  
- Weakness: scalability bottlenecks (memory/runtime) limit analysis to ~5 languages, hindering broader application.  
- Weakness: insufficient comparison to prior computational historical linguistics work and lack of empirical comparison to Bayesian phylogenetic methods (e.g., MrBayes).  
- Weakness: mixed use of cognate and Swadesh lists raises data consistency and bias concerns; orthographic forms may obscure phonetic correspondences.  
- Weakness: methodological choices like neighbor-joining for trees and unclear EM stopping criteria reduce confidence in some results.  
- Weakness: some technical details (e.g., “optimal unigram” definition, rule merging mechanics) are unclear and need clarification.  
- Question: what strategies will the authors pursue to address scalability limitations?  
- Question: how sensitive are results to using cognate lists versus Swadesh lists, and have analyses been run on each separately?  
- Question: have phonetic transcriptions been considered and what effect do they have on results?  
- Question: what exact stopping criterion is used for the EM algorithm?  
- Question: can the authors provide a detailed, illustrative explanation of the rule merging step in the M-step?  
- Additional comment: minor copyedit (change “focus in on” to “focus on”).  
- Additional comment: expand discussion of the data over-weighting technique and its linguistic implications.  
- Additional comment: broaden related work discussion beyond List and Moran (2013) to include more computational historical linguistics tools and studies.
