This paper introduces a Minimum Description Length (MDL)-based method for discovering correspondences between languages, specifically applied to a set of Slavic languages. The authors model correspondences as matching substrings across languages and formulate an MDL objective to balance model complexity and data fit. An Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm is employed for training, and the method is evaluated using distance measures, phylogenetic tree construction, and inspection of learned correspondences. The motivation for utilizing MDL and the rationale behind employing EM are well presented. However, several aspects of the derivations and implementation details require further clarification.

The primary contribution of this work is the application of the MDL principle to the problem of discovering multi-language correspondences *without* imposing strong prior assumptions on the shape or distribution of those correspondences. While statistical methods exist for cognate identification and reconstruction, this work distinguishes itself by offering a more flexible, data-driven approach capable of handling multiple languages simultaneously. A secondary, and significant, contribution lies in the demonstration of this approach on Slavic languages, providing both quantitative and qualitative insights into linguistic relationships and offering a framework for detailed information-theoretic analyses of linguistic similarity beyond pairwise comparisons. Finally, the developed code provides a novel and potentially valuable tool for historical linguistic research.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of MDL:** The use of MDL to address the problem of discovering multi-language correspondences is a strong point. This principle offers a statistically grounded approach to model selection, avoiding the pitfalls of arbitrary assumptions often present in other methods. The focus on succinctness as a criterion for identifying significant correspondences is conceptually sound and well-motivated.
2. **Multi-Language Capability:** Existing methods often focus on pairwise language comparisons. This work’s ability to handle multiple languages simultaneously opens possibilities for more comprehensive and nuanced analyses of linguistic relationships, particularly when considering phylogenetic reconstruction and quantifying similarity between subsets of languages. The four-way analysis presented demonstrates this potential.
3. **Demonstration on Slavic Languages:** The application of the method to a set of Slavic languages provides a concrete and valuable demonstration. The results, including the constructed phylogenetic tree and identified correspondences, are generally consistent with established linguistic knowledge. The analysis of the Czech-Polish and Russian-Bulgarian language pairs offers specific examples of the types of patterns discovered.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Scalability:** The reported memory and runtime bottleneck, limiting the analysis to a maximum of five languages, is a significant drawback. This severely restricts the applicability of the method to larger language families or more complex datasets. The authors need to address how they plan to overcome this limitation, perhaps through algorithmic optimizations or parallelization.
2. **Methodological Choices:** The use of neighbor joining for phylogenetic tree construction, which is known to have limitations (particularly concerning root placement), seems suboptimal given the availability of more sophisticated methods like Bayesian inference or maximum likelihood approaches commonly used in phylogenetics. A comparison to these established methods would strengthen the analysis. Additionally, the stopping criterion for the EM algorithm is not clearly defined.
3. **Data Considerations:** The combination of Swadesh lists and other cognates in the dataset raises concerns about potential biases. Swadesh lists are known to exhibit varying degrees of comparability across languages. Further, using orthographic forms without phonetic transcription might obscure underlying linguistic correspondences, particularly across languages with different scripts. This could lead to the identification of script changes rather than genuine linguistic patterns.
4. **Limited Related Work Discussion:** The paper lacks a thorough discussion of relevant related work, particularly in the areas of computational historical linguistics and Bayesian phylogenetic inference. Mentioning and contextualizing work by researchers like Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen, as well as tools like List & Moran (2013) and established phylogenetic software (paup, MrBayes), would demonstrate a stronger awareness of the existing landscape.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What specific strategies are being considered to address the computational bottleneck and enable the analysis of a larger number of languages?
2. Why was neighbor joining chosen for phylogenetic tree construction, and have you considered comparing the results to those obtained using more sophisticated phylogenetic methods (e.g., Bayesian inference)?
3. What is the stopping criterion for the EM algorithm?
4. Could you elaborate on the potential impact of combining Swadesh lists with other cognate data on the results, and have you explored alternative data preprocessing or analysis strategies to mitigate this?
5. The phrase "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" is unclear. Could you please clarify what this refers to and how it is determined?
6. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the merging step in the maximization step, clarifying how it improves the model?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for future research.
*   Minor grammatical correction: "focus in on" should be "focus on" (line 440).
*   The provided reference to List and Moran (2013) is helpful, and including similar references to relevant tools and approaches in computational historical linguistics would further strengthen the paper.
