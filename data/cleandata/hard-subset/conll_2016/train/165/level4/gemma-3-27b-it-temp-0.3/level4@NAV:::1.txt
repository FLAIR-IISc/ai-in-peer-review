This paper introduces a Minimum Description Length (MDL) based method for discovering cross-lingual correspondences. The authors model relationships between words with shared meaning across a set of Slavic languages, developing a rule-based coding scheme to identify matching substrings and formulating an MDL objective to balance model complexity and data fit. The model is trained using Expectation-Maximization (EM) and evaluated on 13 Slavic languages, with results presented through distance metrics, a phylogenetic tree, and illustrative examples of identified correspondences. 

The motivation behind the approach is sound, and the application of MDL appears well-suited to this problem. The rationale for employing EM is clearly articulated. However, certain derivations within the paper lacked complete clarity.

The authors acknowledge a connection between their MDL objective and Bayesian inference, naturally prompting consideration of established Bayesian phylogenetic methods – such as those implemented in tools like MrBayes – commonly used in biological phylogenetics. An empirical comparison to these methods would likely provide valuable insights.

Regarding related work, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of existing methods for cognate detection, borrowing identification, and other computational approaches in historical linguistics. While acknowledging potential implementation challenges, works by Bouchard-Cote, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen should be discussed. Mentioning List & Moran (2013) would also be relevant. Furthermore, the applicability of phylogenetic inference tools used in biology (e.g., PAUP, MrBayes) could be explored.

Concerning the approach and methodology: 
- The alignment procedure appears to be computationally limited, currently restricting comparisons to a maximum of five languages. Given the goal of constructing phylogenetic trees involving multiple languages, addressing this scalability issue is crucial. It would be helpful to understand the authors’ plans for overcoming this bottleneck.
- The use of neighbor joining for phylogenetic tree construction is known to have limitations, including the need for manual root specification. Exploring more sophisticated phylogenetic methods may be warranted.
- Clarification regarding the EM algorithm’s stopping criterion (convergence or a fixed number of iterations) would be beneficial.

With respect to the data:
- The combination of a cognate dataset and a Swadesh list (which may not consist entirely of cognates) raises potential concerns about its impact on the results. This aspect should be addressed.
- The use of orthographic data may obscure underlying linguistic correspondences, particularly across languages with differing scripts. The learned rules might reflect script changes rather than true linguistic relationships. Utilizing phonetic transcriptions could mitigate this issue.

Several points require further clarification:
- The meaning of “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286) is unclear.
- The merging process within the maximization step was not fully comprehensible.

Finally, a minor editorial note: “focus in on” should be revised to “focus on” (line 440).
