Summary / Claimed contributions
- MDL-based framework that learns statistically salient substring correspondences across multiple languages from aligned cognate tuples using a two-part MDL code.
- EM training: E-step aligns tuples via shortest-path (Dijkstra), M-step merges rules to reduce description length; deterministic and allows prior rules or data over-weighting.
- Empirical analyses on 13 Slavic languages (pairwise NCD + neighbor-joining) and a four-language sDL analysis demonstrating subset-level shared description lengths and illustrative learned correspondences.
- Claimed contributions: tailored MDL code for multi-language correspondences, practical EM with principled alignment and MDL-guided merging, and empirical evidence that MDL distances match linguistic groupings and provide finer-grained subset insights.

Strengths
- Well-motivated modeling: two-part MDL balances model complexity and data fit with minimal distributional assumptions.
- Interpretable training: merge-based M-step encourages longer rules only when supported, producing statistically grounded, interpretable correspondences.
- Empirical relevance: pairwise NCDs and NJ tree broadly align with established Slavic classifications; sDL analysis yields nuanced similarity measures across subsets.
- Deterministic and integrable with prior linguistic knowledge; over-weighting knob allows control of granularity for sparse data.

Weaknesses
- Scalability limits: shortest-path alignment causes memory/runtime bottlenecks, currently limiting simultaneous comparisons to ~5 languages on modest hardware.
- Phylogenetic method choice: reliance on neighbor-joining has known drawbacks (manual rooting, sensitivity); stronger phylogenetic inference methods would strengthen claims.
- Evaluation/baselines lacking: missing comparisons to established computational historical-linguistics and cognate-detection methods or discussions contextualizing differences.
- Data representation risk: raw orthography and mixed scripts may induce script-conversion artifacts rather than phonological/morphological regularities.
- Mixed-dataset concerns: combining Swadesh lists (not all cognates) with curated cognate lists may bias learned rules and distance estimates; impact not analyzed.

Questions to authors
- EM convergence: what is the stopping criterion and do you use regularization or early stopping when over-weighting data?
- M-step merging: please formalize the merge criterion, candidate selection, efficient computation of description-length gain, and per-iteration complexity.
- Unigram code estimation: how is the “optimal unigram for symbol usages” computed exactly (ML/Laplace/data-to-model formula)?
- Scalability plans: do you plan pruning heuristics, A*/beam search, admissible dynamic programming bounds, or rule-length constraints; any empirical runtime/memory trade-offs?
- Phylogenetic alternatives: have you tried ML or Bayesian phylogeny (e.g., MrBayes, PAUP*) using MDL distances or rule-based characters even on small scales?
- Data choices sensitivity: have you tested effects of excluding non-cognates from Swadesh lists or using phonetic transcriptions/transliterations?

Additional comments
- Minor wording: “focus in on” → “focus on.”
- Clarify whether NCDs use the same encoding/model class across languages and whether model sizes are comparable across scripts.
- Report proportions of singleton vs multi-symbol/multi-language rules at various over-weighting levels to quantify granularity effects.
