Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a Minimum Description Length (MDL) framework, with a concrete code (PS-2) and a deterministic learning algorithm, to discover substring-level correspondences among two or more languages. The model treats correspondence rules as associated character strings without imposing parametric assumptions, and it balances model cost and data cost. Training alternates between (i) optimal alignment via a shortest-path formulation and (ii) greedy rule merging guided by MDL. Experiments on Slavic languages include pairwise analyses over 13 languages using Normalized Compression Distance (NCD), a neighbor-joining tree, and a four-language analysis (CS–PL–RU–BG) with an information-theoretic breakdown of shared structure. The authors also introduce data over-weighting as a practical knob to control the granularity of learned rules.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An MDL-based method and deterministic EM-like training procedure to discover multi-way substring correspondences over any subset of languages, with an efficient shortest-path alignment and greedy merging guided by description length.
2) An analysis toolkit that leverages NCD for pairwise distances and introduces shared description lengths (sDL) to quantify how much structure is shared across arbitrary subsets of languages.
3) A case study on Slavic languages demonstrating linguistically interpretable rules and a phylogeny consistent with established classifications, plus a practical mechanism (data over-weighting) to steer the level of detail.

Strengths
- Sound, assumption-light modeling: Using MDL to trade off model complexity and data fit is a principled way to avoid strong parametric assumptions about the shape or distribution of correspondences. The two-part code and reliance on code lengths make the objective interpretable and comparable across settings.
- Multi-language correspondences beyond pairs: The method explicitly supports rules defined on any subset of languages, enabling analyses that go beyond pairwise alignments. The sDL decomposition in the four-language study is a valuable, fine-grained quantification of shared structure that typical pairwise methods cannot provide.
- Deterministic, interpretable rule discovery: The shortest-path alignment and greedy merging yield a deterministic pipeline that produces human-interpretable rules (e.g., palatalization, liquid metathesis, orthographic digraphs, and suffixes). The qualitative examples convincingly show that both superficial (orthographic) and deeper (phonological/morphological) regularities can emerge.
- Empirical plausibility: Pairwise NCDs and the derived neighbor-joining tree align well with known Slavic groupings and known outliers, lending credibility to the approach. The four-language analysis further reveals plausible subgroup structure (e.g., CS–PL and RU–BG).
- Practical flexibility: The ability to inject linguistic knowledge via initialization and to control the level of granularity via data over-weighting are useful in applied settings where experts may want to bias discovery toward certain correspondences.

Weaknesses
- Scalability: The alignment graph and EM steps create memory/runtime bottlenecks that currently cap practical analyses at roughly five languages in the most challenging dataset. This limits the promised “multi-language” potential and constrains phylogenetic applications that often require broader coverage.
- Orthography vs. phonology: All experiments operate on orthographic forms, sometimes across different scripts. This can conflate script conversion with linguistic correspondences and obscure phonological regularities. For languages using different scripts (e.g., Cyrillic vs. Latin), learned rules may disproportionately reflect script mappings rather than sound correspondences.
- Limited comparisons to related methods: The paper lacks empirical comparisons with cognate detection/borrowing approaches and with phylogenetic inference methods used in computational historical linguistics (e.g., List & Moran; Bouchard-Côté; Nakhleh; Warnow) and mature tools in phylogenetics (MrBayes, PAUP, RAxML). Even if full pipelines are hard to run on these datasets, a discussion with partial quantitative or qualitative comparisons would strengthen the case.
- Methodological clarity: Some derivations and implementation details are underspecified, notably the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules,” the exact mechanics and search space of the M-step merging, and EM stopping criteria. These gaps hinder reproducibility and make it harder to assess convergence behavior and sensitivity.
- Dataset heterogeneity: The study uses both Swadesh lists and a separate set of cognates. These sources differ in their selection criteria and lexical domains. It remains unclear to what extent results are comparable across datasets or influenced by dataset-specific biases (e.g., internationalisms vs. basic vocabulary), especially when drawing broader conclusions.

Questions to authors
- Alignment/complexity: What concrete strategies do you envision to scale alignment and EM to more than five languages (e.g., A* with admissible heuristics, pruning, beam search, constrained rule length, suffix array/automata indexing, or batching)? Any empirical evidence for how runtime scales with vocabulary size and alphabet size?
- EM details: What is your stopping criterion (relative MDL improvement, max iterations, alignment stability), and how sensitive are results to it? Do you perform restarts?
- M-step merging: Please describe precisely how candidate merges are generated and evaluated. Are merges restricted by contiguity, maximum length, or frequency thresholds? How do you avoid local minima or merge-order pathologies?
- Code lengths: What exactly is meant by the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules”? Is this a global ML estimate pooled over all alphabets, or per-language distributions? How are alphabet symbols with zero counts treated in the data-to-model coding?
- Orthography vs. phonology: Have you considered normalizing scripts or working with phonetic/phonemic transcriptions? If so, how do the discovered rules and NCD tree change? If not, can you quantify the fraction of rules that are script-conversion vs. genuine sound correspondences?
- Dataset handling: Are the Swadesh and cognate datasets ever combined within a single model, or are they always analyzed separately? Could you report results stratified by source to assess robustness?
- Baselines/comparisons: Could you compare your NCD-based tree to trees derived from standard phylogenetic toolkits (e.g., MrBayes, PAUP) on the same lexical data? For cognate identification, can you compare against established unsupervised methods or report agreement with existing cognate annotations where available?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, data splits, and parameters (e.g., over-weighting α) to facilitate replication?

Additional comments
- Style: “focus in on” → “focus on.”
- The paper is well motivated and the qualitative examples are strong; strengthening methodological clarity, scalability, and empirical comparisons would substantially increase impact.
