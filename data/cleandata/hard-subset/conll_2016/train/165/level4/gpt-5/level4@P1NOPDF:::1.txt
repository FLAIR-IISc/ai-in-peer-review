This paper proposes an MDL-based method for discovering cross-linguistic correspondences. The authors model correspondences between words with the same meaning across a set of Slavic languages, develop codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages, and formulate an MDL objective that balances model description length with data description length. The model is trained with EM and evaluated on 13 Slavic languages. Results are presented via several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and illustrative correspondences.

The motivation and overall formulation are sensible: MDL is a reasonable tool for this problem, and the rationale for using EM is clearly explained. That said, some derivations were not entirely clear to me. The paper also notes a resemblance between the MDL objective and Bayesian inference; this naturally invites comparison with Bayesian phylogenetic methods (e.g., MrBayes). An empirical comparison could be insightful.

Related work
- The paper lacks comparison to methods for borrowing and cognate detection, and to other computational approaches in historical linguistics (e.g., work by Alexandre Bouchard-Côté, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen). Some tools may not be directly applicable to the given dataset, but relevant studies such as List and Moran (2013) should be discussed. There are also applicable tools from biological phylogeny inference (e.g., PAUP, MrBayes) that could serve as baselines or points of reference.

Approach and methodology
- Alignment procedure: The memory/runtime bottleneck is a major limitation, restricting comparison to at most five languages. Given that the task involves multiple languages and phylogenetic reconstruction, it would be valuable to include more languages. What strategies do the authors propose to overcome this bottleneck (e.g., pruning, approximation, batching, or more compact representations)?
- Phylogenetic tree: Neighbor-joining is known to have disadvantages, such as requiring a manually specified root. Have the authors considered more sophisticated methods?
- EM: Do you run EM until convergence, or do you use another stopping criterion? Please clarify the criterion and its practical impact on performance and runtime.

Data
- The study mixes two datasets, one of cognates and one not necessarily (Swadesh lists). Have you assessed how this mixture might affect the results?
- The data are in orthographic form, which can obscure many correspondences—especially across different scripts. As a result, the learned rules may capture script conversion rather than genuine linguistic correspondences. This seems like a limitation that could be mitigated by using phonetic transcriptions.

Unclear points
- What is meant by the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286)?
- The merging procedure in the maximization step was not entirely clear to me; more detail or a worked example would help.

Minor issue
- “focus in on” → “focus on” (line 440).
