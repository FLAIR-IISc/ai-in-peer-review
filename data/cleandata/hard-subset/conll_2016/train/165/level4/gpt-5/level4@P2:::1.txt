Summary and contributions (as I see them)
- The paper introduces an MDL-based framework to discover salient string-level correspondences across two or more languages without assuming a predefined distribution over correspondence shapes. The model encodes both a rule table and the data aligned by these rules, and seeks the model that minimizes total description length.
- An EM-style training procedure alternates between optimal alignments (E-step) and rule-table optimization via rule merging (M-step), yielding a deterministic algorithm that can incorporate prior linguistic knowledge through initialization and optionally control granularity via data over-weighting.
- The approach is applied to Slavic languages. The authors report pairwise NCD distances, a neighbor-joining phylogeny consistent with known groupings, and illustrative correspondence rules, including multi-language correspondences and fine-grained analyses using shared description lengths.

Strengths
- Sound modeling choice and clear motivation: MDL is a natural fit for balancing model complexity and data fit without strong parametric assumptions, and the paper articulates this rationale well.
- Deterministic, interpretable correspondences: The resulting rules are human-readable, cover multiple languages, and include meaningful phonological/orthographic patterns (e.g., digraphs, palatalization, metathesis). This interpretability is valuable for linguistic analysis.
- Multi-language capability beyond pairs: The framework natively handles subsets of languages, enabling analyses that quantify similarity at the level of all language subsets (e.g., shared description lengths), which goes beyond standard pairwise approaches.
- Empirical alignment with linguistic classifications: The NCD-based distances and the inferred phylogeny broadly match established Slavic groupings, lending credibility to the method.
- Practical flexibility: The method supports the injection of linguistic prior knowledge through initialization and offers a tunable “data over-weighting” mechanism to control the granularity of discovered rules when data are sparse.

Weaknesses
- Scalability constraints: The alignment graph and Dijkstra-based search create significant memory and runtime bottlenecks, reportedly limiting analyses to about five languages. This is a substantial limitation for multi-language and phylogenetic studies where larger sets are desirable.
- Limited methodological comparison: The paper lacks empirical comparisons to cognate detection/borrowing and historical linguistics methods (e.g., work by Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh) and to phylogenetic tools used in linguistics/biology (e.g., MrBayes, PAUP). Such baselines would clarify the method’s relative strengths and weaknesses.
- Phylogenetic inference method: Reliance on neighbor joining (with manual rooting) is known to have drawbacks. More robust, model-based phylogenetic methods (e.g., Bayesian or maximum-likelihood approaches) could provide stronger validation or complementary insights.
- Data design choices: Mixing a cognate-focused dataset with Swadesh lists may bias correspondences and distances. The paper does not quantify the impact of this mixture or provide ablations.
- Orthography vs phonology: Working solely with orthographic forms risks conflating script conversion with genuine linguistic correspondences, especially across Latin/Cyrillic scripts. Phonetic or phonemic transcriptions could reduce this confound.
- Clarity of some derivations: The notion of the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” and the precise mechanics/criteria of the M-step merging (and its effect on rule counts and code lengths) are not fully transparent. EM stopping criteria are also unspecified.

Questions to the authors
- EM details: What is the stopping criterion (e.g., change in total description length, max iterations, convergence of counts)? Do you observe monotonic decreases in the objective? Any safeguards against local minima?
- M-step merging: How exactly are candidate merges proposed and selected? How do you update counts and code lengths after a merge, and what is the computational complexity of evaluating the objective change?
- Scalability: What strategies could mitigate the alignment bottleneck (e.g., A* with admissible heuristics, pruning, lattice factorization, beam search, suffix automata/tries, dynamic programming with memoization, or learning a constrained rule inventory first)?
- Comparisons: Can you provide quantitative comparisons with cognate detection/borrowing methods or Bayesian phylogenetic tools (e.g., MrBayes) on subsets where these are applicable?
- Data curation: Have you run separate analyses on (i) only Swadesh lists and (ii) only explicit cognate sets? What is the effect on NCDs and discovered rules?
- Orthography: Have you considered phonetic or phonemic transcriptions (e.g., IPA or standardized transliteration layers) to decouple script differences from correspondences?
- Over-weighting: How sensitive are results to α and per-item weights? Can you provide guidance for setting α without overfitting or distorting the MDL objective?

Additional comments and minor issues
- Style: “focus in on” → “focus on.”
- Explain “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules”: clarify whether you estimate symbol code lengths from aggregated symbol counts across Π using a data-to-model code, and how zero counts are handled.
- Reference coverage: Please consider situating your work against:
  - Johann-Mattis List, Steven Moran. 2013. An Open Source Toolkit for Quantitative Historical Linguistics. ACL 2013 System Demonstrations, 13–18.
  - Andrew Kitchen, Christopher Ehret, Shiferaw Assefa, Connie J. Mulligan. 2009. Bayesian phylogenetic analysis of Semitic languages identifies an Early Bronze Age origin of Semitic in the Near East.
  - Also relevant: work by Alexandre Bouchard-Côté, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, among others; and tools such as PAUP and MrBayes.
- Figures/tables: Consider reporting confidence/uncertainty on distances or tree support (e.g., bootstrapping) and providing ablations (with/without over-weighting, with different initialization).
- Reproducibility: Code/data availability would enhance the impact and allow comparisons by others.

Overall evaluation
This is a well-motivated application of MDL to multilingual correspondence discovery with interpretable outputs and promising empirical signals. Addressing scalability, clarifying the M-step and coding choices, and adding comparisons to established historical/phylogenetic methods would considerably strengthen the work.
