This paper presents an MDL-based method for discovering cross-linguistic correspondences. The authors model correspondences between words with the same meanings across multiple Slavic languages. They define codes for rules that match substrings across two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that trades off the description length of the model and the data encoded under the model. The model is trained with EM and evaluated on 13 Slavic languages. Results are reported via several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and illustrative examples of discovered correspondences.

The motivation and formulation are sound. MDL is a reasonable framework for this problem, and the rationale for using EM is clearly presented. That said, some of the derivations were not entirely clear to me.

The authors note the connection between the MDL objective and Bayesian inference, which invites comparison to Bayesian approaches in (biological) phylogenetics, such as MrBayes. An empirical comparison would be informative.

Related work:
- The paper lacks comparisons to methods for borrowing and cognate detection or other computational approaches in historical linguistics. Relevant work includes studies by Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen. Some may not offer tools directly applicable to this dataset, but List and Moran (2013) should at least be mentioned. There are also applicable tools from biological phylogeny, such as paup and MrBayes.

Approach and methodology:
- Alignment procedure: The memory/runtime bottleneck is a significant limitation, restricting comparisons to at most five languages. Given the multi-language setting and the use of phylogenetic trees, it would be valuable to include more languages. What strategies do the authors envision for overcoming this constraint?
- Phylogenetic tree: Neighbor-joining has known drawbacks (e.g., requiring manual root specification). Have the authors considered more sophisticated methods?
- EM: Do you run EM to convergence, or do you use another stopping criterion?

Data:
- Two datasets are combined: one of cognates and another that is not necessarily cognate-based (Swadesh lists). Have you assessed how this mixture affects the results?
- The use of orthographic forms may obscure many correspondences, especially across different scripts. The learned rules may capture script changes more than genuine linguistic correspondences. This could be mitigated by working with phonetic transcriptions.

Unclear points:
- What is meant by the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules”? (line 286)
- The merging procedure in the maximization step was not entirely clear.

Minor issue:
- “focus in on” → “focus on” (line 440)
