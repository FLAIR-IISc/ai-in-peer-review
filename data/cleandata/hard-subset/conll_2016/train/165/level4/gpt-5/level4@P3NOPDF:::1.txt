Summary
The paper proposes an MDL-based approach to discover cross-linguistic correspondences among words with the same meaning across multiple Slavic languages. The method defines codes for rules that match substrings across two or more languages, and optimizes an MDL objective that trades off model complexity with data description length. Training proceeds via EM, and the approach is evaluated on 13 Slavic languages using several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and illustrative correspondences.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for using MDL to balance rule complexity and coverage, and a thoughtful rationale for EM.
- The focus on multi-language correspondences (rather than only pairwise) is appealing for historical and comparative linguistics.
- The empirical section covers multiple forms of output (distances, tree, examples), which helps readers gauge what the model learns.

Main Concerns and Suggestions
- Comparative baselines and related work: The paper would be strengthened by a more comprehensive engagement with computational historical linguistics and phylogenetic methods. In particular:
  - Cognate and borrowing detection and language family inference: work by Alexandre Bouchard-Côté and collaborators, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and related studies could provide conceptual and empirical baselines.
  - Practical toolkits: List and Moran (2013) offer an open-source toolkit that may enable comparisons on aspects of the pipeline (e.g., cognate clustering, distance computation).
  - Phylogenetic inference: Bayesian and maximum-likelihood methods used in biology (e.g., MrBayes, PAUP*) are applicable in principle and often provide confidence estimates; an empirical or at least conceptual comparison would be insightful.
- Scalability and alignment: The memory/runtime bottleneck that restricts comparisons to at most five languages at a time is a major limitation, especially for tree reconstruction and broader typological conclusions.
  - Please elaborate on the computational bottlenecks in the alignment/rule-induction step and any pruning, sparsity, or approximate inference strategies you considered.
  - Consider progressive or hierarchical strategies (e.g., pairwise or small-clique rule discovery followed by merging), beam search, or suffix-trie/automaton–based substring indexing to reduce memory.
  - A complexity analysis and a discussion of future scalability plans would help.
- Phylogenetic tree construction: Neighbor joining is simple but has known drawbacks (e.g., sensitivity to distance estimation and the need to specify a root).
  - Consider comparing with Bayesian methods (MrBayes) or ML-based approaches and reporting support values (posterior probabilities, bootstraps). At minimum, discuss rooting choices and robustness to distance definitions.
- Data design and representation:
  - Mixing a cognate-labeled dataset with Swadesh lists that are not guaranteed to be cognate-bearing may confound the signal. Please analyze how this mixture affects rule induction and distances (e.g., ablation on the cognate-only subset vs. mixed).
  - Orthographic forms risk conflating script differences with genuine sound correspondences, especially across different writing systems. Where feasible, phonetic or phonemic transcriptions (e.g., standardized IPA) would likely improve the linguistic validity of the learned rules. A small-scale experiment or qualitative analysis on transliterated/phonetic data would be valuable.
- EM details and optimization:
  - Convergence: Do you run EM to numerical convergence (e.g., relative change in MDL objective below a threshold) or use a fixed iteration budget? Please report stopping criteria and runtime.
  - Initialization and stability: How sensitive are results to initialization? Did you perform multiple restarts? Any annealing or smoothing to avoid premature overfitting?
  - M-step specifics: The “merging” operation in the maximization step is underspecified. Pseudocode or a worked example showing how rules are merged, how counts and code lengths update, and how this affects the objective would clarify the algorithm.
  - Hyperparameters and coding choices: Please detail how code lengths are assigned (e.g., symbol priors, rule priors), what “penalties” control rule proliferation, and provide a sensitivity analysis.
- Evaluation clarity:
  - Distances: Define precisely how distances are computed from learned rules, and report basic statistics (e.g., variance across runs, robustness to different code-length schemes).
  - Tree quality: Provide quantitative assessments where possible (e.g., compare to a consensus Slavic classification, compute tree distance metrics, or show bootstrap/posterior support).
  - Correspondence quality: Beyond examples, any intrinsic evaluation against known correspondences or cognate sets (if available) would boost confidence in the method.

Requests for Clarification
- Line 286: “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” is unclear. Do you estimate a global unigram distribution over symbols that parameterizes the code lengths of rule symbols? Please formalize this with notation and explain how it is estimated and applied.
- Merging in the M-step: Which rules are eligible to merge? What is the objective change criterion? How do you update usage counts and code lengths after a merge? What is the computational complexity per iteration?
- Do you treat missing entries uniformly across languages and meanings? How does missingness affect rule induction and distances?
- What root did you choose for the neighbor-joining tree, and why?

Minor Issues
- Style: “focus in on” → “focus on” (line 440).
- Please ensure all references are complete and consistently formatted; provide URLs or DOIs where feasible.
- Consider releasing code and data splits to facilitate replication and comparison.

Overall Assessment
This is a promising and timely application of MDL to cross-linguistic correspondence discovery, with a well-motivated objective and an EM-based learning procedure. The current version would benefit from clearer derivations (especially coding choices and the M-step merging), a more robust and scalable implementation plan, and stronger empirical context through comparisons to existing methods and phylogenetic toolkits. Addressing the data representation issue (orthography vs. phonetics) and providing more rigorous evaluation of distances and trees would significantly strengthen the contribution.
