Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based method for discovering correspondences between languages. The authors model correspondences between words sharing the same meaning across a set of Slavic languages. They devise codes for rules that match substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that balances the description length of the model with the description length of the data given the model. The model is trained with EM and evaluated on 13 Slavic languages. Results are presented using several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of discovered correspondences.

Overall assessment
The motivation and formulation are sensible. MDL is a reasonable framework for this problem, and the rationale for employing EM is clearly presented. That said, some of the derivations were not entirely clear to me.

Connection to Bayesian inference
The paper notes the resemblance between the MDL objective and Bayesian inference. This immediately brings to mind applications of Bayesian methods in (biological) phylogenetic inference (e.g., MrBayes). An empirical comparison here would be insightful.

Related work
- The paper lacks comparison to methods for borrowing and cognate detection and, more broadly, to other computational approaches in historical linguistics. Relevant lines of work include studies by Alexandre Bouchard-Côté, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen. Some of these may not come with readily applicable tools for the given dataset, but List and Moran (2013) should at least be discussed. There are also relevant tools for biological phylogeny inference that could be applied for comparison (e.g., paup, MrBayes).

Approach and methodology
- Alignment procedure: The memory/runtime bottleneck appears to be a major drawback, restricting comparisons to at most five languages. Given the multi-language setting and the interest in phylogenetic trees, it would be valuable to include more languages. I am curious what ideas the authors have for addressing this scaling issue.
- Phylogenetic tree: Building trees with neighbor joining is known to have disadvantages (e.g., the need to specify the root manually). Have the authors considered more sophisticated methods?
- EM details: Do you run EM until convergence, or do you use another stopping criterion?

Data considerations
- The experiments mix two datasets: one of cognates and one not necessarily composed of cognates (Swadesh lists). Have you considered how this mixture might impact the results?
- The data are in orthographic form, which can obscure many correspondences, especially across different scripts. As a result, the learned rules might reflect script changes more than genuine linguistic correspondences. This seems like a shortcoming that could be mitigated by working with phonetic transcriptions.

Unclear points
- What is meant by the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286)?
- The merging performed in the maximization step was not entirely clear to me; more detail would help.

Minor issue
- Edit: “focus in on” → “focus on” (line 440).
