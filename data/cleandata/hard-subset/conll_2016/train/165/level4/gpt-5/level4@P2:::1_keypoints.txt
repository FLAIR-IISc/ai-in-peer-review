- Introduces an MDL-based framework to discover salient string-level correspondences across multiple languages by jointly encoding a rule table and aligned data to minimize total description length.  
- Uses an EM-style procedure alternating between optimal alignments (E-step) and rule-table optimization via rule merging (M-step), yielding a deterministic algorithm that can incorporate linguistic priors and control granularity via data over-weighting.  
- Applies the method to Slavic languages, reporting pairwise NCD distances, a neighbor-joining phylogeny consistent with known groupings, and illustrative multi-language correspondence rules and shared-description-length analyses.

- MDL is a sound, well-motivated modeling choice that balances complexity and fit without strong parametric assumptions.  
- Produces deterministic, interpretable, human-readable correspondence rules capturing meaningful phonological/orthographic patterns.  
- Natively handles multi-language subsets, allowing similarity analysis beyond pairwise comparisons (e.g., shared description lengths).  
- Empirical results (NCDs and inferred phylogeny) broadly align with established Slavic classifications.  
- Method is practically flexible: supports linguistic priors via initialization and tunable data over-weighting for sparse data.

- Scalability is limited by the alignment graph and Dijkstra-based search, constraining analyses to around five languages.  
- Lacks empirical comparisons to established cognate-detection, borrowing, and phylogenetic methods (e.g., work by Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, MrBayes, PAUP).  
- Phylogenetic validation relies on neighbor joining with manual rooting, rather than more robust model-based Bayesian or ML approaches.  
- Mixing cognate-focused data with Swadesh lists may bias correspondences and distances without ablations quantifying the effect.  
- Working with orthography risks conflating script conversion (Latin/Cyrillic) with linguistic correspondences; phonetic/phonemic transcriptions may be preferable.  
- Some derivations and algorithmic details are unclear: optimal unigram estimation, precise M-step merging mechanics/effects, and EM stopping criteria are underspecified.

- What stopping criterion is used for EM, do you observe monotonic decreases in the objective, and what safeguards exist against local minima?  
- How are candidate merges proposed and selected in the M-step, how are counts/code lengths updated, and what is the complexity of evaluating objective changes?  
- What strategies could mitigate the alignment bottleneck (e.g., A*, pruning, beam search, factorization, memoization, or prelearning a constrained rule inventory)?  
- Can you provide quantitative comparisons with cognate-detection/borrowing methods or Bayesian phylogenetic tools (e.g., MrBayes) on applicable subsets?  
- Have you run separate analyses on only Swadesh lists and only explicit cognate sets, and how do NCDs and rules change?  
- Have you considered using phonetic/phonemic transcriptions (IPA or standardized transliteration) to decouple script differences from correspondences?  
- How sensitive are results to the over-weighting parameter α and per-item weights, and how should α be set to avoid overfitting or distorting MDL?

- Minor style suggestion: change “focus in on” to “focus on.”  
- Clarify “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules”: how are symbol code lengths estimated and how are zero counts handled?  
- Broaden reference coverage to include Johann-Mattis List & Steven Moran (2013) and Bayesian phylogenetic work (e.g., Kitchen et al. 2009), and cite relevant researchers/tools (Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, PAUP, MrBayes).  
- Report uncertainty/support for distances and trees (e.g., bootstrapping) and provide ablations (with/without over-weighting, different initializations).  
- Making code and data available would improve reproducibility and enable comparisons by others.

- Overall: A well-motivated MDL application yielding interpretable multilingual correspondences with promising empirical signals, but it needs scalability improvements, clearer M-step/coding details, and comparisons to established historical/phylogenetic methods to be substantially strengthened.
