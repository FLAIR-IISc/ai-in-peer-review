This paper presents an MDL-based method for discovering cross-linguistic correspondences, modeling word-level alignments among multiple Slavic languages. The authors encode rules that match substrings across two or more languages and define an MDL objective that trades off model complexity against data fit. The model is trained with EM and evaluated on 13 Slavic languages, with results reported via several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of learned correspondences.

Overall, the motivation and formulation are sound. MDL is a reasonable framework for this problem, and the rationale for using EM is clearly articulated. That said, some derivations were difficult to follow.

The paper notes a connection between the MDL objective and Bayesian inference, which invites comparison to established Bayesian approaches in phylogenetics (e.g., MrBayes). An empirical comparison would be informative.

Related work and comparisons:
- The paper lacks comparisons to methods for borrowing/cognate detection and other computational approaches in historical linguistics (e.g., work by Alexandre Bouchard-Côté, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen). Even if tools are not directly applicable to the dataset, relevant references should be discussed, including List and Moran (2013). 
- There are also applicable tools from biological phylogenetics (e.g., PAUP, MrBayes) that could serve as baselines or points of comparison.

Approach and methodology:
- Alignment and scalability: The memory/runtime bottleneck is a major limitation, restricting comparisons to at most five languages. Given the multi-language focus and the phylogenetic analysis, broader coverage would be valuable. Please discuss plans or heuristics to mitigate this (e.g., pruning, blocking, approximate inference, or parallelization).
- Phylogenetic inference: Neighbor joining is known to have drawbacks (e.g., requiring manual rooting and being sensitive to distance estimation). Consider more robust methods (e.g., Bayesian or maximum-likelihood tree inference).
- EM training: Do you run EM to convergence? Please specify stopping criteria (e.g., relative improvement threshold, held-out validation, or a maximum number of iterations).

Data:
- The paper combines a cognate-annotated dataset with Swadesh lists that are not necessarily cognate. How might this mixture affect inference and evaluation? A sensitivity analysis would help.
- Using orthographic forms may obscure true correspondences, especially across different scripts. In such cases, learned rules may capture script conversion rather than sound correspondences. Working with phonetic transcriptions could alleviate this issue; please discuss feasibility and expected impact.

Clarity issues:
- What exactly is the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (line 286)? Please define this formally and explain its role in the objective.
- The merging procedure in the M-step is not fully clear. A step-by-step description or pseudocode would help, including the criteria for merging, how conflicts are resolved, and its effect on the code length.

Minor editorial:
- “focus in on” → “focus on” (line 440).
