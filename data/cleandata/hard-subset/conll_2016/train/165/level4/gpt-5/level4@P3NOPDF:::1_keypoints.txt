- Summary: Proposes an MDL-based method with EM to learn cross-linguistic substring-matching rules across 13 Slavic languages and evaluates via distances, a phylogenetic tree, and examples.

- Strength: Clear motivation for MDL to balance rule complexity and coverage and reasonable rationale for using EM.

- Strength: Emphasis on multi-language (not just pairwise) correspondences is valuable for comparative/historical linguistics.

- Strength: Empirical section provides multiple output types (distances, tree, examples) to illustrate learned patterns.

- Concern: Lacks comprehensive engagement with computational historical linguistics and phylogenetic baselines (e.g., work by Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh).

- Concern: Should compare conceptually or empirically to practical toolkits (e.g., List & Moran 2013) for pipeline components like cognate clustering.

- Concern: Phylogenetic methods from biology (Bayesian/ML tools like MrBayes, PAUP*) should be discussed or compared for providing support values.

- Concern: Scalability is limited by a memory/runtime bottleneck restricting comparisons to ≤5 languages, hindering broader analyses.

- Suggestion: Elaborate on alignment/rule-induction bottlenecks and any pruning, sparsity, or approximate strategies considered.

- Suggestion: Consider progressive/hierarchical strategies, beam search, or suffix-trie/automaton substring indexing to reduce memory.

- Suggestion: Provide complexity analysis and future scalability plans.

- Concern: Neighbor-joining tree is simplistic and sensitive to distance estimation and rooting; compare with Bayesian/ML approaches and report support measures.

- Data issue: Mixing cognate-labeled datasets with Swadesh lists (not guaranteed cognate) may confound signal; perform ablation analyses.

- Data issue: Orthographic forms can conflate script/orthography with phonological correspondences; use phonetic/phonemic transcriptions where possible.

- EM details: Clarify stopping criteria (convergence vs fixed iterations), runtimes, and whether multiple restarts or annealing were used to ensure stability.

- EM details: The M-step merging operation is underspecified—provide pseudocode or a worked example showing merges, count/code-length updates, and objective impact.

- EM details: Describe code-length assignments, priors/penalties controlling rule proliferation, and sensitivity analyses for hyperparameters.

- Evaluation clarity: Precisely define how distances are computed from learned rules and report statistics (variance across runs, robustness to code-length schemes).

- Evaluation clarity: Quantitatively assess tree quality (compare to consensus Slavic classification, compute tree distance metrics, bootstrap/posterior support).

- Evaluation clarity: Provide intrinsic evaluation of correspondence quality against known correspondences or cognate sets beyond illustrative examples.

- Clarification request: Formalize “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” and explain estimation and application.

- Clarification request: Specify which rules are eligible to merge, the objective-change criterion, how usage counts and code lengths update, and the per-iteration complexity.

- Clarification request: Explain treatment of missing entries across languages/meanings and how missingness affects induction and distances.

- Clarification request: State the root choice for the neighbor-joining tree and justify it.

- Minor: Fix stylistic errors (e.g., “focus in on” → “focus on”) and ensure references are complete and consistently formatted.

- Minor: Consider releasing code and data splits to facilitate replication and comparison.

- Overall assessment: Promising application of MDL to cross-linguistic correspondence discovery but needs clearer derivations, scalability plans, stronger empirical baselines, and more rigorous evaluation.
