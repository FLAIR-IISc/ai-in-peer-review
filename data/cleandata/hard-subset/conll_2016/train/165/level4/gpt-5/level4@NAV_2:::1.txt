This paper presents a method to discover cross-linguistic correspondences using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors model correspondences between words with shared meanings across several Slavic languages, defining codes for rules that align substrings across two or more languages and formulating an MDL objective that trades off model cost with data encoding cost. Training proceeds via EM, and the approach is evaluated on 13 Slavic languages. Results are summarized via distance-based analyses, a phylogenetic tree, and illustrative correspondences discovered by the model. The motivation and setup are sensible; MDL is a plausible tool for this problem, and the rationale for EM is clearly articulated. That said, a few derivations were not completely transparent to me. The paper also notes MDL’s relationship to Bayesian inference, which naturally suggests comparisons to Bayesian phylogenetic approaches (e.g., MrBayes); an empirical contrast could be instructive.

Related work:
- Missing comparisons to methods for borrowing/cognate detection and other computational historical linguistics techniques (e.g., work by Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen). Even if tooling is not readily applicable to the current dataset, List and Moran (2013) could be discussed. There are also relevant off-the-shelf tools from biological phylogenetics (e.g., PAUP, MrBayes) that might be applicable.

Approach and methodology
- Alignment procedure: The stated memory/runtime bottlenecks are a substantial limitation, effectively capping analyses at about five languages. Given the multi-language setting and the phylogenetic angle, it would be valuable to see how the method scales to more languages. What strategies do the authors envision to mitigate this?
- Phylogenetic tree: Neighbor joining has known drawbacks (e.g., manual root placement). Have the authors considered more advanced tree inference methods?
- Do you run EM to convergence, or do you use another stopping rule?

Data
- Two datasets are combined: one consisting of cognates and one that may not be (Swadesh lists). Have the authors considered potential effects this mixture could have on results?
- The use of raw orthography may obscure many correspondences, especially across different scripts. The induced rules might partly capture script differences rather than linguistic correspondences. Using phonetic transcriptions could help address this.

Unclear points
- What precisely is meant by “the optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules”? (line 286)
- The merging operation in the maximization step needs further clarification.

Minor issue
- “focus in on” -> “focus on” (line 440)
