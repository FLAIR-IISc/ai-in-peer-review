This paper presents an MDL-based approach for uncovering correspondences across languages. The authors model correspondences between words with shared meanings across several Slavic languages. They define codes for rules that align substrings across two or more languages and set up an MDL objective that trades off the complexity of the model and the cost of the data given the model. The model is trained using EM and evaluated on 13 Slavic languages. Results are reported via multiple distance metrics, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of identified correspondences.

The motivation and formulation are sound. MDL appears to be a fitting framework for this task, and the justification for using EM is clearly articulated. That said, some of the derivations were not completely transparent to me.

The authors note the similarity between the MDL objective and Bayesian inference, which brings to mind applications of Bayesian methods in (biological) phylogenetics, e.g., MrBayes. An empirical comparison here could be enlightening.

Related work:
- Missing comparisons to approaches for borrowing and cognate detection, as well as other computational methods in historical linguistics. For instance, work by Alexandre Bouchard-Cote, Tandy Warnow, Luay Nakhleh, and Andrew Kitchen. Some tools may not be applicable to the present dataset, but List and Moran (2013) could at least be acknowledged. There are also relevant tools from biological phylogeny inference that could be applied (paup, MrBayes, etc.).

Approach and methodology
- Alignment procedure: the memory/runtime limitations seem to be a significant drawback, restricting comparisons to at most 5 languages. Given the multi-language setting and the use of phylogenetic trees, it would be valuable to include more languages. I am interested in what strategies the authors have in mind to overcome this.
- Phylogenetic tree: neighbor joining has known downsides (e.g., needing to specify the root manually). Could more advanced methods be considered?
- Do you run EM to convergence, or do you use an alternative stopping criterion?

Data
- Two datasets are combined, one consisting of cognates and one not necessarily so (the Swadesh lists). Have you considered how this mixture might influence the results?
- The data are in orthographic form, which may obscure many correspondences, especially across different scripts. As a result, the learned rules might reflect script conversion rather than genuine linguistic correspondences. This seems like a limitation that could be mitigated by using phonetic transcriptions.

Unclear points
- What is meant by the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules”? (line 286)
- The merging procedure in the maximization step was not entirely clear to me.

Minor issue
- “focus in on” -> “focus on” (line 440)
