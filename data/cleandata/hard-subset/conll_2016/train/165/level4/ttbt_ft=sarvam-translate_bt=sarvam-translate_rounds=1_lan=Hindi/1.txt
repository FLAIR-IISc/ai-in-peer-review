This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between languages based on MDL. The authors develop a model of correspondences between words with similar meanings in several Slavic languages. They develop code for rules matching substrings in two or more languages and formulate an MDL objective that balances the model's description and the data given to the model.

The model is trained with EM and tested on a set of 13 Slavic languages. The results are shown by several distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and examples of the correspondences found.

The motivation and construction of this approach are understandable. The MDL seems like a reasonable tool for attacking the problem, and the motivation for employing EM is well presented. However, I must admit that some derivations were not entirely clear to me.

The authors point to the similarity of the MDL objective to Bayesian inference, and think about the application of Bayesian inference in (biological) phylogenetic inference, for example, using the MrBayes tool. An empirical comparison here could be insightful.

Related Works:
- Lack of comparison methods for borrowing and cognate detection or other computational methods for historical linguistics. For example, studies by Alexander Bouchard-Kot, Tandy Warnow, Lue Nachle, and Andrew Kitchen. Some may not have the tools available to apply in the given datasets, but one can refer to List and Moran (2013). There are also relevant tools for biological phylogenetic inference that can be applied (Pop, MrBayes, etc.).

Approach and Methodology
- Alignment process: Memory/runtime constraint seems to be a major drawback, allowing comparison of a maximum of 5 languages. It would be interesting to see more languages as long as many languages are involved, and there are phylogenetic trees. I am curious what ideas the authors have to deal with this issue.
- Phylogenetic tree: The disadvantages of using neighbor joining to create phylogenetic trees (such as manually specifying the root) are known. What about more sophisticated methods?
- Do you run EM to convergence or is there any other stopping criterion?

Data
- Two datasets are mixed, one of cognates and one of non-cognates (native lists). Have you considered how this might affect the results?
- The data is in orthographic form, which can hide many correspondences. This is especially evident in languages with different scripts. Therefore, the rules learned may indicate script changes rather than actual linguistic correspondences. This seems like a drawback that can be avoided by working at the level of phonetic scripts.

Unclear Points
- "What is the optimal unigram for symbol uses in all rules"? (line 286)
- The merging done in the maximization step was not entirely clear to me.

Minor Issue
- "Focus in on" -> "Focus on" (line 440)
