

# Peer Review

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper introduces an MDL-based approach for discovering statistically significant correspondences between words from multiple languages without imposing arbitrary assumptions about the shape or distribution of these correspondences. The authors propose an efficient algorithm that can handle more than two languages simultaneously, allowing for detailed quantification of linguistic similarities across all subsets of languages. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. **A theoretically grounded method for multi-language correspondence discovery**: The paper successfully applies MDL to discover string-level correspondences without making assumptions about their distribution, providing an objective approach to linguistic analysis.

2. **An algorithm for simultaneous multi-language analysis**: The authors develop a deterministic algorithm that can analyze more than two languages at once, enabling the discovery of linguistic subgroups and detailed quantification of similarities across all language subsets.

3. **Demonstration of linguistic insights through Slavic language analysis**: The paper provides compelling empirical evidence showing the method can correctly identify linguistic groupings and reveal subtle patterns in language relationships, including the identification of Bulgarian-Russian similarity that is not immediately apparent from pairwise analysis.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical soundness and novelty of approach**: The paper's use of MDL for correspondence discovery is theoretically well-motivated and addresses a key limitation of existing methods that impose arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions. The authors clearly articulate how MDL provides a principled way to discover correspondences without bias, which is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Multi-language capability with practical implementation**: The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously is a major advancement over existing pairwise approaches. The paper demonstrates this capability with a four-language analysis (Czech, Polish, Russian, Bulgarian), showing how the method reveals linguistic subgroups and quantifies similarities across all language subsets in a way that pairwise analysis cannot.

3. **Insightful linguistic analysis of Slavic languages**: The experiments provide meaningful linguistic insights, such as identifying Bulgarian and Macedonian as outliers due to their loss of case declension, and revealing that Russian and Bulgarian share significant similarity that is not apparent from pairwise comparisons. The paper's phylogenetic tree based on NCD values aligns well with established linguistic classifications.

4. **Flexible control over level of detail**: The introduction of data over-weighting as a mechanism to control the granularity of discovered correspondences is particularly valuable. This allows linguists to adjust the level of detail according to their needs, making the method more practical for linguistic analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper fails to compare its approach with existing multi-language correspondence discovery methods. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the relative performance and novelty of the proposed method. The authors claim their approach is "without prior assumptions," but this claim needs to be substantiated by showing how it outperforms methods that make different assumptions.

2. **Limited evaluation scope**: The evaluation is restricted to Slavic languages, which are known to have relatively straightforward correspondences. It's unclear how the method would perform on language families with more complex relationships or on languages with very different writing systems. A broader evaluation would strengthen the claims.

3. **Computational complexity and scalability concerns**: The paper notes that their current implementation can process up to five languages within hours on modest hardware, but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of computational complexity or scalability. This limitation could be a significant barrier for practical applications involving more languages or larger datasets.

4. **Lack of discussion on downstream NLP applications**: While the paper mentions applications in "multi-language natural language processing," it doesn't demonstrate how the discovered correspondences could be used in practical NLP tasks. Without this, the contribution's utility for the broader NLP community remains unclear.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a comparison of your method's performance with at least one existing state-of-the-art approach for multi-language correspondence discovery, particularly focusing on how your approach handles the "arbitrary assumptions" problem mentioned in the introduction?

2. The paper mentions that the method can be extended to handle more than five languages. Could you elaborate on the computational bottlenecks and potential strategies for scaling the approach to larger language sets or more complex data?

3. How would you expect your method to perform on language families with very different linguistic structures (e.g., Sino-Tibetan languages or languages with tonal systems)? Have you considered testing on non-Indo-European language families?

4. The paper states that "the algorithm is agnostic to gaps in data," but doesn't discuss how missing data might affect the results. Could you clarify how the method handles languages with incomplete cognate sets?

5. The authors mention potential applications in "multi-language natural language processing," but don't provide concrete examples. Could you elaborate on how the discovered correspondences could be directly applied to improve machine translation or other NLP tasks?
