

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering statistically significant correspondences between multiple languages, addressing limitations of prior work that often assume specific distributions or are limited to pairwise comparisons. The main contributions, in order of strength, are: (1) a theoretically grounded framework for discovering language correspondences without restrictive assumptions, (2) an efficient algorithm for simultaneously analyzing multiple languages and quantifying similarities across all subsets, and (3) a practical demonstration showing meaningful linguistic insights through Slavic language analysis.

Strengths: First, the MDL foundation provides a statistically principled approach that avoids arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions, which is a significant methodological advance over prior work. Second, the ability to analyze multiple languages simultaneously enables nuanced quantification of linguistic similarities beyond pairwise comparisons, as demonstrated in the four-language experiment where the method reveals subtle linguistic groupings. Third, the data over-weighting technique is a clever practical innovation that allows linguists to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences, bridging the gap between statistical significance and linguistic utility.

Weaknesses: First, the paper lacks rigorous comparison against state-of-the-art methods for correspondence discovery, making it difficult to assess the relative performance gains. Second, the evaluation is limited to Slavic languages without demonstrating generalizability to other language families, raising questions about broader applicability. Third, the computational complexity for larger language sets is not adequately addressed, as the paper notes the algorithm "can process up to five languages" with current implementation, which limits practical utility for more extensive linguistic studies. Fourth, the paper doesn't clearly distinguish between statistical significance and linguistic meaningfulness, potentially conflating mathematical patterns with actual linguistic phenomena.

Questions to Authors: How do you quantify the linguistic significance of discovered correspondences beyond statistical measures? Could you provide a comparison with existing methods (e.g., the approach by Wettig et al. 2011) on the same dataset? How would you handle cases where statistical significance conflicts with established linguistic knowledge?
