Contributions:
- MDL-based method to discover string-level correspondences across languages without assuming a correspondence distribution.
- Deterministic algorithm that analyzes more than two languages simultaneously, quantifying similarities across all language subsets.
- Empirical Slavic-language study showing subgroup discovery and subtle patterns (e.g., Bulgarian–Russian similarity) not visible in pairwise analyses.

Strengths:
- Theoretically grounded, novel application of MDL that avoids arbitrary assumptions about correspondences.
- Practical multi-language capability demonstrated on four languages, surpassing pairwise approaches.
- Provides meaningful linguistic insights and a phylogenetic tree aligning with established classifications.
- Data over-weighting mechanism allows flexible control over the granularity of discovered correspondences.

Weaknesses:
- No comparison with state-of-the-art multi-language correspondence methods to validate claims.
- Evaluation limited to Slavic languages; unclear performance on typologically different families or writing systems.
- Scalability concerns: limited to ~5 languages in current implementation with no detailed complexity analysis.
- No demonstration of how discovered correspondences benefit downstream NLP applications.

Questions to authors:
- Please provide comparisons with at least one existing state-of-the-art multi-language correspondence method.
- What are the main computational bottlenecks and possible strategies to scale to larger language sets?
- How would the method handle typologically different families (e.g., Sino-Tibetan or tonal languages)?
- How does the algorithm deal with missing or incomplete cognate data in practice?
- Can you give concrete examples of applying discovered correspondences to improve MT or other NLP tasks?
