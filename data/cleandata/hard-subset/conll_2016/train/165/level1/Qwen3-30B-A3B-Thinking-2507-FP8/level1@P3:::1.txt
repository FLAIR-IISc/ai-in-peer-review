

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering string-level correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about the distribution or shape of these correspondences. The authors present an efficient algorithm for inferring correspondence rules directly from data, with a focus on Slavic languages. As I see them, the paper's main contributions are: (1) a principled application of MDL to cross-lingual correspondence discovery without distributional assumptions, (2) an efficient algorithm for computing optimal alignments across multiple languages, and (3) a framework for quantifying linguistic similarity across all subsets of analyzed languages.

## Strengths
1. **Methodological innovation**: The paper's core contribution is its principled application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery. By avoiding assumptions about correspondence distributions, the method provides objective, unbiased string-level measures of linguistic similarity. This is a significant advancement over previous approaches that often impose arbitrary assumptions (e.g., Markov models) that may lead to "similes" rather than true correspondences. The MDL framework naturally balances model complexity and data fit, providing a theoretically sound foundation for the approach.

2. **Comprehensive evaluation framework**: The paper demonstrates a thorough evaluation across multiple languages, including both pairwise and four-way analyses. The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values aligns well with established linguistic classifications, providing strong empirical validation. The ability to quantify similarities across all language subsets (not just pairwise) is particularly valuable, as existing methods struggle with simultaneous multi-language analysis.

3. **Practical implementation details**: The authors provide clear implementation details, including computational complexity considerations and a useful data over-weighting technique that allows linguists to control the level of detail in the discovered correspondences. The algorithm's deterministic nature is highlighted as a strength for linguistic applications, where reproducibility is crucial.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper's evaluation is restricted to Slavic languages, which are relatively closely related. This limits the assessment of the method's generalizability to more diverse language families (e.g., Indo-European vs. Sino-Tibetan, or languages with vastly different writing systems). Without such evaluation, it's unclear how broadly applicable the approach is, which is a significant limitation for a method claiming to be generally applicable.

2. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art**: The paper mentions related work but fails to provide a direct comparison with current methods for cross-lingual correspondence discovery. For example, how does it compare to Snyder & Barzilay (2008) or Wettig et al. (2011) in terms of accuracy, computational efficiency, or ability to discover non-trivial correspondences? Without such comparisons, the paper cannot convincingly demonstrate the superiority of the proposed approach.

3. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper does not adequately address the method's limitations. The data over-weighting technique is introduced as a useful feature, but the paper doesn't discuss when this might lead to spurious correspondences or how to objectively determine the right amount of over-weighting. The method's sensitivity to data quality (e.g., handling false cognates or incomplete data) is also not addressed, which is crucial for practical applications.

## Questions to Authors
1. How would your method handle cases where languages have significantly different writing systems (e.g., Arabic and Chinese), and what adjustments would be needed to the algorithm?

2. The paper mentions computational constraints for handling more than five languages. What specific bottlenecks would you encounter, and how might they be addressed in future work?

3. The paper assumes the input data consists of true cognates. How would your method perform with noisy or imperfect cognate data, which is common in linguistic datasets?
