

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering correspondences between multiple languages without assuming a specific distribution of correspondences. The authors present an efficient algorithm that can handle more than two languages simultaneously and allows for control over the level of detail in discovered correspondences through data over-weighting. The main contributions are: (1) a theoretically sound MDL approach to correspondence discovery that avoids arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions; (2) an efficient algorithm for inferring correspondence rules from data that handles multiple languages; and (3) a practical method for controlling the granularity of discovered correspondences.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Novelty**: The paper makes a clear and compelling case for using MDL to address a fundamental limitation in existing linguistic correspondence discovery methods. Previous approaches either impose arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions or are limited to pairwise comparisons. The authors correctly identify that MDL provides an objective, statistically well-founded approach that avoids these pitfalls. Their implementation of the MDL principle is well-motivated and properly contextualized within the literature.

2. **Empirical Validation**: The experiments on Slavic languages are well-designed and provide strong evidence for the approach's efficacy. The pairwise NCD analysis correctly recovers expected linguistic groupings (West Slavic, East Slavic, South Slavic), and the four-way analysis demonstrates the method's ability to quantify similarities between all language subsets simultaneously. The phylogenetic tree in Figure 1 aligns well with established linguistic classifications, providing convincing evidence of the approach's validity.

3. **Practical Utility of Data Over-Weighting**: The introduction of data over-weighting as a technique to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences is a particularly valuable contribution. This addresses a practical need for linguists who require different levels of granularity in their analyses. The examples in Table 3 clearly demonstrate how this technique allows for selecting from a desired level of detail, making the method more flexible for real-world applications.

4. **Clear Examples of Discovered Correspondences**: The paper provides rich examples of the discovered correspondences (Tables 3 and 4), showing that the method can identify correspondences at different levels of granularity (phonological, orthographic, morphological) and across different language subsets. This qualitative evidence strongly supports the claim that the method discovers meaningful linguistic patterns.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods**: The paper claims to address limitations of existing approaches but fails to provide a quantitative comparison with current state-of-the-art methods. Without such a comparison, it's difficult to assess the actual improvement offered by the proposed method. The authors should demonstrate how their approach outperforms existing methods on standard benchmarks.

2. **Computational Complexity Concerns**: The paper notes that their implementation can process up to five languages within a few hours on a modest machine, but provides no analysis of how computational complexity scales with the number of languages or dataset size. For a method intended for practical linguistic analysis, this is a significant limitation that needs to be addressed.

3. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: The experiments are exclusively on Slavic languages, which share many similarities. The paper doesn't discuss how the method would perform on more diverse language families with very different writing systems or morphological structures. A broader evaluation would strengthen the claim that the method is generally applicable.

4. **Lack of Quantitative Metrics for "Non-Trivial" Correspondences**: The paper claims to discover "non-trivial associations" but doesn't provide clear quantitative metrics for what constitutes "non-trivial" in this context. The evaluation relies heavily on qualitative analysis, making it difficult to objectively assess the novelty and significance of the discovered correspondences.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how computational complexity scales with the number of languages and dataset size? What specific optimizations were used to achieve the current runtime performance?

2. How does your method compare quantitatively with existing approaches for correspondence discovery? Could you provide metrics on accuracy, precision, or recall compared to established baselines?

3. How would your approach handle languages with fundamentally different writing systems (e.g., Arabic vs. Latin) or very different morphological structures, which weren't covered in your Slavic language experiments?

This paper presents a theoretically sound and practically useful approach to linguistic correspondence discovery. The main strengths are its theoretical foundation using MDL, the demonstration of meaningful linguistic patterns, and the useful data over-weighting technique. However, the lack of comparison with existing methods, computational complexity concerns, and limited scope of evaluation are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed for the paper to be considered for acceptance.
