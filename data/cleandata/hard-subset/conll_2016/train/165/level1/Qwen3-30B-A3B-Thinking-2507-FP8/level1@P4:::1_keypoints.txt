- Proposes an MDL-based framework to discover statistically significant correspondences between multiple languages without assuming correspondence distributions.  
- Introduces an efficient algorithm using deterministic EM that can handle more than two languages simultaneously.  
- Evaluates on Slavic languages, showing the method identifies linguistic subgroups and quantifies similarities across language subsets.  
- Novel MDL formulation grounded in information theory that avoids arbitrary assumptions and provides a principled significance measure.  
- Experiments produce linguistically meaningful results (phylogenetic tree and multiway similarities) beyond pairwise methods.  
- Offers practical control over result granularity via data over-weighting to tune correspondence detail.  
- Lacks thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art cross-lingual correspondence methods on performance and efficiency.  
- Provides insufficient detail on data curation and quality (Swadesh lists, loanwords, and handling of multiple possible cognates).  
- Does not analyze scalability or computational complexity as the number of languages increases beyond small N.  
- Asks for clarification on how true multi-way correspondences (all languages simultaneously) are handled versus aggregated pairwise correspondences.  
- Requests explanation of how the MDL algorithm resolves ambiguous word forms mapping to multiple correspondences and the impact on results.  
- Seeks detailed runtime and memory scaling analysis with respect to number of languages and dataset size.
