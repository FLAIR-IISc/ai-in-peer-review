

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering statistically significant correspondences between multiple languages without imposing arbitrary assumptions about the distribution or shape of correspondences. The authors argue that existing methods for discovering linguistic correspondences are limited to pairs of languages or make restrictive assumptions, which can lead to finding "similes" rather than the true correspondences. Their main contributions are:

1. A novel MDL-based framework for discovering correspondences between multiple languages that avoids arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions.
2. An efficient algorithm for discovering these correspondences that can handle more than two languages simultaneously, with a deterministic training procedure using Expectation-Maximization.
3. A comprehensive evaluation on Slavic languages showing the approach can identify linguistic subgroups and quantitatively analyze similarities between all language subsets.

## Strengths

1. **Novel MDL formulation for linguistic correspondences**: The paper makes a strong case for using MDL to avoid the arbitrary assumptions that plague existing methods. The authors provide a well-justified, principled approach to measuring the statistical significance of correspondences without assuming any underlying distribution. Their two-part MDL formulation, including the model code and data code, is technically sound and properly grounded in information theory. This represents a significant methodological contribution to the field.

2. **Effective evaluation on Slavic languages**: The experiments demonstrate the approach's ability to produce linguistically meaningful results. The pairwise analysis successfully constructs a phylogenetic tree that aligns with established linguistic classifications, while the four-way analysis reveals nuanced similarities between language subsets that pairwise methods cannot capture. The paper effectively shows how the approach can identify linguistic subgroups and quantify similarities at different levels of detail.

3. **Practical control over granularity via data over-weighting**: The authors introduce a useful technique for controlling the level of detail in discovered correspondences by over-representing data. This provides linguists with a practical way to adjust the granularity of results based on their needs, which is a valuable contribution to the field. The paper demonstrates this technique effectively with clear examples showing how it produces correspondences at different levels of detail.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper lacks a thorough comparison with recent approaches for cross-lingual correspondence discovery. While the authors mention related work, they don't provide a detailed comparison of their method against the most relevant recent approaches in terms of performance metrics, computational efficiency, or ability to handle multiple languages. This weakens the claim of novelty and makes it difficult to assess the relative advantages of their approach.

2. **Limited discussion of data quality and limitations**: The paper mentions using Swadesh lists from Wiktionary but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how these lists were curated, validated, or processed to ensure they contain cognates rather than loanwords. This is critical for linguistic correspondence discovery, as the quality of input data significantly impacts results. The authors also don't adequately address how their approach handles cases where words have multiple possible correspondences due to linguistic variation.

3. **Scalability concerns not properly addressed**: While the paper mentions the approach can handle up to five languages within a few hours, it lacks a thorough analysis of computational complexity as the number of languages increases. The authors state they "plan to extend this to higher N in future work" but don't provide any analysis of the scaling behavior or bottlenecks. This is a significant limitation for a method that claims to handle multiple languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the approach can discover correspondences between "any subset of languages," but the examples primarily show pairwise or three-way correspondences. Can you clarify how the method handles true multi-way correspondences (involving all languages simultaneously) versus combinations of pairwise correspondences?

2. The paper doesn't provide a clear analysis of how the MDL approach handles ambiguous word forms that could map to multiple correspondences. How does the algorithm resolve such ambiguities, and what impact might this have on the discovered correspondences?

3. The computational complexity analysis is limited to a single data set. Could you provide more detailed information about how the runtime and memory requirements scale with the number of languages and the size of the data set?
