- Summary: Proposes an MDL-based method and deterministic EM algorithm to discover string-level correspondences across multiple languages, evaluated on Slavic datasets.  
- Contribution: Novel principled application of MDL for multilingual correspondence discovery without strong prior assumptions.  
- Contribution: Efficient deterministic algorithm using EM that can handle more-than-pairwise language analysis.  
- Contribution: Quantitative and qualitative evaluation producing phylogenetic trees, subgroup identification, and shared description-length analyses.  
- Strength: Strong theoretical grounding with clear MDL derivations and encoding scheme.  
- Strength: Moves beyond pairwise comparisons, enabling simultaneous analysis of multiple languages.  
- Strength: Produces linguistically meaningful insights that align with established classifications.  
- Strength: Allows incorporation of linguistic expertise and adjustable granularity via data over-weighting.  
- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation across datasets (Swadesh lists, pan-Slavic cognates) with interpretable examples.  
- Weakness: Experiments are limited to Slavic languages, leaving cross-family generalization untested.  
- Weakness: Scalability concerns—runtime (hours for five languages) may not extend to much larger datasets or many languages.  
- Weakness: Method assumes high-quality, gap-free input data and does not explore noisy/incomplete scenarios.  
- Weakness: Unclear whether discovered correspondences are directly useful or interpretable for downstream tasks (e.g., MT, historical linguistics).  
- Question: How would the method handle non-alphabetic scripts (logographic or syllabic), and are adaptations needed?  
- Question: Can the algorithm scale to hundreds of languages or much larger vocabularies; what are the main bottlenecks and remedies?  
- Question: How robust is the method to noisy or incomplete data, or languages with significant gaps/errors?  
- Overall: Well-motivated and technically sound with clear potential, but needs further work on generalizability, scalability, and robustness.
