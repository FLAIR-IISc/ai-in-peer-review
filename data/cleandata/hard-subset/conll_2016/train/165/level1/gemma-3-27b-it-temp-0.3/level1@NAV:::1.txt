## Review of “Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL”

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between words across multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea – treating correspondences as statistically significant patterns without imposing prior assumptions – is compelling and addresses limitations of existing statistical methods. The authors propose an efficient algorithm for inferring these correspondences and demonstrate its efficacy on a set of Slavic languages, showcasing both pairwise analyses and a more detailed four-language analysis.

The primary contribution of this work lies in extending correspondence discovery beyond pairwise language comparisons. Existing methods often struggle with the computational complexity of analyzing multiple languages simultaneously. This paper successfully demonstrates a scalable approach, enabling the quantification of similarity across all subsets of languages, offering a richer understanding of linguistic relationships than previously possible. A second, significant contribution is the application of MDL to this problem. This provides a statistically grounded framework for identifying meaningful correspondences, avoiding the pitfalls of arbitrary assumptions often found in other approaches. Finally, the detailed experimental evaluation, including both quantitative (NCD-based phylogeny) and qualitative (example correspondences) results, provides strong evidence for the method’s effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Multi-Language Approach:** The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously is a significant advancement. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this allows for a more nuanced understanding of linguistic relationships, revealing patterns not visible in pairwise comparisons.
2. **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The use of MDL provides a robust and statistically principled framework for correspondence discovery. This contrasts with methods relying on ad-hoc assumptions about the distribution of correspondences.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and provide both quantitative and qualitative evidence supporting the method’s efficacy. The inclusion of data over-weighting demonstrates a practical consideration for real-world data limitations.
4. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The deterministic nature of the algorithm is a strength, allowing for reproducible results and facilitating interpretability.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Complexity:** While the authors claim efficiency, the description of the algorithm and the mention of processing limitations (e.g., "up to five languages") suggest potential scalability issues for larger datasets or more languages. A more detailed analysis of the algorithm’s time and space complexity would be beneficial.
2. **Data Dependency:** The results are heavily reliant on the quality and representativeness of the Swadesh lists. The authors acknowledge this, but a discussion of how the method performs with different types of linguistic data (e.g., texts beyond basic vocabulary) would strengthen the paper.
3. **Limited Comparison:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods for cognate identification or linguistic reconstruction. Demonstrating superior performance against established baselines would further validate the proposed approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the algorithm’s time and space complexity, particularly as it scales with the number of languages and the size of the vocabulary?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the initial alignment (null alignment vs. greedy alignment)?
3.  Have you explored the use of different data weighting schemes beyond the simple over-weighting described in Section 4.5? Could adaptive weighting based on data quality be beneficial?
