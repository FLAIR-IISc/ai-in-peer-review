This paper presents an approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors aim to identify statistically significant correspondences without imposing prior assumptions about their shape or distribution, and they demonstrate the method on a set of Slavic languages.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **MDL-based Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The core contribution is the application of the MDL principle to the problem of identifying correspondences across multiple languages. This provides a statistically grounded approach that avoids arbitrary assumptions common in other methods. The formalization of the MDL code, including the encoding of model and data components, is a significant element.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm for Correspondence Inference:**  The authors propose an efficient, deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) to infer correspondence rules directly from data. The detailed explanation of the alignment procedure and the optimization steps within the EM framework demonstrates a practical implementation of the MDL principle.
3. **Empirical Validation on Slavic Languages & Quantitative Analysis:** The experiments on Slavic languages provide a concrete validation of the proposed approach.  The analysis not only confirms the method’s ability to discover linguistically plausible correspondences, but also extends to quantitative assessment of language similarity using Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) and shared Description Length (sDL), offering a novel way to analyze linguistic relationships.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of MDL Application:** Applying MDL to the task of multi-language correspondence discovery is a novel and promising approach. The framework addresses the limitations of existing methods by avoiding strong prior assumptions and offering a statistically sound foundation.
2. **Clear Formalization & Algorithm Description:** The paper provides a detailed and well-defined description of the MDL code and the EM algorithm. The clarity of the mathematical formulation and the explanation of the alignment procedure significantly enhance the reproducibility and understanding of the work. The authors address computational considerations with the discussion of partial order reduction.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The experimental setup is thorough, including pairwise analyses, phylogenetic tree construction, and detailed analysis of four languages. The presentation of both qualitative examples of correspondences and quantitative measures (NCD, sDL) strengthens the evaluation. The impact of data over-weighting is also assessed.
4. **Detailed Analysis and Insights:** The authors go beyond simply presenting results; they offer insightful interpretations of the findings, connecting them to linguistic theory and highlighting the benefits of the approach for quantifying language similarity.  The discussion of why certain languages show greater similarity or divergence is particularly valuable.
5. **Deterministic Approach:** The deterministic nature of the algorithm (before the introduction of data weighting) is a strength, allowing for reproducibility and facilitating verification of the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:** While the authors mention the algorithm can process up to five languages, they acknowledge limitations in memory consumption and runtime. The computational complexity is also discussed. The scalability of the method to larger datasets and a greater number of languages remains a potential concern that needs further investigation.  A more detailed analysis of the bottleneck (memory or runtime) and potential optimizations would be beneficial.
2. **Data Dependency & Limitations:** The quality and representativeness of the input data (Swadesh lists and cognate sets) directly impact the results. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of potential biases in the data and their influence on the learned correspondences would be valuable. The reliance on cognate tuples defined for *all* languages in the four-way analysis limits the scope of analysis and could potentially introduce bias.
3. **Interpretation of Shared Description Length:** While the introduction of sDL is a valuable contribution, the interpretation of its values requires careful consideration. The paper provides some explanation, but a more thorough discussion of how sDL relates to actual linguistic similarity and the limitations of using it as a metric would strengthen the analysis. It's not immediately obvious *why* a higher sDL indicates less shared structure.
4. **Limited Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper focuses primarily on presenting the new approach and its application to Slavic languages. A more direct comparison to existing methods for correspondence discovery, particularly those addressing similar challenges, would help to establish the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach more clearly.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational complexity of the algorithm, particularly concerning the memory usage as the number of languages increases? What optimizations are being considered to improve scalability?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of data (e.g., different Swadesh lists, inclusion/exclusion of certain cognates)? Have you explored techniques for mitigating the impact of noisy or incomplete data?
3.  Could you elaborate on the relationship between the sDL values and the underlying linguistic similarity? Are there specific thresholds or ranges of sDL values that can be interpreted as indicating different levels of similarity?
4.  How does this method compare in terms of accuracy and efficiency to other approaches, such as Bayesian phylogenetic methods or alignment-based approaches, on the same datasets?
