This paper presents an interesting approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea – treating correspondences as associated strings without pre-defined distributional assumptions – is a strong point, and the algorithm appears to be a novel application of MDL to this problem. The experimental evaluation on Slavic languages is well-conducted and demonstrates the method's ability to identify both expected and potentially insightful linguistic patterns.

**Contributions:**

1. **MDL-based Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is the formulation of multi-language correspondence discovery as an MDL problem. This provides a statistically grounded framework, avoiding the arbitrary assumptions often found in other approaches. The authors clearly articulate the benefits of this approach, specifically regarding objectivity and the ability to handle multiple languages simultaneously.
2. **Efficient Algorithm for Correspondence Inference:** The paper proposes a deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) for inferring correspondence rules within the MDL framework. The described optimization strategy (merging compatible rules) is a key algorithmic element, and the discussion of computational complexity, while acknowledging limitations, demonstrates practical considerations.
3. **Empirical Validation on Slavic Languages:** The experimental results, including phylogenetic tree construction and detailed analyses of four languages, provide strong empirical support for the proposed approach. The demonstration of the method’s ability to quantify similarity at different granularity levels, and the exploration of data over-weighting, adds substantial value.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** Applying the MDL principle to discover correspondences between *multiple* languages, rather than just pairs, is genuinely novel. The paper effectively positions itself within existing work and highlights the advantages of its approach.
2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** Grounding the method in MDL provides a solid theoretical basis, offering a clear principle for model selection and avoiding ad-hoc choices. The detailed explanation of the MDL code design (model code and data code) is commendable.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and thoroughly presented. The use of both pairwise analysis (with comparison to linguistic classifications) and the four-way analysis demonstrates the method's versatility. The inclusion of shared description length analysis is a particularly insightful addition.
4. **Clear and Detailed Explanations:** The paper is generally well-written and explains complex concepts (MDL, EM) in a clear and accessible manner. The inclusion of examples, such as the correspondence examples in Table 3, helps to illustrate the method's behavior.
5. **Practical Considerations:** The discussion of computational complexity and the limitations of the current implementation are valuable. The exploration of data over-weighting as a way to control granularity adds to the practical relevance of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:** Despite the discussion of computational complexity, the stated limit of processing “up to five languages” on a standard desktop machine raises concerns about the scalability of the algorithm to larger language sets or more complex datasets. A more thorough discussion of scalability bottlenecks and potential optimizations would be beneficial.
2. **Data Dependency:** The results are heavily reliant on the quality and characteristics of the Swadesh list data. The authors acknowledge this to some extent, but further investigation into the robustness of the method to variations in data quality and different types of linguistic data would strengthen the findings.
3. **Limited Comparative Analysis:** While the paper positions itself relative to existing work, a more direct comparison with other multi-language correspondence methods (even if these methods have their own limitations) would be valuable. Quantitatively comparing the performance of the proposed method against alternatives would provide stronger evidence of its superiority.
4. **Over-Weighting Subjectivity:** The introduction of data over-weighting, while potentially useful, introduces a degree of subjectivity. While the authors acknowledge this, a more rigorous justification for its use and guidance on how to select appropriate weighting parameters would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What are the primary computational bottlenecks limiting the scalability of the algorithm? Are there specific optimizations (e.g., data structures, parallelization) that could be explored to address these bottlenecks?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of input data (e.g., different types of lexical data, different sources)? Have you investigated the robustness of the method to noisy or incomplete data?
3. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with other relevant multi-language correspondence methods, even if those methods are not directly comparable in all aspects?
4. What criteria can be used to objectively determine the appropriate level of data over-weighting for a given dataset and research question?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 (NCD-based phylogenetic tree) could benefit from clearer labeling of the branches and more detailed explanation of the tree's structure.
*   The notation used throughout the paper is generally clear, but some equations could be further clarified (e.g., providing more context for the variables).
*   The final sentence ("Please note that this is purely on the basis of superficial word form similarity.") seems unnecessarily dismissive of the results. While the method does operate on surface forms, it still reveals meaningful linguistic patterns.
