## Review of “Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL”

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors tackle the problem of finding statistically significant, unbiased string-level similarities without pre-defined assumptions about correspondence shapes or distributions, a limitation of many existing methods. The core contribution lies in the development of an efficient, deterministic algorithm based on MDL, capable of handling more than two languages simultaneously. A secondary, but valuable, contribution is the application of this method to a set of Slavic languages, yielding insightful analyses including a phylogenetic tree and detailed quantification of linguistic similarity across various language subsets. Finally, the introduction of data over-weighting provides a mechanism for incorporating linguistic knowledge and controlling the granularity of the discovered correspondences.

The strengths of this submission are compelling. The use of MDL provides a strong theoretical foundation for the approach, grounding the search for correspondences in information theory. The algorithm’s deterministic nature is a significant advantage, promoting reproducibility and interpretability. The experimental results on Slavic languages demonstrate the method’s effectiveness, successfully recovering expected linguistic relationships and revealing subtle similarities between languages. The detailed analysis, going beyond simple pairwise comparisons to explore multi-language correspondences, is a key differentiator from existing work. The introduction of data over-weighting is a clever extension, allowing for a degree of user control over the granularity of results.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses. The scalability of the algorithm, while noted as a current limitation, needs further elaboration. While the authors state it can handle up to five languages, the computational cost and memory requirements should be discussed in more detail. The description of the experimental setup, particularly regarding the choice of Swadesh lists, could be more thorough. The impact of data quality and potential biases within the chosen data set are not adequately addressed. Furthermore, the paper leans heavily on demonstration of the method’s capabilities but lacks a quantitative comparison against existing approaches on the same datasets.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and memory requirements of your algorithm as the number of languages (N) increases?
2. How sensitive are the results to the quality and completeness of the input data (e.g., the Swadesh lists)? Have you considered methods for dealing with missing or ambiguous cognates?
3.  Could you briefly discuss how the results change when comparing to or leveraging already existing, well-established linguistic resources?
4. What experiments are planned to quantitatively compare this work to existing approaches for cognate identification or language similarity?
