This paper presents an approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to find the set of correspondence rules that most succinctly describe the given data, avoiding assumptions about the shape or distribution of correspondences. The authors provide an efficient algorithm for inferring these rules and demonstrate its efficacy on a set of Slavic languages.

The primary contribution of this work is the application of the MDL principle to the problem of multi-lingual correspondence discovery, coupled with an efficient deterministic algorithm for implementation. While the use of MDL in linguistic contexts isn't novel (as acknowledged by the authors), applying it to *multiple* languages without pre-defined assumptions is a valuable step. The algorithm itself, leveraging Expectation-Maximization (EM) and focusing on deterministic inference, is a significant technical component. The second notable contribution is the empirical validation on Slavic languages. The experiments demonstrate the method’s ability to recover linguistically plausible correspondences, confirm established language classifications, and quantify similarities between language subsets. The visualization of these similarities using a phylogenetic tree and shared description lengths provides a compelling analysis. A third, less prominent, contribution is the exploration of data over-weighting as a mechanism to control the granularity and detail of discovered correspondences.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The application of MDL to multi-lingual correspondence discovery without imposing prior assumptions is a significant advancement. Existing statistical approaches often rely on assumptions about correspondence shapes, which can bias results. This work addresses that limitation.
2. **Algorithm Efficiency:** The authors present an efficient, deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules. The use of EM and the optimization techniques (e.g., merging compatible rules) contribute to the algorithm’s practicality. The reported runtime on a desktop machine is a positive indication of its scalability.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments on Slavic languages provide a thorough evaluation of the proposed method. The pairwise analysis, phylogenetic tree construction, and four-way analysis demonstrate the algorithm’s ability to capture different aspects of linguistic similarity. The inclusion of both automatic evaluation (NCD) and qualitative analysis (examining learned correspondences) strengthens the findings.
4. **Clear Presentation of MDL Application:** The paper provides a relatively accessible explanation of the MDL principle and its application to the problem of correspondence discovery. The breakdown of the model code, data code, and alignment procedure is well-structured.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Data Scale:** While the Slavic language dataset is a good starting point, the size of the Swadesh lists is relatively small (207 entries). This limits the statistical power of the method and may lead to overfitting, especially when exploring more complex correspondences. The impact of data size on the reliability of the discovered rules should be discussed more thoroughly.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The paper lacks a comparative evaluation against state-of-the-art methods for cognate identification or multilingual alignment. Comparing the results to established techniques (e.g., those based on probabilistic models or machine learning) would provide a clearer understanding of the proposed method’s advantages and disadvantages.
3. **Over-Weighting Subjectivity:** While the concept of data over-weighting is interesting, the authors acknowledge that it introduces a degree of subjectivity. The criteria for choosing the over-weighting factor are not clearly defined, and the impact of different weighting schemes on the results is not fully explored. This raises concerns about the reproducibility and objectivity of the analysis.
4. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on presenting successful examples of discovered correspondences. A more thorough analysis of errors – i.e., instances where the algorithm fails to identify correct correspondences or generates spurious ones – would provide valuable insights into the method’s limitations and potential areas for improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How does the performance of the algorithm scale with the size of the input data? Specifically, what is the relationship between data size, runtime, and the quality of the discovered correspondences?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the over-weighting factor in the experiments? What strategies could be used to automatically determine an appropriate weighting scheme?
3.  What types of errors are most frequently observed in the learned correspondences? Are there specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., irregular sound changes) that pose particular challenges for the algorithm?
4.  What is the impact of different alignment algorithms (alternatives to Dijkstra's) on the results? Are there situations where a different algorithm might be more efficient or accurate?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a more comprehensive discussion of related work, particularly in the areas of computational linguistics and phylogenetic methods.
*   Providing a visual representation of the alignment graph used in the EM algorithm would help readers understand the algorithm's inner workings.
*   The section on data over-weighting could be expanded with more examples and a discussion of its potential applications in linguistic analysis.
