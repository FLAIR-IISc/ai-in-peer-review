This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea – to identify statistically significant correspondences without imposing prior assumptions about their shape or distribution – is compelling and well-motivated, addressing a limitation of existing statistical approaches. The application to Slavic languages is appropriate, and the experiments demonstrate the method’s ability to uncover non-trivial associations and quantify language similarity.

**Contributions:**

1. **MDL-based Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The most significant contribution is the formulation of a statistically sound framework based on MDL for discovering correspondences across multiple languages simultaneously. This moves beyond pairwise comparisons and offers a more holistic view of linguistic relationships. The avoidance of distributional assumptions is a key strength.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm for Correspondence Inference:** The development of an efficient, deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) to infer correspondence rules is a substantial technical achievement. This allows for reproducible results and provides a clear process for identifying statistically important patterns.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Slavic Languages & Quantification of Similarity:** The application of the method to a set of Slavic languages provides a concrete demonstration of its efficacy. The detailed analysis, including the NCD-based phylogenetic tree and the quantification of similarity between language subsets, offers valuable insights into the relationships between these languages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The use of MDL provides a robust statistical foundation for the approach. The authors clearly explain the MDL principle and how it is applied to the problem of correspondence discovery. The two-part MDL formulation and the encoding scheme are well-defined and justified.
2. **Novelty of Approach:** The approach of learning correspondences deterministically without prior assumptions is genuinely novel. Existing methods often rely on probabilistic models with pre-defined parameters or distributions. This work offers a refreshing alternative.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation, including pairwise analyses with phylogenetic tree construction, and a four-language experiment demonstrating the quantification of similarity between language subsets. The inclusion of both qualitative (example correspondences) and quantitative (NCD values, shared description lengths) results strengthens the evaluation.
4. **Practical Algorithm:** The EM-based algorithm appears computationally feasible, as demonstrated by the experiments on Slavic languages. The description of the algorithm and its complexity analysis are clear and informative. The discussion of potential optimizations (e.g., partial order reduction) is appreciated.
5. **Insightful Results & Discussion:** The results are insightful, confirming known linguistic relationships (e.g., grouping of West Slavic languages) and revealing subtle patterns (e.g., the relationship between Russian and other languages). The discussion of the impact of data over-weighting adds further depth to the analysis.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Dependence & Limited Scope:** While the authors acknowledge the limitations of working with only cognate tuples present in all languages, it restricts the scope of the analysis. Real-world language comparison often involves data with gaps and varying levels of coverage. The performance of the algorithm on more realistically incomplete data needs to be investigated.
2. **Over-Weighting Subjectivity:** The introduction of data over-weighting, while offering flexibility in controlling the level of detail, introduces a subjective element into the analysis. The authors acknowledge this, but the criteria for choosing the appropriate weighting factor are not fully clear. This could potentially lead to biased results.
3. **Scalability Concerns:** Although the algorithm is shown to work for four languages, the computational complexity (mentioned as O(|D| * A^2/2)) raises concerns about its scalability to a larger number of languages or much larger datasets. A more detailed analysis of the scalability limitations is needed.
4. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The paper doesn’t include a direct comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods for cognate identification or language similarity assessment, especially those that *do* impose distributional assumptions. Demonstrating superior performance compared to these methods would further strengthen the contribution.
5. **Limited Explanation of Rule Selection Criteria:** While the algorithm is deterministic, a deeper dive into *why* specific rules are selected and others are discarded would be valuable.  The mechanism of merging rules based on description length is described, but a more intuitive explanation of the driving forces behind the algorithm’s choices would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does the algorithm perform on datasets with significant gaps in data (i.e., cognates not present in all languages)? Could you provide some preliminary results or a discussion of potential modifications to address this issue?
2. What are the guidelines for selecting the appropriate data weighting factor (α) when using the data over-weighting technique? Is there a principled way to determine the optimal value, or is it purely based on linguistic intuition?
3. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the scalability of the algorithm? What are the bottlenecks, and what are potential strategies for improving its performance on larger datasets?
4. Have you considered comparing your approach with existing methods (e.g., those based on probabilistic models) on a common dataset? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
5. Could you elaborate on the intuition behind the algorithm's rule selection process? For example, can you provide examples of cases where seemingly plausible rules were rejected, and explain why?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized.
* The references are relevant and up-to-date.
* The use of tables and figures is effective in presenting the results.
* Consider adding a discussion of potential applications of the method beyond linguistic analysis, such as in machine translation or language learning.
