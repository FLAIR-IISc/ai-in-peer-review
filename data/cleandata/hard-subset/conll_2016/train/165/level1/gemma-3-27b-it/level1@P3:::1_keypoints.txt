- Introduces an MDL-based framework for discovering correspondences across multiple languages simultaneously, avoiding distributional assumptions.  
- Presents an efficient deterministic EM-based algorithm for inferring correspondence rules, enabling reproducible results.  
- Applies the method to Slavic languages, producing NCD-based phylogenetic trees and quantifying language similarity.

- Strong theoretical grounding via a clear two-part MDL formulation and encoding scheme.  
- Novel deterministic approach that learns correspondences without prior shape/distribution assumptions.  
- Comprehensive evaluation with qualitative correspondences, quantitative NCD values, phylogenetic trees, and a four-language experiment.  
- Algorithm appears computationally feasible with clear complexity analysis and suggested optimizations (e.g., partial order reduction).  
- Results confirm known relationships, reveal subtle patterns, and include useful discussion of data over-weighting effects.

- Depends on cognate tuples present in all languages, limiting applicability to incomplete real-world data.  
- Data over-weighting introduces subjectivity; criteria for choosing the weighting factor are unclear.  
- Computational complexity (O(|D| * A^2/2)) raises concerns about scalability to many languages or much larger datasets.  
- No direct comparison to existing state-of-the-art probabilistic methods to benchmark performance.  
- Insufficient intuitive explanation of why specific rules are selected or discarded beyond description-length merging.

- How does the algorithm perform on datasets with many missing cognates, and what modifications are possible?  
- What principled guidelines exist for selecting the data weighting factor (α)?  
- Can you provide a more detailed analysis of scalability bottlenecks and improvement strategies?  
- Have you compared the approach to probabilistic or other existing methods on common datasets, and what were the results?  
- Can you give intuition and examples showing why seemingly plausible rules were rejected by the algorithm?

- Paper is well-written and clearly organized.  
- References are relevant and up-to-date.  
- Tables and figures effectively present results.  
- Consider discussing potential applications beyond linguistic analysis (e.g., machine translation, language learning).
