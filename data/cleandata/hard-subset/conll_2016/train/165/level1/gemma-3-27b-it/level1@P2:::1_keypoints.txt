- Proposes an MDL-based framework for multi-language correspondence discovery, avoiding arbitrary distributional assumptions.  
- Introduces a deterministic EM-based algorithm with rule-merging optimization and discussion of computational complexity.  
- Provides empirical validation on Slavic languages, showing ability to detect expected and novel linguistic patterns and quantify similarity at multiple granularities.  
- Novel application of MDL to correspondences across multiple languages rather than just pairs.  
- Strong theoretical grounding via MDL and clear MDL code design (model code and data code).  
- Comprehensive experimental evaluation including pairwise, four-way analyses, phylogenetic trees, and shared description length.  
- Clear, accessible explanations of complex concepts with illustrative examples (e.g., Table 3).  
- Practical discussion of implementation limits and exploration of data over-weighting to control granularity.  
- Scalability concerns: current implementation reportedly handles up to ~5 languages, raising questions about larger datasets.  
- Heavy dependence on Swadesh list data; robustness to other or noisy datasets is not demonstrated.  
- Limited direct quantitative comparison with other multi-language correspondence methods.  
- Data over-weighting introduces subjectivity without rigorous justification or guidance on parameter selection.  
- Question: What are the primary computational bottlenecks and potential optimizations (data structures, parallelization)?  
- Question: How sensitive are results to different input data types, sources, or noisy/incomplete data?  
- Question: Can the authors provide more detailed comparisons with other relevant multi-language methods?  
- Question: What objective criteria should guide choosing the level of data over-weighting?  
- Suggestion: Improve Figure 1 with clearer branch labels and a more detailed explanation of tree structure.  
- Suggestion: Clarify notation and provide more context for some equations and variables.  
- Suggestion: Rephrase the final sentence that downplays results as merely “superficial word form similarity,” since surface-form analyses can still yield meaningful patterns.
