**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a method for automatically discovering correspondences between words from multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors introduce an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules and demonstrate its efficacy on a set of Slavic languages. The approach is based on treating correspondences as associated strings of characters without assuming an underlying distribution. The algorithm uses a two-part MDL code to encode the model and the data, and employs Expectation-Maximization to find good solutions.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel Application of MDL**: The authors apply the MDL principle to the problem of discovering correspondences between multiple languages, which is a new and significant contribution.
2. **Efficient Algorithm**: The proposed algorithm is efficient and can handle multiple languages, making it a valuable tool for linguistic analysis.
3. **Objective and Unbiased Results**: The approach provides objective and unbiased results, as it does not rely on prior assumptions about the distribution of correspondences.

**Strengths**

1. **Theoretical Foundations**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed approach, based on the MDL principle.
2. **Efficient Algorithm**: The algorithm is efficient and can handle multiple languages, making it a valuable tool for linguistic analysis.
3. **Experimental Results**: The experimental results demonstrate the efficacy of the approach on a set of Slavic languages.
4. **Flexibility**: The approach allows for the integration of linguistic knowledge and can be used for fine-grained analysis of similarity between languages.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational Complexity**: The algorithm has a high computational complexity, which may limit its applicability to large datasets.
2. **Data Requirements**: The approach requires a large amount of data to be effective, which may be a limitation for under-resourced languages.
3. **Overfitting**: The approach may be prone to overfitting, especially when the data is over-represented.
4. **Linguistic Assumptions**: While the approach does not rely on prior assumptions about the distribution of correspondences, it does assume that the correspondences are statistically significant, which may not always be the case.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of computational complexity, especially for large datasets?
2. Can you provide more details on how the approach handles under-resourced languages, and what strategies you would recommend for dealing with limited data?
3. How do you plan to prevent overfitting, especially when the data is over-represented?
4. Can you provide more insights into the linguistic assumptions underlying the approach, and how they may impact the results?

**Additional Comments**

The paper provides a valuable contribution to the field of linguistic analysis, and the proposed approach has the potential to be widely applicable. However, the authors should consider addressing the issues of computational complexity, data requirements, and overfitting to make the approach more robust and widely applicable.
