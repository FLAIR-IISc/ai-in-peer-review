**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach to automatically discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors aim to identify statistically significant correspondences without imposing arbitrary assumptions on their distribution or shape. They present an efficient algorithm for inferring good sets of correspondence rules directly from data and demonstrate its efficacy on a set of Slavic languages. The approach is based on a two-part MDL code, which consists of a stack of two-part codes, and uses a combination of Expectation-Maximization (EM) and Dijkstra's algorithm to find optimal alignments.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel Application of MDL**: The authors apply the MDL principle to the problem of discovering correspondences between multiple languages, which is a new and innovative approach.
2. **Efficient Algorithm**: The proposed algorithm is efficient and can handle multiple languages simultaneously, making it a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
3. **Objective and Unbiased Results**: The MDL-based approach provides objective and unbiased results, which is a major advantage over existing methods that rely on prior assumptions or linguistic knowledge.

**Strengths**

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical foundation for the proposed approach, which is based on the well-established MDL principle.
2. **Experimental Results**: The authors present experimental results on a set of Slavic languages, which demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed approach.
3. **Flexibility and Customizability**: The algorithm can be easily adapted to different languages and datasets, making it a flexible and customizable tool for linguists and researchers.
4. **Interpretability**: The results obtained from the algorithm are easy to interpret, which is essential for understanding the correspondences between languages.
5. **Scalability**: The algorithm can handle multiple languages simultaneously, making it a scalable solution for large-scale linguistic analysis.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational Complexity**: The algorithm's computational complexity may be a limitation for very large datasets or complex linguistic analyses.
2. **Data Quality**: The quality of the input data is crucial for the algorithm's performance, and poor-quality data may lead to suboptimal results.
3. **Overfitting**: The algorithm may suffer from overfitting, especially when dealing with small datasets or limited linguistic resources.
4. **Linguistic Knowledge**: While the algorithm can handle multiple languages simultaneously, it may not be able to capture subtle linguistic nuances or complexities that require expert knowledge.
5. **Comparison to Existing Methods**: The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison to existing methods for discovering correspondences between languages.
