**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper proposes a method for automatically discovering correspondences between words from multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors claim that their approach can identify statistically significant correspondences without imposing arbitrary assumptions on the distribution or shape of correspondences. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel application of the MDL principle to the problem of correspondence discovery between multiple languages.
2. An efficient algorithm for inferring correspondence rules from data, which can handle more than two languages.
3. Experimental results demonstrating the effectiveness of the approach on a set of Slavic languages.

**Strengths of the Paper**

1. **Novel application of MDL**: The authors' use of MDL to discover correspondences between multiple languages is a novel and interesting approach. MDL is a well-established principle in machine learning, and its application to this problem is a significant contribution.
2. **Efficient algorithm**: The proposed algorithm is efficient and can handle more than two languages, which is a significant improvement over existing approaches.
3. **Experimental results**: The authors provide experimental results on a set of Slavic languages, which demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach. The results are well-presented and easy to understand.

**Weaknesses of the Paper**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The authors only evaluate their approach on a set of Slavic languages, which may not be representative of all languages. A more comprehensive evaluation on a diverse set of languages would strengthen the paper.
2. **Lack of comparison to existing approaches**: The authors do not compare their approach to existing methods for correspondence discovery, which makes it difficult to assess the significance of their contribution.
3. **Over-reliance on MDL**: The authors' approach relies heavily on the MDL principle, which may not be the best choice for all types of languages or data. A more nuanced discussion of the limitations of MDL would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to extend your approach to handle more than five languages, given the current computational limitations?
2. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of your approach to existing methods for correspondence discovery?
3. How do you plan to address the potential limitations of MDL, such as its sensitivity to the choice of model class?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed approach and experimental results. However, the authors could benefit from a more comprehensive evaluation and comparison to existing approaches to strengthen their contribution.
