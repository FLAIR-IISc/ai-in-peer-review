**Summary and Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors claim that their method can automatically infer objective string-level correspondences from data without imposing arbitrary assumptions on the distribution or shape of correspondences. The primary contributions of this work are:

1. **MDL-based approach**: The authors introduce an MDL-based approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages, which provides a statistically well-founded method for identifying the best model for given data.
2. **Efficient algorithm**: The authors propose an efficient, deterministic algorithm for inferring good sets of correspondence rules directly from data, which can be used to analyze multiple languages simultaneously.
3. **Experimental evaluation**: The authors evaluate their approach on a set of Slavic languages and demonstrate its efficacy in discovering non-trivial associations and quantifying linguistic similarity.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The authors' use of MDL to discover correspondences between multiple languages is a novel and promising approach that addresses the limitations of existing methods.
2. **Efficient algorithm**: The proposed algorithm is efficient and can be used to analyze multiple languages simultaneously, making it a valuable tool for linguists and NLP researchers.
3. **Experimental evaluation**: The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation of their approach, including a comparison with linguistic classifications and an analysis of the discovered correspondences.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited scope**: The authors' evaluation is limited to a set of Slavic languages, and it is unclear how well their approach will generalize to other language families or linguistic phenomena.
2. **Over-reliance on data**: The authors' approach relies heavily on the quality and quantity of the data, and it is unclear how well their method will perform with noisy or limited data.
3. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: The authors do not provide a comparison of their approach to state-of-the-art methods in the field, making it difficult to evaluate the significance of their contributions.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of over-reliance on data, and what methods do they propose to handle noisy or limited data?
2. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the discovered correspondences, including their linguistic significance and potential applications?
3. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to handle more complex linguistic phenomena, such as morphological or syntactic correspondences?

**Additional Comments**

* The authors' use of MDL is well-motivated, and their approach has the potential to provide a more objective and statistically well-founded method for discovering correspondences between multiple languages.
* The experimental evaluation is thorough, but the authors could benefit from providing more detailed analyses of the discovered correspondences and their linguistic significance.
* The authors' approach has the potential to be applied to a wide range of NLP tasks, including machine translation, language modeling, and linguistic analysis.
