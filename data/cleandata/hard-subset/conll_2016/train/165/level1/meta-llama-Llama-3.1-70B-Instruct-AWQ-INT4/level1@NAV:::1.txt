**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors introduce an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules from data, which can be used for pairwise and multi-language analysis. The approach is evaluated on a dataset of Slavic languages, and the results show that it can automatically discover non-trivial associations, allowing for both quantitative and qualitative analysis of multiple languages.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Novel Application of MDL**: The authors apply the MDL principle to the problem of discovering correspondences between multiple languages, which is a new and innovative approach.
2. **Efficient Algorithm**: The proposed algorithm is efficient and can handle multiple languages, making it a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
3. **Quantification of Linguistic Similarity**: The authors provide a method for quantifying linguistic similarity between languages, which is a valuable contribution to the field of linguistics.

**Strengths**

1. **Robustness to Noise**: The MDL-based approach is robust to noise in the data, which is a significant advantage over other methods.
2. **Flexibility**: The algorithm can handle multiple languages and can be used for both pairwise and multi-language analysis.
3. **Interpretability**: The results of the algorithm are interpretable, providing insights into the linguistic structure of the languages being analyzed.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational Complexity**: The algorithm has a high computational complexity, which may limit its applicability to large datasets.
2. **Overfitting**: The authors note that the algorithm may overfit the data, particularly when the data is limited.
3. **Lack of Linguistic Evaluation**: The authors do not provide a thorough linguistic evaluation of the results, which may limit the validity of the approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting in the algorithm?
2. Can you provide more details on the linguistic evaluation of the results, and how you plan to validate the approach?
3. How do you plan to extend the algorithm to handle more than five languages, as mentioned in the paper?
