### Summary of the Paper
This paper presents a novel method for discovering orthographic correspondences between multiple languages simultaneously. The core of the approach is the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which seeks the set of correspondence rules that provides the most compact description of a given set of cognate words. The authors formulate this as an optimization problem and propose an efficient, deterministic, EM-like algorithm to find a good set of rules. The method is demonstrated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The evaluation includes a pairwise analysis that reconstructs a linguistically plausible phylogenetic tree, and a more detailed four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian. For the latter, the paper introduces a novel metric, "shared Description Length" (sDL), to quantify the degree of similarity between any subset of the languages under consideration.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A Principled Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is a novel, assumption-light method for finding correspondences between more than two languages. Unlike many existing methods that are restricted to pairwise comparisons, this model can identify rules that span any subset of the N input languages (e.g., a rule connecting Czech and Polish, another connecting Russian and Bulgarian, and a third connecting all four). The use of the MDL principle provides a statistically sound, objective function for model selection that avoids strong prior assumptions about the distribution or shape of correspondence rules.

2.  **A Novel Metric for Quantifying Multi-Way Language Similarity:** The paper introduces the concept of "shared Description Length" (sDL) as a way to analyze the output of their model. This metric allows for a fine-grained, quantitative breakdown of how much structural similarity exists between various subgroups of languages. This moves beyond the simple tree-based visualizations common in phylogenetics and provides a more nuanced view of complex inter-language relationships, as demonstrated in the four-language experiment (Figure 3).

### Strengths
1.  **Methodological Novelty and Soundness:** The application of MDL to the problem of discovering correspondences across multiple languages is novel and well-motivated. The framework is elegant in its ability to naturally handle correspondences between arbitrary subsets of languages without being explicitly programmed to do so. The greedy, iterative optimization procedure (merging rules that yield the greatest compression) is an intuitive and sensible heuristic for navigating the large search space. This provides a strong, data-driven alternative to methods that rely on stricter prior assumptions.

2.  **Powerful Multi-Language Analysis Capability:** The paper's key advantage over much prior work is its genuine multi-language capability. The four-way analysis is a compelling demonstration of this strength. The sDL metric (Figure 3) provides insights that would be difficult or impossible to obtain from a series of pairwise comparisons alone. For example, it simultaneously quantifies the strength of the West Slavic (CS-PL) and East/South Slavic (RU-BG) pairings, the structure unique to each language, and the common structure shared by all four. This is a significant step forward for computational comparative linguistics.

3.  **Clear and Linguistically Plausible Results:** The experimental results strongly support the validity of the approach. The pairwise NCD analysis produces a phylogenetic tree (Figure 1) that correctly groups the Slavic languages into their established West, East, and South branches, providing an excellent sanity check. Furthermore, the example correspondences shown in Tables 3 and 4 are linguistically meaningful, capturing phenomena ranging from simple orthographic substitutions (e.g., Polish `cz` to Czech `č`) to more complex patterns like liquid metathesis (e.g., Russian `оло` to Bulgarian `ла`).

4.  **Practical Control over Granularity:** The introduction of the "data over-weighting" parameter `alpha` (Section 4.5) is a pragmatic and valuable feature. While it deviates from a purely objective MDL formulation, it acknowledges that researchers may be interested in correspondences that are linguistically relevant but not statistically dominant in a small dataset. This allows a user, such as a linguist, to control the level of detail in the output, trading statistical purity for potentially more comprehensive or insightful rule sets.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Scalability and Scope of Experiments:** The paper's main selling point is its multi-language capability, yet the authors state their implementation is computationally constrained to "up to five languages" on a standard desktop machine. This is a significant limitation that curtails the method's practical application to larger language families. Furthermore, the experiments are confined entirely to the closely related Slavic languages, which share significant lexical and structural similarities. The method's performance on more diverse language groups (e.g., combining Slavic, Germanic, and Romance languages) or on languages with fundamentally different orthographies remains unevaluated.

2.  **Lack of Comparison to Baselines:** The work is presented without any quantitative comparison to alternative methods. While the phylogenetic tree is qualitatively correct, its quality is not compared to trees generated from simpler and standard methods, such as those based on normalized Levenshtein distance over the cognate lists. Similarly, the discovered correspondences are presented as examples, but there is no evaluation against a gold standard set of sound change rules from historical linguistics. Without baselines, it is difficult to assess whether the complexity of the MDL approach provides a significant advantage over simpler, faster alternatives.

3.  **Clarity of the MDL Formulation:** While the high-level idea of the MDL code is presented, the details in Section 4 are dense and could benefit from more intuition. For instance, the data-to-model codes for encoding the rule table and alphabets are presented as formulae without a clear explanation for why these specific forms (based on weak number composition) were chosen or what they represent conceptually. A small, worked example illustrating the calculation of L(M) and L(D|M) for a toy problem would greatly improve the paper's accessibility to a broader audience.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the computational bottleneck that limits the method to N=5 languages? Is the primary constraint memory usage from the graph construction in the alignment step, or CPU time? Are there clear paths to improving scalability, for example through approximation algorithms or implementation optimizations, that would allow for analysis of larger language groups?

2.  The `alpha` parameter for data over-weighting seems very useful for exploratory analysis. How sensitive are the final results to the choice of `alpha`? Could you provide a brief analysis or graph showing how key metrics (e.g., number of rules, total description length) change as `alpha` increases? This would provide valuable guidance for users of your method.

3.  Have you considered applying your method to languages with very different writing systems (e.g., Russian, Georgian, and Armenian)? This would require a mapping to a common character set (like IPA), but it would be a strong test of your model's ability to find correspondences that are not purely based on superficial orthographic similarity.
