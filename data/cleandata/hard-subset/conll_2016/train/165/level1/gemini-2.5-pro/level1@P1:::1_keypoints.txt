- Paper introduces an MDL-based method to discover orthographic correspondences across multiple languages using a deterministic, EM-like optimization.  
- Method demonstrated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, with pairwise phylogeny and a detailed four-way Czech–Polish–Russian–Bulgarian analysis.  
- Introduces shared Description Length (sDL) as a novel metric to quantify multi-way language similarity and subgroup structure.  

- Contribution: principled, assumption-light framework that discovers correspondences spanning any subset of N languages via MDL model selection.  
- Contribution: sDL metric enabling fine-grained, quantitative analysis of shared structure beyond tree-based visualizations.  

- Strength: novel and well-motivated application of MDL with an intuitive greedy merging heuristic for rule discovery.  
- Strength: genuinely multi-language analysis capability demonstrated in a compelling four-way experiment.  
- Strength: results are linguistically plausible—phylogeny matches Slavic branches and example correspondences capture realistic phenomena.  
- Strength: practical control over granularity via the data over-weighting parameter alpha for exploratory or linguistically relevant rules.  

- Weakness: limited scalability—implementation reportedly constrained to about five languages, limiting applicability to larger families.  
- Weakness: experiments confined to closely related Slavic languages; performance on typologically diverse languages or scripts is untested.  
- Weakness: no quantitative comparisons to baseline methods or evaluations against gold-standard sound-change rules.  
- Weakness: MDL formulation is dense and lacks intuitive explanation or a worked toy example for L(M) and L(D|M).  

- Question: clarify the computational bottleneck limiting N≈5—memory (alignment graph) vs CPU—and potential scalability improvements.  
- Question: how sensitive are results to the alpha over-weighting parameter; provide analysis of metrics (rule count, description length) vs alpha.  
- Question: have you tried typologically or orthographically diverse languages (e.g., different scripts) and how would you handle mapping to a common representation (e.g., IPA)?
