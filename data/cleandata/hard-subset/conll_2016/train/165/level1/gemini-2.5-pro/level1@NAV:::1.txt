**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel method for discovering orthographic correspondences across multiple languages simultaneously. The approach is framed under the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which seeks the set of correspondence rules that most compactly describes a given list of cognates. The authors propose a deterministic, EM-like algorithm that iteratively refines a rule set to minimize the total description length. The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A principled framework for extending correspondence discovery from the traditional pairwise setting to a multi-language context. This is a significant contribution, enabling a more nuanced analysis of language families. The proposed "shared Description Length" (sDL) metric is a novel and effective tool for quantifying the complex similarities between any subset of the languages under study.
2.  An efficient, deterministic algorithm for finding statistically significant correspondences without strong prior assumptions about their structure. The method also includes a practical data-weighting mechanism that allows users to control the granularity of the discovered rules.

**Strengths**

1.  The primary strength is the novelty and significance of the multi-language analysis. Moving beyond pairwise comparisons to a framework that can quantify shared structure among arbitrary subgroups of languages (as shown in Figure 3) is a powerful conceptual advance for computational linguistics and historical linguistics.
2.  The methodology is sound and well-motivated. Using the MDL principle provides an objective, information-theoretic foundation for model selection, avoiding ad-hoc parameter tuning and assumptions about the underlying distribution of correspondences.
3.  The empirical results on Slavic languages are compelling. The pairwise analysis successfully reconstructs a phylogenetically plausible tree, validating the core distance metric. More importantly, the four-way analysis effectively demonstrates the unique capabilities of the method by revealing subtle inter-group relationships that are invisible to standard pairwise approaches.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The scalability of the proposed algorithm is a major concern. The authors mention a practical limit of "up to five languages" and acknowledge computational bottlenecks. Given that the core contribution is the multi-language setting, the lack of analysis on how runtime and memory scale with the number of languages (N) is a significant omission. The problem appears to have components that are exponential in N, which may render it infeasible for larger language groups.
2.  The paper lacks a comparison to relevant baselines. While multi-language correspondence discovery is novel, the results could have been contextualized by comparing the pairwise NCD phylogeny to trees generated by other sequence-based methods, or by comparing the discovered rules to those from other cognate analysis tools. Without this, it is difficult to assess the method's practical advantages over existing approaches.
3.  The description of the MDL code in Section 4 is overly dense, making it difficult to fully grasp the model's components and their motivations. A clearer, more intuitive explanation or a running example would significantly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the scalability limitations of your approach? Specifically, how does the complexity of the alignment and maximization steps depend on the number of languages N? Are there any heuristics or approximations that could make the method practical for analyzing larger language families (e.g., N > 10)?
