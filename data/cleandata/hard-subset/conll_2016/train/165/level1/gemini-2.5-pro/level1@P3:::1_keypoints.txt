- Proposes an MDL-based method to discover correspondence rules that succinctly describe cognate tuples across multiple languages.
- Method handles more than two languages and identifies correspondences that span any subset of languages.
- Uses an EM-like iterative algorithm starting from single-character rules and greedily merging by compression gain.
- Evaluated on 13 Slavic languages; pairwise distances reconstruct a linguistically plausible phylogenetic tree.
- Introduces Shared Description Length (sDL) to quantify shared structure among any combination of languages.
- sDL enables fine-grained analysis beyond hierarchical trees, revealing subgroup and pan-Slavic patterns.
- Algorithm is deterministic and produces interpretable correspondence rules; includes a data over-weighting parameter for controlling granularity.
- MDL formulation is theoretically sound, balancing model complexity and data fidelity without strong prior assumptions.
- Multi-way analysis (e.g., CS, PL, RU, BG) effectively recovers known subgroupings and quantifies shared vs. unique structure.
- Empirical results and example alignments show linguistically plausible rules at orthographic, phonological, and morphological levels.
- Scalability is under-addressed: current implementation bottlenecks limit experiments to five languages and may scale poorly with N.
- Lacks direct comparisons to other computational phylogenetics or state-of-the-art cognate systems, making relative gains unclear.
- Greedy M-step merging may suffer from local optima; robustness and alternative optimization strategies are not evaluated.
- Requests complexity analysis of the E-step (alignment graph size and Dijkstra runtime) and barriers to scaling beyond five languages.
- Asks for practical guidance on setting the data over-weighting parameter α and risks of producing spurious rules when α is high.
- Requests concrete examples of top-ranked rules contributing to sDL for CS-PL, RU-BG, and the shared four-way component.
