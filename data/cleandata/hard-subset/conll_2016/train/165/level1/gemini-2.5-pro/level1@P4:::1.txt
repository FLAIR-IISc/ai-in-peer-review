### Review

**Summary of the Paper**
This paper introduces a method for automatically discovering character-level correspondences from cognate lists across multiple languages. The approach is framed under the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which seeks the set of correspondence rules that provides the most compact description of the data. An iterative, EM-like algorithm is proposed to jointly learn a set of correspondence rules and the optimal alignment of the cognates with these rules. The method is evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The evaluation includes a pairwise analysis that successfully reconstructs a linguistically plausible phylogenetic tree, and a more detailed four-way analysis that uses a novel "shared Description Length" metric to quantify similarities among all subsets of the languages.

**Main Contributions**
1.  **A Principled Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is the application of the MDL principle to discover string correspondences simultaneously across more than two languages. This formulation naturally handles rules that apply to any subset of the languages being analyzed, a significant generalization over methods restricted to pairwise comparisons.
2.  **A Novel Quantitative Measure of Multi-Language Similarity:** The paper introduces the concept of "shared Description Length" (sDL) as an information-theoretic metric to quantify the degree of regularity shared among arbitrary subsets of languages. This provides a more fine-grained view of linguistic relationships than can be captured by a single distance matrix or phylogenetic tree.
3.  **A Deterministic Search Algorithm for Rule Discovery:** The paper presents a deterministic, greedy algorithm inspired by Expectation-Maximization to search the complex space of possible correspondence rules. The algorithm iteratively aligns the data with the current rule set and refines the rule set by merging patterns that yield the greatest reduction in the total description length.

**Strengths**
1.  **Technical Soundness:** The methodology is well-grounded in the MDL principle, providing a clear objective function that avoids arbitrary assumptions about the distribution or structure of correspondence rules. The formulation of the two-part code for the model and the data is detailed and appears technically sound. This principled foundation lends credibility to the results.
2.  **Generalization to Multiple Languages:** A key strength of the work is its ability to operate on more than two languages simultaneously. This is a critical feature for comparative linguistics, where relationships are often complex and not reducible to a series of pairwise comparisons. The four-way analysis experiment convincingly demonstrates this capability, revealing subgroup structures (CS-PL and RU-BG) and other subtle relationships within the data.
3.  **Insightful Evaluation and Analysis:** The introduction and use of the sDL metric for evaluation is a notable strength. Figure 3, which breaks down the total description length by language subset, offers a rich, quantitative visualization of linguistic similarities. This form of analysis is more informative than standard phylogenetic trees, allowing for nuanced observations, such as quantifying the complexity of individual languages (e.g., Czech) relative to the shared structures.
4.  **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the proposed method is explained clearly. The components of the MDL code and the steps of the EM-like training procedure (initialization, alignment via Dijkstra's algorithm, and greedy merging) are described with sufficient detail to understand the approach.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Scalability of the Method:** The paper notes that the current implementation can process up to five languages, which points to significant scalability concerns. The alignment step, formulated as a shortest-path problem, appears to have a computational complexity that grows rapidly with the number of languages (N). The paper provides complexity in terms of |R| (maximum rule applications), but the dependency of |R| on N is not analyzed. This limitation restricts the method's applicability to small-scale language group comparisons and undermines the claim of an "efficient" algorithm.
2.  **Limited Novelty of the Core Algorithm:** While the application to multilingual correspondence discovery is novel, the core algorithmic approach—an MDL-based greedy search for patterns—builds directly on existing work, as acknowledged by the citation to Tatti and Vreeken (2012). The main novelty lies in the specific formulation of the MDL code for this linguistic task, rather than in a new optimization technique. The greedy nature of the maximization step is also a potential weakness, as it may converge to locally optimal solutions, a point that is not extensively discussed.
3.  **Inconsistency in the "Data Over-Weighting" Proposal:** The introduction of the `α` parameter for "data over-weighting" in Section 4.5 appears inconsistent with the paper's central claim of providing an objective, assumption-free MDL-based method. This parameter functions as a user-defined knob that moves the method away from the pure MDL principle and introduces subjectivity. Its motivation and use (e.g., to eliminate all singleton rules) feel ad-hoc and lack a rigorous information-theoretic justification.
4.  **Reliance on Orthographic Data:** The experiments are conducted exclusively on raw orthographic data. This is a limitation, as true linguistic regularities are often more apparent at the phonological level. Using orthography introduces noise from language-specific spelling conventions (e.g., the correspondence between Polish 'szcz' and Czech 'št' is a conflation of phonological and orthographic facts). The finding that Cyrillic Serbo-Croatian is closer to other Cyrillic-script languages demonstrates that the method is capturing surface-level script similarities, which may not always align with deeper linguistic relationships.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity, particularly concerning the growth of the alignment graph as a function of the number of languages N? Are there feasible approximations or alternative search strategies that could extend the method's applicability to a larger number of languages?
2.  The M-step uses a greedy merging strategy. Have you analyzed the sensitivity of the final rule set to this greedy approach? It seems plausible that this could lead to premature convergence on locally optimal rules. Have you considered alternatives, such as beam search, to explore a wider part of the search space?
3.  The "data over-weighting" parameter `α` seems to compromise the objectivity promised by the MDL framework. Could you provide a stronger theoretical justification for this modification? Are there alternative, more principled ways to encourage the discovery of more complex, non-singleton rules, perhaps by altering the model cost `L(M)` to penalize simpler rules more heavily?
