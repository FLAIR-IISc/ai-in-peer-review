### Summary of the paper

This paper presents a novel method for automatically discovering orthographic correspondences between multiple languages simultaneously. The authors frame this as a model selection problem and leverage the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to find the set of correspondence rules that most succinctly describes a given set of cognates. They propose a deterministic, EM-like algorithm to search for this optimal set of rules. The method is evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The experiments show that the approach can reproduce known phylogenetic relationships through a pairwise distance metric (NCD) and, more significantly, can provide a fine-grained, quantitative analysis of the similarities between any subset of languages using a proposed "shared Description Length" (sDL) metric.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Principled Framework for N-way Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is the application of the MDL principle to the problem of discovering correspondences across more than two languages at once. This moves beyond the limitations of traditional pairwise methods and provides a formal, assumption-light framework for identifying statistically significant patterns shared by any subset of the languages under study.
2.  **A Novel Metric for Quantifying Group Similarity:** The paper introduces the concept of "shared Description Length" (sDL), a direct and insightful outcome of their MDL model. This metric allows for a quantitative breakdown of similarity, measuring the complexity of patterns shared exclusively by specific subgroups of languages (e.g., pairs, triples). This is a more nuanced form of analysis than what can be achieved with pairwise distances or phylogenetic trees alone.
3.  **An End-to-End Algorithm and Implementation:** The authors provide a complete, deterministic algorithm for this task, including initialization, an EM-style optimization procedure, and a practical mechanism (`data over-weighting`) for users to control the granularity of the discovered rules.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Novelty and Soundness:** The application of MDL to this multi-language discovery task is novel and theoretically sound. By seeking the most compressive model, the method avoids making strong prior assumptions about the distribution or shape of correspondence rules, a weakness pointed out in related work. This principled approach lends significant credibility to the results.
2.  **Generalization to Multiple Languages:** The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously is a key strength and a clear advance over much of the existing work. The four-way analysis in Section 5.3 is particularly compelling, as it demonstrates a capability that is infeasible for many other methods. The resulting sDL visualization (Figure 3) provides a rich, quantitative summary of the complex relationships between the four languages, correctly identifying the known West Slavic (CS-PL) and East/South Slavic (RU-BG) pairings while also quantifying the structure common to all four.
3.  **Strong and Validating Experimental Results:** The experimental design is effective. The pairwise analysis (Section 5.2) serves as a successful sanity check, demonstrating that the NCD metric derived from the model reproduces the established linguistic classification of Slavic languages in a phylogenetic tree (Figure 1). This grounds the method in established knowledge before the paper showcases its more novel N-way analysis capabilities. The qualitative examples of learned rules (Tables 3 and 4) are convincing and linguistically plausible, ranging from simple orthographic mappings (cz,č) to more complex phenomena like liquid metathesis (ла,оло).
4.  **Practical Controllability for Exploratory Analysis:** The "data over-weighting" technique (Section 4.5) is a thoughtful, practical feature. While it deviates from a pure, objective MDL framework, it provides a crucial "knob" for a user (e.g., a linguist) to explore rules at different levels of statistical significance. This transforms the tool from a purely automatic system into an interactive one for hypothesis generation, which greatly enhances its potential utility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Scalability Concerns:** The paper's primary contribution is multi-language analysis, yet its scalability in the number of languages (`N`) appears to be a major weakness. The authors acknowledge bottlenecks for `N > 5` (Section 4.3) and the model formulation itself includes a summation over `2^N - 1` subsets (Section 4.1), suggesting exponential complexity in `N`. The complexity of the alignment step is also unclear in its dependence on `N`. This limitation severely curtails the method's applicability to larger language families and needs to be addressed more directly.
2.  **Lack of Quantitative Evaluation for Learned Rules:** The evaluation of the discovered correspondences is entirely qualitative. While the examples shown are plausible, the paper would be much stronger if it included a quantitative comparison against a gold standard of known phonological or orthographic rules for a language pair like Czech-Polish. This would provide objective evidence that the MDL-selected rules are not just statistically significant but also linguistically meaningful and accurate.
3.  **Clarity of the MDL Formulation:** Certain aspects of the model code description in Section 4 are opaque. Specifically, the justification for including rule usage counts (`TΠ`) as part of the model code `L(M)` is unclear. Typically, the cost of encoding the data given the model, `L(D|M)`, would be based on the frequency of rule usage. Placing this in `L(M)` seems to conflate model complexity with data encoding cost. A clearer explanation of this theoretical choice would improve the paper's rigor.
4.  **Ambiguity of the "Over-Weighting" Parameter:** The `α` parameter for data over-weighting is introduced as a useful feature, but its use in the experiments is not well-defined. The authors state they increased it "until there are no more singleton rules" (Section 5.2), which seems arbitrary and makes the results in Table 3 difficult to reproduce. A more systematic discussion of how to set this parameter, or an analysis of its sensitivity, is needed to validate its use.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's time and space complexity with respect to the number of languages, `N`? Given the explicit mention of a `2^N - 1` term and the graph-based alignment, exponential scaling seems likely. What are the primary bottlenecks, and do you see a path towards tractable approximations that could scale to larger language groups (e.g., `N=10` or more)?
2.  Regarding your MDL formulation for `L(Π)`, could you clarify the rationale for including the data-to-model code based on total rule usage `TΠ` within the model cost `L(M)` rather than as part of the data cost `L(D|M)`? Does this formulation correspond to a known variant of MDL, and what are the theoretical benefits of this choice over a more standard separation of model and data description lengths?
3.  The sDL analysis is a highlight of the paper. Have you considered using this metric to explore more complex linguistic hypotheses? For example, one could potentially measure the "uniqueness" of a subgroup's shared structure by comparing its sDL to the sDLs of its supersets (e.g., comparing `sDL(CS, PL)` against `sDL(CS, PL, RU)`) to distinguish innovations from shared inheritance. Do you see potential for such analyses?
