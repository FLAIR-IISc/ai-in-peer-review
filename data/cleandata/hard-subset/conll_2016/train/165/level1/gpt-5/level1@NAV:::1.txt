Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes an MDL-based framework to automatically discover string-level correspondences across two or more languages, including rules active on arbitrary subsets of languages. It defines a two-part MDL code (PS-2) for rule tables and data, uses shortest-path alignment with Dijkstra’s algorithm, and a deterministic EM-like training with greedy rule merging. Experiments on Slavic languages evaluate via NCD-derived phylogenies, qualitative rule inspection, and a four-way “shared description length” (sDL) analysis.
As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) A modeling and coding formulation that enables discovering subset-wise multi-language correspondences and quantifying shared structure via sDL.
2) A practical, deterministic learning procedure combining optimal alignment and merge-based maximization without parametric assumptions on rule shapes.
3) An empirical study on Slavic languages showing plausible phylogeny and interpretable correspondences.

Strengths
- General, assumption-light framework: The MDL code allows correspondences of arbitrary length and any subset of languages; examples show heterogeneous rules (e.g., cz–č, dzie–dě, metathesis ла–оло, and stems ziem–zem), supporting versatility without hand-crafted priors.
- Interpretable information-theoretic analyses: NCD-based phylogeny aligns with established groupings (CS-PL and RU-BG), and sDL offers a novel, fine-grained way to quantify shared structure across all subsets, revealing, e.g., RU-BG and CS-PL affinities and Czech’s larger individual cost.
- Determinism with optional prior knowledge: The deterministic merge strategy plus the option to seed rules supports reproducibility and controlled incorporation of linguistic expertise.
- Practical alignment formulation: Casting alignment as shortest paths yields exact E-steps and transparent costs tied to the MDL objective.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation and missing baselines: No quantitative comparison to established cognate alignment/phonological correspondence methods; no precision/recall or held-out compression; evidence is largely anecdotal plus NCD.
- Orthography-only inputs: Script and digraph effects are acknowledged but not controlled; lack of phonemic normalization conflates orthography with sound change, weakening linguistic validity.
- Scalability constraints: Complexity (O(|D|·|R|^2) E-step) and memory bottlenecks limit to small datasets (≈200–800 items, ≤5 languages). No scaling curves or efficiency baselines.
- Subjective data overweighting: The α-weighting departs from MDL’s objectivity; no principled selection of α, risking cherry-picking finer-grained but spurious rules.
- Insufficient ablations: No study of sensitivity to smoothing, code choices (LN, data-to-model composition), merge order, or local optima; correctness of coding terms is not empirically stress-tested.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report quantitative baselines (e.g., cognate identification accuracy, alignment F1) and held-out compression?
- How do results change under transliteration or phonemic normalization?
- Provide runtime/memory scaling with languages, vocabulary, and rule set size.
- How is α chosen; can you tie granularity control to MDL-based model selection or MDL-normalized significance tests?
- Will code/data be released to ensure reproducibility?
