Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes an MDL-based framework to automatically discover statistically important string-level correspondences among two or more languages, without assuming a parametric form for correspondences. The model encodes a set of multi-language correspondence rules and the data under a two-part code, and learns rules with an EM-like procedure that alternates optimal alignment (shortest-path with Dijkstra) and greedy rule merging to minimize total description length.
- It introduces a notion of data overweighting (α > 1) to force inclusion of near-significant rules when desired for downstream tasks.
- Experiments on Slavic languages use Swadesh lists (13 languages) and a 4-language cognate set to (i) produce pairwise NCD distances and a phylogeny consistent with linguistic classifications, (ii) demonstrate interpretable learned rules, and (iii) quantify similarity over all subsets via “shared description length” (sDL).

Contributions as I see them (ordered by strength)
1) A practical, interpretable MDL formulation for learning multi-way string correspondences and using them to quantify similarity across any subset of languages (via sDL), going beyond pairwise-only approaches.
2) An EM-style deterministic learning algorithm with an exact alignment step and greedy rule-merging M-step that scales to small multi-language datasets and yields linguistically meaningful correspondences.
3) Empirical evidence on Slavic data showing that the method recovers accepted groupings and reveals subset-specific similarities.

Strengths
- Clear, principled objective grounded in MDL. The modeling choice of two-part codes for both model and data, with explicit code designs (universal code for integers, data-to-model codes, rule usage codes), makes the trade-off between rule complexity and data fit explicit and inspectable. This supports objective discovery without strong priors on the shape of correspondences.
- Multi-language capability with subset-specific rules. The model natively handles rules defined on any subset of languages, and the sDL analysis provides a novel, fine-grained quantification of shared structure across all subsets (e.g., showing RU–BG similarity comparable to CS–PL in the 4-way analysis). This is difficult with pairwise-only methods and is the paper’s main conceptual advance.
- Interpretability of learned rules. The examples include orthographic, phonological, and morphological correspondences (e.g., cz–č, g–h, (eć, et), liquid metathesis ла–оло), as well as longer stem-level correspondences. The method discovers granular rules in a data-driven order of statistical significance, which is valuable to linguists and downstream tasks.
- Empirical validation aligns with linguistic knowledge. The NCD matrix and neighbor-joining tree recover West/East/South Slavic groupings and known outliers (e.g., BG/MK due to morphology, PL due to palatalization and digraphs). In the 4-way setting, sDL highlights nuanced subgroupings and RU’s broader influence—all qualitatively consistent with expectations.
- Deterministic training with the option to incorporate domain knowledge. Starting from singleton rules and allowing seeding with known rules is attractive for reproducible, linguist-in-the-loop analyses. Laplace correction and the greedy merge heuristic are well-motivated and simple.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical scope and lack of quantitative baselines. Experiments are confined to relatively small lists (207 and 778 entries), from related Slavic languages, mostly in raw orthography. There is no comparison to alternative approaches (e.g., supervised/unsupervised cognate alignment, pair-HMMs, Bayesian sound correspondences, Morfessor-style models, neural alignment) on discovery quality, alignment accuracy, or downstream tasks. The phylogeny result is encouraging but relatively easy on this family; broader tests (cross-family, noisy cognacy, non-cognates) are needed.
- Orthography vs. phonology conflation and script effects. Working on raw orthography blends orthographic conventions (e.g., Latin vs. Cyrillic, digraphs) with sound correspondences, which can bias the learned rules and distances (the paper notes SC Latin/Cyrillic behaving differently). Without phonemic normalization or grapheme-to-phoneme mapping, some “correspondences” reflect scripts or diacritics rather than sound change, complicating linguistic interpretation and cross-script generalization.
- Incomplete clarity and justification of coding choices. While the code structure is outlined, several technical details are underspecified: the precise data-to-model code instantiations, the combinatorial terms (e.g., weak compositions) and their derivations, and how rule usage probabilities are estimated and smoothed in practice. The encoding of Π includes both rule descriptions and usage counts, but the rationale for particular combinatorial penalties could be elaborated, and sensitivity to these choices is not explored.
- Efficiency and scalability are modest. The E-step and M-step complexities (O(|D|·|R|^2) and O(|D|·A^2/2)) lead to practical limits of ≲5 languages and hours of runtime on small datasets. There is no analysis of scaling in number of languages, rule table size growth, or pruning strategies beyond partial order reduction. For practical multi-language applications (≥10 languages, thousands of forms), this may be a bottleneck.
- Subjectivity via data overweighting and evaluation without statistical tests. While α-weighting is a useful knob, it departs from MDL’s objectivity and risks overfitting longer idiosyncratic patterns. The paper demonstrates qualitative changes but does not quantify when overweighting improves or harms generalization. More generally, the empirical section lacks statistical testing (e.g., stability under resampling) or error bars, and tree rooting is chosen manually.

Questions to authors
- Can you release code and data to support reproducibility? If not, can you provide exact preprocessing, alignment, and parameter settings (including tie-breaking in merges and stopping criteria)?
- How sensitive are results to the coding design (e.g., choice of data-to-model codes, Laplace smoothing constant, inclusion of the combinatorial terms in L(Π))? Any ablations?
- How does the approach behave with non-cognates or partial cognacy? Do you filter for cognacy, or can the model handle noise gracefully?
- What is the convergence criterion for EM/greedy merging? Do you ever un-merge or prune rules? How do you handle overlapping/competing long rules to avoid “locking” errors?
- Could phonemic transcription (or grapheme normalization) reduce script-induced artifacts? Did you attempt romanization or G2P and compare?

Additional comments
- The references contain formatting issues and incomplete citations; please fix for camera-ready.
- Clarify whether deletions/insertions across languages are explicitly modeled (empty strings allowed per language suggests yes). Examples demonstrating indel behavior would help.
- The manual rooting of the tree should be justified or replaced with a standard method (e.g., midpoint rooting), and robustness of the topology to data perturbations should be reported.

Overall recommendation
Promising and principled work with clear interpretability and a novel multi-language angle; however, empirical validation is narrow and methodological details need sharpening. Strengthen with broader evaluations, clearer coding derivations, and a scalability plan.
