- Proposes an MDL-based framework to discover multi-language string correspondences without assuming parametric forms, using a two-part code and EM-like alternating alignment and greedy rule merging.
- Introduces data overweighting (α > 1) to force inclusion of near-significant rules for downstream tasks.
- Experiments on Slavic Swadesh lists and a 4-language cognate set produce pairwise NCD distances, a phylogeny consistent with linguistics, interpretable rules, and a “shared description length” (sDL) for subset similarity.

Contributions:
- Practical, interpretable MDL formulation for multi-way string correspondences and subset similarity via sDL.
- Deterministic EM-style algorithm with exact alignment and greedy rule merging that scales to small multi-language datasets.
- Empirical evidence on Slavic data recovering known groupings and subset-specific similarities.

Strengths:
- Clear, principled MDL objective with explicit code designs exposing the rule complexity vs. fit trade-off.
- Native multi-language capability and sDL provide fine-grained quantification of shared structure across subsets.
- Learned rules are interpretable, recovering orthographic, phonological, morphological, and stem-level correspondences.
- Empirical results qualitatively align with linguistic knowledge (Slavic subgroupings and known outliers).
- Deterministic, reproducible training with options for domain seeding and sensible heuristics like Laplace smoothing.

Weaknesses:
- Limited empirical scope (small datasets, single family) and no quantitative baselines or broader evaluations.
- Conflation of orthography and phonology and script effects risk learning script-specific rather than phonemic correspondences.
- Several coding-choice details and derivations are underspecified and sensitivity to these choices is unexplored.
- Scalability and runtime are limited (practical limits ≲5 languages, hours runtime) with no thorough scaling analysis.
- Data overweighting introduces subjectivity without statistical tests or robustness analyses; evaluation lacks error bars and statistical validation.

Questions for authors:
- Will code and data be released, or can exact preprocessing, parameters, tie-breaking, and stopping criteria be provided?
- How sensitive are results to coding design choices (data-to-model codes, Laplace constant, combinatorial penalties)?
- How does the method handle non-cognates, partial cognacy, or noisy data—do you filter cognacy or is the model robust?
- What are the convergence criteria, can rules be un-merged or pruned, and how do you avoid locking errors with overlapping long rules?
- Did you try phonemic transcription, romanization, or G2P to reduce script-induced artifacts and how did that compare?

Additional comments:
- Fix reference formatting and incomplete citations.
- Clarify explicit modeling of insertions/deletions and provide examples of indel behavior.
- Justify manual tree rooting or use a standard rooting method and report topology robustness to perturbations.

Overall recommendation:
- Promising, principled, and interpretable multi-language approach, but needs broader empirical validation, clearer coding derivations, and a scalability plan before acceptance.
