**Summary and Contributions**

This paper presents a method for discovering orthographic correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The central idea is to find a set of correspondence rules that provides the most compact description of a given list of cognates. The authors propose a deterministic, EM-like algorithm that starts with singleton character rules and iteratively merges them based on which merge provides the greatest reduction in total description length. The primary contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  The application of a specific two-part MDL code, adapted from work on event sequence mining, to the problem of discovering multi-lingual orthographic correspondences.
2.  A novel analysis metric, "shared Description Length" (sDL), designed to quantify similarities among subsets of languages in a multi-language setting, moving beyond traditional pairwise comparisons.
3.  An empirical reconstruction of the Slavic language family tree that aligns with established linguistic classifications, serving as a validation of the pairwise distances derived from the model.

**Strengths**

1.  The paper addresses a well-motivated and challenging problem: discovering systematic correspondences among more than two languages simultaneously. Moving beyond the standard pairwise paradigm is a valuable research direction, and the paper's motivation is clear and compelling.
2.  The use of the MDL principle offers a theoretically grounded, non-probabilistic alternative to more common Bayesian or maximum likelihood approaches. It provides a formal criterion for balancing model complexity with data fidelity. The successful reconstruction of the Slavic phylogenetic tree (Figure 1) demonstrates that the derived NCD metric captures a meaningful signal of linguistic relatedness, providing a solid sanity check for the approach.
3.  The proposed algorithm is deterministic, which is a commendable property that ensures reproducibility of the results, a feature often lacking in methods relying on stochastic search or random initialization.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Comparison to Baselines:** The most significant weakness of this paper is the complete absence of empirical comparisons to any established baseline methods. The introduction cites several statistical approaches to cognate identification and correspondence discovery (e.g., Snyder and Barzilay, 2008), but the experiments do not include any of them. Without such a comparison, it is impossible to judge whether the proposed complex MDL machinery offers any advantage over simpler or more established techniques. The reported results, such as the phylogenetic tree, could potentially be reproduced by much simpler string similarity metrics. The claims of superiority remain entirely unsubstantiated.
2.  **Questionable Methodological Purity and Overstated Claims:** The authors claim their method avoids "arbitrary assumptions," yet the formulation of the MDL code itself is a sequence of non-trivial design choices that constitute a strong set of assumptions about what makes a "good" model. More problematically, the introduction of the "data over-weighting" parameter `α` in Section 4.5 directly contradicts the paper's central premise of using MDL for objective, data-driven discovery. This parameter appears to be an ad-hoc fix to coax the model into finding more granular, linguistically plausible rules that the "objective" MDL score would otherwise reject. The necessity of such a mechanism suggests a potential deficiency in the core model's ability to capture the desired phenomena.
3.  **Limitations of the Search Algorithm:** The maximization step of the algorithm relies on a greedy merge strategy: at each step, it combines the two patterns that yield the best immediate improvement in description length. While deterministic, this approach is highly susceptible to local optima. The authors briefly acknowledge this ("occasionally we may miss some of the more subtle correspondence rules") but do not analyze its potential impact. For a non-convex problem like this, a greedy search is a weak guarantee of finding a high-quality solution, and the resulting rule set may be an artifact of the search path rather than the "true" optimal model.
4.  **Superficial Evaluation and Interpretation:** The evaluation is limited in scope. First, it relies exclusively on raw orthography, which can obscure or misrepresent genuine phonological correspondences that are central to historical linguistics. Second, the interpretation of the novel sDL metric is entirely qualitative and post-hoc. For instance, the conclusion that Russian "heavily influence[s] the other languages" based on the bar chart in Figure 3 is a speculative leap. The metric simply reflects the shared description cost within their specific model and dataset; attributing this to historical influence requires external evidence not provided in the paper. The metric's actual linguistic utility remains undemonstrated.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you justify the decision to not compare your method against any of the existing statistical approaches mentioned in the related work? A comparison, even on the pairwise task, seems essential to demonstrate the contribution of this new framework.
2.  The "data over-weighting" mechanism seems to run counter to the principled and objective nature of MDL that you advocate for. Could you elaborate on why this user-defined parameter is necessary? Does its inclusion suggest that the standard MDL objective is misaligned with the goal of discovering linguistically meaningful correspondences in this domain?
3.  Given that the greedy search is likely to find a local optimum, have you investigated the sensitivity of your results to the search strategy? For example, how different are the learned rule sets and sDL values if a different search algorithm (e.g., beam search) is employed? This would help gauge the robustness of the reported findings.
