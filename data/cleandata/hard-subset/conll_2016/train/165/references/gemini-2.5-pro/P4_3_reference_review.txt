### Summary of the Paper

This paper introduces a method for automatically discovering string-level correspondences between multiple languages from cognate lists. The approach is based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which posits that the best model (a set of correspondence rules) is the one that most succinctly describes the data. The method is designed to be assumption-free regarding the shape or distribution of correspondences. An iterative, EM-like algorithm is proposed to infer the rule set, starting from single-character rules and greedily merging them to find statistically significant multi-character and multi-language patterns. The authors evaluate their method on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. They conduct a pairwise analysis using Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) to construct a phylogenetic tree, which aligns with known linguistic classifications. They also perform a four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian, introducing a novel metric called Shared Description Length (sDL) to quantify the similarities between any subset of the analyzed languages.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A novel, unsupervised MDL-based framework for discovering string correspondences across multiple languages simultaneously.** This approach avoids the limitations of methods restricted to pairwise comparisons and does not require prior assumptions about the structure of the correspondences, allowing for the discovery of complex, multi-character rules.
2.  **The introduction of Shared Description Length (sDL) as a metric for fine-grained, multi-way language similarity analysis.** Unlike traditional pairwise distance measures, sDL quantifies the amount of shared structure between any given subset of languages in the analysis, offering a more detailed view of their interrelationships.
3.  **An empirical demonstration on 13 Slavic languages.** The work validates its derived distance metric by reproducing a standard linguistic classification in a phylogenetic tree and showcases the analytical power of the sDL metric by revealing subgroup similarities in a four-language case study.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Novel Formulation:** The use of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle provides a well-founded, objective framework for the task of correspondence discovery. By seeking the most compressed representation of the data, the method avoids arbitrary assumptions about rule distributions that are common in other statistical approaches. The ability to handle more than two languages in a single, unified model is a significant and novel contribution compared to the bulk of related work, which focuses on pairwise analysis.

2.  **Powerful Multi-way Similarity Quantification:** The proposed Shared Description Length (sDL) metric is a key strength. It provides a more nuanced and detailed analysis of language relationships than is possible with pairwise distances alone. The visualization in Figure 3 effectively demonstrates this, quantifying not only the structure shared by all four languages but also that shared exclusively by subgroups (e.g., CS-PL, RU-BG) and the complexity unique to each language. This represents a novel tool for computational linguistic analysis.

3.  **Technical Soundness and Clarity:** The methodology is presented with sufficient clarity. The two-part MDL code is broken down into its model `L(M)` and data `L(D|M)` components. The optimization procedure, which alternates between aligning data with current rules (E-step) and merging rules to improve the model (M-step), is intuitive and deterministic. Framing the alignment task as a shortest-path problem is a standard and computationally sound approach.

4.  **Convincing Experimental Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the method's capabilities. The pairwise analysis on 13 languages, which produces a phylogenetic tree (Figure 1) consistent with established linguistic subgroupings (West, East, South Slavic), serves as a strong validation of the model's ability to capture meaningful linguistic distances. The four-way analysis further showcases the unique strengths of the approach, identifying known subgroups and other subtle similarities.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Scalability Concerns:** The paper reports that the current implementation can process "up to five languages" and identifies the alignment procedure as a computational bottleneck due to its combinatorial nature. This is a major limitation for a method whose primary claim is its applicability to multiple languages. The paper lacks a formal analysis of the computational complexity with respect to the number of languages (N) and string length. The promise to "extend this to higher N in future work" is insufficient; the submission should provide more insight into the scalability challenges and potential solutions.

2.  **Absence of Comparative Evaluation:** The method is evaluated in isolation, without comparison to any baseline or alternative approaches. While multi-language correspondence discovery is not a standard task, the outputs could be compared to established methods in related areas. For instance, the phylogenetic tree in Figure 1 could be quantitatively compared to trees generated by traditional computational phylogenetic methods. The discovered correspondence rules could be qualitatively compared to known sound laws or to alignments produced by other tools, even if those are pairwise. Without such comparisons, it is difficult to objectively assess the performance and practical advantages of the proposed method.

3.  **Ambiguity of the "Data Over-weighting" Technique:** The introduction of the `α` parameter (Section 4.5) to over-weight the data term appears to contradict the paper's central motivation of using MDL for an objective, assumption-free analysis. This user-tuned parameter makes the method subjective, moving it "away from the objective territory of MDL." The justification for finding "near-significant" rules is vague, and no principled guidance is offered for setting `α`. This feature feels ad-hoc and undermines the otherwise principled nature of the framework.

4.  **Opaque Details in the MDL Formulation:** While the overall structure of the MDL code is described, certain components lack sufficient justification. For example, the use of a specific data-to-model code involving a combinatorial term for encoding the rule table `L(Π)` is not clearly motivated for a reader not already deeply familiar with MDL literature. A more intuitive explanation for why this specific formulation is chosen over simpler alternatives would improve the technical clarity and reproducibility of the work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of your alignment algorithm, specifically with respect to the number of languages N? What are the primary algorithmic or implementation barriers to scaling beyond five languages, and what concrete strategies are you considering to address this limitation?
2.  The paper lacks a comparison to other methods. Could you discuss how the quality of your inferred phylogenetic tree compares to trees generated by established methods in computational phylogenetics? Furthermore, how do the specific correspondences discovered by your model compare, even qualitatively, to those identified by linguists or other statistical alignment tools?
3.  The "data over-weighting" parameter `α` introduces subjectivity into an otherwise objective framework. Could you provide a more rigorous justification for this technique? Is there a principled way to select a value for `α`, or is it purely a mechanism for exploratory analysis?
