### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a method for automatically discovering orthographic correspondence rules from cognate lists spanning multiple languages. The approach is framed under the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which seeks the set of rules that most concisely describes the given data. The core of the method is an iterative, EM-like algorithm that starts with single-character rules and greedily merges them to form more complex correspondence patterns, optimizing the overall description length at each step. The authors evaluate their method on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, first performing a pairwise analysis to generate a phylogenetic tree that aligns well with known linguistic classifications, and second, conducting a novel four-way analysis to quantify the shared similarities between all subsets of four selected languages.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A principled framework for discovering correspondences between more than two languages simultaneously.** While pairwise analysis is common, this work provides a method to model and quantify n-way relationships directly. The introduction of "Shared Description Length" (sDL) is a valuable and intuitive metric for analyzing the output of this multi-language model.
2.  **An unsupervised and deterministic algorithm based on the MDL principle.** The method avoids prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of correspondence rules, instead learning them directly from data. Its deterministic nature makes the results reproducible, which is a significant advantage for linguistic analysis.
3.  **A novel, fine-grained quantitative analysis of language similarity.** The four-way analysis in Section 5.3 demonstrates the power of the multi-language approach, revealing subtle relationships (e.g., quantifying the similarity between CS-PL, RU-BG, and CS-PL-RU-BG) that are obscured in standard pairwise phylogenetic trees.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Novelty:** The application of MDL to the multi-language correspondence problem is well-motivated and provides a strong, information-theoretic foundation. The core idea of modeling the problem as finding the most compact encoding of both the rules (the model) and the data is elegant. The extension to more than two languages is the key novelty and is a significant step beyond most existing work, which is limited to pairwise comparisons. This allows the model to capture complex, multi-way patterns directly.
2.  **Compelling Experimental Validation and Analysis:** The experiments are well-designed and the results are convincing. The pairwise analysis resulting in the phylogenetic tree (Figure 1) serves as an excellent sanity check, demonstrating that the learned similarity metric (NCD) successfully recovers established linguistic subgroups of the Slavic family. The subsequent four-way analysis is the paper's strongest empirical contribution, offering a much richer and more nuanced picture of language similarity than a simple tree structure. The sDL visualization (Figure 3) is particularly insightful.
3.  **Clarity of the High-Level Approach:** The paper does a good job of explaining the intuition behind the method in Section 2. The EM-like procedure—iterating between aligning the data with the current rules (E-step) and merging rules to improve the model (M-step)—is intuitive. Starting from a "null alignment" and building up complexity is a sensible bottom-up approach to a difficult combinatorial problem.
4.  **Practicality and Controllability:** The introduction of the "data over-weighting" parameter `α` (Section 4.5) is a pragmatic and useful feature. While the authors correctly note that it moves away from pure objectivity, it provides a valuable tool for linguists or other users to control the granularity of the discovered rules. This allows the method to be adapted for different goals, such as finding broader, more statistically robust patterns versus discovering more specific, potentially rarer correspondences. ### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Scalability Concerns:** The paper admits that the current implementation can only process "up to five languages" on a standard desktop machine due to combinatorial bottlenecks in the alignment procedure. This is a major limitation for a method whose primary contribution is its ability to handle multiple languages. The computational complexity seems to grow exponentially with the number of languages, which severely restricts its applicability to larger-scale comparative linguistics (e.g., analyzing the entire Indo-European family). The paper should be more upfront about these limitations and discuss the theoretical complexity more formally.
2.  **Greedy Search and Potential for Suboptimal Solutions:** The maximization step relies on a deterministic, greedy strategy of merging the single best pair of rules at each iteration. This approach is susceptible to finding local optima. An early merge that appears optimal at the time could prevent the formation of a globally better, more complex rule later on. While the authors briefly acknowledge this ("occasionally we may miss some of the more subtle correspondence rules"), the potential impact of this greedy heuristic on the quality of the final rule set is not sufficiently discussed or evaluated.
3.  **Lack of Extrinsic Evaluation or Comparison to Baselines:** The evaluation is entirely intrinsic and qualitative—the NCDs and the resulting tree "look right" based on known linguistics. There is no comparison to any other method, not even a simple baseline. For instance, how do the pairwise NCDs compare to distances derived from other methods, such as those based on cognate identification or phonetic alignments? For the multi-way analysis, while novel, its utility is not demonstrated in a downstream task (e.g., cognate reconstruction, machine translation). Comparing against a baseline, such as applying a pairwise method to all pairs and attempting to synthesize a multi-way result, would help justify the complexity of the proposed MDL formulation.
4.  **Clarity of the MDL Code Formulation:** While the high-level concept is clear, the detailed mathematical description of the MDL code in Section 4.1 is extremely dense and difficult to follow. The formula for `L(Π)` in particular, with its sums over 2^N subsets and its combination of universal integer codes and data-to-model codes, is presented without a clear, step-by-step example. This makes the method very difficult to reproduce from the paper alone. A small, worked example (e.g., encoding two rules for two languages) would dramatically improve the paper's clarity. (Minor point: The reference list is also poorly formatted and contains incomplete entries).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational bottleneck? Is the primary limitation memory (storing the alignment graph) or time (running Dijkstra)? What are the practical and theoretical barriers to scaling the method to a larger number of languages, for example, N=10?
2.  The greedy, deterministic nature of the M-step seems like a potential weakness. Have you investigated the sensitivity of the final rule set to the greedy choices? For example, have you considered alternative search strategies like beam search to explore a wider range of rule merges at each step, potentially leading to better solutions?
3.  The data over-weighting parameter `α` is a useful tool but introduces subjectivity. How did you choose the level of over-weighting for the examples in Table 3? Was it simply increased until no singleton rules remained, or was there another criterion? Could this parameter be set in a more principled way, perhaps based on a desired level of model complexity?
