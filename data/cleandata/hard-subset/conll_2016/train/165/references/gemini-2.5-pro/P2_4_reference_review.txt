### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a method for discovering systematic orthographic correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to find a set of correspondence rules (e.g., Polish `szcz` ↔ Czech `št`) that provides the most succinct description of a given set of cognate words. The method employs an EM-like algorithm to iteratively refine a model of these rules, starting from single-character mappings. The authors demonstrate the approach on a dataset of Slavic languages, performing both pairwise and four-way analyses.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel method for quantifying similarity between subsets of languages.** The proposed "Shared Description Length" (sDL) metric moves beyond traditional pairwise distance matrices and phylogenetic trees. It provides a fine-grained, information-theoretic measure of the complexity shared by any group of languages within a larger set (e.g., the specific shared structure between Czech-Polish vs. Russian-Bulgarian within a four-language analysis). This is the most significant contribution.
2.  **A principled, assumption-light framework for correspondence discovery.** By using MDL, the method avoids imposing prior assumptions on the shape or distribution of correspondence rules, a limitation of some previous statistical approaches. The model learns rules of varying lengths and involving different numbers of languages directly from the data.
3.  **A flexible technique ("data over-weighting") for user-guided exploration.** The authors introduce a parameter to adjust the trade-off between model complexity and data fit, allowing a user to encourage the discovery of longer, more detailed correspondence rules that might not be strictly statistically significant on a small dataset.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Principled Approach:** The paper grounds its approach in the well-established MDL principle. The motivation to avoid arbitrary assumptions about the nature of correspondences is compelling and represents a clear advantage. Treating the problem as finding the most efficient "code" for the data is an elegant way to frame unsupervised pattern discovery.
2.  **Novel and Insightful Multi-Language Analysis:** The four-way analysis using Shared Description Lengths (sDL) is a major strength. Figure 3 provides a rich, quantitative visualization of the relationships between Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian that cannot be captured by a simple phylogenetic tree. It simultaneously shows the strength of the West Slavic (CS-PL) and East/South Slavic (RU-BG) pairings, the complexity of the core Pan-Slavic structure (the four-way bar), and the unique complexity of each individual language. This is a powerful tool for computational historical linguistics.
3.  **Solid and Validating Experimental Results:** The pairwise analysis serves as a strong sanity check. The resulting NCD matrix and phylogenetic tree (Figure 1) successfully reconstruct the known major groupings of the Slavic languages (West, East, South). The authors also provide plausible linguistic explanations for the observed outliers (e.g., loss of case in Bulgarian/Macedonian, orthographic complexity in Polish), demonstrating a good understanding of both their method and their data.
4.  **Interpretability of Results:** The method outputs human-readable correspondence rules of varying complexity, from simple phonetic shifts (`g`↔`h`) to complex orthographic patterns (`dzie`↔`dě`) and morpheme-level correspondences (`ziem`↔`zem`). This makes the model's output directly useful for linguistic analysis, unlike methods that produce opaque embeddings or distance scores.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Scalability Limitations:** The paper's primary claim is its ability to analyze *multiple* languages, yet the authors state their implementation is limited to "up to five languages" due to computational bottlenecks. The main phylogenetic analysis of 13 languages was conducted through a series of pairwise comparisons, not a single 13-way analysis. The novel multi-language analysis was only demonstrated on four languages. This is a critical weakness that undermines the main selling point of the paper. The method's practical utility for larger-scale comparative linguistics is questionable without addressing this.
2.  **Conflation of Orthography and Phonology:** The analysis is performed exclusively on orthographic data. While this is a valid choice, the paper draws conclusions about "linguistic similarity" that may be more reflective of spelling conventions than deeper phonological or etymological relationships. For example, the finding that the Cyrillic and Latin versions of Serbo-Croatian have different similarity profiles is an artifact of the script, not the language itself. The paper would be stronger if it more explicitly acknowledged this limitation and discussed the potential differences if applied to phonetically transcribed data.
3.  **Clarity of the MDL Formulation:** Section 4, which details the MDL code, is extremely dense. The formula for encoding the rule table `L(Π)` is complex and presented without sufficient intuition or a running example. Key components, such as the data-to-model code represented by the binomial coefficient term, are mentioned but not adequately explained for a non-expert in MDL. This lack of clarity hinders the paper's reproducibility.
4.  **Greedy Optimization and Local Optima:** The maximization step of the algorithm is greedy, always merging the single pair of rules that yields the greatest reduction in description length. This deterministic approach is prone to finding local optima. The paper briefly acknowledges this ("we may miss some of the more subtle correspondence rules") but does not analyze the severity of the issue or compare it to a non-deterministic alternative. While determinism can be a feature, its potential drawbacks in a large, non-convex search space deserve more attention.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific nature of the scalability bottleneck? Is it primarily memory (size of the alignment graph) or runtime (Dijkstra's algorithm over this graph)? What are the prospects for overcoming this with algorithmic improvements (e.g., pruning, beam search) to enable analysis of, for instance, 10 or more languages simultaneously?
2.  The "data over-weighting" parameter `α` seems to be a pragmatic solution for finding more granular rules, but it moves away from the objective nature of MDL and re-introduces a subjective user choice. How was this parameter set in your experiments (e.g., for Table 3)? Is there a principled way to guide its selection, or is it purely a tool for manual exploration?
3.  Given that the method operates on orthography, have you considered its application to phonetically transcribed data (e.g., using IPA)? How do you hypothesize the discovered correspondences and the sDL-based analysis would change? Would you expect them to reveal deeper, more historically stable linguistic relationships obscured by modern spelling conventions?
