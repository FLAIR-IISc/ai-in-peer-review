**Summary of the Paper**
This paper proposes a method for discovering systematic string correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to find a set of correspondence rules that provides the most succinct description of a given set of cognate tuples. The authors formulate this as an optimization problem solved using an EM-like procedure, which starts with single-character rules and iteratively merges them to form more complex rules that improve the overall compression of the data. The method is designed to be deterministic and assumption-free regarding the distribution or shape of correspondences. The evaluation is conducted on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The authors present a pairwise analysis, generating a phylogenetic tree that aligns with known linguistic classifications, and a four-way analysis that introduces a novel "shared Description Length" (sDL) metric to quantify similarities among subsets of languages.

**Contributions**
1.  **A formal MDL framework for multi-language correspondence discovery:** The primary contribution is the application and specific formulation of the two-part MDL principle to the problem of finding correspondences in more than two languages simultaneously. This includes the design of the code for both the model (the rule set) and the data, along with a deterministic, EM-like optimization algorithm to infer these rules.
2.  **A novel metric for quantifying multi-way language similarity:** The "shared Description Length" (sDL) is a direct result of the multi-language framework. It offers a more granular way to analyze linguistic relationships than traditional pairwise distance matrices by quantifying the complexity of structures shared by specific subsets of the languages under analysis.

**Strengths**
1.  **Principled Methodological Foundation:** The use of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle is a significant strength. It provides a well-founded, information-theoretic basis for model selection that avoids arbitrary assumptions about parameter distributions, which are common in Bayesian approaches. The goal of finding the most succinct explanation for the data is an elegant and powerful concept.
2.  **Generalization to Multiple Languages:** The ability to simultaneously analyze more than two languages is a notable advantage over many existing methods, which are often limited to pairwise comparisons. This capability directly enables more nuanced analyses, such as the proposed sDL metric, which can reveal complex subgroupings and shared features that are obscured in a purely pairwise analysis.
3.  **Deterministic and Interpretable Approach:** The proposed algorithm is deterministic, which ensures reproducibility. The iterative merging process, where rules are grown based on their statistical significance, is intuitive. The resulting correspondence rules are directly interpretable as string-level patterns, which could be valuable for linguistic analysis.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper's most significant weakness is the complete absence of any comparison to existing methods. While the introduction mentions prior work in cognate identification and morphological alignment (e.g., Snyder and Barzilay, 2008), the proposed method is not benchmarked against any of them, even in the simpler pairwise setting. Without a baseline, it is impossible to assess whether this complex MDL-based approach offers any tangible benefit over simpler, established techniques like sequence alignment models with learned substitution costs or other statistical methods for discovering sound correspondences. The claim that it finds "true" correspondences is unsubstantiated without this comparison.
2.  **Evaluation is Insular and Lacks External Validation:** The evaluation is entirely based on internal metrics (NCD, sDL) and qualitative inspection of the resulting phylogenetic tree and example alignments. The primary evidence of success is that the generated tree "looks right" according to established linguistic classification. This is a weak form of validation. A much stronger evaluation would involve an extrinsic, task-based assessment (e.g., using the learned rules for cognate identification or machine translation) or comparison against a gold-standard set of alignments handcrafted by linguists. As it stands, the utility and correctness of the discovered correspondences remain unproven.
3.  **Methodological Contradiction in Data Over-Weighting:** The authors initially champion the objectivity of the MDL principle, which "forego[es] making assumptions" (Sec. 3). However, they later introduce an ad-hoc "data over-weighting" parameter `alpha` (Sec. 4.5) to find rules that are not statistically significant. This directly contradicts the core philosophy of MDL. It appears to be a heuristic to compensate for the model's inability to find linguistically desirable but low-frequency patterns from the available data. This undermines the paper's claims of objectivity and suggests that the principled framework may not be sufficient on its own.
4.  **Reliance on Orthography:** The analysis is performed exclusively on orthographic forms. Linguistic correspondences are fundamentally phonological, and orthography often obscures them (e.g., Polish 'sz' vs. Czech 'š') or introduces noise (e.g., silent letters). While using raw text is convenient, it is a major simplification that limits the linguistic validity of the discovered "correspondences." The paper does not adequately discuss the limitations or potential pitfalls of this choice.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Why was the proposed method not compared against any established baseline methods for correspondence discovery, even for the pairwise analysis? A comparison to models like those used in historical phonology (e.g., SCA) or unsupervised sub-word alignment would provide crucial context for the performance and contribution of your work.
2.  The "data over-weighting" parameter `alpha` seems to run counter to the principled and objective nature of MDL that is emphasized earlier in the paper. Could you elaborate on the justification for this addition? Does its necessity suggest a limitation in the pure MDL formulation for this specific linguistic task?
3.  Could the quality of the learned alignments be evaluated more rigorously? For example, by comparing them to a set of gold-standard alignments provided by linguists, or by measuring their utility in a downstream task such as transliteration or cognate identification, and comparing that performance to a baseline system.
