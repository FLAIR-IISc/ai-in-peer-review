**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces a method for automatically discovering orthographic correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to find a set of correspondence rules that provides the most compressed representation of a given list of cognate words. The authors propose a deterministic, EM-like algorithm that starts with simple single-character rules and iteratively merges them to discover more complex, statistically significant patterns. The method is demonstrated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, where it is used to construct a phylogenetic tree and, more uniquely, to perform a fine-grained analysis of the similarities between various subsets of four specific languages (Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian).

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel framework for quantifying multi-language similarity beyond pairwise comparisons.** The primary contribution is the "shared Description Length" (sDL) metric, derived naturally from the MDL model. This allows for a quantitative analysis of the structural similarity shared by any subset of the languages under consideration (e.g., the specific regularities shared only by Czech and Polish within a four-language analysis). This provides a more nuanced view of linguistic relationships than is possible with standard phylogenetic trees built from pairwise distances.
2.  **An unsupervised, non-parametric algorithm for multi-lingual correspondence discovery.** The paper presents a concrete algorithm that learns correspondence rules of arbitrary length and structure directly from orthographic data. By leveraging MDL, it avoids strong a priori assumptions about the distribution or form of these rules, a common limitation of other statistical methods. The algorithm's native ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a key feature.
3.  **A user-guided method for controlling correspondence granularity.** The proposed "data over-weighting" technique, while a departure from pure MDL, offers a pragmatic knob for users to explore correspondences at different levels of detail. This allows a linguist to find patterns that may be meaningful but lack sufficient statistical evidence in a small dataset, trading statistical objectivity for potentially greater linguistic insight.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled and Well-Motivated Theoretical Framework.** The use of MDL is an excellent fit for this problem. It provides a theoretically sound, information-theoretic basis for model selection, naturally balancing the complexity of the rule set against its ability to explain the data. This avoids the need for arbitrary thresholds or extensive parameter tuning. The extension of this principle to develop the sDL metric for subset analysis is a strong and insightful conceptual contribution.
2.  **Methodological Soundness and Clarity.** The proposed EM-like algorithm is a sensible and practical approach to a computationally hard optimization problem. The procedure—initializing with singletons, aligning data in the E-step (via Dijkstra's algorithm), and greedily merging rules in the M-step—is described clearly and is easy to follow. The deterministic nature of the core algorithm is a significant advantage for reproducibility and interpretability, which are crucial for linguistic analysis.
3.  **Convincing Experimental Results and Analysis.** The experiments on Slavic languages effectively demonstrate the method's capabilities. The pairwise analysis, which recovers a linguistically plausible phylogenetic tree, serves as a strong validation of the underlying model and distance metric. The four-way analysis is the paper's key achievement, successfully showcasing the unique power of the multi-language approach. The sDL analysis in Figure 3 is particularly compelling, offering a rich, quantitative picture of the relationships between the West and East Slavic subgroups that a simple tree cannot provide.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant Scalability Concerns.** The paper states that the implementation can handle "up to five languages" and requires several hours for the four-language experiment on a desktop machine. This is a major practical limitation for the method's application in historical linguistics, where analyses can involve dozens of languages. The complexity is not fully characterized in terms of the number of languages (N), but the mention of iterating over 2^N - 1 subsets for the model code suggests the approach may not scale gracefully. The performance bottleneck needs to be addressed or at least analyzed more thoroughly for the method to be widely applicable.
2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work.** The related work section is sparse and does not position the work adequately with respect to existing methods for multiple sequence alignment in computational linguistics. Several established toolkits and models (e.g., based on probabilistic models of sound change, or tools like LingPy) also infer correspondences across multiple languages. Without a quantitative or at least a thorough qualitative comparison to these state-of-the-art methods on a shared task or dataset, it is difficult to evaluate the practical advantages of the proposed MDL approach over existing techniques.
3.  **The "Data Over-weighting" Technique Lacks Principled Guidance.** While presented as a useful feature for experts, the α parameter for data over-weighting feels ad-hoc and undermines the primary appeal of MDL as an objective, data-driven method. The paper offers no guidance on how to select α, other than the arbitrary-sounding suggestion to increase it "until there are no more singleton rules." This turns a principled method into a heuristic one. A more rigorous treatment is needed, perhaps including a sensitivity analysis of how the rule set and sDL measures change with α, or a discussion of the risks of discovering spurious patterns when α is high.
4.  **Lack of Clarity in the MDL Formulation Details.** While the high-level concept is clear, the detailed mathematical formulation of the model code, L(M), in Section 4.1 is dense and difficult to parse. For instance, the exact derivation and components of the data-to-model codes for both the rule table (Π) and the alphabets (Σi) could be explained more transparently. A small, concrete numerical example walking through the calculation of L(M) and L(D|M) for a toy problem would greatly improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of your algorithm, particularly with respect to the number of languages, N? The alignment step seems like a potential bottleneck. What is the main factor preventing scaling beyond five languages, and do you see a clear path to improving this?
2.  Regarding the data over-weighting parameter α: How sensitive are the discovered rules and the sDL-based conclusions to the choice of α? Is there a risk that a large α could cause the model to simply memorize the data by creating highly specific rules, and how would you guard against this?
3.  The sDL metric is a very interesting contribution. How do you see its linguistic interpretation in relation to established concepts from historical linguistics, such as shared innovations (synapomorphies) versus shared retentions (symplesiomorphies)? Does sDL distinguish between these?
