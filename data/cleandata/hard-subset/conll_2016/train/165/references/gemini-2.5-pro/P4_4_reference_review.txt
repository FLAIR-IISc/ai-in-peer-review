### Summary of the paper

This paper introduces a method for automatically discovering string-level correspondences between multiple languages from lists of cognates. The approach is founded on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which posits that the best model (a set of correspondence rules) is the one that allows for the most compact description of the given data. The authors formalize this with a two-part code, defining the cost of encoding the model and the cost of encoding the data given the model. To find an optimal set of rules in a large, non-convex search space, they propose a deterministic, iterative algorithm analogous to Expectation-Maximization. The algorithm starts with a simple "null alignment" and progressively builds more complex rules by greedily merging existing ones that yield the largest reduction in total description length. The method is evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The authors demonstrate its efficacy through a pairwise analysis that reconstructs a linguistically plausible phylogenetic tree, and a novel four-way analysis that quantifies the complex network of similarities between all subsets of four languages.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A principled, non-parametric framework for multi-language correspondence discovery.** The primary contribution is the application of the MDL principle to simultaneously discover correspondence rules between an arbitrary number of languages without imposing prior assumptions on their form or distribution. This allows for the discovery of complex, many-to-many character mappings (e.g., (szcz, št)). This multi-way analysis capability is a significant advance over prevalent pairwise methods.
2.  **A novel metric for quantifying multi-language similarity.** A direct outcome of the MDL framework is the "shared Description Length" (sDL), a method for quantifying the amount of shared structure among any subset of the languages being analyzed. This provides a more nuanced and detailed view of linguistic similarity than what can be derived from a simple phylogenetic tree, as demonstrated in the four-language experiment.
3.  **A deterministic algorithm for rule inference.** The paper proposes an efficient, deterministic, greedy algorithm to infer correspondence rules. By iteratively merging the most statistically significant rule pairs, the method provides a reproducible way to explore the correspondence space, which contrasts with stochastic search methods.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Theoretical Foundation.** The methodology is well-grounded in the information-theoretic MDL principle. This provides a clear, objective function to optimize and avoids the need for arbitrary parameter tuning or assumptions about the underlying distribution of correspondences, a common weakness in other statistical approaches. The formulation of the two-part code appears technically sound.
2.  **Novelty of Multi-Way Analysis.** The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously is a key strength and point of novelty. This moves beyond the limitations of pairwise comparisons, which can be computationally prohibitive to scale and cannot capture rules that are only evident when three or more languages are considered together. The four-way analysis of Slavic languages provides compelling evidence of this capability, revealing subgroup similarities (CS-PL, RU-BG) and other subtle relationships within a single, unified model.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The algorithm, including the initialization, alignment (E-step), and rule merging (M-step), is described with sufficient detail to understand its mechanics. The deterministic nature of the core algorithm is a significant advantage for scientific reproducibility, as the results are dependent only on the data, not on random initialization.
4.  **Convincing Empirical Validation.** The experimental results strongly support the utility of the approach. The pairwise analysis, culminating in the Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) matrix and the phylogenetic tree in Figure 1, successfully recovers the established linguistic classification of the Slavic language family. This serves as excellent validation. The more novel four-way analysis provides linguistically plausible insights, such as identifying Czech as having the most complex individual orthography and Russian's similarity to multiple subgroups.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Scalability of the Alignment Procedure.** The paper acknowledges that the alignment step, formulated as a shortest-path problem, presents computational bottlenecks. The complexity is related to |R|, the number of possible rule applications, but the paper does not clarify how |R| scales with the number of languages, N. The combinatorial explosion of possible segmentations across N strings suggests that this scaling is exponential, which would severely limit the method's applicability beyond the reported N=5. The claim of being "agnostic to the number of input languages" holds in theory but appears to be constrained in practice. A more thorough analysis of the computational complexity and scalability is needed.
2.  **Absence of Comparative Evaluation.** The work is presented in isolation, without quantitative comparison to any existing methods. While the multi-language focus is novel, the method could have been compared to other approaches on the pairwise task. For example, how does the NCD-based phylogeny compare to trees generated by other computational phylogenetics methods? How do the discovered pairwise rules compare in quality or coverage to those found by other systems (e.g., those based on weighted finite-state transducers)? Without such a comparison, it is difficult to objectively assess the performance of the proposed method relative to the state of the art.
3.  **Potential Sensitivity to Noisy Data.** The method operates directly on orthographic cognate lists and seems to lack an explicit mechanism for handling noise, such as false cognates, borrowings, or transcription inconsistencies. The MDL framework implicitly penalizes overly complex models, which provides some robustness against overfitting, but it is unclear how the greedy rule-merging process would be affected by a small number of erroneous or atypical data points. This could potentially block the formation of valid general rules or lead to the creation of spurious ones. An evaluation of the model's robustness to such noise would strengthen the paper.
4.  **Limitations of the Greedy Search.** The M-step employs a greedy heuristic of merging the single best pair of rules at each iteration. This is a reasonable choice for a hard optimization problem but is susceptible to local optima. The paper notes that this may cause it to "miss some of the more subtle correspondence rules." A deeper discussion of the potential failure modes of this greedy approach would be beneficial. For example, a globally optimal complex rule might only be reachable via a sequence of merges that are individually sub-optimal, a path this algorithm cannot take.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding scalability, could you provide more detail on how the size of the alignment graph and the term |R| (maximum rule applications) grow as the number of languages N increases? Have you considered any pruning strategies or approximate search algorithms for the alignment step to mitigate this computational bottleneck and extend the method to a larger N?
2.  The "data over-weighting" technique using the α parameter is presented as a useful tool for linguistic exploration but deviates from the objective MDL principle. How sensitive are the types and granularity of learned rules to the choice of α? Is there a principled way to guide its selection, or is it intended to be set purely by trial and error based on expert judgment?
3.  In the rule-merging M-step, are candidate merge pairs restricted to rules that are adjacent in the current alignment of a data item? If so, could this locality constraint prevent the discovery of long-distance dependencies or discontinuous patterns within words? If not, how is the set of candidate pairs managed, as the number of all possible pairs could be very large?
