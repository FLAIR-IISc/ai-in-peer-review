### Summary
This paper presents a novel method for discovering string-level correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to find a set of correspondence rules that provides the most compact description of a given set of cognates. The authors propose a deterministic, EM-like algorithm that iteratively aligns data with a set of rules (E-step) and improves the rule set by greedily merging the pair of rules that yields the largest compression (M-step). The method is notable for its ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously without making prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of the correspondence rules. The authors introduce "Shared Description Length" (sDL) as a metric to quantify similarity among any subset of the analyzed languages. The approach is evaluated by constructing a phylogenetic tree for 13 Slavic languages and by performing a detailed four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian, demonstrating its ability to uncover known linguistic subgroups and quantify their inter-relationships.

### Contributions
1.  **A Principled Method for Multi-Language Similarity Quantification:** The primary contribution is the introduction of "Shared Description Length" (sDL), a novel, information-theoretic measure for quantifying the similarity between any subset of languages within a larger group. This moves beyond traditional pairwise distance metrics (like NCD) and allows for a more fine-grained analysis of complex linguistic relationships, which the authors demonstrate effectively in their four-way analysis of Slavic languages.
2.  **A Deterministic Algorithm for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The paper proposes a concrete MDL-based model and an associated EM-like algorithm to infer correspondence rules. The algorithm's deterministic nature, resulting from its greedy optimization strategy, is a significant feature, making results reproducible and potentially more interpretable for linguistic analysis compared to stochastic methods.
3.  **A Practical Technique for Controlling Rule Granularity:** The introduction of a "data over-weighting" parameter (`α`) is a valuable practical contribution. It provides a mechanism for users (e.g., linguists) to guide the model towards discovering less statistically frequent but potentially more linguistically meaningful, longer correspondence patterns, with a clear understanding that this introduces a subjective bias.

### Strengths
1.  **Novelty and Power of Multi-Way Analysis:** The paper's strongest aspect is its successful departure from the limitations of pairwise analysis. By modeling multiple languages jointly, it can capture and quantify shared structures that are invisible to pairwise methods. The sDL concept (Figure 3) is an excellent example of this, providing a rich, multi-faceted view of language relationships that a simple phylogenetic tree cannot offer.
2.  **Well-Founded Theoretical Framework:** The choice of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle is well-justified and appropriate for the task of discovering structure without strong prior assumptions. The authors clearly explain the two-part code formulation and connect it to a regularized maximum likelihood approach, providing a solid theoretical foundation for their model.
3.  **Strong and Intuitive Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Slavic languages are convincing. The pairwise analysis successfully reconstructs a phylogenetically plausible tree, serving as a solid sanity check and validating the underlying model. The four-way analysis effectively showcases the unique strengths of the approach, identifying known subgroups (West Slavic vs. East/South) and providing plausible explanations for the observed sDL values.
4.  **Clarity and Interpretability:** The proposed algorithm, based on iterative alignment and rule merging, is conceptually intuitive. The examples of learned correspondences (Table 3 and 4) clearly demonstrate what the model learns, from simple phonetic/orthographic rules like (g,h) to more complex morphological patterns like (eć,et). This interpretability is a significant advantage for potential applications in computational linguistics.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Significant Scalability Concerns:** The paper admits that the current implementation has a computational bottleneck, processing "up to five languages" within a few hours. This is a major limitation for a method whose central claim is its ability to handle multiple languages. The paper does not analyze the source of this complexity (is it exponential in the number of languages, N?) or propose potential solutions. This weakness curtails the practical applicability of the method to larger language families.
2.  **Greedy and Potentially Suboptimal Search:** The M-step of the algorithm employs a greedy strategy, merging the single best pair of rules at each iteration. While this ensures determinism, it is a local optimization technique that is not guaranteed to find a globally optimal set of rules. The model could potentially get stuck in a poor local minimum, especially early in training, and miss important but "subtle" correspondence rules, a point the authors briefly acknowledge. The impact of this greedy approach is not evaluated against other potential search strategies.
3.  **Lack of Comparative Evaluation:** The paper evaluates its method in isolation. While the results are internally consistent and plausible, there is no quantitative comparison to other methods for cognate analysis or phylogenetic reconstruction. For the pairwise analysis, comparing the generated NCD values or the resulting tree topology to those from other established methods (e.g., based on lexical similarity, phonetic edit distance) would have provided a stronger baseline for judging the method's performance.
4.  **Complexity of the MDL Formulation:** While principled, the exact formulation for the model cost, `L(M)`, is quite complex, involving universal codes for integers, data-to-model codes, and sums over all `2^N - 1` subsets of languages. This level of detail may present a barrier to reproducibility. A more streamlined presentation or the inclusion of pseudo-code for the cost calculation would improve the paper's clarity and utility for other researchers.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the computational complexity with respect to the number of languages, N? Is the bottleneck in the graph construction for the alignment step? Are there promising avenues for approximation (e.g., pruning the search space for alignments) that could scale the method to larger language groups (e.g., N=10 or more)?
2.  The greedy merging strategy is central to your algorithm's determinism. Have you considered or explored the trade-offs of using a less restricted search, such as a beam search, which would maintain multiple hypotheses for rule merges at each step? This might find better models at the cost of some determinism and computational overhead.
3.  The "data over-weighting" parameter `α` is a very useful tool for exploration. In your experiments, this was set manually "until there are no more singleton rules". Could you comment on the sensitivity of the learned rules to this parameter? Do you have any guidance or heuristics for setting it in a more principled way, perhaps based on a desired level of rule granularity or by optimizing on a held-out set?
