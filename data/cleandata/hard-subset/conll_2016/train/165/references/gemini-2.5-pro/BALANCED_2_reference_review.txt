### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents an unsupervised method based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle for discovering string-level correspondence rules from cognate lists spanning multiple languages. The core idea is that the best set of correspondence rules is the one that allows for the most compressed representation of the model and the data combined. The authors propose an EM-like algorithm that deterministically learns rules of arbitrary complexity (e.g., many-to-many character mappings) by iteratively aligning the data and greedily merging rules to improve the total description length. The method is demonstrated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, where it is used to construct a linguistically plausible phylogenetic tree and, more novelly, to perform a fine-grained, information-theoretic analysis of similarities among language subsets.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel framework for multi-way language similarity analysis.** The primary contribution is the method's ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously. The proposed "shared Description Length" (sDL) metric provides a principled way to quantify the structural similarity between *any subset* of the analyzed languages. This offers a much more nuanced view of language relationships than what is possible with pairwise comparisons and traditional tree-based models.
2.  **A non-parametric, deterministic algorithm for correspondence discovery.** The paper introduces a concrete algorithm that learns complex correspondence rules without imposing strong prior assumptions on their form or distribution. Its deterministic nature is a significant benefit for reproducibility and interpretability, making it a potentially valuable tool for linguistic analysis.
3.  **An insightful empirical demonstration on Slavic languages.** The experiments effectively showcase the method's capabilities. The pairwise analysis successfully reproduces known linguistic classifications, serving as a solid validation of the core approach. The four-way analysis demonstrates the new kinds of insights enabled by the multi-language framework, such as quantifying the relative similarity of language sub-groups (e.g., Czech-Polish vs. Russian-Bulgarian) within a single, coherent model.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled and Novel Multi-Language Analysis.** The paper's ability to model more than two languages at once is a significant advance. The sDL analysis (Section 5.3.1) is particularly compelling, moving beyond a single tree structure to quantify shared complexities between all 2^N - 1 subsets of languages. This allows for the discovery of sub-groupings and other cross-cutting similarities within a unified information-theoretic framework, which is a powerful new direction for computational historical linguistics.
2.  **Methodological Soundness and Transparency.** The approach is well-grounded in the MDL principle, providing a clear objective function. The EM-like greedy search strategy is intuitive, and the decision to build up complex rules from a "null alignment" based on statistical significance is an elegant way to avoid the strong structural biases present in many other models. The algorithm's deterministic nature is a laudable feature that enhances replicability.
3.  **Flexibility and Potential for Linguistic Interaction.** The authors introduce "data over-weighting" (Section 4.5) as a pragmatic tool for exploring rules at different levels of statistical significance. While this deviates from pure MDL, it makes the system more flexible for exploratory analysis. This feature, combined with the ability to initialize the model with pre-existing rules, creates a promising bridge between purely data-driven methods and expert-in-the-loop linguistic discovery.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Evaluation and No Comparison to Baselines.** The paper's evaluation is entirely intrinsic, relying on the alignment of its results with existing linguistic consensus for Slavic languages. The work would be substantially stronger with a comparison to other computational methods for correspondence discovery or phylogenetic reconstruction (e.g., methods based on finite-state transducers or probabilistic models). Even if many baselines are pairwise, a comparison could be constructed for the phylogenetic tree task (Section 5.2) by running a pairwise baseline on all language pairs and building a tree from the resulting distance matrix. Furthermore, an extrinsic evaluation on a downstream task, such as cognate identification or phonetic reconstruction, would provide a more objective measure of the quality and utility of the learned correspondences.
2.  **Significant Scalability Concerns.** The authors note that their implementation is limited to approximately five languages due to computational bottlenecks in the alignment step (Section 4.3). The time complexity is given in terms of `R`, the "maximum number of possible rule applications," but the relationship between `R` and the number of languages `N` is not specified. Given the combinatorial nature of multi-string alignment, `R` likely grows exponentially with `N`, which would severely limit the method's applicability to larger-scale studies. A more explicit analysis of the complexity with respect to `N` and a more concrete discussion of potential scaling strategies are needed to assess the method's practicality beyond small sets of languages.
3.  **Clarity of the MDL Formulation and Justification of Deviations.** While the high-level idea is clear, the detailed MDL code formulation in Section 4 is dense and difficult to parse, hindering reproducibility. The justification for specific choices, such as the use of the weak number composition for the "data-to-model" code, could be explained more intuitively. More critically, the introduction of the data over-weighting parameter `α` (Section 4.5) fundamentally breaks with the MDL principle the paper is built on. The authors acknowledge this but do not fully address the theoretical implications. The claim that rules are still discovered in order of "statistical significance" is difficult to assess when the statistics themselves are being artificially manipulated. This feature, while pragmatic, needs a more rigorous discussion of how to interpret its results.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the computational complexity of the alignment step with respect to the number of languages, N? Specifically, how does the term R (maximum number of possible rule applications) in your complexity analysis grow with N?
2.  The shared Description Length (sDL) is a very interesting metric. Is it possible that this metric could be influenced by universal but trivial phenomena (e.g., common consonants shared across all languages by chance) rather than exclusively capturing unique shared history? How does the model distinguish between meaningful shared innovations and background similarity?
