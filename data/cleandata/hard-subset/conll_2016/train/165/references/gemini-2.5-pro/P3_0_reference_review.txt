### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel method for discovering orthographic correspondence rules between multiple languages simultaneously. The core of the approach is the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which seeks the set of rules that most succinctly describes a given corpus of cognates. The authors formulate this as a two-part coding problem—encoding the model (the rule set) and the data given the model—and propose a deterministic, EM-like algorithm to find a good solution. The key contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Principled Multi-Language Framework:** The primary contribution is the extension of correspondence discovery from the typical pairwise setting to an arbitrary number of languages within a single, unified framework. This allows for the quantification of shared regularities not just between pairs, but among any subset of the languages under study, as demonstrated by the "shared Description Length" (sDL) metric.
2.  **A Concrete MDL-based Algorithm:** The paper details a specific MDL encoding scheme and an iterative optimization procedure to discover correspondence rules of arbitrary length without pre-specified distributional assumptions. The deterministic nature of the algorithm, based on greedy rule-merging, ensures reproducibility.
3.  **A Practical Extension for User Control:** The authors introduce a "data over-weighting" parameter (α) that allows a user to control the granularity of the discovered rules, trading off model simplicity for the ability to capture less frequent, but potentially linguistically interesting, patterns.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Soundness and Novelty:** The use of MDL is well-motivated for this task, as it provides a principled way to balance model complexity against data fit without relying on arbitrary priors. Generalizing this to handle more than two languages is a significant and non-trivial step forward. The resulting `sDL` analysis (Figure 3) is particularly powerful, offering a more nuanced view of language similarity than a simple phylogenetic tree by quantifying the complexity shared by specific subgroups (e.g., CS-PL, RU-BG, and all four languages). This is an insightful analysis tool that is a direct benefit of the multi-language approach.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Slavic languages are well-designed and convincing. The pairwise analysis, which results in a phylogenetically plausible tree (Figure 1), serves as an excellent sanity check and validates that the underlying NCD metric captures known linguistic relationships. This builds confidence in the method before the more novel four-way analysis is presented. The explanations provided for the positions of languages like Bulgarian/Macedonian and Polish demonstrate a good understanding of the data and the method's behavior.
3.  **Clarity and Interpretability:** The paper is clearly written, and the methodology is explained in sufficient detail. The choice to use a deterministic optimization procedure is a strength, as it makes the results fully reproducible. Furthermore, the discovered correspondences shown in the tables are interpretable and align with linguistic intuition, ranging from simple phonetic shifts (g,h) to complex orthographic patterns involving palatalization (dzie,dě) and liquid metathesis (ла,оло). This makes the output of the model potentially very useful for linguistic analysis.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Scalability Concerns:** The paper states that the current implementation can process "up to five languages...within a few hours on a 4GB RAM...single core desktop machine." This is a major practical limitation for a method whose main contribution is multi-language capability. The computational complexity is given in terms of abstract variables (`R`, `A`) without a clear analysis of how these scale with the number of languages `N` or the length of words. The claim of an "efficient" algorithm seems overstated given these limitations. A more thorough complexity analysis and discussion of the scalability bottleneck is needed for the reader to assess the method's practicality for larger-scale comparative linguistics.
2.  **Conflation of Orthography and Phonology:** The method operates exclusively on orthographic strings. While this is a valid design choice, its implications are not sufficiently discussed. The discovered "correspondences" are a mixture of phonetic, purely orthographic (e.g., `cz` vs. `č`), and morphological phenomena. This conflation can be problematic, as orthographic similarity does not always map cleanly to phonological or etymological relatedness. The analysis of Serbo-Croatian (SCl vs. SCc) hints at this, showing the script's influence. A discussion on how the method would perform on phonetically transcribed data, or on languages with less transparent orthographies, would significantly strengthen the paper.
3.  **Lack of Intrinsic Evaluation of Rules:** The evaluation is primarily extrinsic (the phylogenetic tree) or illustrative (showing examples). There is no intrinsic evaluation of the quality or correctness of the discovered rules themselves. For instance, the learned rules are not compared against a gold standard from historical linguistics or against the output of other (even pairwise) systems on the same data. Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the proposed MDL objective and search procedure are truly superior at finding high-quality correspondences, or just at producing a set of rules that is good at data compression.
4.  **Inconsistency in Philosophical Claims:** The paper initially motivates MDL as an "objective, unbiased" approach that foregoes assumptions. However, the introduction of the "data over-weighting" parameter `α` in Section 4.5 directly contradicts this. This parameter is a subjective knob for a user to tune the model's complexity penalty, much like a standard regularization hyperparameter. While practically useful, its introduction should be framed as a pragmatic departure from the pure MDL philosophy, rather than a seamless feature of it. The claim of objectivity is weakened by the reliance on this parameter to produce the more detailed rules shown in Table 3.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity, specifically with respect to the number of languages `N`? What is the primary bottleneck—memory from the graph construction or the CPU time in the M-step's pairwise merging search? Are there potential heuristics (e.g., beam search for rule merging) that could make the method more scalable?
2.  Your examples of learned rules are compelling but highlight the mix of orthographic, phonetic, and morphological patterns. Could you discuss how a linguist is meant to disentangle these phenomena from your model's output? Have you considered applying your method to phonetically transcribed data (e.g., IPA), and how might you expect the results and the `sDL` scores to differ?
3.  Regarding the `α` parameter for data over-weighting: this seems to function as a regularization term. How would a user choose a value for `α` in a principled manner for a new dataset, beyond subjective inspection of the output? How does this parameter affect the NCD calculations and the resulting phylogenetic tree, which are presented as objective results?
