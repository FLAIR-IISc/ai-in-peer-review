### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a method for discovering string-level correspondence rules between multiple languages from cognate lists. The approach is based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which posits that the best model is the one that most succinctly describes the data. The authors propose a two-part code for a model (a set of correspondence rules) and the data encoded with that model. An iterative, EM-like algorithm is used to find a good set of rules by starting with single-character rules and greedily merging them to maximize compression. The method is demonstrated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, where it is used to construct a phylogenetic tree from pairwise distances and, more novelly, to perform a fine-grained analysis of similarities between subsets of four languages.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Analysis:** The primary contribution is the generalization of correspondence discovery from language pairs to an arbitrary number of languages. The proposed "Shared Description Length" (sDL) metric is a novel and powerful tool for quantifying the degree of regularity shared among any given subset of languages, providing a much richer analysis than what is possible with a collection of pairwise comparisons.
2.  **A Principled, Non-Parametric Model:** The paper formulates the discovery task within the MDL framework, providing a statistically sound and objective method for learning correspondence rules. This approach avoids imposing prior assumptions on the length, shape, or distribution of rules, allowing them to be learned directly from the data.
3.  **A Practical Method with User Control:** The paper presents a deterministic algorithm for rule inference and introduces a "data over-weighting" technique. This `α` parameter provides a practical mechanism for users (e.g., linguists) to explore rules that may not be statistically significant in a small dataset, effectively allowing them to tune the granularity of the discovered correspondences.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Soundness:** The use of the MDL principle is a significant strength. It provides a well-founded, information-theoretic basis for model selection that naturally balances model complexity with data fit. This avoids the need for arbitrary parameter tuning or assumptions about the underlying distributions of correspondences, leading to more objective results.
2.  **Novel Multi-Way Analysis:** The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously is a key advantage over most existing work. The four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian is a compelling demonstration. The sDL analysis in Figure 3, which quantifies the shared complexity between all 15 subsets of the four languages, provides a level of detail and insight that is not achievable with standard pairwise methods.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the method's capabilities. The pairwise analysis on 13 Slavic languages reproduces a linguistically plausible phylogenetic tree (Figure 1), confirming that the underlying NCD metric captures known language relationships. This successful validation on a standard task lends credibility to the results of the more novel multi-language analysis.
4.  **Interpretability and Plausibility of Results:** The qualitative examples of learned correspondences are linguistically sensible. The method discovers a range of phenomena, from simple phonetic/orthographic mappings like (g,h) and (cz,č) to more complex morphological patterns like liquid metathesis (ла,оло) and verb endings (eć,et). This demonstrates that the model is capturing meaningful linguistic regularities.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Scalability Concerns:** The paper's main claim is its ability to handle multiple languages, but it also acknowledges "bottlenecks in memory consumption as well as in runtime" and that the current implementation can process "up to five languages." The complexity of the alignment step, which is central to the algorithm, is not clearly characterized with respect to the number of languages `N`. This appears to be a major limitation on the practical applicability of the method for "more than two" languages, and the promise of analyzing larger language groups remains unproven.
2.  **Reliance on Greedy Optimization:** The M-step of the training procedure relies on a greedy strategy of merging the single best pair of rules at each iteration. While computationally efficient, this local optimization is not guaranteed to find a globally optimal set of rules. The paper notes that this may cause it to "miss some of the more subtle correspondence rules," but the potential severity of this issue is not explored. The results could be highly dependent on the path taken by this greedy search.
3.  **Clarity of the MDL Formulation:** While the high-level idea of the MDL code is clear, the detailed mathematical formulation in Section 4.1 is dense and difficult to parse. For instance, the exact purpose and derivation of the combinatorial term `log( ( TΠ − 1 |Π| − 1 ) )` in the rule table encoding `L(Π)` are not explained intuitively. A clearer exposition, perhaps with a small, concrete example walking through the calculation, would significantly improve the paper's comprehensibility and reproducibility.
4.  **Limited Evaluation:** The evaluation is entirely intrinsic, based on the plausibility of a phylogenetic tree and qualitative inspection of learned rules. While valuable, the paper would be much stronger if it included an extrinsic, task-based evaluation. The introduction mentions machine translation and cognate reconstruction as applications. Using the learned rules in one of these downstream tasks and comparing performance against other correspondence discovery methods would provide a more objective measure of their quality and utility.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please elaborate on the computational complexity of the alignment procedure, specifically with respect to the number of languages `N`? Is the growth in runtime/memory polynomial or exponential in `N`? What are the prospects for scaling the method beyond the five-language limit mentioned?
2.  The greedy M-step is a potential weakness. Have you investigated how sensitive the final model is to this greedy choice? For example, have you experimented with alternative search strategies (like beam search) or analyzed how different initializations might affect the final rule set?
3.  In the four-way analysis (Section 5.3.1), you interpret the large individual sDL for certain languages (e.g., Russian's effect on other subsets) as evidence of linguistic influence. However, could a large sDL for a single language also arise from high internal regularity that is best captured by many singleton rules specific to that language? How can your model distinguish between inter-language influence and intra-language complexity?
