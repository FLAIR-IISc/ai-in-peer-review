### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a novel method for automatically discovering string-level correspondences across multiple languages from lists of cognate words. The core of the approach is the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which posits that the best model (here, a set of correspondence rules) is the one that provides the most compact description of the data. The authors formalize this with a two-part code, covering the cost of encoding the rules themselves and the cost of encoding the data using those rules. An iterative, EM-like algorithm is proposed to find a good set of rules by starting with single-character rules and greedily merging them based on which merge provides the greatest compression. The method is evaluated on a set of 13 Slavic languages, first in pairwise comparisons to construct a phylogenetic tree, and then in a four-way analysis to demonstrate a more fine-grained quantification of language similarity.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Principled, Multi-Language Framework for Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is the successful application of the MDL principle to the problem of finding correspondences in more than two languages simultaneously. Unlike many existing methods that are restricted to pairwise comparisons, this approach is inherently multi-lingual, which better reflects the practice of the historical-comparative method. The formulation is elegant and avoids strong prior assumptions about the nature or distribution of correspondence rules.

2.  **A Novel Metric for Quantifying Language Subgroup Similarity:** The concept of "Shared Description Length" (sDL) is a direct and insightful outcome of the MDL framework. It provides a quantitative measure of the structural complexity shared by any given subset of languages in the analysis. This moves beyond a simple distance metric or phylogenetic tree, allowing for a more nuanced understanding of the relationships, as demonstrated effectively in the four-way Slavic analysis (Figure 3).

### Strengths

1.  **Theoretical Soundness and Objectivity:** The use of the MDL principle provides a strong, information-theoretic foundation for the method. It avoids the need for arbitrary hyperparameters or priors on rule distributions that afflict many statistical models. This makes the "pure" version of the model (without data over-weighting) an objective tool for analyzing the regularities present in the data itself, which is a significant scientific advantage.

2.  **Generalizability to N languages:** The ability to handle more than two languages is a key strength and a clear advance over much of the related work that focuses on pairwise alignment. This is crucial for historical linguistics, where evidence is often drawn from an entire language family. The sDL analysis shows that this capability yields genuinely new insights that would be obscured by a series of pairwise comparisons.

3.  **Flexibility and Interpretability of Learned Rules:** The method is capable of learning correspondences of varying lengths and types, from single-character phonetic shifts (g,h) to complex orthographic clusters (szcz,št) and morphological endings (eć,et). The examples provided in Tables 3 and 4 demonstrate that the learned rules are linguistically plausible and interpretable. The deterministic nature of the core algorithm also ensures reproducibility.

4.  **Convincing Experimental Validation:** The experiments on the Slavic languages are well-executed and persuasive. The pairwise analysis and the resulting phylogenetic tree (Figure 1) successfully replicate established linguistic classifications, serving as a strong sanity check and validation of the underlying distance metric (NCD). The more novel four-way analysis provides a compelling demonstration of the method's unique analytical power, correctly identifying the West (CS-PL) and East/South (RU-BG) pairings while also quantifying the structure shared by all four languages.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Scalability Concerns:** The authors candidly state that their current implementation can handle up to five languages on a standard desktop machine. The alignment procedure, which involves finding a shortest path in a graph whose nodes represent index tuples across N languages, is likely exponential in N. This is a major practical limitation, as comparative studies often involve dozens of languages. The paper would be stronger if it addressed this limitation more directly, perhaps by discussing the theoretical complexity and potential heuristics or approximations for scaling to larger N.

2.  **Limited Extrinsic or Comparative Evaluation:** The evaluation is entirely intrinsic. The quality of the model is judged by its ability to produce a sensible phylogenetic tree and plausible-looking alignments. There is no comparison to other computational methods for correspondence discovery or cognate analysis. Furthermore, the utility of the discovered rules is not tested in a downstream task, such as cognate reconstruction or improving machine translation lexicons. While the results are promising, a comparison against a baseline or state-of-the-art method would be necessary to fully gauge the method's performance.

3.  **The "Data Over-Weighting" Parameter Undermines the Core Principle:** The introduction of the α parameter in Section 4.5 is a pragmatic but theoretically questionable addition. A primary strength of the MDL approach is its objectivity and freedom from tunable parameters. Adding an arbitrary weight `α` re-introduces the subjectivity that MDL is meant to eliminate. It's unclear how one would choose `α` in a principled way, and it effectively transforms the method from a discovery procedure into an exploration tool guided by user intuition. This "feature" feels more like a patch for data sparsity issues than a core component of the model.

4.  **Greedy Optimization:** The M-step of the training procedure relies on a greedy strategy of merging the two rules that yield the greatest immediate improvement in description length. While this is computationally tractable, it is susceptible to finding local optima. The paper acknowledges this possibility but suggests it is not a major issue in practice. However, it remains a theoretical weakness; more complex or subtle correspondence patterns that require a non-greedy sequence of merges to be discovered might be missed by the algorithm.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding scalability: Could you please elaborate on the time and space complexity of the alignment procedure with respect to the number of languages N? Are there any promising avenues for making this tractable for larger language families (e.g., N > 10), such as beam search during alignment or other pruning strategies?

2.  Regarding the "data over-weighting" parameter `α`: In your experiments, how was the value of `α` chosen to generate the alignments marked with an asterisk (*)? Was it set manually until a desired level of detail was achieved? Could this parameter be potentially set automatically, for example by targeting a certain ratio of `L(M)` to `L(D|M)`?

3.  The model operates on raw orthography. Have you considered how this approach would handle languages with very different writing systems (e.g., one logographic, one alphabetic) or phonetically non-transparent orthographies (like English or French)? Would a pre-processing step to convert words to a phonetic representation (like IPA) be a necessary or beneficial extension to the framework?
