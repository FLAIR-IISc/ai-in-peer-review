### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel method for discovering systematic string-level correspondences among multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The central idea is to define the best set of correspondence rules as the one that provides the most compact description of a given set of cognate tuples. The method avoids making prior assumptions about the distribution or shape of the rules. The authors propose a deterministic, EM-like greedy algorithm that iteratively builds a set of correspondence rules by merging simpler patterns. The framework is evaluated on a set of 13 Slavic languages, first through pairwise comparisons that reconstruct a plausible phylogenetic tree, and second through a more detailed four-way analysis that showcases the method's ability to handle multiple languages simultaneously.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A principled and novel information-theoretic framework for simultaneously discovering correspondence rules among *more than two* languages. This is a significant advance over much of the existing work, which is often limited to pairwise comparisons, and allows for a more holistic analysis of language families.
2.  The introduction and application of "Shared Description Length" (sDL) as a quantitative, fine-grained measure for analyzing linguistic similarity. This metric moves beyond simple pairwise distances and allows for the quantification of shared structure among any subset of the languages being analyzed, offering a more nuanced and insightful view of their relationships.
3.  An efficient, deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules. While greedy, its deterministic nature ensures reproducibility, and its ability to be initialized with prior linguistic knowledge makes it a potentially valuable tool for linguists.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Principled Framework:** The application of MDL to the multi-language correspondence problem is elegant and well-motivated. It provides a solid theoretical foundation for defining a "good" set of correspondences without resorting to arbitrary distributional assumptions. The ability to natively handle more than two languages at once is a key conceptual and practical advantage over many existing methods, which would require an infeasible number of pairwise runs to approximate a multi-way analysis.

2.  **Insightful Fine-grained Analysis:** The "Shared Description Length" (sDL) analysis presented in Section 5.3.1 is a standout feature of the paper. It provides a much richer and more detailed picture of linguistic relationships than a single phylogenetic tree can offer. The visualization in Figure 3 effectively demonstrates how the model quantifies similarities not just between pairs (CS-PL, RU-BG) but also triplets and the entire quartet of languages. This offers novel, quantifiable insights, such as the relative strength of different subgroupings and the influence of a specific language (Russian) on various subsets.

3.  **Convincing and Well-Designed Experiments:** The experiments provide strong support for the proposed method. The pairwise analysis on 13 Slavic languages serves as an effective validation, as the resulting NCD-based phylogenetic tree largely aligns with established linguistic classifications. This builds confidence in the underlying model. The subsequent four-way analysis then successfully demonstrates the unique capabilities of the multi-language framework, supported by clear qualitative examples and the quantitative sDL analysis.

4.  **Practicality and Interpretability:** The algorithm is deterministic, which is a significant advantage for reproducibility and for users who want to understand the model's behavior without the stochasticity of other methods. Furthermore, the "data over-weighting" mechanism is a pragmatic and well-explained feature. While it knowingly deviates from pure MDL objectivity, it grants the user (e.g., a linguist) valuable control over the granularity of the discovered rules, allowing for a trade-off between statistical significance and descriptive detail.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Scalability Concerns:** The paper acknowledges computational bottlenecks, stating the current implementation handles up to five languages. However, the analysis of this limitation is brief. The complexity of the alignment step (E-step) is given as O(|D|·|R|²), but it is not clear how R (maximum rule applications) scales with the number of languages N. Since the alignment graph size grows combinatorially with N, this is a critical limitation for applying the method to larger language families. A more detailed analysis of the scalability and potential pruning or optimization strategies would strengthen the paper.

2.  **Greedy Optimization and Local Optima:** The M-step employs a greedy strategy of merging the best pair of rules at each iteration. While this ensures determinism, it is highly susceptible to local optima. An early, suboptimal merge could prevent the discovery of a more globally optimal, linguistically plausible rule later on. The paper acknowledges this possibility ("we may miss some of the more subtle correspondence rules") but does not investigate its impact. A discussion of this limitation, or a comparison with alternative search strategies (e.g., beam search), would be necessary to understand the robustness of the discovered models.

3.  **Lack of Intrinsic Evaluation of Rules:** The evaluation is primarily extrinsic (the phylogenetic tree is sensible) and illustrative (a few examples of rules are shown). There is no intrinsic, quantitative evaluation of the quality of the learned correspondence rules themselves. The primary output of the model is the rule set Π, yet its quality is not directly assessed against any gold standard, such as rules established by historical linguists. Without this, it is difficult to judge whether the model is learning linguistically meaningful regularities or simply exploiting statistical quirks in the orthography of the cognate lists.

4.  **Clarity of the MDL Formulation:** While the high-level idea is clear, the formal description of the model code L(M) in Section 4.1 is dense and may be difficult for non-experts to penetrate. For instance, the use of a data-to-model code based on "weak number composition" is stated without sufficient intuition or justification for this specific choice. A small, concrete numerical example walking through the calculation of L(M) and L(D|M) for a toy dataset would significantly improve the accessibility and clarity of the technical contribution.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the scalability limitations? Specifically, how does the size of the alignment search space (and thus `R` in your complexity analysis) grow as a function of the number of languages `N`? Have you explored any pruning or approximation strategies in the Dijkstra alignment step to make the method more tractable for, say, 6-10 languages?
2.  Your greedy M-step is deterministic but prone to local optima. Have you performed any experiments with alternative search strategies (e.g., beam search for rule merges, or randomized restarts) to assess how much the greedy approach might be missing in terms of final model quality (i.e., total description length)? How stable are the high-level findings (like the sDL distributions) to small perturbations in the data or algorithm?
3.  The "data over-weighting" parameter `α` is presented as an exploratory tool. Is there a more principled way to set this value? How do the quantitative results, such as the NCD matrix and the resulting phylogenetic tree, change as `α` is increased? Does a higher `α` consistently lead to more or less linguistically plausible groupings, or are the effects unpredictable?
