**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces a method for automatically discovering systematic string correspondences between multiple languages simultaneously. The approach is based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which posits that the best model (in this case, a set of correspondence rules) is the one that provides the most compact description of the data (a list of cognate tuples). The authors propose an EM-like iterative algorithm to infer these rules, starting from a "null alignment" and greedily merging patterns to improve the total description length. The method is evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, where it is used to construct a phylogenetic tree from pairwise distances and to perform a more detailed four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian, introducing a "shared Description Length" metric to quantify similarity among language subsets.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery Framework:** The primary contribution is a formal framework that extends correspondence discovery beyond pairwise comparisons to an arbitrary number of languages. This allows for the simultaneous inference of rules like (PL:szcz, CS:št, RU:сч, BG:щ), which is a notable advance over methods restricted to pairs.
2.  **An Information-Theoretic Similarity Metric for Language Subsets:** The paper proposes the "shared Description Length" (sDL) as a way to quantify the amount of shared structure between any subset of the analyzed languages. This offers a more granular view of linguistic similarity than a single, consolidated distance metric or phylogenetic tree.

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled Theoretical Foundation:** The use of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle is well-suited for this unsupervised discovery task. It provides a theoretically sound, non-Bayesian approach to balance model complexity against data fit, aiming for an objective discovery process without imposing strong distributional priors on the correspondences.
2.  **Generalizability beyond Pairwise Analysis:** The method's ability to handle more than two languages at once is a significant strength. This capability is leveraged effectively in the four-way analysis (Section 5.3) to reveal subtler relationships, such as the strong similarity between the CS-PL and RU-BG subgroups within a larger analysis, that would be obscured in a purely pairwise approach.
3.  **Plausible Experimental Results:** The application to Slavic languages yields results that align well with established linguistic knowledge. The NCD-based phylogenetic tree correctly groups the languages into their West, East, and South Slavic branches, lending credibility to the underlying similarity measures produced by the model.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Complete Lack of Empirical Baselines:** The paper makes strong claims about its efficacy but fails to compare its results to any existing computational methods. The field has numerous approaches for cognate identification and phonetic/orthographic correspondence discovery. Without quantitative comparisons—for instance, on alignment quality, reconstruction accuracy, or even the resulting phylogenetic topology—it is impossible to assess whether this complex MDL framework offers any tangible benefit over simpler or alternative methods.
2.  **The "Data Over-weighting" Technique Undermines the Core Premise:** The introduction of the α parameter (Section 4.5) to "over-weight" the data term is a major conceptual flaw. The paper's central claim is that it offers an "objective, unbiased" analysis rooted in the MDL principle. This parameter transforms the method into a subjective, user-tuned system. The justification for its use is to find "near-significant" rules, but the selection criteria given in the experiment ("over-weight our data until there are no more singleton rules") is entirely ad-hoc and post-hoc. This feature fundamentally contradicts and invalidates the paper's claims of objectivity.
3.  **Methodological Opacity and Questionable Heuristics:** The technical description of the algorithm lacks clarity in several areas. The M-step of the "EM" procedure is not a maximization but a greedy heuristic that merges the two patterns yielding the best compression gain. This raises questions about convergence guarantees and sensitivity to the order of operations. The paper labels this as "deterministic," but this only refers to the execution path; the greedy nature does not guarantee optimality. Furthermore, the precise mathematical justification for the "data-to-model code" formula in L(Π) (the log-multinomial term) is asserted rather than explained, leaving its appropriateness unclear.
4.  **Significant Deficiencies in Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of presentation flaws that suggest a lack of care. The reference list is severely malformed, containing processing artifacts (e.g., `? \Q2004\E`) and incomplete entries, making it unusable. In Section 1, the text incorrectly states the model will be presented in "Section 4," skipping over the actual Section 3. Captions for tables and figures are inconsistent ("T 1" vs. "F 1"). There are numerous typos and grammatical errors (e.g., "Much of these focus...", "and and SCc"). These issues collectively detract from the paper's professionalism and readability.
5.  **Unsubstantiated Claims and Interpretations:** The analysis of Figure 3 concludes that Russian "heavily influence[s] the other languages," which is presented as a "highly satisfying result." This is a strong linguistic claim of causation (influence) based purely on a statistical measure of orthographic similarity. The method itself provides no evidence for directionality or influence, and this over-interpretation of the results is unwarranted. Additionally, the claim of an "efficient" algorithm is questionable given the reported runtime of "a few hours" for just five languages on dated hardware.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The M-step of your algorithm is a greedy local search. Could you comment on its convergence properties? How sensitive is the final model to the order in which patterns are merged, and have you explored alternative, less myopic optimization schemes for the M-step?
2.  The "data over-weighting" parameter α appears to be a critical component for generating the detailed alignments in Table 3. Could you propose a principled, data-driven method for setting α that does not rely on arbitrary, post-hoc goals like eliminating all singleton rules? Without such a method, how can the objectivity of the MDL framework be maintained?
3.  Why were no baseline methods included in the evaluation? A comparison, even against a simple pairwise alignment method iterated over all pairs, would be crucial for contextualizing the performance and contribution of your more complex multi-language model.
4.  Could you provide a clearer derivation or a specific reference for the "weak number composition" formula used for the data-to-model code in both L(Σi) and L(Π)? The current explanation is insufficient to fully understand the model code.
