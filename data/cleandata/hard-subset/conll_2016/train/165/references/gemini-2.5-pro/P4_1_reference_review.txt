### Summary
This paper presents a novel method for discovering systematic string-level correspondences between multiple languages. The approach is framed under the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which seeks the set of correspondence rules that most succinctly describes a given corpus of cognate tuples. The method makes no prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of these rules. The core of the contribution is an efficient, deterministic algorithm, analogous to Expectation-Maximization (EM), that iteratively builds a model. It starts with a simple model (a "null alignment" of single characters) and greedily merges rule patterns that yield the largest compression of the data. The authors evaluate their method by performing pairwise and four-way analyses on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The pairwise analysis successfully reconstructs a linguistically plausible phylogenetic tree. The four-way analysis demonstrates the method's unique ability to quantify similarities between all subsets of the analyzed languages, offering a more granular view than traditional pairwise comparisons.

### Contributions
1.  **A Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery Framework:** The primary contribution is a principled framework for discovering string correspondences simultaneously across more than two languages (N > 2). Unlike most existing methods which are limited to pairwise analysis, this approach can model and quantify the complex relationships within a group of languages. This is demonstrated through the novel "shared Description Length" (sDL) metric, which measures the amount of shared structure among any subset of the languages being analyzed.
2.  **An MDL-based Model and Inference Algorithm:** The paper formulates the problem of correspondence discovery using the two-part MDL principle. This provides a technically sound, assumption-free foundation for model selection that naturally balances model complexity with data fit. The accompanying EM-like deterministic algorithm for inferring the correspondence rules is a key part of this contribution.
3.  **A Data Over-weighting Mechanism for User-Guided Exploration:** The paper introduces a mechanism to weight the data term in the MDL objective function. This allows a user, such as a linguist, to explore statistically less significant but potentially interesting correspondence rules by effectively increasing the amount of evidence in the data. This adds a practical layer of flexibility to the otherwise objective statistical method.

### Strengths
1.  **Novelty and Significance of Multi-Language Modeling:** The ability to analyze more than two languages jointly is a significant advance. Pairwise methods can only approximate multi-way relationships by chaining comparisons, potentially missing complex shared innovations or retentions across language subgroups. The four-way analysis of Slavic languages (Section 5.3) compellingly demonstrates this strength, providing a detailed breakdown of shared structures (e.g., CS-PL vs. RU-BG, and the structure shared by all four) that a series of pairwise analyses could not easily reveal. This opens up new possibilities for computational comparative linguistics.
2.  **Principled Theoretical Foundation:** The use of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle is a major strength. It provides an objective, information-theoretic criterion for what constitutes a "good" set of correspondences, avoiding the need for arbitrary assumptions about rule distributions or priors that are common in Bayesian approaches. This formal grounding makes the results more interpretable as it directly links the discovery of rules to their statistical significance in compressing the data. The formulation of the model code `L(M)` and data code `L(D|M)` is clear and technically sound.
3.  **Reproducibility and Clarity:** The proposed inference algorithm is deterministic, which is a highly valuable property for scientific research as it ensures full reproducibility. The paper is well-written, and the steps of the algorithm (initialization, expectation via alignment, maximization via merging) are explained clearly. The connection between the merging strategy and statistical significance (more frequent sub-patterns are considered first) is intuitive and well-argued.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments effectively validate the approach. The pairwise analysis (Section 5.2) serves as a successful sanity check, showing that the NCD metric derived from the model can reconstruct a phylogenetic tree of Slavic languages that aligns well with established linguistic classifications. This grounds the method in known facts before the more novel multi-way analysis is presented, lending credibility to its results.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Scalability Concerns:** The paper explicitly notes computational bottlenecks, stating that the current implementation can process "up to five languages... within a few hours on a 4GB RAM, 2.5GHz single core desktop machine." The alignment procedure is identified as a shortest-path problem in a graph whose size is combinatorial with the number of languages. This is a significant practical limitation. The paper does not provide a clear analysis of how the complexity scales with the number of languages (N), the length of the cognate strings, or alphabet sizes. Without this, it is difficult to assess whether the approach is feasible for the larger-scale analyses common in historical linguistics (e.g., involving dozens of languages).
2.  **Limited Empirical Comparison to Prior Work:** The introduction positions the work against prior statistical approaches but does not include any direct, quantitative comparison. While the authors' method is novel in its multi-language capability, it would be strengthened by comparing its pairwise results (e.g., the quality of the learned rules or the resulting phylogeny) against established pairwise methods on the same data. Such a comparison would help to situate the method's performance and justify the additional complexity of the joint modeling approach, even in the N=2 case.
3.  **Ambiguity of the Data Over-weighting Technique:** The introduction of the `α` parameter to over-weight the data term (Section 4.5) is presented as a practical feature but departs from the principled MDL framework. The choice of `α` appears subjective. The paper states that for the experiments, data was over-weighted "until there are no more singleton rules," which is an ad-hoc stopping criterion. This feature, while potentially useful for exploration, undermines the paper's central claim of providing an "objective" analysis. The implications of this deviation from the formal MDL principle are not fully discussed.
4.  **Qualitative Evaluation of Learned Rules:** The evaluation of the discovered correspondence rules is limited to showing a few illustrative examples in tables. While insightful, this is a qualitative assessment. The paper would be more convincing if it included an extrinsic evaluation of the rules' quality. For example, the learned rules could be used in a downstream task like cognate reconstruction or transliteration, and their performance could be measured and compared against rules generated by another system. This would provide a more objective measure of their linguistic utility.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of your algorithm, particularly with respect to the number of languages N? Given the stated limitations for N=5, what are the primary algorithmic or memory bottlenecks, and do you foresee any practical optimizations that could extend the method's applicability to larger language families?
2.  The data over-weighting mechanism introduces a subjective parameter `α`. Does this compromise the statistical interpretation of the description length, and could you elaborate on a more principled way to guide the choice of `α` or the stopping criterion for increasing it, beyond the ad-hoc method used in the experiments?
3.  While the multi-language focus is novel, how does your method perform on standard pairwise tasks compared to existing methods? A quantitative comparison on a task like cognate identification or a metric comparing phylogenetic trees could help demonstrate that the joint modeling does not sacrifice performance in the simpler, well-studied pairwise setting.
