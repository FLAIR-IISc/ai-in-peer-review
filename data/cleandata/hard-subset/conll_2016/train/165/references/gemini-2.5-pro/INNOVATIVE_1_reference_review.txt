### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel, non-parametric method for discovering systematic string correspondences between multiple languages. The approach is founded on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which posits that the best model (here, a set of correspondence rules) is the one that provides the most compact description of the data (cognate lists). The method employs an EM-like algorithm that starts with simple, single-character rules and greedily merges them to form more complex, multi-character, multi-language rules that yield the largest savings in description length. The framework is notable for making no prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of correspondence rules.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A bold, assumption-free framework for multi-language correspondence discovery.** The primary contribution is the conceptual shift away from pairwise, feature-based, or structurally constrained models. By treating correspondences as arbitrary associated strings and leveraging the principled framework of MDL, the authors propose a powerful and flexible method that can operate on more than two languages simultaneously. This represents a significant new direction for research in computational historical linguistics.
2.  **The introduction of "Shared Description Length" (sDL) as a fine-grained similarity metric.** A direct benefit of the multi-language model is the ability to quantify the amount of shared structure between any given subset of languages. This moves beyond the coarse, tree-based representation of language relatedness to a richer, more nuanced analysis that can highlight complex relationships, as compellingly demonstrated in the four-way analysis of Slavic languages.
3.  **A pragmatic "data over-weighting" technique to bridge statistical and linguistic significance.** The authors introduce a simple modification to the MDL objective (`α` parameter) that allows an expert user to guide the model towards discovering more detailed and complex rules that may be linguistically plausible but lack sufficient statistical evidence in a limited dataset. This is a clever and practical feature that enhances the tool's utility for linguistic exploration.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Ambition:** This paper stands out for its conceptual novelty. The decision to abandon common structural constraints and develop a fully multi-lingual, non-parametric discovery algorithm is a bold and refreshing approach to a classic problem. The application of MDL in this context is elegant, providing a solid theoretical foundation for "letting the data speak for itself." This work is not an incremental improvement on an existing method but a proposal for a new way of thinking about the problem.
2.  **Enables Powerful New Analyses:** The framework's ability to analyze multiple languages at once is not just a quantitative improvement; it unlocks qualitatively new types of analysis. The sDL metric (Figure 3) is a prime example. Being able to see and quantify the complexity shared by *all four* languages versus the complexity shared by just Czech and Polish, or Russian and Bulgarian, provides a far more detailed picture of their inter-relationships than a simple phylogenetic tree can offer. This is a significant advance for data-driven linguistic typology and analysis.
3.  **Convincing and Well-Chosen Experiments:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the method's potential. The reconstruction of the Slavic family tree from pairwise NCD values serves as a solid sanity check, showing that the model's similarity metric aligns with established linguistic knowledge. More importantly, the example alignments (Tables 3 & 4) and the four-way sDL analysis provide compelling evidence that the model discovers linguistically plausible rules of varying granularity and reveals subtle inter-group similarities.
4.  **Thoughtful and Pragmatic Design:** The inclusion of the data over-weighting mechanism shows a keen awareness of the practical needs of the target audience (linguists). It acknowledges that pure statistical optimality on small corpora may not be the sole desideratum and provides a transparent way for users to explore the space of near-significant patterns. This turns the method from a purely automatic discovery tool into a flexible, interactive analysis aid.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few areas that could be strengthened. I see these as opportunities for future work rather than fundamental flaws.

1.  **Scalability Limitations:** The authors frankly state that the current implementation is limited to approximately five languages due to computational complexity in the alignment step. While this is understandable for a novel method proving its concept, it does temper the promise of a truly "multi-language" framework. A more in-depth discussion of the source of this complexity (e.g., the size of the alignment graph) and potential algorithmic improvements or principled approximations to address it would make the paper's contributions even more impactful.
2.  **Heuristic Nature of the Optimization:** The maximization step relies on a deterministic, greedy search (merging the two best patterns at each step). This approach is sensible but susceptible to local optima. The quality of the final rule set could be sensitive to the order of merges. The work would be stronger with a brief discussion of this limitation, perhaps including an experiment on a small dataset to show how much the results vary if a degree of randomness is introduced (as suggested in the conclusion), which would give a better sense of the search space's ruggedness.
3.  **Clarity of the MDL Formulation:** The formal description of the model code `L(M)` is quite dense. While the components are standard in the MDL literature, the overall formula for `L(Π)` is complex, and the paper moves through its justification very quickly. A small, illustrative example walking through the calculation of the code length for a toy model with two or three rules would greatly improve the accessibility and reproducibility of the method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The data over-weighting parameter `α` is a very compelling feature. Your experimental method was to increase it until no singleton rules remained in the alignments. Have you explored other heuristics for setting this value? For instance, does plotting the total description length or the number of discovered rules against `α` reveal an "elbow" point that might suggest a natural trade-off between model complexity and data fit?
2.  Regarding the greedy M-step, how stable is the discovery process? For example, if you run the algorithm on two different 80% subsamples of the same dataset, do the top-ranked (by contribution to code length savings) discovered rules remain largely the same?
3.  How does the model handle noise, such as non-cognate pairs accidentally included in the input list? Would it be robust and simply describe the unrelated words using singleton rules, or is there a risk it might invent spurious, complex rules to account for them?
