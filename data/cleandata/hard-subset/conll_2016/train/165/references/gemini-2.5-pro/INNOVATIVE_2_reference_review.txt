### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel, unsupervised method for discovering orthographic correspondence rules between multiple languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is that the best set of correspondence rules is the one that allows for the most compressed representation of a given corpus of cognates. The authors propose an EM-like deterministic algorithm that starts with single-character rules and iteratively merges them to form longer, more complex correspondences based on which merges yield the greatest reduction in total description length. The framework is demonstrated on a set of 13 Slavic languages, showing its ability to construct a linguistically plausible phylogenetic tree from pairwise analyses and, more impressively, to perform a simultaneous four-way analysis that quantifies shared structure between all subsets of the languages.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A highly novel, assumption-free framework for discovering correspondence rules between multiple languages simultaneously. The formulation of this problem in MDL terms is elegant and represents a significant conceptual advance over prior work, which is often limited to pairwise comparisons or relies on strong distributional assumptions.
2.  The introduction of a new metric, Shared Description Length (sDL), which leverages the multi-way output of the model to provide a fine-grained, information-theoretic quantification of similarity across any subset of the languages being analyzed. This offers a much richer perspective than a single phylogenetic tree.
3.  A pragmatic and clever mechanism for user-guided exploration via data over-weighting (`alpha`). This allows a user to trade off statistical objectivity for linguistic detail, making the method a potentially powerful tool for exploratory linguistic analysis.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and a Bold New Direction:** The paper's greatest strength is its conceptual novelty. By eschewing priors and distributional assumptions in favor of a first-principles MDL approach, the authors propose a fundamentally new way to think about correspondence discovery. The idea of "growing" rules from singletons via compressive merges is both intuitive and powerful. This is precisely the kind of bold, high-risk/high-reward research that can open up new avenues, moving the field beyond incremental improvements on existing paradigms.
2.  **True Multi-Way Language Analysis:** The ability to move beyond pairwise comparisons is a crucial contribution. Most existing methods would require an explosion of pairwise analyses to approximate what this method does in a single run. The four-way analysis and the sDL metric are the standout results of the paper, demonstrating a capacity for nuanced analysis (e.g., simultaneously quantifying the CS-PL bond, the RU-BG bond, and the overarching pan-Slavic structure) that is simply not possible with other approaches. This is a significant step forward for computational comparative linguistics.
3.  **Principled and Interpretable Framework:** The method is deterministic, which aids reproducibility, and produces directly interpretable output in the form of string-to-string rules. Furthermore, the introduction of the `alpha` parameter for data over-weighting is a very insightful addition. It acknowledges that strict statistical significance may not always align with linguistic utility and provides a controllable "knob" for experts to delve deeper into the data. This blend of mathematical principle (MDL) with practical flexibility is a major asset.
4.  **Compelling Proof-of-Concept:** The experiments on Slavic languages, while not exhaustive, are well-chosen and effectively demonstrate the potential of the approach. The generated phylogenetic tree aligns well with established linguistic groupings, and the specific rules and alignments shown in Tables 3 and 4 are linguistically plausible and illustrative of the model's capabilities.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Greedy Optimization:** The M-step of the algorithm relies on a deterministic, greedy merging strategy. This is computationally efficient but is susceptible to finding local optima. For example, an early, suboptimal merge of two shorter rules could prevent the later discovery of a more powerful, longer correspondence that contains them. While the authors briefly acknowledge this, the paper would be stronger with some analysis of this limitation, perhaps by showing results from different random data subsets or discussing alternative, more robust search strategies (even if they are left for future work).
2.  **Absence of Extrinsic Evaluation:** The evaluation is purely intrinsic and qualitative (i.e., does the phylogeny look correct and are the rules sensible?). While the results are encouraging, the practical value of the discovered rule sets is not validated. An extrinsic evaluation on a downstream task, such as cognate reconstruction or improving statistical machine translation between related languages, would provide much stronger evidence for the method's utility. Without this, the work remains a compelling but unproven concept.
3.  **Scalability Limitations:** A key selling point is multi-language analysis, yet the authors state the implementation is bottlenecked at around five languages. This is a significant practical limitation. Given the combinatorial complexity, this is understandable, but the paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the specific computational bottlenecks and potential optimization strategies (e.g., pruning, beam search for alignment) that could mitigate them in future work. As it stands, the ambition of the method is somewhat ahead of its current technical reach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The greedy M-step is a potential weakness. Have you considered incorporating a "split" operation in addition to the "merge" operation to allow the model to backtrack from suboptimal merges? Or have you experimented with a stochastic search process, like the simulated annealing you briefly mention in the conclusion, to better explore the search space?
2.  The `alpha` parameter is a fascinating idea for balancing statistical purity with exploratory goals. Your current method for setting it seems to be "increase it until no singletons remain." Could you envision a more principled way for a user to select `alpha`? For example, is there a way to plot the total description length (or its components) as a function of `alpha` to identify an "elbow point" that suggests a natural trade-off?
3.  Could you clarify the partitioning of description length in the sDL calculation? Your definition suggests that a rule like `(a,b,c,d)` would contribute *only* to `sDL(CS,PL,RU,BG)`. This seems to miss the fact that this rule also represents shared structure between, for instance, the CS-PL pair. Is there an alternative formulation where the cost of a k-way rule could be seen as contributing to the shared information of all `(k choose j)` subsets?
