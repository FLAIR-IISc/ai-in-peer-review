**Summary & Contributions**

The paper proposes a method for discovering string-level correspondences across multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to find a set of correspondence rules that provides the most compact description of a given list of cognate tuples. The authors formulate this as an optimization problem and present a deterministic, EM-like algorithm that iteratively aligns the data and refines the rule set by merging patterns. The method is applied to a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, producing pairwise language distances, a phylogenetic tree, and a multi-way analysis of shared linguistic structures for a subset of four languages.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1.  The formulation of multi-language correspondence discovery as an MDL optimization problem. This provides a principled, non-parametric framework for identifying significant patterns without strong prior assumptions on their form or distribution.
2.  An algorithm capable of simultaneously analyzing more than two languages, allowing for a novel quantification of n-way similarities between language subgroups (the "shared Description Length" or sDL analysis).

**Strengths**

1.  **Principled Theoretical Framework:** The use of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle is a methodologically sound choice for the stated goal of discovering correspondences without imposing arbitrary assumptions. It provides a formal, information-theoretic basis for balancing model complexity (the cost of the rule set) with data fidelity (the cost of encoding the data with the rules), thus naturally guarding against overfitting.
2.  **Generalization to Multiple Languages:** A significant strength of the proposed approach is its ability to operate on more than two languages simultaneously. Most traditional alignment methods are pairwise, and extending them to a multi-language setting is often non-trivial. The paper's demonstration of a four-way analysis and the resulting shared Description Length (sDL) visualization is a compelling showcase of this capability, offering a more nuanced view of linguistic relationships than a simple phylogenetic tree can provide.
3.  **Interpretability and Determinism:** The output of the model is a set of explicit correspondence rules (e.g., (szcz, št)), which are directly interpretable by linguists. Furthermore, the core algorithm is deterministic, which is a desirable property for reproducibility and systematic analysis, contrasting with stochastic methods where results can vary between runs.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Complete Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The most significant weakness of this work is the absence of any empirical comparison to existing methods. The field of computational historical linguistics has a rich history of algorithms for cognate identification and phonetic alignment, including methods based on sequence alignment (e.g., Needleman-Wunsch with learned substitution scores), weighted finite-state transducers (WFSTs), and various probabilistic models. The paper's experiments are conducted in a vacuum; the results (e.g., the phylogenetic tree) confirm well-known linguistic facts, which is a necessary sanity check but a very low bar for evaluation. Without quantitative comparisons against established baselines on a shared task, it is impossible to assess whether this novel MDL-based approach offers any real advantage in terms of accuracy, efficiency, or the quality of the discovered correspondences.
2.  **Methodological Contradiction of "Data Over-weighting":** The introduction of the data over-weighting parameter `α` in Section 4.5 fundamentally undermines the paper's central claim of providing an "objective, unbiased" analysis rooted in the MDL principle. The purpose of MDL is precisely to find the model that is best supported by the *given* data, not some hypothetical, larger dataset. Introducing an ad-hoc `α` parameter to "discover" rules that are otherwise not statistically significant is a form of principled overfitting. It transforms the method from a formal information-theoretic approach into a user-tuned heuristic, re-introducing the subjective judgment the MDL framework was meant to eliminate. This suggests that the pure MDL model may not be sufficient for discovering linguistically useful rules on its own.
3.  **Concerns Regarding Scalability:** The paper reports that the implementation can process "up to five languages within a few hours on a 4GB RAM... single core desktop machine." This suggests severe scalability limitations. The alignment procedure is described as a shortest-path problem on a graph where nodes are index tuples, which hints at a state space that grows exponentially with the number of languages. The complexity analysis is not rigorous enough to assuage these concerns. For a method whose primary advantage is handling multiple languages, a more thorough discussion of its computational complexity and practical limits is essential.
4.  **Limited Novelty of the Optimization Algorithm:** The training procedure is a greedy, iterative heuristic. The "Maximization Step" consists of merging the two patterns that yield the greatest reduction in description length. This kind of greedy merging search is a standard technique. While its application within this specific MDL formulation is new, the algorithmic contribution itself appears incremental. The paper acknowledges that this can lead to sub-optimal solutions but does not adequately address the severity of this problem.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you justify the decision not to compare your method against any of the well-established baselines for cognate alignment or phylogenetic reconstruction from lexical data? For example, how would you expect your pairwise NCD results to compare with distances derived from more conventional alignment algorithms?
2.  The "data over-weighting" mechanism (Section 4.5) appears to contradict the objective principles of MDL. If the goal is to find linguistically plausible but statistically borderline rules, would a more principled Bayesian approach with an appropriate prior not be a more suitable framework than modifying the MDL objective function in this ad-hoc manner?
3.  Could you provide a more formal analysis of the computational complexity of the alignment procedure, particularly with respect to the number of languages N? What are the specific bottlenecks that currently limit the method to N=5, and are there clear paths to improving scalability?
