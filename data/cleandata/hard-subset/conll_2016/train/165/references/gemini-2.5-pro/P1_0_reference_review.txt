**Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel, unsupervised method for discovering string-level correspondences across multiple languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to find a set of correspondence rules (tuples of strings) that provides the most compact description of a given corpus of cognates. The method employs an iterative, EM-like algorithm to deterministically search for the optimal rule set, starting from an alignment of single characters. The authors evaluate their approach on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. Their main contributions, as I see them, are:

1.  **A Principled Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is a method that can simultaneously infer correspondences among more than two languages, a significant step beyond common pairwise approaches. The associated "shared Description Length" (sDL) metric offers a novel and powerful way to quantify the complex n-way similarities between language subgroups.
2.  **An Effective Application of MDL to a Linguistic Problem:** The paper successfully formulates the problem of correspondence discovery in the well-founded information-theoretic framework of MDL. This allows for a data-driven approach that avoids strong prior assumptions about the nature of the correspondences, leading to more objective results.
3.  **A Detailed Orthographic Analysis of Slavic Languages:** The experiments provide a valuable data-driven analysis of the Slavic language family. The method successfully reconstructs a linguistically plausible phylogeny and, through the multi-language analysis, offers new quantitative insights into the relationships between Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian.

**Strengths**

1.  **Generalization to Multiple Languages:** The ability to move beyond pairwise comparisons is a major strength and the most exciting aspect of this work. The four-way analysis presented in Section 5.3, along with the sDL visualization, is compelling. It provides a much richer and more nuanced view of language relationships than a simple phylogenetic tree, quantifying the amount of shared structure between all 15 subsets of the four languages. This is a valuable new analytical tool for computational linguistics.
2.  **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The use of the MDL principle is well-motivated and elegant. It provides a formal definition of model optimality (i.e., maximal compression) that naturally handles the trade-off between model complexity and data fit, thus preventing overfitting. This principled approach avoids the need for arbitrary parameter tuning or distributional assumptions that plague many other statistical models.
3.  **Deterministic and Interpretable Method:** The proposed algorithm is deterministic, ensuring reproducibility, which is a significant advantage for scientific analysis. The output—a set of correspondence rules—is directly interpretable. The examples provided (e.g., (g,h), (cz,č), (ла,оло)) clearly link the model's statistical findings to known phonological, orthographic, and historical linguistic phenomena. The proposed "data over-weighting" mechanism, while a departure from pure MDL, is a pragmatic and intuitive feature for linguists wanting to explore correspondences at different levels of granularity.
4.  **Convincing Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while limited to one language family, are well-executed. The pairwise analysis, which uses the derived Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) to build a phylogenetic tree, successfully recovers the known West, East, and South Slavic groupings. This serves as a strong sanity check and validates the reasonableness of the model's learned similarities.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Significant Scalability Concerns:** The paper admits to "bottlenecks in memory consumption as well as in runtime" but understates the severity of this issue. The complexity of the alignment step, which involves finding a shortest path in a graph whose size grows combinatorially with the number of languages `N`, is a major limitation. The claim of a multi-language approach is weakened if it is only practical for `N` up to 4 or 5. The paper would be much stronger with a formal analysis of the complexity with respect to `N` and a discussion of potential optimizations or approximations required to scale to larger groups of languages.
2.  **Lack of Comparative Evaluation:** The method is presented in a vacuum, with no comparison to existing work. While the multi-language focus is novel, the pairwise analysis could have been compared against other cognate analysis or string similarity methods (including some cited, like Snyder and Barzilay, 2008). How does the NCD-based phylogeny compare to trees built using standard methods like edit distance? How does the quality of the discovered rules compare to those found by other systems on a shared task? Without any baseline comparisons, it is hard to assess the practical advantages of this method over alternatives.
3.  **Opaque Description of the MDL Code:** Certain parts of the model description in Section 4 are difficult to follow. The formulation for `L(Π)` (the rule table cost) is particularly dense, and the motivation for using a "data-to-model code" (the binomial coefficient term) for rule usages and alphabet counts is not clearly explained to a non-expert. A simple, concrete example walking through the cost calculation for a toy dataset with two rules would greatly improve the clarity and reproducibility of the work.
4.  **Evaluation is Primarily Qualitative:** The evaluation relies on the visual plausibility of the phylogenetic tree and a handful of curated examples of learned rules. While these are insightful, a more quantitative and systematic evaluation would strengthen the paper's claims. For instance, the learned rules could be evaluated against a gold-standard set of sound correspondences from historical linguistics, or applied to a downstream task such as cognate identification or machine transliteration to provide an extrinsic measure of their quality.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the scalability of the alignment procedure (Section 4.3)? Specifically, how does the number of nodes and edges in the alignment graph grow as a function of the number of languages `N`? Are there heuristics or pruning strategies that could make the method practical for, say, `N=10` languages?
2.  The "data over-weighting" technique is a pragmatic solution for finding less-attested rules. Have you considered alternative, more principled modifications within the MDL framework to achieve a similar goal, for example, by altering the prior on rule length or complexity within the `L(M)` term itself?
3.  In the sDL analysis (Figure 3), the sDL for the CS-PL subgroup appears substantially larger than that for the RU-BG subgroup. However, you state that "grouping RU-BG seems as good a choice as grouping CS-PL". Could you please clarify the basis for this conclusion, as it seems to contradict the direct visual evidence in the chart?
