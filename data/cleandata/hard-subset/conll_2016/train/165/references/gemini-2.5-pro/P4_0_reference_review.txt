### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a method for automatically discovering string-level correspondences between multiple languages from cognate data. The approach is framed under the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which posits that the best model (in this case, a set of correspondence rules) is the one that provides the most succinct description of the data. The method is designed to be deterministic and does not make prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of the correspondence rules.

The core of the method is an iterative, EM-like algorithm. The E-step aligns the cognate data using the current set of rules by finding the shortest path in a weighted graph. The M-step improves the model by greedily merging existing rules to create more complex, statistically significant correspondences that lead to a greater reduction in the total description length. The framework is notable for its ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously. The authors also propose a "data over-weighting" technique to allow users to explore correspondences at varying levels of statistical significance. The method is evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, first through pairwise comparisons to construct a phylogenetic tree, and second through a detailed four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel formulation of multi-language correspondence discovery as a Minimum Description Length problem. This provides a principled, objective framework for simultaneously inferring correspondence rules among more than two languages, moving beyond the limitations of traditional pairwise approaches.
2.  A practical, deterministic algorithm for optimizing the MDL objective. The algorithm iteratively builds complex rules from simpler ones based on their statistical significance in the data, providing an intuitive search procedure for a complex, non-convex problem.
3.  An information-theoretic method for fine-grained analysis of multi-language similarity. The "shared Description Length" (sDL) metric, derived directly from the MDL model, allows for quantifying the degree of regularity shared among any subset of the languages being analyzed, offering a more detailed view than a single phylogenetic tree.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Soundness of the Multi-Language Framework:** The paper's primary strength lies in its novel application of MDL to discover correspondences among an arbitrary number of languages simultaneously. This is a significant conceptual advance over most existing methods, which are restricted to pairwise comparisons. The MDL formulation is technically sound and provides a well-founded objective function for balancing model complexity against data fit, thus avoiding arbitrary assumptions about the nature of correspondences. The resulting ability to compute "shared Description Length" (sDL) for any subset of languages is a powerful and direct benefit of this multi-way approach, enabling a more nuanced analysis of linguistic relationships.

2.  **Clarity of Methodological Exposition:** The paper provides a clear and detailed description of the proposed method. The decomposition of the MDL objective into the model code L(M) and data code L(D|M) is laid out systematically. The EM-like training procedure, involving an alignment step (E-step) and a rule-merging step (M-step), is well-motivated and logically explained. This clarity aids in understanding the mechanics of the algorithm and enhances the reproducibility of the work.

3.  **Compelling Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the utility of the proposed method. The pairwise analysis of 13 Slavic languages serves as a strong validation, producing a phylogenetic tree (Figure 1) that aligns well with established linguistic classifications. This confirms that the underlying similarity metric (NCD) is meaningful. The four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian is particularly insightful, as it showcases the unique strengths of the multi-language approach. The visualization of shared description lengths (Figure 3) provides a quantitative and interpretable breakdown of the complex inter-relationships among the four languages, a result that would be difficult to achieve with pairwise methods alone. The provided examples of learned rules are concrete and illustrative.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Scalability Concerns:** The paper reports that the implementation can process "up to five languages...within a few hours on a...single core desktop machine." This suggests that the method's computational complexity is a major bottleneck. The time complexity is given in terms of internal variables (e.g., |R|, the number of rule applications), but it is not analyzed with respect to the number of languages (N), which is the key parameter for scalability. The combinatorial nature of aligning strings across multiple languages implies that the complexity likely grows exponentially with N, severely limiting the method's applicability to larger-scale comparative studies. This lack of scalability analysis is a significant weakness.

2.  **Lack of Comparative Evaluation:** The paper does not provide a quantitative comparison to any existing methods for cognate analysis or phylogenetic reconstruction. While the authors argue their method is novel in its multi-language capacity, a comparison on the pairwise task would be essential for context. For instance, how do the learned NCD values and the resulting phylogenetic tree compare to those generated by established methods in computational historical linguistics? Without such a baseline, it is difficult to objectively assess the quality of the model's output relative to the state of the art.

3.  **Ambiguity in the MDL Formulation:** Certain details of the MDL code in Section 4.1 are not sufficiently explained, hindering reproducibility. The use of a "data-to-model code" involving a binomial coefficient to encode rule usage counts (`L(Π)`) and alphabet symbol counts (`L(Σi)`) is presented without a clear justification or reference to the specific coding scheme being employed (e.g., Shtarkov's universal code for multinomial distributions). A more thorough explanation of why this specific mathematical form is the correct MDL representation would be necessary for a reader to fully understand and re-implement the model code.

4.  **Limitations of the Deterministic Greedy Search:** The M-step of the algorithm employs a greedy strategy, always merging the pair of rules that yields the greatest immediate improvement in description length. While this makes the algorithm deterministic, it is highly susceptible to local optima. The authors briefly acknowledge this, stating they "may miss some of the more subtle correspondence rules," but do not investigate the extent of this problem. The robustness of the results could be sensitive to this greedy heuristic, and the suggestion of using simulated annealing in the conclusion implies that the deterministic nature, presented as a strength, may also be a practical limitation.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's time and space complexity as a function of the number of languages, N? What are the primary factors contributing to the scalability bottleneck for N > 5, and how effective are the mentioned partial order reductions as N increases?

2.  The data over-weighting parameter α is introduced as a pragmatic tool for linguists. However, it deviates from the objective MDL framework. Is there a principled way to select a value for α? How sensitive are the discovered rules to the choice of α, and what safeguards exist to prevent the model from learning spurious patterns when data is heavily over-weighted?

3.  Could you please clarify the specific "data-to-model" coding schemes used for the rule usage counts and alphabet unigrams? Providing a reference or a brief derivation for the combinatorial terms in `L(Π)` and `L(Σi)` would greatly improve the clarity and reproducibility of your model description.
