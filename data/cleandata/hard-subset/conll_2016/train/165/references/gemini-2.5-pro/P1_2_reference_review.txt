### Summary of the Paper

This paper presents a method for automatically discovering correspondence rules between multiple languages from cognate lists. The approach is framed under the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which posits that the best model (here, a set of correspondence rules) is the one that provides the most compact description of the data. The authors propose a deterministic, EM-like algorithm that starts with a "null alignment" (single characters) and iteratively merges rules to improve the overall compression of the data. The method is not restricted to language pairs and can analyze multiple languages simultaneously. The authors evaluate their approach on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. They conduct a pairwise analysis, generating a linguistically plausible phylogenetic tree, and a four-way analysis (CS-PL-RU-BG) where they introduce a novel metric, "shared Description Length" (sDL), to quantify the similarity between all possible subsets of the analyzed languages.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Principled, Multi-Language Framework for Correspondence Discovery:** The main contribution is the formulation of multi-language correspondence discovery as a two-part MDL problem. This provides a parameter-free, objective function for model selection that avoids strong prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of correspondence rules, a limitation the authors identify in prior work. The associated deterministic, EM-like optimization algorithm is a key part of this contribution.
2.  **A Novel Method for Quantifying Language Subgroup Similarity:** The paper introduces the "shared Description Length" (sDL) analysis. This is a significant contribution as it moves beyond simple pairwise distance metrics and phylogenetic trees. The sDL provides a fine-grained, information-theoretic quantification of the structural similarities shared by any subset of languages within a larger group, offering a more nuanced view of linguistic relationships.

### Strengths

1.  **Elegant and Well-Founded Theoretical Framework:** The use of the MDL principle is a major strength. It provides a solid statistical foundation for the problem of model selection without resorting to priors or assumptions about the data distribution. The idea of finding the set of rules that most succinctly describes the cognates is both intuitive and powerful. This principled approach directly addresses the paper's stated goal of discovering "true" correspondences without bias.
2.  **Novel and Insightful Multi-Way Analysis:** The four-way analysis and the introduction of sDL are highlights of the paper. While pairwise phylogenetic reconstruction is a standard evaluation, the sDL analysis (Figure 3) demonstrates the unique capability of the proposed method. It allows for a direct quantification of shared structures between, for example, Czech-Polish versus Russian-Bulgarian, and even three-way groupings, all within a single consistent model. This is a novel and potentially very useful tool for computational historical linguistics.
3.  **Deterministic and Interpretable Algorithm:** The proposed training procedure is deterministic, starting from a simple initial state and iteratively building complexity. This is a valuable property, as it ensures reproducibility and makes the learning process more transparent compared to stochastic methods. The fact that rules are grown by merging smaller, statistically significant patterns provides some insight into their relative importance.
4.  **Solid Empirical Validation on Slavic Languages:** The experiments on the Slavic language family are well-executed. The pairwise NCD analysis produces a phylogenetic tree (Figure 1) that aligns well with established linguistic classifications (West, East, South Slavic branches). This serves as a strong sanity check and grounds the credibility of the model before the more novel multi-way analysis is presented.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Scalability Concerns:** The paper states that the current implementation can only handle up to five languages due to memory and runtime bottlenecks. This is a major limitation for a method whose primary novelty is its applicability to multiple languages. The paper should provide more detail on the source of this combinatorial explosion. Is it in the construction of the alignment graph? The number of potential merge candidates in the M-step? This weakness currently confines the method's utility to small-scale studies and undermines its potential for analyzing larger language families or sub-families.
2.  **Ad-hoc "Data Over-Weighting" Technique:** The introduction of the `α` parameter in Section 4.5 to over-weight the data term feels like a contradiction to the paper's core philosophy. The main appeal of the MDL framework is its objectivity and avoidance of arbitrary parameters. This technique re-introduces a subjective, user-defined parameter that lacks clear guidance on its selection. While potentially useful for exploratory analysis, it weakens the claim that the method is finding the objectively "best" model and seems like a workaround for the model's potential failure to capture linguistically relevant but less frequent patterns.
3.  **Lack of Comparison to Alternative Methods:** The evaluation is entirely internal; the results are compared to linguistic consensus but not to the outputs of other computational methods. It would strengthen the paper to include a comparison to other unsupervised correspondence-finding or cognate-alignment algorithms (e.g., those based on graphical models or other alignment techniques). For instance, how does the quality of the learned NCD-based phylogeny compare to a tree built using a standard sequence-based distance metric like normalized edit distance? How do the learned multi-character correspondences compare to those from other systems?
4.  **Clarity of the MDL Formulation:** While the overall idea is clear, the specific mathematical formulation of the code length in Section 4.1, particularly `L(Π)`, is dense and difficult to parse. The purpose of the data-to-model code term involving the large combinatorial factor is not intuitively explained. A small, concrete, step-by-step example of how `L(M)` and `L(D|M)` are calculated for a toy dataset (e.g., two cognates and three rules) would greatly improve the clarity and reproducibility of the method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the precise source of the computational bottleneck that limits your method to N=5 languages? Is this a fundamental limitation of the shortest-path-on-a-graph formulation for multi-sequence alignment, or is it an aspect of your current implementation that could be substantially optimized (e.g., through more aggressive pruning or alternative search algorithms)?
2.  Regarding the data over-weighting parameter `α`: How is a user meant to choose this value in a principled way? Does its use not undermine the paper's central claim of providing an objective, assumption-free method for discovering statistically significant rules? Could the goal of finding more detailed rules be achieved in a more integrated way, for example by adjusting the model code `L(M)` to have a different complexity penalty?
3.  The sDL analysis is very compelling. However, how sensitive are the relative proportions of shared description length (as shown in Figure 3) to the specific cognate list used? Have you experimented with different datasets to see if these information-theoretic measures of subgrouping are stable?
