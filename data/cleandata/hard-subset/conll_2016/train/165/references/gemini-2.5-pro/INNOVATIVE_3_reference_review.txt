### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel, unsupervised method based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to discover systematic string correspondences from cognate lists across multiple languages. The core idea is that the best set of correspondence rules is the one that allows for the most compact description of the data. The authors propose a deterministic, EM-like algorithm to infer these rules and demonstrate its utility on a dataset of Slavic languages, performing both pairwise and multi-language analyses.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel MDL Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is the formulation of correspondence discovery as a data compression problem. This is a bold and elegant approach that moves away from methods reliant on strong parametric assumptions. Critically, the framework is inherently designed to handle more than two languages simultaneously, a significant step beyond the common pairwise paradigm.
2.  **A New Information-Theoretic Metric for Language Similarity:** The paper introduces "Shared Description Length" (sDL), a novel method for quantifying the amount of shared structure between any subset of the languages being analyzed. This provides a much more granular and insightful view of linguistic relationships than a single phylogenetic tree, as demonstrated in the four-way language analysis.
3.  **An Interpretable, Exploration-Oriented Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm produces an explicit and linguistically interpretable set of correspondence rules. Furthermore, the inclusion of a "data over-weighting" parameter is a clever, pragmatic feature that allows users to guide the discovery process towards rules that may be linguistically interesting, even if they fall just below the threshold of statistical significance in a purely objective model.

### Strengths

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The paper's greatest strength is its fresh perspective. Applying MDL to discover a "grammar" of multi-language correspondences without prior assumptions about rule form or distribution is a fantastic idea. It is a principled, first-principles approach to a long-standing problem in historical and computational linguistics. This work represents a significant conceptual advance, not an incremental improvement on existing methods.
2.  **True Multi-Language Analysis:** The ability to move beyond pairwise comparisons is a major contribution. Language evolution is not a series of pairwise events, and this model's ability to identify rules that hold across three, four, or N languages—as well as rules that hold only for specific subgroups (e.g., CS-PL vs. RU-BG within the 4-language set)—is a powerful feature. This is the kind of bold step the field needs.
3.  **Powerful and Insightful Analytical Capability:** The proposed sDL metric is a highlight of the paper. The visualization in Figure 3, which breaks down the total model complexity into components shared by every possible subset of the four languages, is extremely compelling. It offers a rich, quantitative story about language relationships that goes far beyond what a simple distance matrix or phylogenetic tree can show. It reveals not just *that* CS and PL are related, but quantifies the complexity of the structure they share exclusively.
4.  **Pragmatism and Interpretability:** The method is not just a theoretical exercise. The outputs (the rules themselves) are directly interpretable, as shown in Tables 3 and 4. I particularly appreciate the introduction of the data over-weighting mechanism. While it deviates from pure MDL objectivity, it shows a keen understanding of the needs of the target audience (linguists), who may be interested in exploring hypotheses rather than just finding the single, statistically optimal model. It smartly turns a potential methodological compromise into a useful feature for exploratory analysis.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few areas that could be strengthened. My comments are offered in the spirit of improving what is already a very strong submission.

1.  **Greedy Search and Local Optima:** The M-step of the training algorithm employs a greedy strategy of merging the two patterns that yield the greatest immediate improvement in description length. This is a sensible heuristic for a complex search space, but it is vulnerable to finding locally optimal solutions and may miss more complex patterns that require several "sub-optimal" merges to become apparent. A brief discussion of the potential impact of this and perhaps a small experiment comparing it to a less greedy alternative (e.g., beam search) would make the methodological claims more robust.
2.  **Unclear Scalability Limits:** The paper commendably reports the practical limits of the current implementation (up to five languages). However, the bottleneck is not fully analyzed. Is the primary constraint memory from the alignment graph, or runtime? Understanding the source of the complexity would be valuable for assessing the feasibility of applying this method to larger language families. While the current experiments are sufficient as a proof-of-concept, this is a key question for the method's future impact.
3.  **Evaluation Could Be Broadened:** The evaluation is effective in demonstrating that the method produces results consistent with known linguistics (the Slavic phylogeny) and discovers sensible rules. This is a perfectly valid and necessary form of evaluation for a novel method. However, to fully establish its utility, an extrinsic evaluation on a downstream task would be beneficial. For instance, how well do the learned rules perform in a cognate reconstruction or transliteration task compared to rules derived from other unsupervised or supervised methods? This is not a reason to reject the paper, as the conceptual contribution is strong enough on its own, but it would provide a more objective measure of the quality of the discovered models.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The Shared Description Length (sDL) is a very promising metric. How is it normalized? The raw bit values in Figure 3 are hard to compare without knowing the total description length of the model (1852 bits for CS-PL is mentioned, but not for the 4-way model). More importantly, how does the composition of the input cognate list affect the sDL distribution? For instance, would a list with many internationalisms heavily inflate the four-way sDL, and how should a user account for that in their interpretation?
2.  The data over-weighting parameter `α` is an interesting tool for exploration. Have you considered any methods for guiding its selection? For example, could one plot the number of learned rules or the total description length as a function of `α` to identify a point of diminishing returns or an "elbow" that suggests a natural trade-off?
3.  Regarding the greedy search: Your intuition that sub-patterns of a good pattern will also be useful is sound. However, have you observed cases where this fails, for instance, where a complex correspondence like a metathesis rule is composed of individually weak sub-patterns that are never selected for merging?
