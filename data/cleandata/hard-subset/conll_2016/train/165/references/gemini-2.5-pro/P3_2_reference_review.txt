### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel, non-parametric method for discovering systematic string correspondences among multiple languages from cognate lists. The approach is founded on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which posits that the best model (a set of correspondence rules) is the one that most concisely describes the data. The authors propose an EM-like deterministic algorithm that starts with single-character rules and iteratively merges them to build up a set of more complex correspondence rules, guided by the objective of minimizing the total description length. The method is evaluated on a set of 13 Slavic languages. A pairwise analysis using a derived Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) successfully reconstructs the known linguistic phylogeny. A more detailed four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian introduces a new metric, Shared Description Length (sDL), to quantify the similarities across all subsets of these languages.

In my view, the primary contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Principled Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The paper's main contribution is the formalization of multi-language correspondence discovery as an MDL-based optimization problem. This approach is powerful as it is non-parametric (making no prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of rules) and naturally extends beyond pairs to an arbitrary number of languages, a key limitation of much previous work.
2.  **A Novel Quantitative Metric for Multi-Faceted Language Similarity:** The introduction of Shared Description Length (sDL) is a significant conceptual contribution. It moves beyond a single distance metric by providing a detailed, information-theoretic breakdown of the shared complexity between every subset of the languages being analyzed. This allows for a much more nuanced and fine-grained analysis of linguistic relationships than traditional phylogenetic trees.
3.  **A Practical Algorithm with User-Controllable Granularity:** The paper presents an effective greedy algorithm to find good sets of correspondence rules. Furthermore, the proposed data over-weighting mechanism is an elegant and practical way for users (e.g., linguists) to guide the model towards discovering rarer or more complex patterns that may be of linguistic interest but lack sufficient statistical evidence in the raw data.

### Strengths

1.  **Methodological Elegance and Soundness:** The application of the MDL principle is a perfect fit for the problem of discovering structure without prior assumptions. It provides a theoretically sound, objective function that naturally balances model complexity with data fidelity. The formulation of the model code and data code is clear and well-justified. This stands in contrast to many ad-hoc statistical methods and provides a strong foundation for the work.
2.  **Superior Analytical Power:** The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously and quantify the results with sDL is a major advance. The four-way analysis in Section 5.3 is the most compelling part of the paper, demonstrating how the method can uncover subgroupings (CS-PL, RU-BG) and identify language-specific complexities (the high individual sDL for Czech) within a single, unified analysis. This is a powerful tool for computational historical linguistics that goes far beyond what pairwise comparisons can offer.
3.  **Interpretability and Practical Utility:** The output of the model—a set of explicit string-to-string correspondence rules like `(szcz,št)`—is directly interpretable by linguists. The examples provided in Tables 3 and 4 clearly show that the learned rules capture a range of phenomena from orthographic conventions to phonological changes. The data over-weighting feature further enhances the tool's utility, allowing an expert to explore the data at different levels of detail.
4.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments, while confined to Slavic languages, are well-executed. The pairwise NCD analysis resulting in a linguistically plausible phylogenetic tree (Figure 1) serves as an excellent sanity check, validating the core mechanics of the model. This builds confidence for the more novel four-way analysis, whose results are also linguistically plausible and insightful.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Scalability Concerns:** The paper states the method works for "up to five languages" on a standard desktop machine and notes bottlenecks in both memory and runtime. This is a major practical limitation for a method whose primary novelty is its applicability to multiple languages. The complexity analysis (`O(|D| · |R|^2)` for the E-step) is concerning, as the number of possible rule applications `R` in the alignment graph seems likely to grow exponentially with the number of languages. The paper would be much stronger with a more detailed discussion of these scalability limits and potential optimizations (e.g., pruning, beam search) that could extend its reach.
2.  **Lack of Comparative Evaluation:** The related work section is brief, and the paper does not quantitatively compare its output to any other existing method. While other methods may be limited to pairs, it would be highly informative to compare the quality of the pairwise correspondences found by this MDL approach against those from a well-established baseline (e.g., based on statistical alignment models) on the same data. Without such a comparison, it is difficult to assess whether the learned rules are not only discovered in a more principled way but are also of higher quality.
3.  **Potential Sub-Optimality of Greedy Search:** The M-step employs a greedy strategy of merging the best pair of rules at each iteration. While deterministic and efficient, this approach is susceptible to local optima and may miss important correspondences. The paper briefly acknowledges this ("we may miss some of the more subtle correspondence rules"), but the potential impact is understated. A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to this greedy approach, or an exploration of alternative search mechanisms, would be beneficial to assess the robustness of the findings.
4.  **Absence of Extrinsic Evaluation:** The evaluation is entirely intrinsic, based on reproducing phylogenies and the plausibility of learned rules. While valuable, an extrinsic evaluation would provide stronger evidence of the method's utility. For example, could the learned correspondence rules be used to improve a downstream NLP task, such as cognate detection, transliteration, or statistical machine translation between related languages? Demonstrating utility on an external task would make a more compelling case for the significance of the work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding scalability: Could you provide more detail on how the complexity of the alignment graph (and thus the E-step) scales with the number of languages `N`? Is the primary limitation CPU time for the shortest-path search, or memory consumption for storing the graph? Are there straightforward pruning heuristics that could be applied without fundamentally changing the MDL objective?
2.  The greedy M-step is a potential weakness. Have you investigated how much the final set of rules and the sDL measurements vary if a different greedy choice is made, or if a small amount of non-determinism (e.g., considering the top-k merges) is introduced? How confident are you that the reported sDLs are stable and not an artifact of the specific search path taken?
3.  The finding in the four-way analysis that the RU-BG sDL is nearly as large as the CS-PL sDL is interesting, as it groups an East and a South Slavic language closely together. While your data is orthographic, is this primarily due to the shared Cyrillic script and modern loanwords, or does it reflect deeper structural similarities that are captured by your model? How much do you think the choice of cognate set (including internationalisms) influences this specific result?
