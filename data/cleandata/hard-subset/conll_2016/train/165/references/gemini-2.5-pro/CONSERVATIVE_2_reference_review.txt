### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces an unsupervised method for discovering orthographic correspondence rules from parallel word lists (cognates) across multiple languages. The core of the approach is the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which posits that the best model (i.e., set of correspondence rules) is the one that allows for the most compact description of the data. The authors propose an EM-like iterative algorithm that starts with singleton character rules and greedily merges them to form more complex correspondence patterns if doing so reduces the total description length. The primary contributions of this work, as I assess them, are:

1.  **A novel formulation of multi-language correspondence discovery as an MDL optimization problem.** This provides a non-probabilistic, parameter-free framework for simultaneously analyzing string-level similarities in more than two languages, a notable departure from common pairwise methods.
2.  **The introduction of "shared Description Length" (sDL) as an analytical tool.** Derived from the components of the MDL code, this metric offers a way to quantify the degree of shared orthographic structure among any subset of the languages being analyzed, providing a more granular view of language similarity than a single phylogenetic tree.
3.  **An experimental application to Slavic languages.** The authors demonstrate their method on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, producing a plausible phylogenetic tree from pairwise distances and a detailed multi-language analysis of a four-language subset.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled Methodological Foundation:** The use of the Minimum Description Length principle is a methodologically sound choice for this discovery task. It provides a formal, information-theoretic criterion for balancing model complexity (the cost of representing the correspondence rules) against data fidelity (the cost of encoding the word lists using those rules). This approach elegantly sidesteps the need for explicit prior distributions over rule parameters, which are often difficult to justify.
2.  **Generalization to Multiple Languages:** The ability to model correspondences among more than two languages simultaneously is a significant strength. Traditional pairwise approaches can become computationally infeasible or methodologically complex when extended to multiple languages. This method naturally handles the N-way case, enabling analyses like the shared Description Length which can reveal complex subgrouping relationships that might be obscured in a series of pairwise comparisons.
3.  **Interpretability of Results:** The output of the model is a set of explicit string-to-string correspondence rules, which are directly interpretable by linguists. The examples provided, such as (g,h) and (dzie,dě), demonstrate that the model can capture both simple and complex orthographic/phonological patterns. This transparency is valuable for qualitative linguistic analysis.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Complete Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The most significant flaw of this paper is its failure to compare the proposed method against any existing work. The introduction criticizes prior statistical approaches but provides no empirical evidence that this MDL-based method is superior, or even comparable. There is a substantial body of work on cognate alignment and correspondence discovery using methods such as weighted finite-state transducers, Bayesian models, and neural networks. Without a quantitative comparison on a standardized task (e.g., cognate identification, reconstruction accuracy), the claims of the paper remain unsubstantiated. The evaluation is entirely intrinsic and descriptive, making it impossible to gauge the method's actual performance in the context of the field.
2.  **Heuristic and Potentially Sub-optimal Search Procedure:** The core of the learning algorithm is a deterministic, greedy search procedure where pairs of rules are merged at each step. While computationally efficient, this greedy approach is highly susceptible to finding poor local optima in what is acknowledged to be a non-convex search space. The quality of the final rule set is entirely dependent on the initial greedy decisions. The authors themselves note that the method may miss "subtle correspondence rules," but the severity of this issue is not explored. A more rigorous approach would involve either theoretical justification for the greedy strategy or an empirical comparison with more robust search techniques (e.g., beam search).
3.  **Ad-hoc Modification Undermines Core Principles:** The introduction of the "data over-weighting" parameter `α` is a pragmatic but theoretically weak modification. A key advantage of the MDL framework is its principled, objective trade-off between model and data complexity. By introducing an arbitrary weighting factor, the authors re-introduce the kind of subjective user judgment and hyperparameter tuning that MDL is meant to avoid. This weakens the paper's central claim of providing an "objective" analysis and makes the method less self-contained.
4.  **Evaluation Lacks Quantitative Rigor:** The experimental validation relies on qualitative assessments. The generated phylogenetic tree is visually compared to known linguistic classifications, but no formal tree comparison metric (e.g., Robinson-Foulds distance to a gold-standard tree) is used. The analysis of shared Description Lengths is interesting but remains an internal metric of the proposed model. The utility of the discovered rules is not demonstrated on any extrinsic, task-based evaluation, which would be necessary to prove their practical value.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given the extensive literature on computational historical linguistics and correspondence discovery, could you justify the decision not to include any established methods as baselines? A comparison, even on a pairwise task, would be critical for positioning this work and substantiating its contributions.
2.  The greedy merging strategy is a significant simplification of a difficult optimization problem. Have you investigated its sensitivity to the order of merges or explored alternative search methods? How confident can we be that the deterministic path taken by the algorithm leads to a meaningful set of correspondences, rather than an arbitrary local optimum?
3.  The sDL metric is presented as a novel contribution for quantifying similarity. How does this measure differ from or improve upon simpler, established information-theoretic metrics, such as calculating the cross-entropy between language models (e.g., character n-gram models) trained on the respective cognate lists?
