### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a novel, deterministic method for discovering string-level correspondences between multiple languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The proposed approach models the problem as finding a set of correspondence rules that most succinctly describes a given corpus of cognate tuples. The core of the method is an EM-like algorithm that iteratively aligns data with a current rule set and then improves the rule set by merging rules that yield the greatest reduction in total description length. The authors evaluate their method on a set of 13 Slavic languages, performing both pairwise and four-way analyses.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel Application of MDL for Multi-Language Correspondence:** The primary contribution is the formulation of the multi-language correspondence discovery problem within the MDL framework. This provides a principled, non-probabilistic approach that inherently avoids strong distributional assumptions and can handle more than two languages simultaneously.
2.  **A New Metric for N-Way Language Similarity (sDL):** The paper introduces the concept of "Shared Description Length" (sDL) as a tool to quantify the amount of shared structure among any subset of the languages being analyzed. This moves beyond simple pairwise distance metrics and offers a more granular, information-theoretic analysis of linguistic relationships.

### Strengths

1.  **Principled Theoretical Framework:** The use of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle provides a theoretically grounded, non-probabilistic framework for model selection. This is an elegant way to approach the problem, as it naturally balances model complexity (the cost of encoding the correspondence rules) with data fit (the cost of encoding the data using those rules), thus guarding against overfitting without requiring arbitrary priors.
2.  **Generalizability to N > 2 Languages:** A key strength is the method's inherent ability to model correspondences among more than two languages simultaneously. Many existing computational approaches are designed for pairwise comparison, and extending them to a multi-language setting is often non-trivial. This work directly addresses the multi-way problem, which is a significant advantage and allows for more complex linguistic analyses, as demonstrated by the four-way experiment.
3.  **Deterministic and Interpretable Approach:** The proposed training procedure is deterministic, which is a desirable property for replicability and analysis. Starting from a "null alignment" and greedily merging the most statistically significant patterns provides a clear, interpretable process for how correspondences are learned. The resulting rules, such as `(szcz,št)` or `(ла,оло)`, are also directly interpretable by linguists.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The most significant weakness of this work is the complete absence of comparison to established computational methods for cognate identification or correspondence discovery. While the paper positions itself as a new paradigm, it operates in a well-studied area. There are numerous statistical and algorithmic approaches (e.g., those based on weighted finite-state transducers, Bayesian models, or other alignment algorithms) that could serve as baselines. The evaluation consists of generating a phylogenetic tree and comparing it qualitatively to known linguistic facts. This is a necessary sanity check, but it is not sufficient. Without quantitative comparisons to prior work on a shared task (e.g., cognate identification, reconstruction accuracy), it is impossible to assess whether this new MDL-based method offers any empirical advantage over existing, proven techniques.
2.  **The "Data Over-weighting" Mechanism is Ad-hoc and Undermines the MDL Principle:** The authors introduce a weighting parameter `α` to find "near-significant" correspondences. This fundamentally undermines the paper's central claim of providing an "objective" MDL-based analysis. MDL's strength is that it provides a formal criterion for what is statistically significant. The introduction of `α` transforms the objective function into a tunable one, moving the approach from a principled method to a heuristic one. The paper provides no guidance on how to set `α`, stating that it allows a user to "choose a desired level of detail." This subjectivity contradicts the very principles of MDL the paper relies on and makes the results in Table 3 (marked with `*`) difficult to interpret in a rigorous way.
3.  **Evaluation is Limited to Orthography:** The evaluation is conducted exclusively on orthographic data, which is a significant limitation. Linguistic correspondence, particularly for historical reconstruction, is fundamentally a phonological phenomenon. Using raw orthography, especially across different writing systems (Latin and Cyrillic), conflates orthographic conventions with phonological reality. The authors' own NCD results show a measurable difference between Latin and Cyrillic Serbo-Croatian, highlighting that the model is sensitive to surface-level script differences rather than just the underlying language. A more convincing evaluation would have used phonetically transcribed data (e.g., IPA), which is standard practice in computational historical linguistics.
4.  **Scalability Concerns and Practical Limitations:** The authors acknowledge that their current implementation faces computational bottlenecks for more than five languages. While the framework is theoretically general, this practical limitation curtails its utility for analyzing larger language families, which is often a goal in comparative linguistics. This weakness should be stated more prominently as a key limitation of the current work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Why were no comparisons made to existing computational methods for correspondence discovery or cognate analysis (e.g., methods by Snyder & Barzilay 2008, or other alignment-based systems)? A comparison on a task like cognate clustering or reconstruction would provide a much stronger argument for your method's utility.
2.  Could the authors elaborate on the selection of the data-weighting parameter `α`? How does one choose an appropriate value without simply tuning it to produce visually appealing alignments? Does its introduction not compromise the objectivity that is presented as a primary benefit of using MDL?
3.  The analysis relies solely on orthography. How would you expect the results (both the NCD-based phylogeny and the specific sDL distributions) to differ if phonetic transcriptions were used? Given that many discovered correspondences are phonological in nature (e.g., liquid metathesis), wouldn't a phonetic representation provide a cleaner signal and a more robust test of the method's linguistic validity?
