### Summary and Contributions

This paper presents a method for automatically discovering string-level correspondence rules between multiple languages from lists of cognate words. The approach is founded on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which posits that the best model (a set of correspondence rules) is the one that most compactly describes the given data. The method is designed to be data-driven, avoiding prior assumptions about the structure or distribution of correspondences. An efficient, deterministic EM-like algorithm is proposed to infer the rule set by iteratively aligning the data and merging rules that lead to the largest reduction in the total description length. The framework is evaluated on a set of 13 Slavic languages, first in pairwise comparisons to construct a phylogenetic tree, and then in a novel four-way analysis that quantifies similarities between all subsets of the languages.

In my assessment, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel, principled framework for discovering correspondence rules between more than two languages simultaneously.** By moving beyond the standard pairwise paradigm, the method allows for a more holistic analysis of language families. This multi-language approach is the most significant contribution, as it enables the discovery of complex relationships that involve subgroups of languages.
2.  **The introduction of Shared Description Length (sDL) as a fine-grained, quantitative metric for linguistic similarity.** Derived directly from the MDL model, sDL allows for the quantification and comparison of shared structure across any subset of the languages being analyzed. This provides a more detailed view of linguistic relationships than what is possible with pairwise distance matrices or phylogenetic trees alone.
3.  **A deterministic, iterative algorithm for inferring correspondence rules.** The proposed EM-like procedure, which starts from a simple "null alignment" and greedily merges rules based on their contribution to data compression, provides a transparent and reproducible method for model inference.

### Strengths

1.  **Novelty and Significance of the Multi-Language Formulation:** The paper's primary strength lies in its ability to analyze multiple languages at once. Most computational methods for correspondence discovery are restricted to language pairs. The proposed framework is a substantial step forward, and its utility is convincingly demonstrated in the four-way analysis of Slavic languages (Section 5.3). This analysis reveals subgroup structures (e.g., CS-PL vs. RU-BG) and cross-group similarities within a single, unified model, which would be difficult to extract from a collection of pairwise analyses. The resulting sDL metric is an insightful and direct benefit of this formulation.
2.  **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The use of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle provides a solid, information-theoretic basis for the method. This grounding allows the model to be objective and data-driven, avoiding the need for arbitrary assumptions (e.g., prior distributions on rule shapes) that can bias the results. The technical details of the two-part code are, for the most part, clearly articulated, lending credibility to the approach.
3.  **High Clarity and Detailed Exposition:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The authors clearly explain the intuition behind their approach (Section 2), provide a detailed mathematical formulation of the MDL code (Section 4), and describe the training procedure systematically. The deterministic nature of the core algorithm further enhances clarity and aids in reproducibility.
4.  **Insightful Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments effectively demonstrate the method's capabilities. The pairwise analysis (Section 5.2) serves as a successful sanity check, producing a phylogenetic tree of Slavic languages that aligns well with established linguistic classifications. The subsequent four-way analysis is the most compelling part of the evaluation, showcasing the unique strengths of the multi-language approach and the descriptive power of the sDL metric (Figure 3).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Significant Scalability Limitations:** The paper acknowledges that the alignment procedure is a computational bottleneck, with the current implementation limited to "up to five languages". The combinatorial nature of the multi-language alignment problem is a major hurdle for applying this method to larger language families. The paper would be substantially stronger if it included a more formal analysis of the algorithm's complexity with respect to the number of languages (N) and offered concrete strategies or heuristics to mitigate this issue, rather than deferring it to future work.
2.  **Lack of Comparative Evaluation:** The experiments, while insightful, are entirely intrinsic. The paper does not quantitatively compare its results to any other statistical methods for correspondence discovery or phylogenetic reconstruction, even in the more standard pairwise setting. Without a comparison to established baselines (e.g., methods based on PMI, HMMs, or other alignment algorithms), it is difficult to objectively gauge the performance of the proposed method in terms of the quality of the discovered rules or the accuracy of the resulting language tree.
3.  **Unclear Justification for "Data Over-Weighting":** The introduction of the data over-weighting technique (Section 4.5) feels ad-hoc and undermines the paper's central claim of providing an objective, assumption-free method. This technique introduces a subjective, user-defined hyperparameter (`α`) that directly contradicts the MDL principle of balancing model and data complexity. While it may be pragmatically useful, its inclusion clouds the otherwise clear theoretical framework. A more critical discussion of this departure from the MDL principle and guidance on selecting `α` would be necessary.
4.  **Potential Confusion in the Model Code Formulation:** The description of the model code for the rule table, `L(Π)`, in Section 4.1 is confusing. The formula appears to include terms for encoding the number of rules per language subset (`LN(|ΠCi| + 1)`) as well as a separate "data-to-model code" based on the total counts of all rule applications (`LN(TΠ) + ...`). This raises concerns about redundancy, as information about rule frequencies seems to be encoded twice within the model description `L(M)`. This crucial part of the technical formulation requires clarification to ensure its soundness.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the formulation of the rule table code, `L(Π)`, in Section 4.1? Specifically, what is the motivation for including both the term for the number of rules per subset (`LN(|ΠCi| + 1)`) and the data-to-model code based on total rule occurrences `TΠ`? Is there a risk of encoding similar information twice, and if not, what distinct purpose does each component serve?
2.  Given the stated scalability bottleneck for N > 5 languages, have you considered using approximate search methods (e.g., beam search) for the alignment step instead of an exact algorithm like Dijkstra's? What would be the expected trade-off between computational feasibility for larger N and the optimality of the discovered alignments and rules?
3.  The data over-weighting parameter `α` moves the method away from the objectivity of MDL. How sensitive are the types of discovered rules to the specific value of `α`? Is there any principled approach or heuristic you can suggest for setting this parameter, or is it intended purely as a tool for subjective exploration by a linguistic expert?
