### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel, principled framework for discovering systematic string-level correspondences across multiple languages simultaneously. The core idea is to leverage the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which posits that the best model (here, a set of correspondence rules) is the one that provides the most compact description of the data. The authors propose a deterministic, EM-like algorithm that iteratively aligns data and refines the set of correspondence rules by merging patterns that yield the greatest compression. The method's key strengths are its ability to operate on more than two languages at once and its avoidance of prior assumptions about the structure or distribution of correspondences. Experiments on a set of 13 Slavic languages demonstrate the approach's potential, producing a linguistically plausible phylogenetic tree from pairwise analyses and offering a novel, fine-grained quantification of language-subset similarities in a four-way analysis.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel MDL-based framework for multi-language correspondence discovery.** This is a significant conceptual contribution, moving the task from pairwise, assumption-heavy models to a non-parametric, multi-way formulation. The idea of learning correspondences of arbitrary length and structure by optimizing a global compression objective is both elegant and powerful.
2.  **The introduction of "shared Description Length" (sDL) as a metric for similarity.** This provides a new tool for computational comparative linguistics. Instead of just a single tree, sDL allows for a nuanced, quantitative analysis of the similarities between *all* subsets of the languages under consideration, revealing a more complex web of relationships.
3.  **A pragmatic algorithm and exploration mechanism.** The paper presents a concrete, deterministic algorithm for this complex optimization problem. Furthermore, the "data over-weighting" mechanism is a clever and practical feature that allows a human expert to guide the discovery process and explore rules at different levels of statistical significance.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Ambition:** The paper's core idea is bold and refreshing. By framing the problem through the lens of MDL, the authors sidestep many of the limiting assumptions (e.g., specific phonological models, fixed-size windows) that constrain prior work. The ambition to discover correspondences of arbitrary length (e.g., `(szcz,št)`) across an arbitrary number of languages simultaneously sets a new direction for this area of research. This is precisely the kind of high-risk, high-reward work that pushes the field forward.
2.  **Powerful Multi-way Analysis:** The leap from pairwise to multi-way analysis is the paper's strongest practical contribution. The standard output of comparative methods is a phylogenetic tree, which inherently loses information by forcing a hierarchical structure. The sDL analysis shown in Figure 3 is far more insightful, quantifying the shared structure specific to CS-PL, RU-BG, and even the full four-language set. This opens the door to asking more sophisticated questions about language contact and subgrouping.
3.  **Principled yet Flexible Framework:** The MDL principle provides a solid, information-theoretic foundation for the model, creating an objective trade-off between rule complexity and data fit. This grounding is crucial. At the same time, the authors acknowledge the needs of a potential user (e.g., a linguist) with the "data over-weighting" parameter. This is a very insightful addition, transforming the tool from a purely objective statistical engine into an interactive discovery aid that allows for exploring linguistically interesting hypotheses that might not meet a strict significance threshold.
4.  **Compelling Qualitative Evidence:** While the task lacks standardized benchmarks, the experimental results serve as a strong proof of concept. The pairwise NCDs and the resulting phylogenetic tree for 13 Slavic languages align well with known linguistic facts, which validates the underlying measure of similarity. More importantly, the examples of learned correspondences are non-trivial and linguistically plausible, capturing everything from simple sound changes `(g,h)` to complex phenomena like liquid metathesis `(ла,оло)`, demonstrating the model's expressive power.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Scalability Limitations:** The paper is forthright about the method's computational bottlenecks, stating that the current implementation is limited to roughly five languages. For a method whose primary advantage is multi-way analysis, this is a significant practical constraint that limits its immediate applicability to larger-scale comparative studies. The underlying shortest-path alignment problem appears to be combinatorial in the number of languages, and it is unclear whether this is a fundamental barrier or an implementation-specific one.
2.  **Greedy Optimization:** The maximization step relies on a greedy heuristic: iteratively merge the single best pair of rules. This deterministic approach is simple and intuitive, but it is not guaranteed to find a global optimum for the MDL objective function. The model could easily get trapped in a local optimum, potentially missing a more powerful set of correspondences that would require a non-greedy sequence of merges to discover. The potential impact of this greedy strategy on the quality of the final model is not explored.
3.  **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experiments are confined entirely to the orthography of Slavic languages. While this is a reasonable starting point, it leaves the method's generalizability in question. It is not clear how it would handle phonological transcriptions (which might be sparser), or language families with vastly different scripts and morphological systems. The validation, while insightful, is largely qualitative. The paper would be stronger if it included an analysis on a synthetic dataset with known ground truth to better quantify the algorithm's recall and precision.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The scalability issue is the most pressing practical concern. Have you explored approximations to the optimal alignment procedure (e.g., beam search in the graph) that could trade a small amount of optimality for a significant improvement in runtime and memory, thereby enabling analysis of a larger number of languages?
2.  The greedy merging strategy is a potential weakness. Have you considered or experimented with other search strategies? For instance, would a stochastic approach like simulated annealing (as mentioned briefly in the conclusion) or allowing "split" operations in addition to merges lead to significantly better (i.e., more compressive) models? How stable are the results with respect to the order of merges?
3.  The `alpha` parameter for data over-weighting is a great feature for exploration, but its selection seems ad-hoc. Is there a more principled way to guide its selection? For example, could one plot the total description length as a function of `alpha` and look for an "elbow point" that suggests a natural level of rule granularity?
