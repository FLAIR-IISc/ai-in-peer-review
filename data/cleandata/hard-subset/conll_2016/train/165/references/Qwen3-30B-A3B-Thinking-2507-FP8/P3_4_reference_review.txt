

# Peer Review: "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL (Minimum Description Length)-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about the distribution or shape of correspondences. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a novel MDL-based framework for discovering correspondences between multiple languages, (2) an efficient, deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules directly from data, and (3) a demonstration of the approach on Slavic languages showing it can identify linguistic subgroups while quantifying similarities across all language subsets.

## Strengths

1. **Methodological Rigor**: The paper makes a strong methodological contribution by addressing a genuine limitation in existing approaches. Most previous work on linguistic correspondences either assumes a specific distribution of correspondences or is limited to pairwise comparisons. The MDL approach elegantly avoids these pitfalls by using a statistically well-founded principle that seeks the most concise description of the data. The detailed technical formulation of the PS-2 MDL code demonstrates deep understanding of both MDL principles and linguistic data representation.

2. **Experimental Validation**: The experiments provide compelling evidence that the approach aligns with linguistic expectations. The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values correctly groups languages by their linguistic classification (West, East, and South Slavic). The four-way analysis reveals nuanced similarities between language subsets that would be missed in pairwise analyses, demonstrating the method's unique value for multi-language linguistic analysis.

3. **Practical Implementation**: The authors provide a clear description of the algorithm's implementation, including the alignment procedure using Dijkstra's algorithm and the training procedure using EM. The discussion of data over-weighting as a way to incorporate linguistic knowledge is particularly insightful and addresses a practical need for linguists.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparative Evaluation**: The paper fails to provide a meaningful comparison with state-of-the-art methods for discovering linguistic correspondences. While the authors mention existing approaches, they don't quantitatively demonstrate how their method improves upon them. For instance, they don't compare the accuracy of discovered correspondences against established linguistic knowledge or show how their method outperforms existing statistical approaches on the same dataset. Without this, it's difficult to assess the true novelty and value of their contribution.

2. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: The evaluation is restricted to Slavic languages, which may not be representative of other language families. The paper doesn't discuss whether the approach generalizes to non-Slavic language families or how it might handle languages with very different morphological structures (e.g., agglutinative vs. fusional languages). This limits the perceived applicability of the method to broader linguistic research.

3. **Lack of Linguistic Validation**: The paper presents examples of discovered correspondences but doesn't provide evidence that these correspondences are linguistically meaningful. For instance, they show examples like (g,h) and (cz,č), but don't explain how these align with established linguistic knowledge. A linguist's assessment of the discovered correspondences would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a quantitative comparison between your method and at least two existing approaches for discovering linguistic correspondences? Specifically, how does your method perform on metrics like precision, recall, or F1-score for discovered correspondence rules?

2. How would your method handle languages with significantly different writing systems (e.g., Latin vs. Cyrillic vs. non-alphabetic scripts)? The current evaluation is limited to Slavic languages with similar writing systems.

3. The paper mentions that the approach can "quantify the similarity between all subsets of analyzed languages," but doesn't explain how this differs from existing multi-language similarity measures. Could you clarify the theoretical and practical advantages of your information-theoretic quantification over other approaches?

## Conclusion

The paper presents a theoretically sound approach to discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distribution. The methodological foundation is strong, and the experimental results provide promising evidence of the approach's validity. However, the lack of comparative evaluation against existing methods and limited scope of linguistic validation prevent the paper from fully demonstrating its contribution to the field.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods, expanding the evaluation to include non-Slavic language families, and providing linguistic validation of the discovered correspondences. With these improvements, this work could make a substantial contribution to the field of computational historical linguistics.

Overall, the paper has strong theoretical foundations and promising experimental results, but requires more substantial evidence of its novelty and practical utility to be considered for publication.
