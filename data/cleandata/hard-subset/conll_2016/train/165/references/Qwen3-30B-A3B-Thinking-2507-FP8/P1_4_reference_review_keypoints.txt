- MDL-based approach for discovering correspondences across multiple languages without assuming shape or distribution of correspondences.
- Technical development of the PS-2 MDL code, including model/data code and alignment via Dijkstra's algorithm.
- Introduction of shared Description Length (sDL) as a metric to quantify similarity across all subsets of languages.

- Fills a gap versus pairwise methods by discovering multi-language correspondences and identifying linguistic subgroups.
- Clear, thorough technical exposition suitable for reproducibility.
- Strong empirical validation on Slavic languages: phylogenetic tree from NCD aligns with established classifications and a four-way analysis demonstrates multi-language capability.
- Practical utility for linguists, including phylogenetic construction and a data over-weighting technique to control granularity.

- Lacks direct experimental comparison with state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Wettig et al., Snyder & Barzilay) on the same data.
- Evaluation limited to relatively homogeneous Slavic languages, limiting claims of generalizability.
- Computational complexity and scalability are unclear; current implementation handles up to five languages in hours on a 4GB RAM machine.
- No error analysis or discussion of failure modes and correspondence types the method struggles with.

- Request for direct comparison to Wettig et al. (2011) and Snyder & Barzilay (2008) on the same Slavic data.
- Ask how the data over-weighting parameter α affects discovered rules and guidance for choosing α.
- Ask whether the method has been applied to non-Slavic families to test generalizability.

- Overall: solid, novel MDL-based contribution with sound technical development and effective experiments; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address comparisons, broader evaluation, scalability, and error analysis.
