

# Review: "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach to discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about their distribution. The authors develop a deterministic algorithm that uses Expectation-Maximization to learn correspondence rules directly from data. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. **A principled MDL-based framework for correspondence discovery** that avoids the arbitrary assumptions common in existing statistical approaches, enabling objective, unbiased string-level measures of linguistic similarity.
2. **An efficient algorithm for multi-language correspondence discovery** that can handle more than two languages simultaneously, allowing for detailed quantification of similarities across all language subsets.
3. **Linguistically meaningful insights** through comprehensive analysis of Slavic languages, demonstrating how the method identifies linguistic subgroups and quantifies similarities in ways not possible with pairwise approaches.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery**: The paper makes a compelling case for using MDL as a principled alternative to existing approaches that impose arbitrary assumptions about correspondence patterns. By framing correspondence discovery as a model selection problem where the "best" model is the one that describes the data most succinctly, the authors address a fundamental limitation of previous work. This approach aligns with the goal of discovering "true" correspondences hidden in the data rather than the closest similes from assumed distributions.

2. **Effective multi-language analysis**: The paper successfully demonstrates how the method can handle more than two languages simultaneously. The four-language analysis (Section 5.3) provides nuanced insights into Slavic language relationships that pairwise approaches cannot capture. For example, the paper shows how some correspondences link only two or three languages, while others apply to all four, revealing subtle linguistic patterns that were previously unobservable.

3. **Clear experimental validation**: The authors provide thorough validation through both pairwise and multi-language analyses. The phylogenetic tree in Figure 1 aligns well with established linguistic classifications, and the example correspondences in Table 3 demonstrate the method's ability to discover meaningful linguistic patterns at different granularities. The paper effectively shows how the method can quantify similarities between all subsets of analyzed languages, not just pairwise comparisons.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper mentions existing statistical approaches but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with the most recent methods for correspondence discovery. Without such a comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the proposed method offers a significant improvement over the current state of the art. For example, the paper could have compared against neural network-based approaches or recent probabilistic models that also aim to discover linguistic correspondences.

2. **Limited scope of analysis**: The paper only analyzes Slavic languages, which limits the generalizability of the claims. The method's applicability to other language families or language pairs with different typological characteristics isn't demonstrated. While the Slavic analysis is detailed, the paper doesn't provide evidence that the method would work equally well for other language groups, such as languages with very different writing systems or morphological complexity.

3. **Ambiguity in data over-weighting technique**: The "data over-weighting" technique (Section 4.5) is introduced as a way to include correspondences that are "almost statistically significant," but the paper lacks clear justification for this approach. This technique moves the method away from the objective MDL framework, undermining one of the paper's key claims. The paper doesn't provide guidelines for choosing appropriate weights or explain how to determine when over-weighting is appropriate.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your method compare to recent neural network-based approaches for correspondence discovery, such as those by Snyder et al. (2010) or Schulz et al. (2004)? Have you considered including such comparisons in the paper?

2. The paper only analyzes Slavic languages. How would you expect your method to perform on language families with different typological characteristics, such as languages with very different writing systems (e.g., Arabic vs. Chinese) or morphological complexity (e.g., agglutinative vs. isolating languages)?

3. Could you provide more justification for the "data over-weighting" technique and explain how to choose appropriate weights for different language pairs or language sets? Without clear guidelines, this technique risks introducing subjectivity that contradicts the paper's objective MDL framework.

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper that makes a strong theoretical contribution to the field of linguistic correspondence discovery. The MDL-based approach is principled and addresses a key limitation of existing methods. The experimental validation is thorough and provides meaningful linguistic insights. However, the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods and the limited scope of the analysis are significant weaknesses. The paper would be much stronger with a more comprehensive comparison to existing approaches and evidence of the method's applicability beyond Slavic languages.

With these issues addressed, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the field, offering a principled, objective approach to discovering linguistic correspondences that can handle multiple languages simultaneously and provide nuanced insights into language relationships.
