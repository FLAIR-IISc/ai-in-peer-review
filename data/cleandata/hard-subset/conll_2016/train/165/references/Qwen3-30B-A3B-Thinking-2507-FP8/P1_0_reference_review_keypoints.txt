- Novel MDL-based framework for discovering multi-language correspondences without prior assumptions about correspondence distributions.
- EM-based algorithm that deterministically learns correspondence rules and handles more than two languages simultaneously.
- Data over-weighting technique to control granularity of discovered correspondences for linguists.
- Theoretically sound and principled use of MDL, aligning with expected linguistic classifications.
- Demonstrated capability to analyze multiple languages at once, revealing nuanced multi-way correspondences missed by pairwise methods.
- Practical utility for linguists: over-weighting reveals correspondences at different detail levels (phonological, orthographic, morphological).
- Lacks comprehensive quantitative comparison with state-of-the-art methods (e.g., F1, accuracy) to substantiate improvement claims.
- Insufficient evaluation and guidance for the over-weighting technique, including its effects and how to choose weights.
- Scalability concerns: current implementation handles up to five languages in a few hours; complexity and optimizations for larger sets not discussed.
- Request for quantitative comparisons on accuracy and efficiency versus existing approaches.
- Request for examples where over-weighting produces incorrect correspondences and criteria for when it is excessive.
- Request to identify computational bottlenecks and possible optimizations to scale to 10+ languages.
- Evaluation on Slavic languages is well-executed but the claim that string-level similarity strongly reflects linguistic classification may be overstated.
- Method focuses on orthographic correspondences without phonetic transcriptions, limiting scope; extension to phonetic data or deeper linguistic features is suggested.
