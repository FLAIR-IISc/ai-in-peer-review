

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach to discover linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about the distribution or shape of these correspondences. The authors present a deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules directly from data, with applications to Slavic language analysis.

The main contributions, in order of strength, are:
1. A theoretically sound MDL-based framework for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without restrictive assumptions.
2. An efficient algorithm that enables detailed quantification of string-level similarities across multiple languages simultaneously, rather than being limited to pairwise comparisons.
3. Demonstrated effectiveness through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages and detailed correspondence discovery across four languages (Czech, Polish, Russian, Bulgarian).

## Strengths

1. **Technical Soundness of MDL Framework**: The paper provides a rigorous theoretical foundation for applying MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery. The authors carefully derive their two-part MDL code (L(M) + L(D|M)), with detailed explanations of model code (L(M)) and data code (L(D|M)). Their encoding scheme for correspondence rules, rule tables, and alphabets is mathematically well-founded and properly grounded in information theory. The use of universal codes for integers and Shannon-optimal codes for symbols demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying theory.

2. **Novelty in Multi-language Analysis**: The paper makes a significant contribution by extending correspondence discovery beyond pairwise comparisons to multiple languages simultaneously. This capability allows for more detailed quantification of linguistic similarities across language groups, which is not possible with most existing approaches. The four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian reveals nuanced linguistic relationships that pairwise analysis would miss, such as the identification of three-way vowel correspondences without requiring four-way rules.

3. **Empirical Validation with Linguistic Consistency**: The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that their approach produces results consistent with established linguistic classifications. Their phylogenetic tree for 13 Slavic languages correctly groups languages by their linguistic classifications, with only expected outliers (e.g., Bulgarian and Macedonian as outliers in the South Slavic group, Polish as an outlier in West Slavic due to digraphs and palatalization). The discovered correspondences (e.g., (g,h), (cz,č), verb endings) are linguistically meaningful and demonstrate the method's ability to identify both phonological and orthographic patterns.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparative Evaluation**: While the paper demonstrates that their method produces linguistically consistent results, it lacks a direct comparison with existing state-of-the-art methods for correspondence discovery. Without such a comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the proposed approach provides meaningful improvements over prior work. For example, how does their MDL-based approach compare to the methods of Wettig et al. (2011) or Snyder and Barzilay (2008) in terms of accuracy, computational efficiency, or ability to discover meaningful correspondences?

2. **Ambiguity in Data Over-weighting Technique**: The paper introduces "data over-weighting" as a technique to include near-significant correspondences, but the explanation is insufficiently detailed. The authors state that "assigning some data entry a weight of 2 intuitively duplicates that entry in the data set," but don't provide guidance on how to determine appropriate weights or how this might affect the statistical interpretation of results. This technique moves the approach away from the objective MDL framework, yet the paper doesn't clearly discuss the trade-offs between objectivity and the ability to discover more detailed correspondences.

3. **Lack of Discussion on Failure Cases**: The paper presents positive results but doesn't address potential failure cases or limitations of the approach. For instance, it doesn't discuss how the method handles languages with very different orthographic systems, cases where cognate identification is ambiguous (e.g., false cognates), or when there is insufficient data to discover meaningful correspondences. The claim that "occasionally we may miss some of the more subtle correspondence rules" is acknowledged but not substantiated with specific examples.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper assumes the input data consists of true cognates, but in practice, cognate identification is challenging and often contains errors. How does your method handle cases where the input data contains false cognates or loanwords that might be mistaken for genuine cognates?

2. The paper mentions that "with our current implementation, we can process up to five languages from our most complicated data set within a few hours." Could you provide more specific computational complexity analysis for larger numbers of languages (e.g., six or more) to help understand the scalability of your approach?

3. The paper discusses integrating linguistic knowledge through initialization (null alignment or greedy alignment from given rules), but doesn't elaborate on specific mechanisms for incorporating expert linguistic knowledge. Could you provide examples of how a linguist might use this feature to guide the discovery process?

## Additional Comments

The paper's focus on string-level orthographic correspondences is well-justified for their research question, but the authors should clarify whether their method could be extended to handle phonological or morphological correspondences beyond the orthographic level. The reference to "superficial word form similarity" in the conclusion suggests this limitation is acknowledged, but it would be helpful to explicitly state the scope of their approach.

The paper demonstrates strong technical merit in its theoretical formulation and empirical application, but would be strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly through a more comprehensive evaluation against existing methods and clearer discussion of limitations.
