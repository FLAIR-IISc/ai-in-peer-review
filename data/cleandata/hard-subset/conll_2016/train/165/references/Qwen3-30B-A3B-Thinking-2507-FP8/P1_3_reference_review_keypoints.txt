- Proposes an MDL-based framework to discover linguistic correspondences across multiple languages without prior assumptions about distribution or shape.  
- Introduces an efficient EM-based algorithm that learns correspondence rules and can analyze more than two languages simultaneously, quantifying similarities among all subsets.  
- Validates method experimentally (pairwise and four-way analyses on Slavic languages), showing it identifies linguistic subgroups and quantifies subset similarities.

- The MDL approach is theoretically sound and novel, offering a principled alternative to methods that rely on arbitrary assumptions.  
- Evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, with results (including a phylogenetic tree) aligning with linguistic classifications and showing multi-way similarity quantification.  
- The "data over-weighting" technique is a practical solution to low-statistics issues, allowing control over discovery granularity.

- Lacks systematic empirical comparison with state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Wettig et al. 2011; Snyder & Barzilay 2008), making it hard to assess relative performance and novelty.  
- Overstates discovering the "true" correspondences without discussing that MDL finds the most succinct model, not necessarily the true linguistic mappings, and omits handling of missing data.  
- No analysis of computational complexity or empirical scaling with number of languages/dataset size, limiting assessment of practicality for larger families.  
- Limited linguistic interpretation of discovered correspondences; paper provides examples but lacks deeper explanation linking results to known linguistic phenomena.

- Request detailed comparisons with relevant prior work (theoretical assumptions and empirical performance) and explicit claims of how this method addresses their limitations.  
- Ask for concrete examples linking observed regularity differences in the four-way Slavic analysis to known linguistic phenomena.  
- Request evidence that rules are discovered in statistical-significance order, especially in over-weighted settings.  
- Ask how the method handles datasets with gaps (e.g., missing Swadesh items) and whether meaningful correspondences can still be found.

- Paper is well-written and valuable, but should address comparative analysis, limitations (including missing data), computational scaling, and deeper linguistic interpretation to strengthen the contribution.
