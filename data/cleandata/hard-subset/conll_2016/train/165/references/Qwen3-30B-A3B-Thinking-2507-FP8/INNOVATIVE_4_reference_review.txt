

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary
This paper proposes using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to discover linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without imposing assumptions about the distribution or shape of these correspondences. The authors present a deterministic algorithm that infers correspondence rules directly from data, enabling simultaneous analysis of more than two languages. They apply their approach to Slavic languages, demonstrating how it can identify linguistic subgroups and quantify similarities between all subsets of languages, not just pairwise comparisons.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel approach to multi-language correspondence discovery using MDL without assumptions about correspondence distributions
2. An efficient algorithm for inferring correspondence rules across multiple languages simultaneously
3. A demonstration of how the approach can quantify linguistic similarity across all language subsets, not just pairwise

## Strengths

1. **Novel Direction for Multi-Language Analysis**: This paper makes a significant contribution by addressing a real gap in the literature—most existing methods are limited to pairwise analysis or make restrictive assumptions about correspondence distributions. The authors' bold approach of using MDL to discover correspondences without assumptions is genuinely novel and addresses a meaningful problem in computational linguistics. Their method enables the quantification of similarities across all language subsets, which is a substantial advancement over previous work.

2. **Practical Demonstration with Linguistic Insights**: The authors demonstrate their approach on Slavic languages, showing it can identify linguistic subgroups (e.g., grouping Czech and Polish together, Russian and Bulgarian together) that align with linguistic classifications. The paper provides concrete examples of discovered correspondences (both phonological and orthographic) that reveal meaningful linguistic patterns. This practical application demonstrates the approach's potential utility for linguists.

3. **Flexible Granularity Control**: The paper's introduction of "data over-weighting" (Section 4.5) is a particularly clever and practical extension. This allows linguists to control the granularity of discovered correspondences by adjusting data weights, effectively letting them choose the desired level of detail. This user-oriented feature makes the approach more adaptable to real linguistic analysis needs.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Justification of Novelty**: While the authors claim their approach is the first to handle multiple languages without assumptions, they don't provide a thorough comparison with prior work that might have addressed this problem. Previous approaches like Wettig et al. (2011) used MDL for etymological relationships, but the authors don't clearly explain why their multi-language extension is novel rather than a straightforward extension of existing MDL approaches. This weakens their claim of novelty.

2. **Lack of Comparison with Baselines**: The paper doesn't compare their approach with existing methods on the same dataset. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether their method provides meaningful improvements over current state-of-the-art. The authors should demonstrate how their approach outperforms previous methods on standard benchmarks.

3. **Limited Evaluation of Linguistic Value**: While the paper shows the approach can identify linguistic subgroups, it doesn't establish whether the discovered correspondences have practical value for linguists beyond what could be found through manual analysis. The paper needs to demonstrate how these correspondences provide new insights or improve upon existing linguistic knowledge.

4. **Unaddressed Computational Scalability**: The authors mention they can process up to five languages within a few hours on modest hardware, but they don't discuss how the approach scales to larger numbers of languages or larger datasets. This is a significant limitation for a method intended for broader linguistic applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach specifically differ from previous MDL-based methods that have been applied to linguistic analysis (e.g., Wettig et al., 2011), and why couldn't those methods handle multiple languages simultaneously without assumptions?

2. Can you provide a quantitative comparison of your method's performance against existing approaches on a standard linguistic dataset, demonstrating clear improvements?

3. Beyond identifying linguistic subgroups, what specific linguistic insights or applications does your method enable that previous approaches couldn't provide?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to multi-language correspondence discovery that addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. While the methodological details are sound and the practical demonstration is promising, the paper would benefit from stronger justification of its novelty, clearer comparisons with prior work, and a more thorough evaluation of the linguistic value of the discovered correspondences.

Given my preference for novel concepts over methodological perfection, I believe this paper makes a significant contribution to the field by opening up a new direction for multi-language linguistic analysis. The potential to quantify similarities across all language subsets, not just pairwise, represents a meaningful advancement.

I recommend acceptance, with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses noted above in their revision to strengthen the paper's claims and impact. The novelty of the approach and its potential to reshape how linguists analyze language relationships outweigh the current methodological limitations.
