

# Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

## Summary and Contributions
The paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences across multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about their distribution. The authors develop an efficient algorithm that learns correspondence rules directly from data, and apply it to Slavic languages to demonstrate its effectiveness in identifying linguistic subgroups and quantifying string-level similarities.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. A novel application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery that avoids assumptions about correspondence distributions, providing objective string-level measures of linguistic similarity.
2. An efficient algorithm for learning correspondence rules that grows according to statistical significance, with a clear implementation of the two-part MDL code for this specific problem.
3. Demonstrations of the approach's effectiveness through detailed analyses of Slavic languages, including pairwise and four-way comparisons that reveal nuanced linguistic relationships beyond what pairwise methods can capture.

## Strengths
1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper provides a compelling justification for using MDL, clearly explaining why it's well-suited for this problem. The authors thoroughly address the limitations of existing approaches (arbitrary assumptions, limited to pairs of languages) and demonstrate how their method overcomes these issues. The detailed explanation of the MDL code and its implementation provides strong theoretical grounding for the approach.

2. **Effective empirical validation**: The pairwise analysis successfully constructs a phylogenetic tree that aligns with established linguistic classifications. The four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian reveals nuanced linguistic relationships that would be difficult to capture with pairwise approaches. The paper's demonstration of how the algorithm identifies linguistic subgroups while quantifying similarities across all subsets of languages is particularly impressive.

3. **Practical implementation and scalability**: The authors provide a clear explanation of computational complexity and demonstrate that their approach can handle up to five languages on modest hardware. The discussion of data over-weighting as a way to control the granularity of discovered correspondences is a valuable practical contribution that addresses a real need in linguistic analysis.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of comparative evaluation**: The paper makes strong claims about overcoming limitations of existing methods but fails to provide a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art approaches. Without direct performance comparisons (e.g., on metrics like accuracy of correspondence discovery or computational efficiency), it's difficult to assess how much better this method is than alternatives. This is a significant omission for a paper claiming to advance the field.

2. **Superficial linguistic analysis**: While the paper presents discovered correspondences, it lacks depth in explaining their linguistic significance. For instance, the observation that "Bulgarian and Macedonian are outliers" is stated but not connected to the specific rules discovered by the algorithm. A more detailed linguistic interpretation of the discovered correspondences would strengthen the paper's contribution to linguistics.

3. **Insufficient discussion of over-weighting risks**: The paper acknowledges that data over-weighting "moves results away from the objective territory of MDL," but doesn't provide evidence that this approach doesn't lead to spurious correspondences. A more rigorous analysis of when over-weighting is appropriate, how to determine optimal weights, and how to avoid overfitting would be necessary for the method to be adopted by linguists.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper states that "Bulgarian is in fact very similar to Russian" based on the four-way analysis, but this contradicts established linguistic classifications that place Bulgarian in the south Slavic group distinct from the east Slavic group (Russian, Ukrainian, etc.). Could the authors clarify how their findings align with or challenge existing linguistic theories?

2. In the four-way analysis, the paper shows that "there is more regularity in some of the languages than others." Could the authors provide specific examples of correspondence rules that most strongly support the observed linguistic relationships, particularly the unexpected similarity between Bulgarian and Russian?

3. The paper mentions that "the algorithm identifies the linguistic grouping between Czech and Polish" but doesn't explain how this is reflected in the discovered correspondence rules. Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the specific rules that support this classification?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a theoretically sound and practically implementable approach to discovering linguistic correspondences across multiple languages. The method addresses important limitations of existing approaches and demonstrates promising results on Slavic languages. However, the lack of comparative evaluation with state-of-the-art methods and insufficient linguistic interpretation of the discovered correspondences represent significant weaknesses that need to be addressed before the paper can be considered for publication. With these improvements, the paper has the potential to make a meaningful contribution to both computational linguistics and historical linguistics.

**Recommendation: Minor Revision**
