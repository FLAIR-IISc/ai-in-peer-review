

# Review: "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary
This paper proposes using Minimum Description Length (MDL) to discover linguistic correspondences across multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about the shape or distribution of these correspondences. The authors present an algorithm that treats correspondences as simple character string associations and uses MDL to identify the most concise model for the data. They demonstrate their approach on Slavic languages, claiming it can quantify similarities between multiple languages simultaneously, build phylogenetic trees, and discover linguistic correspondences at varying levels of granularity.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery across multiple languages
2. A deterministic algorithm for learning correspondence rules from data
3. Quantification of linguistic similarities across multiple language subsets

## Strengths
1. The technical description of the MDL encoding scheme is detailed and well-structured, showing clear understanding of the theoretical foundations. The paper provides a thorough explanation of how they encode rules, the alphabet, and the data.

2. The phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages aligns with established linguistic classifications, demonstrating the approach produces linguistically plausible results. The paper correctly identifies expected groupings (e.g., west vs. east Slavic languages) and explains deviations (e.g., Bulgarian/Macedonian as outliers).

3. The paper provides a valuable demonstration of how to quantify linguistic similarity across multiple language subsets simultaneously, which is a limitation of many existing pairwise approaches.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of novelty and insufficient comparison with established methods**: The paper claims to address limitations of "existing statistical approaches" but fails to provide a meaningful comparison with state-of-the-art methods for linguistic correspondence discovery. The authors cite Wettig et al. (2011), which also uses MDL for linguistic applications, yet don't position their work relative to this prior research. Without a thorough comparison against established baselines (e.g., methods by Snyder et al., Schulz et al.), the paper cannot convincingly demonstrate that their approach offers meaningful improvements.

2. **Insufficient validation against linguistic knowledge**: The paper claims to produce "objective, unbiased string-level measures" but provides no evidence that their discovered correspondences align with established linguistic knowledge. For instance, they present example correspondences like (g,h) and (cz,č) but don't verify whether these are linguistically valid. Without comparison to expert-verified correspondences, the paper cannot establish the accuracy of their method.

3. **Over-reliance on data over-weighting**: The paper introduces "data over-weighting" as a way to discover more detailed correspondences, but this fundamentally undermines the MDL principle by introducing subjective user input. The paper acknowledges this "moves results away from the objective territory of MDL," yet presents this as a positive feature. This makes the results highly dependent on the user's judgment rather than being truly data-driven.

4. **Lack of thorough analysis of limitations**: The paper doesn't address critical limitations of their approach, such as how it handles languages with very different writing systems, sparse data, or languages with complex morphological structures. The claim that their algorithm "can process up to five languages" suggests significant scalability issues, yet they don't discuss how this limitation impacts practical applications.

5. **Questionable claims about "objectivity"**: The paper repeatedly claims to provide "objective" results, but the use of data over-weighting and the lack of comparison with established linguistic knowledge undermine this claim. The paper doesn't provide any statistical analysis to support the significance of the discovered correspondences beyond their MDL scores.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does your method compare quantitatively to established approaches (e.g., Snyder et al. 2010, Wettig et al. 2011) in terms of accuracy of discovered correspondences?

2. Can you provide specific examples where your method discovered correspondences that were linguistically validated but missed by previous approaches?

3. What evidence do you have that the correspondences discovered by your method (e.g., (g,h), (cz,č)) align with established linguistic knowledge, rather than being artifacts of your encoding scheme?

4. How do you plan to handle languages with significantly different writing systems (e.g., Latin vs. Cyrillic) or very sparse data?

## Overall Recommendation
This paper presents a technically detailed approach to linguistic correspondence discovery using MDL, but it falls short of providing sufficient evidence for its novelty or superiority over existing methods. The claims about "objective" results are undermined by the reliance on data over-weighting, and the lack of thorough comparison with established baselines makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of the work. While the phylogenetic analysis aligns with linguistic expectations, this is a minimal validation that doesn't demonstrate the method's accuracy in discovering specific correspondences.

The paper would benefit from a more rigorous comparison with established methods, validation against expert-verified linguistic correspondences, and clearer discussion of the limitations of their approach. Without these elements, the paper fails to provide convincing evidence that their MDL-based approach offers meaningful improvements over existing methods for linguistic correspondence discovery.

**Decision: Reject** - The paper lacks sufficient evidence of novelty and thorough validation against established baselines, which is essential for a conference paper in this field.
