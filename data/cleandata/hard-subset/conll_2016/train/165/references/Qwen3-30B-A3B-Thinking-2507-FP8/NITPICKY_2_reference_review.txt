

# Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

## Summary
The paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without assuming a specific distribution of rules. The authors present a deterministic algorithm to learn correspondence rules directly from data and demonstrate their approach on Slavic languages, showing how it can quantify similarities between language pairs and groups.

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)
1. An MDL-based method for discovering language correspondences without arbitrary distributional assumptions
2. A deterministic algorithm that learns correspondence rules according to statistical significance
3. A detailed information-theoretic analysis of Slavic language similarities beyond pairwise comparisons

## Strengths
1. The theoretical foundation using MDL is sound and well-motivated. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing statistical approaches that rely on arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions, making their data-driven approach valuable for linguistic analysis.

2. The four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian demonstrates a significant methodological advance over pairwise approaches, providing nuanced insights into linguistic similarities that were previously impossible to quantify.

3. The paper provides a clear explanation of the MDL principle and its application to the problem, making the approach accessible to linguists and computational linguists.

## Weaknesses
1. **Critical missing examples**: The paper repeatedly refers to examples without providing them. For instance, it states "the Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian forms of the pan-Slavic word for happiness" but never shows these forms. Similarly, it describes three possible alignments for a "happiness example" but doesn't present the actual data to align. This omission makes it impossible to verify their claims about alignment quality and correspondence rules. (Page 1)

2. **Severe formatting and typographical errors**: The paper contains numerous formatting issues that undermine its credibility:
   - "mutual intelligibility." (missing 'l' in "intelligibility")
   - "Szemerenyi, 1970" (should be "Szemerényi, 1970" with proper accents)
   - "N∑ i=1" (should be "N∑_{i=1}")
   - "πn ̸=ϵ" (should be "π_n ≠ ε")
   - Inconsistent use of "MDL" vs "Mdl" and "Slavic" vs "slavic"
   - Missing spaces in mathematical expressions (e.g., "L(π) = N∑ n=1" should be "L(π) = ∑_{n=1}^N")
   (Pages 1, 3, 4)

3. **Lack of clarity on key technical concepts**: The paper fails to properly explain crucial elements:
   - The meaning of "code(σ)" (optimal unigram for symbol usages) is never defined or computed
   - The application of "Laplace correction" is described without specifying how it's implemented
   - The justification for "data over-weighting" is presented as a practical trick without theoretical grounding
   (Pages 4, 5, 6)

4. **Unsubstantiated claims**: The paper makes assertions without evidence:
   - "It is possible that a rule R is deemed good on the basis of the entire data set when in fact it is suboptimal for some subset S of the data." No examples or evidence are provided.
   - "The Serbo-Croatian data in Latin script was assessed to be slightly closer to the other languages that use Latin script, while the Cyrillic version was deemed more similar to other languages using Cyrillic." This is confusing since Serbo-Croatian in Cyrillic is the same language as in Latin script, just written differently.
   (Pages 5, 8)

5. **Missing figures**: The paper refers to "Figure 1" and "Figure 2" but doesn't include them in the submission. This is particularly problematic as these figures are central to the results. (Pages 8, 9)

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide the actual "happiness" example with the Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian forms that you reference in the introduction?

2. Please clarify the exact meaning and computation of "code(σ)" - what is the optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules, and how is it derived?

3. Could you explain how "data over-weighting" is theoretically justified beyond being a practical trick for obtaining more detailed correspondences?

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a theoretically sound approach with potential value for linguistic analysis, the numerous minor but critical flaws in presentation, formatting, and explanation undermine its credibility. The missing examples, formatting errors, and lack of clarity on key technical concepts make it difficult to evaluate the paper's claims. The authors have made a significant contribution to the field, but the current version of the paper fails to communicate this contribution effectively.

Given the severity of the presentation issues, I recommend rejection. The paper's potential value is overshadowed by these fundamental shortcomings that prevent a proper evaluation of its technical contributions.
