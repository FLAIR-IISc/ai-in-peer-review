- Proposes an MDL-based framework for discovering correspondences between multiple languages, plus a deterministic inference algorithm and empirical Slavic analysis.

- Strength: Novel application of MDL that avoids arbitrary distributional assumptions and extends beyond pairwise methods.

- Strength: Rigorous empirical validation on 13 Slavic languages producing phylogenies consistent with linguistic classifications.

- Strength: Introduces the shared Description Length (sDL) metric to quantify shared structure beyond pairwise comparisons.

- Weakness: No direct, quantitative comparison to state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Snyder & Barzilay 2008; Wettig et al. 2011).

- Weakness: Limited evaluation scope—only Slavic languages and current implementation scales to ~5 languages, so generalizability is unclear.

- Weakness: Underdeveloped discussion and demonstration of practical utility for linguistic analysis or NLP applications.

- Weakness: Reproducibility concerns—no code provided and computational claims lack specific benchmarking details.

- Question: How does the approach compare to state-of-the-art methods in accuracy (e.g., F1) and computational efficiency with concrete metrics?

- Question: Have you evaluated the method on non-Slavic families (e.g., Sino-Tibetan, Niger-Congo) and how do results differ?

- Question: Can you provide concrete examples of how discovered correspondences would be used in linguistic analysis or NLP tasks?

- Overall: The MDL framework and sDL metric are valuable and empirically promising, but missing comparisons, broader evaluations, and practical demonstrations; recommend weak acceptance pending these additions.
