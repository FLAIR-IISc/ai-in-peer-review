- Proposes an MDL-based framework to discover correspondences between multiple languages without assuming correspondence distribution or shape.  
- Claims an efficient, deterministic algorithm to infer correspondence rules directly from data.  
- Demonstrates the approach on Slavic languages, identifying subgroups and quantifying similarities across subsets.

- Methodologically rigorous: uses PS-2 MDL code to avoid pitfalls of prior pairwise or assumption-heavy methods.  
- Experimental validation: phylogenetic tree and four-way analysis align with linguistic expectations and reveal nuanced multi-language similarities.  
- Practical implementation: clear algorithmic description (Dijkstra alignment, EM training) and useful discussion of data over-weighting to incorporate linguistic knowledge.

- Lacks comparative evaluation against state-of-the-art methods and quantitative metrics (precision/recall/F1) to show improvement.  
- Evaluation scope is limited to Slavic languages, leaving generalization to other families and different morphological systems untested.  
- Lacks linguistic validation: discovered correspondences are shown but not assessed against established linguistic knowledge or expert judgment.

- Request to provide quantitative comparisons with at least two existing correspondence-discovery methods on standard metrics.  
- Question about handling languages with different writing systems (Latin, Cyrillic, non-alphabetic) and greater typological diversity.  
- Ask to clarify how the information-theoretic similarity quantification differs from and improves upon existing multi-language measures.

- Overall: strong theoretical foundation and promising results but needs comparative evaluation, broader language testing, and linguistic validation to demonstrate novelty and practical impact.
