

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering systematic linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about their distribution. The authors develop an efficient, deterministic algorithm to infer correspondence rules directly from data. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. A principled MDL-based framework for discovering linguistic correspondences that avoids the arbitrary assumptions common in existing statistical approaches.
2. An efficient algorithm implementing the MDL principle for correspondence discovery, with a novel two-part code design tailored to linguistic data.
3. Empirical validation showing the approach can successfully identify linguistic subgroups while quantifying similarities between all subsets of languages in a four-way Slavic analysis.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness of MDL Application**: The paper makes a compelling case for using MDL to avoid arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions, which is a significant limitation of existing methods. The authors correctly identify that many current approaches impose assumptions that lead to finding "closest similes from the assumed distribution" rather than the "true" correspondences. Their implementation of MDL for this specific linguistic problem is theoretically well-founded and properly grounded in coding theory.

2. **Empirical Demonstration of Multi-language Analysis**: The paper successfully demonstrates the value of their approach for simultaneous analysis of multiple languages (four-way Slavic analysis), which is a significant advancement over pairwise methods. The results show how their method can identify linguistic subgroups (e.g., west, east, and south Slavic) while quantifying similarities between all language subsets. The shared Description Length analysis (Figures 2-3) provides a novel information-theoretic quantification of linguistic similarity that goes beyond traditional phylogenetic approaches.

3. **Practical Utility for Linguists**: The deterministic nature of the approach is particularly valuable for linguists, as it allows for the integration of linguistic knowledge and provides reproducible results. The data over-weighting technique (Section 4.5) is a practical innovation that gives linguistic experts control over the level of detail in discovered correspondences, bridging the gap between theoretical linguistics and computational methods.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comprehensive Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims existing methods make "arbitrary assumptions" but fails to provide a thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art approaches for linguistic correspondence discovery. Without a rigorous comparison showing how the MDL approach outperforms current methods on standard benchmarks, it's difficult to assess the actual contribution of this work. The authors should include a comparative evaluation against at least two recent methods (e.g., from Snyder et al. or Schulz et al.).

2. **Limited Scope of Evaluation**: The evaluation is restricted to Slavic languages, which are relatively closely related. To demonstrate broader applicability, the authors should test their approach on more diverse language families (e.g., Indo-European vs. Uralic) or languages with more significant structural differences. The current evaluation may not adequately demonstrate the method's robustness to varying linguistic distances.

3. **Computational Scalability Concerns**: The paper acknowledges computational limitations, stating the current implementation handles "up to five languages... within a few hours on a 4GB RAM, 2.5GHz single core desktop machine." For a conference like CONLL, which often deals with large-scale linguistic data, this scalability limitation is significant. The authors should address how they plan to scale their approach to handle more languages or larger datasets, perhaps by discussing potential parallelization strategies or approximation methods.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims the approach handles "more than two languages" without limitation, yet the computational constraints are explicitly limited to about five languages. Could you provide more details on the computational complexity as a function of the number of languages, and how you plan to scale your approach to handle more languages or larger datasets?

2. The paper emphasizes the deterministic nature of the approach as a strength, but this could lead to suboptimal solutions if the algorithm gets stuck in local minima. How do you address the potential issue of convergence to suboptimal solutions due to the deterministic nature of your training procedure?

3. The data over-weighting technique is presented as a way to include less statistically significant correspondences, but the paper doesn't provide guidance on how to determine the appropriate over-weighting level. Is there a principled way to choose this parameter, or is it purely based on expert judgment? How do you prevent over-interpretation of the results when using this technique?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a theoretically sound approach to linguistic correspondence discovery with clear potential value for linguists. The main contribution is the principled application of MDL to avoid arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions, and the empirical validation on Slavic languages demonstrates the approach's utility for multi-language analysis.

However, the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods and the limited scope of the evaluation weaken the paper's contribution claim. The computational scalability concerns also need to be addressed for broader applicability.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following conditions: (1) thorough comparison with at least two recent methods for linguistic correspondence discovery, (2) evaluation on more diverse language families to demonstrate broader applicability, and (3) detailed discussion of computational scaling strategies. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the paper and demonstrate the full value of the proposed approach.
