

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about correspondence distributions. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) a novel MDL framework for multi-language correspondence discovery, (2) an efficient deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules, and (3) a detailed analysis of Slavic languages demonstrating the approach's effectiveness in phylogenetic analysis and quantifying linguistic similarity. The most significant contribution is the MDL framework, followed by the algorithm, with the empirical analysis serving as validation.

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of MDL to multi-language correspondence discovery**: The paper successfully identifies a gap in existing literature—statistical approaches to linguistic correspondences typically make arbitrary assumptions about distributions, limit to language pairs, or fail to integrate linguistic knowledge. The authors persuasively argue that MDL provides a principled approach to overcome these limitations. Their application of MDL to this specific problem is both novel and well-justified, as they clearly articulate how MDL avoids the pitfalls of previous methods.

2. **Rigorous empirical validation on Slavic languages**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation using Swadesh lists for 13 Slavic languages and a four-language Slavic cognate set. The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values aligns with established linguistic classifications, which is a strong validation of their approach. The paper demonstrates that their method can identify linguistic subgroups (e.g., identifying Bulgarian and Macedonian as outliers in the south Slavic group) while quantifying similarities across language subsets—a capability that goes beyond pairwise analysis.

3. **Innovative "shared Description Length" (sDL) metric**: The paper introduces a novel metric for quantifying the amount of structure shared between language subsets. This metric provides a valuable tool for linguistic analysis that goes beyond traditional pairwise comparisons. The sDL analysis reveals nuanced linguistic relationships (e.g., that Russian and Bulgarian share more structure than expected, while Czech has the highest individual description length due to its diacritics) that would be difficult to capture with existing methods.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper claims to overcome limitations of existing approaches but fails to provide a direct comparison with the most relevant methods (e.g., Snyder and Barzilay, 2008; Wettig et al., 2011). Without such a comparison, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed approach actually improves upon existing methods in terms of accuracy, computational efficiency, or other metrics. This is a significant weakness for a paper claiming to advance the state of the art.

2. **Limited scope of evaluation**: While the Slavic language evaluation is thorough, the paper doesn't demonstrate the approach's generalizability to non-Slavic languages or language families with different morphological structures. The authors acknowledge that their current implementation can process up to five languages, but without testing on diverse language families, the broader applicability of their method remains unclear.

3. **Underdeveloped discussion of practical utility**: The paper mentions potential applications in linguistic analysis and NLP but doesn't provide concrete examples of how the discovered correspondences would be used in practice. The "data over-weighting" technique is introduced as a way to include near-significant correspondences, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how this benefits linguistic analysis or NLP tasks. Without showing practical utility, the contribution appears more theoretical than applied.

4. **Reproducibility concerns**: While the algorithm is described in detail, the paper doesn't provide implementation details or code availability. The computational complexity analysis (e.g., "we can process up to five languages... within a few hours on a 4GB RAM, 2.5GHz single core desktop machine") lacks specific benchmarking data that would help researchers assess the method's scalability.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to state-of-the-art methods in terms of accuracy (e.g., F1 score for correspondence discovery) and computational efficiency? Please provide specific metrics from a direct comparison.

2. Have you tested your approach on non-Slavic language families (e.g., Indo-European, Sino-Tibetan, or Niger-Congo)? If so, how do the results differ from your Slavic language findings?

3. Can you provide concrete examples of how the discovered correspondences (e.g., the rules in Table 3) would be used by linguists in actual linguistic analysis or NLP applications?

## Overall Assessment

The paper presents a theoretically sound approach to a meaningful problem in computational linguistics. The MDL framework is well-motivated, and the empirical evaluation on Slavic languages is thorough and convincing. The introduction of the sDL metric is particularly valuable for linguistic analysis. However, the lack of comparison with existing methods and limited scope of evaluation prevent me from fully endorsing the paper as a major contribution. With additional comparisons, broader evaluation, and clearer demonstration of practical utility, this could be an excellent paper. As it stands, it's a solid contribution that advances the field but doesn't yet demonstrate clear superiority over existing approaches.

I recommend *weak acceptance* with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses, particularly the comparison with state-of-the-art methods and broader evaluation scope, in the camera-ready version.
