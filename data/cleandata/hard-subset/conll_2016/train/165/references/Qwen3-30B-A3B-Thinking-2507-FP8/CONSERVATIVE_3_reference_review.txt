

# Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

## Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about the distribution or shape of correspondences. The authors claim their method can objectively quantify string-level similarities across multiple languages, identify linguistic subgroups, and avoid arbitrary assumptions common in existing statistical approaches. The approach uses a two-part MDL code with an EM-based training procedure, tested on Slavic languages using Swadesh lists.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. An MDL-based framework for discovering linguistic correspondences across multiple languages
2. A novel encoding scheme for correspondence rules using MDL
3. Empirical demonstration on Slavic languages showing alignment with linguistic classifications

## Strengths
1. The theoretical foundation using MDL is well-grounded in statistical theory. MDL provides a principled approach to model selection that avoids arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions, which aligns with good statistical practice.

2. The paper provides a clear mathematical formulation of the model code and data code, including detailed explanations of the encoding scheme. This level of technical detail is valuable for reproducibility.

3. The phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages produces results consistent with established linguistic classifications, suggesting the approach can capture meaningful linguistic structure.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of rigorous comparison to established baselines**: The paper claims to avoid "arbitrary assumptions" but fails to demonstrate how their approach improves upon or differs from existing methods. They cite Wettig et al. (2011) who also used MDL for linguistic analysis but don't clarify how their approach differs substantially. Without comparison to established methods (e.g., Schulz et al., 2004; Snyder et al., 2010), it's impossible to assess if the claimed advantages are real.

2. **Questionable novelty**: The paper's core contribution appears to be an application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery, but this is not sufficiently differentiated from prior work. Wettig et al. (2011) already applied MDL to etymological relationships between languages. The paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate why their specific implementation offers novel insights beyond what's already been done.

3. **Methodological contradiction**: The paper introduces "data over-weighting" to include rules that aren't statistically significant, which directly contradicts the MDL principle's purpose of guarding against overfitting. This undermines the paper's claim of providing "objective, unbiased string-level measures" and introduces subjective human judgment, which is antithetical to the approach's stated goals.

4. **Limited empirical validation**: The evaluation is restricted to Slavic languages with no evidence of how the method scales to other language families. The paper claims to quantify similarities "between all subsets of analyzed languages," but doesn't provide evidence that this approach is more informative or accurate than standard pairwise analysis.

5. **Insufficient detail on algorithmic advantages**: The paper describes the algorithm but doesn't demonstrate why it's superior to simpler approaches. For instance, the paper claims their method can handle "more than two languages" but doesn't quantify the computational or analytical benefits compared to standard pairwise methods.

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents a theoretically grounded approach using MDL, the lack of rigorous comparison to established methods, questionable novelty, and methodological contradiction in the "data over-weighting" technique significantly undermine its contribution. The empirical validation is limited to a single language family and doesn't convincingly demonstrate advantages over existing approaches.

The paper's strength lies in its clear theoretical exposition of the MDL framework, but this alone is insufficient for a meaningful contribution to the field. To strengthen the paper, the authors would need to provide a thorough comparison to established baselines, clearly differentiate their approach from prior work, and remove the "data over-weighting" technique that contradicts their stated methodology.

## Recommendation
Reject. The paper makes an incremental application of a known technique without sufficient evidence of its advantages over existing methods. The claimed novelty is unsubstantiated, and the methodological contradiction in the data over-weighting technique weakens the paper's core argument. Without rigorous validation against established baselines, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty and significance for publication at CoNLL.
