- Proposes an MDL-based framework to discover linguistic correspondences across multiple languages without assuming correspondence distributions.  
- Presents an efficient deterministic EM-based algorithm to learn correspondence rules directly from data.  
- Demonstrates multi-language analysis that quantifies similarities for all language subsets, not only pairwise comparisons.  

- Strong theoretical foundation: two-part MDL formulation grounded in coding theory avoids arbitrary assumptions of prior methods.  
- Meaningful empirical validation: Slavic language experiments produce linguistically plausible correspondences and phylogenetic alignments.  
- Practical implementation: complete system with alignment (Dijkstra), EM training, and a data over-weighting mechanism to control detail.  

- Lacks thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., neural or unsupervised morphological alignment baselines).  
- Insufficient linguistic evaluation of discovered correspondences relative to established sound-change knowledge.  
- Scalability concerns: current implementation handles up to ~5 languages in hours, unclear for larger sets or complex phenomena.  
- Data over-weighting introduces subjectivity, undermining the claimed objectivity of MDL without guidelines for choosing weights.  

- Questions: how will scalability to 10+ languages be addressed; can authors provide deeper linguistic validation of rules; how to choose data over-weighting factors?  
- Overall: strong theoretical and empirical work with recommendation to accept pending minor revisions to add state-of-the-art comparisons, deeper linguistic analysis, and scalability/weighting guidance.
