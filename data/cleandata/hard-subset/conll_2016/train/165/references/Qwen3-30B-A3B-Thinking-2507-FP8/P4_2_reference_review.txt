

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper introduces a Minimum Description Length (MDL) approach to discover string-level correspondences between multiple languages without imposing arbitrary assumptions about the shape or distribution of these correspondences. The main contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. A novel application of the MDL principle to automatically discover linguistic correspondences between multiple languages, avoiding the distributional assumptions that plague existing statistical methods.

2. An efficient, deterministic algorithm for learning correspondence rules that grows according to statistical significance, using an Expectation-Maximization framework with a carefully designed MDL code.

3. A data over-weighting technique that allows linguistic experts to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences by adjusting the relative weights of model and data terms.

## Strengths

1. **Novel and well-motivated application of MDL**: The paper effectively addresses a key limitation in existing approaches to linguistic correspondence discovery. The authors correctly identify that most statistical methods "impose arbitrary assumptions on the distribution or shape of correspondences," which can lead to finding "not the 'true' correspondences hidden in the data, but their closest similes from the assumed distribution." Their application of MDL to this problem is conceptually sound and well-justified by the theoretical foundations of the MDL principle.

2. **Rigorous technical formulation**: The paper provides a detailed and mathematically precise formulation of the two-part MDL code (L(M) + L(D|M)), including careful treatment of alphabet encoding, rule table representation, and data alignment. The encoding schemes for rules, alphabets, and data are well-considered and address potential biases (e.g., avoiding unnecessary encoding of zero-length strings). The technical depth of this formulation is a significant strength.

3. **Empirical validation with meaningful linguistic insights**: The experiments demonstrate the approach's practical utility through both pairwise and four-way analyses of Slavic languages. The phylogenetic tree constructed from Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) values aligns with established linguistic classifications, and the four-way analysis reveals nuanced linguistic relationships (e.g., between Russian and Bulgarian) that pairwise methods would miss. The paper effectively shows how their method quantifies similarity across all subsets of languages, which is a significant advancement over existing approaches limited to pairwise comparisons.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: While the paper identifies limitations in existing approaches, it does not provide a direct empirical comparison with current methods on the same dataset. The authors claim their method avoids assumptions about correspondence distributions, but they don't demonstrate how this translates to improved accuracy or better correspondence discovery compared to methods that do make such assumptions. A quantitative comparison would strengthen their novelty claim.

2. **Insufficient discussion of computational limitations**: The paper notes that their implementation can process "up to five languages from our most complicated data set within a few hours," but doesn't discuss how these computational constraints affect the practical applicability of the method. It's unclear how the method would scale to larger language sets or more complex linguistic phenomena, which is critical for assessing its real-world utility.

3. **Lack of detailed evaluation of the data over-weighting technique**: While the paper introduces data over-weighting as a useful extension, it doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how different weight parameters affect the discovered correspondences. The paper states that "new rules will be discovered in the 'correct' order" but doesn't quantify how this might change with different weights or provide guidance for linguistic experts on choosing appropriate weights.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims their method avoids assumptions about correspondence distributions, but how does it compare to methods that make specific assumptions (e.g., Markov models) in terms of accuracy on standard linguistic datasets? Could you provide a comparative evaluation?

2. The paper mentions the algorithm can be extended to higher numbers of languages, but the computational limitations seem significant. Could you provide more details about the expected scaling behavior as the number of languages increases (e.g., time complexity as a function of N)?

3. The data over-weighting technique is presented as a useful extension, but it's not clear how the choice of weight affects the discovered correspondences. Could you provide more guidance on how linguistic experts should select appropriate weight parameters for different linguistic analysis tasks?
