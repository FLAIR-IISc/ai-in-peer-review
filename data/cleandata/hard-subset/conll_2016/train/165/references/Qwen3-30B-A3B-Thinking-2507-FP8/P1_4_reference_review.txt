

# Peer Review: "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about the shape or distribution of those correspondences. The authors argue that existing statistical approaches to linguistic correspondence discovery are limited to pairwise comparisons or make strong assumptions about the data, and they present an efficient, deterministic algorithm to infer correspondence rules directly from data. The paper demonstrates their approach on Slavic languages, showing it can identify linguistic subgroups while quantifying similarities across all subsets of analyzed languages.

My assessment of the paper's main contributions (in order of strength):

1. **A novel MDL-based approach for multi-language correspondence discovery**: The paper's most significant contribution is its application of MDL to discover correspondences across multiple languages without making assumptions about their distribution or shape. This addresses a clear gap in the literature, as most existing approaches are limited to pairwise comparisons.

2. **Technical development of the PS-2 MDL code**: The paper provides a thorough technical development of how MDL can be adapted to linguistic correspondence discovery, including detailed explanations of the model code, data code, and alignment procedure.

3. **Quantitative analysis of linguistic similarity across language subsets**: The introduction of shared Description Length (sDL) as a metric for quantifying similarity across all subsets of languages is a valuable contribution that goes beyond pairwise comparisons.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a genuine gap in the literature**: The paper correctly identifies a limitation in existing approaches—most methods are limited to pairwise comparisons or make strong assumptions about the distribution of correspondences. The authors' approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages without such assumptions is both novel and valuable for linguistic analysis. Their demonstration of how the approach can identify linguistic subgroups while quantifying similarities across all language subsets is particularly compelling.

2. **Clear technical exposition**: The paper provides a thorough and well-organized explanation of the MDL framework as applied to linguistic correspondence discovery. The authors clearly define their model class, explain the two-part MDL code, and detail the alignment procedure using Dijkstra's algorithm. The technical depth is appropriate for the intended audience and provides sufficient detail for reproducibility.

3. **Effective empirical validation**: The experiments on Slavic languages are well-designed and provide strong evidence of the approach's effectiveness. The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values aligns with established linguistic classifications, demonstrating the method's ability to capture meaningful linguistic relationships. The four-way analysis (CS-PL-RU-BG) showcases the unique capability of the approach to handle more than two languages simultaneously, which is a significant advantage over existing methods.

4. **Practical utility for linguists**: The paper demonstrates how the approach can be used for practical linguistic analysis, including phylogenetic tree construction and detailed correspondence discovery. The data over-weighting technique (Section 4.5) is particularly valuable, as it allows linguists to control the granularity of discovered correspondences based on their specific needs.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: While the paper mentions related work (Wettig et al., 2011; Snyder and Barzilay, 2008), it doesn't provide a comprehensive comparison with these methods on the same data. Without such a comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the proposed approach offers a significant improvement over existing methods. For example, the paper states that "most existing statistical approaches are afflicted by a number of problems," but doesn't provide evidence that their method solves these problems more effectively than alternatives.

2. **Limited scope of evaluation**: The paper's evaluation is restricted to Slavic languages, which form a relatively homogeneous language family. This limits the generalizability of the claims about the method's effectiveness. To strengthen their contribution, the authors should demonstrate their approach on more diverse language families with greater linguistic distance.

3. **Computational complexity concerns**: The paper states that their current implementation can process up to five languages within a few hours on a 4GB RAM machine. For larger language sets or more complex data, this could become impractical. The paper doesn't address how the computational complexity scales with the number of languages or the size of the data set, which is a significant limitation for real-world applications.

4. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper doesn't provide any analysis of where the method fails or what types of correspondences it struggles to discover. Understanding the limitations of the approach is crucial for guiding future improvements and for users to know when to apply the method.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a direct comparison between your approach and the methods of Wettig et al. (2011) and Snyder and Barzilay (2008) on the same Slavic language data? This would help establish whether your method offers a clear improvement over existing approaches.

2. How does the data over-weighting technique (Section 4.5) affect the quality of discovered correspondences? Specifically, how does the choice of α impact the discovered rules, and what criteria would you recommend for selecting an appropriate α value?

3. Have you considered applying your method to non-Slavic language families (e.g., Indo-European, Uralic, or Afro-Asiatic) to test its generalizability beyond the Slavic context?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics by proposing a novel MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences across multiple languages without making restrictive assumptions. The technical development is sound, the empirical evaluation is well-executed, and the approach demonstrates clear utility for linguistic analysis. While the paper has some limitations regarding comparison with state-of-the-art methods and scope of evaluation, the core contribution is significant and well-supported. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above. The paper has the potential to be a useful tool for linguists studying language relationships and evolution.
