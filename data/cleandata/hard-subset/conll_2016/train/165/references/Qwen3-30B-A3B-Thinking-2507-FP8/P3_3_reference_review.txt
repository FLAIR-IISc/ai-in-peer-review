

# Peer Review: "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making restrictive assumptions about correspondence patterns. The authors claim their method can handle more than two languages simultaneously, providing detailed quantification of string-level similarities across language subsets. The core contribution is an efficient algorithm that uses MDL to find the most succinct description of correspondence rules, validated through experiments on Slavic languages.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A principled application of MDL to multi-language correspondence discovery, avoiding restrictive assumptions about correspondence patterns
2. An efficient algorithm for learning correspondence rules across multiple languages simultaneously
3. A novel metric (shared Description Length) for quantifying linguistic similarity across language subsets

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness of MDL Approach**: The authors make a compelling case for using MDL as a principled approach to correspondence discovery. Unlike many existing statistical methods that impose arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions, MDL provides a statistically well-founded framework that balances model complexity with data fit. This aligns well with the problem's requirements and avoids the "finding the closest simile from the assumed distribution" issue they rightly identify as problematic in prior work.

2. **Linguistically Valid Results**: The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values (Figure 1) correctly identifies linguistic groupings, including the outlier status of Bulgarian and Macedonian due to their loss of case declension, and Polish's outlier status due to its frequent digraphs. This demonstrates that the method captures meaningful linguistic patterns rather than just statistical coincidences, which is crucial for linguistic applications.

3. **Novel Quantification of Multi-Language Similarity**: The four-way analysis (Czech-Polish-Russian-Bulgarian) is particularly strong, as it reveals how the method can detect subtle linguistic patterns that pairwise analyses would miss. The shared Description Length (sDL) metric provides a valuable way to quantify similarity across all language subsets, showing that Russian and Bulgarian share significant structure, while Czech has the highest individual description length due to its diacritic complexity. This represents a significant advancement over traditional pairwise approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Rigorous Comparison to State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims existing approaches have limitations but fails to provide a meaningful comparison with the most relevant methods. For instance, they don't compare their MDL approach to Wettig et al. (2011), which also uses MDL for etymological relationships, or to more recent multi-language methods. Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether their approach truly advances the state of the art or merely presents a different implementation of similar ideas.

2. **Over-Weighting as a Theoretically Unsound Solution**: The "data over-weighting" technique (Section 4.5) is presented as a way to handle insufficient statistical evidence, but it's acknowledged to move results away from the objective MDL framework. The authors don't provide a principled method for determining the appropriate weight, making this feel more like a pragmatic workaround than a theoretically sound solution. This undermines the paper's claim of providing "objective results."

3. **Limited Theoretical Analysis of Algorithm**: The paper describes an EM-based algorithm but provides no theoretical analysis of its convergence properties or optimality guarantees. The authors note that the algorithm is "deterministic," but EM methods are known to converge to local optima. Without addressing how likely the method is to find the global optimum or how sensitive results are to initialization, this claim is unsubstantiated. The paper also doesn't discuss the computational complexity beyond empirical runtime measurements.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach specifically improve upon Wettig et al. (2011), which also uses MDL for etymological relationships? What are the key differences in methodology and results?

2. The paper states that the algorithm can process "up to five languages" within a few hours on modest hardware. What are the theoretical time and space complexities of your algorithm, and how do you expect them to scale with more languages? Is there a fundamental limit to the number of languages you can analyze?

3. In the four-way analysis, you note that "there is more regularity in some of the languages than others." Can you provide specific linguistic examples of what this means in terms of established linguistic phenomena, rather than just statistical patterns?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the application of MDL in linguistic correspondence discovery. The core methodology is theoretically sound, and the results demonstrate meaningful linguistic insights. However, the lack of rigorous comparison to existing methods and the questionable use of data over-weighting significantly weaken the paper's claims of novelty and advancement. With proper comparisons and theoretical analysis, this work could be a strong contribution to the field.

The paper presents a promising approach that advances the state of the art in multi-language linguistic analysis, but it needs to better substantiate its claims of novelty and superiority over existing methods. The linguistic validity of results is strong, but the methodological justification requires strengthening. I recommend acceptance with major revisions addressing the weaknesses identified above.
