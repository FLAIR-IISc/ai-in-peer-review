

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering systematic linguistic correspondences across multiple languages without making assumptions about the distribution or shape of these correspondences. The authors present an efficient algorithm using Expectation-Maximization to learn correspondence rules directly from data, demonstrating their approach on Slavic languages through pairwise and four-way analyses. The key innovation lies in their ability to quantify linguistic similarity across all subsets of languages simultaneously, rather than being limited to pairwise comparisons.

## Primary Contributions (as I see them)
1. **Novel framework for unbiased multilingual correspondence discovery:** The paper's most significant contribution is its principled application of MDL to discover linguistic correspondences without arbitrary distributional assumptions, enabling analysis of more than two languages simultaneously.
2. **Objective quantification of linguistic similarity across language subsets:** The approach provides a new way to measure linguistic similarity not just between pairs, but across all possible subsets of languages, revealing subtle patterns missed by traditional pairwise analyses.
3. **Practical demonstration on Slavic languages:** The paper successfully applies the method to Slavic languages, demonstrating its ability to recover expected linguistic groupings and reveal nuanced patterns in language relationships.

## Strengths

1. **Truly novel direction in computational linguistics:** The paper's core contribution is its bold approach to linguistic correspondence discovery without making assumptions about the distribution of correspondences. This is a significant departure from existing work that often imposes arbitrary constraints. The ability to analyze multiple languages simultaneously and quantify similarity across all subsets represents a genuinely new direction in the field, which is exactly what the conference should prioritize.

2. **Objective, data-driven linguistic analysis:** The paper's focus on avoiding assumptions about correspondence distributions is particularly compelling. By treating correspondences as simply associated strings of characters with no assumed underlying distribution, the authors provide an objective, unbiased approach to measuring linguistic similarity—a refreshing contrast to methods that force data into preconceived distributional models.

3. **Demonstration of nuanced linguistic insights:** The four-way analysis (Czech-Polish-Russian-Bulgarian) reveals subtle linguistic patterns that pairwise analyses would miss, such as the identification of Russian-Bulgarian similarity as being as significant as Czech-Polish. This demonstrates the practical utility of the approach and shows how it can provide richer insights into language relationships than traditional methods.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in the algorithmic approach:** While the application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery is novel, the actual algorithmic implementation (EM with two-part MDL code) builds heavily on existing work (Tatti and Vreeken, 2012). The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish its specific contribution from the broader MDL literature, which weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Lack of clear linguistic insights:** The discovered correspondences (e.g., (g,h), (cz,č), (ć,t)) are largely phonological/orthographic patterns that linguists would already recognize. The paper doesn't demonstrate how these discoveries lead to new linguistic hypotheses or explanations, which would significantly strengthen the contribution.

3. **Inadequate comparison with existing methods:** The evaluation is limited to qualitative analysis of the discovered correspondences and phylogenetic trees. There's no quantitative comparison with existing methods in terms of accuracy, scalability, or ability to discover meaningful linguistic patterns, making it difficult to assess the actual improvement over prior work.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach's ability to quantify similarity across all language subsets provide meaningful insights beyond what traditional phylogenetic methods can offer, particularly in terms of linguistic theory?

2. The paper mentions "data over-weighting" as a technique to include "near-significant" correspondences. How do you ensure this doesn't introduce bias, and what specific linguistic questions would this technique help answer?

3. The paper claims to "quantify the similarity between all subsets of our four analyzed languages" - could you elaborate on how this differs from existing approaches that also analyze multiple languages simultaneously?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to linguistic correspondence discovery that fundamentally changes how we can analyze multilingual relationships. While the algorithmic implementation builds on existing MDL work, the paper's core contribution—applying MDL to discover correspondence rules without distributional assumptions and enabling simultaneous analysis of multiple languages—is genuinely innovative and addresses a significant gap in the field. The demonstration on Slavic languages effectively shows the practical utility of the approach.

I am willing to overlook the methodological limitations (such as the lack of comprehensive comparison with existing methods) because the paper introduces a significant new conceptual direction. The ability to quantify linguistic similarity across all language subsets, rather than being limited to pairwise comparisons, represents a substantial conceptual advance.

I recommend acceptance, as this paper opens up a promising new research direction in computational linguistics that could significantly impact how we analyze language relationships. The novelty of the approach outweighs the current limitations in methodological completeness.
