

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about the distribution or shape of the correspondences. The authors claim to provide an objective, unbiased string-level measure of linguistic similarity through an efficient, deterministic algorithm. They demonstrate their approach on Slavic languages using pairwise and four-way analyses.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. An encoding scheme for linguistic correspondence discovery using MDL (though not truly novel)
2. An algorithm for inferring correspondence rules from data (based on a flawed understanding of MDL)
3. A demonstration of the approach on Slavic languages (limited in scope and potentially misleading)

## Strengths
1. The paper's conceptual framework of using MDL for linguistic correspondence discovery is interesting, even if the implementation is flawed.
2. The authors provide a detailed description of their encoding scheme and algorithm, which is valuable for understanding their approach.
3. The experimental results generally align with linguistic expectations, showing that languages from the same group have lower NCD values.

## Weaknesses

1. **Fundamental misunderstanding of MDL**: The paper conflates the MDL principle with their specific encoding scheme. The MDL principle is a general framework for model selection, but the authors present their encoding as the MDL solution without proper justification. Their statement "we employ the Minimum Description Length principle" is misleading because they've designed a specific encoding, not applied the principle correctly. MDL is not about creating a particular encoding but about finding the model that minimizes the total description length. This is a critical conceptual flaw that undermines the entire approach.

2. **Conceptual confusion between orthography and phonology**: The paper claims to discover "purely phonological rules" (e.g., (g,h)) and "purely orthographic ones" (e.g., (cz,č)), but they work exclusively with orthographic data without any phonetic transcriptions. This is a fundamental misunderstanding of linguistic correspondence discovery, which is primarily concerned with phonological patterns. By working with orthography alone, they're missing the point of their own approach and potentially discovering artifacts of orthographic conventions rather than linguistic patterns.

3. **Flawed "data over-weighting" approach**: The paper proposes "data over-weighting" to include "near-significant" correspondences by optimizing L(M) + αL(D|M) with α ≠ 1. This directly contradicts the core principle of MDL, which is designed to prevent overfitting by balancing model complexity and data description. The authors admit this "moves results away from the objective territory of MDL and introduces a user's subjective judgment," yet they present it as a valid technique. This is a major methodological flaw that invalidates their claims of objectivity.

4. **Technical errors in the model**: The paper describes a "null alignment" using "singleton rules" (rules containing exactly one symbol from exactly one language), but these are not correspondence rules between languages—they're merely descriptions of individual languages. This conceptual error undermines the entire modeling approach. Additionally, the paper states they use "the optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" but doesn't explain how they derive this or how it relates to MDL.

5. **Lack of statistical evidence**: The paper claims to "confirm that string-level similarity between languages is a strong reflection of linguistic classification" but provides no statistical evidence for this claim. They simply present a phylogenetic tree based on NCD values without testing whether the observed patterns are significantly different from random chance. The NCD values in Table 2 show patterns consistent with linguistic classification, but this is expected given the data selection (Swadesh lists are compiled to contain cognates).

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your "data over-weighting" approach align with the MDL principle, which is designed to prevent overfitting by balancing model complexity and data description?

2. How do you distinguish between phonological and orthographic correspondences in your approach, given that you're working solely with orthographic data?

3. Can you provide statistical evidence that the string-level similarities you've discovered are significantly different from random chance?

4. Why did you choose to work with orthographic data rather than phonetic transcriptions, given that linguistic correspondences are primarily phonological?

5. How do you handle the fact that your "null alignment" (using only singleton rules) is not actually an alignment between languages, but rather a description of individual languages?

## Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting concept, the fundamental misunderstanding of the MDL principle, the conceptual confusion between orthography and phonology, the flawed "data over-weighting" approach, and the technical errors in the model are substantial flaws that undermine the entire work. The paper fails to provide the rigorous methodology and clear conceptual foundation required for a computational linguistics paper. The approach described does not actually discover linguistic correspondences as claimed, and the results are likely artifacts of their specific encoding scheme rather than meaningful linguistic insights.
