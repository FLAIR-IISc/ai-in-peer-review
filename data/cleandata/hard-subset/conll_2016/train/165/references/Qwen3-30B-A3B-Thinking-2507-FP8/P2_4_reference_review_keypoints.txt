- MDL-based algorithm for discovering correspondence rules directly from data without assuming correspondence shape or distribution.
- Novel data over-weighting technique allowing linguists to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences.
- Method to quantify linguistic similarity across multiple languages simultaneously, revealing nuanced multi-way relationships.
- Principled MDL formulation that avoids arbitrary distributional assumptions and seeks the most succinct data description.
- Strong empirical demonstration on Slavic languages, producing a phylogeny aligned with linguistic classifications and meaningful correspondences.
- Practical over-weighting shows how to guide discovery toward finer-grained correspondences (e.g., Polish–Czech examples).
- Insufficient comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, leaving unclear whether this advances the field.
- Evaluation restricted to closely related Slavic languages, limiting generalizability to distant language families or different scripts.
- Lacks thorough linguistic validation tying discovered rules to known historical sound changes or resolving contradictions with linguistic knowledge.
- Incomplete analysis of computational complexity and unclear scalability beyond the reported "up to five languages."
- Question: How should the method handle cases where statistical correspondences contradict established historical linguistics?
- Question: What specific bottlenecks prevent scaling to more languages and what are the requirements for a 10-language analysis?
- Question: How was the Slavic language data selected and were there biases (overrepresentation of certain pairs or word types)?
- Overall: Novel, technically sound, and well-motivated work with valuable practical technique, but needs broader comparisons, wider evaluation, and deeper linguistic validation before acceptance.
