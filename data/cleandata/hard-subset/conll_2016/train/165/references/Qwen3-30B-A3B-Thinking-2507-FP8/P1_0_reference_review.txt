

# Peer Review: "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL (Minimum Description Length) approach for discovering systematic correspondences between multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about the shape or distribution of these correspondences. The authors argue that existing statistical approaches are limited to pairwise comparisons, impose assumptions about correspondence distributions, or cannot integrate linguistic knowledge. Their key contributions are:

1. **Novel MDL-based framework for multi-language correspondence discovery**: The paper provides a principled, statistically grounded method for identifying linguistic correspondences across multiple languages without prior assumptions about their distribution.

2. **Efficient algorithm for multi-language alignment**: The authors introduce an EM-based algorithm that learns correspondence rules deterministically and handles more than two languages simultaneously.

3. **Data over-weighting technique**: The paper proposes a method to control the granularity of discovered correspondences by strategically over-representing data, allowing linguists to select the desired level of detail.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical soundness and principled approach**: The paper makes a strong case for using MDL as the optimal framework for correspondence discovery. By avoiding arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions, the method provides objective, unbiased measures of linguistic similarity. The authors demonstrate that their approach aligns with expected linguistic classifications, which is a compelling validation of the method's theoretical soundness.

2. **Demonstrated capability for multi-language analysis**: The paper successfully demonstrates the ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously, which represents a significant advancement over existing pairwise approaches. The four-language analysis (Czech, Polish, Russian, Bulgarian) reveals nuanced linguistic relationships that would be missed by pairwise methods, such as the identification of three-way correspondences where four-way correspondences are statistically insignificant.

3. **Practical utility for linguists**: The data over-weighting technique provides a valuable tool for linguists to control the granularity of discovered correspondences. The paper shows how this technique can reveal correspondences at different levels of detail (e.g., phonological rules, orthographic rules, verb endings), which has clear practical applications in linguistic analysis.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper claims that existing methods have limitations but fails to provide a thorough comparison with recent approaches. Without quantitative comparisons (e.g., F1 scores, accuracy metrics) against established methods, it's difficult to assess the true improvement offered by this approach. The paper mentions "recent statistical approaches" but doesn't specify which ones or how this method outperforms them.

2. **Limited evaluation of the over-weighting technique**: While the paper introduces data over-weighting as a useful technique, it doesn't provide sufficient evidence of its effectiveness or potential pitfalls. The authors acknowledge that over-weighting moves results "away from the objective territory of MDL" but don't demonstrate how much this affects the quality of discovered correspondences or provide guidelines for selecting appropriate weights.

3. **Scalability concerns**: The paper states that with current implementation, the method can process up to five languages within a few hours on a modest machine. For larger language sets or more complex data, this might become impractical. The paper doesn't discuss computational complexity in detail or potential optimizations to scale the method to handle more languages.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide quantitative comparisons between your method and state-of-the-art approaches for correspondence discovery, particularly in terms of accuracy and efficiency? This would help clarify the advantages of your approach.

2. The paper mentions that over-weighting "moves results away from the objective territory of MDL." Could you provide specific examples where over-weighting led to incorrect correspondences, and how you would determine when over-weighting has gone too far?

3. What are the main computational bottlenecks in your current implementation, and what potential optimizations could be applied to scale the method to handle larger numbers of languages (e.g., 10+ languages)?

## Additional Comments

The paper's evaluation on Slavic languages is well-executed and demonstrates the method's ability to recover expected linguistic groupings. However, the paper's claim that "string-level similarity between languages is a strong reflection of linguistic classification" might be overstated, as the paper doesn't address the relationship between string-level similarity and deeper linguistic features like syntax or semantics.

The paper's focus on orthographic correspondences (without phonetic transcriptions) is appropriate for their method but limits the scope of analysis. It would be valuable to discuss how this approach might be extended to handle phonetic data or other linguistic features.

The authors have made a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics by providing a principled method for multi-language correspondence discovery. With some additional experimental comparisons and scalability analysis, this work could make a significant impact in both theoretical and applied linguistics.
