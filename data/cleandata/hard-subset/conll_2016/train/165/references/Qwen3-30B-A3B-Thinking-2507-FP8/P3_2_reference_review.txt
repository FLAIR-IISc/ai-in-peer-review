

# Peer Review: "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach to discover linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about the distribution of these correspondences. The authors argue that existing methods are limited by arbitrary assumptions, inability to integrate linguistic knowledge, or restriction to pairwise analysis. Their approach treats correspondences as simple string associations and uses a two-part MDL code to find the most succinct description of the data. They present an EM-based algorithm for learning correspondence rules and demonstrate it on Slavic languages, showing how it can be used for phylogenetic analysis and quantifying similarities across multiple language subsets.

## Main Contributions
1. **A principled MDL-based framework for multi-language correspondence discovery** that avoids arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions.
2. **An efficient, deterministic algorithm** for learning correspondence rules from data without restricting to pairwise language comparisons.
3. **Novel insights into Slavic language relationships** revealed through simultaneous analysis of multiple languages.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper correctly identifies a key limitation of existing approaches—making arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions—and properly applies MDL, which avoids this pitfall. The MDL principle is well-suited for this problem as it provides a statistically grounded way to find the most succinct description of the data without prior assumptions.

2. **Novel multi-language analysis capability:** The ability to simultaneously analyze multiple languages (as demonstrated in the four-way CS-PL-RU-BG analysis) represents a significant methodological advance. Most existing approaches are limited to pairwise comparisons, which misses subtle relationships that emerge only when analyzing multiple languages together.

3. **Practical demonstration with meaningful linguistic insights:** The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values aligns well with established linguistic classifications. The analysis reveals nuanced relationships, such as the closer similarity between Russian and Bulgarian than previously suggested by pairwise comparisons, and identifies unexpected outliers like the position of Sorbian languages.

4. **Flexible level of detail through data over-weighting:** The authors' approach to controlling the granularity of discovered correspondences via data over-weighting is particularly valuable for linguists who may want to focus on different levels of linguistic detail.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods:** The paper claims existing approaches have limitations but fails to provide a rigorous comparison with the most relevant methods. For instance, Wettig et al. (2011) also used MDL for etymological relationships, but the paper doesn't explain how their approach differs or improves upon this work. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess the true novelty of the contribution.

2. **Limited scope of evaluation:** The paper only evaluates on Slavic languages, which limits the generalizability of the claims. To demonstrate the broader applicability of the method, the authors should test it on other language families with known linguistic relationships. The current evaluation doesn't provide evidence that the approach works well beyond Slavic languages.

3. **Theoretical justification for encoding scheme:** While the paper describes their encoding scheme in detail, it lacks strong theoretical justification for why this specific encoding is optimal. The choice of LN as the universal code for integers and the specific way rules are encoded appears somewhat ad hoc without proper justification.

4. **Computational scalability concerns:** The paper notes that their current implementation can process up to five languages within a few hours on modest hardware. For larger language sets or more complex datasets, this could become computationally intractable. The paper doesn't discuss how to scale the approach to handle larger numbers of languages, which is a significant limitation for practical applications.

5. **Data over-weighting as a pragmatic workaround:** While the data over-weighting technique is useful, the paper acknowledges it "moves results away from the objective territory of MDL." The lack of a principled method to determine appropriate weight values makes this a somewhat unsatisfactory solution to the problem of discovering more detailed rules when evidence is limited.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you plan to validate the correspondence rules discovered by your algorithm against established linguistic knowledge? The paper mentions "linguistic classification" but doesn't provide a rigorous validation framework.

2. The paper claims the algorithm can provide "highly detailed quantification" of string-level similarities, but doesn't define what constitutes "highly detailed" or provide a metric for evaluating the quality of discovered correspondences.

3. The paper mentions that the algorithm is "deterministic" but doesn't discuss how sensitive it is to initialization (null alignment vs. greedy alignment). How do different initializations affect the final results?

## Conclusion

This paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing an MDL-based approach to multi-language correspondence discovery that avoids the arbitrary assumptions of existing methods. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the experimental results on Slavic languages provide meaningful linguistic insights. However, the lack of comparison with relevant state-of-the-art methods, limited evaluation scope, and computational scalability concerns weaken the paper's claims of novelty and general applicability.

The paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing with Wettig et al. (2011), expanding the evaluation to other language families, providing a more rigorous theoretical justification for the encoding scheme, and addressing computational scalability. As it stands, the paper makes an important methodological contribution but needs to better demonstrate its advantages over existing approaches. With these improvements, the paper could make a significant contribution to the field of computational linguistics.
