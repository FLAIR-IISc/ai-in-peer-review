

# Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

## Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach to discover linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without assuming the shape or distribution of these correspondences. The authors develop an efficient algorithm that uses MDL to find the most succinct description of linguistic correspondences across multiple languages, enabling simultaneous analysis of more than two languages. They demonstrate their approach on Slavic languages, showing it can identify linguistic subgroups, quantify similarities across all language subsets, and integrate linguistic expertise through data over-weighting.

## Primary Contributions
1. A novel MDL-based method for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages simultaneously, without making assumptions about correspondence distributions.
2. An approach to quantifying linguistic similarity across all subsets of analyzed languages, not just pairwise comparisons.
3. A practical mechanism (data over-weighting) for integrating linguistic expertise into the objective MDL framework, allowing control over the level of detail in discovered correspondences.

## Strengths

1. **Truly Novel Framework for Multi-Lingual Correspondence Discovery**: The paper addresses a fundamental limitation of existing approaches—most prior work focuses on pairwise language comparisons or makes strong assumptions about correspondence patterns. By using MDL to discover correspondences without distributional assumptions and extending to more than two languages simultaneously, the authors present a bold and genuinely novel approach. This represents a significant conceptual shift from traditional linguistic analysis methods.

2. **Practical Integration of Linguistic Expertise**: The data over-weighting technique is particularly innovative—it's not just a technical trick, but a thoughtful bridge between objective data analysis and linguistic expertise. This allows linguists to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences, making the approach more practically useful than purely objective methods. This is a rare example of a technical method that directly addresses a real-world need in linguistic research.

3. **Demonstrated Utility on a Meaningful Linguistic Problem**: The experiments on Slavic languages produce results that align with established linguistic classifications, showing the approach can correctly identify linguistic subgroups and quantify similarities in ways that match linguistic knowledge. The four-way analysis revealing subtle relationships between Russian and other Slavic languages (e.g., the strong similarity between Russian and Bulgarian) demonstrates the value of analyzing multiple languages simultaneously.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Differentiation from Prior MDL Work**: The authors cite Wettig et al. (2011), who also used MDL for modeling changes in etymologically related words, but don't sufficiently distinguish their approach from this prior work. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how their multi-language extension differs from or improves upon existing MDL applications to linguistic correspondence discovery.

2. **Undermined Claim of Objectivity**: The paper claims to provide "objective, unbiased string-level measures of linguistic similarity," yet the data over-weighting technique explicitly introduces subjectivity by allowing users to assign different weights to data points. This contradiction weakens the paper's theoretical foundation and claim of objectivity.

3. **Limited Evaluation Against State-of-the-Art**: While the paper demonstrates the approach's utility on Slavic languages, it doesn't compare performance against existing methods in a rigorous way. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the proposed method represents a significant improvement over the current state of the art.

4. **Computational Scalability Concerns**: The paper mentions that processing five languages takes "a few hours on a 4GB RAM, 2.5GHz single core desktop machine," but doesn't discuss how this scales with more languages or larger datasets. For a method that's supposed to handle multiple languages, this is a significant omission that affects practical applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your method specifically differ from the MDL approach used by Wettig et al. (2011), and why was their approach limited to pairwise comparisons?

2. Could you elaborate on how your data over-weighting technique avoids the pitfalls of overfitting, given that you're explicitly moving away from the objective MDL framework?

3. Have you considered extending your approach to handle more than five languages, and if so, what computational challenges would you anticipate?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to linguistic correspondence discovery with significant potential impact. The multi-language analysis capability and the data over-weighting technique represent genuinely innovative contributions that address important gaps in the literature. While the paper has some methodological weaknesses, the novelty and potential utility of the approach outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly the need to better differentiate from prior work and clarify the relationship between the objective MDL framework and the data over-weighting technique.

The paper exemplifies the kind of bold, novel thinking that advances the field—moving beyond incremental improvements to propose a fundamentally new approach to a well-established problem. This aligns perfectly with the conference's values of prioritizing significant new concepts over methodological perfection.
