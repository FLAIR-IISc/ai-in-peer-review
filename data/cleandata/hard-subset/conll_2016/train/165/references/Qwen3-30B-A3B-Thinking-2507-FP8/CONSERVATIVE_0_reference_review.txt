

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach to discover string-level linguistic correspondences across multiple languages without making assumptions about the distribution or shape of these correspondences. The authors present an algorithm that uses Expectation-Maximization to infer correspondence rules from cognate data, with a focus on Slavic languages. They claim their method can handle more than two languages simultaneously, unlike existing approaches, and introduce a data over-weighting technique to include "near-significant" correspondences.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A technical implementation of MDL for linguistic correspondence discovery
2. A four-way analysis of Slavic languages (Czech, Polish, Russian, Bulgarian)
3. A data over-weighting technique to handle insufficient statistical evidence

## Strengths
1. The paper correctly identifies a limitation of existing approaches: many statistical methods impose arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions. MDL provides a theoretically sound alternative for model selection without such assumptions.

2. The detailed technical description of the MDL-based algorithm and its implementation is thorough, including the encoding schemes for rules, alphabets, and data.

3. The four-way analysis demonstrates the method's ability to quantify similarities across multiple languages simultaneously, which is a genuine advancement over pairwise methods.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of comparison with established baselines**: The paper makes strong claims about avoiding "arbitrary assumptions" but fails to compare its results against established methods for correspondence discovery (e.g., the approaches by Wettig et al., 2011; Snyder and Barzilay, 2008). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the authors' approach actually improves upon existing methods or simply produces different results. The paper claims to "avoid finding the closest similes from the assumed distribution" but doesn't provide evidence that their results are linguistically more accurate than those from methods that make assumptions.

2. **Methodological inconsistency with MDL principles**: The authors introduce a "data over-weighting" technique (Section 4.5) that explicitly moves away from the objective MDL framework they claim to follow. By assigning weights to data entries or using α ≠ 1 in the optimization, they introduce subjective bias. The paper admits this "moves results away from the objective territory of MDL and introduces a user's subjective judgment." This undermines the core claim of the paper and makes the method less principled than advertised.

3. **Insufficient validation of linguistic relevance**: The paper presents example correspondences but fails to validate them against linguistic knowledge. For instance, they claim to discover "purely phonological rules such as (g,h)" but provide no evidence that these correspondences are linguistically meaningful. A proper validation would require comparing their discovered rules against established linguistic knowledge, not just showing that the algorithm can produce some patterns.

4. **Limited scope and generalizability**: The experiments are restricted to Slavic languages, with no evidence of how the method would perform on other language families. The four-way analysis is impressive for the specific data set but doesn't demonstrate scalability or general applicability. The paper mentions they can handle up to five languages, but with significant computational constraints (processing "up to five languages... within a few hours on a 4GB RAM, 2.5GHz single core desktop machine").

5. **Lack of error analysis**: The paper provides no discussion of cases where the algorithm fails to discover meaningful correspondences or produces linguistically implausible results. A proper evaluation would include such an analysis to understand the method's limitations.

## Questions to Authors
1. How do you justify the claim that your method "avoids arbitrary assumptions" when the MDL principle itself makes assumptions about the nature of good models (through the description length metric)?

2. Can you provide specific examples where your method discovers linguistically meaningful correspondences that existing methods fail to capture, with evidence that these correspondences are indeed better?

3. How would you respond to the criticism that your data over-weighting technique contradicts the MDL principle and introduces subjectivity into what was supposed to be an objective method?

## Overall Assessment
This paper presents an interesting application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery, but it fails to meet the standards of rigorous validation expected in computational linguistics. The lack of comparison with established methods, the inconsistent application of MDL principles through the over-weighting technique, and the absence of linguistic validation of results are serious weaknesses. While the four-way analysis is a useful demonstration, it doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support the paper's broader claims about the superiority of their approach. The method appears to be a technical implementation of MDL rather than a genuinely novel contribution that advances the state of the art.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it included:
1. Comparisons with established baselines
2. Linguistic validation of discovered correspondences
3. A clear demonstration of how their method improves upon existing approaches
4. Discussion of the limitations of their approach

As it stands, the paper makes an interesting technical contribution but fails to provide the necessary evidence to support its claims of superiority over established methods. I cannot recommend acceptance in its current form.
