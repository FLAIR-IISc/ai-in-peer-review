- Summary: Proposes an MDL-based method to discover string-level correspondences across multiple languages without restrictive assumptions, with experiments on Slavic languages.

- Contribution: Principled application of MDL for multi-language correspondence discovery that avoids restrictive correspondence-pattern assumptions.

- Contribution: Efficient algorithm that learns correspondence rules across more than two languages simultaneously.

- Contribution: Introduces shared Description Length (sDL) as a novel metric to quantify similarity across language subsets.

- Strength: The MDL approach is theoretically well-founded, balancing model complexity and data fit better than ad-hoc statistical methods.

- Strength: Results produce linguistically meaningful groupings (e.g., Bulgarian/Macedonian outliers, Polish digraph behavior), suggesting real linguistic signal capture.

- Strength: Multi-way analyses (e.g., four-way) reveal subtleties missed by pairwise methods, showing the value of sDL for multi-language similarity.

- Weakness: No rigorous comparison to relevant prior work (e.g., Wettig et al. 2011) or recent multi-language methods, weakening claims of novelty.

- Weakness: Uses "data over-weighting" as an ad-hoc fix for weak evidence, which departs from the MDL framework and lacks a principled weighting method.

- Weakness: Lacks theoretical analysis of the EM-based algorithm (convergence, optimality, sensitivity to initialization) and formal complexity bounds.

- Question: How does this method specifically improve on Wettig et al. (2011) and what are the methodological/result differences?

- Question: What are the algorithm's theoretical time/space complexities and expected scaling limits as the number of languages increases?

- Question: Can the authors provide concrete linguistic examples (not just statistical patterns) illustrating the asserted regularities across languages?

- Overall: Promising and linguistically valid MDL-based contribution, but requires stronger comparisons and theoretical justification; recommend acceptance with major revisions.
