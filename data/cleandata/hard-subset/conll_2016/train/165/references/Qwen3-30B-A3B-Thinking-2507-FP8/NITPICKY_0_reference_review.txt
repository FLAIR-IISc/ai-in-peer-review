

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about the distribution or shape of these correspondences. The authors present a deterministic algorithm that infers correspondence rules directly from data and evaluate it on Slavic languages, demonstrating its application for phylogenetic analysis and quantification of string-level similarities among multiple languages.

## Main Contributions
1. An MDL-based approach for discovering correspondence rules without assumptions about their distribution
2. An efficient algorithm for inferring correspondence rules using MDL
3. Demonstration of the approach's utility on Slavic languages for multi-language analysis

## Strengths
1. The theoretical foundation using MDL is sound and well-motivated, avoiding the arbitrary assumptions made by many existing statistical approaches for correspondence discovery.

2. The paper provides a clear, well-structured description of the algorithm and its implementation details, including the MDL code design and alignment procedure.

3. The experimental evaluation on Slavic languages is comprehensive, showing phylogenetic analysis and quantification of linguistic similarities across multiple language groups.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent Claims About Scalability and Scope**: The paper repeatedly claims to be "not limited to pairs of languages" while only demonstrating the approach with four languages at a time. The abstract states "quantification of string-level similarities among more than two languages," but Section 5.3 shows only four-language analysis. There's no evidence of how the algorithm handles five or more languages, nor is there any discussion of computational complexity scaling with the number of languages. The claim "our algorithm is agnostic to the number of input languages" is not substantiated by any evidence beyond the four-language case.

2. **Technical Inconsistencies in Algorithm Description**: 
   - The paper claims to be "deterministic" but Section 4.4 states: "It is possible that a rule R is deemed good on the basis of the entire data set when in fact it is suboptimal for some subset S of the data." This directly contradicts the claim of determinism.
   - The time complexity claims in Section 4.4 appear inconsistent. For the E-step, the paper claims O(|D| · |R|²) complexity, but the description of the expectation step doesn't seem to require O(|R|²) per data point. The justification for this complexity is unclear.

3. **Inadequate Justification for Methodological Choices**: 
   - The paper doesn't provide a clear comparison to existing methods for multiple language correspondence discovery, making it difficult to assess the novelty or superiority of their approach.
   - The "data over-weighting" technique (Section 4.5) is introduced without clear justification for why it's necessary or how it affects the statistical validity of the results. The paper states this "moves results away from the objective territory of MDL and introduces a user's subjective judgment," yet it's presented as a useful technique without clear guidelines for its proper application.

4. **Formatting and Reference Inconsistencies**: 
   - References are inconsistently formatted (e.g., "Szemerenyi, 1970" vs. "Szemerenyi1970").
   - The bibliography contains incomplete entries (e.g., "EuroComSlav Basiskurs - der panslavische Wortschatz, A. Angelov, Angelov.,? \Q2004\E, Angelov.," which appears to have formatting errors).
   - Inconsistent use of spaces and punctuation (e.g., "mutual intelligibility. Beyond that" should have a space after the period).

5. **Lack of Clarity in Experimental Results**: 
   - The paper claims to "quantify the similarity between all subsets of our four analyzed languages" but doesn't provide meaningful linguistic insights from these quantifications. The sDL analysis in Figures 2-3 is presented without clear interpretation of what these values reveal about the linguistic relationships.
   - The "over-weighted" alignments in Table 3 are marked with asterisks but without explanation of how the weights were determined or why these particular alignments were chosen.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does the algorithm scale to more than four languages? Have you tested it with five or more languages, and if so, what were the results and computational requirements?
2. Can you clarify the apparent contradiction between claiming the algorithm is deterministic and describing cases where it might miss certain rules due to data subset issues?
3. The paper states that "data over-weighting... introduces a user's subjective judgment." What specific guidelines would you recommend for linguists to determine appropriate weighting factors for different language pairs or subsets?

## Overall Assessment
While the paper presents an interesting application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery, the substantial technical inconsistencies, unsubstantiated claims about scalability, and formatting issues significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The algorithm description contains contradictions and lacks sufficient detail to assess its validity and novelty. The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive for the four-language case, fails to demonstrate the claimed advantages over existing methods for multiple-language analysis.

The paper's technical flaws and inconsistencies outweigh its theoretical interest, making it unsuitable for publication at CONLL without significant revisions. I recommend rejection.
