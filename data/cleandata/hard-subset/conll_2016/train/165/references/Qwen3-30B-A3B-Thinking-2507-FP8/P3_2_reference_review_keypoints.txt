- Proposes an MDL-based method to discover string-level correspondences across multiple languages without assuming correspondence distributions.  
- Claims a principled MDL framework for multi-language correspondence discovery that avoids arbitrary assumptions.  
- Presents an efficient, deterministic EM-based algorithm for learning correspondence rules without restricting to pairwise comparisons.  
- Demonstrates the method on Slavic languages and derives insights for phylogenetics and multi-language similarity quantification.  
- Strong theoretical foundation: MDL is well-suited to avoid prior assumptions and find succinct data descriptions.  
- Novel capability to analyze multiple languages simultaneously, revealing relationships missed by pairwise methods.  
- Practical demonstration yields linguistically meaningful results, e.g., NCD-based phylogeny aligning with classifications and nuanced Slavic relationships.  
- Offers control over granularity via data over-weighting, useful for focusing on different linguistic detail levels.  
- Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods, notably lacking detailed contrast with Wettig et al. (2011).  
- Evaluation limited to Slavic languages, reducing evidence for generalizability to other families.  
- Encoding scheme lacks strong theoretical justification; choices (e.g., LN code, rule encoding) seem ad hoc.  
- Computational scalability concerns: current implementation handles up to five languages in hours, unclear how to scale to larger sets.  
- Data over-weighting is a pragmatic but unprincipled workaround that departs from MDL’s objective framework and lacks guidance for weight selection.  
- Question: How will discovered correspondence rules be validated against established linguistic knowledge?  
- Question: What precisely constitutes "highly detailed" quantification and how will correspondence quality be measured?  
- Question: How sensitive is the deterministic algorithm to initialization choices (null vs. greedy alignment)?  
- Conclusion: Important methodological contribution with strong foundation and meaningful Slavic results, but needs comparisons, broader evaluation, encoding justification, and scalability solutions to strengthen claims.
