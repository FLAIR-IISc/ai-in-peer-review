

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach to discover linguistic correspondences across multiple languages without assuming a prior distribution for those correspondences. The authors claim their method can simultaneously analyze more than two languages and quantify similarities between all language subsets, rather than just pairwise comparisons. They demonstrate their approach on Slavic languages using Swadesh lists and a set of pan-Slavic words, showing phylogenetic trees and example correspondences.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Application of MDL to multi-language linguistic correspondence discovery (with significant overlap with prior work)
2. Algorithmic implementation of a deterministic EM-based approach for learning correspondence rules
3. Demonstration of linguistic similarity quantification across language subsets

## Strengths
1. The paper provides a clear algorithmic description of their approach and demonstrates it on a linguistically meaningful task.
2. The phylogenetic tree they construct aligns with established linguistic classifications, which is a positive validation of their approach's ability to capture expected linguistic patterns.
3. The paper acknowledges the limitations of their method, particularly regarding the "data over-weighting" technique (Section 4.5).

## Weaknesses

### 1. Lack of clear novelty and insufficient comparison with established baselines
The paper claims to be the first to apply MDL to multi-language correspondence discovery "without prior assumptions about their shape or distribution." However, they cite Wettig et al. (2011) who already used MDL for modeling changes in etymologically related words. The paper fails to demonstrate how their approach is substantially different or superior to existing methods for linguistic correspondence discovery, particularly those by Snyder et al. (2010) and Schulz et al. (2004), which they cite as related work but do not compare against. Without rigorous comparison to established baselines, it's impossible to assess whether their method actually provides meaningful improvements.

### 2. The "data over-weighting" technique undermines the MDL principle
Section 4.5 introduces "data over-weighting" as a way to include correspondence rules that are "almost statistically significant." The authors explicitly acknowledge this moves results "away from the objective territory of MDL and introduces a user's subjective judgment." This is a critical weakness because it fundamentally contradicts the theoretical foundation of their approach. The paper claims to provide "objective, unbiased string-level measures of linguistic similarity," but this technique introduces subjectivity, undermining their core claim.

### 3. Insufficient evidence for the claimed advantage of multi-language analysis
The paper claims their approach "can be used for efficient, highly detailed quantification of string-level similarities among more than two languages," but fails to demonstrate how this is superior to existing pairwise approaches. The four-language analysis (Section 5.3) simply shows alignments that could be constructed by combining pairwise analyses. The paper doesn't provide evidence that their multi-language approach yields different or more accurate results than standard pairwise methods. Without such evidence, the claimed advantage remains unsubstantiated.

### 4. Limited theoretical contribution
The paper builds upon established MDL theory and existing linguistic methods without making significant theoretical advances. The algorithmic details (Section 4) are an extension of prior work on MDL-based pattern discovery (Tatti and Vreeken, 2012), but the paper doesn't clearly articulate how this extension is novel or particularly valuable for linguistic correspondence discovery.

### 5. Inadequate discussion of limitations
The paper acknowledges computational constraints (Section 4.3), but doesn't discuss how these limitations affect the practical applicability of their method. They state they can process "up to five languages" but don't compare this to existing methods' scalability. The paper also fails to address how their approach handles languages with different orthographic systems (e.g., Latin vs. Cyrillic scripts), which is a significant challenge in multi-language linguistic analysis.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your method provide advantages over existing pairwise approaches when analyzing four languages simultaneously? Can you provide evidence that your multi-language analysis yields different or more accurate results than combining pairwise analyses?
2. Given that "data over-weighting" undermines the MDL principle, why do you recommend using this technique? What evidence do you have that the resulting correspondences are linguistically meaningful rather than artifacts of the weighting procedure?
3. How do you plan to handle languages with different writing systems (e.g., Latin vs. Cyrillic scripts) in your framework, and how does this affect the validity of your correspondences?

## Recommendation
This paper presents an incremental adaptation of existing MDL techniques to a linguistic problem, but lacks sufficient evidence to demonstrate meaningful novelty or advantages over established methods. The claimed contribution of multi-language correspondence discovery is not substantiated with rigorous comparisons to baselines or evidence of superior performance. The "data over-weighting" technique further undermines the theoretical foundation of the approach. I recommend rejection.

The paper would benefit from a thorough comparison with established baselines, clearer demonstration of how their multi-language approach provides advantages over pairwise methods, and a more rigorous discussion of how to handle the limitations they acknowledge. Without these, the contribution remains insufficient for publication at CoNLL.
