

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about the distribution of correspondences. The authors argue that existing statistical approaches for discovering linguistic correspondences suffer from limitations such as imposing assumptions about correspondence distributions, not allowing integration of linguistic knowledge, or being limited to pairs of languages. They present a deterministic algorithm that infers correspondence rules directly from data using the Minimum Description Length principle.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:

1. A novel application of MDL to discover linguistic correspondences across multiple languages without arbitrary assumptions about the distribution of correspondences
2. An empirical demonstration of the approach on Slavic languages, showing it can identify linguistic subgroups and quantify similarities between language subsets
3. An efficient algorithm for inferring correspondence rules deterministically from data

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and clear problem statement**: The paper effectively articulates the limitations of existing approaches to linguistic correspondence discovery (arbitrary assumptions, limited to pairwise analysis) and makes a compelling case for why MDL is a suitable principle for addressing these limitations. The authors clearly explain how MDL provides a statistically well-founded approach that avoids making assumptions about correspondence distributions, which is a significant advantage over existing methods.

2. **Thorough empirical evaluation**: The paper provides a comprehensive evaluation on a substantial dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The pairwise analysis successfully constructs a phylogenetic tree that aligns with linguistic classifications, while the four-way analysis (Czech-Polish-Russian-Bulgarian) demonstrates the ability to identify subtle linguistic similarities that would be missed in pairwise analyses. The use of NCD to compute pairwise distances and the detailed analysis of shared description lengths provide strong empirical evidence for the approach's effectiveness.

3. **Novel handling of multiple languages simultaneously**: The paper successfully demonstrates how their approach can be used to analyze more than two languages at once, providing a more nuanced understanding of linguistic similarities than pairwise analysis alone. The four-way analysis examples (Figure 4) reveal how the algorithm can identify correspondence rules that link only two or three languages, with the other language(s) described by separate patterns. This capability represents a significant advance over existing methods limited to pairwise comparisons.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: While the paper mentions existing statistical approaches, it fails to provide a direct comparison with the current state-of-the-art methods for discovering linguistic correspondences. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to evaluate whether the proposed approach actually outperforms existing methods or simply provides an alternative approach with similar performance. The authors should have included a thorough comparison with at least one strong baseline method to validate their claims.

2. **Limited theoretical analysis**: The paper presents the MDL-based approach and the algorithm but lacks a thorough theoretical analysis of the algorithm's properties. There's no discussion of the convergence properties of the EM-based algorithm, no formal complexity analysis of the algorithm, and no discussion of the trade-offs between different aspects of the approach. The paper mentions computational bottlenecks but doesn't provide a clear understanding of how the algorithm scales with the number of languages or the size of the dataset.

3. **Questionable novelty of the core algorithm**: While the application to linguistic correspondences is novel, the core algorithmic approach appears to be a straightforward adaptation of the Tatti and Vreeken (2012) method for discovering serial episodes. The paper doesn't sufficiently articulate how their approach differs from or improves upon this prior work. The authors could have better explained the specific adaptations required to apply this method to linguistic correspondence discovery and why these adaptations are necessary.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare in accuracy and efficiency to existing state-of-the-art methods for discovering linguistic correspondences? Could you provide a direct comparison with at least one strong baseline method?

2. The paper states that "with our current implementation, we can process up to five languages from our most complicated data set within a few hours." What is the theoretical time complexity of your algorithm, and how does it scale with the number of languages? What are the main bottlenecks that limit the number of languages you can handle?

3. The paper mentions "data over-weighting" as a technique to include more detailed rules. How does this technique affect the statistical significance of the discovered correspondences, and what criteria should linguists use to determine the appropriate level of over-weighting?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated and empirically validated approach to discovering linguistic correspondences across multiple languages using MDL. The method successfully identifies linguistic subgroups and quantifies similarities between language subsets in a way that goes beyond pairwise analyses. However, the lack of comparison to existing methods and limited theoretical analysis prevent me from fully endorsing the paper as a significant contribution to the field. The authors have demonstrated a useful application of MDL to a meaningful problem, but the paper would benefit from a stronger comparison to existing work and a more thorough theoretical analysis of the algorithm.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses identified above, particularly regarding comparisons with existing methods and theoretical analysis of the algorithm. The paper has strong empirical results that demonstrate the value of the approach, but it needs to better position itself within the existing literature to establish its novelty and significance.
