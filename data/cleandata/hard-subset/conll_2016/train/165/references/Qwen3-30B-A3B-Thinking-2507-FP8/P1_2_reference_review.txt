

# Peer Review: "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about correspondence distributions. The authors present an efficient algorithm that infers correspondence rules directly from data, enabling analysis of more than two languages simultaneously. The main contributions are:

1. A principled MDL framework for discovering linguistic correspondences without imposing arbitrary assumptions about their shape or distribution
2. An algorithm that quantifies linguistic similarity across all subsets of analyzed languages (not just pairwise)
3. Empirical validation showing the method successfully identifies linguistic subgroups while quantifying similarities in Slavic languages

## Strengths

1. **Novel methodological approach addressing a clear gap**: The paper tackles a fundamental limitation in existing work on linguistic correspondences - most methods are limited to pairwise comparisons or make arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions. The MDL approach is well-suited to this problem as it provides an objective, statistically grounded method to find the most concise description of the data. This is a significant contribution to computational linguistics and historical linguistics.

2. **Strong empirical validation with linguistic alignment**: The experiments on Slavic languages produce phylogenetic trees that align with established linguistic classifications. The paper demonstrates that their approach identifies expected linguistic groupings (west, east, and south Slavic) and even reveals subtle relationships (e.g., the similarity between Russian and Bulgarian). The results are convincing and provide concrete evidence of the method's utility.

3. **Quantification of similarity across all language subsets**: This is the most significant contribution of the paper. Unlike previous work limited to pairwise comparisons, the authors' method allows for detailed information-theoretic quantification of similarity across all language subsets simultaneously. Figure 3 in particular provides a nuanced understanding of linguistic relationships that would be impossible with pairwise methods.

4. **Technical rigor and reproducibility**: The paper provides sufficient technical details about the MDL code design, alignment procedure, and training process. The authors clearly explain their implementation choices and provide justification for their approach to encoding correspondence rules. This level of detail makes the work reproducible and practical for other researchers.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of evaluation**: While the Slavic language experiments are well-executed, the paper lacks evaluation on other language families. This limits the generalizability of the claims about the method's effectiveness. The authors should demonstrate their approach on at least one non-Slavic language family to strengthen their claims.

2. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper claims to overcome limitations of existing statistical approaches but fails to provide direct comparisons with other methods that also aim to discover correspondences between multiple languages. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess the relative performance and advantages of their approach.

3. **Ambiguity around data over-weighting**: Section 4.5 introduces data over-weighting as a way to include "almost significant" correspondences, but this contradicts the paper's claim of providing "objective results." The paper doesn't sufficiently explain how to choose the appropriate weight parameter, making the method less objective in practice. This is a significant methodological weakness.

4. **Insufficient discussion of computational complexity**: The authors mention they can process up to five languages on a desktop machine but provide no analysis of how computational complexity scales with the number of languages. For a method that aims to handle "more than two languages," this is a critical gap that needs addressing for practical applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your method compare to recent neural network-based approaches for multi-language correspondence discovery, particularly those that handle multiple languages simultaneously?

2. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how computational complexity scales with the number of languages and the size of the dataset? This would be crucial for assessing the method's practical applicability.

3. The paper claims to provide "objective results" but the data over-weighting technique introduces subjectivity. How should linguists choose the appropriate weight parameter to maintain objectivity while still capturing subtle correspondences?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound approach to discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages. The method addresses a genuine limitation in existing work and provides a valuable tool for linguists to quantify linguistic similarities across language subsets. While the empirical validation on Slavic languages is strong, the paper would benefit from broader evaluation across language families, more direct comparisons with existing methods, and clarification of the data over-weighting technique.

The main contribution - the ability to quantitatively analyze linguistic similarities across all language subsets simultaneously - is significant and represents a meaningful advancement in the field. With the suggested improvements, this paper would make a strong contribution to the computational linguistics and historical linguistics communities. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
