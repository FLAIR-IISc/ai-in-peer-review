

# Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

## Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without imposing arbitrary assumptions about their distribution or shape. The authors develop an efficient, deterministic algorithm to infer correspondence rules directly from data, enabling simultaneous analysis of more than two languages. The paper demonstrates the approach on Slavic languages, showing how it can quantify string-level similarities across multiple language subsets and reveal linguistic subgroups.

## Main Contributions (ordered by strength)
1. An MDL-based framework for discovering linguistic correspondences that avoids arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions.
2. An efficient, deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules from data that can handle more than two languages simultaneously.
3. Empirical validation showing the approach can quantitatively reveal linguistic relationships and subgroups across multiple languages.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness of MDL Application**: The paper provides a well-structured and technically rigorous application of the MDL principle to linguistic correspondence discovery. The authors carefully justify their coding scheme (universal code for integers, Shannon-optimal codes) and demonstrate how their approach avoids the limitations of previous statistical methods that impose arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions. The two-part MDL formulation is properly implemented and theoretically grounded.

2. **Novelty in Multi-language Analysis**: The paper's most significant contribution is its ability to handle multiple languages simultaneously without pairwise constraints. Previous approaches are limited to pairs of languages or make strong assumptions about correspondence patterns. The four-way analysis in Section 5.3 demonstrates how the method reveals nuanced similarities that pairwise analyses would miss, such as identifying that Bulgarian and Russian share significant linguistic structure despite being in different Slavic subgroups.

3. **Linguistically Meaningful Results**: The empirical results align with established linguistic classifications. The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values matches expected linguistic groupings (e.g., grouping Czech and Polish as West Slavic, Bulgarian and Macedonian as South Slavic), and the discovered correspondences include linguistically meaningful patterns (e.g., phonological rules like (g,h), orthographic patterns like (cz,č), and verb endings like (ć,t)). The four-way analysis reveals subtle relationships that would be missed by pairwise approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Experimental Scope**: The paper only demonstrates the approach on Slavic languages, which are known to have strong historical relationships. While the results are consistent with linguistic knowledge, the approach's generalizability to more diverse language families (where linguistic relationships are less clear) is not demonstrated. A more convincing evaluation would include experiments on non-Indo-European languages or languages with weaker historical connections.

2. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper mentions previous work but doesn't provide a thorough comparison with existing methods on the same datasets. Without quantitative comparison of results (e.g., accuracy, F1 scores), it's difficult to assess how much better the proposed approach performs relative to alternatives. The claim that their method "avoids finding the closest similes from the assumed distribution" lacks empirical validation.

3. **Ambiguity in Data Over-Weighting Technique**: The paper introduces "data over-weighting" to handle insufficient statistical evidence for certain rules, but doesn't provide a clear methodology for determining when and how much to over-weight data. This technique, while potentially useful for linguists, introduces subjectivity that contradicts the paper's claim of providing "objective" results. The paper would be stronger with specific guidelines for when and how to apply this technique.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper mentions that the current implementation can process up to five languages within a few hours on modest hardware. What is the computational complexity of the algorithm as the number of languages increases beyond five, and how would the authors address scalability for larger language sets?

2. In the four-way analysis, the paper identifies that some rules link only two or three languages. What specific statistical criteria determine when a rule is considered "statistically significant" for a subset of languages versus being rejected as insufficiently supported? How does the algorithm handle borderline cases?

3. The paper claims the algorithm is "deterministic" but mentions that non-determinism could be integrated via techniques like simulated annealing. How would the introduction of non-determinism affect the objective nature of the results, and under what circumstances would a researcher choose to use the deterministic versus non-deterministic version?

## Additional Comments
The paper is well-written with clear technical explanations and effective use of tables and figures to present results. The theoretical foundation in MDL is appropriately applied, and the approach fills a genuine gap in the literature for multi-language correspondence discovery. The work has clear potential for linguistic applications, particularly for linguists seeking objective methods to quantify language similarities.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution by providing a theoretically sound, assumption-free method for discovering linguistic correspondences across multiple languages. While the experimental scope could be expanded, the methodological innovation and linguistic validity of the results represent a solid contribution to computational linguistics.
