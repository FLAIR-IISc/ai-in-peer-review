

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about the shape or distribution of those correspondences. The authors claim three main contributions:

1. An MDL-based algorithm for discovering correspondence rules directly from data without imposing arbitrary assumptions
2. A novel data over-weighting technique to allow linguists to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences
3. A demonstration of how the method can quantify linguistic similarity across multiple languages simultaneously, revealing nuanced relationships beyond pairwise analyses

## Strengths

**1. Novel application of MDL to multilingual correspondence discovery.** The paper makes a strong case for using MDL as a principled approach to linguistic correspondence discovery. Unlike previous methods that impose assumptions about correspondence distributions, MDL provides a statistically grounded framework for identifying the most succinct description of the data. The authors clearly articulate how this avoids the pitfalls of existing approaches, particularly the issue of finding "closest similes from the assumed distribution" rather than the "true" correspondences. This is a significant methodological contribution.

**2. Effective demonstration on Slavic languages.** The paper provides compelling empirical validation of the approach through detailed analyses of Slavic languages. The pairwise NCD analysis produces a phylogenetic tree that aligns well with established linguistic classifications, and the four-way analysis (Czech-Polish-Russian-Bulgarian) successfully identifies linguistic subgroups while quantifying similarities across all subsets of languages. The example correspondences (e.g., (cz,č), (g,h), (eć,et)) demonstrate the approach's ability to uncover linguistically meaningful patterns at different granularities.

**3. Practical data over-weighting technique.** The paper's introduction of data over-weighting as a way to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences is particularly valuable. This bridges the gap between objective statistical analysis and linguistic expertise, allowing linguists to guide the discovery process toward the level of detail they require. The experimental results showing how over-weighting leads to more detailed correspondences (e.g., from "zem" to "ziem" in the Polish-Czech example) demonstrate the practical utility of this technique.

## Weaknesses

**1. Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods.** While the paper claims existing methods have limitations, it doesn't provide a thorough comparison with recent approaches that may have addressed these issues. For instance, the paper cites Wettig et al. (2011) and Snyder et al. (2010) but doesn't directly compare their results with the proposed MDL approach. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the proposed method truly advances the state of the art.

**2. Limited scope of evaluation.** The experiments are restricted to Slavic languages, which are relatively closely related. The paper doesn't demonstrate how the method would perform on more distantly related language families or languages with different writing systems (e.g., Chinese, Arabic, or languages with very different phonological structures). The lack of evaluation on non-Slavic languages limits the generalizability of the claims.

**3. Ambiguous implementation details for linguistic validity.** While the paper presents example correspondences, it lacks a thorough linguistic validation of these findings. It doesn't discuss whether the discovered rules align with known historical sound changes in Slavic languages or how the method would handle cases where linguistic knowledge contradicts the statistical findings. Without this, it's difficult to assess the linguistic validity of the approach.

**4. Incomplete computational complexity analysis.** The paper mentions that their implementation handles up to five languages within a few hours on modest hardware but doesn't provide a comprehensive analysis of how the algorithm's complexity scales with the number of languages or data size. The stated time complexities (O(|D|·|R|²) and O(|D|·A²/2)) are somewhat vague without concrete evidence of how these scale in practice for larger datasets.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach handle cases where linguistic knowledge contradicts the statistical findings (e.g., where a statistically significant correspondence doesn't align with established historical linguistics)? What safeguards would you recommend for linguists using this method?

2. The paper states that the algorithm can process "up to five languages" within a few hours. What specific bottlenecks prevent scaling to more languages, and what would be the computational requirements for a 10-language analysis?

3. Could you provide more details about how you selected the Slavic language data? Were there any biases in your data collection process that might affect the results, such as overrepresentation of certain language pairs or word types?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a novel and well-motivated application of the MDL principle to multilingual correspondence discovery. The method has clear theoretical advantages over existing approaches, and the empirical results demonstrate its effectiveness on Slavic languages. The data over-weighting technique is particularly valuable for practical applications. However, the limited scope of evaluation, insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods, and lack of thorough linguistic validation prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. With additional comparisons to recent methods, broader language coverage in experiments, and more detailed linguistic validation, this paper could make a significant contribution to the field.

The paper is technically sound, well-written, and addresses an important problem in computational linguistics. With the suggested improvements, it would be a strong candidate for publication.
