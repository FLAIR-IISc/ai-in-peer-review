

# Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

## Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions. The authors claim their method can handle more than two languages simultaneously, unlike many existing approaches. They demonstrate their method on Slavic languages using Swadesh lists and a set of Slavic cognates, presenting phylogenetic trees and example correspondences.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery (though not novel as Wettig et al. 2011 already used MDL)
2. A deterministic algorithm for discovering correspondence rules (though not truly deterministic)
3. Four-way analysis of Slavic languages (limited in scope and dataset size)

## Strengths
1. The paper correctly identifies a gap in existing approaches to linguistic correspondence discovery, particularly the limitation of most methods to pairwise language comparisons.
2. The MDL framework is theoretically sound and well-suited for model selection problems.
3. The phylogenetic tree they produce aligns with established linguistic classifications, which provides some validation of their approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with established baselines**: The paper fails to compare its MDL approach with existing statistical methods (Schulz et al., 2004; Snyder et al., 2010; Wettig et al., 2011), making it impossible to determine if their method actually improves upon current approaches. The authors cite Wettig et al. (2011) as having used MDL for linguistic analysis but don't clarify how their approach differs or improves upon this prior work. Without this comparison, the claimed novelty and advantages of their method remain unsubstantiated.

2. **Overstated novelty and methodological issues**: The paper claims to avoid "arbitrary assumptions about the shape or distribution" of correspondences, but MDL itself is a statistical method that makes assumptions about data encoding. More critically, the algorithm uses Expectation-Maximization, which is inherently non-deterministic (it can converge to local optima), contradicting the paper's claim of a "deterministic algorithm." The paper doesn't discuss convergence properties or sensitivity to initialization, which are crucial for such an approach.

3. **Insufficient validation and limited scope**: The experiments are restricted to a single language family (Slavic) with very small datasets (778 entries for the four-way analysis). This is insufficient to validate the approach across diverse linguistic phenomena or language families. The paper claims to "quantify similarity between all subsets of analyzed languages," but doesn't provide clear metrics or evidence for this claim. The "data over-weighting" technique (Section 4.5) further undermines the objectivity claim, as it introduces subjective user choices to force the discovery of more rules.

4. **Lack of linguistic validation**: The paper makes no effort to involve linguists in validating the discovered correspondence rules. Without linguistic expertise, it's impossible to determine if the discovered rules are meaningful from a linguistic perspective or if they're merely artifacts of the algorithm. The paper claims the rules are "objectively" discovered, but this claim is undermined by the over-weighting technique and lack of linguistic review.

5. **Questionable claims about novelty**: The paper states "How, then, can we discover correspondences between more than two languages without prior assumptions about their shape or distribution? This is the question we answer in this paper." However, the paper fails to acknowledge that Wettig et al. (2011) already used MDL for exactly this purpose, and the authors don't explain how their approach differs from or improves upon this prior work.

## Conclusion
While the MDL framework is theoretically sound, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that their approach offers meaningful improvements over existing methods. The lack of comparison with established baselines, insufficient validation across diverse language data, and methodological inconsistencies (particularly regarding the deterministic nature of the algorithm) significantly undermine the paper's claims. The authors' "data over-weighting" technique further compromises their objectivity claim.

The paper would benefit from a thorough comparison with existing methods, validation on more diverse language families with larger datasets, and proper linguistic validation of the discovered rules. Without these, the contribution of this work remains unclear and unproven.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your MDL approach specifically differ from and improve upon Wettig et al. (2011), which also used MDL for linguistic analysis?
2. Can you provide a quantitative comparison of your method with established baselines (e.g., Schulz et al., 2004; Snyder et al., 2010) on a standard dataset?
3. How do you address the non-deterministic nature of your EM-based algorithm, and what evidence do you have that your results aren't sensitive to initialization?
