

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach to discover linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without assuming a prior distribution of these correspondences. The authors argue that existing statistical methods for correspondence discovery suffer from arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions, limited to pairwise language comparisons, or inability to integrate linguistic knowledge. Their key contributions are:

1. **Novel MDL framework for multi-language correspondence discovery** that avoids assumptions about correspondence shapes or distributions
2. **Efficient deterministic algorithm** using EM for learning correspondence rules directly from data
3. **Demonstration of multi-language analysis capabilities** showing the approach can quantify similarities between all language subsets, not just pairwise comparisons

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation**: The paper makes a compelling case for MDL as the ideal framework for correspondence discovery. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches (e.g., "imposing assumptions leads to finding not the 'true' correspondences hidden in the data, but their closest similes from the assumed distribution") and position MDL as a principled solution. The two-part MDL formulation they develop is well-justified and properly grounded in coding theory. This is the paper's strongest contribution, as it provides a statistically sound foundation that avoids the pitfalls of previous approaches.

2. **Meaningful empirical validation**: The Slavic language analysis is well-chosen as it provides a linguistically rich domain with established classifications. The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values aligns well with known linguistic groupings (e.g., identifying Bulgarian and Macedonian as outliers in the south Slavic group). The four-way analysis (CS-PL-RU-BG) effectively demonstrates the approach's ability to quantify similarities between all subsets of languages, which is a significant advantage over pairwise methods. The paper provides concrete examples of discovered correspondences that reflect linguistic phenomena (e.g., phonological rules like (g,h), orthographic rules like (cz,č), and verb endings like (ć,t)).

3. **Practical implementation with useful extensions**: The paper details a complete implementation of the algorithm, including the alignment procedure (using Dijkstra's algorithm for shortest-path problems) and training procedure (using EM). The introduction of data over-weighting as a way to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences is particularly valuable for linguists, as it allows them to tailor the analysis to their specific needs while maintaining the algorithm's deterministic nature.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: While the paper criticizes existing approaches, it lacks a thorough comparison with the most relevant recent methods. For instance, it doesn't compare against approaches like those using neural networks for cross-lingual correspondence discovery or recent unsupervised learning methods for morphological alignment. Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess the relative performance and novelty of the proposed method. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a quantitative comparison with at least one strong baseline method.

2. **Insufficient linguistic evaluation**: The paper demonstrates that the method can identify broad linguistic groupings, but it doesn't provide a detailed evaluation of the linguistic significance of the discovered correspondences. For example, the paper shows rules like (cz,č) and (g,h), but doesn't explain how these align with established Slavic sound changes. A more thorough linguistic analysis would strengthen the paper's contribution to the field.

3. **Scalability concerns**: The paper mentions that their current implementation can process up to five languages within a few hours on modest hardware. For a conference like CONLL that often deals with large-scale language processing, this may be seen as a significant limitation. The paper doesn't discuss how the approach would scale to larger language sets or more complex linguistic phenomena, which is important for practical applications.

4. **Ambiguity in data over-weighting**: While the data over-weighting technique is presented as a useful extension, it introduces subjectivity that conflicts with the paper's claim of "objective" results. The paper acknowledges that this "moves results away from the objective territory of MDL and introduces a user's subjective judgment." Without clear guidelines for determining appropriate weight values, this technique may limit reproducibility and scientific rigor.

## Questions for Authors

1. How do you plan to address scalability limitations for larger language sets (e.g., 10+ languages), which would be necessary for many practical applications in computational linguistics?

2. Can you provide more detailed linguistic validation of the discovered correspondences beyond the broad groupings shown in Figure 1? For example, are the specific rules like (cz,č) consistent with known Slavic sound changes?

3. What would be your recommendation for determining the appropriate data over-weighting factor in different linguistic contexts? Is there a principled way to select this factor, or is it purely subjective?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written paper with a strong theoretical foundation that addresses a genuine limitation in current approaches to linguistic correspondence discovery. The MDL-based approach is well-motivated, and the empirical validation on Slavic languages is convincing. The ability to analyze multiple languages simultaneously is a significant contribution that opens up new possibilities for linguistic analysis.

However, the paper would be substantially strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by including a comparison with state-of-the-art methods and providing more detailed linguistic evaluation of the discovered correspondences. The scalability concerns should also be addressed to better position the work for practical applications.

Given the strong theoretical contribution and meaningful empirical validation, I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the comparison with existing methods and provide more detailed linguistic evaluation. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics and has clear potential for application in linguistic analysis.
