

# Peer Review: "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making prior assumptions about the distribution or shape of these correspondences. The authors address the limitation of existing methods, which often impose arbitrary assumptions, fail to integrate linguistic knowledge, or are limited to pairwise language comparisons. Their approach uses the Minimum Description Length principle to identify the most succinct description of the data, which they argue corresponds to the "true" linguistic correspondences.

The main contributions of this work (in order of strength) are:

1. A principled MDL-based framework for discovering linguistic correspondences without making prior assumptions about their distribution or shape, enabling objective, unbiased string-level measures of linguistic similarity.
2. An efficient EM-based algorithm for learning correspondence rules from data that can analyze more than two languages simultaneously, providing detailed quantification of similarities among all subsets of languages.
3. Experimental validation demonstrating the approach's ability to identify linguistic subgroups and quantify similarities between all subsets of analyzed languages, with a focus on Slavic languages.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Soundness and Novelty**: The paper makes a strong case for using MDL as a principled approach to linguistic correspondence discovery, avoiding the pitfalls of existing methods that rely on arbitrary assumptions. The authors correctly note that MDL provides a statistically well-founded approach without requiring prior assumptions about the distribution of correspondences. This is a significant contribution to computational linguistics, as it addresses a fundamental limitation of existing approaches.

2. **Comprehensive Evaluation**: The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors demonstrate their method's effectiveness through both pairwise analyses (showing a phylogenetic tree that aligns with linguistic classifications) and a four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian. The ability to quantify similarities between all subsets of languages (not just pairwise) is a substantial advancement over existing methods. The results in Tables 2-4 provide clear evidence of the method's effectiveness in identifying linguistic subgroups.

3. **Practical Utility of Data Over-Weighting**: The introduction of "data over-weighting" is a clever and practical solution to the problem of insufficient statistical evidence for discovering certain correspondences. This technique allows linguists to control the level of detail in the discovered correspondences, which is particularly valuable for linguistic analysis where experts may want to focus on specific levels of granularity. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this approach in Table 3.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparative Analysis with State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims that existing methods have limitations but fails to provide a thorough comparison with the most relevant state-of-the-art approaches. While they mention Wettig et al. (2011) and Snyder and Barzilay (2008), they don't systematically compare their method's performance (e.g., accuracy, computational efficiency) against these alternatives. Without such a comparison, it's difficult to assess the novelty and superiority of their approach. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including such a comparative analysis.

2. **Insufficient Discussion of Limitations**: The paper claims their method discovers "the true" correspondences, but MDL identifies the most succinct description of the data, not necessarily the "true" linguistic correspondences. The authors don't adequately discuss this important distinction or the limitations of their approach. For example, how does their method handle cases with many gaps in the data (e.g., when some languages lack words for certain concepts in the Swadesh list)? This limitation is particularly relevant for real-world linguistic data, which often has missing values.

3. **Lack of Computational Complexity Analysis**: While the paper mentions that their implementation can process up to five languages within a few hours on a standard desktop machine, it lacks a detailed analysis of how computational complexity scales with the number of languages or dataset size. This is a critical omission for assessing the practical applicability of the method to larger language families. The paper should provide theoretical complexity analysis and empirical scaling results to demonstrate the method's feasibility for more extensive analyses.

4. **Limited Linguistic Interpretation**: The paper presents examples of discovered correspondences but doesn't provide deep linguistic analysis of why these correspondences exist or how they relate to known linguistic phenomena. For instance, the paper mentions that "Bulgarian and Macedonian are outliers due to their loss of case declension," but doesn't elaborate on how the discovered correspondences reflect this linguistic fact. A stronger linguistic interpretation would significantly enhance the contribution to the field.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with existing methods (e.g., Wettig et al. 2011, Snyder and Barzilay 2008) in terms of both theoretical assumptions and empirical performance? How does your approach specifically address the limitations of these methods?

2. In the four-way analysis, you mention that "there is more regularity in some of the languages than others." How does this finding relate to known linguistic phenomena in Slavic languages, and could you provide more specific examples from your data?

3. The paper states that "new rules will be discovered in the 'correct' order; that is, ordered by statistical significance." Can you provide specific examples from your experiments that demonstrate this ordering, particularly for the over-weighted cases?

4. How would your approach handle cases where the data has significant gaps (e.g., when some languages don't have words for certain concepts in the Swadesh list)? Would your method still be able to discover meaningful correspondences in such scenarios?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics. The authors have demonstrated a clear understanding of the problem and have developed a principled approach to address it. With the suggested improvements to the comparative analysis, discussion of limitations, and linguistic interpretation, this paper could become a significant contribution to the field. The authors should consider addressing the weaknesses noted above in the revision to strengthen their submission.
