

# Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper proposes a Minimum Description Length (MDL)-based approach to discover linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about their distribution. The main contributions are:
1. A novel application of MDL to discover linguistic correspondences across multiple languages (not limited to pairwise comparisons)
2. An efficient, deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules directly from data
3. A detailed analysis of Slavic languages demonstrating how the approach can quantify similarities across all language subsets

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery**: The paper makes a compelling case for using MDL as a principled approach to discover linguistic correspondences without arbitrary assumptions. Unlike existing statistical approaches that often impose distributional assumptions, this work provides a statistically well-founded method that directly learns correspondence rules from data. The authors effectively demonstrate how MDL can be applied to this problem in a way that avoids the pitfalls of prior assumptions.

2. **Demonstration of multi-language analysis capability**: The paper's ability to analyze four languages simultaneously (CS-PL-RU-BG) represents a significant advance over prior work limited to pairwise comparisons. The shared description length analysis (Figure 3) provides a rich, multi-dimensional view of linguistic similarities that would be impossible with pairwise methods. This allows for highly detailed quantification of linguistic similarity across language subsets, which is a major contribution to the field.

3. **Linguistically meaningful results**: The paper demonstrates that the approach yields linguistically valid results. The phylogenetic tree (Figure 1) aligns with established linguistic classifications, and the discovered correspondences (Table 3) include phonological, orthographic, and morphological patterns that linguists would recognize (e.g., palatalizations like "dzie" vs "dě"). The paper successfully shows how the approach can identify subtle linguistic patterns like the differences in vowel systems across Slavic languages.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper claims to overcome limitations of existing approaches but fails to provide a rigorous comparison with relevant state-of-the-art methods for linguistic correspondence discovery. Without quantitative comparisons of accuracy, computational efficiency, or quality of discovered correspondences against existing methods, it's difficult to assess the true novelty and advantage of the proposed approach. The authors should benchmark against recent methods like those using EM for morphological alignment (Snyder and Barzilay, 2008) or etymological relationship discovery (Wettig et al., 2011).

2. **Limited evaluation of robustness**: The paper focuses exclusively on Slavic languages, which are relatively closely related. To demonstrate the method's generalizability, it would be valuable to test the approach on more distantly related languages or languages with different structural properties (e.g., agglutinative vs. fusional languages). The paper also doesn't address how the algorithm handles noisy data or gaps in cognate data, which is common in linguistic datasets.

3. **Theoretical limitations of the MDL approach**: While MDL provides a principled framework, the paper doesn't fully address how the MDL tradeoff affects the discovery of correspondence rules. The paper mentions "data over-weighting" as a way to include less statistically significant rules, but this undermines the objective nature of the MDL approach by introducing user subjectivity. The authors should clarify how to balance statistical significance with linguistic utility.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to the state-of-the-art methods for linguistic correspondence discovery in terms of accuracy (e.g., F1 score on known correspondence rules) and computational efficiency? Could you provide a quantitative comparison?

2. The paper mentions that your algorithm can process "up to five languages" within a few hours. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity (time and space) as a function of the number of languages and data size?

3. The paper claims the approach is "deterministic," but the training procedure uses an EM algorithm which can converge to local optima. How does your method ensure the solution is globally optimal rather than a local optimum?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a novel application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery with promising results on Slavic languages. The ability to analyze multiple languages simultaneously represents a significant methodological advance. However, the lack of comparison with existing methods and limited evaluation of robustness prevents me from fully endorsing the paper for acceptance. With stronger comparative experiments and more thorough evaluation of the method's limitations, this work could make an important contribution to the field. I recommend a major revision addressing the weaknesses identified above.
