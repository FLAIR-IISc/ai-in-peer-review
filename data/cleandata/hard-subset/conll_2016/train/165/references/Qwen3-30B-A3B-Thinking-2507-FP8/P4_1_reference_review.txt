

# Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering systematic correspondences between multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about the distribution or shape of these correspondences. The authors develop an efficient, deterministic algorithm to infer correspondence rules directly from data, enabling simultaneous analysis of more than two languages. The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. A novel MDL-based framework for discovering language correspondences without arbitrary assumptions, providing objective, unbiased string-level measures of linguistic similarity.
2. An efficient algorithm for discovering correspondence rules that can handle multiple languages simultaneously, overcoming limitations of existing pairwise approaches.
3. Experimental validation demonstrating the method's effectiveness in quantifying linguistic similarities among Slavic languages, including a four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian.

## Strengths

1. **Novel approach to language correspondence discovery**: The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature by proposing a method that avoids the arbitrary assumptions common in existing statistical approaches. By using MDL, the authors establish a statistically well-founded approach that identifies the most succinct description of the data, resulting in objective, unbiased measures of linguistic similarity. This is a significant technical contribution that aligns with the theoretical foundations of information theory.

2. **Demonstrated utility through comprehensive experiments**: The authors successfully apply their method to Slavic languages, constructing phylogenetic trees that align with established linguistic classifications. The four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian provides a detailed quantification of linguistic similarities that goes beyond pairwise comparisons, revealing subtle relationships between languages that would be missed by traditional approaches. The method's ability to identify linguistic subgroups while quantifying similarities between all subsets of analyzed languages is particularly compelling.

3. **Thoughtful handling of practical considerations**: The authors thoughtfully address the limitations of their approach through the concept of "data over-weighting," which allows linguistic experts to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences. This bridges the gap between objective MDL principles and practical linguistic analysis needs. The deterministic nature of the algorithm, with optional non-deterministic extensions, makes it particularly suitable for linguistic applications where reproducibility and interpretability are important.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with existing methods**: The paper fails to provide a direct comparison with existing approaches for discovering language correspondences. While the authors mention limitations of current methods, they don't evaluate their approach against these methods in terms of performance, computational efficiency, or quality of discovered correspondences. This makes it difficult to assess the novelty and significance of their contribution.

2. **Limited experimental scope**: The evaluation is restricted to closely related Slavic languages, without testing the method on more distantly related language families or languages with significantly different writing systems. The paper doesn't discuss how the method would perform on languages with limited data or non-Slavic languages, which is crucial for assessing the generalizability of the approach.

3. **Insufficient analysis of computational complexity**: While the authors mention their implementation can handle up to five languages within a few hours on a modest machine, they provide no thorough analysis of how the algorithm scales with the number of languages, data set size, or complexity of correspondences. This is important for understanding the practical limitations of the method and its applicability to larger language sets.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a quantitative comparison of your method with existing approaches for discovering language correspondences, particularly those that claim to handle multiple languages or avoid arbitrary assumptions?

2. How would you expect your method to perform on languages from different families (e.g., Indo-European vs. Sino-Tibetan) or languages with significantly different writing systems (e.g., Latin script vs. Cyrillic vs. Chinese characters)?

3. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of your algorithm, particularly how it scales with the number of languages and the size of the data set?

## Conclusion

The paper presents a technically sound approach to discovering language correspondences using MDL principles. The method's ability to handle multiple languages simultaneously and provide objective measures of linguistic similarity is a significant contribution to the field. However, the lack of comparison with existing methods and limited experimental scope are notable weaknesses that need to be addressed. With additional evaluation and comparison, this work has the potential to make a meaningful contribution to linguistic analysis and computational linguistics.
