

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach to discover linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about the distribution of correspondences. The authors claim their method can handle more than two languages simultaneously, which is a significant limitation of existing approaches. The main contributions appear to be:

1. An MDL-based framework for discovering string-level correspondences between multiple languages
2. An efficient EM-based algorithm for learning correspondence rules
3. A demonstration of the approach on Slavic languages showing improved quantification of linguistic similarity

However, the paper's claimed contributions require careful evaluation of the technical details and experimental validation.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation**: The paper correctly applies the MDL principle, which is well-suited for model selection without prior assumptions. The authors properly acknowledge the connection between MDL and Bayesian inference, making a theoretically sound choice for their approach.

2. **Multi-language Capability**: The paper successfully demonstrates the ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously (four-way analysis), which is a genuine limitation of many existing approaches that are limited to pairwise comparisons.

3. **Quantitative Analysis**: The paper provides a detailed information-theoretic quantification of linguistic similarity across language subsets, which is a valuable contribution to linguistic analysis that goes beyond standard phylogenetic trees.

## Weaknesses

1. **Mathematical Notation Inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous mathematical notation errors that undermine the technical credibility. For example:
   - The equation "L(M) = N∑ i=1 (L(Σi)) + L(Π)" should be "L(M) = Σ_{i=1}^N L(Σ_i) + L(Π)" with proper summation notation.
   - The equation "L(Π) = 2N−1∑ i=1 LN(|ΠCi |+ 1) + ∑ π∈ΠCi L(π) + LN(TΠ) + log( ( TΠ − 1 |Π| − 1 ) )" incorrectly uses "log( ( TΠ − 1 |Π| − 1 ) )" instead of the binomial coefficient notation.
   - The paper states "L(π) = N∑ n=1 πn ̸=ϵ ( LN(|πn|) + ∑ σ∈πn L(code(σ)) )" with inconsistent indexing and unclear notation (e.g., "πn ̸=ϵ" should be "π_n ≠ ε").

2. **Incomplete Experimental Validation**: The paper claims to handle "more than two languages" but only demonstrates four-way analysis. There's no evidence of scalability to five or more languages, despite the authors stating they "can process up to five languages from our most complicated data set within a few hours." This is a significant gap in the experimental validation of their core claim.

3. **Lack of Comparison to Baselines**: The paper doesn't provide a rigorous comparison to existing methods for multi-language correspondence discovery. Without benchmarking against state-of-the-art approaches, it's impossible to determine if their method actually improves upon existing work.

4. **Unsubstantiated Claims about Data Over-Weighting**: The paper claims that "over-representing the data" allows for "a desired level of detail for inferred correspondences," but provides no evidence that this actually leads to linguistically meaningful results. The experimental results in Table 3 show some examples, but there's no quantitative analysis of how over-weighting affects the quality of the discovered correspondences.

5. **Formatting and Reference Issues**: The paper contains numerous reference formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "EuroComSlav Basiskurs - der panslavische Wortschatz, A. Angelov, Angelov.,? \Q2004\E, Angelov.,") and inconsistent use of mathematical notation throughout. These are not merely superficial errors but indicate a lack of attention to detail that undermines the paper's rigor.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "our algorithm is agnostic to the number of input languages," but only demonstrates four-way analysis. What evidence do you have that your algorithm can scale effectively to five or more languages without significant performance degradation?

2. The paper states that "the algorithm will have enough evidence to include larger correspondence rules than before" when over-weighting data, but provides no quantitative metrics to measure the quality of the discovered correspondences. How do you objectively measure whether over-weighting improves the linguistic validity of the discovered rules?

3. The paper doesn't provide a comparison to existing approaches for multi-language correspondence discovery. How does your method compare to approaches like the one by Wettig et al. (2011) in terms of both accuracy and computational efficiency?

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents an interesting application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery, the numerous mathematical notation errors, incomplete experimental validation, and lack of comparison to baseline methods are significant issues. The paper's claim of handling "more than two languages" is not sufficiently supported by the experimental evidence provided, and the mathematical errors throughout the paper raise serious concerns about the technical rigor of the work.

The paper's minor flaws are not just superficial; they indicate a lack of attention to detail that is critical for a technical paper. Given the stringent requirements of a top-tier conference like CoNLL, I recommend rejection on the basis of these substantial methodological and presentation issues.
