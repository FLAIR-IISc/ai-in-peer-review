

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary
The paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about the shape or distribution of these correspondences. The authors claim to extend previous work by enabling analysis of more than two languages simultaneously, using a deterministic algorithm based on the Minimum Description Length principle. They demonstrate their approach through pairwise analyses of Slavic languages and a four-language analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. A deterministic MDL-based algorithm for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without assuming their distribution
2. A novel application of the PS-2 MDL code to linguistic correspondence discovery
3. A detailed information-theoretic analysis of Slavic language similarities across multiple language subsets

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a meaningful gap in linguistic analysis by proposing a method that can handle more than two languages simultaneously, which is a significant limitation of most existing approaches. The authors provide a theoretically sound foundation for their approach using the Minimum Description Length principle, which is well-suited for discovering patterns without prior assumptions.

2. The paper demonstrates a clear application of the method to Slavic languages, producing phylogenetic trees and language similarity metrics that align with established linguistic classifications. The results in Tables 2 and 3 show that their approach produces linguistically meaningful correspondences, including phonological, orthographic, and morphological patterns.

3. The authors introduce an interesting concept of "data over-weighting" that allows for controlling the granularity of discovered correspondences, which could be valuable for linguistic analysis. This provides a practical way for linguists to adjust the level of detail in the discovered correspondences based on their specific needs.

## Weaknesses
1. **Critical technical errors in the MDL formulation**: The paper contains a fundamental error in its MDL formulation: "L(M) + L(D|M) = − log p(M)− log p(D|M)." This is incorrect - the correct formulation should be L(M) + L(D|M) = -log p(M) - log p(D|M), but the paper has a double negative that makes the equation mathematically incorrect. This is a serious flaw that undermines the theoretical foundation of the paper.

2. **Severe formatting and reference issues**: The references are completely mangled and not formatted according to standard academic conventions. For example, the reference for Angelov (2004) appears as "EuroComSlav Basiskurs - der panslavische Wortschatz, A. Angelov, Angelov.,? \Q2004\E, Angelov.," which is not properly structured. This indicates a lack of attention to detail that suggests the paper may not have undergone proper proofreading.

3. **Missing critical information**: The paper claims to present "three possible alignments" but fails to actually show them in a clear format. The description "a) s z c z š t b) s z c z š t c) szcz št" is confusing and doesn't clearly indicate what the alignments are. This is a critical omission that makes it difficult for readers to understand the core algorithm.

4. **Inconsistent and erroneous notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout, such as "log(.) = log2(.)" but then uses log without specifying the base in formulas. There are also numerous typos like "ormore" (should be "or more"), "bit1" (should be "bits"), and "mutual intelligibility. Beyond" (should be "mutual intelligibility, beyond").

5. **Incomplete experimental validation**: The paper claims to handle "more than two languages" but the four-language experiment is limited to just Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian, which are not all from the same linguistic subgroup. The paper doesn't demonstrate the method on three or more languages in a way that clearly shows the advantage over pairwise analysis. The phrase "We plan to discuss potential applications... in future work" appears multiple times, suggesting the paper is not complete.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you clarify the mathematical error in the MDL formulation (L(M) + L(D|M) = − log p(M)− log p(D|M))? Is this a typo, and if so, what is the correct formulation?

2. Why does the paper claim to analyze "more than two languages" when the only multi-language analysis presented is limited to just four specific languages (Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian), which are not all from the same linguistic subgroup?

3. The paper mentions "data over-weighting" as a way to control the granularity of discovered correspondences, but doesn't provide clear guidelines on how to determine the appropriate weight for different linguistic contexts. Could you elaborate on how a linguist should choose the optimal weight for a given analysis?

4. The paper states "We use ISO 639-1 and ISO 639-3 codes to identify the languages" but doesn't provide a key explaining what the codes (e.g., "usb", "lsb", "CS") represent. This makes Table 2 difficult to interpret without additional context.

## Final Recommendation
I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an interesting problem and has some promising ideas, the presence of critical technical errors (particularly in the MDL formulation), severe formatting issues, and significant omissions in the presentation of key algorithmic details make it difficult to evaluate the validity and contribution of the work. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance when the paper contains such fundamental errors that undermine its theoretical foundation. The minor issues I've identified are not just superficial - they indicate a lack of rigor that would prevent readers from trusting the results or implementing the method.
