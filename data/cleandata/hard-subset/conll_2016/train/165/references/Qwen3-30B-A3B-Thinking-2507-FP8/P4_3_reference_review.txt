

# Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

## Summary and Main Contributions

The paper introduces an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about the distribution or shape of these correspondences. The authors propose a deterministic algorithm that infers correspondence rules directly from data, enabling simultaneous analysis of more than two languages.

Based on objective assessment, the main contributions are:

1. A novel application of the MDL principle to discover linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without prior assumptions about their distribution, providing an objective, unbiased approach to string-level linguistic similarity.
2. An efficient, deterministic algorithm for multi-language correspondence discovery that enables fine-grained information-theoretic quantification of linguistic similarity across all subsets of analyzed languages, not just pairwise comparisons.
3. A detailed phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages that demonstrates the practical utility of the approach, showing how it can identify linguistic subgroups while quantifying similarities between all language subsets.

## Strengths

1. **Novel MDL Application**: The paper provides a technically sound application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery, which is a significant contribution to the field. The authors effectively argue that existing approaches either impose arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions or are limited to pairwise comparisons. Their MDL formulation avoids these limitations by directly modeling the data without assumptions, and the PS-2 MDL code is well-justified and theoretically grounded in coding theory. This represents a genuine methodological advancement over prior work.

2. **Multi-language Analysis Capability**: The paper successfully demonstrates the ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously, which is a significant limitation of previous approaches. The four-language experiment (Czech, Polish, Russian, Bulgarian) provides a clear example of how the method can quantify similarities across all language subsets, offering a more detailed picture of linguistic relationships than pairwise analysis. The shared Description Length analysis (Section 5.3.1) is particularly compelling as it provides an information-theoretic measure of linguistic similarity at multiple granularities.

3. **Objective and Deterministic Approach**: The authors emphasize that their method is objective (no assumptions about correspondence distributions) and deterministic (no randomization), which is valuable for linguistic applications where reproducibility and verifiability are important. The ability to initialize the model with linguistic knowledge while maintaining determinism represents a practical advantage for linguists who want to integrate expert knowledge with data-driven discovery.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Evaluation Scope**: The paper only evaluates the approach on Slavic languages using Swadesh lists and a set of internationalisms. While this is a well-chosen domain for demonstrating the method, the paper lacks evidence of generalizability to other language families or language types (e.g., non-Indo-European languages, languages with very different orthographic systems). This limitation is significant because linguistic correspondences can vary substantially across language families, and the authors claim their method is broadly applicable.

2. **Insufficient Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims that existing approaches have limitations, but it does not provide a systematic comparison with current state-of-the-art methods for multi-language correspondence discovery. The authors mention that their approach avoids arbitrary assumptions, but they don't provide quantitative evidence that their method outperforms existing approaches in terms of accuracy or efficiency. A comparison with methods like those from Snyder & Barzilay (2008) or Wettig et al. (2011) would strengthen the claims.

3. **Data Over-Weighting Compromise**: While the paper introduces data over-weighting as a practical solution for cases with insufficient statistical evidence, this technique moves the approach away from the objective MDL principle toward a more subjective user-defined process. The paper acknowledges this trade-off but doesn't provide a principled way to determine the appropriate level of over-weighting for different datasets. The experimental results in Section 5.2 show that over-weighting can lead to more detailed correspondences, but without clear guidelines for when and how to apply this technique, its practical utility remains limited.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the algorithm can process up to five languages within a few hours on a standard desktop machine. Could you provide more details about the computational complexity of your approach and how it scales with the number of languages? What would be the expected runtime for analyzing 10 languages?

2. In the four-language experiment (Section 5.3), the authors note that the algorithm identifies Czech and Polish as sharing significant portions of description length. However, the paper doesn't discuss how this finding relates to established linguistic classifications. Could you elaborate on how your findings align with or differ from established linguistic knowledge about the relationship between Czech and Polish?

3. The paper mentions that the approach is deterministic, but the training procedure involves an EM algorithm. How does the algorithm maintain determinism despite the iterative nature of the EM procedure, and what safeguards are in place to prevent different initializations from leading to different results?
