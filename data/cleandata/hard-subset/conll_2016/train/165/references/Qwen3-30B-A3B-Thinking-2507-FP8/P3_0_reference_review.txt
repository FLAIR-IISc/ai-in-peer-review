

# Peer Review: "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary & Contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences across multiple languages without imposing assumptions about correspondence distributions. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) an MDL framework for objective, distribution-free correspondence discovery; (2) an efficient algorithm that quantifies similarity across all language subsets, not just pairwise; and (3) an extension for data over-weighting to control the granularity of discovered correspondences.

## Strengths

1. **Methodological Innovation in Correspondence Discovery**: The paper makes a compelling argument for MDL as a principled alternative to existing statistical approaches that often rely on arbitrary assumptions. By framing correspondence discovery as a model selection problem where the "best" model is the one that provides the most concise description of the data, the authors address a fundamental limitation in current approaches. Their two-part MDL formulation is well-motivated and correctly applied, avoiding the pitfalls of Bayesian approaches that require prior assumptions.

2. **Multi-Subset Similarity Quantification**: The paper's most significant contribution is demonstrating how their approach can quantify linguistic similarity across all subsets of analyzed languages, not just pairwise. Figure 3 provides a clear, information-theoretic representation of how languages share structure at different levels of granularity. This is a substantial advancement over existing pairwise methods, which cannot capture the nuanced relationships revealed in the four-language analysis.

3. **Linguistically Plausible Results**: The discovered correspondences (Table 3) show a good balance of phonological, orthographic, and morphological patterns. The algorithm correctly identifies expected patterns like (cz,č) and (g,h), while the over-weighting technique produces more detailed alignments that match linguistic expectations. The phylogenetic tree in Figure 1 aligns well with established Slavic language classifications, validating the approach's linguistic relevance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparative Baselines**: The paper claims to address limitations of existing approaches but fails to provide a rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods. Without demonstrating how their approach outperforms alternatives (e.g., the Bayesian approaches mentioned in the related work), the claimed advantages remain unsubstantiated. A quantitative comparison on standard benchmarks would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Limited Data Scope and Domain Generalizability**: All experiments are conducted on Slavic languages, which are relatively closely related. The paper doesn't demonstrate the approach on more diverse language families where correspondence patterns might be less regular or more complex. The Swadesh list data (which contains mostly nouns, verbs, and adjectives) may not capture the full range of linguistic phenomena relevant to correspondence discovery.

3. **Contradiction Between "Objective" Claim and Data Over-Weighting**: The paper claims to provide "objective, unbiased string-level measures" but then introduces data over-weighting as a technique to include "near-significant" correspondences. This directly contradicts the objectivity claim, as it allows users to bias results by adjusting the weight parameter. The paper doesn't justify why this subjective adjustment is acceptable or how to choose the appropriate weight.

4. **Computational Scalability Limitations**: The paper acknowledges that processing more than five languages becomes computationally challenging (Section 4.3), but provides no analysis of how the algorithm's complexity scales with language count. For a method that aims to be applicable to large language families, this represents a significant limitation that requires addressing.

5. **Lack of Linguistic Validation**: While the paper presents examples of discovered correspondences, it lacks input from linguists to validate whether these patterns are meaningful to the field. The paper states "the discovered correspondences are of different granularities and linguistic character" but doesn't provide linguistic analysis to support this claim. The absence of linguistic expert review diminishes the credibility of the results.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a direct comparison with at least one existing state-of-the-art method for correspondence discovery (e.g., Snyder & Barzilay 2008 or Wettig et al. 2011) on the same dataset, demonstrating quantitative improvements in accuracy or coverage?

2. How would your approach handle language pairs with very different writing systems (e.g., Arabic and English) or languages with vastly different morphological complexity (e.g., agglutinative vs. isolating languages)?

3. The paper mentions that "Czech has the largest number of diacritically-modified symbols" as an explanation for its high individual description length. Could you provide a more detailed linguistic explanation for why this particular characteristic would impact the MDL-based correspondence discovery process?

4. The data over-weighting technique appears to be a significant departure from the MDL principle. How do you reconcile this with the paper's claim of providing "objective" results, and what criteria do you propose for selecting the appropriate weight parameter?

## Conclusion

This paper presents a novel application of MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery with significant methodological value. The multi-subset similarity quantification represents a meaningful advancement over existing pairwise approaches. However, the lack of comparative baselines, limited data scope, and contradiction between the "objective" claim and the data over-weighting technique are substantial weaknesses that need to be addressed. With stronger experimental validation and better justification for the data over-weighting technique, this work could make a valuable contribution to computational historical linguistics. As it stands, the paper's claims are partially supported but require more rigorous evaluation to fully establish their significance.
