- Proposes an MDL-based framework to discover string-level correspondences between multiple languages without assuming correspondence distributions.  
- Presents a deterministic algorithm that learns correspondence rules directly from data and can handle >2 languages simultaneously.  
- Demonstrates multi-language analysis on Slavic languages, quantifying similarity across all language subsets.  

- Strong theoretical foundation: MDL provides a principled alternative to assumption-laden approaches.  
- Comprehensive experiments: pairwise and multi-language analyses align with linguistic classifications and show nuanced subset similarities.  
- Clear methodological exposition: detailed MDL coding, alignment procedure, and EM-based training with MDL-based regularization.  

- Lacks rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods and quantitative benchmarks (accuracy, runtime, etc.).  
- Uses raw orthography without addressing phonetic/phonological limitations that may miss linguistically significant correspondences.  
- Scalability concerns: current runtime/memory suggest limits beyond ~5 languages on modest hardware.  
- Limited discussion of concrete NLP applications and how discovered correspondences would be integrated.  

- How would phonetic or morphological input change discovered correspondences, especially for Slavic orthographic complexities?  
- What are the practical computational limits and how can the method scale to much larger language families?  
- How does the method distinguish expected groupings from novel, subtle linguistic relationships?  
- How should practitioners choose the appropriate level of data over-weighting for different tasks or language families?  

- Overall: theoretically sound and promising, but needs state-of-the-art comparisons, discussion of orthographic limits, scalability fixes, and concrete application examples; recommend acceptance with major revisions.
