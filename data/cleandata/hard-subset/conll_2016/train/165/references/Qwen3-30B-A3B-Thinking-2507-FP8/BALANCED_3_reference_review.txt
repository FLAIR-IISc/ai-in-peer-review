

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach to discover linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without making distributional assumptions. The authors present an efficient algorithm that uses the Minimum Description Length principle to identify the most succinct set of correspondence rules directly from data. Their method is designed to be deterministic, objective, and capable of handling more than two languages simultaneously.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A principled MDL-based framework for correspondence discovery** - The paper provides a statistically well-founded approach that avoids the arbitrary assumptions common in existing methods. The authors successfully apply MDL to linguistic correspondence discovery, treating correspondences as associated strings without assuming underlying distributions.

2. **An efficient algorithm for multi-language analysis** - The authors present a novel algorithm that extends beyond pairwise language analysis to simultaneously analyze multiple languages, enabling more detailed quantification of linguistic similarities across language subsets.

3. **Quantitative measurement of linguistic similarity across language subsets** - The paper introduces the concept of "shared description length" (sDL) to quantify the amount of structure shared between different subsets of languages, providing a more nuanced understanding of linguistic relationships.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation:** The paper makes a compelling case for using MDL to discover linguistic correspondences. The authors correctly identify the limitation of existing methods that impose arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions, and their MDL approach provides a statistically rigorous alternative. The paper's detailed explanation of the MDL formulation and encoding scheme demonstrates deep understanding of the theoretical underpinnings.

2. **Demonstrated multi-language capability:** The paper's most significant contribution is showing that their method can analyze more than two languages simultaneously. The four-language analysis (Czech-Polish-Russian-Bulgarian) is particularly impressive, revealing nuanced linguistic relationships that pairwise analyses would miss. The shared description length (sDL) metric provides a novel way to quantify linguistic similarity across language subsets.

3. **Clear presentation and practical implementation:** The authors provide a thorough explanation of their algorithm, including the encoding scheme, alignment procedure, and training process. The implementation details, including the use of Dijkstra's algorithm for alignment and the EM approach for optimization, are well-motivated and clearly presented. The paper's evaluation on Slavic languages is comprehensive, with multiple analyses (NCD, phylogenetic trees, sDL) that convincingly demonstrate the method's utility.

4. **Practical utility for linguists:** The authors recognize the needs of linguists by developing a deterministic approach that can incorporate linguistic knowledge. The data over-weighting technique is particularly valuable, allowing linguists to adjust the level of detail in discovered correspondences based on their expertise.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparative evaluation:** The paper doesn't provide a clear comparison of their method against existing approaches. Without benchmarking against state-of-the-art methods on a common dataset or against linguistic gold standards, it's difficult to assess the true novelty and advantages of their approach. The paper demonstrates their method works, but doesn't convincingly show how it improves upon existing methods.

2. **Limited discussion of scalability and computational complexity:** While the authors mention their implementation can handle up to five languages on a desktop machine, they don't provide a thorough analysis of computational complexity. It would be valuable to understand how the algorithm scales with the number of languages and dataset size, especially since they mention plans to extend to higher N.

3. **Over-reliance on orthographic data:** The paper uses raw orthographic data without transcriptions, which limits its applicability to cases where phonological correspondences are more relevant. The authors don't address how their method would handle languages with different writing systems or complex orthographies, which is a significant limitation for a method aiming to be broadly applicable.

4. **Insufficient linguistic interpretation:** While the paper shows example correspondences, it doesn't deeply analyze what these correspondences mean linguistically. The conclusion notes the analysis is "purely on the basis of superficial word form similarity," but doesn't clarify how these correspondences relate to established linguistic phenomena or how they might be interpreted by linguists.

5. **Lack of discussion on missing data:** The paper states they use only entries with words for all languages in question, but doesn't address how the method handles missing data in general, which is a common issue in linguistic datasets and could significantly impact results.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about how the discovered correspondence rules compare to established linguistic knowledge? Are there specific examples where your method discovered correspondences that contradict or confirm existing linguistic theories?

2. How would your method handle languages with different writing systems (e.g., Latin vs. Cyrillic)? Would you need to preprocess the data to a common script, and how would this affect the discovered correspondences?

3. Can you provide more information about the computational complexity of your algorithm and how it scales with the number of languages and dataset size?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution by introducing an MDL-based approach to linguistic correspondence discovery that avoids distributional assumptions and can handle multiple languages simultaneously. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the method's practical utility for linguists is well-justified. However, the lack of comparative evaluation against existing methods and limited discussion of scalability and data handling limitations prevent this from being a transformative contribution.

**Decision Recommendation:** Accept

The paper presents a novel and well-executed approach with clear potential for impact in computational linguistics and historical linguistics. With the suggested improvements addressing the weaknesses (particularly adding comparative evaluation and discussing limitations more thoroughly), this work would be a strong contribution to the field. The method's ability to quantify linguistic similarity across language subsets is particularly valuable and represents a significant advancement over existing pairwise approaches.
