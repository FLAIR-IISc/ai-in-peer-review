

# Peer Review: "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

## Summary & Main Contributions

This paper proposes an MDL-based approach to discover systematic string-level correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about the shape or distribution of these correspondences. The authors present a deterministic algorithm that can handle more than two languages simultaneously, addressing a key limitation of existing pairwise approaches. The main contributions are:

1. A novel MDL-based framework for discovering linguistic correspondences that avoids arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions
2. An efficient algorithm for learning correspondence rules directly from data that can handle multiple languages
3. A detailed analysis of Slavic languages showing how the method quantifies similarity across all language subsets, not just pairwise comparisons

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Foundation and Novelty**: The paper makes a strong case for using MDL to avoid the limitations of existing approaches that rely on assumptions about correspondence distributions. The authors effectively demonstrate how MDL provides a principled approach to model selection without imposing arbitrary constraints. This is a significant theoretical contribution to the field.

2. **Comprehensive Experimental Validation**: The paper provides thorough validation through both pairwise and multi-language analyses. The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values aligns with known linguistic classifications, and the four-language analysis demonstrates the method's ability to capture nuanced similarities across language subsets. The inclusion of data over-weighting as a mechanism to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences is particularly insightful.

3. **Clear Methodological Exposition**: The paper provides a detailed, well-structured explanation of the MDL code, including the model code and data code. The authors' explanation of the alignment procedure and training algorithm using EM is particularly clear and well-justified. The discussion of how the algorithm avoids overfitting through MDL's natural regularization is sound.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art**: The paper claims that existing approaches "impose arbitrary assumptions" but fails to provide a rigorous comparison with relevant methods. Without benchmarking against established approaches (e.g., Snyder & Barzilay 2008; Wettig et al. 2011), it's difficult to assess whether the authors' method truly advances the state of the art. The paper should include quantitative comparisons of accuracy, computational efficiency, or other relevant metrics.

2. **Insufficient Justification for Orthographic Approach**: The paper uses "raw orthographic form, without transcriptions of any kind," yet doesn't adequately address how this choice affects the discovered correspondences. For Slavic languages, phonological differences (like palatalization) are linguistically significant but may not be fully captured by orthography alone. The paper should discuss this limitation and whether phonetic transcriptions would yield more meaningful correspondences.

3. **Scalability Concerns**: The authors note they can process "up to five languages from our most complicated data set within a few hours on a 4GB RAM, 2.5GHz single core desktop machine." This suggests significant computational limitations for larger language families or more languages. The paper should discuss these scalability constraints and potential solutions for larger-scale applications, which would be critical for practical adoption.

4. **Limited Discussion of Practical Applications**: While the paper mentions potential applications in "multi-language natural language processing," it lacks concrete examples of how these discovered correspondences could be integrated into NLP systems. The authors should provide specific use cases demonstrating the practical value of their method beyond linguistic analysis.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper uses orthographic data exclusively. How would incorporating phonetic or morphological analysis affect the discovered correspondences, particularly for Slavic languages with complex orthographic systems? Could this potentially reveal correspondences that aren't evident in the orthographic data alone?

2. The paper claims to handle "more than two languages simultaneously," but the experiments only demonstrate results for two and four languages. What are the practical computational limits of your approach, and how would you scale it to handle larger language families (e.g., Indo-European family with 10+ languages)?

3. The paper mentions that "the algorithm furthermore identifies the linguistic grouping between Czech and Polish." However, Czech and Polish are both West Slavic languages, which is well-known. How does your method distinguish between this expected grouping and more subtle, previously unknown linguistic relationships?

4. The paper discusses "data over-weighting" as a way to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences. How would you recommend linguists determine the appropriate level of over-weighting for different linguistic tasks or language families?

## Overall Assessment

This paper presents a theoretically sound approach to discovering linguistic correspondences that addresses a genuine limitation of existing methods. The MDL framework provides a principled alternative to assumption-laden statistical approaches, and the experimental validation is thorough for the Slavic languages analyzed. However, the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art methods, insufficient discussion of the orthographic limitation, and unclear scalability issues are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. With these concerns resolved, this work could make a meaningful contribution to computational linguistics and historical linguistics.

The paper is well-written and demonstrates a strong understanding of the problem space, but it would benefit from more concrete comparisons with existing methods and a clearer articulation of the method's practical advantages for NLP applications. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address these concerns.
