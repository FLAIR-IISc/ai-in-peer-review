- Proposes an MDL-based framework to discover string-level correspondence rules across multiple languages from cognate lists without a fixed parametric form.  
- Encodes rule tables and aligned data using a two-part MDL code and searches for models minimizing total description length.  
- Uses exact alignments computed as shortest paths in a DAG and an EM-style loop alternating exact alignment (E) and greedy rule merging (M).  
- Demonstrates pairwise NCD-based phylogeny and a four-language analysis on Slavic languages, introducing a shared description length (sDL) metric for language subsets.  
- Introduces “data over-weighting” to allow experts to promote larger correspondences.  

- Strength: Principled, flexible MDL formulation that models arbitrary-length string tuples including insertions/deletions, covering orthographic, phonological, and morphological correspondences.  
- Strength: Exact alignment via Dijkstra on a DAG and a clear E–M alternation provides an intuitive, statistically grounded search heuristic.  
- Strength: sDL offers a novel multi-language similarity analysis beyond pairwise measures, capturing shared structure across subsets.  
- Strength: Empirical results on Slavic languages produce plausible correspondences and largely recover known subgroupings.  
- Strength: Expert-in-the-loop controls (seed rules, over-weighting) allow users to steer granularity and incorporate prior knowledge.  

- Weakness: Lacks comparisons to established baselines (PMI/LexStat/PHMM/ALINE/Bayesian methods), quantitative rule quality metrics, ablations, and simple baselines for trees.  
- Weakness: Key parts of the MDL specification (L(Π), data-to-model codes, combinatorial terms, alphabet unigram coding) are under-specified and risk double-counting.  
- Weakness: Scalability is limited (at most five languages on small datasets, hours runtime) and the M-step search is only heuristically explored without pruning or parallelization details.  
- Weakness: Ad hoc data over-weighting departs from MDL objectivity, lacks principled guidance for choosing α, and may encourage overfitting.  
- Weakness: Data are limited and noisy (small Slavic wordlists, mixed orthographies, Wiktionary inconsistencies); no normalization/transliteration or demonstration beyond Slavic.  

- Question: Please precisely derive L(Π) and L(D|M), define weak number composition, and clarify code(π)/code(σ) to avoid double-counting.  
- Question: What candidate merges are considered in the M-step—only adjacent co-occurring rules or any pair—and how do you prevent spurious long rules?  
- Question: How is convergence ensured or cycling mitigated beyond “no merge reduces description length”?  
- Question: Can you provide quantitative evaluations against gold standards or on synthetic data with planted correspondences?  
- Question: How sensitive are rules, NCDs, and trees to α weighting and initialization; any stability/bootstrap analyses?  
- Question: Can you compare against at least one strong baseline (e.g., LexStat/PMI) and simpler NCD/Levenshtein baselines?  

- Suggestion: Improve reference formatting, clarify dataset preprocessing (tokenization, diacritics, Cyrillic/Latin harmonization), and publish code/data for replication.  
- Suggestion: Explore scalability improvements (A* with admissible heuristics, beam search, rule pruning, batching, parallelization) and ablations on graph optimizations.  
- Suggestion: Consider phonemic normalization or feature-aware coding to better capture phonological structure across scripts and orthographic idiosyncrasies.  

- Overall: Promising principled approach with qualitative success, but requires stronger methodological clarity, specification, scalability, and empirical validation to be competitive.
