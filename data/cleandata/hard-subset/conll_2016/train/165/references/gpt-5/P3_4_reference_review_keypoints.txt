- MDL-based framework discovers string-level correspondences across multiple languages without parametric rule assumptions; deterministic EM alternates shortest-path alignment and greedy rule merging to minimize two-part MDL.

- Produces interpretable, variable-length, multi-language correspondence rules and supports seeding priors and a “data over-weighting” knob to trade statistical stringency for granularity.

- Empirically recovers plausible Slavic correspondences, induces an NCD-based phylogeny aligned with known subgrouping, and introduces a shared Description Length metric for multi-way similarity.

- Contribution 1: Concrete MDL formulation and deterministic algorithm for arbitrary-length, multi-way correspondences with optimal alignment and greedy merging.

- Contribution 2: Analysis protocol using MDL-derived distances (NCD) and a shared DL statistic, demonstrated on Slavic data.

- Contribution 3: Practical data over-weighting mechanism to steer learned rule granularity when signals are near MDL thresholds.

- Strength: Clear MDL framing yields compact, linguistically sensible and interpretable correspondence rules (e.g., palatalization, metathesis).

- Strength: Model and alignment naturally extend to N languages, enabling multi-way correspondences beyond pairwise methods.

- Strength: Deterministic training and rule-seeding allow reproducibility and easy injection of linguistic priors.

- Strength: Shared DL metric provides a novel way to quantify how structure is distributed across language subsets and yields plausible qualitative insights.

- Strength: Induced phylogeny and sensitivity to script effects show empirical face validity.

- Weakness: Limited and unbalanced evaluation—restricted to Slavic data and short lists, no gold-standard correspondence evaluation, extrinsic tasks, or comparisons to strong baselines.

- Weakness: Orthography vs. phonology conflation—working on raw orthography conflates script/spelling conventions with phonological correspondences.

- Weakness: Ambiguities in code design and potential double-counting—unclear derivation of L(Π) vs. L(D|M) and whether usage counts are encoded twice.

- Weakness: Under-specified algorithmic details and guarantees—merge compatibility, candidate generation, monotone decrease of MDL, and local minima avoidance are unclear.

- Weakness: Scalability and robustness concerns—E-step worst-case complexity, memory bottlenecks, lack of experiments beyond five languages, and missing sensitivity analyses.

- Question: Provide precise derivation of L(Π) and L(D|M), clarifying what is encoded where and whether usage statistics are double-counted.

- Question: Define the merge operation: what makes two rules “compatible,” how are merges constructed, and are splits/deletions allowed; how many candidates per M-step?

- Question: Clarify convergence and stopping: is monotone MDL decrease guaranteed per EM iteration, what is the stopping criterion, and provide objective traces and merge counts.

- Question: NCD protocol sensitivity: how does model adaptivity affect NCDs, and can you provide bootstrap confidence intervals for distances and trees?

- Question: Baselines and ablations: compare to Levenshtein, LexStat/SCA, pair-HMM/transducer models, and run on phonemic transcriptions to disentangle orthography effects.

- Question: Over-weighting guidance: how should users set α, are induced rules stable across α, what is overfitting risk, and why not exclude single-language rules instead?

- Overall: Thoughtful, interpretable MDL approach with promising multi-language capabilities, but needs clearer statistical derivation, broader empirical comparisons, and work on orthography/phonology and scalability to increase impact.
