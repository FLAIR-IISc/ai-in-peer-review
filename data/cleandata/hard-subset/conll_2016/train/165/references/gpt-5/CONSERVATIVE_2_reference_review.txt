Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework to discover variable-length correspondence rules among multiple languages, learned via an EM-like procedure: alignments are computed as shortest paths over rule applications, and a greedy maximization merges rules that most reduce description length. The approach produces pairwise Normalized Compression Distances (NCD) and a phylogeny on Slavic languages, and introduces a “shared Description Length” (sDL) diagnostic to quantify similarity across all subsets of a set of languages. The authors also discuss a user-controlled “over-weighting” of the data to force larger correspondences.

As I see them, the contributions are:
1) An MDL coding scheme for multi-way string-to-string correspondence rules with a deterministic greedy learning algorithm (alignment via Dijkstra; merging by global description-length gain).
2) An exploratory application to Slavic languages: pairwise NCD-based tree and qualitative analyses of induced rules; a four-language sDL analysis illustrating multi-way correspondences.
3) A practitioner-oriented “data over-weighting” knob to bias toward longer rules (acknowledged as deviating from pure MDL).

Strengths
- Clear objective and principled unsupervised framework: The two-part MDL formulation and greedy EM-like training are described coherently. The model accommodates sparsely populated tuple rules across languages and allows optional injection of prior rules, which is appealing for linguists.
- Qualitative plausibility of induced rules: Examples capture recognizable sound/orthographic correspondences (e.g., cz–č, g–h, palatalizations) and Cyrillic/Latin script effects. These are intuitive and foster confidence that the method finds linguistically meaningful patterns.
- The NCD phylogeny broadly matches known Slavic groupings: Despite using raw orthography, the pairwise distances recover the major West/East/South branches and the expected BG/MK behavior, indicating the approach captures informative string regularities.
- Potential to go beyond pairwise analyses: The formulation for N>2 languages and the sDL decomposition offer a way to quantify shared structure across all subsets, which is not readily available in conventional pairwise correspondence models.
- Determinism and transparency: The deterministic training (given fixed tie-breaking) and explicit cost accounting are attractive for reproducibility in principle and for expert-in-the-loop analyses.

Weaknesses
- Lack of rigorous evaluation against established baselines: There is no quantitative comparison to strong, well-known methods for orthographic/phonological correspondences and cognate modeling (e.g., PMI/lexstat-style alignment in LingPy, PHMMs, ALINE, weighted edit distance with learned costs). The evaluation relies on qualitative examples and a tree consistent with prior knowledge, without measuring improvements over simpler baselines (e.g., character n-gram distances, Needleman–Wunsch with tuned costs). This makes it difficult to assess actual gains.
- Limited and confounded data setting: Experiments use orthographic forms (not phonemic), Swadesh lists plus internationalisms/pan-Slavic words. Borrowings and script conventions confound historical correspondences. No tests beyond Slavic (e.g., across families) or robustness to noise/missing data are provided. No gold-standard alignments or reconstruction tasks are used, and no statistical significance or error bars are reported.
- Coding scheme and training details lack formal guarantees and clarity: The “data-to-model” coding and weak number composition terms are not fully specified or justified (prefix-free property/Kraft inequality not demonstrated). The greedy merge procedure has no convergence or consistency guarantees; sensitivity to initialization and tie-breaking is not studied. It is unclear whether the decompositions avoid double counting between L(M) and L(D|M).
- Scalability claims not substantiated: Although the method is described as agnostic to N, the implementation handles up to five languages and exhibits O(|D|·|R|^2) E-step complexity with memory bottlenecks. No comparative run-time/memory benchmarks, pruning analyses, or large-N experiments are given. This undermines the practicality for multi-language NLP.
- “Over-weighting” undermines MDL’s objectivity without a principled selection: Introducing α to upweight L(D|M) is a subjective tuning that risks overfitting; yet the paper uses overweighted results for illustration without criteria for choosing α or evaluating its downstream impact on distances/phylogeny. This blurs the line between descriptive modeling and user-driven pattern forcing.
- Reproducibility and presentation: Preprocessing (tokenization of digraphs/diacritics, script handling), smoothing constants, and parameter settings are not specified; code/data availability is not stated. References are incomplete and inconsistently formatted, and several technical details (partial order reduction, exact rule usage coding) are underspecified.

Questions to authors (for response)
- Baselines: Can you compare quantitatively to PMI-based correspondence induction (e.g., LexStat/LingPy), PHMMs, ALINE, and tuned edit-distance models on (i) tree quality (e.g., Robinson–Foulds distance to a standard Slavic tree), (ii) alignment quality against a small gold set, and (iii) predictive tasks (e.g., cognate reconstruction accuracy via cross-validation)?
- Coding details: Please provide a precise derivation of the data-to-model codes used for rule usage and alphabet unigrams, and demonstrate prefix-freeness/Kraft compliance. How do you avoid double counting between L(Π) and L(D|M)?
- Alignment mechanics: Can rules emit empty strings for some languages freely? What prevents degenerate segmentations with many insertions/deletions? Is LN(|d|) the only segmentation penalty? How are digraphs/multigraphs (e.g., cz, szcz) tokenized—single symbols or sequences?
- Determinism and stability: How sensitive are results to tie-breaking, rule enumeration order, and initializations (null vs seeding with prior rules)? Please report variance across runs with different but reasonable orderings.
- Over-weighting α: How is α chosen? Can you report performance curves vs α and its effect on NCD and sDL? Is there a cross-validation or MDL-consistent heuristic to control complexity inflation?
- Scalability: Please provide run-time/memory as a function of N, |D|, and |R|, and detail the partial order reduction. What are the practical limits today, and what approximations (e.g., A*, beam search, pruning) are most promising to reach larger N?

Overall, the paper presents an interesting MDL framing and yields linguistically plausible qualitative patterns, but the absence of rigorous quantitative comparisons and the reliance on subjective overweighting make it difficult to endorse at this stage. Strengthening with solid baselines, formal coding details, and more comprehensive evaluation would significantly improve the case.
