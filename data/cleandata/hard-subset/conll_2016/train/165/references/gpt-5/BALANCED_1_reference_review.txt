Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework for discovering string-level correspondence rules across multiple languages without parametric assumptions on rule shape or distribution. The model treats correspondence rules as tuples of substrings (possibly empty for some languages), scores models with a two-part code L(M)+L(D|M), and learns rules via an EM-style procedure: exact alignments using Dijkstra on a graph (E-step), followed by greedy rule merges that reduce description length (M-step). The authors also introduce a “shared Description Length” (sDL) to quantify structure shared by any subset of languages. Experiments on Slavic languages show: (i) pairwise NCDs roughly recover known subfamilies and (ii) a four-way analysis (CS-PL-RU-BG) highlighting cross-subset regularities. They additionally propose “data over-weighting” to elicit finer-grained rules.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) An MDL formulation and deterministic algorithm for discovering multi-language correspondence rules of arbitrary length directly from orthographic data, including an exact alignment procedure and a greedy merge-based maximization.
2) A multi-language analysis tool, sDL, that quantifies how much structure is shared by any subset of languages (beyond pairwise).
3) An empirical case study on Slavic languages demonstrating plausible correspondences and phylogenetic structure; and a knob (over-weighting) to trade off statistical conservatism vs. rule granularity.

Strengths
1) Clear, principled MDL objective and interpretable outputs. The two-part code is carefully laid out, the modeling choices are motivated, and the induced rules (e.g., cz–č, metathesis, palatalization) are interpretable to linguists. The framework can incorporate initial rule sets, supporting expert priors.
2) Multi-language generalization beyond pairwise alignment. The ability to learn rules defined over arbitrary subsets, and to quantify shared structure via sDL, is a real advantage over purely pairwise methods and yields analyses (e.g., RU–BG vs. CS–PL) not easily accessible otherwise.
3) Exact alignment and deterministic training pipeline. Casting alignment as shortest paths and using a deterministic greedy merge makes the behavior predictable and replicable (modulo EM local minima), which is valuable for linguistic analysis and auditability.
4) Empirical sanity checks. The pairwise NCD matrix and NJ tree largely recover recognized Slavic subfamilies, including expected outliers (BG/MK, Sorbian, Polish). The qualitative examples show rules of different granularity that align with linguistic intuitions.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to existing literature and missing comparisons. MDL-based pattern mining for sequences (e.g., Tatti & Vreeken 2012) and probabilistic/cognate-based correspondence discovery (e.g., LexStat; PMI-based sound correspondence; Bouchard-Côté et al.; Jäger; Rama et al.) are closely related. The paper does not empirically compare against strong baselines (PMI/ALINE/Needleman–Wunsch with learned costs, LexStat, Bayesian sound change models) or show advantages in accuracy, robustness, or scalability.
2) Evaluation is largely qualitative and reconstructs known groupings. Beyond NCD-based trees aligning with conventional classifications, there is no quantitative evaluation: no gold alignments, no cognate identification accuracy, no reconstruction tasks, no extrinsic downstream use (e.g., cross-lingual lexicon induction). The four-language sDL analysis is insightful but lacks statistical tests or concrete utility measures.
3) Scalability and practicality concerns. The alignment and M-step complexities are high; the current implementation caps at 5 languages and takes hours on modest hardware. There is no systematic study of scaling in N, vocabulary size, or rule count, nor memory profiling. This limits applicability to larger multilingual settings.
4) Methodological clarity gaps. Several key choices need more formalization: (i) the rule-merge operation (compatibility constraints, search space pruning); (ii) handling of empty strings/insertions/deletions and their regularization; (iii) details of the data-to-model coding terms (especially in L(Π)) and potential double counting; (iv) how L(X,Y) is computed for NCD (train jointly or evaluate with fixed models?). Without these, reproducibility and theoretical soundness are harder to assess.
5) Over-weighting undermines MDL objectivity and is under-justified. While pragmatic, introducing α to overweight data blurs the statistical interpretation. There is no principled guidance for choosing α, no ablation on its effects, and examples rely on over-weighting to remove singletons. A Bayesian or hierarchical MDL alternative could achieve similar effects more principledly.

Questions to Authors
1) NCD computation: Do you train one model jointly on (X,Y) to obtain L(X,Y), and separate models for L(X,X) and L(Y,Y)? Or do you fit one model and reuse it for encoding? Please clarify the exact protocol and its implications for symmetry.
2) Rule merging: What are the formal compatibility constraints for merging two rules? Do you restrict merges to substrings that co-occur adjacently in alignments? How do you avoid combinatorial explosion in candidate merges?
3) Alignment graph: Are deletions/insertions modeled as rules with ε on some sides? How do you regularize the use of many-to-ε rules to prevent degenerate segmentations?
4) Data-to-model codes: Could you provide derivations for the composition terms in L(Π) and L(Σi), and justify the choice of coding counts from rules (vs. from aligned data) to ensure no double counting across model/data parts?
5) Over-weighting: How sensitive are results to α and to per-item weighting? Can you provide a principled criterion (e.g., MDL model selection across α, or validation performance) to choose α?
6) Reproducibility: Will code and preprocessed datasets (including transliteration/normalization steps) be released? What are the exact iterations/early-stopping criteria and Laplace constants used?

Decision
Reject.

Rationale: The core idea—an MDL framework for multi-language correspondence discovery with deterministic learning—is interesting and the outputs are interpretable. However, the paper lacks empirical comparisons to strong baselines, provides mostly qualitative validation on small datasets, and leaves important methodological details underspecified. Scalability is limited, and the over-weighting mechanism, while practical, is not principled. Strengthening these aspects would considerably improve the paper.

Suggestions for improvement
- Add quantitative evaluations: (i) gold-standard correspondences/alignments where available; (ii) cognate identification or cross-lingual lexicon induction; (iii) reconstruction tasks; (iv) predictive compression on held-out data. Report ablations (with/without over-weighting, with/without priors).
- Compare against established baselines (LexStat/PMI-based, ALINE, dynamic time warping with learned costs, Bayesian sound change/PHMM models) under controlled settings.
- Clarify and formalize the rule-merge operation, alignment graph construction, and coding terms; include complexity analyses and pruning strategies. Consider approximate alignment (e.g., A*, beam search) or batching to scale beyond 5 languages.
- Provide guidance or a principled alternative to over-weighting (e.g., hierarchical priors, MDL with richer model classes, or model selection over α).
- Expand datasets beyond Slavic and beyond Swadesh lists; include phonemic representations to disentangle orthography from phonology, and test on diverse language families.
