Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework to discover correspondence rules among more than two languages directly from orthographic cognate tuples, without imposing a parametric form on correspondences. The model defines a two-part code over a rule table Π and data alignments; alignments are computed as shortest paths in a graph; parameters are optimized via an EM-like loop where the E-step finds optimal alignments and the M-step greedily merges rules that reduce total description length. The approach yields pairwise Normalized Compression Distances (NCD) and an information-theoretic measure of shared description length (sDL) across all language subsets. Experiments on Slavic languages produce a plausible phylogeny and interpretable correspondences; a four-language analysis illustrates sDL-based comparisons.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A deterministic MDL formulation and training algorithm for inducing multi-language correspondence rules of arbitrary length, with an alignment-as-shortest-path formulation.
2) A proposal to quantify similarity among all subsets of languages via decomposition of model description length (sDL), enabling multi-way rather than pairwise-only analysis.
3) An empirical case study on Slavic languages showing that the induced rules are linguistically sensible and that NCD-derived distances agree with known groupings.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and interpretability. Modeling correspondences as arbitrary string tuples across N languages with no prior distributional assumptions is elegant and produces directly interpretable rules (e.g., cz~č, liquid metathesis, palatalizations). The ability to seed with prior rules supports expert-in-the-loop use.
- Deterministic MDL framework bridging pattern mining and alignment. Leveraging two-part MDL with a code over rule usage and a shortest-path alignment affords a principled trade-off between model complexity and fit, avoiding ad hoc penalties and parameter priors.
- Multi-language generalization beyond pairwise models. The framework naturally handles N>2 languages with a single model, enabling analyses of cross-language regularities that pairwise approaches miss; the sDL idea is a nice first step toward multi-way similarity quantification.
- Empirical plausibility. The NCD-based phylogeny for 13 Slavic languages aligns with established groupings and highlights expected outliers (Bulgarian/Macedonian, Sorbian, Polish). The four-way analysis produces linguistically meaningful subsets and differences between i/i/j correspondences and Slavic adjectival morphology.
- Practical insights and flexibility. The discussion of data overweighting as a knob to surface near-significant rules acknowledges real data sparsity and provides a pragmatic mechanism for experts to control granularity.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation and lack of baselines. The evaluation is qualitative and relies on expected phylogeny shape. There is no comparison to widely used alternatives for correspondence/cognate modeling (PMI-based alignments; LexStat/ALINE; pair-HMMs; Bayesian sound law induction such as Bouchard-Côté et al., Hruschka et al.). No intrinsic metrics (precision/recall of discovered correspondences vs. a gold subset) nor extrinsic tasks (cognate identification, reconstruction accuracy).
- Orthography-only data confounds signal. All modeling is performed on raw orthography, yielding results partly driven by script and orthographic conventions (e.g., Serbian Latin vs Cyrillic, digraphs in Polish). This limits linguistic validity and inflates apparent similarity due to writing systems rather than phonology. Phonemic normalization or G2P would strengthen claims.
- Methodological specifics and coding details need clarification. Several parts of the code length derivations are under-specified or possibly inconsistent: the exact data-to-model codes used for rule usage and alphabet unigrams; the role of LN(TΠ) and the weak composition terms; the statement “we know that none of the symbols … will have a non-zero count” seems mistaken; how ε entries are penalized; whether codes are prefix-free and whether Kraft equality holds for the stacked scheme. Without a precise accounting, reproducibility and theoretical soundness are hard to assess.
- Learning algorithm scalability and search strategy. While E-step uses Dijkstra, the candidate generation for M-step “merge two patterns” is not fully defined: which rule pairs are considered compatible, do merges depend on adjacency in current alignments, and how is the combinatorial explosion controlled? Complexity bounds are coarse; experiments involve at most five languages and ≤778 tuples; no wall-clock/runtime scaling with |Π|, average word length, or alphabet size is reported.
- The sDL measure may not reflect shared structure in use. sDL sums model costs L(π) for rules defined on specific subsets, but ignores their usage in L(D|M). It can be dominated by the mere number/length of rules rather than their contribution to encoding the data. Without usage-weighting or normalization, interpreting sDL as “similarity” is questionable.

Questions to authors
- Code design: Please provide precise formulas for all code components, including the data-to-model codes for rule usage and alphabet symbol probabilities, and clarify the term with log((T−1 choose |Π|−1)). Are you using enumerative codes for weak compositions of counts across rules/symbols? How do you ensure prefix-freeness?
- ε handling and sparsity: How are empty entries in π penalized? Do you disallow all-empty or near-empty rules? What prevents the proliferation of language-internal substrings?
- M-step candidate generation: What is the exact set of candidate merges per iteration, and how is compatibility defined? Do you only merge rules that co-occur adjacently in alignments, or any pair with overlapping support? What are stopping criteria?
- Overweighting: How is α chosen in practice? Can you report sensitivity analyses showing impact on rule inventory and on downstream quantities (NCD, sDL)?
- Evaluation: Can you add comparisons to standard baselines (e.g., PMI-based alignment/LexStat) on the same Slavic data, and/or a small gold mapping of known correspondences to estimate precision/recall? Could you perform held-out description length or cross-validation to assess generalization?

Additional comments
- Please correct typos and clarify ambiguous statements (e.g., “none … will have a non-zero count”). Provide implementation details (max rule length, Laplace parameter, partial order reduction technique) and release code/data for reproducibility.
- Consider adding phonemic transliteration experiments to mitigate orthography confounds.
- sDL could be augmented with usage-weighted contributions (e.g., sum over π of L(π)+contribution to L(D|M)) or normalized by total description length per subset.

Overall, this is a thoughtful and potentially useful MDL framing for multilingual correspondences with interpretable outputs. Strengthening the theoretical specification and adding comparative and predictive evaluations would significantly improve its impact.
