Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework to discover systematic orthographic/phonological correspondences across more than two languages, learned deterministically from cognate tuples. The authors formalize a two-part code over alphabets and a correspondence rule table, align data using shortest paths over a weighted graph, and optimize via an EM-like procedure that merges rules when this decreases the total description length. They demonstrate the approach on Slavic languages, deriving NCD-based distances and a phylogeny, reporting interpretable rules, and introducing a “shared Description Length” (sDL) to quantify structure shared by language subsets. They also propose “data overweighting” to control the granularity of discovered rules.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An MDL formulation (PS-2 code) and deterministic EM-style algorithm for discovering multilingual correspondence rules with no parametric assumptions on rule shapes or distributions, including an exact shortest-path alignment procedure.
2) A methodology to quantify inter-language similarity beyond pairs via sDL over rule subsets, with empirical analysis on four Slavic languages demonstrating subgroup structure and finer-grained similarities.
3) A practical mechanism (data overweighting) to trade off model objectivity versus rule granularity, enabling expert-guided exploration of correspondence detail.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and generality of the MDL framework: The two-part code over alphabets and rules directly embodies the modeling goal (compactly describing multi-language cognate tuples) without strong priors on rule shapes. The approach naturally supports more than two languages, which is a meaningful step beyond many pairwise-focused prior works.

- Deterministic, interpretable learning with linguistically meaningful outputs: The EM-like merge procedure produces rules that are easy to inspect (e.g., cz–č, ć–t, liquid metathesis patterns), and the authors illustrate how rule granularity changes with overweighting. The ability to seed with expert rules is attractive for linguistic workflows.

- Beyond-pair similarity analysis: The sDL notion is a simple yet insightful way to quantify how much structure is shared by any subset of languages. The four-way analysis shows not only the expected CS–PL and RU–BG relations but also nuanced cross-subset similarities (e.g., substantial overlap of RU–BG in the four-way setting), going beyond what a tree captures.

- Empirical demonstration aligns with known linguistic structure: The NCD-based phylogeny for 13 Slavic languages broadly recovers the canonical classification and script effects (Latin vs. Cyrillic SC), lending plausibility to the method despite modest data sizes.

- Transparent complexity discussion and implementation choices: The paper explains graph-based alignment, partial-order reduction, EM step complexities, and gives practical limits (N up to ~5 on commodity hardware), which is useful for assessing feasibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation and lack of baselines: There is no quantitative comparison against standard alignment/correspondence discovery methods (e.g., PHMMs, ALINE/Kondrak-style sound correspondences, cognate alignment with feature-based costs, Jäger’s methods, supervised/weakly-supervised transduction). Showing that MDL-based rules yield better likelihoods, alignment accuracy, or phylogeny reconstructions than strong baselines would substantively strengthen the claim of effectiveness.

- Data size and confounds: The main datasets are small (Swadesh lists and a 778-cognate set) and use raw orthography, mixing script and orthographic conventions with phonology. Several reported patterns and NCD effects can plausibly be driven by script/orthography rather than phonology (e.g., SC Latin vs. Cyrillic, Czech diacritics inflating per-language description lengths). A phonemic transcription experiment and/or script normalization would clarify what portion of the signal is phonological vs. orthographic.

- Under-specified coding choices and theoretical grounding: Some components of L(Π) and the data-to-model codes are described briefly and could admit multiple interpretations. The choice of weak number composition and the handling of zero counts, Laplace correction, and code(code(π)) usage could be formalized more rigorously, with derivations or pointers. There are no guarantees on convergence quality or identifiability, and no sensitivity analyses to initialization, smoothing strength, or search ordering.

- Overweighting is ad hoc: While practically useful, the α weighting trades MDL objectivity for user control without a clear criterion for selecting α or guarding against introducing spurious rules. An ablation showing stability of discovered rules as α varies (and how this interacts with dataset size) would be helpful.

- Scalability and robustness: The E-step O(|D|·|R|^2) and M-step O(|D|·A^2) complexities may limit broader applicability; no strategies like pruning, beam search, or stochastic approximations are evaluated. The handling of missing data is mentioned but not tested. It is unclear how performance degrades with noisier cognate sets or non-cognate contamination.

Questions to authors
- Can you provide a more formal derivation of L(Π), including the exact enumeration used for the data-to-model codes and how zero-count symbols/rules are handled? How sensitive are results to these coding choices?

- How does the method compare empirically to PHMM alignment with learned substitution/indel costs, ALINE, or recent neural alignment/transduction baselines on the same data (alignment likelihood, tree accuracy, or rule precision/recall if a small gold set is available)?

- What is the impact of phonemic transcription or grapheme-to-phoneme normalization on both the discovered rules and the NCD/sDL analyses? Do conclusions (e.g., RU–BG closeness) persist?

- How should practitioners choose α for overweighting? Are there diagnostics (e.g., MDL rate–distortion curves, stability of rules across α) to avoid overfitting?

- How robust is the method to non-cognate noise and partial cognate sets? Could you show performance as contamination increases?

Additional comments
- Reproducibility: Please clarify code availability, data preprocessing (e.g., tokenization, handling of digraphs), and all hyperparameters.

- Related work: Consider citing additional work on automatic sound correspondences and phylogeny from string-based distances to situate novelty more clearly.

- Editorial: Several references have formatting issues; figures/tables would benefit from clearer captions and axis labels for sDL plots.
