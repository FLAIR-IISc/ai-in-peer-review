Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper presents an MDL-based framework to discover systematic string-level correspondences across multiple languages without imposing parametric assumptions on rule form or distribution. A deterministic EM-style algorithm alternates between globally optimal alignments (via shortest-path decoding) and greedy rule merging to minimize a two-part MDL objective over a rule table and data.
- The approach yields interpretable, variable-length, multi-language correspondence rules and can incorporate prior knowledge and a user-controlled “data over-weighting” knob to trade off statistical stringency and granularity.
- Empirically, the method recovers plausible correspondences for Slavic languages, induces an NCD-based phylogeny aligned with known subgrouping, and introduces a “shared Description Length” metric to quantify similarity among all subsets in a 4-language setting.

My view of the main contributions (in order)
1) A concrete MDL formulation and deterministic learning algorithm for discovering arbitrary-length, multi-way string correspondences with optimal alignment via graph shortest paths and greedy rule merging.
2) An analysis protocol using MDL-derived distances (NCD) and a shared Description Length statistic to quantify multi-way similarity, demonstrated on Slavic data.
3) A practical mechanism (data over-weighting) to steer the learned rule granularity when true signals are near, but below, MDL significance thresholds.

Strengths
- Clear MDL framing and interpretability: The paper articulates a principled two-part code that explicitly penalizes complex rule sets, yielding compact, meaningful correspondence rules (e.g., palatalization, metathesis, orthographic digraphs). The examples demonstrate that the learned rules are linguistically sensible and readable.
- Multi-language generality: Unlike pairwise cognate alignment models, the model class and alignment procedure naturally extend to N languages, enabling multi-way correspondences and analyses (e.g., the 4-way CS-PL-RU-BG study).
- Deterministic training and ability to inject knowledge: The initialization and merge procedure give reproducibility and a straightforward path to integrate linguistic priors by seeding rules or reweighting subsets.
- Quantification beyond pairwise comparisons: The shared DL metric provides a novel, informative view of how structure is distributed across language subsets. The qualitative conclusions (e.g., RU-BG vs. CS-PL relatedness; Czech overhead due to diacritics) are plausible and insightful.
- Empirical face validity: The induced phylogeny from NCD mirrors the standard Slavic classification, and the method captures script effects (Latin vs. Cyrillic) that one would expect under orthographic modeling.

Weaknesses
- Limited and unbalanced evaluation: The experiments are restricted to Slavic languages and mostly short Swadesh lists plus internationalisms. There is no quantitative evaluation against gold standard correspondences, no extrinsic task (cognate identification, reconstruction), and no comparison to strong baselines (e.g., Levenshtein-based distances, LexStat/SCA, ALINE, pair-HMM or transducer-based models, or prior MDL/Bayesian models of sound change). Showing that trivial baselines produce similar phylogenies would contextualize the contribution.
- Orthography vs. phonology conflation: The model operates on raw orthography, which intertwines script and spelling conventions with phonological correspondences. The observed SC Latin vs. Cyrillic behavior illustrates this confound. Without phonemic normalization or parallel analyses in phonemic/IPA form, it is hard to assess whether linguistic generalizations are phonological or merely orthographic.
- Ambiguities in the code design and potential double counting: The description of L(Π) includes a data-to-model code for rule usage counts and then L(D|M) also encodes each data entry using code(π). It is unclear whether the parameter code is being paid twice or whether counts are handled in a strictly MDL-consistent way (enumerative code vs. plug-in code). A clearer derivation of all terms, their roles, and Kraft-compliant coding would strengthen the statistical soundness.
- Algorithmic details and guarantees: The merge operation (“two compatible rules”) and candidate generation are under-specified. Does the M-step guarantee monotone decrease of total description length over full EM iterations? What is the precise compatibility relation and resulting rule construction (concatenation, overlap, skipping)? How do you avoid catastrophic local minima besides Laplace smoothing? A trace plot of objective vs. iteration and ablations would help.
- Scalability and robustness: The E-step alignment has high worst-case complexity, and memory bottlenecks are acknowledged; experiments beyond five languages were not attempted. There is no analysis of runtime vs. average word length, rule-table size, or number of languages, nor stability under resampling or sensitivity to the over-weighting parameter. The shared DL comparisons across separately trained models may be sensitive to model code overhead and not directly comparable without normalization.

Questions to authors
- MDL code derivation: Please provide a precise derivation of L(Π) and L(D|M), specifying which quantities are encoded where (rule identities, counts, and usage probabilities), and clarify how the data-to-model code interacts with the per-token code(π) in L(D|M). Is there any double-counting of usage statistics? Are you using an enumerative code for counts and then a uniform allocation over compositions?
- Merge operation: What exactly constitutes “compatibility” of two rules, and how is the merged rule constructed (concatenation of adjacent alignments, allowing insertions/deletions, overlaps)? Do you ever split or delete rules, or only merge? How large is the candidate set per M-step?
- Convergence and stopping: Do you guarantee monotone decrease of the total MDL objective after each complete EM iteration (E then M)? What is the stopping criterion? Please include an objective trace and the number of merges until convergence on your datasets.
- NCD protocol: NCD typically assumes a fixed compressor; here, models differ between L(X,X) and L(X,Y). How sensitive are the resulting distances to this adaptivity? Could you report bootstrap confidence intervals for NCDs and the induced tree?
- Baselines and ablations: How does your phylogeny compare to one built from average Levenshtein distance or from a simple pair-HMM aligner with uniform priors? Could you run your method on phonemic transcriptions (or SCA sound classes) to disentangle orthographic effects?
- Over-weighting: How should users set α in practice? Do the induced rules remain stable across a range of α, and can you quantify overfitting risk? Would it be cleaner, for reconstruction scenarios, to exclude single-language rules a priori rather than overweight?

Overall, this is a thoughtful, well-motivated MDL approach with interpretable outputs and promising multi-language capabilities. Strengthening the statistical clarity of the code, expanding empirical comparisons, and addressing orthography/phonology and scalability would substantially increase its impact.
