- Proposes an MDL-based framework to discover multi-language string correspondences, treating correspondences as arbitrary tuples and optimizing a two-part code table.  
- Uses an EM-like procedure alternating optimal multiway alignment (Dijkstra) and greedy merges to minimize description length.  
- Introduces shared description length (sDL) for multiway similarity and a data over-weighting knob (α > 1) to surface near-significant rules.  
- Applied to Slavic languages for pairwise NCD-based phylogeny and four-way sDL analyses with qualitative case studies.  

- Strength: Conceptually simple, assumption-light model with an explicit coding scheme offering interpretability and cross-script applicability.  
- Strength: Extends naturally beyond pairwise alignment to N languages and enables sDL decomposition for subset-shared structure.  
- Strength: Qualitative results are linguistically plausible and interpretable (known sound/orthographic correspondences, sensible phylogeny).  
- Strength: Deterministic training and ability to seed rules appeals to reproducibility and linguistic control.  

- Weakness: Evaluation is limited and largely qualitative with no comparisons to established baselines or quantitative metrics (tree distance, held-out compression, cognate accuracy).  
- Weakness: Coding design is under-specified—data-to-model codes, combinatorial terms, and prefix-free guarantees lack derivation or justification.  
- Weakness: Merging algorithm details (candidate generation, tie-breaking, convergence) are underspecified, limiting reproducibility and soundness.  
- Weakness: No code or implementation details (preprocessing, tie-breaking, smoothing, stopping criteria), undermining reproducibility and determinism claims.  
- Weakness: Over-weighting (α) departs from MDL objectivity without principled selection, risking overfitting to orthographic artefacts.  
- Weakness: Scalability concerns—runtime/memory scaling not analyzed and current implementation limited to ~5 languages and “a few hours.”  

- Q: Can you compare to standard alignment/cognate systems (ALINE, LexStat/LingPy, PMI/PHMMs) on tree accuracy, held-out compression, or cognate detection?  
- Q: Please provide formal derivations/proofs for the coding scheme (prefix-free, normalization) and sensitivity analyses for coding choices.  
- Q: How are candidate merges enumerated, ties resolved, and stopping/convergence handled; do you consider splits or escaping local minima?  
- Q: How is orthographic preprocessing handled (digraphs, diacritics); have you tried sound-class or IPA mapping to reduce script effects?  
- Q: How is α chosen (global or per-item); can you show held-out generalization as α varies to guide principled selection?  
- Q: Can you provide empirical scaling curves and describe pruning/approximation strategies to scale beyond five languages?  

- Overall: Moderate novelty and promising idea but under-specified and under-evaluated; needs clearer coding derivations, quantitative baselines, reproducibility, and scalability analysis to be fully convincing.
