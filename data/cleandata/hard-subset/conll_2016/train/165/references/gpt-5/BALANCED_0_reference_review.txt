Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework (PS-2 code) for discovering string-level correspondence rules across multiple languages without assuming a parametric form for correspondences. It casts alignment as shortest-path search using Dijkstra, learns rule probabilities/costs via an EM-like procedure with greedy rule merging, and introduces a “data over-weighting” knob to encourage longer, multi-character rules. Empirically, it applies the method to Slavic languages to (i) derive pairwise Normalized Compression Distances (NCD) that recover known phylogenetic structure, and (ii) quantify higher-order similarities among language subsets via “shared Description Length” (sDL).

My view of the main contributions (in order of strength):
1) An unsupervised, interpretable MDL formulation and deterministic training procedure for discovering multiway string correspondences, including a practical alignment algorithm and a greedy code-length–reducing merge strategy.
2) A multi-language similarity analysis tool (sDL) that quantifies structure shared by any subset of languages, going beyond pairwise distances; demonstrated on a four-language Slavic case study.
3) A case study showing that MDL-induced NCDs recover the Slavic phylogeny and that learned rules are linguistically interpretable; plus the proposal of data over-weighting as a tunable granularity control.

Strengths
- Clear, principled objective with interpretable outputs: The MDL formulation avoids strong priors about correspondence shape, yet yields a compact set of human-readable rules (e.g., cz–č, liquid metathesis), which is valuable for linguistic analysis. The ability to seed rules enables integrating expert knowledge.
- Handles more than two languages: The model admits rules defined on arbitrary subsets of languages, and the sDL analysis leverages this to quantify fine-grained, subset-specific regularities that pairwise-only methods miss.
- Deterministic learning and practical alignment: Casting alignment as shortest-path decoding is sensible and deterministic; the greedy merge step is aligned with MDL, growing longer rules only when justified by compression.
- Empirical sanity checks and interpretability: The NCD-based tree largely matches the linguistic classification of Slavic languages, and the qualitative correspondences are linguistically plausible. The Cyrillic/Latin Serbo-Croatian behavior is a nice sanity check on orthography effects.
- Useful knob for practitioners: The data over-weighting mechanism is a pragmatic way to control rule granularity when statistical evidence is insufficient, potentially helping downstream tasks like cognate reconstruction.

Weaknesses
- Limited and mostly qualitative evaluation; lack of baselines: There is no quantitative comparison to established tools for cognate alignment or phylogenetics (e.g., PHMMs, ALINE, LexStat/LingPy, Levenshtein/PHYLIP pipelines), nor to prior MDL/MDI-based language distance measures. The tree and examples are encouraging, but not sufficient for a CoNLL-standard empirical validation.
- MDL code specification lacks clarity and derivations: The L(Π) and L(Σi) terms rely on “data-to-model codes” and “weak number composition” without full derivations or references precise enough to reproduce; it is unclear exactly how code(π) and code(σ) are estimated and updated, how zero counts and Laplace correction are handled, and whether the encoding double-counts or omits necessary components. This undermines reproducibility and the core claim of principled MDL modeling.
- Over-weighting deviates from MDL without principled guidance: Introducing α to scale L(D|M) is reasonable pragmatically, but there is no sensitivity analysis, selection criterion, or quantification of its impact on correspondences, NCDs, or trees. This risks arbitrary outcomes and blurs the line between objective MDL and user-driven tuning.
- Scope and data limitations: Experiments are confined to curated Slavic cognate sets in raw orthography, which conflates orthographic and phonological variation. Generality to other families, noisy cognate sets, or non-cognate contamination is not assessed. Dataset sizes are small; there is no held-out or cross-lingual transfer evaluation (e.g., reconstruction accuracy, held-out compression).
- Scalability and complexity: The approach reportedly handles up to five languages after hours of computation on small hardware, with E-step O(|D|·|R|^2) and M-step O(|D|·A^2). There is limited discussion of pruning, caching, or parallelization, and no complexity-vs-performance tradeoff analysis. This may hinder applicability to larger vocabularies or more languages.

Questions to authors
- MDL code details:
  - Please provide complete derivations (or precise references) for the data-to-model codes used in L(Π) and L(Σi), especially the “weak number composition” terms. How exactly are code(π) and code(σ) computed from counts, and where are counts gathered (only from rules or also from data usage)?
  - How is Laplace correction applied in estimating rule probabilities/costs? What prior mass is used?
  - Why is it valid to omit certain terms (e.g., alphabet size encoding) and how does this affect model comparison across different language sets?
- Alignment graph and efficiency:
  - How is the alignment graph constructed (nodes, edges, admissible rule applications) and reduced (partial order reduction)? What are R and A in practice for your datasets? Any heuristics (e.g., max rule length, beam) used to curb explosion?
  - Do you have complexity or memory usage plots vs. number of languages, rule-table size, and average word length?
- Over-weighting:
  - How do you choose α and per-tuple weights in practice? Could you show sensitivity analyses for α on learned rules, NCDs, and the tree topology?
  - Can you relate over-weighting to a principled Bayesian/MDL prior or to tempered likelihood, to justify its use more formally?
- Evaluation:
  - Can you compare against standard baselines (LexStat/ALINE/PHMM/Levenshtein) on the same Swadesh data for tree accuracy (e.g., quartet score, RF distance) and alignment quality (if available)?
  - Can you report held-out compression or reconstruction accuracy (e.g., predict one language’s word from others) to quantify generalization?
  - How robust are results if using IPA or a coarse phonemic mapping rather than raw orthography?
- Data and reproducibility:
  - Will you release code, data, and trained models to facilitate replication and further linguistic analysis?

Recommendation
Reject, primarily due to the lack of rigorous quantitative evaluation and baselines, insufficient specification of the MDL code for reproducibility, and limited experimental scope. The modeling idea is promising, the outputs are interpretable, and the multi-language sDL analysis is interesting. With clearer derivations, stronger empirical comparisons, scalability improvements, and broader evaluation (including held-out metrics and phonetic normalization), this work could become a solid contribution.
