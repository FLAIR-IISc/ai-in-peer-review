Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework to automatically discover string-level correspondence rules across multiple languages. The model treats correspondences as arbitrary tuples of strings (one per language) without assuming parametric distributions, and searches for a code table that minimizes two-part description length of model plus data. An EM-like training alternates between optimal multiway alignment (shortest path with Dijkstra) and greedy merge operations that reduce total code length. The approach is applied to Slavic languages for (i) pairwise analyses via NCD to infer a phylogeny and (ii) a four-language analysis using a “shared description length” (sDL) decomposition to quantify multiway similarity. The paper also introduces a “data over-weighting” knob (α > 1) to surface near-significant rules.

As I see them, the core contributions are:
1) A deterministic MDL formulation for learning multi-language correspondence rules (beyond pairs), including a concrete coding scheme and an EM-with-merging optimization procedure.
2) An analysis methodology for multiway similarity via shared description lengths (sDL), enabling finer-grained views than pairwise distances.
3) A qualitative case study on Slavic languages, showing a phylogeny broadly consistent with linguistic expectations and interpretable correspondences.

Strengths
1) Conceptual simplicity and assumption-light modeling. Treating correspondences as arbitrary string tuples and letting MDL decide their utility avoids strong parametric assumptions common in earlier work. This makes the framework flexible and, in principle, applicable across scripts and language families. The explicit coding scheme anchors the objective and provides interpretability.

2) Extending beyond pairwise alignment. Many prior approaches are inherently pairwise or scale poorly beyond two languages. The proposed alignment graph and coding objective naturally extend to N languages and enable the sDL decomposition, which is a useful idea for quantifying what structure is shared by any subset of languages. The four-way sDL plots are an informative illustration.

3) Qualitative results are plausible and interpretable. The induced rules include well-known sound/orthographic correspondences (e.g., cz–č, g–h, palatalizations, liquid metathesis), and the NCD-based phylogeny for Slavic is broadly sensible, including the effect of scripts (Latin/Cyrillic) and known outliers (BG/MK) due to morphological differences. These lend face validity to the approach.

4) Deterministic training with the option to inject linguistic priors. The ability to seed with known rules and obtain deterministic outcomes (given fixed tie-breaking) is attractive for linguists who want reproducible, controllable analyses.

Weaknesses
1) Limited and largely qualitative evaluation; no baselines. The paper does not compare against established methods for correspondences/cognates/alignment (e.g., ALINE/Kondrak, sound-class-based alignment and LexStat in LingPy, PMI/PHMM-based alignment, Jäger/List/Rama cognate detection, stochastic edit distance). There are no quantitative metrics (e.g., tree distance to a gold phylogeny, held-out compression, rule generalization on a test set, or cognate identification accuracy). The evidence is mostly case studies on a single family (Slavic). This makes it difficult to judge progress over the state of the art or robustness.

2) Methodological clarity and rigor of the code design need strengthening. Several coding choices are underspecified or hard to verify:
- Data-to-model codes are described informally (“weak number composition”), and key combinatorial terms appear without derivation or justification of Kraft–McMillan compliance.
- The rule-table encoding mixes counts, subset stratification, and a data-to-model term; it is not fully clear how prefix-free coding is ensured overall or how choices affect invariance to alphabet size and script.
- The merging step (“merge the two patterns with highest decrease in description length”) is plausible but lacks details on candidate generation, tie-breaking, and convergence behavior. Without proofs or more precise algorithms, reproducibility and soundness are limited.

3) Reproducibility and experimental detail. No code is released. Important implementation details are missing: preprocessing of orthographies (tokenization, normalization, digraph handling, multiword items), stopping criteria, tie-breaking in Dijkstra and merges, Laplace smoothing parameters, partial order reduction specifics, and the exact choice of α and per-item weights for over-weighting. The claim of determinism depends critically on these choices.

4) Over-weighting undermines MDL’s objectivity without principled guidance. While α-weighting is a pragmatic knob, it departs from the principled MDL criterion and risks overfitting to long substrings or orthographic idiosyncrasies. The paper shows qualitative examples but provides no principled selection strategy (e.g., via validation) or quantitative analysis of trade-offs.

5) Scalability and practicality. The alignment graphs can be large; the current implementation handles up to five languages “within a few hours” on modest hardware. There is no analysis of how runtime/memory scale with vocabulary size, alphabet size, or rule set size, nor strategies like beam search, pruning, or batching across items. This limits broader applicability.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
1) Baselines and metrics: Can you compare against standard alignment/cognate systems (e.g., ALINE, LexStat/LingPy, PMI-based models, PHMMs) on (i) phylogenetic tree accuracy (e.g., Quartet or RF distances), (ii) held-out compression, and/or (iii) cognate identification accuracy? Even on Slavic, a quantitative comparison would clarify benefits.

2) Coding scheme: Please provide formal derivations for the data-to-model codes you use (alphabets and rule usage), with proofs or citations guaranteeing prefix-free, normalized codes and explaining the combinatorial terms in L(Π) and L(Σi). How sensitive are results to these choices?

3) Merging step and convergence: How do you enumerate candidate merges efficiently and resolve ties? Do you ever un-merge or consider splits? What is your stopping criterion, and do you observe local minima that trap useful rules?

4) Preprocessing and symbolization: How do you handle digraphs, diacritics, and letter-to-sound ambiguities? Have you tried sound-class or IPA mapping to reduce script effects (e.g., SC Latin vs Cyrillic) and do results improve?

5) Over-weighting: How do you choose α (globally or per-item)? Can you report held-out compression or generalization as α varies to guide users toward principled settings?

6) Scalability: Can you report empirical scaling curves (languages, vocabulary size) and outline pruning/approximation strategies to push beyond five languages?

Overall assessment
Novelty is moderate (MDL + pattern mining adapted to multiway correspondences is interesting), soundness is plausible but under-specified, clarity and reproducibility need work, and the empirical evaluation is too limited and lacks baselines. With stronger quantitative comparisons, clearer coding derivations, and more rigorous experimental methodology, this line of work could be impactful. As it stands, I view it as a promising but not yet fully convincing submission.
