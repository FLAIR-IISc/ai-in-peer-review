Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework to discover string-level correspondence rules across two or more languages without assuming a particular parametric form. The model encodes a set of variable-length, multi-language correspondence rules and uses an EM-style training loop: optimal alignments under the current code are computed via shortest-path search, and rules are greedily merged in the maximization step to minimize total description length. The approach supports deterministic training and can incorporate prior linguistic knowledge through initialization. Experiments on Slavic languages show that pairwise NCD distances recover the known phylogeny and that a new “shared description length” (sDL) analysis can quantify similarities across all subsets of four languages. The authors also introduce “data over-weighting” as a practical knob to encourage learning longer rules when evidence is marginal.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A two-part MDL coding scheme and deterministic EM-style algorithm for inducing variable-length, multi-language correspondence rules with optimal alignments (via Dijkstra) and greedy rule merging under MDL.
2) An information-theoretic analysis tool for multi-language similarity, including the shared description length (sDL) for language subsets, demonstrated on four Slavic languages.
3) An empirical demonstration that the induced code yields pairwise NCDs consistent with Slavic phylogeny and produces linguistically sensible correspondences; the “over-weighting” idea offers a practical control over rule granularity.

Strengths
- Methodological clarity and sound grounding in MDL: The model is a clean two-part code that avoids strong priors on correspondence shape, and the optimization objective is well-motivated. The alignment-as-shortest-path formulation with exact decoding per E-step is a solid choice.
- Multi-language capability beyond pairwise alignment: The framework naturally handles more than two languages, enabling analyses that standard pairwise methods struggle with. The sDL idea is a useful and intuitive way to quantify structure shared by any subset of languages.
- Qualitative linguistic plausibility: The reported rules capture known phenomena (e.g., palatalization, digraphs, metathesis, suffix correspondences) and even expose script effects (Latin vs Cyrillic), which supports the method’s sensitivity to real patterns.
- Determinism and knowledge injection: The ability to initialize from expert-specified rules and train deterministically is attractive for linguistic applications where reproducibility and interpretability matter.
- Practical knob for granularity: The over-weighting mechanism is a simple, effective way to elicit longer correspondences when the data are sparse, which can be useful for reconstruction tasks.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical evaluation and baselines: The main quantitative result is that NCD recovers an expected phylogeny, which many reasonable baselines could also achieve. There is no comparison to established methods for sound correspondence or cognate modeling (e.g., PMI-based approaches such as LexStat/LingPy, PHMMs, or other alignment/segmentation models), no quantitative accuracy on known gold correspondences, and no evaluation on downstream tasks (e.g., cognate identification or reconstruction).
- Orthography confounds and data curation: Using raw orthographies and mixing scripts risks learning script-specific or orthography-driven correspondences rather than phonological ones. While this effect is discussed, there is no experiment with phonemic normalization/transliteration to assess robustness, nor any cross-family evaluation to demonstrate generality.
- Scalability and complexity: The approach currently scales to about five languages and shows O(|D|·|R|^2) E-step cost; memory/runtime bottlenecks are acknowledged. There is no exploration of approximations or engineering (e.g., pruning, WFSTs, A*/beam search) to support larger language sets or longer words, limiting practical impact.
- MDL coding details and reproducibility: Several coding components (e.g., data-to-model codes for rule usage, the combinatorial terms in L(Π), how code(π) is derived from counts) are not fully specified, making it hard to verify prefix-free coding and avoid double counting. Stopping criteria, tie-breaking, initialization variants, and other implementation details are not described, and code/data availability is not mentioned.
- Over-weighting departs from MDL and is under-justified: While practical, it introduces subjective tuning without a principled selection strategy or analysis of its effect on NCD/sDL. It risks overfitting and blurs the paper’s “objective” MDL claim without quantitative safeguards.

Questions to Authors
- How exactly are L(X,X), L(Y,Y), and L(X,Y) computed for NCD? Are models trained and evaluated on the same data? Could you provide cross-validated NCD (train the code on one split, encode held-out words) to assess generalization?
- Please detail the derivation of L(Π) and the rule-usage coding: how do you define code(π) and the combinatorial terms, and how do you ensure no double-counting between L(M) and L(D|M)? A worked example would help.
- What constitutes “compatible” rules for merging in the M-step? Are merges limited to concatenation of adjacent rules observed in alignments? Is there a max rule length? How do you avoid combinatorial explosion?
- Determinism: How do you break ties (both in alignment and in merge selection)? Do different initializations (null vs. seeded) converge to the same model? Please report sensitivity analyses.
- Over-weighting: How do you select α in practice? How sensitive are the induced rules, NCD, and sDL to α? Can you provide a curve showing rule sizes and MDL vs. α, and any safeguards against overfitting?
- Orthography vs phonology: Have you tried phonemic transcription or consistent transliteration to reduce script effects? How do results change?
- Can you compare against at least one strong baseline (e.g., LexStat/PMI, PHMM-based aligners) on a task with quantitative ground truth (e.g., known correspondences, cognate identification)?

Additional comments
- Several references have formatting issues; please correct and expand related work to include modern correspondence-induction and cognate-detection literature.
- Clarify stopping criteria, regularization (Laplace constant), and provide pseudocode for training and alignment with complexity parameters.
- Consider normalizing sDL by total model cost to ease cross-setting comparison; add uncertainty estimates (e.g., bootstrap) for trees and sDL.

Recommendation: Weak reject. The core idea is elegant and promising, and the qualitative results are compelling, but the current submission lacks rigorous empirical comparisons, clarity on coding details, and evidence of robustness. With stronger baselines, quantitative evaluation, clearer derivations, and improved scalability/robustness analyses, this work could make a solid contribution.
