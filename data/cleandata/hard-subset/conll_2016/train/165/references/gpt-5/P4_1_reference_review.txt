Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes an MDL-based method to discover string-level correspondence rules among more than two languages without imposing prior assumptions on rule shapes or distributions. The model uses a two-part MDL code to jointly encode (i) a rule table associating substrings across N languages and (ii) the data aligned with these rules. Alignments are found as shortest paths in a rule-application graph (via Dijkstra), and rules are learned with a deterministic EM-like procedure: E-step aligns data given current rule costs; M-step greedily merges rule pairs that most reduce total description length. A data over-weighting mechanism (scaling the data term by α>1) is introduced to encourage inclusion of longer rules when evidence is marginal. Experiments on Slavic languages produce pairwise NCD distances and an inferred phylogeny consistent with linguistic groupings, and a four-way analysis quantifies shared structure via “shared description length” across all language subsets.

Contributions (as assessed):
1) An MDL formulation and deterministic learning algorithm for multi-language substring correspondences, including a concrete coding scheme and an EM-with-greedy-merging optimization.
2) A methodology to quantify cross-language similarity beyond pairs using NCD and the proposed shared description lengths over all subsets, demonstrated on Slavic languages.
3) A practical mechanism (data over-weighting) to trade off MDL objectivity for more fine-grained correspondences when data are sparse.

Strengths
- Principled MDL framework with explicit model and data codes: The paper specifies a two-part code for alphabets and rule tables (including use of universal integer codes and data-to-model codes), and a data code based on best-usage coding. This provides a clear optimization objective grounded in information theory, aligning with prior MDL work and avoiding strong distributional assumptions on rules.

- Multi-language capability and interpretable outputs: The formulation naturally handles N>2 languages and yields human-interpretable correspondence rules of varying granularity (e.g., orthographic digraphs, phonological alternations, affixes, stems). The four-way examples illustrate that the method can discover rules linking subsets of languages, supporting nuanced analysis.

- Empirical evidence consistent with linguistic knowledge: Pairwise NCD distances and the derived neighbor-joining tree recover established Slavic groupings and reasonable outliers (e.g., Bulgarian/Macedonian). This supports that the discovered correspondences capture real structure rather than artifacts.

- Quantification beyond pairwise similarity: The shared description length (sDL) analysis provides a novel, information-theoretic quantification of structure shared across all subsets of languages (e.g., CS-PL vs RU-BG vs four-way), revealing patterns not visible in pairwise-only approaches.

- Determinism and knowledge injection: The algorithm is deterministic given initialization, and it supports seeding with known rules. This is attractive for reproducible linguistic analysis and for incorporating expert knowledge.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation and lack of baselines: The paper does not compare against established methods for cognate alignment/correspondence discovery (e.g., supervised/unsupervised cognate alignment, morphological alignment, Snyder & Barzilay-style methods), nor does it evaluate alignment quality against gold correspondences. The primary validation is the NCD tree and qualitative examples; no extrinsic or intrinsic quantitative metrics are reported.

- Orthography/script confounds and reliance on raw forms: Operating on raw orthography conflates phonological and orthographic correspondences (including script), as acknowledged by the Serbo-Croatian Latin/Cyrillic divergence. The claim of “objective” similarity is undermined when script and diacritics drive model complexity and distances. No experiments on phonemic transcriptions or robustness across scripts/normalizations are presented.

- Scalability and computational efficiency: Alignment via Dijkstra over a combinatorial graph and greedy merging leads to memory/runtime bottlenecks; the method is reported to handle up to five languages on modest hardware for small datasets (Swadesh lists and 778-item set). There is no clear path or complexity guarantee for larger vocabularies, longer words, or more languages, limiting practical applicability.

- Under-specified technical details and reproducibility: Several components require more precision to assess soundness and to reproduce results: (i) full specification/derivation of the data-to-model codes used for rules and alphabets; (ii) how code(σ) and code(π) are estimated and updated; (iii) definition of “compatible” rules and the mechanics of the merge operator; (iv) Laplace correction setting; (v) tie-breaking and convergence behavior of the greedy M-steps; and (vi) sensitivity to initialization versus seeded rules. Code availability is not indicated.

- Data over-weighting departs from MDL without principled selection: The α>1 weighting to induce richer rules is useful in practice but breaks the MDL objective. The paper does not provide guidance on selecting α, quantify the effect on distances or rule stability, or analyze sensitivity. This makes the knob subjective and may bias cross-language comparisons.

Questions to authors
- Baselines and metrics: Can you compare to cognate alignment or correspondence discovery baselines (e.g., alignment F1 against a small annotated set, or performance in a reconstruction/translation task)? Even limited-scale quantitative evaluation would strengthen claims.

- Coding details: Please precisely define the data-to-model codes used for L(Π) and L(Σi), including the “weak number composition” and assumptions about known alphabet sizes. How are code(σ) and code(π) estimated from counts, and at which points are they updated?

- Merge operation: What exactly defines two rules as “compatible,” and how is the merged rule formed? Is there a bound on candidate merges considered per M-step, and how are ties broken to ensure determinism?

- Complexity and scalability: Provide empirical runtime/memory vs number of languages, average word length, and dataset size. What pruning or partial-order reductions are used in the alignment graph? Any strategies (e.g., beam search, A*) to scale to larger N?

- Initialization and sensitivity: How sensitive are results to starting from the null alignment vs seeded rules? Do seeded initializations materially change the learned rule sets and NCD/sDL outcomes?

- Over-weighting α: How is α chosen in practice? Please report sensitivity analyses showing how α affects (i) the rule inventory, (ii) NCD distances, and (iii) sDL profiles.

- Orthography vs phonology: Have you tried phonemic transcriptions or script normalization? To what extent do the results persist when orthographic variation is reduced? Can you quantify the script effect observed for Serbo-Croatian?

- Handling missing data: Although the algorithm can handle gaps, experiments filtered to complete tuples. Could you demonstrate performance with missing entries, and does it materially affect correspondence discovery or distances?
