- Proposes an MDL-based PS-2 code framework to discover string-level correspondence rules across multiple languages without assuming parametric forms, learned via an EM-like deterministic E-step (shortest-path alignment) and greedy M-step (rule merges).
- Introduces a concrete coding scheme for model and data (alphabets, rule tables with subset-of-languages patterns, usage counts) to compute description lengths, enabling NCD distances and multi-way shared Description Length (sDL) analyses.
- Empirical application to Slavic Swadesh + internationalisms shows interpretable rules, an NCD matrix and neighbor-joining tree consistent with known groupings, and sDL insights into shared structures and script effects.

- Strength: MDL objective yields compact, human-interpretable rules with explicit trade-off between model complexity and data fit, aligning with linguistic analysis goals.
- Strength: Native N-way handling and sDL provide a principled information-theoretic method to quantify structure shared by any subset of languages beyond pairwise methods.
- Strength: Empirical signals (NCD and tree) are coherent with linguistic knowledge, including plausible deviations explained by morphology and script differences.
- Strength: Deterministic training with option to seed linguistic priors and control granularity makes outcomes repeatable and practically useful for linguists.
- Strength: Clear algorithmic instantiation of alignment as shortest-path over an N-way lattice with complexity discussion and partial order reduction mentioned.

- Weakness: Evaluation is limited and fragile—restricted to Slavic, small lists, with no quantitative alignment/rule gold-standard comparison or baselines (PHMM, ALINE, LexStat, modern models).
- Weakness: Scalability concerns—alignment graph can explode; experiments capped at ~5 languages and runtime is hours; complexity bounds lack precise definitions and partial-order reduction is underspecified.
- Weakness: Coding scheme lacks clarity and formal derivations; potential inconsistencies (e.g., data-to-model terms, L(Π) composition, treatment of empty components) risk biasing results and need rigorous justification.
- Weakness: Introduction of ad-hoc data over-weighting α undermines MDL objectivity without guidance on selection or analysis of its impact on NCD/sDL and overfitting.
- Weakness: Reproducibility and presentation issues—no code/data release mentioned, preprocessing and implementation details (orthography normalization, digraphs, pruning, smoothing, stopping criteria) are incomplete, and references/typos distract.

- Question: Please derive the data-to-model terms in L(Π) and L(Σi), clarify how code(π) and code(σ) are computed from counts, and explain the apparent typo about symbol counts.
- Question: Precisely define the N-language alignment graph (nodes/edges, multi-step edges), specify partial order reduction, and state any limits on rule length or branching and pruning heuristics.
- Question: How are candidate rule pairs selected for greedy merges, what is the stopping criterion, are splits/removals considered, and are there monotonicity/convergence guarantees?
- Question: Provide sensitivity analyses for initialization (null vs seeded), Laplace smoothing constants, and α over-weighting, with ablation/stability results.
- Question: Can you compare against established cognate alignment/sound correspondence baselines and test on a non-Slavic family to show generality?
- Question: Will you release code and data, and can you provide full preprocessing and implementation details to enable replication?

- Overall: Compelling MDL framing and interpretable multi-way analyses, but requires stronger empirical validation, clearer coding derivations, scalability analysis, and better reproducibility to meet publication standards.
