Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework to discover string-level correspondences across multiple languages without assuming a parametric form for correspondences. The method represents rules as tuples of strings (allowing empty sides), learns rule costs via an MDL code, aligns cognate tuples via shortest-path search in a rule-graph, and iteratively refines rules using an EM-like procedure with greedy merges. The authors introduce “data over-weighting” to push inclusion of larger rules, and illustrate results on Slavic Swadesh lists and a small set of cognates, reporting NCD-based phylogenies and an information-theoretic measure of shared description length across 2–4 languages.

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An MDL code and deterministic heuristic for learning multi-way (N-language) substring correspondence rules coupled with exact shortest-path alignment under learned costs.
2) An exploratory analysis demonstrating NCD-based trees and “shared description length” (sDL) visualizations for Slavic languages, including 4-way correspondences.
3) The notion of “data over-weighting” to trade off model complexity vs. rule granularity.

Strengths
- Clear, principled framing via MDL. The authors work through a two-part code, separate model and data terms, and connect rule usage costs to code lengths. This is a coherent information-theoretic perspective that is attractive for unsupervised structure discovery and is under-explored for multilingual correspondences.
- Multi-language alignment beyond pairs. Most prior algorithmic treatments focus on pairwise correspondences. The ability to align 3–4 languages jointly and quantify multi-way shared structure (sDL) is interesting and could be useful for exploratory comparative linguistics.
- Deterministic search and exact alignment subroutine. The use of Dijkstra for exact best-path alignment given a rule set is conceptually clean, and the greedy merge heuristic is transparent. This can appeal to practitioners who value reproducibility and inspectability over black-box stochastic procedures.
- Qualitative rules are plausible. The examples show reasonable correspondences (digraph-to-single-letter, palatalizations, liquid metathesis, endings), suggesting the approach can capture linguistically meaningful patterns from raw orthography without heavy feature engineering.
- The NCD tree broadly matches known Slavic groupings. While unsurprising, it provides a sanity check that the induced compression model is not grossly mis-specified.

Weaknesses
- Lack of rigorous evaluation and baselines. There is no quantitative evaluation of rule quality or alignment accuracy (e.g., against phonemic gold, expert correspondences, or synthetic ground truth), no held-out compression or predictive evaluation, and no comparison to strong, established baselines: LexStat/LingPy (List), PMI-based segment alignment (e.g., Jäger), PHMM/Pair-HMM or WFST-based string alignment, or simple orthographic Levenshtein/PMI clustering for phylogeny. The phylogeny result is not assessed with standard metrics (e.g., Robinson–Foulds) nor compared to baseline trees (e.g., from edit distances or off-the-shelf NCD compressors). Given the paper’s claims of generality and objectivity, the absence of such validation is a critical gap.
- Methodological ambiguities and untested design choices. Several coding details are under-specified: the data-to-model code (“weak number composition”) is not defined; the L(Π) combinatorial terms are not derived/justified; how unigram symbol codes are estimated from rules vs. data is unclear; the compatibility criterion for merging rules is not formalized. There are no ablations showing sensitivity to these choices. Without clarity and tests, it is hard to trust the claimed objectivity.
- Orthographic confounds and data choices. Modeling raw orthography across scripts (Latin/Cyrillic) and counting Serbo-Croatian twice (Latin and Cyrillic) introduce script-level effects that the model then “discovers” (the paper acknowledges this). This undermines claims about linguistic correspondences, especially when phonemic transcriptions are standard in this literature. There is no analysis showing robustness to transliteration or phonemic normalization.
- Determinism vs. “over-weighting” tension. The “data over-weighting” knob explicitly violates the MDL objective and injects subjective control over model complexity, yet the paper emphasizes objectivity/determinism. No principled guidance is offered for choosing α, nor any quantitative analysis of its effect on overfitting, stability, or reproducibility. This weakens the core claim that MDL protects against overfitting and yields unbiased correspondences.
- Scalability and convergence. Complexity analyses are high-level (O(|D|·|R|^2) E-step, O(|D|·A^2) M-step). Reported practical limits are “up to five languages in a few hours on 4GB RAM,” with no systematic scaling study vs. number of languages, word length, or rule set size. The EM-like procedure with greedy merges has no convergence guarantees; no restarts or annealing are used despite acknowledged non-convexity. This raises concerns about local optima and sensitivity to initialization.

Questions to Authors
- Evaluation:
  - Can you provide quantitative comparisons to established baselines (e.g., LexStat/LingPy, PMI-based segment alignment, PHMM), both for pairwise correspondences and phylogeny? For trees, please report distances to accepted gold (e.g., RF distance) and to baseline trees built from edit distance or compressor-based NCD.
  - Can you evaluate on synthetic datasets with planted correspondences (including multi-way rules) to measure recovery under noise and data sparsity?
  - Do you have any expert judgments or phonemic gold for a subset to score alignment quality and rule precision/recall?
- Method details:
  - Please formally define the “weak number composition” and derive L(Π), including the combinatorial terms and offsets. How are code(σ) and code(π) estimated and updated? What exactly constitutes “compatibility” when merging two rules?
  - What are the stopping criteria for the EM/greedy procedure? Any guarantees on monotonic decrease of total description length?
- Robustness:
  - How sensitive are results to orthographic variation: if you transliterate everything to a common script or use phonemic transcriptions, how do trees and rules change? What happens if SC Latin/Cyrillic are not treated as separate “languages”?
  - How sensitive are the outputs to α in data over-weighting? Can you show curves of model size, held-out compression, and tree quality vs. α?
- Scalability:
  - Can you report runtime/memory vs. number of languages, vocabulary size, and average word length? Any pruning or beam search that preserves optimality guarantees in alignment?

Recommendation: Weak reject. The MDL perspective and multi-way alignment are interesting, and the qualitative examples are promising. However, the paper lacks rigorous empirical validation against established baselines, relies on orthographic forms that confound linguistic signal, and leaves key modeling choices under-specified. Strengthening the evaluation (quantitative comparisons, robustness tests, and ablations), clarifying the code design, and demonstrating scalability would substantially improve the case for acceptance.
