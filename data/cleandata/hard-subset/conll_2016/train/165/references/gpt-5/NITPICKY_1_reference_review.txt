Summary and Contributions
- This paper proposes an MDL-based framework to discover string-level correspondence rules across two or more languages, without assuming a parametric form for correspondences. It encodes both model and data via a two-part code, aligns cognate tuples via shortest paths, and learns rules with an EM-style procedure that greedily merges patterns to improve description length.
- It applies the method to Slavic languages: (i) pairwise models yield NCD distances and a neighbor-joining tree consistent with broad linguistic groupings; (ii) a four-language model (CS–PL–RU–BG) yields a “shared Description Length” (sDL) decomposition that quantifies similarities among all subsets.
- It introduces a “data over-weighting” knob (α) to drive discovery of longer rules when statistical evidence is marginal.

Strengths
- Conceptually appealing MDL framing: Treating correspondences as arbitrary character strings with no priors is clean and flexible, and MDL offers a principled trade-off between model complexity and fit. The connection to enumerative (data-to-model) codes is appropriate in spirit.
- Multi-language capability: Moving beyond pairwise alignments is a real need. The ability to align N>2 languages simultaneously and quantify shared structure among all subsets (sDL) is interesting and can yield insights that pairwise-only approaches miss.
- Qualitative correspondences look plausible: Examples capture orthographic, phonological, and morphological regularities (e.g., cz–č; g–h; palatalizations; metathesis), indicating the model can recover linguistically meaningful patterns from raw orthography.
- NCD-based tree broadly aligns with known Slavic groupings: The clustering of West/East/South Slavic and the Cyrillic/Latin script effects are sensible, lending face validity to the approach.
- Deterministic training (in principle) and the option to seed with prior rules: Useful for linguists who want reproducibility and/or to inject expert knowledge.

Weaknesses
- Methodological underspecification and inconsistencies in the code design: Key elements are unclear or self-contradictory. Examples:
  - L(Π) and the data-to-model terms: “Additionally … the data-to-model code is given by the weak number composition …” is not formally defined; the combinatorial terms (TΠ−1 choose |Π|−1) and analogous alphabet terms are introduced without precise derivation, assumptions (e.g., independence, exchangeability), or consistency checks.
  - The relationship between code(σ), L(Σi), and the enumerative code is ambiguous. Are symbol codes estimated from rule-side counts only, or jointly with data alignments? How are code(π) (rule usage codes) computed exactly?
  - “PS-2 MDL code” is not defined; the extension over Tatti & Vreeken (2012) is not rigorously spelled out.
  - The description length expressions mix universal integer codes LN with multinomial enumerative codes, but the counting conventions and conditioning sets are not clearly specified. This undermines reproducibility and theoretical soundness.
- Alignment and training details are incomplete:
  - Graph construction: what constitutes an edge (compatibility constraints, maximal rule span), how partial order reduction works, and how costs are computed during E-steps are not fully described.
  - M-step “merge two patterns” is not precisely defined: What counts as compatibility/adjacency? Are merges local (within an alignment instance) or global (across occurrences)? How are ties broken to preserve determinism?
  - Complexity claims (E: O(|D|·|R|^2), M: O(|D|·A^2/2)) are not derived; variables R/A are vaguely defined and do not reflect dependence on N or alphabet sizes. Scaling is poor (“few hours” for 5 languages), with no profiling or memory analysis.
- Evaluation is limited and lacks baselines:
  - No comparison to established methods for cognate alignment/correspondences (e.g., ALINE, PHMMs, LexStat/ASJP-style distances, string transducer approaches, or Bayesian/EM models in prior work). This makes it hard to assess whether MDL offers superior accuracy, robustness, or interpretability.
  - The NCD is borrowed terminology; with two-part MDL approximations it is not guaranteed to be a metric. No empirical checks (e.g., triangle inequality violations) or variance analyses (e.g., with bootstrapping) are provided.
  - Quantitative metrics (precision/recall of correspondences on annotated gold, or downstream impact on tasks like cognate detection or reconstruction) are absent. The sDL plots are descriptive but not validated.
- Data and modeling choices reduce linguistic validity:
  - Raw orthography only (no phonemic normalization) conflates digraphs and diacritics with phonology, likely inflating perceived distances (e.g., Polish digraphs). The paper acknowledges this but does not attempt mitigations.
  - The α over-weighting heuristic undermines the objective MDL criterion and risks overfitting; there is no systematic sensitivity analysis or principled selection strategy, yet it is used to shape the rule inventory.
- Reproducibility and presentation issues:
  - No code or data release; many crucial hyperparameters unspecified (stopping criteria, Laplace smoothing magnitude, initialization choice impacts).
  - References are severely malformed and incomplete; figures/tables are inconsistently labeled; numerous typos and formatting glitches. This is below conference standards.

Questions to Authors
- Provide a complete, unambiguous specification of L(M) and L(D|M): formal definitions of all counts, conditioning sets, and the exact enumerative codes used (with derivations).
- How exactly are code(π) and code(σ) computed and updated across EM iterations? Are symbol codes re-estimated from rule-side counts or from full alignments? Are alphabets language-specific or pooled?
- Define “compatible rules” and the merge operation precisely. Are merges constrained to substrings observed contiguously in alignments? Do you allow overlapping merges?
- How is determinism ensured (tie-breaking, traversal order)? Under what conditions does the algorithm converge?
- How is L(X,Y), L(X,X), L(Y,Y) computed for NCD (retrained per pair? shared alphabets?) and does your approximation satisfy basic metric properties empirically?
- Can you report quantitative comparisons to at least one baseline and a small gold set of known correspondences?

Additional Comments (minor but numerous)
- Many typos/spacing issues (e.g., “andBulgarian”, “How, then,” commas, inconsistent use of italics/bold in Table 2).
- Undefined terms/notations: PS-2, weak number composition; clarify binomial notation.
- Table/figure references inconsistent (F 1 vs Figure 1); ensure all figures are present and legible.
- References are not in a consistent bibliographic format and contain placeholders (“? \Q2004\E”); this must be fixed.
- State dataset sizes clearly (Swadesh vs internationalisms, 207 vs 778), and specify preprocessing.

Overall, despite an interesting idea, the paper lacks methodological clarity, rigorous evaluation, and polish. I do not recommend acceptance in its current form.
