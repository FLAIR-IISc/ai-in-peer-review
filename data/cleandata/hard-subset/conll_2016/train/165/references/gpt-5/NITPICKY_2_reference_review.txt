Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper proposes using a two-part MDL framework to induce multiway string-to-string correspondence rules across N languages, learned deterministically via an EM-like procedure that alternates optimal alignments (via shortest paths) and greedy rule merges that reduce total description length.
- It uses the induced models to compute an MDL-based Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) for pairwise language similarity and constructs a phylogeny for 13 Slavic languages. It also introduces a “shared Description Length” (sDL) analysis to attribute model cost to language subsets in a four-language setting.
- It discusses “data over-weighting” (α-scaling of L(D|M)) to subjectively push the model toward longer, less frequent rules when data is sparse.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) A deterministic MDL formulation for learning multi-language string correspondences (model class, code design, alignment via Dijkstra, and greedy merge training).
2) An exploratory application to Slavic languages showing that MDL-induced NCD reflects known groupings and that sDL can decompose similarities across subsets.
3) A pragmatic, though non-MDL, α-weighting scheme to control granularity of discovered rules.

Strengths
- Multiway correspondences without parametric assumptions: The model allows rules spanning arbitrary subsets of languages and strings of arbitrary length, which is uncommon; most prior work is pairwise or assumes specific distributions. The learned rules are interpretable and include phonological, orthographic, and morphological patterns.
- Deterministic training with interpretable outputs: Starting from singleton rules and greedily merging by MDL decrease yields a reproducible pipeline and rules that can be examined by linguists; the examples (e.g., cz–č, palatalizations, liquid metathesis) illustrate this interpretability.
- Empirical clustering aligns with known taxonomy: The NCD-based tree recovers broad West/East/South Slavic structure and explains outliers (e.g., BG/MK case loss, Polish digraphs), suggesting the MDL objective captures meaningful structure even on raw orthography.
- Practical multi-language analysis: The four-way analysis illustrates that the approach scales beyond pairs and can attribute structural cost to subsets, offering a potentially useful diagnostic (even if the exact sDL definition may need refinement).
- Integration of expert knowledge possible: The framework supports initialization with known rules and a tunable α to probe different granularities, which could be valuable in expert-in-the-loop settings.

Weaknesses
- MDL code design is underspecified and possibly inconsistent: The paper mixes a data-to-model code for rule usages and for symbol unigrams with little rigor. In L(Π), the terms LN(TΠ) + log((TΠ−1 choose |Π|−1)) are justified as “data-to-model code” for rule usages, but then L(D|M) also encodes rule usages via code(π). This risks double-counting. For alphabets, L(Σi) uses LN(TΣi)+log((TΣi−1 choose |Σi|−1)), but the connection between these counts, code(σ), and L(D|M) is unclear. The statement “none of the symbols … will have a non-zero count” appears erroneous. Overall, the derivation does not meet the rigor expected for MDL and should be precisely formalized (what is in M vs in D|M; which counts are transmitted where; no hidden parameter leakage).
- Subset encoding and rule specification are incomplete: Rules omit explicit subset indicators per rule; the text says counts per subset are transmitted, but a decoder must know each rule’s subset identity to interpret omitted entries. It is not clear how this is encoded without additional bits. The choice of 2^N−1 categories needs exact coding details and costs; otherwise L(M) is underestimated.
- sDL definition is questionable as a similarity measure: sDL sums L(π), i.e., model code length for defining rules, not their contribution to data encoding. This conflates model complexity with shared regularity; a rare but long rule can dominate sDL even if it explains little data. Cross-model comparisons (pair vs four-way) also suffer from different Σ, |Π|, and T; normalization is missing. A usage-weighted or contribution-to-L(D|M) attribution would be more meaningful.
- Evaluation is limited and confounded by orthography: Data are raw orthographic forms (including script differences), which likely drives some of the NCD results (authors note SC Latin vs Cyrillic effects). No experiments with phonological/IPA normalization, no robustness checks across transliterations, and no comparisons against established baselines (e.g., Levenshtein-based distances, LexStat, PHMM/transducer-based methods, LZ-based NCD). No statistical significance or stability analyses are reported.
- Algorithmic details and complexity: The alignment is framed as Dijkstra on a large graph; complexity claims O(|D|·|R|^2) or O(|D|·A^2/2) are not well justified, and the memory/runtime characteristics are only anecdotal (“few hours” for up to 5 languages on 4GB). No pruning or admissible heuristics are specified beyond “partial order reduction.” Reproducibility is hindered by the absence of code and precise implementation details.
- “Data over-weighting” undercuts the claimed objectivity: While pragmatic, α ≠ 1 contradicts the MDL principle and is used in reported examples to remove singleton rules. The paper should clearly separate objective MDL results from α-tuned explorations and analyze sensitivity to α.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Please provide a precise, non-ambiguous derivation of L(M) and L(D|M): which histograms are encoded where; how code(σ) and code(π) are computed; how subset indicators per rule are encoded; and why there is no double-counting between L(Π) and L(D|M).
- How exactly are L(X,X) and L(X,Y) computed for NCD? Do you re-train per-language and per-pair models and include both L(M) and L(D|M) or only L(D|M)? How do you ensure fair comparability across different model classes?
- Can you report comparisons with baseline distances (e.g., Levenshtein average distance, LexStat scores, gzip-based NCD) and with phonologically normalized data to assess orthography effects?
- For sDL, can you provide a usage-weighted attribution (e.g., by Shapley-style decomposition of L(D|M)) and normalization across models? Current sDL seems dominated by model definition length.
- Clarify the alignment graph: node/edge counts, pruning, admissible heuristics, and why Dijkstra is preferable to dynamic programming formulations. Provide empirical scaling curves in N, |Π|, and average word length.
- What α values were used in over-weighted examples? How sensitive are induced rules and NCDs to α?

Overall, while the high-level idea is promising, the MDL formulation and evaluation need significantly more rigor and breadth. In its current form, I cannot recommend acceptance.
