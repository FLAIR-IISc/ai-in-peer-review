Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes an MDL-based framework (PS-2 code) to discover string-level correspondence rules across multiple languages (beyond pairs). The method defines a two-part MDL objective, aligns cognate tuples via shortest-path search over a rule graph, and iteratively updates rule probabilities and the rule set via an EM-like loop with greedy rule merging. The authors also introduce “data over-weighting” (α > 1) to force inclusion of longer rules deemed “near-significant.” Experiments on Slavic languages report pairwise NCD-based distances and a four-way analysis (CS-PL-RU-BG) with “shared Description Length” (sDL) diagnostics and qualitative examples.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An explicit MDL coding scheme and deterministic greedy search procedure for discovering multi-string correspondence rules across more than two languages, including subset-specific rules (defined on any subset of the N languages).
2) An alignment formulation as a shortest-path problem with exact decoding given a current rule set, and an EM-like training loop that merges rules if they reduce total description length.
3) A qualitative case study on Slavic languages showing plausible phylogenetic structure and interpretable correspondences, and an exploratory multi-language diagnostic (sDL).

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and interpretability: Framing correspondence discovery directly as code length optimization yields readable, linguistically meaningful rules (e.g., cz~č, palatalizations, metathesis). The multi-language, subset-defined rule notion is a natural extension beyond pairwise alignments.
- Exact alignment given a model: Casting alignment as a shortest-path problem and using Dijkstra gives exact optimal alignments under current costs, which is preferable to heuristic decoders.
- Deterministic, knowledge-injectable pipeline: The null-initialization and the option to seed with rule sets to incorporate expert knowledge are well-motivated; the greedy MDL improvement criterion is transparent.
- Multi-language analysis: Moving beyond pairwise analyses to quantify structures shared by different subsets of languages is interesting. The reported sDL profiles and subset-specific rules illustrate nontrivial cross-language regularities.
- Sanity of qualitative findings: The NCD tree and example correspondences broadly align with known Slavic groupings and orthographic tendencies, which supports face validity.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete and sometimes questionable MDL specification: Several parts of the coding scheme are under-defined or inconsistent. Examples: 
  • L(Π): the decomposition mixes per-subset LN(|ΠCi|+1), per-rule L(π), and a data-to-model term LN(TΠ)+log((TΠ−1 choose |Π|−1)) without full justification of the enumeration and with unclear independence assumptions. 
  • The “data-to-model code” for unigrams and for rule usages is invoked but not derived; code(σ) and code(π) are used as if known optimal codes, yet the parameterization and estimation (counts, normalization, smoothing) are not fully specified. 
  • Laplace correction is applied, but its interaction with MDL (two-part codes) is not justified. 
  • The sDL metric sums L(π) over rule subsets to quantify “shared structure,” but L(π) belongs to the model code, not usage; it depends on the chosen code family and is not normalized across models, making cross-model comparisons questionable. Overall, the theory needs tighter derivations and explicit assumptions.
- Algorithmic gaps and claims: The training is called EM, yet the M-step is a greedy merge of two patterns that yields the largest decrease; no proof that this maximizes L(M)+L(D|M) (or even monotonically decreases it) under the stated code. Determinism may depend on tie-breaking and candidate generation. The complexity claims O(|D|·|R|^2) and O(|D|·A^2/2) are asserted without derivation. The use of Dijkstra is plausible, but standard dynamic programming for multi-sequence alignment is not contrasted; “partial order reduction” is mentioned without details. No stopping criterion or guarantees are provided.
- Evaluation is largely qualitative and lacks baselines: No comparison to established cognate alignment/phonetic correspondence methods (e.g., PHMMs, ALINE, LingPy/SCA, pairwise MDL/MDI, Morfessor-like models, Bayesian string transducers). No held-out compression, no ablations (e.g., without subset rules; without overweighting), no sensitivity analysis (e.g., α, Laplace α, maximum rule length), and no quantitative accuracy against any gold correspondences. The NCD-based tree, while sensible, is a weak test relative to extensive prior work.
- Data and preprocessing concerns: The use of raw orthography across mixed scripts and digraph-rich orthographies introduces confounds the paper acknowledges but does not control for (no transliteration/phonemization, grapheme segmentation, or grapheme-to-phoneme normalization). Tokenization of digraphs, diacritics handling, case, punctuation, and alphabet definitions Σi are not precisely described. Using only complete tuples may bias the sample; curation and de-duplication are not discussed.
- “Data over-weighting” undermines the MDL objective: Introducing α>1 to force longer rules departs from the stated objective and risks cherry-picking. No guidance for choosing α, no reporting of how often overweighted rules differ from the objective model, and such “subjective” tuning is used in examples, blurring conclusions.
- Scalability and exponential components: The rule-classification over 2^N−1 language subsets is exponential in N, with no analysis of how costs scale in practice. The graph construction and memory issues are acknowledged but without concrete bounds or strategies.

Questions to authors
1) Provide complete derivations for L(Π), L(Σi), and L(D|M): How exactly are code(π) and code(σ) computed? What are the parameter estimates, smoothing, and enumerations underlying the “data-to-model” binomial composition terms?
2) EM vs greedy merge: In what sense is the M-step an EM maximization? Do you guarantee monotonic decrease of L(M)+L(D|M)? How are candidate merges enumerated, how are ties broken, and what is the stopping criterion?
3) sDL definition and comparability: Why is summing L(π) (model code) an appropriate measure of shared structure? How do you normalize to compare across different models/pairs? Could you report usage-based contributions as well?
4) NCD details: How exactly are L(X,X), L(Y,Y), and L(X,Y) computed (same model class, shared Π vs separate Π)? Is symmetry and triangle inequality approximately satisfied empirically?
5) Preprocessing: How are alphabets Σi defined for languages with digraphs and diacritics? Any transliteration to common script or phonemic representation? How are tokenization and grapheme segmentation handled?
6) Over-weighting: What α values were used where? Can you quantify how overweighting changes learned rules and downstream distances? Any principled criterion to choose α?
7) Baselines and ablations: Can you compare against PHMMs/ALINE/LingPy or MDL serial episode baselines? Also report held-out compression and ablations (no subset rules; capped rule length; no Laplace).

Overall recommendation
Reject. The idea is promising and results are plausible, but the paper has substantial issues in the formal MDL specification, algorithmic rigor, evaluation design, and reproducibility. Many minor (but numerous) technical and formatting flaws further reduce confidence. With stronger theory, clearer algorithms, principled evaluation against baselines, and careful preprocessing, this could become a solid contribution.
