Summary and main contributions
- This paper proposes an MDL-based, deterministic method to discover string-level correspondence rules across more than two languages. The model treats correspondences as arbitrary associated strings with no parametric assumptions, and learns them via an EM-style procedure that alternates optimal multiway alignment (shortest paths over an index-tuple graph) and greedy rule merges guided by description length.
- It introduces a concrete two-part MDL code for this setting (extending prior MDL work on serial episodes), including encoding of rule strings, rule-usage distributions, and per-alphabet symbol codes. The method can incorporate domain knowledge by initializing with seed rules and includes a principled “data over-weighting” knob to bias toward larger patterns.
- Empirically, it applies the approach to Slavic languages (Swadesh lists and pan-Slavic terms), shows that pairwise NCD derived from the model recovers a plausible phylogeny, and demonstrates multi-language analyses via “shared description length” (sDL) decomposition that quantifies similarities among all subsets of four languages.

Strengths
- Conceptual generality and interpretability. Modeling correspondences as free-form multi-string rules without distributional assumptions is well aligned with MDL’s spirit and yields interpretable patterns (e.g., cz–č, g–h, palatalization clusters, metathesis). The capacity to jointly analyze more than two languages and to quantify structure shared by arbitrary subsets is a substantive conceptual advance over pairwise methods.
- Principled objective with deterministic learning. The two-part MDL objective gives a clear trade-off between model complexity and data fit, avoiding ad-hoc regularizers. The deterministic training procedure (null initialization + greedy merges + exact shortest-path alignments) is attractive for linguistic analysis and reproducibility in principle.
- Multiway alignment formulation. Casting optimal alignment over N languages as a shortest-path problem is clean and allows exact alignments for the current model, which strengthens the EM loop’s grounding. The partial order reduction note shows attention to practical tractability.
- Empirical evidence matches known structure. The NCD-based tree largely recovers accepted Slavic subgroupings, and the sDL analysis surfaces plausible cross-subset regularities (e.g., RU–BG sharing and the influence of orthographic inventory on Czech’s individual description length). The Latin vs. Cyrillic effect is captured as expected, illustrating sensitivity to real orthographic differences.
- Practical knobs and knowledge injection. Allowing initialization with known rules and controlled data over-weighting provides useful handles for practitioners who want finer-grained patterns when objective evidence is marginal, without redesigning the objective.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation and lack of baselines. The evaluation is largely qualitative (examples) and structural (a single tree). There is no comparison to established baselines for phonological/orthographic correspondences or cognate modeling (e.g., PMI-based sound correspondences, pair-HMM aligners, ALINE-type distances), no quantitative tree accuracy (e.g., quartet or Robinson–Foulds distance against a gold standard), and no extrinsic task (cognate identification, reconstruction, or cross-lingual lexicon induction). This makes it difficult to assess effectiveness beyond plausibility.
- Orthography confounds and data curation. The method operates on raw orthography; many “correspondences” reflect script and spelling idiosyncrasies rather than phonology (as the paper notes). Swadesh entries need not be true cognates across all languages, and the paper filters only on availability, not cognacy. Without phonemic normalization or cognacy control, discovered patterns and distances may conflate etymology with script conventions, weakening linguistic claims.
- Clarity and rigor of the coding scheme. Several parts of the code specification are hard to follow or appear internally inconsistent. For example:
  - L(Π) mixes rule encoding and a “data-to-model code” for rule usage; the role of LN(TΠ) + log((TΠ−1 choose |Π|−1)) versus the per-use code(π) in L(D|M) is not fully reconciled with standard two-part MDL practice (where one typically encodes parameters and then data with −log p). 
  - The definition of code(π) (rule-use code) and code(σ) (symbol code) reuses names and could be conflated. 
  - The “weak number composition” is mentioned but not defined; counts’ source and normalization are not fully specified.
  Greater precision or an appendix with derivations would improve reproducibility and confidence in optimality claims.
- Algorithmic details and scalability. The EM mechanics are underspecified in critical places: how candidate merges are enumerated and pruned; stopping criteria; how Laplace smoothing interacts with merges; and how often re-estimation happens. Complexity statements are coarse (e.g., O(|D|·|R|^2) for E-step) and do not account for graph size (nodes/edges) nor memory. The method reportedly scales to up to five languages on modest hardware; that limits applicability to richer multilingual settings unless more engineering/algorithmic advances are shown.
- Reproducibility and presentation issues. Code/data availability is not stated. Hyperparameters (e.g., α for over-weighting) and training schedules are not reported. Some references are incomplete/garbled, and several equations and notations are imprecise, which collectively hinder replication.

Questions to authors
- Coding scheme: Please precisely define the “data-to-model code” components in L(Π), the role of LN(TΠ) + log((TΠ−1 choose |Π|−1)), and how this interacts with per-use coding in L(D|M). Are you using ML plug-in codes (−∑ count(π) log count(π)/TΠ) or uniform composition codes, or both? An explicit derivation would help.
- Candidate merges and stopping: How are merge candidates generated per M-step (all pairs of compatible rules globally, or constrained locally)? What is the stopping criterion, and do you re-align after each accepted merge?
- Data filtering: How did you ensure cognacy across languages in Swadesh lists? Did you test robustness when non-cognates/noise are present?
- Baselines and metrics: Can you compare against a PMI-based sound correspondence model or pair-HMM alignment in pairwise settings, and report tree distances to a gold classification? For the four-way analysis, can you validate sDL conclusions against independent linguistic judgments?
- Effect of orthography vs. phonology: What happens if you romanize Cyrillic or map to phonemic transcriptions? Does the inferred tree or sDL structure change materially?

Overall assessment
- Novelty: Moderate. Applying MDL with a bespoke code to multiway string correspondences and introducing sDL is interesting, though related strands exist for pairwise alignment/mining.
- Quality: Mixed. The core idea is sound and examples are promising, but experimental validation is limited and some technical details are unclear.
- Clarity: Moderate. High-level ideas are clear; formal details and references need tightening.
- Significance: Potentially meaningful for unsupervised multilingual form-level analysis, but current evaluation is insufficient to establish impact in NLP.

With stronger baselines, clearer coding derivations, better-controlled data, and a quantitative evaluation, this could be a compelling contribution.
