Summary and main contributions
- An MDL-based framework (two-part code) for learning variable-length correspondence rules across N≥2 languages from cognate tuples, with no parametric assumptions on rule distributions. The model encodes rule strings per language subset, aligns data via shortest-path search, and iteratively refines rules with a deterministic EM-like procedure and greedy merges.
- Information-theoretic analyses of cross-linguistic similarity: (i) pairwise NCD computed from learned codes to derive a Slavic phylogeny; (ii) a “shared Description Length” (sDL) metric that attributes model complexity to specific language subsets in a 4-language setting.
- Case study on Slavic languages (13-way pairwise and a 4-way CS–PL–RU–BG analysis) demonstrating plausible multi-granular correspondences and offering a “data over-weighting” knob to induce larger rules.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and flexibility of the MDL formulation. Framing correspondences as arbitrary string tuples over language subsets and choosing models by minimal description length avoids strong, often unrealistic parametric priors. The code design accounts for sparsity (subset-specific rule encoding), and the alignment as shortest-path is clean and principled.
- Beyond pairwise correspondences. The ability to discover rules that involve any subset of languages is a distinctive and useful feature relative to many pairwise methods. The 4-way analysis and sDL visualization are intriguing tools to quantify which structure is shared across which subsets.
- Deterministic, interpretable learning path. Initialization with singletons, Laplace smoothing, and greedy merging by MDL gain give a transparent learning trajectory that linguists can inspect or steer with seed rules. The learned rules shown (e.g., palatalizations, metathesis, morphology) are linguistically sensible.
- Practical insights into scripts and orthography. The observation that SC Latin vs. Cyrillic induces clustering by script highlights the sensitivity to orthography; while a caveat for phylogeny, it demonstrates the method’s ability to capture writing-system correspondences, which could be valuable for transliteration or orthography harmonization tasks.
- Potentially useful knob (data over-weighting) for practitioners. While not MDL-pure, the α-weight provides a pragmatic control to elicit larger rules when resources are limited or when one aims at reconstruction applications.

Weaknesses
- Limited and partly confounded evaluation. There is no quantitative comparison to established baselines (e.g., ALINE/Kondrak; PMI-based sound correspondences/Jäger; LexStat/List & Moran; PHMM/PMI aligners; Bayesian cognate and sound change models such as Bouchard-Côté et al.). The phylogeny is not assessed with standard measures (e.g., Robinson–Foulds distance to a reference tree, bootstrap). The reliance on small Swadesh lists and on internationalisms risks conflating borrowing and orthographic convention with inheritance, and the script mixing (Latin vs. Cyrillic) further confounds genealogical signals.
- Scalability and efficiency claims are weak. The alignment graphs can become memory-intensive, and the authors report practical limits around N≈5. The E-step complexity O(|D|·|R|^2) and M-step O(|D|·A^2) suggest scalability concerns for longer words, larger alphabets, and higher N. Without pruning strategies, batching, or approximate decoding, the approach may be hard to apply to larger multilingual datasets.
- MDL code specification needs tighter justification. The paper mixes data-to-model codes for usage distributions into L(Π) and encodes alphabet symbol distributions in L(Σi), but it is not fully clear how these relate to L(D|M) without double-counting. The derivations for the weak composition terms and their role relative to Shannon-optimal usage coding in L(D|M) should be clarified. The sDL metric sums L(π) (model code lengths), not their contribution to data encoding; it is unclear whether this best reflects shared structure as used in practice.
- Over-weighting α breaks MDL optimality. While practically appealing, the method departs from the MDL principle without a formal analysis of the induced bias. Claims that rules still emerge “in the correct order” lack theoretical or empirical support (e.g., sensitivity analyses). This raises concerns for conclusions drawn from over-weighted models.
- Missing related work and context. Important strands of historical NLP and phylogenetics are not discussed (e.g., ALINE; PMI/PHMM approaches; LexStat/SCA; Bayesian phylogenetic models for lexical data; recent work on multilingual cognate induction and sound correspondence inference). Positioning relative to these would help assess novelty and applicability.

Questions to authors
- NCD computation: Precisely how are L(X,X) and L(X,Y) computed? Do you train on X (or X∪Y) and encode the same data again, or do you use cross-coding? Please specify training/encoding splits and whether code tables are re-estimated per condition.
- MDL code details: Can you provide a full derivation of L(Π) and L(Σi), including the data-to-model terms and why they belong to L(M) rather than L(D|M)? How do you avoid double-counting usage information across the two parts?
- sDL definition: Why sum L(π) rather than each rule’s contribution to L(D|M) (e.g., ∑usage(π)·L(code(π))) to quantify shared structure? Have you compared both choices?
- Sensitivity and robustness: How sensitive are results to initialization (null vs. seeded), Laplace constant, and α? Please include ablations and variance across random seeds (if any randomized component is used).
- Scalability: What are typical |R| and |A| observed in your runs? Can you report wall-clock, peak memory, and dataset characteristics (avg. token length) per experiment? Any heuristics (beam search, A* with admissible heuristics, rule length caps) to scale beyond five languages?
- Baselines: Could you compare against ALINE/PMI/PHMM/LexStat on the same data (pairwise trees and correspondences), and report quantitative agreement with a reference Slavic tree?

Additional comments
- Consider including a phonemic version of the data to decouple orthography from phonology, and treat Latin/Cyrillic as the same language with different scripts to test robustness.
- Reproducibility: Please release code, data splits, and learned rule tables.
