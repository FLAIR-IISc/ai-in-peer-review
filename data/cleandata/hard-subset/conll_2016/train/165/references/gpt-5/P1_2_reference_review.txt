Summary and claimed contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework to automatically discover string-level correspondence rules across two or more languages from cognate lists, without assuming a particular parametric form. The model encodes a set of correspondence rules (tuples of strings over language-specific alphabets) and the data aligned by these rules using a two-part MDL code, and searches for models that minimize total description length. Alignments are computed exactly as shortest paths in a graph; model learning proceeds by an EM-style loop that alternates exact alignment (E) and greedy rule merging to reduce description length (M). The approach is demonstrated on Slavic languages for (i) pairwise NCD-based phylogeny and (ii) a four-language analysis, including a proposed “shared description length” metric over language subsets. The paper also introduces “data over-weighting” to let experts promote larger correspondences.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An MDL formulation and deterministic algorithm for learning multi-language correspondence rules at the string level, with exact alignment by shortest paths and greedy merge-based model refinement.
2) An information-theoretic analysis of multi-language similarity via shared description length over all subsets of languages, going beyond pairwise comparisons.
3) An empirical case study on Slavic languages showing that NCD derived from the learned code recovers known subgroups and yields plausible correspondences; plus the notion of controlled data over-weighting to tune correspondence granularity.

Strengths
- Principled, flexible modeling: Framing correspondences as MDL coding of rule tables and aligned data is conceptually clean and avoids strong distributional assumptions. The rule class (tuples of arbitrary-length strings with possible empty entries) is expressive enough to cover orthographic, phonological, and morphological correspondences, including deletions/insertions across languages.

- Exact alignment and clear EM structure: Casting alignment as shortest-path in a weighted DAG and using Dijkstra for exact solutions is a solid engineering choice. The alternation between exact E-steps and greedy M-steps grounded in description-length reduction provides an intuitive search heuristic that prioritizes statistically supported merges.

- Multi-language analysis beyond pairwise: The shared description length (sDL) analysis is novel and informative, quantifying structure shared by all subsets, not just pairs. The four-way Czech–Polish–Russian–Bulgarian results illustrate how three-way and two-way rules capture real asymmetries (e.g., handling of i/j).

- Empirical plausibility on a real family: The NCD-based phylogeny largely mirrors established Slavic groupings, with reasonable deviations (e.g., BG/MK outlier behavior due to morphology and orthography; PL idiosyncrasies). The qualitative correspondences shown are linguistically sensible (e.g., palatalizations, digraphs, metathesis).

- Expert-in-the-loop control: Allowing initialization from seed rules and the proposed data over-weighting offer practical avenues for linguists to steer granularity and integrate prior knowledge, potentially making the tool more useful in descriptive work.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation and lack of baselines: The paper does not compare against established methods for cognate alignment or sound correspondence discovery (e.g., PMI-based models, LexStat/LingPy, PHMMs, ALINE, Bayesian phylogenetic methods). There is no quantitative evaluation of rule quality (precision/recall against gold correspondences), no ablations, and no comparisons against simple baselines (e.g., Levenshtein-based clustering for NCD/trees). As a result, it is hard to assess the method’s practical advantage beyond plausibility.

- Incomplete specification and potential inconsistencies in the code: Key parts of L(Π) and the use of data-to-model codes are under-specified. The combinatorial terms (e.g., weak number composition, uniform coding over counts) and how rule usage probabilities translate into code(π) risk double-counting or miscounting without a fully explicit derivation. Similarly, the alphabets’ unigram codes are estimated from rule strings rather than raw data, but it’s unclear how counts propagate and how Laplace correction interacts with coding. A more rigorous, unambiguous presentation is needed.

- Scalability and practicality: The current implementation scales to at most five languages on small datasets within hours. Many realistic comparative settings involve more languages and larger cognate sets. The M-step search space is only heuristically explored (pairwise merges), with complexity dominated by |D| and the number of applicable rules per item; no pruning, batching, or parallelization details are provided.

- Ad hoc “data over-weighting” undermines MDL objectivity: While practical, reweighting α in L(M)+αL(D|M) departs from the MDL principle and risks encouraging overfitting or artifacts. There is no principled guidance on selecting α, nor an analysis of how weighting affects discovered rules, NCDs, or trees.

- Data limitations and noise: Experiments are restricted to Slavic languages using small wordlists (Swadesh and a curated list), with mixed orthographies and potential inconsistencies in Wiktionary transcriptions. The reliance on raw orthography conflates phonology and script conventions; no normalization/transliteration is attempted. Generalizability beyond Slavic or to nonalphabetic scripts is not demonstrated.

Questions to authors
- Can you precisely derive L(Π) and L(D|M), including the data-to-model codes used, and clarify how code(π) and code(σ) are computed to avoid double-counting? Please define “weak number composition” and the exact enumerations you code uniformly over.

- What is the candidate set for merges in the M-step? Are merges restricted to rules that co-occur adjacently within alignments, or can any pair be merged? How do you avoid creating spurious long rules?

- How do you ensure convergence or mitigate cycling? Do you use a stopping criterion other than “no merge reduces description length”?

- Can you report quantitative evaluations of discovered correspondences against a gold standard (e.g., known Slavic sound laws) or via synthetic data with planted correspondences?

- How sensitive are results (rules, NCDs, trees) to the α weighting and to initialization with or without seed rules? Any stability or bootstrap analyses?

- Could you compare with at least one strong baseline (e.g., LexStat/PMI sound correspondences for the same data) and with simpler NCD baselines from standard compressors or Levenshtein-based distances?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Improve reference formatting and completeness; several entries are malformed. Clarify dataset preprocessing (tokenization, handling of diacritics, Cyrillic/Latin harmonization). Consider publishing code and data to enable replication.

- Explore scalability improvements (A* search with admissible heuristics, beam search, rule candidate pruning, batching, parallelization). An ablation on graph optimizations (partial order reduction) would help.

- Consider phonemic normalization or feature-aware coding to better capture phonological structure across scripts and orthographic idiosyncrasies.

Overall, the paper addresses an important problem with a principled idea and promising qualitative results, but needs stronger methodological clarity and empirical validation against baselines to meet the standards of a computational linguistics venue.
