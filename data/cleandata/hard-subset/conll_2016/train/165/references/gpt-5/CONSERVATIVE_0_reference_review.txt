Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes an MDL-based framework for discovering substring correspondences across multiple languages. Models are sets of “correspondence rules” (tuples of strings), learned with an EM-style procedure: E-step aligns cognate tuples using a shortest-path formulation; M-step greedily merges rules that reduce total description length. An optional data overweighting parameter biases the search toward longer rules.
- It introduces “shared Description Length” (sDL) as an information-theoretic measure quantifying structure shared by any subset of languages, enabling analyses beyond pairwise comparisons.
- A case study on Slavic languages reports pairwise NCD distances (yielding a phylogeny broadly in line with linguistic classifications) and a 4-language analysis (CS-PL-RU-BG) illustrating learned correspondences and sDL distributions.

Strengths
- Clear, principled objective: The MDL formulation is explicit, with a two-part code that attempts to balance model complexity and data fit. This clarity is welcome and offers a reproducible target objective (at least in principle).
- Multilingual scope beyond pairs: Treating correspondences as N-ary tuples and quantifying shared structure across arbitrary subsets is a meaningful step beyond pairwise aligners. The sDL plots are an interesting way to summarize which subsets carry most of the regularity.
- Deterministic pipeline and interpretable output: The approach yields human-inspectable rules (e.g., cz ~ č, ł ~ l), and the deterministic merge strategy is appealing to practitioners wary of stochasticity.
- Alignment as shortest-path: Casting alignment with a given rule table as a shortest-path problem is sound and allows use of established algorithms with partial order reduction, which is neat from an algorithmic standpoint.
- Coherent qualitative results: The pairwise NCDs and the inferred tree roughly recover known Slavic groupings, and the four-way examples show linguistically plausible correspondences of varying granularity.

Weaknesses
- Lack of rigorous empirical validation against established baselines: The paper does not compare to standard string/phonetic alignment and cognate modeling baselines (e.g., pair-HMMs with learned substitution matrices, PMI-based/ALINE/Needleman–Wunsch variants, LexStat/PMI approaches, or modern cognate identification/phonological correspondence induction systems). The evidence is largely qualitative (a plausible tree) and anecdotal (example rules), which is insufficient to establish superiority or even parity with well-known methods.
- Evaluation design is weak and indirect: The primary quantitative result is that the NCD-based phylogeny roughly matches known classifications, which many simple baselines are known to reproduce on Swadesh-style lists. There is no intrinsic evaluation of alignments/correspondences (precision/recall against a gold standard), no held-out generalization (e.g., reconstructing unseen cognates), no downstream impact (e.g., improvements in cognate detection or MT transliteration).
- Orthography-only modeling without normalization: Operating solely on raw orthography mixes orthographic conventions with phonological correspondences. Claims about discovering “true” correspondences are undermined without IPA/phonologized forms or at least robust orthographic normalization. Several reported rules appear orthography-driven. The method’s sensitivity to script and diacritics is suggested by SCl vs SCc results, but not systematically controlled.
- Subjective “data overweighting” undermines MDL objectivity: Introducing α to overweight data to force longer rules conflicts with the MDL justification and introduces a tunable knob that can materially change the learned rule inventory. No principled guidance or sensitivity analysis is provided; moreover, reporting results “until there are no more singleton rules” seems ad hoc.
- Scalability and optimization concerns: The M-step merges are claimed O(|D|·A^2/2), and the E-step O(|D|·|R|^2). The implementation is reported to handle up to five languages on a modest machine, but the paper does not provide systematic runtime/memory scaling, pruning strategies, or stopping criteria. For N>5 or larger lexicons, the approach may be impractical. The non-convex EM-plus-merging procedure likely suffers from local minima and strong dependence on initialization; there is no study of stability across runs or initializations (null vs seeded).
- MDL coding choices and comparability questions: The code for L(Π) and the use of data-to-model codes (weak composition terms) are only sketched; crucial details (e.g., enumeration, effect of alphabet size and symbol inventories across language pairs) are not fully justified. It is unclear whether description lengths—and hence NCDs—are comparable across pairs when model classes differ. The definition NCD(X,Y)=L(X,Y)−min(L(X,X),L(Y,Y))/max(...) with model-dependent codes risks violating the spirit of compressor-independence; the paper does not validate metric behavior or robustness to modeling choices.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Can you compare against established alignment/correspondence induction methods (e.g., pair-HMMs with learned substitutions, PMI/ALINE/LexStat, CRF-based aligners)? At minimum, report quantitative metrics on standard datasets (alignment accuracy vs gold, cognate clustering F1) and show where MDL helps.
- Robustness to orthography: How do results change with phonemic/IPA transcriptions, or after orthographic normalization (e.g., mapping digraphs, diacritics)? Can you disentangle orthographic from phonological correspondences quantitatively?
- Overweighting α: How sensitive are learned rules and sDL/NCD outcomes to α? Can you provide a principled criterion for choosing α, or eliminate it via a hierarchical code or MDL refinement?
- NCD and comparability: How do you ensure L(X,X), L(Y,Y), and L(X,Y) are comparable when models (alphabets, Π, code tables) differ? Have you tested whether the induced NCD satisfies symmetry/triangle inequality empirically across compressors/model choices?
- Scalability: What are the practical limits in N and |D|? What pruning or candidate selection do you use in the M-step? What is the stopping criterion? Please provide runtime/memory benchmarks and sensitivity to initialization; do results vary materially across seeds/rule seeding?
- Generalization: Can you demonstrate held-out performance (e.g., reconstructing unseen forms from partial observations) to show that the learned rules are predictive rather than descriptive?

Additional comments
- The references contain numerous formatting errors and missing details; please fix.
- Clarify whether ε on any side is allowed (deletions/insertions) and whether rules must be contiguous substrings.
- Consider releasing code and data splits for reproducibility.

Overall, while the MDL formulation and multilingual framing are interesting, the current empirical validation is insufficient for a strong claim of methodological contribution. A thorough comparison to established baselines, a more rigorous evaluation protocol, and clearer analysis of robustness and scalability would be needed for acceptance.
