Summary and main contributions
- A general MDL-based framework for discovering string-level correspondence rules across more than two languages without parametric assumptions about rule shape or distribution. The model uses a two-part code over alphabets and a rule table that permits rules over arbitrary subsets of languages, learned via an EM-style procedure with deterministic greedy merges and shortest-path alignment.
- A multi-way similarity analysis: beyond pairwise NCD, the paper introduces “shared Description Length” (sDL) to quantify how much structure is jointly shared by any subset of languages, enabling fine-grained similarity profiling (e.g., across CS-PL-RU-BG).
- Empirical demonstration on Slavic languages showing that the learned correspondences are interpretable and that NCD-based phylogeny and sDL analyses reflect known linguistic groupings; plus a pragmatic “data over-weighting” mechanism that steers rule granularity and allows expert control.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and breadth: Modeling correspondences as unconstrained string tuples over arbitrary language subsets under a principled MDL code is a bold, general formulation. It bypasses strong priors typical in earlier work (e.g., fixed alignment models) and cleanly unifies modeling and selection via code length.
- Multi-language, not merely pairwise: The ability to learn and use rules that tie 2..N languages simultaneously is a genuine step beyond pairwise cognate alignment methods. The sDL analysis is particularly compelling for quantifying which subsets share more reducible structure.
- Interpretability and linguistic plausibility: The induced rules capture phonological, orthographic, and morphological regularities (e.g., palatalization, digraphs, endings), and the NCD phylogeny recovers standard Slavic groupings, with sensible insights (e.g., BG/MK outlier behavior; Polish digraph complexity).
- Deterministic learning with expert steerability: The EM + greedy-merge scheme, shortest-path alignment, and the option to inject initial rules make the method attractive for linguists who want reproducibility and a way to incorporate knowledge. The “over-weighting” knob pragmatically controls the granularity of rules without hard-coding assumptions.
- Clear potential for broader impact: A general correspondence learner that is agnostic to scripts and language count can be valuable for historical linguistics, cognate reconstruction, cross-lingual lexicon induction, and similarity measurement—likely inspiring follow-up work.

Weaknesses
- Limited evaluation and absence of baselines: Experiments are confined to Slavic datasets and small vocabulary (Swadesh + internationalisms), with no quantitative comparison to established alignment/cognate methods (e.g., PHMMs, ALINE, log-linear string transducers, Snyder & Barzilay). Without baselines or extrinsic tasks (e.g., cognate identification accuracy, reconstruction), it is hard to assess effectiveness beyond plausibility.
- Scalability constraints and incomplete complexity discussion: The method currently handles up to five languages in hours on modest hardware; graphs can become memory-heavy. While understandable, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of complexity drivers (alphabet size, rule set growth, average word length) and practical pruning/approximation strategies (e.g., A*, beam, heuristic bounds).
- MDL code clarity and rigor: Some parts of the coding scheme read as under-specified or dense (e.g., data-to-model terms, weak number compositions, offsets, subset enumeration). A more formal derivation and ablations (e.g., different universal codes, effects of Laplace smoothing) would strengthen confidence that the code is both correct and the main driver of results.
- Orthography-centric evidence and potential confounds: Using raw orthography (incl. digraphs and diacritics) mixes orthographic conventions with phonology. The observed similarities and outliers may partially reflect script and spelling choices. No experiments with phonemic transcriptions or orthography-normalized variants are provided to disentangle these effects.
- “Over-weighting” undermines objectivity without guidance: While practical, the α-scaling deviates from pure MDL and may bias discovery in non-transparent ways. The paper does not provide guidelines for choosing α, nor analyzes sensitivity or the impact on learned rule distributions.

Questions to authors
- Baselines: Can you compare against a pairwise PHMM or ALINE on the same data (e.g., quality of alignments, number/length of induced correspondences), and/or report cognate identification or reconstruction performance using your learned rules? Even limited comparisons would help.
- Alignment details: Are alignments strictly monotonic with ε insertions/deletions permitted per language? How are empty entries penalized, and how do you prevent degenerate ε-heavy rules?
- Sensitivity to representation: What changes when inputs are phonemic transcriptions or when diacritics/digraphs are normalized? Do the phylogeny and sDL patterns persist?
- Over-weighting α: How should practitioners set α? Can you show a curve of rule inventory size/granularity vs α, and whether main qualitative conclusions (e.g., phylogeny) are stable across α?
- Scalability: Could you outline concrete pruning or heuristic search strategies you consider (e.g., rule preselection by frequent substrings, A* with admissible heuristics, beam search), and provide complexity measurements vs N and |Σ|?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and datasets (including the curated four-language set)? That would materially increase the paper’s impact.

Recommendation
Lean accept. The paper advances a novel, general, and ambitious MDL formulation for multi-language correspondence discovery, with interpretable outputs and a promising new way to quantify shared structure across language subsets. The evaluation is limited and the coding/complexity details could be clearer, but given the conceptual contribution and potential to open a new direction, I recommend acceptance contingent on clarifying the code, adding at least a minimal baseline comparison, and strengthening the discussion of scalability and representation sensitivity.
