Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes a nonparametric MDL-based framework (a two-part code, extending serial-episode coding) to discover variable-length string correspondences across multiple languages simultaneously, without assuming a parametric form, priors over rule shapes, or limiting to pairs. It combines a Dijkstra-based exact alignment procedure with an EM-like training that greedily merges rules guided by description length.
- It introduces information-theoretic tools to quantify multilingual similarity beyond pairwise comparisons: using NCD for phylogenetic reconstruction and “shared Description Length” (sDL) to measure structure shared by any subset of languages. This yields interpretable, fine-grained similarity profiles over all subsets in a 4-language setting.
- It offers a practical “data over-weighting” knob to elicit longer, near-significant rules when data are scarce, demonstrating how one can trade objectivity for exploratory detail. Experiments on Slavic languages show sensible trees and linguistically meaningful correspondences (e.g., digraphs, palatalizations, metathesis, stems).

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and boldness: Framing multilingual correspondence discovery as MDL-based compression over sets of variable-length string tuples, with no prespecified rule distributions, is a refreshing departure from common pairwise/parametric approaches (e.g., PHMMs, Bayesian sound change models). The ability to discover multi-grapheme correspondences and sparsely populated rules is a strong fit to the real structure of historical correspondences.
- Multilingual generality with fine-grained quantification: The sDL analysis is a neat, principled way to apportion how much structure is shared by every subset, revealing patterns that pairwise analyses miss (e.g., RU-BG vs. CS-PL nuances). That the inferred phylogeny and subgroup patterns align with known Slavic classifications supports the utility of the framework.
- Interpretability and determinism: The learned rules are directly readable by linguists; the deterministic greedy-merge strategy and the option to seed with expert rules provide a compelling path for human-in-the-loop analysis. The examples include both phonological and orthographic regularities (cz~č, dzie~dě, g~h, ла~оло), which is valuable for linguistic insight.
- Clear algorithmic ideas: The mapping of alignment to shortest paths with exact decoding, partial order reduction, and an EM-style training loop with MDL-guided merges is elegant and easy to reason about. The extension of two-part MDL codes to the multilingual rule-table and usage coding is a solid contribution in itself.
- Practical flexibility: The proposed data over-weighting gives practitioners a controlled mechanism to increase granularity when evidence is borderline, especially useful for under-resourced languages or multi-language analyses where data sparsity is acute.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation and comparison: The experiments rely on small Swadesh and internationalism lists, with no quantitative comparisons against established baselines (e.g., PHMM-based alignment, Bayesian sound correspondence models, Snyder & Barzilay for multilingual morphology, or cognate identification systems). There is no evaluation of rule quality (precision/recall against expert correspondences), no held-out compression/perplexity, and no predictive task (e.g., cognate prediction/reconstruction) to assess generalization.
- Orthography-level analysis and script confounds: All analyses are on raw orthography, not phonemic/phonetic representations, conflating writing system idiosyncrasies with true sound correspondences. The SC Latin vs Cyrillic split and the observed proximity effects likely reflect script overlap; transliteration or phonemic normalization would strengthen the claims. It’s unclear how sensitive the results are to alphabet design choices.
- Incomplete clarity of the coding scheme and training specifics: Some components of L(Π), the data-to-model codes, the exact handling of ε (insertions/deletions), and Laplace smoothing are described at a high level. The derivations (e.g., combinatorial terms for usage coding) and normalization constants are not fully spelled out, making reproduction difficult. The greedy merge strategy may depend on tie-breaking; convergence and stopping criteria are not specified.
- Scalability and complexity: The stated O(|D|·|R|^2) E-step and A^2 M-step with memory bottlenecks limit N to ≈5 in hours on a single core. The paper does not provide strategies beyond partial order reduction to push to higher N or larger datasets (e.g., pruning, beams, WFST composition, approximate decoding), which constrains applicability.
- Over-weighting blurs the objective MDL story: While pragmatically useful, mixing objective MDL with subjective reweighting can encourage cherry-picking. The paper would benefit from clearer guidance on when/how to use α≠1, and from keeping objective and exploratory results clearly separated.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Can you provide quantitative comparisons to baselines on standard datasets (e.g., cognate identification, alignment F1, or reconstruction accuracy), as well as held-out compression? Even small-scale comparisons would help gauge effectiveness.
- How are ε operations handled in rules and coding? Are empty components allowed in πi and how are they costed?
- Please clarify the derivation of the data-to-model code terms in L(Π) and L(Σ): what exact enumeration is assumed, and how are TΠ, |Π|, and counts integrated to yield the log binomial terms? A short appendix with full derivations would help.
- What are the convergence and stopping criteria? How sensitive are results to initialization, Laplace constants, and tie-breaking in greedy merges?
- Can you report scalability experiments (time/memory) vs. N and |D|, and discuss possible approximate search (beam/A*, WFST, pruning of low-support rules) to handle more languages?
- How robust are the results to transliteration or G2P to IPA? Could you show the same analysis with phonemic representations to disentangle orthography?
- Could you release code and data to improve reproducibility?

Overall, I find the core idea novel and promising, with compelling interpretability and a genuinely multilingual angle. The empirical section needs stronger quantitative validation and clearer coding/training details, but the conceptual contribution is significant and worth discussion at CoNLL.
