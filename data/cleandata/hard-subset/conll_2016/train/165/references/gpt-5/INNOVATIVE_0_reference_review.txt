Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework to discover cross-lingual correspondence rules directly from data, without assuming a parametric form for correspondences and without restricting to language pairs. The approach aligns cognate tuples via shortest-path search, learns a code table of multi-language string correspondences by an EM-like iterative procedure with greedy merges, and leverages information-theoretic quantities (e.g., NCD, and a new shared Description Length metric) to quantify similarity across arbitrary subsets of languages. Experiments on Slavic languages show a phylogeny consistent with linguistic classifications and illustrative multi-way correspondences.

Contributions (as I see them), ordered by strength:
1) A general, assumption-light MDL model for discovering multi-language string correspondences (beyond pairwise), with a deterministic EM-style learning procedure and shortest-path alignment.
2) An information-theoretic analysis toolkit for multi-language similarity, notably the shared Description Length over arbitrary subsets, enabling fine-grained, multiway similarity quantification and interpretations.
3) A practical “data over-weighting” knob that trades objectivity for interpretability by promoting longer, linguistically salient rules when data are sparse.

Strengths
1) Conceptual novelty and ambition. Treating correspondences as sets of associated strings across N languages and choosing among them purely by MDL (without prior shape/distribution assumptions) is a clean and bold framing. Extending beyond pairwise settings addresses a real gap in cross-linguistic modeling and opens new analysis avenues.

2) Multi-way quantification of similarity. The shared Description Length construct provides a genuinely new way to measure how much structure is common across arbitrary language subsets, going beyond trees to reveal fine-grained clusterings and asymmetries (e.g., CS-PL vs. RU-BG, and RU’s broader shared structure). This is a valuable conceptual and practical contribution.

3) Interpretable rules discovered across levels. The learned correspondences are linguistically meaningful (phonological, orthographic, morphophonological, even stems), and the deterministic, merge-based growth gives an intuitively appealing “from simple to significant” trajectory. The ability to seed linguistic knowledge and obtain deterministic results is attractive to linguists.

4) Methodological coherence with MDL. The coding scheme (two-part MDL, data-to-model codes, length-aware rule encoding that avoids penalizing sparsity) ties the model’s complexity control directly to the learned correspondences. Using shortest-path alignment with partial-order reductions is a sensible engineering choice.

5) Empirical signals are promising. The NCD-based phylogeny aligns with accepted groupings, and the four-way analyses yield insightful qualitative examples and quantitative sDL patterns. The observed distinctions (e.g., script effects, Czech diacritics) suggest the method is capturing real structure.

Weaknesses
1) Limited empirical validation and absence of baselines. There are no quantitative comparisons against established cognate alignment/correspondence discovery methods (e.g., PMI-based aligners, PairHMM/ALINE, LexStat/CLTS-like approaches, or Bayesian phylogenetic models) and no intrinsic precision/recall evaluation of learned rules, nor extrinsic tasks (cognate projection, reconstruction, MT features). The current evidence is largely qualitative plus NCD trees.

2) Scalability and practicality. The approach currently handles up to five languages in hours on modest hardware, with combinatorial bottlenecks in memory and runtime. The multi-way promise is undersold by experiments limited to four languages, while the larger 13-language study reverts to pairwise training. A clearer scaling story (with pruning or approximations) is needed.

3) Ambiguities and clarity in the code specification. Some parts of L(Π) and the data-to-model coding are under-specified or contain likely typos (e.g., “none of the symbols … will have a non-zero count”). It is not fully clear how counts and code lengths are updated (Laplace parameterization), how subsets are enumerated and encoded, or how the merging heuristic guarantees progress without pathological local minima. More precise algorithmic detail would improve reproducibility.

4) Orthographic confounds and data choices. Working in raw orthography across mixed scripts means correspondences may reflect script and orthographic idiosyncrasies rather than phonology. The SC Latin vs. Cyrillic analysis shows a script effect, but the method’s linguistic generality would be better demonstrated with phonemic transcriptions or orthographic normalization.

5) Data over-weighting lacks principled guidance. While useful in practice, the α control departs from MDL’s objectivity and risks overfitting to idiosyncratic patterns. There is no procedure or sensitivity analysis for choosing α at corpus or item level, and no evaluation of the trade-offs this knob induces.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Can you add comparisons against strong baselines for cognate alignment/sound correspondences (e.g., PMI-based, PairHMM/ALINE, LexStat), reporting rule quality and/or downstream utility?
- Please clarify the L(Π) formulation, the exact data-to-model coding used, and the apparent “non-zero count” typo. Could you provide pseudo-code for the M-step (rule merging, candidate generation, and cost recomputation)?
- What is the complexity as a function of N, |Π|, and average tuple length? What pruning/approximate strategies (e.g., beam search, A* with admissible heuristics, suffix/prefix constraints) can extend N beyond 5?
- How is Laplace correction parameterized? Is performance sensitive to it? Any ablations?
- Can you normalize to phonemic representations or otherwise control for orthography (e.g., grapheme-to-phoneme, digraph handling) and report the impact on learned rules and phylogeny?
- How do you select α for over-weighting? Could you provide a sensitivity study and guidance for practitioners?
- Will you release code and datasets to ensure reproducibility?

Overall, this is a bold and promising direction that brings MDL to multi-way correspondence discovery and introduces a compelling analytic toolkit. Despite limited validation and some clarity/scalability gaps, the conceptual novelty and potential impact are high. I lean toward acceptance conditional on strengthening empirical comparisons and tightening the methodological exposition.
