Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework to automatically discover systematic string correspondences across multiple languages from cognate tuples, without assuming any specific distributional form for the correspondences and without restricting to language pairs. The approach frames alignment as a shortest-path problem with rule costs derived from a two-part MDL code, and iteratively refines rules via a deterministic EM-like procedure that greedily merges patterns that most reduce total description length. The authors introduce analyses based on Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) and a new notion of shared Description Length (sDL) to quantify similarity among any subset of languages.

Contributions, as I see them:
1) A general, assumption-light MDL formulation (PS-2 code) for discovering multi-language correspondence rules directly from orthographic data, including a deterministic EM-style learning procedure with alignment-as-shortest-path.
2) A methodology to quantify cross-language similarity beyond pairs via sDL over rule tables, enabling detailed subset-level analyses; plus NCD-based phylogenies derived from learned models.
3) A practical mechanism to steer rule granularity via data overweighting and an option to inject linguistic priors (initial rule sets), trading strict objectivity for expert-controllable detail.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and ambition: The paper goes beyond the common pairwise setting and priors over correspondence forms, offering an MDL-driven alternative that discovers many-to-many string rules across multiple languages. The formulation is cleanly grounded in two-part MDL and coding-theoretic principles, with sensible use of data-to-model codes and universal integer codes. This is a fresh and promising direction for multilingual historical/comparative analysis and cognate modeling.

- Multiway analysis with interpretable outputs: The ability to learn rules defined on arbitrary subsets of languages and to quantify shared structure via sDL is genuinely new (to my knowledge) and yields insights that pairwise methods miss. The four-language analysis is particularly compelling: it exposes subset-regularities and differences in a principled, information-theoretic way.

- Qualitative linguistic plausibility: The discovered correspondences (e.g., palatalization, digraphs, liquid metathesis, morphological endings) are linguistically meaningful. The NCD-based phylogeny aligns with Slavic classifications, and the method correctly captures script effects (Latin vs. Cyrillic SC) and known idiosyncrasies (Bulgarian/Macedonian morphology, Polish palatalization).

- Determinism and expert control: Starting from singleton rules, deterministic greedy merges, and the option to seed rule sets make the process reproducible and useful for linguists who wish to incorporate prior knowledge. The overweighting mechanism, while subjective, provides a practical handle to surface longer rules when data is sparse, which could be valuable in low-resource settings.

- Clear algorithmic articulation: The alignment formulation as shortest-path with Dijkstra, partial-order reduction, and explicit time complexities demonstrates practical awareness. Even with modest hardware, the method handles up to five languages—nontrivial for this problem.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical evaluation and lack of baselines: The paper does not compare against established methods for phonological/orthographic correspondences or cognate alignment (e.g., PHMMs, PMI-based aligners, ALINE, Morfessor-like segmenters, recent cognate discovery systems). No quantitative metrics (precision/recall on known correspondences, held-out compression, or cognate prediction performance) are reported. This makes it hard to assess relative effectiveness beyond the plausible qualitative findings.

- Orthography rather than phonology: Operating purely on orthographic strings (with mixed scripts) conflates phonological correspondence with script conventions. The SC Latin vs. Cyrillic effect supports this concern: similarity partly reflects script rather than sound correspondences. A phonemic normalization (e.g., IPA) or at least a controlled transliteration study would strengthen claims about linguistic correspondences.

- Scalability and efficiency constraints: The alignment graphs can create runtime/memory bottlenecks; reported capacity tops out at five languages on modest hardware. The E-step complexity O(|D|·|R|^2) and M-step O(|D|·A^2) suggest challenges for larger datasets or higher N. The paper lacks a systematic scalability study (runtimes vs. N, vocabulary size, rule table growth).

- Overweighting is ad hoc: While practical, the α parameter departs from MDL’s objectivity and risks cherry-picking rules. There is no principled criterion for choosing α, no sensitivity analysis, and no held-out validation to prevent overfitting to overweighted data. Guidance on when and how to use α would be important for adoption.

- Reproducibility details: Some coding details (e.g., precise data-to-model code instantiations, handling of epsilon components, tie-breaking in merges, smoothing choices) are under-specified, and there is no mention of code/data release. The PS-2 code is introduced but not contrasted concretely to related MDL encodings beyond Tatti & Vreeken (2012), which would help clarify novelty at the coding level.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Can you provide quantitative comparisons to baselines on standard datasets (e.g., precision/recall of induced sound correspondences, cognate alignment accuracy, or held-out compression rates)? Even small controlled studies would help.

- How sensitive are your results to orthography? Have you tried phoneme-level or IPA-transcribed data, or consistent transliteration across scripts, and how do the learned correspondences change?

- How should α (overweighting) be selected? Could you propose a criterion based on held-out compression or MDL with a hyperprior, and provide a sensitivity analysis?

- What are typical sizes of |R| and A in your runs, and how do they scale with N and |D|? Any engineering tricks beyond partial-order reduction to keep graphs and rule tables manageable?

- Will you release code and datasets to facilitate replication?

Recommendation
I find this paper novel and bold in its MDL-centric reframing of multilingual correspondence discovery and its subset-level similarity analysis (sDL). Despite empirical and scalability limitations, the conceptual contribution and demonstrated potential justify acceptance. I recommend acceptance, contingent on clarifying evaluation and providing more guidance on α and reproducibility.
