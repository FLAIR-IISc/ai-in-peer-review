Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework to automatically discover string-level correspondence rules across multiple languages (N ≥ 2), without imposing a parametric form on the distribution of correspondences. The method defines a two-part MDL code over alphabets and a rule table, aligns cognate tuples via shortest-path search, and iteratively refines rules by deterministic greedy merges informed by description length. The authors also introduce a “data over-weighting” knob to encourage larger, more detailed rules when statistical evidence is marginal.

I see the primary contributions as:
1) Conceptual/methodological: A general MDL formulation for multi-language correspondence discovery that avoids pairwise limitations and strong modeling assumptions, with a deterministic, EM-like learning procedure based on code-length–guided rule merges.
2) Analytical tools: Information-theoretic measures derived from the learned models (NCD and “shared Description Length”, sDL) to quantify similarity across all subsets of languages, enabling analyses beyond pairwise trees.
3) Empirical illustration: Application to 13 Slavic languages (pairwise and 4-way analyses), yielding a sensible phylogeny and interpretable correspondence rules, and demonstrating the effect of data over-weighting for finer-grained patterns.

Strengths
- Novel, bold framing of multi-language correspondences via MDL: The paper drops strong parametric assumptions and sidesteps pairwise-only setups by coding rules as tuples of strings across N languages, including sparse entries (ε). This is a clean, principled approach that feels genuinely new compared to most cognate alignment methods that are either pairwise or rely on fixed substitution models. The ability to handle arbitrary-length correspondences across more than two languages is particularly compelling.
- Deterministic, interpretation-friendly learning: The rule-induction heuristic—greedy merges guided by description length—ties model growth to statistical evidence and yields interpretable rules (e.g., (cz,č), (dzie,dě), (ла,оло)). Determinism and the option to seed with linguistic priors make the approach attractive to linguists who value reproducibility and control over randomness.
- From pairwise distances to subset-level quantification: The sDL idea is a nice addition. It turns the learned code into a quantitative lens on which subsets (pairs, triples, quadruples) share structure, going beyond trees to richer similarity profiles. The 4-way CS–PL–RU–BG analysis shows this can surface nuanced patterns (e.g., RU–BG affinity alongside CS–PL) that are hard to read off from pairwise NCDs alone.
- Pragmatic “over-weighting” control for exploratory analysis: While it departs from pure MDL, the α-weighting and per-item weights are a practical mechanism to elicit larger, linguistically useful rules in under-resourced scenarios. The paper illustrates that this shifts from singleton-heavy analyses to richer correspondences without scrambling the discovery order.
- Empirical sanity checks on Slavic: The NCD-derived tree largely matches linguistic classification, and the method recovers rules reflecting orthographic, phonological, and morphological phenomena. Even with orthographic input only, the outcomes are sensible and interpretable.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation and baselines: The evaluation is primarily illustrative. There are no quantitative comparisons to established methods (e.g., ALINE, PMI/lexstat/lingpy, PairHMM-based aligners, Bouchard-Côté-style models) on tasks such as cognate identification, alignment quality, or proto-form reconstruction. The phylogeny result, while sensible, is a well-trodden demonstration. Without head-to-head baselines, it’s hard to gauge the practical advantage of MDL here.
- Scalability and runtime constraints: Alignment is solved via Dijkstra on large graphs and the learning alternates with greedy merges; the method currently handles up to five languages on modest hardware “within a few hours.” Complexity bounds are given but practical scaling to longer words, larger lexica, or N > 5 remains uncertain. The paper positions the method as general for N, but the current engineering limits this in practice, and no strategies (e.g., pruning, beam search, A*, batching) are evaluated.
- Ambiguity about EM and determinism: The training is described as EM-like but the M-step is a greedy merge step, not a standard maximization over parameters; global optimality is not guaranteed (which is fine), but determinism hinges on tie-breaking and implementation details not discussed. Clarifying convergence behavior, sensitivity to initialization (null vs seeded), and stability across runs would strengthen the claims.
- Over-weighting risks and guidance: The α-weighting is a useful control, but it undermines MDL’s objectivity and may encourage overfitting or language-internal patterns that masquerade as cross-language correspondences. There is no principled guidance for choosing α or per-item weights; no diagnostics are reported to prevent pathological merges.
- Reproducibility and presentation issues: Important practical details are missing: code availability, preprocessing/transliteration choices across scripts, full hyperparameters, and how partial order reduction is implemented. Some equations (e.g., L(Π) components, data-to-model codes) are under-explained and the notation is occasionally hard to follow. The references have many formatting glitches.

Questions to authors
- Clarify the precise “data-to-model” terms in L(Π): what is the exact enumeration and how do LN(TΠ) and the weak composition term interact with L(D|M) to avoid double-counting usage information?
- How is decoding guaranteed to be prefix-free/uniquely decodable given variable-length, sparsely populated rule tuples and ε entries? Are there constraints that prevent pathological overlaps?
- How are ties handled in the greedy merge step, and how sensitive are the final rules to tie-breaking? Any empirical evidence of stability across seeds?
- Can you report runtime/memory as a function of N, |D|, and average word length, and discuss any pruning/heuristics that preserved optimality in practice?
- Could you provide at least one quantitative comparison to a strong baseline (e.g., lexstat/PMI or ALINE) on cognate alignment quality or cognate clustering?

Overall recommendation
Leaning accept (weak accept). The paper advances a fresh, principled direction—an MDL lens for multi-language correspondences with interpretable outcomes and new analytic tools (sDL). Despite limited quantitative evaluation and scalability questions, the conceptual contribution is significant and likely to spur follow-up work in computational historical linguistics and cross-lingual analysis.
