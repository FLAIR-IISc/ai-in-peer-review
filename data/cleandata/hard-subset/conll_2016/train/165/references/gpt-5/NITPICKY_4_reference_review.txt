Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes an MDL-based method to discover string-level correspondence rules across multiple languages directly from orthographic cognate lists. The model encodes a set of n-ary “correspondence rules” (tuples of strings, one per language, possibly empty), and uses a two-part MDL objective L(M)+L(D|M). Alignments are obtained as shortest paths in a multi-tape graph; training proceeds by an EM-like loop with a greedy merge-based maximization step inspired by serial episode mining. The method is used to compute an MDL-based Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) for all pairs of 13 Slavic languages to infer a phylogeny, and to analyze shared description lengths among subsets of four languages (CS-PL-RU-BG). The authors also introduce “data over-weighting” (scaling L(D|M) by α>1) to push the discovery of longer rules.

My view of the contributions (in order):
1) A concrete MDL formulation and deterministic greedy algorithm for discovering multi-language string correspondence rules and using them to quantify similarity among more than two languages.
2) A qualitative multi-way analysis of Slavic languages (including four-way shared description lengths) and a pairwise NCD tree that roughly matches known classifications.
3) A heuristic “over-weighting” mechanism (α-scaling of L(D|M)) to bias the learned rule granularity.

Strengths
- Multi-language formulation beyond pairwise models: The model directly supports n-ary correspondences and quantifies similarity among arbitrary subsets (e.g., shared description lengths), which is less common than strictly pairwise cognate alignment approaches. The four-way sDL analysis is an interesting descriptive capability.
- Clear MDL motivation and connection to pattern set mining: Building on two-part MDL and the Tatti–Vreeken coding perspective provides a principled objective and an interpretable pattern set (rules), with plausible transfer from serial episode mining to string correspondences.
- Deterministic greedy learning pipeline: The use of a null-alignment initialization and a merge-based greedy M-step yields reproducible models (modulo tie-breaking), which is attractive for linguistic auditability compared to stochastic search.
- Qualitative examples reasonably reflect known Slavic phenomena: The paper shows rules for palatalizations, metathesis, digraphs, and suffix correspondences that align with well-known phenomena, lending face validity.
- Lightweight computational infrastructure: Despite combinatorial complexity, the approach is implementable without heavy neural or specialized toolchains, which can be valuable for linguists working with small datasets.

Weaknesses
- Serious issues in evaluation and baselines: The empirical evaluation is limited to (i) a phylogeny that could plausibly be recovered by much simpler baselines (e.g., normalized Levenshtein/ALINE/LexStat/PMI-based sound correspondence distances; even character n-gram compression), and (ii) descriptive four-way sDL plots. There is no quantitative comparison to existing cognate/phylogeny methods, no ablation (e.g., effect of multi-language vs pairwise training), and no held-out evaluation. Without these, it is hard to assess whether MDL provides a real benefit over simpler or established methods.
- Confounds due to orthography and script: The data are in raw orthography, and the results explicitly reflect script differences (Latin vs Cyrillic Serbo-Croatian), which are trivial orthographic effects rather than linguistic correspondences. No phonemic normalization or transliteration is applied. This undermines the claim of discovering linguistic correspondences and inflates apparent distinctions (e.g., BG/MK short endings vs other languages). A minimal normalization pipeline is necessary.
- Unclear and possibly flawed coding details: The MDL specification has ambiguities/typos and potential double-counting. Examples: “Since we know that none of the symbols of any alphabets will have a non-zero count” seems incorrect (should allow zeros). The data-to-model codes for code(σ) and code(π) are not fully specified (do you use empirical -log counts? how are counts encoded?). The L(Π) expression mixes per-rule content costs and a weak composition of usage counts but does not clearly justify independence assumptions or whether TΠ is already determined by D|M (risk of double-encoding counts). LN(T) terms for Σi and Π may be unnecessary or inconsistent with standard two-part MDL for multinomials. These details matter because the reported NCD and sDL depend sensitively on the code.
- Algorithmic specifics and complexity are underspecified: The M-step merges “the two patterns that give the highest decrease,” but the search space, tie-breaking, stopping criteria, and proof of monotone MDL decrease are not provided. Complexity claims O(|D|·|R|^2) (E) and O(|D|·A^2/2) (M) lack definitions of R and A in terms of data length, rule lengths, and N; no convergence or iteration counts are reported; scalability is limited to ≤5 languages over hours, which curtails the claimed generality.
- “Over-weighting” undermines the MDL principle: Scaling L(D|M) by α>1 to force longer rules is acknowledged to be subjective, but it is used to illustrate key examples. Without a principled selection of α, this opens the door to cherry-picking and compromises the objectivity MDL affords. No sensitivity analysis or guidelines are provided.
- Presentation and referencing quality are below venue standards: Numerous formatting issues (missing spaces, broken figure/table labels “T 2”, “F 1”, in-text footnotes), unclear table captions, and severely broken references (placeholders like “? \Q2004\E”, inconsistent author names, missing venues) make the paper difficult to verify and are unacceptable for publication.

Questions to authors
- MDL specification:
  • Precisely define code(σ) and code(π). Do you use empirical frequencies with -log p plus a data-to-model code for frequencies? How do you avoid double-counting counts across L(M) and L(D|M)?
  • Justify the LN(TΣi) and LN(TΠ) terms: are T’s determined by D|M? If so, why encode them in L(M)? If not, how are they decoupled from D?
  • The statement about symbols “not having a non-zero count” appears contradictory. Please clarify the intended weak composition and whether zero counts are allowed.
- Alignment and EM:
  • What is the termination criterion? Do you guarantee that each M-step reduces L(M)+L(D|M)? How do you break ties deterministically?
  • Can you report iteration counts, runtime breakdown, and memory usage as a function of N, average token length, and number/length of rules?
- NCD computation:
  • How exactly are L(X,X), L(Y,Y), and L(X,Y) computed? Are models trained separately per language and jointly per pair, or is a universal model reused? Does your “compressor” satisfy NCD’s conditions (or is NCD used heuristically)?
- Data and preprocessing:
  • Why not normalize scripts or use phonemic transcriptions to reduce orthographic confounds? Can you report how the tree changes under transliteration or phoneme mapping?
  • Provide dataset statistics (token lengths, alphabet sizes per language), and release code/data for reproducibility.
- Over-weighting:
  • How should α be chosen? Can you provide a sensitivity analysis and show that key conclusions are stable across α? Could a hierarchical MDL prior achieve similar effects without ad hoc scaling?

Recommendation: Reject. The idea of MDL-based multi-language correspondence discovery is interesting, and the qualitative examples are promising, but the paper falls short in evaluation, theoretical clarity of the code, handling of orthographic confounds, algorithmic detail, and presentation quality. Substantial revisions with stronger baselines, rigorous MDL specification, proper preprocessing, and corrected references would be needed.
