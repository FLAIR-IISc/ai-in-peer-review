Summary and main contributions
- The paper proposes an MDL-based framework (PS-2 code) to discover string-level correspondence rules across multiple languages simultaneously, without assuming a parametric form for correspondences. Models are learned deterministically via an EM-like procedure: E-step aligns data using current rule costs (shortest-path over a rule-application graph with Dijkstra), M-step greedily merges rule pairs that most reduce total description length.
- It introduces a concrete coding scheme for model and data: encoding alphabets, rule table structure (including subset-of-languages patterns), and usage counts via data-to-model codes; and uses this to compute description lengths for languages and their joint encodings, enabling NCD-based distances and multi-way “shared Description Length” (sDL) analyses.
- Empirically, the method is applied to Slavic data (Swadesh lists plus internationalisms). Pairwise NCDs yield a phylogeny consistent with established groupings; learned rules are interpretable (e.g., palatalization, digraphs, stems); and a four-language analysis quantifies structure shared by all subsets (e.g., CS–PL and RU–BG groupings, script effects).

Strengths
- Sound unsupervised objective with interpretability: The MDL formulation forces an explicit trade-off between model complexity and data fit, producing compact, human-interpretable rules with clear usage costs. This aligns well with linguistic goals (explain regularities without strong priors) and makes the outputs inspectable (e.g., (cz, č), (dzie, dě), (ла, оло), stems).
- Beyond pairwise: The framework handles N-way correspondences natively. The sDL analysis is a neat, information-theoretic way to quantify how much structure is shared by any subset of languages, revealing finer-grained relations that pairwise-only methods obscure (e.g., RU’s broader shared structure).
- Coherent empirical signals: The NCD matrix and neighbor-joining tree reflect well-known Slavic groupings, including meaningful deviations (BG/MK as outliers due to morphology; script effects in SC Latin/Cyrillic). These are plausible consequences of the encoding and data, lending face validity.
- Deterministic training with option for linguistic priors: Starting from singleton rules and merging by maximal DL gain gives repeatable outcomes; the ability to seed with expert rules and to control granularity via data over-weighting is pragmatic for linguists who need specific levels of detail.
- Concrete algorithmic instantiation: The alignment problem is crisply formulated as shortest-path over an N-way lattice of indices; complexities are discussed; partial order reduction is mentioned. This bridges theory and implementable practice.

Weaknesses
- Limited and fragile evaluation: The experiments are restricted to Slavic languages and relatively small lists. There is no quantitative evaluation of alignment quality or rule correctness against any gold standard (e.g., expert-annotated correspondences), nor any comparison to strong baselines (e.g., pair-HMM/PHMM aligners, ALINE, LexStat-style scoring, modern cognate identification/regular sound correspondence models). As a result, it’s hard to judge absolute effectiveness beyond plausibility.
- Scalability constraints and incomplete complexity analysis: The alignment graph can explode combinatorially; the method processes “up to five languages” in hours on modest hardware. The E-step O(|D|·|R|^2) and M-step O(|D|·A^2) bounds are high-level, but the definitions of R and A relative to rule length/coverage are under-specified; the partial order reduction strategy is not detailed. Without more analysis or engineering, applicability to larger N or vocabularies is unclear.
- Coding scheme clarity and rigor: Several parts of the code design are opaque or possibly inconsistent. For example, the “data-to-model” terms for rule usage and alphabet unigrams (weak compositions) are used but not derived; the statement “we know that none of the symbols of any alphabets will have a non-zero count” seems erroneous/typo. The L(Π) formula adds counts and a uniform composition term; it is not clear how this interacts with the usage probabilities for code(π) to avoid double-counting. The treatment of empty components per rule (ϵ) and the subset-encoding overhead could bias against sparse cross-language rules; the authors mention this but do not analyze its impact. Overall, more formal justification or an appendix with derivations would help.
- Ad hoc “data over-weighting”: Introducing α to over-weight the data term to surface near-significant rules is practical but undermines MDL’s objectivity. There is no guidance for selecting α, nor any assessment of how over-weighting affects NCD, sDL, or overfitting. This blurs the line between objective compression and user-tuned modeling.
- Reproducibility and presentation issues: No code or data release is mentioned; preprocessing details (orthography normalization, handling of digraphs, tokenization) are not fully specified. References are malformed; several typos and formatting glitches exist. Important implementation choices (graph construction, pruning, Laplace smoothing magnitudes, stopping criteria) are not given, impeding replication.

Questions to authors
- Coding design: Please clarify the derivation of the data-to-model terms in L(Π) and L(Σi). How exactly are code(π) and code(σ) computed from counts, and how do the composition terms avoid double-coding usage statistics? What did you intend by “none of the symbols… will have a non-zero count”?
- Alignment graph: How are nodes/edges constructed for N languages (precisely which multi-step edges exist), and what partial order reduction is applied? Do you limit maximum rule length or branching? Any heuristics to prune unlikely edges?
- Greedy merge M-step: How do you select candidate pairs to merge efficiently, and what is the stopping criterion? Do you ever consider splitting/removing rules, or only merges? Any guarantees on monotonic decrease and convergence?
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to initialization (null vs seeded rules), Laplace smoothing constants, and α for data over-weighting? Can you provide ablations or stability analyses?
- Evaluation/baselines: Can you compare against established cognate alignment/sound correspondence methods on the same data (precision/recall of alignments/rules), and/or include a non-Slavic family to show generality? Will you release code and data?

Overall, the paper presents a compelling MDL framing and interesting multi-way analyses with interpretable outputs, but the empirical validation and methodological clarity need strengthening to meet the bar.
