Summary
This paper proposes an MDL-based method to automatically discover string-level correspondence rules across two or more languages. The model class consists of sets of multi-string “correspondence rules” (tuples of substrings, one per language, possibly empty), without parametric assumptions on their shapes or distributions. The objective uses a two-part MDL code: L(M) encodes alphabets and a table of correspondence rules with associated usage codes; L(D|M) encodes the best alignment of each cognate tuple via those rules. Learning proceeds by an EM-like loop: E-step aligns the data using Dijkstra’s shortest path on a graph of possible rule applications; M-step greedily merges rules that most reduce total description length. The authors also introduce “data over-weighting” to push the model toward longer rules when data are scarce. Experiments on Slavic languages (Swadesh lists and some internationalisms) show pairwise NCDs that yield a sensible phylogeny and qualitative examples of induced rules. A four-language experiment quantifies shared description lengths across all subsets.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An MDL formulation and deterministic greedy learning procedure for discovering multi-language substring correspondences, extending serial-episode MDL encodings to multi-string alignment.
2) A practical alignment procedure via shortest-path search over rule applications, enabling simultaneous multi-language alignment (beyond pairwise).
3) An information-theoretic analysis of language similarity via shared description lengths across all subsets, demonstrated on four Slavic languages.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity of the MDL objective. Modeling correspondences as code-length tradeoffs (model vs. data) elegantly avoids ad-hoc priors and directly rewards reusable, systematic rules. The two-part code design and use of data-to-model codes connect well to MDL practice and make the objective interpretable.
- Multi-language capability beyond pairwise. Most sound correspondence-induction approaches are pairwise; here the same machinery scales (in principle) to N languages and naturally yields rules defined on any subset. The sDL analysis is a neat byproduct that quantifies shared structure among all subsets.
- Deterministic, transparent learning. A greedy merge strategy that always picks the best DL reduction makes the process traceable and introspectable, which is attractive to linguists. The E-step alignment via exact shortest path (with partial order reduction) yields globally optimal alignments under the current rule table.
- Qualitative correspondences are plausible. The examples capture phonological and orthographic correspondences (e.g., cz–č, g–h, liquid metathesis ла–оло, palatalization dzie–dě) and inflectional endings. The method’s ability to learn rules of varying granularity (including stems) is useful.
- The pairwise NCD tree is largely consistent with known Slavic groupings. Despite small data, the NJ tree reasonably matches West/East/South Slavic clusters and reflects script effects (Latin vs Cyrillic), indicating the model captures real regularities in orthography and morphology.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical rigor and scale. The evaluation uses small datasets (Swadesh lists and 778 items for a subset), no held-out likelihood/compression, and no systematic comparisons to standard baselines (e.g., ALINE, LexStat/PMI, Bayesian sound change models, PHMM-based aligners, LingPy pipelines) on tasks like cognate alignment or phylogeny reconstruction. The NJ result is largely expected; without quantitative benchmarks, the evidence of superiority or distinctiveness is weak.
- Heavy reliance on raw orthography and mixed scripts. Operating on unnormalized orthography (Latin vs Cyrillic; diacritics; digraphs) conflates orthographic idiosyncrasies with phonological correspondences. The observed groupings partly reflect script rather than sound structure (the authors acknowledge SC Latin/Cyrillic differences). A phonemic or standardized transliteration evaluation is needed to substantiate linguistic claims.
- Methodological ambiguities in the code design. Several parts of L(M) and L(D|M) need clarification: the exact role and computation of code(π) and code(σ), whether the data-to-model codes are double-counting usage statistics, and the rationale for including terms like LN(TΠ) + log( (TΠ−1 choose |Π|−1) ) in L(Π) (naming it a data-to-model code but placing it in the model term). It is hard to verify optimality and avoid leakage between model and data codes from the current description.
- Greedy EM and convergence/optimality concerns. The M-step merges the single best pair at a time; this is path-dependent and can lock in suboptimal longer rules, potentially suppressing competing rules (the authors note this). No annealing, restarts, or principled search over multiple merges is explored, and there is no convergence or approximation guarantee to the MDL optimum.
- “Data over-weighting” undermines the MDL justification. Introducing an arbitrary α to up-weight L(D|M) helps induce longer rules but departs from the MDL principle and risks overfitting to small datasets. No guidance is provided for choosing α, nor are sensitivity analyses reported. If the goal is expert-guided emphasis, alternative regularization or Bayesian priors might be more principled.

Questions to Authors
- Code specification: Please provide a precise, self-contained derivation of L(Π) and L(D|M). In particular: how are code(σ) and code(π) computed (what counts go into each; are they re-estimated each EM iteration)? Why is LN(TΠ) + log( (TΠ−1 choose |Π|−1) ) included in L(Π), and does this double-count with L(D|M)? How do you prevent leakage of data statistics into the model term?
- Alignment graph construction: How are edges instantiated efficiently for multi-language rules? What partial order reduction is used? What is R (max rule applications) in practice, and how does runtime scale with N and vocabulary size?
- Determinism and tie-breaking: When multiple merges yield identical DL reduction, how are ties broken? Are alignments deterministic given fixed rule costs? Could different tie-breaking lead to materially different rule sets?
- Over-weighting: How is α selected? Can you report performance (e.g., on held-out compression or external tasks) vs. α and show that qualitative conclusions are stable?
- Baselines and quantitative evaluation: Can you compare to established correspondence/phylogeny methods on publicly used datasets, reporting quantitative metrics (tree accuracy vs. gold, B-cubed for aligned segments, or held-out compression)?
- Orthography vs phonology: Have you tried a phonemic transcription or consistent transliteration to minimize script effects? How do results change?

Additional comments
- Reproducibility would benefit from releasing code, data, and parameter settings (e.g., smoothing, stopping criteria, merge scheduling). Reference formatting and section numbering need cleanup. The claim of handling missing data is not exercised; a controlled experiment with missing entries would strengthen the case.
