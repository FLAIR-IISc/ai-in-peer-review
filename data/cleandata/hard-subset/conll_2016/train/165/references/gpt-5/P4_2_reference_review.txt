Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes an MDL-based framework to discover systematic string-level correspondences across multiple languages without imposing a parametric form on correspondences. Models consist of a set of multi-way correspondence rules over character strings. Data are aligned to rules via shortest-path search; parameters are learned by an EM-like procedure with greedy rule merging, and model selection follows a two-part MDL code. The method supports more than two languages and yields information-theoretic similarity measures, including pairwise NCD and a “shared description length” (sDL) for any subset of languages. Experiments on Slavic languages illustrate that the approach recovers plausible correspondences and phylogenetic structure; a data overweighting knob is introduced to elicit finer-grained rules.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) An MDL model and deterministic training procedure for discovering multi-way string correspondences (model class, two-part code, alignment via Dijkstra, EM with greedy rule merging). Primary novelty is the coding formulation and its extension to multi-language correspondences without a fixed correspondence vocabulary.

2) An analysis framework for multi-language similarity quantification using sDL across arbitrary subsets, complementing NCD-based pairwise distances.

3) A case study on Slavic languages demonstrating that the learned correspondences are linguistically plausible and that induced distances produce a reasonable phylogeny; qualitative examination of the effect of data overweighting.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity of the MDL objective for correspondence discovery. The two-part code formalizes the trade-off between model complexity (number/length of rules, symbol distributions) and data fit (alignment costs). Avoiding a parametric prior for rule shapes makes the problem formulation flexible and aligns with MDL’s strengths.

- Multi-way generalization beyond pairwise correspondences. The model class allows rules that involve any subset of languages, and the alignment/search procedure is agnostic to the number of languages. The sDL metric concretely illustrates how to decompose shared vs language-specific structure across CS–PL–RU–BG, revealing interpretable patterns (e.g., substantial four-way shared structure and CS/PL and RU/BG subgroup sharing).

- Deterministic, interpretable learning procedure. Starting from singleton rules and greedily merging patterns that most reduce description length provides a transparent growth mechanism. The formulation connects to serial episode mining work and yields interpretable rules of varying granularity (e.g., phonological, orthographic, morphological).

- Empirical evidence that induced similarities track known linguistic groupings. The NCD matrix and the neighbor-joining tree for 13 Slavic languages recover west/east/south Slavic groupings and plausible outliers (e.g., BG/MK due to morphology; script effects for Serbo-Croatian). This supports the method’s ability to capture meaningful structure.

- Practical alignment formulation. Casting alignment as shortest-path search with partial order reduction and using Laplace correction ensures every datum is encodable and keeps the E-step well-defined.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation and lack of quantitative baselines. The evaluation relies on qualitative examples, a phylogeny coherence check, and sDL visualizations. There is no comparison to established methods for cognate alignment or correspondence discovery (e.g., PMI-based alignments, pair-HMM/ALINE-style models, LexStat/ASJP), no quantitative accuracy on held-out alignment or cognate prediction, and no ablation of components (e.g., effect of greedy merges vs. no merges).

- Orthography-driven correspondences and data curation concerns. All experiments are on raw orthographic forms; the observed script effects (Latin vs Cyrillic Serbo-Croatian) indicate the model may conflate script/orthography with phonological correspondences. Without phonemic normalization or grapheme-to-phoneme mapping, it is difficult to claim discovery of “linguistic correspondences” rather than orthographic regularities. Data sources (Wiktionary Swadesh lists, collected cognates) and preprocessing are only briefly described; reproducibility details are missing.

- Ambiguities and possible inconsistencies in the coding formulation. The L(Π) term combines counts, subset-classification overhead, and a data-to-model code; the roles of LN(TΠ), the composition term log((TΠ−1 choose |Π|−1)), and the per-subset LN(|ΠCi|+1) could be double-counting or at least need clearer derivation. The definition of code(σ) is tied to alphabet unigrams via a data-to-model code, yet L(π) uses these codes; the order of transmission and parameter-free nature should be made explicit to avoid circularity. Similarly, code(π) in L(d|M) is not fully specified (is it the optimal usage code derived from counts?).

- Scalability and computational complexity. The E-step is stated as O(|D|·|R|^2) with a Dijkstra alignment graph that can become large; the M-step is O(|D|·A^2/2). The current implementation handles up to five languages “within a few hours” on modest hardware, which limits claims about analyzing “more than two languages” at scale. There is no profiling, pruning strategy analysis, or complexity bound in terms of alphabet sizes and maximum rule length.

- The overweighting heuristic weakens the “objective MDL” claim without controlled analysis. Introducing α to upweight L(D|M) is reasonable as a practical knob, but there is no systematic study of its impact on rule precision/recall, stability, or downstream tasks. The paper positions overweighting as yielding desired granularity, but this blurs the strong claim of assumption-free inference.

Questions to authors
- Coding details: Please provide a precise derivation of L(Π) and L(Σi). How are LN(TΠ), the per-subset LN(|ΠCi|+1), and the composition term combined without double-counting? What exactly is code(π) in L(d|M), and how is it computed from counts? In what order are the data-to-model codes transmitted to avoid circular dependencies between L(π) and code(σ)?

- Candidate generation and merging: How are candidate rule merges selected at each M-step? Are all pairs considered, or only those co-occurring in alignments? How is determinism ensured in the presence of ties?

- Alignment graph: What is the node/edge complexity in terms of N, maximum rule length, and string lengths? What partial order reduction is applied, and how does it affect optimality?

- Data preprocessing: What normalization was applied (case-folding, diacritics, transliteration)? Were homographs/homophones disambiguated? Could you report results on phonemic transcriptions to separate orthographic from phonological effects?

- Baselines and metrics: Can you compare against a pair-HMM/PMI baseline on the same data (e.g., alignment likelihood, B-Cubed on gold segmentations if available, or tree accuracy vs. gold classification)? For the four-way setting, is there a quantitative measure demonstrating improved fit vs. pairwise-only models?

- Overweighting: How is α chosen? How stable are the learned rule sets across α and across random subsets (e.g., bootstrap)? Can you quantify the trade-off between granularity and overfitting?

Additional comments
- Reference formatting and some notation have inconsistencies; consider tightening presentation and providing released code/data for reproducibility.
