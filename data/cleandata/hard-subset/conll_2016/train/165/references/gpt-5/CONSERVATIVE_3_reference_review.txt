Summary and contributions
The paper proposes an MDL-based framework to discover string-level correspondence rules across two or more languages, learned via EM with shortest-path alignment and greedy rule merges. The method is claimed to make minimal distributional assumptions, be deterministic, and support multi-language analyses. Experiments on Slavic Swadesh lists and a small set of internationalisms produce pairwise NCD distances and a phylogenetic tree that broadly matches known groupings, and a four-language analysis using “shared description length” (sDL) to quantify overlap among all subsets.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A concrete MDL coding scheme (PS-2) that encodes multiway correspondence rules and their usage, combined with a deterministic EM-style training and Dijkstra-based alignment to discover correspondences across N languages.
2) An application of the learned MDL code to produce pairwise NCD and a phylogenetic tree for Slavic languages, and a multiway similarity analysis via shared description length.
3) A practical “data over-weighting” knob (α) to control the granularity of discovered rules.

Strengths
- Clear, principled coding formulation: The paper gives a reasonably detailed two-part MDL code that ties together symbol unigrams, rule tables by language subsets, and data-to-model codes. The connection to prior MDL work on serial episodes is appropriate, and the shortest-path view of alignment is intuitive and implementable.
- Multi-language alignment ambition: Moving beyond pairwise correspondences is a real need in computational historical linguistics. The framework does, in principle, allow rules with arbitrary subsets of languages, and the sDL analysis is a neat way to quantify shared structure across all subsets.
- Qualitative plausibility of learned rules: The examples for Czech–Polish and Russian–Bulgarian include plausible correspondences (e.g., cz–č, g–h, eć–et, palatalizations), suggesting the approach can recover meaningful patterns even from orthographic input without phonological normalization.
- Coarse phylogenetic signal: The pairwise NCD-derived distances produce a tree that largely matches known Slavic subgroupings, and the Latin vs Cyrillic Serbo-Croatian difference illustrates sensitivity to orthographic conventions, which is informative if not entirely desirable.

Weaknesses
- Limited and unconvincing evaluation against established baselines: The paper does not compare against standard methods for cognate alignment or sound correspondence discovery (e.g., ALINE, PHMM/Pair-HMM-based aligners, LexStat/lingpy, PMI-based approaches, or Bayesian models of sound change). There are no quantitative metrics (precision/recall of alignments, cognate identification accuracy, or alignment likelihood) nor quantitative phylogeny validation (e.g., RF distance to a reference tree). As a result, it is impossible to assess whether the proposed method is competitive, more accurate, or more robust than established baselines.
- Orthography-only data and confounds: All analyses are on raw orthography, mixing scripts and diacritics. The pairwise NCDs and learned correspondences likely capture orthographic conventions as much as phonology (e.g., the Latin vs Cyrillic effect). Without standard phonological normalization or mapping to sound classes, results risk reflecting script and spelling rather than sound correspondences. This undermines the claim of “objective” correspondences.
- Scalability and practicality: The paper reports handling up to five languages on modest hardware “within a few hours,” but provides no systematic runtime/memory scaling, no bounds on rule length, and no pruning analysis. The EM with greedy merges has quadratic factors in the number of rule applications, and alignment graphs can blow up combinatorially. For practical multi-language applications (beyond N≈4–5), the approach appears not yet scalable.
- Claims of objectivity and determinism are overstated: MDL still embeds modeling choices (code design, data-to-model coding, Laplace smoothing, null initialization, greedy merge heuristic). These choices are inductive biases. The “data overweighting” α explicitly departs from MDL optimality and injects subjective control. The search is non-convex; greedy merges can get stuck. Determinism does not imply lack of bias.
- NCD formulation and comparability concerns: It appears L(X,Y) and L(X,X) are computed from pair-specific models; if so, distances across different pairs may not be directly comparable and metric properties are not guaranteed. The paper should clarify whether a single universal code is used across comparisons or pairwise-trained codes, and how this affects interpretability.
- Reproducibility gaps: No code release is mentioned; dataset construction from Wiktionary and other sources is only briefly described; parameter settings, stopping criteria, rule-length limits, tie-breaking, and convergence criteria are not detailed. The “overweight until no singleton rules” heuristic is ad hoc and unreported quantitatively.

Questions to authors
- Baselines and metrics: Can you compare your alignments and correspondences to ALINE, PHMM, LexStat/lingpy, or PMI-based models on the same Slavic data? For phylogeny, can you compute RF distance to a reference tree and compare to distances from those baselines?
- Normalization: What happens if you transliterate to a unified script and/or map to sound classes (e.g., SCA or IPA)? Do your NCDs and learned correspondences become more phonologically interpretable and less orthography-driven?
- NCD details: Are L(X,X), L(Y,Y), and L(X,Y) all computed under a single model trained on X∪Y, or are they derived from separately trained codes? How do you ensure comparability across pairs and adherence to metric properties?
- Scalability: What are typical R and A values, rule-length distributions, and graph sizes for N=3,4,5? Any pruning strategies (max rule length, frequency thresholds, beam search) beyond partial order reduction? Could you report runtime and memory vs N and dataset size?
- Search and convergence: Do you use multiple restarts or any annealing to escape local optima? How sensitive are results to initialization (null vs seeded) and Laplace smoothing? Any ablation studies?
- Overweighting α: How do you choose α in practice? Can you show a curve of downstream metrics (e.g., alignment F1 vs α) to justify its use and to avoid overfitting long, idiosyncratic rules?
- Missing data: You state the method is agnostic to gaps, yet experiments filter to complete tuples. How does the model handle missing entries in practice, especially for multiway rules and sDL computation?

Overall, the idea of an MDL-based multiway correspondence learner is interesting, and the qualitative examples are promising. However, without rigorous comparisons to established methods, quantitative evaluation, phonological normalization, and clearer scalability and reproducibility, the submission does not yet meet the level of empirical validation I would expect for acceptance.
