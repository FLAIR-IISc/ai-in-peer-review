Summary and main contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework for discovering multi-way string correspondences across languages from lists of cognate tuples. The model treats correspondences as associated character strings without parametric assumptions, and selects a set of correspondence rules that minimize a two-part MDL objective over the model and the data. An EM-like training procedure alternates optimal alignments (via shortest paths in a weighted graph) and greedy model updates that merge rules when they reduce total description length. Experiments on Slavic languages report pairwise Normalized Compression Distances (NCD) and a neighbor-joining tree, qualitative rule examples, and a four-language analysis using a “shared Description Length” (sDL) decomposition. The paper also introduces a pragmatic “data over-weighting” knob to surface near-significant rules.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) An MDL formulation and deterministic algorithm for discovering multi-language, variable-length string correspondences directly from data, with an EM-style procedure (shortest-path alignment + MDL-guided greedy merging).
2) A multi-language analysis capability beyond pairwise models, including an information-theoretic decomposition (sDL) that quantifies structure shared by arbitrary subsets of languages.
3) A qualitative evaluation on Slavic languages showing plausible correspondences and a phylogeny broadly consistent with linguistic classifications, plus a practical “over-weighting” mechanism to control rule granularity.

Strengths
- Principled, assumption-light objective: The two-part MDL formulation with explicit model and data codes offers a clean, bias-minimizing way to select correspondences, avoiding ad hoc priors or hard constraints on rule shape/length. This makes the discovered correspondences interpretable and, in principle, reproducible.
- Handles more than two languages: The framework is natively formulated for N languages, which is uncommon in the literature that often focuses on pairwise correspondences or alignment. The sDL analysis is a useful lens to quantify which subsets share structure and to tease apart asymmetries among languages.
- Clear, interpretable modeling choices: Rules are human-readable multi-character correspondences; the deterministic merging procedure grows rules by statistical evidence; seeding with expert rules is supported. The qualitative examples (e.g., cz–č, palatalizations, metathesis) show that linguistically meaningful patterns emerge naturally.
- Plausible empirical outcomes: The NCD-based phylogeny broadly matches known Slavic groupings and highlights expected outliers (Bulgarian/Macedonian; Upper/Lower Sorbian; orthography effects for Serbo-Croatian). The four-way analysis suggests subtle interdependencies (e.g., RU–BG), aligning with linguistic intuition.
- Methodological connections and extensibility: The approach relates to MDL pattern mining (e.g., KRIMP/SLIM/serial episodes) and shortest-path alignment, offering a solid foundation for future scaling and hybridization (e.g., initialization from linguistic priors, annealing).

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation and lack of baselines: There is no comparison against established methods for cognate alignment or sound correspondences (e.g., ALINE, LexStat/ASJP/PMI-based approaches, Jäger/Wieling methods), no intrinsic evaluation (alignment accuracy) against gold standards, and no extrinsic evaluation (e.g., cognate prediction, reconstruction, or cross-lingual transfer). The phylogeny is presented without quantitative metrics (e.g., RF distance, bootstrap), and the sDL analysis lacks validation.
- Orthography vs phonology confound: All experiments use raw orthography across diverse scripts, conflating orthographic conventions with phonological correspondences. This likely explains parts of the results (diacritics, digraphs) and blurs linguistic generalization. At minimum, a phonemic transcription or grapheme-to-phoneme approximation should be tested, or orthographic normalization/controls reported.
- Scalability and practicality: Although the method is N-agnostic in principle, experiments only go up to N=4, and the current implementation handles up to five languages with multi-hour runtimes on modest hardware. Complexity is stated but with vague parameters (R, A). There are no runtime/memory plots, convergence curves, or pruning strategies beyond “partial order reduction,” leaving scalability uncertain.
- Methodological clarity/detail gaps: The model code L(Π) and data-to-model code components are presented tersely, with nonstandard terms (“weak number composition”) and without derivations or ablations. The M-step greedily merges two patterns per iteration; criteria for candidate generation, tie-breaking, and avoidance of spurious long merges are not fully specified. The Laplace correction and its effect on convergence and stability are not analyzed. NCD computation details (how L(X,Y) is estimated and symmetry ensured) are not fully spelled out.
- Departure from principled MDL via “over-weighting”: The α-weighted objective is useful pragmatically but undercuts the claimed objectivity. No guidance is provided on choosing α, nor are there ablations showing when overweighting improves downstream tasks. The interplay between α and overfitting is not analyzed.
- Reproducibility and presentation issues: No code or data release is indicated (beyond pointing to Wiktionary). References and figure mentions are inconsistent or incomplete, and some formulae lack definitions (e.g., counts and combinatorial terms). This reduces clarity and hinders replication.

Questions to authors
- Evaluation: Can you provide quantitative comparisons to baseline correspondence/aligners (e.g., ALINE, PMI-based models, LexStat) on standard datasets, and/or intrinsic alignment accuracy against available gold standards? Could you include extrinsic tasks (cognate prediction, missing-form reconstruction) with train/test splits?
- Orthography control: How sensitive are your results to phonemic vs orthographic input? Can you report experiments with phonetic transcriptions or normalized alphabets to separate orthographic artifacts from phonological correspondences?
- NCD and sDL details: Precisely how are L(X,X) and L(X,Y) computed (shared vs separate model training, symmetry, and coding choices)? For sDL, why sum model code lengths L(π) rather than usage-weighted quantities? Can you provide a normalization to mitigate biases from differing alphabet sizes and diacritics?
- Algorithmic specifics and scalability: Please detail candidate generation for merges, tie-breaking, pruning, and stopping criteria; provide runtime/memory scaling with |D|, alphabet sizes, and N; and show convergence behavior. Any plans for beam search, batching, or GPU acceleration for larger N?
- Over-weighting: How should α be selected? Can you show its impact on downstream performance and rule interpretability, and provide a principled alternative (e.g., hierarchical MDL priors, minimum-support constraints) that preserves objectivity?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, processed datasets, and scripts used to generate the phylogeny and sDL plots?
