## Review of “Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL”

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to discovering systematic correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors argue that existing methods often impose arbitrary assumptions, and their method aims to find correspondences without such priors, operating directly from data. They present an efficient, deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) and demonstrate its efficacy on Slavic languages, showcasing both pairwise analysis and a four-language experiment, highlighting the algorithm's ability to identify linguistic subgroups and quantify similarities.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A principled, assumption-minimal approach to multi-way correspondence discovery:** The use of MDL provides a statistically sound framework, moving away from ad-hoc assumptions commonly found in other methods. This is the core strength of the paper.
2. **A deterministic algorithm for correspondence learning:** The EM-based algorithm is a practical contribution, enabling automated discovery of patterns without reliance on stochastic search or pre-defined parameters.  The focus on determinism is notable.
3. **Demonstration of scalability beyond pairwise comparisons:** The four-language experiment successfully showcases the method's ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously, opening doors to more complex analyses.



**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The MDL framework provides a strong justification for the approach. The authors correctly connect it to related concepts like compression distance and Bayesian inference, clearly articulating its benefits. The two-part MDL formulation is appropriate and well-explained.
2. **Novelty of Approach:**  While MDL has been used in related areas (etymological modeling, as cited), its application to *discovery* of correspondences across *multiple* languages in a deterministic and assumption-minimal way is genuinely novel.  This distinguishes it from previous work focused on cognate identification or reconstruction with pre-defined models.
3. **Clear Algorithmic Description:** The paper presents a well-defined algorithm, detailing the EM steps and initialization procedures.  The explanation of how the model code and data code are constructed is sufficiently clear, although complex.  The discussion of computational complexity is valuable.
4. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Slavic languages, while limited in scope, demonstrate the method’s ability to capture meaningful linguistic relationships. The comparison with linguistic classifications and the demonstration of subgroup identification are positive results.  The inclusion of NCD-based phylogenetic trees is a useful visualization.
5. **Data Over-Weighting as a Control Mechanism:** The introduction of data over-weighting is a clever addition, providing a way to tune the granularity of the discovered correspondences and potentially incorporate linguistic expertise.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Evaluation & Data Scope:** The experiments are limited to a relatively small dataset (Swadesh lists) and a single language family (Slavic).  While a good starting point, this restricts the generalizability of the findings.  A larger, more diverse dataset is needed to demonstrate the robustness of the approach.  The authors acknowledge this but it remains a significant limitation.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Baselines:** The paper would be strengthened by a comparison to existing methods, even if those methods are primarily designed for pairwise comparisons.  Adapting existing techniques to a multi-way setting and comparing performance (e.g., in terms of alignment accuracy, recall, or tree similarity) would provide a stronger assessment of the proposed approach.  Simply stating the shortcomings of existing approaches is not sufficient.
3. **Complexity of Implementation & Scalability Concerns:** The authors mention memory and runtime bottlenecks, limiting the number of languages that can be processed.  While they acknowledge this and plan future work, it’s a significant practical limitation.  The computational complexity discussion is appreciated, but more detail on the bottlenecks and potential optimization strategies would be helpful.
4. **Subjectivity in Data Preparation:** The choice of using only cognates present in *all* languages could introduce bias, potentially excluding valuable information about less consistent correspondences.  The impact of this decision on the results isn't fully discussed.
5. **Interpretation of Results:**  While the learned correspondences are interesting, the paper lacks a deeper linguistic analysis of *why* certain patterns emerge.  Connecting the discovered rules to established linguistic theories or historical sound changes would enhance the significance of the results.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Regarding scalability:** Can you provide more details on the specific bottlenecks in the implementation? What optimizations are you considering to address the memory and runtime issues?  Could parallelization strategies be employed?
2. **Regarding data preparation:** How sensitive are the results to the requirement that cognates be present in all languages? Have you experimented with different data inclusion criteria?
3. **Regarding baseline comparison:** Are you aware of any existing methods that could be adapted for multi-way correspondence discovery? If so, why weren't they included in the evaluation? If not, why is it difficult to adapt them?
4. **Regarding linguistic interpretation:**  Can you elaborate on the linguistic significance of the discovered rules? Do they align with known sound changes or historical relationships within the Slavic language family?
5. **Regarding data weighting:** What criteria were used to select the over-weighting factor in the examples presented in Section 5?  Is there a systematic way to determine an optimal weighting factor based on the data?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The notation is generally consistent, but a glossary of symbols would be helpful.
* The figures are informative and well-labeled.
* The references are relevant and up-to-date.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and promising paper.  The MDL-based approach is novel and theoretically sound, and the deterministic algorithm is a significant practical contribution. However, the limited evaluation and lack of comparison to baselines are weaknesses that need to be addressed.  Addressing the questions above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, contingent on the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
