This paper presents a novel application of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to discover correspondences between multiple languages simultaneously. The primary contribution is a deterministic algorithm leveraging MDL to infer correspondence rules without imposing prior assumptions about their shape or distribution, addressing a long-standing problem in historical linguistics and multilingual NLP. A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration of this approach on Slavic languages, yielding both a phylogenetic analysis and detailed quantification of language similarity beyond pairwise comparisons. Finally, the introduction of data over-weighting as a mechanism to inject linguistic knowledge and control granularity is a notable addition.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The application of MDL to multi-lingual correspondence discovery is genuinely novel. The field has been dominated by methods relying on statistical assumptions or pairwise comparisons. Bypassing these limitations with a purely data-driven, information-theoretic approach is a significant step forward. The deterministic nature of the algorithm is also refreshing in a field often reliant on stochastic methods.
2. **Beyond Pairwise Comparisons:** The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously is a key strength. Existing methods struggle with the combinatorial explosion that occurs when scaling to more languages. This paper effectively tackles that challenge, opening doors to more comprehensive linguistic analysis. The demonstration with four Slavic languages is compelling.
3. **Quantifiable Linguistic Similarity:** The use of NCD and shared Description Lengths provides a quantifiable measure of linguistic similarity that is grounded in information theory. This is more objective than many existing methods and allows for a more nuanced understanding of language relationships. The visualization of shared description lengths is particularly insightful.
4. **Data Over-Weighting as a Control Mechanism:** The introduced data over-weighting provides a clever mechanism to balance objectivity with the need for linguistic expertise. This allows users to fine-tune the granularity of the discovered correspondences, a practical consideration for real-world applications.
5. **Clear Presentation & Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the MDL framework and the proposed algorithm. The code and data used in the experiments seem readily available, facilitating reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Validation Beyond Phylogeny:** While the phylogenetic tree aligns with established linguistic classifications, the validation is primarily based on external consistency. A more rigorous evaluation, perhaps using held-out cognates or a comparison with other established correspondence datasets, would strengthen the claims.
2. **Computational Scalability:** The authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks, limiting the analysis to a relatively small number of languages. While they suggest future work to address this, it's a significant limitation in the current implementation. Further optimization is crucial.
3. **Orthographic Focus & Lack of Phonological Integration:** The approach focuses solely on orthographic forms, ignoring underlying phonological variations. While this is a deliberate choice to avoid imposing prior knowledge, it limits the depth of linguistic analysis. Integrating phonetic information could significantly enhance the accuracy and relevance of the discovered correspondences.
4. **Sensitivity to Data Quality:** The deterministic nature of the algorithm, while a strength, may also be a weakness. Poorly curated or inconsistent data could lead to spurious correspondences. A discussion of data quality control and robustness to noise would be beneficial.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods (beyond NCD):** The paper primarily compares against linguistic classifications. A more direct comparison of the discovered correspondences with those generated by existing statistical methods (even if those methods are limited to pairwise comparisons) would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the methods used for data cleaning and ensuring consistency in the orthographic forms used across the different languages?
2.  What are the current computational bottlenecks preventing scaling to a larger number of languages, and what specific optimization strategies are you exploring to address these limitations?
3.  Have you considered incorporating phonetic information or phonological rules into the model? If so, what challenges did you encounter, and what potential benefits do you foresee?
4.  Can you provide examples of spurious correspondences that arose from the algorithm with noisy data, and describe how the data over-weighting parameter was used to mitigate these issues?
5.  How do the discovered correspondences compare to those generated by established (but potentially pairwise-limited) methods like Levenshtein distance-based approaches or cognate identification algorithms?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper represents a significant advance in the field of computational linguistics. Despite minor weaknesses, the novelty of the approach and the potential for future applications outweigh the concerns. I am inclined towards acceptance, recognizing the risk of overlooking methodological perfection in favour of a truly innovative idea. The authors should be encouraged to address the questions above to further strengthen the paper.
