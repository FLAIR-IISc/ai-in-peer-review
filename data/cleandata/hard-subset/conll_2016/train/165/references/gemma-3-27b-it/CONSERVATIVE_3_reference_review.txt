This paper proposes a Minimum Description Length (MDL) approach for discovering correspondences between multiple languages, aiming to overcome limitations of existing statistical methods that often rely on distributional assumptions or are limited to pairwise comparisons. The authors present an algorithm and demonstrate it on Slavic languages, analyzing both pairwise similarities and relationships between four languages.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a deterministic algorithm for finding string-level correspondences based on MDL principles, which *potentially* offers a more objective approach than methods reliant on prior assumptions about correspondence shapes. The application to multiple languages (beyond pairwise) is a secondary, but notable, aspect. The experimental validation using Slavic languages and comparison with linguistic classifications provides *some* empirical support for the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges in discovering language correspondences and the limitations of current statistical approaches. The motivation for seeking an assumption-free method is well-presented.
2. **Methodological Rigor (in Principle):** The grounding in MDL provides a statistically sound framework. The use of a deterministic algorithm, while potentially limiting exploration, aims for reproducibility and avoids the influence of chance.  The detailed explanation of the code and the EM approach are commendable.
3. **Multi-Language Extension:** Extending the analysis beyond pairwise comparisons is a valuable step, addressing a significant limitation of many existing methods. The ability to quantify similarities between all subsets of languages is a potentially useful feature.
4. **Demonstrated Functionality:** The experiments, while limited in scope (discussed in weaknesses), do demonstrate that the algorithm *can* identify plausible correspondences and generate a phylogenetic tree aligning with established linguistic classifications. The example alignments provided are illustrative.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Rigorous Baseline Comparison:** The most significant weakness is the near-complete absence of comparison to established methods. The paper mentions existing work but doesn't demonstrate how the proposed approach performs *relative* to them.  Simply stating that other methods have limitations isn't sufficient; a direct, quantitative comparison is crucial. How does this MDL approach compare, for example, to cognate identification methods based on probabilistic models (e.g., Levenshtein distance with appropriate weighting)? Without this, the claim of offering a superior “objective” approach remains unsubstantiated.
2. **Limited Data & Scope:** The experiments are performed on a relatively small dataset (Swadesh lists) and a limited number of languages. While the Slavic languages provide a good test case, it's unclear how well the method will generalize to languages with different typological features or larger vocabularies. The reliance on cognate tuples, while understandable for initial validation, limits the method's applicability to broader linguistic analysis.
3. **Over-Weighting as a "Fix" for Data Sparsity:** The introduction of data over-weighting as a means to address insufficient statistical evidence feels ad-hoc and introduces subjectivity.  While acknowledging this, framing it as a way to inject "linguistic expertise" is problematic; it undermines the claim of an objective, data-driven approach. A more robust solution would be to explore methods for handling data sparsity inherent in the MDL framework itself.
4. **Computational Scalability:**  The authors acknowledge limitations in scalability. Processing only up to five languages is a significant constraint, especially given the goal of analyzing multiple languages simultaneously.  While future work is planned to address this, it currently restricts the practical applicability of the method.  Furthermore, the complexity analysis provided is not fully convincing without details about the practical performance on larger datasets.
5. **Interpretability of Rules:** While the algorithm identifies correspondences, the interpretation of these rules and their linguistic significance isn’t fully explored. The presented examples are helpful, but a more in-depth analysis of the types of correspondences discovered and their relationship to known sound changes or morphological processes is needed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a quantitative comparison of your method against at least two established baseline methods for cognate identification or language similarity analysis (e.g., methods based on Levenshtein distance, probabilistic models, or existing statistical phylogenetic approaches).  Include metrics beyond NCD to demonstrate performance.
2.  How sensitive is the algorithm to the choice of data (e.g., the specific Swadesh list used, the inclusion/exclusion of certain cognates)?  Have you explored the impact of data quality and quantity on the results?
3.  Can you elaborate on the rationale behind the data over-weighting technique? What specific linguistic principles guide the decision of how much to over-weight the data?  Are there alternative approaches to address data sparsity within the MDL framework?
4.  What are the primary bottlenecks in the algorithm's computational scalability? What specific optimizations are planned to address these limitations and enable analysis of larger datasets?
5.   Beyond simply demonstrating that the algorithm generates plausible correspondences, can you provide evidence that the discovered rules reveal linguistically meaningful patterns or insights that were not previously known?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper's writing is generally clear and well-organized. However, the frequent use of abbreviations (e.g., NCD, MDL) without sufficient initial explanation can make it difficult for readers unfamiliar with the field to follow the arguments.  A glossary of terms might be helpful. The reference to "Likomanova (2004) and (Angelov, 2004)" needs to be fully specified (title, journal/conference).
