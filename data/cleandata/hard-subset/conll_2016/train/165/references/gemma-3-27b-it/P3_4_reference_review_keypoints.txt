- Summary: Proposes a novel MDL-based, deterministic method to discover correspondences across multiple languages and validates it on Slavic languages to identify subgroups and quantify similarities.

- Contribution 1: Introduces a principled, assumption-free MDL framework for multi-way linguistic comparison enabling discovery of previously unseen patterns.

- Contribution 2: Presents a deterministic, efficient algorithm (EM with singleton initialization) that promotes reproducibility and linguistic interpretability.

- Contribution 3: Demonstrates the method on Slavic languages, producing plausible correspondences, phylogenetic trees, and similarity measures.

- Strength 1: The MDL approach is novel and theoretically well-grounded (two-part MDL appropriate for the task).

- Strength 2: Determinism and interpretability allow linguists to inspect and refine learned correspondences.

- Strength 3: Method scales to more than two languages, offering richer insights than pairwise comparisons.

- Strength 4: Results are insightful—phylogeny largely matches established classifications and correspondences capture phonological/orthographic patterns; shared description lengths are a useful metric.

- Strength 5: Paper is clearly written and well-structured with detailed MDL/code explanations.

- Weakness 1: Computational complexity and long runtimes (hours on modest hardware) limit applicability to larger datasets; needs complexity analysis and optimizations.

- Weakness 2: Heavy dependence on limited data (Swadesh lists) and requirement for fully defined cognates reduces robustness; handling missing data would be valuable.

- Weakness 3: Lacks direct empirical comparison with existing statistical correspondence-discovery methods (accuracy and runtime).

- Weakness 4: Data over-weighting adds subjectivity; impact of different weighting schemes and best-practice guidance are missing.

- Weakness 5: Limited error analysis—no thorough discussion or examples of incorrect/spurious correspondences.

- Question 1: What is the detailed algorithmic complexity, current bottlenecks, and possible optimizations?

- Question 2: How sensitive are results to initialization (null vs. greedy) and have other initializations been tested?

- Question 3: Have other data sources (parallel corpora, etymological databases) been considered to improve robustness?

- Question 4: How was the optimal over-weighting factor chosen and balanced between objectivity and linguistic intuition?

- Question 5: Can you provide examples of incorrect correspondences and explain their causes?

- Additional comment 1: Figure numbering is out of order (Fig. 2 before Fig. 1).

- Additional comment 2: Discuss potential applications beyond phylogenetics (e.g., machine translation, language learning).

- Overall recommendation: Strong, innovative paper—recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
