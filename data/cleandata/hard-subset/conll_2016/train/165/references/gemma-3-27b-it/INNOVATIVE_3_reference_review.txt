This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core contribution is framing the problem not as finding the *best* correspondence based on pre-defined distributions, but as identifying the *most succinct* description of the observed data – effectively letting the data itself reveal the underlying patterns. The authors propose a deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) for inferring these correspondences, and demonstrate its efficacy on Slavic languages, showcasing both pairwise comparisons and a more detailed analysis of four languages simultaneously.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** This is the paper’s strongest asset. The application of MDL to multilingual correspondence discovery, particularly the deliberate avoidance of distributional assumptions, is a genuinely fresh perspective. Most existing work relies on statistical models with inherent biases. This work attempts to bypass that limitation, which is a significant conceptual leap.
2. **Handling Multiple Languages:** The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously, without falling back to pairwise comparisons, is a substantial practical advantage. This allows for uncovering relationships and quantifying similarities across a linguistic landscape that traditional methods struggle with. The four-way analysis demonstrates this capability effectively.
3. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The authors’ commitment to a deterministic algorithm is commendable. While potentially limiting in some scenarios, it offers transparency and reproducibility, vital for linguistic analysis where interpretability is paramount. The EM framework is well-motivated and appears sound, even if scalability remains a concern.
4. **Informative Experimental Results:** The results presented are compelling. The phylogenetic tree generated from the NCD values aligns well with established linguistic classifications, and the example correspondences are intuitively plausible. The demonstration of data over-weighting as a method for controlling granularity is a useful addition.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:** The authors acknowledge the computational limitations, mentioning processing five languages on a standard desktop. This severely restricts the applicability of the method to larger language families or more extensive datasets. While a deterministic algorithm offers advantages, its practical use is undermined if it can only handle small-scale problems.
2. **Limited Validation Beyond Slavic Languages:** The experiments are entirely focused on Slavic languages. While this allows for in-depth analysis, it limits the generalizability of the findings. Testing on languages from different families (e.g., Indo-European, Sino-Tibetan) is crucial to assess the robustness of the approach.
3. **Orthographic Focus & Lack of Phonological Integration:** The current implementation operates solely on orthographic forms, ignoring underlying phonological information. This is a significant limitation as orthography can be misleading. Incorporating phonological transcriptions, even as an optional step, would substantially enhance the accuracy and linguistic relevance of the discovered correspondences.
4. **Data Dependency & Over-Weighting:** The reliance on Swadesh lists and the introduction of data over-weighting feel somewhat *ad hoc*. While over-weighting offers a way to influence the granularity of results, it introduces a degree of subjectivity that contradicts the otherwise objective nature of the MDL approach. A more principled method for handling data imbalance would be preferable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What are the primary bottlenecks in the algorithm's runtime? Can algorithmic optimizations (e.g., parallelization, efficient data structures) be employed to improve scalability?
2.  Do you anticipate significant changes in the discovered correspondences if phonological transcriptions were used instead of, or in addition to, orthographic forms? Can you sketch out how the model would need to be adapted to incorporate phonological features?
3. Beyond linguistic expertise, what objective criteria could be used to determine an appropriate level of data over-weighting? 
4. How does the algorithm handle languages with drastically different writing systems (e.g., alphabetic vs. logographic)?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the MDL approach. The references are appropriate. The choice to focus on Slavic languages is understandable given the data available, but future work should prioritize expanding the scope of evaluation. The discussion of the limitations in Section 5 is appreciated.



Despite the scalability and validation limitations, I lean towards **acceptance**. The novelty of the approach, its ability to handle multiple languages, and the promising initial results outweigh the weaknesses. This paper represents a significant step forward in automated linguistic analysis, and the authors have opened up a new avenue for research in multilingual correspondence discovery. The methodological imperfections are acceptable given the groundbreaking nature of the idea.
