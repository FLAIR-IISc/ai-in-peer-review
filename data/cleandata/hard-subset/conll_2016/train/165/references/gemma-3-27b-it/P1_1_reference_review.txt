## Peer Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to identify sets of correspondence rules that succinctly describe the data, without imposing prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of these rules. The authors propose a deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) to infer these rules and demonstrate its efficacy through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages, including pairwise comparisons and a four-language experiment. They also introduce a data over-weighting technique to allow for more detailed, albeit less statistically rigorous, analysis.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **MDL-based Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The application of MDL to this problem is a significant contribution. It provides a principled, statistically sound approach to identifying correspondences, avoiding the pitfalls of arbitrary assumptions inherent in many existing methods. This allows for a more objective and data-driven analysis.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm & Scalability:**  The development of a deterministic EM-based algorithm for inferring correspondence rules is valuable. While the paper acknowledges scalability limitations, the demonstrated ability to process up to five languages within a reasonable timeframe is encouraging, and the authors outline plans for improvement.
3. **Detailed Quantification of Linguistic Similarity:** The extension of linguistic similarity quantification beyond pairwise comparisons, enabled by the MDL framework, is novel. The ability to analyze relationships between subsets of multiple languages provides a more nuanced understanding of linguistic connections and historical relationships.



**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The grounding of the approach in MDL provides a strong theoretical foundation.  The clear explanation of the two-part MDL code and its connection to information theory is commendable. The authors convincingly argue that this approach avoids the bias introduced by prior assumptions.
2. **Novelty and Objectivity:** The method's avoidance of arbitrary assumptions and deterministic nature sets it apart from many statistical approaches in historical linguistics. This offers a refreshing degree of objectivity in a field often reliant on expert intuition. 
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Slavic languages provide a convincing demonstration of the approach's effectiveness. The phylogenetic tree generated from the NCD values aligns well with established linguistic classifications, indicating the validity of the discovered correspondences. 
4. **Data Over-Weighting as a Tool:** The introduction of data over-weighting is a clever mechanism that allows researchers to control the level of detail in the learned correspondences, balancing statistical rigor with linguistic insight. This offers a practical way to tailor the analysis to specific research questions.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology, even for readers unfamiliar with MDL. The inclusion of examples (Table 3 & 4) helps to illustrate the learned correspondences.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:**  The acknowledged limitations in scalability remain a significant concern. Processing only up to five languages on a standard desktop machine is restrictive.  While future work is planned, the current practical applicability is limited, particularly for languages with large vocabularies.  The computational complexity discussion (O(|D| · |R|2) and O(|D|·A2/2) ) feels a bit hand-wavy without concrete details about |R| and |A| for real-world datasets.
2. **Data Dependency and Preprocessing:** The method’s reliance on accurate cognate lists is a limitation.  The quality of the results is inherently tied to the quality and completeness of the initial data. The lack of discussion regarding automatic cognate identification or methods to handle noisy data is a drawback. The simple orthographic form without normalization (e.g. different transliterations of Cyrillic) may affect results.
3. **Limited Evaluation Beyond Phylogeny:** While the phylogenetic analysis is a good starting point, the evaluation could be strengthened with more sophisticated metrics and comparisons to existing methods.  For example, comparing the learned correspondences to a gold standard (if available) or evaluating their usefulness in downstream tasks like machine translation would be valuable.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:**  The paper does not provide a detailed error analysis of the learned correspondences. Identifying the types of errors made by the algorithm and exploring the reasons behind them could provide valuable insights for future improvement. 
5. **Complexity of MDL explanation**: The MDL explanation, while thorough, may be challenging for those not already familiar with the principle.  A more accessible introduction or simplified explanation could broaden the paper's appeal.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Scalability:** Can you provide more specific details on the computational bottlenecks limiting scalability? What are the typical values of |R| and |A| for the datasets you used, and what optimizations are you considering to address these limitations?
2. **Data Preprocessing:** What steps, if any, were taken to ensure the quality and consistency of the cognate lists? Have you explored methods for handling noisy data or automatically identifying cognates?
3. **Evaluation Metrics:** Do you have plans to evaluate the learned correspondences using metrics beyond phylogenetic accuracy? For example, could these correspondences be used to improve machine translation performance, and if so, what results could be expected?
4. **Error Analysis:**  Could you provide examples of cases where the algorithm produced incorrect or suboptimal correspondences, and explain the potential reasons for these errors?
5. **Data Over-weighting:** Can you provide a more concrete example of how a linguist would strategically choose the value of *α* in the data over-weighting scheme? What linguistic considerations would inform this choice?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list seems incomplete.
*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of related work in unsupervised morphological segmentation.
*   Figures 1-3 are a bit small and could be improved for readability.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and innovative paper that presents a promising new approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages. While there are some limitations, particularly regarding scalability and evaluation, the strengths of the approach – its theoretical soundness, objectivity, and ability to analyze relationships beyond pairwise comparisons – are significant.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses and answering the questions posed above.
