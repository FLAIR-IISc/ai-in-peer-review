## Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

**Summary:**

This paper proposes a novel approach to discovering systematic correspondences between languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to identify the set of correspondence rules that most succinctly describe the data, without imposing prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of those rules. The authors present an efficient, deterministic algorithm for inferring these rules and demonstrate its effectiveness on Slavic languages, showcasing both pairwise analyses and a more detailed four-language experiment.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **MDL-based Framework for Correspondence Discovery:** The application of the MDL principle to the problem of linguistic correspondence is a conceptually sound and potentially valuable contribution. It offers a principled way to balance model complexity and data fit, potentially avoiding the biases introduced by other statistical approaches.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm for Rule Inference:**  The authors’ development of a deterministic algorithm to implement their MDL approach is a significant practical achievement. This contrasts with many statistical methods that rely on sampling or optimization techniques. Determinism promotes reproducibility and interpretability.
3. **Extension to Multiple Languages:** The method explicitly targets and successfully handles the complexities of analyzing correspondences across *more* than two languages, a common limitation of existing methods. This unlocks possibilities for more comprehensive linguistic analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The use of MDL provides a robust statistical justification for the method. The authors clearly explain the core principles of MDL and how they apply to the problem of correspondence discovery. The connection to coding theory and information content is well-articulated.
2. **Novelty of Approach:** While MDL has been used in related areas, its application to *unsupervised* discovery of multi-lingual correspondences with a *deterministic* algorithm represents a novel combination of ideas.  This is a genuinely fresh perspective.
3. **Clear and Detailed Algorithm Description:** The algorithm is described in sufficient detail, allowing a reader to understand its key steps and computational complexity.  The explanation of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) process and the alignment procedure is particularly strong.
4. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Slavic languages are well-designed and demonstrate the method’s ability to recover plausible and linguistically meaningful correspondences. The phylogenetic tree reconstruction corroborates the approach's utility. The inclusion of both pairwise and four-way analysis provides a good breadth of evaluation.
5. **Discussion of Data Over-Weighting:** The authors demonstrate awareness of potential limitations (data sparsity) and proactively address them by exploring data over-weighting, providing a valuable tuning parameter.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** While the experiments are well-executed, they are limited to Slavic languages and a single type of data (Swadesh lists and a supplementary cognate list).  The generalizability of the approach to other language families and data types (e.g., parallel corpora, loanword data) remains unclear.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods for cognate identification and linguistic reconstruction.  While the introduction mentions these methods, the results section doesn’t compare directly. Without this, it’s hard to assess how much improvement is offered.
3. **Complexity & Scalability:** The authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks, especially with increasing numbers of languages. While the algorithm works on up to five languages, a more detailed discussion of scalability and potential optimizations (beyond partial order reduction) would be valuable.  The stated runtime on a single core desktop machine raises concerns about practicality for larger datasets.
4. **Presentation of Results:** The presentation of the learned correspondences in Table 3 is a little difficult to parse.  It would be beneficial to highlight the specific changes being modeled by each rule more clearly (e.g., using a more visual alignment format).  The significance of *which* rules are learned (and why) could be highlighted more effectively.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Baseline Comparison:** Could you provide a direct comparison to at least one established method for cognate identification or linguistic reconstruction (e.g., Levenshtein distance-based methods, Bayesian phylogenetic methods)? How does your method perform in terms of precision, recall, and F1-score?
2. **Scalability:** What is the theoretical computational complexity of your algorithm as a function of the number of languages (N) and the size of the data (D)?  Are there any planned optimizations to address the scalability issues?
3. **Generalizability:** Do you anticipate that the method would perform well on language families with drastically different typological characteristics than Slavic languages (e.g., isolating languages like Vietnamese or polysynthetic languages like Inuktitut)?  What modifications, if any, might be necessary?
4. **Data Weighting Justification:** What criteria did you use to select the over-weighting factor in your experiments? How sensitive are the results to the choice of this parameter?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured.
*   The references are relevant and up-to-date.
*   The mention of potential future work on incorporating non-deterministic elements is intriguing.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some weaknesses, I believe this paper represents a significant contribution to the field of computational linguistics. The MDL-based approach, deterministic algorithm, and ability to handle multiple languages are all compelling features. With some improvements in the areas of evaluation and scalability, this work has the potential to make a substantial impact.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
