This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea – to identify the most succinct set of correspondence rules directly from data, without imposing strong prior assumptions – is promising and addresses a recognized challenge in computational linguistics. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through experiments on Slavic languages, including phylogenetic analysis and detailed quantification of similarity.

**Contributions:**

1. **MDL-based Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is the formulation of a language correspondence discovery problem within the MDL framework. This provides a statistically sound basis for identifying meaningful relationships between languages, moving beyond methods that rely on arbitrary assumptions.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm for Rule Inference:** The development of an efficient, deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules is a significant practical contribution. The algorithm’s reliance on Expectation-Maximization (EM) and partial order reduction for graph search demonstrate a thoughtful approach to computational efficiency.
3. **Multi-Language Analysis & Quantification:**  The ability to analyze correspondences across more than two languages simultaneously, and to quantify similarity between subsets of languages, provides a richer and more nuanced understanding of linguistic relationships than traditional pairwise comparisons. The shared Description Length (sDL) metric is a novel and insightful way to assess the degree of shared structure between languages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The application of MDL to the problem of language correspondence is a genuinely novel approach.  Existing statistical methods often make assumptions about the distribution of correspondences, which this work avoids.
2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation:**  The grounding in MDL provides a solid theoretical basis for the method, offering a principled way to balance model complexity and data fit. The clear explanation of the two-part MDL code is well-presented.
3. **Clear Algorithm Description:** The algorithm is described in sufficient detail, including the alignment procedure based on shortest-path search and the EM training process.  The discussion of computational complexity is also valuable.
4. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Slavic languages provide compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness.  The agreement between the inferred phylogenetic tree and known linguistic classifications is encouraging.
5. **Informative Results and Analysis:** The examples of learned correspondences (Table 3) are illustrative, and the discussion of the impact of data over-weighting (Section 5.3) is insightful.  The sDL metric offers a quantifiable measure of similarity that is not readily available from other methods.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Dependency & Generalizability:** The experiments are limited to Slavic languages, raising questions about the method’s generalizability to other language families with different structural characteristics. The choice of Swadesh lists, while standard, represents a specific data type and may not capture the full complexity of linguistic relationships.
2. **Computational Scalability:** While the algorithm is described as efficient, the authors acknowledge limitations in processing higher numbers of languages (Section 4.3).  The bottleneck appears to be the graph search for optimal alignments. This could be a significant constraint for larger-scale analyses.
3. **Limited Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper would be strengthened by a more direct comparison to existing approaches for language correspondence discovery, such as those based on cognate identification and phylogenetic reconstruction.  A quantitative comparison of performance metrics would be particularly valuable.
4. **Over-Weighting Subjectivity:**  The introduction of data over-weighting (Section 4.5) adds a degree of subjectivity to the process. While the authors acknowledge this, a more thorough discussion of the potential biases introduced by this technique would be beneficial.  Clear guidelines for selecting the appropriate over-weighting factor would also be helpful.
5. **Presentation of Results:** Some figures (e.g., Figure 2 & 3) are difficult to interpret without more context. Explicitly labeling the different components contributing to the sDL, or highlighting the most significant rules, would improve clarity.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does the performance of the algorithm scale with the number of languages? Can you provide more detailed performance benchmarks for larger datasets?
2. Have you investigated the use of alternative graph search algorithms (e.g., A*) to improve the efficiency of the alignment procedure?
3. What strategies could be employed to mitigate the subjectivity introduced by data over-weighting?  Could a more automated approach be developed to determine appropriate weighting factors?
4. How sensitive is the algorithm to errors or noise in the input data? Have you explored techniques for data cleaning or error correction?
5. Could you elaborate on the potential applications of the different levels of granularity in the learned correspondences (as highlighted in Table 3)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to "wiktionary" (Footnote 2) could be expanded to include a specific date of access, as content on wikis is dynamic.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of using orthographic forms as input, and potential ways to address this (e.g., phonetic transcription).
*   The phrase "linguistic classification" is used frequently. Clarifying what specifically is meant by this—specifically, which classification scheme the authors are referencing—would be useful.



Overall, this is a well-written and thoughtfully researched paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics. While some areas could be strengthened through further experimentation and comparison to existing methods, the core ideas are innovative and the results are promising. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
