This paper proposes a Minimum Description Length (MDL) approach to discover correspondences between multiple languages, aiming to overcome limitations of existing pairwise methods that rely on assumptions about correspondence distributions. The authors present an efficient, deterministic algorithm and demonstrate its efficacy through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages, claiming to quantify string-level similarities and identify linguistic subgroups.

The primary contribution, as *I* see it, is a formalized, deterministic application of MDL to the problem of linguistic correspondence discovery, specifically extending beyond pairwise comparisons. While the underlying principle of MDL isn’t novel, its application to this domain, coupled with the algorithm designed to implement it, is the core strength. A secondary contribution lies in the detailed experimental analysis on Slavic languages, providing a concrete demonstration of the approach and generated data for comparison.

**Strengths:**

1. **Deterministic Approach:** The authors’ commitment to a deterministic algorithm is commendable. This removes the element of chance inherent in many statistical methods, lending increased confidence to the discovered correspondences and reproducibility of results. This is crucial for linguistic analysis where interpretability is paramount.
2. **Beyond Pairwise Comparisons:**  The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously is a significant advantage. Existing methods often require cumbersome pairwise comparisons followed by attempts to synthesize the results.  This work offers a more direct approach to understanding relationships across multiple languages. The analysis presented in Section 5.3 demonstrates this potential with the quantification of similarities between language subsets.
3. **Formalized MDL Application:** The paper meticulously details the MDL code, including the model code L(M) and the data code L(D|M). This detailed description enables scrutiny and potential adaptation of the method by other researchers. The breakdown into encoding individual rules and alphabets is a clear and logical structuring of the problem.
4. **Insightful Data Analysis:** The pairwise NCD analysis (Section 5.2) and resulting phylogenetic tree align well with established linguistic classifications. This confirms the method's ability to capture meaningful linguistic relationships, and the observation regarding variations in language representation (Latin vs. Cyrillic Serbo-Croatian) shows sensitivity to orthographic differences.
5. **Discussion of Data Over-Weighting:** The introduction of data over-weighting as a mechanism to incorporate linguistic expertise and control the granularity of the results is a thoughtful addition. Recognizing the potential limitations of purely data-driven approaches and offering a way to guide the discovery process is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods:**  The paper asserts existing statistical approaches are “afflicted by a number of problems” but provides limited direct empirical comparison. While acknowledging Wettig et al. (2011) which also uses MDL, a more comprehensive benchmark against state-of-the-art cognate identification or phylogenetic reconstruction techniques would strengthen the claims of novelty and superiority. Simply stating the shortcomings of other methods isn't enough; demonstrating the improvement is necessary.
2. **Computational Scalability Concerns:** The authors acknowledge scalability limitations (processing up to five languages on a modest machine). While the algorithm is efficient *in principle*, its practical applicability to larger language families or more extensive datasets is questionable. This needs to be more thoroughly addressed, perhaps with a discussion of potential optimizations or parallelization strategies.
3. **Overly Detailed Technical Presentation:** While the thoroughness of the MDL code description is a strength, it also contributes to the paper’s density. The lengthy mathematical formulations in Sections 3 and 4 might obscure the core ideas for readers less familiar with information theory. A more intuitive explanation alongside the formalisms would be beneficial.  Furthermore, the frequent references to bit lengths and coding theory feel tangential to the primary linguistic goal.
4. **Limited Data Set Diversity:** The use of Swadesh lists, while common, represents a relatively restricted data source. Swadesh lists are known to be susceptible to borrowing and semantic shift, which could introduce noise into the analysis. Expanding the dataset with more diverse text types could improve the robustness of the results.
5. **Insufficient Discussion of Rule Interpretation:** While examples of learned correspondences are provided, the paper lacks a deeper discussion of their linguistic significance.  For instance, the example alignments in Table 3 could benefit from annotation explaining *why* certain rules were favored, and what linguistic processes they might reflect.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a quantitative comparison of your approach to at least one established cognate identification or phylogenetic reconstruction method on the same datasets? Specifically, what is the accuracy, precision, recall, or F1-score of your method compared to existing techniques?
2.  What are the key bottlenecks in the algorithm's computational complexity? What strategies could be employed to improve scalability to handle larger language families and more extensive datasets (e.g., parallelization, approximation algorithms)?
3.  How sensitive is the algorithm to the quality of the input data? Would incorporating phonetic transcriptions or other forms of linguistic annotation improve the accuracy and robustness of the discovered correspondences?
4.  The data over-weighting method seems promising, but introduces subjectivity. What criteria would you recommend for selecting an appropriate weighting factor? Can you provide a more principled approach to setting α in the L(M) + αL(D|M) objective?
5.  Can you elaborate on the linguistic interpretation of some of the discovered rules?  Specifically, what do the observed correspondences suggest about the historical relationships and sound changes between the analyzed languages?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper's formatting is generally good, but there are minor inconsistencies in the use of boldface and italics.
*   The references are comprehensive, but would benefit from clearer formatting (e.g., consistent use of et al.).
*   The statement in the conclusion that the analysis is "purely on the basis of superficial word form similarity" seems dismissive of the insightful results presented. While the method operates on surface forms, it demonstrably captures underlying linguistic relationships.



Overall, this paper presents a novel and potentially valuable approach to linguistic correspondence discovery. However, the lack of comparison to existing methods, concerns about scalability, and the overly technical presentation detract from its overall impact. While the method shows promise, further refinement and validation are needed before it can be considered a significant contribution to the field. I lean towards **rejection** due to the substantial number of weaknesses, despite the inherent novelty of the approach.
