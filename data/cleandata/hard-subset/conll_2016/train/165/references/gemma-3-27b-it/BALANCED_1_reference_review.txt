This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea – treating correspondences as simply associated strings without imposing prior assumptions – is compelling and offers a promising alternative to existing statistical methods. The authors demonstrate the method's efficacy through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages, showcasing its ability to quantify similarities among multiple languages and confirm linguistic classifications.

**Contributions:**

1. **MDL-based Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is the formulation of a statistically sound framework for identifying linguistic correspondences across multiple languages, leveraging the MDL principle to avoid arbitrary assumptions about the shape or distribution of correspondences. This provides a potentially more objective approach than methods relying on predefined models.
2. **Efficient Deterministic Algorithm:** The authors present an efficient, deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) to infer correspondence rules directly from data. This is a significant practical contribution, as it enables the analysis of multiple languages without the computational limitations of many existing approaches. The algorithm's deterministic nature is also a benefit, lending credibility to the discovered correspondences.
3. **Empirical Validation & Detailed Analysis:** The experiments on Slavic languages provide a strong empirical validation of the proposed method.  The analysis goes beyond simple pairwise comparisons to quantify similarities between all subsets of languages, revealing subtle linguistic relationships and providing a richer understanding of language relatedness.  The use of shared Description Lengths is particularly insightful.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The application of MDL to the problem of multi-language correspondence discovery is a novel and interesting idea. It offers a statistically rigorous way to approach the problem without the biases inherent in other methods.
2. **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The paper is grounded in a solid theoretical foundation – the MDL principle – and provides a clear explanation of how it's applied to the problem. The connection to coding theory and information theory is well-articulated.
3. **Algorithm Efficiency:** The authors demonstrate the feasibility of their approach by presenting an efficient algorithm capable of handling multiple languages. The stated runtime on a standard desktop machine is encouraging.
4. **Detailed Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The phylogenetic tree generated from the NCD values aligns well with established linguistic classifications. The four-way analysis and the sDL calculations provide valuable insights into the relationships between the languages.
5. **Clarity of Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with the specific domain. The terminology is clearly defined, and the explanations of the algorithm and the MDL principle are accessible.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Limitations & Representativeness:** The experiments are limited to Slavic languages and rely on Swadesh lists, which may not be fully representative of the complexities of natural language. The generalizability of the method to other language families and different types of linguistic data is unclear. While acknowledged, the limited data size could constrain the discovery of subtle but important patterns.
2. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison with existing state-of-the-art methods for cognate identification and language relatedness. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of existing methods, a direct comparison would provide a stronger justification for the proposed approach.  For example, how does it perform against methods utilizing probabilistic models or machine learning?
3. **Impact of Data Over-Weighting:** While the introduction of data over-weighting provides a useful mechanism for incorporating linguistic knowledge, it also introduces a degree of subjectivity. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the potential impact of this parameter on the results and a sensitivity analysis to assess its robustness. The justification for choosing specific over-weighting values seems somewhat ad-hoc.
4. **Computational Complexity Bottlenecks:** While the authors state the algorithm is efficient, the mention of bottlenecks in memory consumption and runtime during alignment suggests there could be limitations to scalability with increasing numbers of languages or larger datasets. A more detailed analysis of the computational complexity would be helpful.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on positive results. A more thorough discussion of cases where the algorithm fails to identify expected correspondences, or produces incorrect ones, would strengthen the analysis and provide insights into potential areas for improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the algorithm, particularly with respect to the number of languages and the size of the dataset?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the over-weighting parameter? Did you explore different values and assess their impact on the discovered correspondences and the resulting phylogeny?
3.  What are the limitations of using Swadesh lists as the primary data source? How might the results differ if you were to use a more comprehensive corpus of text?
4.  Have you considered incorporating phonetic information into the correspondence rules? Would this improve the accuracy and granularity of the discovered patterns?
5.  Could you elaborate on the observed differences between the Cyrillic and Latin script Serbo-Croatian analyses and what implications this might have for cross-script linguistic analysis?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The link to the etetoolkit website for generating the phylogenetic tree is helpful.
*   The detailed description of the MDL code and the encoding scheme is commendable.
*   The explanation of the Expectation-Maximization step is clear and concise.
*   Consider including a small table illustrating the different types of correspondence rules discovered (e.g., phonological, orthographic, morphological).



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and promising approach to multi-language correspondence discovery. While there are some weaknesses, the strengths of the paper – its theoretical foundation, algorithmic efficiency, and detailed empirical evaluation – outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly a more thorough comparison to existing methods and a deeper discussion of the impact of data over-weighting and computational complexity.
