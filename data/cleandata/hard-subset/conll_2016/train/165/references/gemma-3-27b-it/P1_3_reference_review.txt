## Peer Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors argue that existing statistical methods often impose arbitrary assumptions, and propose a deterministic algorithm that infers correspondence rules directly from data without prior assumptions about their shape or distribution. They demonstrate their approach on Slavic languages, showing its ability to quantify linguistic similarities and identify language groupings.

**Main Contributions (as perceived by this reviewer):**

1. **A principled, assumption-free framework:** The application of MDL to the problem of linguistic correspondence is a solid contribution.  The explicit rejection of prior assumptions and focus on succinct data description is a valuable shift in perspective within this field.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The development of a deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules is a noteworthy accomplishment. Deterministic algorithms provide reproducibility and facilitate interpretability, crucial aspects for linguistic analysis.
3. **Multi-lingual extension:** Extending the analysis beyond pairwise comparisons to multiple languages simultaneously is a significant step forward. This allows for a more nuanced and comprehensive understanding of linguistic relationships.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The use of MDL provides a strong statistical justification for the approach.  The authors clearly articulate the theoretical underpinnings and connect it to existing work in coding theory and information theory. The explanation of the two-part MDL is accessible.
2. **Clear Methodology:** The paper provides a detailed explanation of the algorithm, including the Expectation-Maximization (EM) steps and the encoding scheme.  The discussion of alignment procedures and computational complexity is helpful.
3. **Compelling Experimental Results:** The experiments on Slavic languages demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach. The NCD-based phylogenetic tree aligns well with established linguistic classifications. The examples of learned correspondences are insightful and illustrate the range of patterns discovered.
4. **Novel Quantification of Similarity:** The introduction of “shared Description Lengths” (sDL) is a clever way to quantify the similarities between language subsets, providing a more granular understanding of linguistic relationships than simple pairwise comparisons.
5. **Addressing Overfitting:** The discussion of data over-weighting as a mechanism to address data scarcity or desired granularity is insightful. The acknowledgement of the trade-off between objectivity and linguistic expertise adds to the credibility of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:** While the authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks, the stated ability to process "up to five languages" is limiting. The performance on larger datasets and more complex languages is a significant concern.  A more detailed discussion of scalability limitations and potential optimization strategies is needed.
2. **Data Dependency & Representativeness:** The results are heavily reliant on the quality and representativeness of the Swadesh lists. The limitations of Swadesh lists as a sole source of linguistic data should be acknowledged more explicitly.  The choice of cognates influences the discovered correspondences.
3. **Limited Evaluation Beyond Phylogeny:** While the phylogenetic tree is a useful visualization, the evaluation is somewhat limited.  More robust quantitative evaluation metrics (beyond NCD) and comparisons to other established methods would strengthen the claims. A comparison with existing cognate identification methods (e.g., those based on edit distance or probabilistic models) would be highly beneficial.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** The impact of individual components of the MDL framework (e.g., the choice of the universal code for integers, the Laplace correction) on the overall performance is not thoroughly investigated. An ablation study would help determine the contribution of each component.
5. **Presentation could be more concise:** The paper is somewhat verbose. Streamlining the presentation, particularly in Section 4, would improve readability. The frequent re-iteration of the algorithm's goals becomes repetitive.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Scalability:** What are the main computational bottlenecks limiting scalability to more languages and larger datasets? What optimization strategies are being considered or implemented to address these limitations?
2. **Data Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of cognates? Have you explored the impact of using different datasets or different subsets of cognates on the learned correspondences and the resulting phylogenetic tree?
3. **Comparison to Existing Methods:** How does your approach compare to established methods for cognate identification and linguistic reconstruction (e.g., Levenshtein distance, Bayesian phylogenetic methods) in terms of accuracy, computational cost, and interpretability?  Please provide quantitative comparisons where possible.
4. **Impact of Over-Weighting:** How does the degree of data over-weighting affect the resulting correspondences and the overall interpretation? Is there a principled way to determine an appropriate level of over-weighting based on the data?
5. **Alternative Alignment Metrics:**  Did you explore other alignment metrics besides the one described in Section 2, and if so, why was this one chosen?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation could be improved for clarity.
*   Consider providing a publicly available implementation of the algorithm to encourage reproducibility and further research.
*   The phrase "objective results" is used several times. While the MDL approach is statistically principled, it's important to acknowledge that any analysis is influenced by data choices and model parameters.



Overall, this is a promising and well-motivated work with a solid theoretical foundation and compelling experimental results. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance, pending satisfactory responses to the questions and minor revisions to improve clarity and conciseness.
