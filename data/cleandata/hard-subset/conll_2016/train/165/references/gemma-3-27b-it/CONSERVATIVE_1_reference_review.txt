This paper proposes a Minimum Description Length (MDL) approach for discovering correspondences between multiple languages, aiming to overcome limitations of existing statistical methods that rely on assumptions about correspondence shapes or are limited to pairwise comparisons. The core contribution is an efficient, deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules directly from data without *a priori* assumptions. The authors demonstrate this on Slavic languages, performing both pairwise phylogenetic analysis and a four-language experiment.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Comparative Baseline:** The most significant weakness is the almost complete absence of comparison to established methods. The introduction mentions several existing statistical approaches (Schulz et al., 2004; Snyder et al., 2010; Wettig et al., 2011; Snyder and Barzilay, 2008), yet the paper lacks any empirical evaluation *against* them. Without demonstrating superiority (or even competitive performance) to existing techniques, the claim of overcoming their limitations remains unsubstantiated.  Simply stating the problems with existing methods isn't sufficient; demonstrating that the proposed MDL approach *solves* those problems is crucial. This is particularly problematic given the claim of avoiding arbitrary assumptions; demonstrating that this avoidance actually leads to better results is essential.
2. **Overly Detailed Implementation, Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The paper dedicates a large portion to detailing the specific MDL code and alignment procedure. While methodological rigor is appreciated, the experimental evaluation feels comparatively limited. Focusing on a single language family (Slavic) restricts the generalizability claims. Furthermore, the relatively small datasets used (even acknowledging the challenges of obtaining aligned cognates) may not sufficiently stress-test the algorithm's scalability and robustness.
3. **Subjectivity in Data Weighting:** The introduction of data over-weighting (Section 4.5) re-introduces a degree of subjectivity that undermines the stated goal of an objective, unbiased approach.  While the authors acknowledge this, the potential for tuning this parameter to achieve desired results raises concerns about reproducibility and the validity of the "objective" findings. The justification for this addition - dealing with insufficient statistical evidence - feels like an admission of limitations rather than a strength.
4. **Limited Discussion of Linguistic Significance:** While the paper presents some example correspondences, the linguistic interpretation of these findings is superficial. Beyond stating that the algorithm identifies expected groupings, there's little discussion of *why* these correspondences are linguistically interesting or how they contribute to our understanding of Slavic language evolution. The discussion of vowel correspondences, for instance, lacks depth and connection to established linguistic theory.
5. **Computational Complexity Concerns:** The authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks. While they state they can process up to five languages, this limitation is significant, especially considering the stated goal of analyzing more than two languages.  The lack of a detailed discussion of computational complexity and potential optimizations is a weakness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of MDL:**  Applying the MDL principle to the problem of linguistic correspondence discovery *is* a novel idea. MDL offers a theoretically sound framework for model selection, and its use in this context is a worthwhile exploration.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The deterministic nature of the algorithm is a positive feature, promoting reproducibility and interpretability. This contrasts with many statistical approaches that rely on randomization and sampling.
3. **Ability to Handle Multiple Languages:** The algorithm’s capability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously is a genuine advantage over many existing methods. This allows for a more holistic and nuanced understanding of linguistic relationships.
4. **Clear Methodological Presentation:** Despite the complexity, the paper provides a relatively clear and detailed description of the MDL code and alignment procedure. This transparency is essential for enabling others to understand and potentially build upon the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a detailed comparison of your method's performance against at least one well-established baseline method (e.g., a cognate identification algorithm, a phylogenetic reconstruction method).  Provide quantitative results and a discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the data over-weighting parameter? Please provide a quantitative analysis of how different values of α affect the discovered correspondences.
3.  What is the computational complexity of the algorithm, and what specific optimizations have been implemented to address the identified bottlenecks?
4.  Can you provide a more in-depth linguistic analysis of the discovered correspondences, relating them to established linguistic theories and historical language changes? 
5.  How does the choice of cognate lists (e.g. Swadesh lists) influence the discovered correspondence rules?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 (NCD-based tree) needs clearer labeling and potentially a different visualization style to enhance readability.
*   The discussion of the "shared Description Lengths" (Section 5.3.1) feels somewhat disconnected from the rest of the paper. A more explicit explanation of its linguistic significance would be helpful.
*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of potential error sources and limitations of the data (e.g., variations in orthography, semantic shifts).
