## Peer Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering systematic correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors argue that existing statistical methods often rely on restrictive assumptions and are limited to pairwise language comparisons. Their method aims to identify correspondences without *a priori* assumptions about their shape or distribution, and is designed to handle more than two languages simultaneously.  The core contribution is an efficient, deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) to infer correspondence rules directly from data, demonstrated through analyses of Slavic languages.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **MDL-based Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence:** The application of the MDL principle to the problem of discovering multi-language correspondences is a valuable contribution. It provides a theoretically sound and potentially unbiased framework for identifying patterns in linguistic data without strong prior assumptions.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm for Correspondence Inference:** The proposed EM-based algorithm offers a practical solution for implementing the MDL framework. The determinism of the algorithm is a strength, as it eliminates randomness and provides reproducible results.
3. **Demonstration on Slavic Languages:** The application of the method to a dataset of Slavic languages provides a concrete demonstration of its effectiveness and allows for comparison with established linguistic classifications. The exploration of data weighting offers a useful avenue for controlling the granularity of discovered correspondences.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The use of MDL, coupled with a deterministic algorithm, distinguishes this work from many existing statistical approaches that rely on probabilistic models or similarity metrics.  The focus on *unbiased* discovery is a compelling aspect of the work.
2. **Scalability to Multiple Languages:**  A key strength is the explicit design to handle more than two languages simultaneously.  This is a significant advantage over methods limited to pairwise comparison, enabling a more holistic analysis of linguistic relationships.
3. **Clear Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear and well-motivated explanation of the MDL principle and how it’s applied to the problem of correspondence discovery. The mathematical formulation is rigorous and easy to follow.
4. **Practical Demonstration & Results:** The experimental results on Slavic languages, including the phylogenetic tree and example correspondences, are convincing and demonstrate the potential of the approach. The analysis of the impact of data weighting provides a useful control over the level of detail in the discovered correspondences.
5. **Emphasis on Determinism:** The determinism of the algorithm contributes to reproducibility and trustworthiness of the results.  This is particularly important in a field where subtle variations in method can lead to different conclusions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Evaluation beyond Slavic Languages:** The evaluation is currently limited to Slavic languages. While this provides a good initial demonstration, the generalizability of the approach to other language families with different typological characteristics needs to be investigated.  The authors acknowledge this as future work, but it’s a significant limitation in the current submission.
2. **Complexity of Implementation & Scalability Concerns:**  The authors mention computational bottlenecks and limitations in processing higher numbers of languages. While they are working on improvements, the current implementation's scalability remains a concern, especially for very large datasets or a larger number of languages. More details about computational cost are needed.
3. **Reliance on Cognate Lists:** The method currently requires pre-identified cognate lists as input.  The quality and completeness of these lists significantly impact the results.  Addressing the challenges of automatic cognate identification, even as a future direction, would strengthen the work.
4. **Subjectivity in Data Weighting:** While the data weighting technique offers control over granularity, it introduces a degree of subjectivity. The authors acknowledge this, but the lack of clear guidelines for choosing appropriate weighting factors could lead to biased results.
5. **Limited Comparison to Existing Methods:** While the paper critiques existing methods, a more direct quantitative comparison to state-of-the-art approaches on the same dataset (e.g., cognate reconstruction methods) would better establish the advantages of the proposed approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Scalability:** Can you provide more detailed information on the computational complexity of the algorithm and the bottlenecks encountered when scaling to a larger number of languages?  What specific optimizations are planned to address these limitations?
2. **Data Weighting:** What criteria can be used to guide the selection of appropriate data weighting factors?  Can you propose a more objective or automated method for determining these weights?
3. **Cognate Identification:**  Do you foresee incorporating automatic cognate identification into the framework? If so, what challenges would need to be addressed?
4. **Comparison to Existing Methods:**  Could you provide a quantitative comparison of your results with those obtained using established cognate reconstruction methods on the same dataset?
5. **Rule Interpretation:**  Are there any linguistic insights gleaned from the discovered rules that go beyond what is already known about the relationships between these languages? Specifically, what novel patterns or correspondences were identified?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured.
*  The inclusion of the phylogenetic tree is helpful for visualizing the relationships between the languages.
*  The discussion of the limitations of the approach is appreciated.
* The use of the term “objective” throughout the paper should be clarified. While the approach minimizes assumptions, the choice of data and even the implementation details inevitably introduce some degree of subjectivity.



Overall, this is a strong submission with a novel and promising approach to a challenging problem. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
