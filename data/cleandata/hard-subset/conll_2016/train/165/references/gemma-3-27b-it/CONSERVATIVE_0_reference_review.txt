This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between multiple languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core contribution is an algorithm that deterministically infers correspondence rules directly from data, without imposing prior assumptions about their shape or distribution. The authors demonstrate the approach on Slavic languages, showing its ability to quantify string-level similarities and identify linguistic subgroups. A secondary, though potentially valuable contribution, is the extension of MDL-based analysis to more than two languages simultaneously, allowing for more detailed quantification of similarity.

**Strengths:**

1. **Methodological Rigor (to a degree):** The authors attempt a principled approach to a difficult problem. The use of MDL provides a theoretically sound framework for model selection and avoids some of the arbitrariness inherent in other statistical methods. The deterministic nature of the algorithm is a positive attribute, promoting reproducibility and interpretability.
2. **Extension to Multiple Languages:**  Existing work often focuses on pairwise language comparisons.  The ability to analyze more than two languages concurrently is a genuine advance, potentially unlocking insights that are inaccessible with pairwise methods. The demonstration of this capability with the four-language experiment is a notable strength.
3. **Clear Presentation of the MDL Framework:** The paper provides a reasonably clear explanation of the MDL principle and how it's applied to the problem of correspondence discovery. The authors make a concerted effort to detail the encoding scheme and optimization process.
4. **Demonstrated Functionality:** The experiments on Slavic languages produce results that are, at least, plausible. The phylogenetic tree generated from the NCD values aligns with known linguistic classifications, lending some credibility to the approach. The example correspondences, while limited in scope, appear reasonable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** This is the most significant weakness. The paper *mentions* existing statistical approaches but provides absolutely no empirical comparison against them. It's impossible to assess the value of this new method without knowing how it performs relative to established techniques (e.g., cognate identification based on edit distance, probabilistic models of sound change).  The claim of avoiding arbitrary assumptions is weakened without demonstrating superior performance to methods that *do* make those assumptions.  Simply stating it avoids assumptions doesn't make it better.
2. **Data Set Limitations and Representativeness:** The use of Swadesh lists, while common, is a simplification. Swadesh lists are known to be prone to borrowing and may not accurately reflect deeper linguistic relationships.  The limited size of the data sets (reported in Table 1) raises concerns about the statistical power of the analysis.  Moreover, the focus solely on cognates makes the method heavily reliant on pre-existing lexical knowledge, which seems to contradict the goal of discovering correspondences *de novo*.
3. **Complexity and Scalability:** The authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks and the limited number of languages (up to five) that can be processed. While they suggest future work to address scalability, this limitation currently restricts the practical applicability of the method. The reliance on Dijkstra's algorithm, while conceptually clear, raises concerns about efficiency for larger datasets.
4. **Over-Weighting as a Subjective Parameter:** The introduction of data over-weighting, while potentially useful for controlling the granularity of the results, reintroduces a subjective element into the supposedly objective MDL framework. The authors acknowledge this, but the lack of a clear criterion for selecting the appropriate weighting factor is concerning. It feels like a "knob" to tune to get the desired results rather than a principled method.
5. **Limited Analysis of Results:** While the authors present a phylogenetic tree and some example correspondences, the analysis of the results is relatively superficial. A more detailed examination of the learned rules, including an assessment of their linguistic plausibility and a comparison to known sound changes, would strengthen the paper.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a comprehensive comparison of your method to at least three established baselines for discovering linguistic correspondences (e.g., Levenshtein distance-based cognate identification, Bayesian phylogenetic methods). Include quantitative results (e.g., precision, recall, F1-score) and a discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of data set? Have you experimented with different data sources (e.g., parallel corpora, historical texts) to assess the robustness of the method?
3.  What strategies are being considered to address the scalability limitations of the algorithm? Can you provide a more detailed estimate of the computational complexity and expected performance for larger datasets?
4.  What objective criteria can be used to select the appropriate data weighting factor?  Can you provide a rationale for the values used in your experiments?
5.  Can you provide a more detailed linguistic analysis of the discovered correspondences? Specifically, how do the learned rules relate to known sound changes and historical linguistic processes in the Slavic language family?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper feels somewhat preliminary. While the ideas are interesting and the MDL framework is well-defined, the lack of comparison to existing methods and the limitations in scalability and data analysis significantly weaken the contribution. Addressing these issues would substantially improve the paper.
