This paper proposes a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The primary contribution is the application of MDL to a multi-lingual setting *without* imposing prior assumptions on the shape or distribution of correspondences, a significant departure from much of the existing work. A secondary contribution is the efficient, deterministic algorithm developed for inferring these correspondence rules. Finally, the application to Slavic languages and the demonstration of the approach’s ability to quantify linguistic similarity, including identifying language subgroups, represents a valuable demonstration.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Conceptual Leap:** The core idea of applying MDL to multi-lingual correspondence discovery *without* pre-defined distributions is genuinely novel. Existing approaches often rely on assumptions that can bias results. This work’s commitment to data-driven discovery is refreshing and potentially transformative. This is a bold move and I greatly appreciate it.
2. **Deterministic Approach:** The development of a deterministic algorithm is a significant strength. This provides transparency and reproducibility, crucial for linguistic analysis where interpretability is paramount. While stochastic methods are common, a deterministic approach allows for rigorous examination of the discovered rules.
3. **Multi-lingual Capability:** The ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a substantial advancement. Pairwise comparisons are limiting, and this approach offers a more holistic view of linguistic relationships. The demonstration on four Slavic languages convincingly illustrates this capability.
4. **Information-Theoretic Quantification:**  Presenting linguistic similarity through an information-theoretic lens (NCD and shared description lengths) is a strong point.  It moves beyond qualitative assessments and offers a quantifiable measure of relationships, opening doors to further computational analysis.
5. **Illustrative Experiments & Detailed Analysis:** The experimental results, while not exhaustive, are compelling. The phylogenetic tree aligns well with established linguistic classifications. The detailed breakdown of shared description lengths provides granular insight into the relationships between languages and is fascinating.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:** The authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks and limited scalability (currently up to five languages). While understandable for initial work, this is a significant practical limitation. The computational complexity noted in the paper (O(|D| · |R|2) for the E-step) suggests scaling will be a major challenge.
2. **Data Dependency & Limited Dataset:** The experiments rely on Swadesh lists, a standard but inherently limited dataset. Swadesh lists represent a narrow slice of language and may not generalize well to other linguistic phenomena. The limited size of the dataset (even after filtering for complete data) could impact the robustness of the discovered rules.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods (Beyond NCD):** While the NCD comparison is useful, there's a lack of direct comparison to other state-of-the-art methods for cognate identification or alignment. How does this approach perform *specifically* against techniques like Edit Distance with linguistic constraints, or Bayesian phylogenetic methods for language comparison? This is not critical, as the novelty of the approach is high, but would strengthen the evaluation.
4. **Over-weighting as a Subjective Parameter:** While the introduction of data over-weighting is interesting, it introduces a subjective parameter (α) that moves away from the purely objective nature of MDL. The authors rightly acknowledge this, but it still weakens the argument for a completely unbiased approach.
5. **Limited Discussion of Linguistic Implications:** While the paper demonstrates the *ability* to discover correspondences, there’s a relatively limited discussion of what these specific correspondences *mean* linguistically. A deeper analysis connecting the discovered rules to known sound changes or historical relationships would significantly enhance the paper.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What specific optimizations are planned to address the scalability limitations of the algorithm?  Can you provide a more concrete estimate of the maximum number of languages that could be processed with anticipated improvements?
2. Beyond Swadesh lists, what types of data (e.g., parallel corpora, historical texts) would be most suitable for this approach, and how would the algorithm need to be adapted to handle such data?
3.  You mention the potential for simulated annealing to introduce non-determinism. How would this affect the interpretability of the discovered rules, and what benefits would it offer over the deterministic approach?
4. Regarding the over-weighting parameter α, what strategies would you recommend for selecting an appropriate value, given the trade-off between objectivity and desired level of detail? Is there a principled way to determine α based on linguistic expertise or data characteristics?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper's presentation is generally clear, but some sections (particularly Section 4) could benefit from more concise explanations and clearer visual aids. The use of mathematical notation is appropriate but occasionally dense. A glossary of terms would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with MDL or information theory.




Overall, this is a promising and exciting paper that presents a novel approach to a challenging problem. The novelty of the idea and the demonstrated results outweigh the identified weaknesses. While scalability and data limitations are concerns, the potential impact of this work is significant. **I recommend acceptance, with the caveat that the authors address the reviewer’s concerns regarding scalability and comparison to existing methods in a future iteration.** The boldness of the approach and the potential to reshape how we analyze linguistic relationships are strong arguments in its favor.
