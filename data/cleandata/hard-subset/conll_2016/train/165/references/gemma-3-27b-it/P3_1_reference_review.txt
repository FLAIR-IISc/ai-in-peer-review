## Review of “Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL”

**Summary:** This paper presents a novel approach to discovering systematic correspondences between languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors propose a deterministic algorithm to infer these correspondences directly from data, avoiding assumptions about the underlying distribution of changes. They demonstrate the method on Slavic languages, showing its ability to quantify string-level similarities and identify linguistic subgroups.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **MDL Application to Multi-Language Correspondence:** Applying the MDL principle to discover correspondences across *multiple* languages (beyond pairwise comparison) is a significant contribution. Existing methods often struggle with scaling beyond two languages due to combinatorial complexity. This work offers a framework for tackling that problem.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The development of a deterministic algorithm for correspondence inference is valuable. This provides transparency and reproducibility, a contrast to many statistical approaches that rely on stochastic optimization. 
3. **Detailed Quantification of Similarity:** The ability to quantify similarity between *subsets* of languages (e.g., comparing Czech-Polish vs. Russian-Bulgarian) offers a granularity of linguistic analysis not easily achieved with traditional methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The combination of MDL with a deterministic algorithm for multi-language correspondence discovery is a genuinely novel contribution. While MDL has been used in related areas (reconstruction, change modeling), its application here fills a gap in the literature.
2. **Rigorous Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear and mathematically sound description of the MDL framework. The two-part MDL formulation is well-explained, and the connection to coding theory is clearly articulated. The explanation of the alignment procedure and the EM-like optimization is convincing.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Slavic languages provide compelling evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The alignment of cognates is plausible, and the constructed phylogenetic tree aligns well with established linguistic classifications. The NCD-based analysis is a good choice for evaluating the quality of the discovered correspondences.
4. **Demonstration of Granularity in Analysis:** The four-way analysis and the concept of ‘shared description length’ (sDL) are particularly strong points. The ability to quantify similarities between different language subsets highlights the potential of this approach for deeper linguistic insights. The analysis of how different language groups share complexity is a very interesting and practically relevant result.
5. **Well-Written and Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured.  The technical details are presented in a clear and accessible manner, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Limitations & Representativeness:** The reliance on Swadesh lists, while a common starting point, is a limitation. Swadesh lists are notoriously sensitive to semantic shift and borrowing, and may not be representative of the full range of linguistic changes. The reliance on cognates *already known* to exist biases the analysis. A more robust evaluation would involve testing the method on a more diverse corpus, including texts with varying levels of cognate density.
2. **Scalability Concerns:** While the authors mention being able to process up to five languages, the computational complexity (O(|D| · |R|2)) is a potential bottleneck.  A detailed analysis of the scaling behavior and exploration of optimization techniques (beyond partial order reduction) is needed to demonstrate the method's applicability to larger language families. The claim of a few hours on a relatively modest machine for 5 languages doesn't fully address this.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Baseline Methods:** The paper lacks a direct comparison to existing statistical methods for correspondence discovery, particularly those that don’t impose strong distributional assumptions. Demonstrating superior performance (in terms of accuracy, completeness, or efficiency) compared to state-of-the-art methods would strengthen the claims of novelty and effectiveness.  Simply stating that existing methods have limitations isn't sufficient.
4. **Subjectivity in Data Over-Weighting:** While the data over-weighting technique is a useful addition, it introduces a degree of subjectivity.  The justification for choosing a specific weighting factor isn't fully explained, and the potential impact on the objectivity of the results needs to be carefully considered. The authors correctly acknowledge this, but further discussion is warranted.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** A more in-depth error analysis would be valuable. Identifying the types of errors the algorithm makes (e.g., false positives, false negatives) and exploring the reasons behind those errors could provide insights for improving the method.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of initial conditions for the EM algorithm (e.g., the initial null alignment)? Have you experimented with different initialization strategies?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and memory usage of the algorithm, specifically as it relates to the number of languages and the size of the data? Are there any planned optimizations to improve scalability?
3.  How would the method perform on languages with significantly different typological features or a smaller number of cognates?  Are there strategies for adapting the method to such scenarios?
4.  Regarding data over-weighting, can you provide a more principled justification for selecting the weighting factor? Have you investigated the impact of different weighting factors on the results?
5.  Are there plans to release the code and data used in the experiments to facilitate reproducibility and further research?

**Additional Comments:**

* The use of figures and tables is well done and effectively illustrates the results.
* The references are relevant and up-to-date.
* The exploration of using the sDL as a metric for language relatedness is intriguing and deserves further investigation.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem. While there are some weaknesses related to data limitations, scalability, and comparative evaluation, the strengths of the paper outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, contingent on addressing the questions raised above and considering the suggested improvements. The work has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of computational linguistics and historical linguistics.
