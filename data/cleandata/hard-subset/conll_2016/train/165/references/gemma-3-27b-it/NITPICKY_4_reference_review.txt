The paper proposes a Minimum Description Length (MDL) based approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages, aiming to overcome limitations of existing statistical methods that often rely on assumptions about the shape or distribution of correspondences. The authors present an efficient, deterministic algorithm and demonstrate its efficacy through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages, showcasing both pairwise comparisons and four-language analysis. They highlight the ability to quantify string-level similarities and identify linguistic subgroups.

The primary contribution, as presented by the authors, is the novel application of MDL to the multi-language correspondence discovery problem, coupled with a deterministic algorithm for efficient inference. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of the approach's effectiveness on Slavic languages, providing both qualitative examples of learned correspondences and quantitative analysis of linguistic similarity using NCD and shared description lengths.

**Strengths:**

1. **Formalization via MDL:** The application of the MDL principle provides a theoretically sound foundation for the approach. This moves beyond ad-hoc statistical measures and offers a principled way to balance model complexity and data fit. The authors correctly identify the benefits of MDL, especially in avoiding arbitrary assumptions.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The emphasis on a deterministic algorithm is a notable strength. In a field often dominated by probabilistic models, a deterministic approach offers transparency and reproducibility, simplifying interpretation of results. This facilitates linguistic analysis by removing randomness.
3. **Multi-Language Capability:** The algorithm’s ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a significant advantage over many existing methods. This unlocks the potential for more nuanced and comprehensive linguistic comparisons. The four-way analysis showcases this strength, revealing relationships not readily apparent in pairwise studies.
4. **Detailed Analysis & Quantification:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting correspondences. The use of NCD and shared description lengths allows for a quantitative assessment of linguistic similarity, providing valuable insights beyond qualitative observation. The dissection of shared description lengths into language subsets is especially insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Algorithm:** While the application of MDL is a solid contribution, the algorithm itself – using Expectation-Maximization (EM) with a graph-based alignment procedure – is not particularly novel. EM is a well-established technique, and the graph search for optimal alignment is a standard approach. The authors need to more strongly justify *why* this particular implementation of EM is advantageous beyond the MDL framework.
2. **Data Set Limitations & Representativeness:** The use of Swadesh lists, while convenient, is a significant limitation. Swadesh lists are known to be susceptible to borrowing and may not accurately reflect the full range of linguistic similarities. The statement that the data is "deemed representative" is insufficient justification. A more robust and diverse dataset is needed to demonstrate generalizability.
3. **Overly Detailed Implementation Descriptions:** The paper dedicates an excessive amount of space to minute implementation details (e.g., encoding string lengths, describing the graph search) that detract from the core conceptual contributions. These details are unlikely to be of broad interest to the community and could be significantly condensed or relegated to an appendix. The level of detail feels like a technical report rather than a concise research paper.
4. **Insufficient Comparison to Existing Work:** While the introduction mentions limitations of existing approaches, the paper lacks a thorough comparative evaluation. The authors should demonstrate how their approach performs relative to state-of-the-art methods on benchmark datasets, ideally using metrics beyond just NCD. A direct comparison would solidify the claims of superiority.
5. **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper suffers from occasional awkward phrasing and a lack of conceptual flow. For example, the discussion of data over-weighting feels tacked on and lacks a clear justification beyond addressing potential data scarcity. A more streamlined and coherent presentation would enhance readability. The formatting inconsistencies (e.g., varying use of italics and boldface) are also distracting.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What specific aspects of the EM implementation are crucial for the performance of the MDL-based approach? Can you provide a theoretical or empirical justification for these choices?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of dataset? Have you tested the approach on other datasets beyond Swadesh lists, and if so, what were the findings?
3.  Can you provide a more rigorous comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods for multi-language correspondence discovery? What are the strengths and weaknesses of your approach relative to these methods?
4.  What is the computational complexity of the algorithm in practice, considering the graph search and EM iterations? How does this scale with the number of languages and the size of the dataset?
5.  The introduction of data over-weighting seems almost as a post-hoc attempt to adjust for limitations in the approach. Is there a principled way to determine the optimal over-weighting factor, or is it purely a heuristic?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is incomplete.
*   Figure 1 needs a clearer label indicating the root of the tree.
*   The discussion of the limitations of Swadesh lists is too brief.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between your approach and existing methods.
*   Proofread carefully for minor grammatical errors and inconsistencies.

**Recommendation:**

Reject. While the application of MDL is a sound idea and the multi-language capability is a positive aspect, the lack of novelty in the algorithm, the limitations of the dataset, the overly detailed implementation descriptions, and the insufficient comparison to existing work outweigh these strengths. The paper, in its current form, doesn’t present a sufficiently significant contribution to warrant acceptance. A major revision addressing these weaknesses would be necessary for reconsideration.
