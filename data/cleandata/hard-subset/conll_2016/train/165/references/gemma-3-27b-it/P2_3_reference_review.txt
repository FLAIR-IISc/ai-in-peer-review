## Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors propose a deterministic algorithm to infer correspondence rules directly from data, avoiding assumptions about the shape or distribution of these rules. They demonstrate their method on Slavic languages, showcasing its ability to identify linguistic subgroups and quantify similarity across languages. The core contribution lies in extending correspondence discovery beyond pairwise comparisons, enabling a more holistic analysis of linguistic relationships.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **MDL-based Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence:** The application of MDL to the problem of finding correspondences between *more than two* languages is a significant conceptual contribution.  Existing methods frequently struggle with scalability or rely on pairwise comparisons, making this a valuable extension.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The development of a deterministic algorithm for rule inference is a strong point. This offers transparency and reproducibility, unlike methods relying on stochastic optimization, which can be difficult to interpret and replicate.
3. **Demonstration on Slavic Languages:**  The empirical evaluation on Slavic languages provides a concrete illustration of the method's effectiveness and allows for comparison with known linguistic classifications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:**  While MDL has been used in related areas (e.g., etymological reconstruction), applying it directly to discover multi-language correspondences *without* pre-defined assumptions is a novel direction. The focus on a purely data-driven, unbiased approach is commendable.
2. **Theoretical Foundation:**  The grounding in MDL provides a solid statistical foundation.  The paper clearly articulates the trade-off between model complexity and data description length, aligning with the core principles of MDL.
3. **Clear Algorithm Description:**  The algorithm is described in a relatively clear and concise manner, particularly the use of Expectation-Maximization (EM) and the rationale behind initialization with singleton rules. The details regarding the graph-based alignment procedure and the optimization steps are well-explained.
4. **Informative Results:** The pairwise NCD analysis and the phylogenetic tree construction demonstrate the method’s ability to produce results consistent with established linguistic knowledge. The detailed example correspondences and the Shared Description Length (sDL) analysis provide valuable insights into the learned relationships.
5. **Flexibility via Data Over-Weighting:** Introducing the concept of data over-weighting provides a mechanism to incorporate linguistic expertise and control the granularity of discovered correspondences. This enhances the practical applicability of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:**  While the authors mention being able to process up to five languages, the computational bottleneck described (memory consumption, runtime) raises concerns about the scalability to larger datasets or more languages. This limitation needs to be addressed, potentially through more efficient data structures or parallelization techniques.
2. **Limited Scope of Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to Slavic languages. While this allows for focused analysis and comparison with existing linguistic knowledge, it doesn’t demonstrate the generalizability of the method to other language families with different linguistic characteristics.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper lacks a rigorous comparison to existing methods for correspondence discovery, particularly those focused on pairwise comparisons. Quantitatively demonstrating superior performance (e.g., in terms of accuracy or F1-score) would strengthen the claims of novelty and effectiveness.
4. **Data Dependency:** The quality and coverage of the input data (Swadesh lists) are critical. The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed discussion of the limitations of Swadesh lists and potential biases would be beneficial.  The choice of cognates significantly influences the learned correspondences.
5. **Presentation Could Be More Concise:** While the paper is well-written, some sections (e.g., the detailed mathematical formulation of MDL) could be more concise and focused on the key contributions of the paper.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Scalability:** What specific optimizations are planned to address the computational bottlenecks and improve the scalability of the algorithm to larger datasets and more languages?
2. **Generalizability:**  Do you have preliminary results or plans for evaluating the method on language families beyond Slavic languages (e.g., Indo-European, Austronesian)?
3. **Comparison to Baselines:**  Could you provide a quantitative comparison of your method to existing techniques for correspondence discovery, even if those techniques are primarily designed for pairwise comparisons (e.g., using a shared set of cognates and evaluating the accuracy of the discovered rules)?
4. **Data Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the learned correspondences to the choice of cognates and the quality of the input data? Have you explored techniques for mitigating the effects of noise or incomplete data?
5. **Rule Evaluation:** Beyond the MDL score, are there any other metrics or linguistic criteria that you use to evaluate the quality or usefulness of the discovered correspondence rules?




**Additional Comments:**

* The figures are well-chosen and effectively illustrate the key results.
* The references are comprehensive and relevant.
* The writing quality is generally good, although some sections could benefit from more concise wording.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics. The MDL-based framework for multi-language correspondence discovery is novel and theoretically sound. The deterministic algorithm and the empirical evaluation on Slavic languages provide strong evidence of its effectiveness. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding scalability and lack of comparison to existing methods, I believe these can be addressed in a revision. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
