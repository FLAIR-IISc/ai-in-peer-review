This paper proposes a method for discovering correspondences between multiple languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to identify the most succinct set of correspondence rules that describe the observed cognate data, without imposing prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of these rules. The authors present an efficient, deterministic algorithm for inferring these rules and demonstrate its efficacy on Slavic languages, performing both pairwise analyses and a four-language experiment.

The primary contribution of this work, as I see it, is the application of MDL to a multi-lingual correspondence discovery problem *without* strong parametric assumptions. While MDL has been used previously in related areas (as acknowledged by the authors), its application to finding correspondences across *more than two* languages, and in a completely non-parametric way, represents a potentially useful methodological extension. The accompanying algorithm, while complex, is presented with sufficient detail to allow for replication. A secondary, supporting contribution is the demonstration of the method on Slavic languages, including a phylogenetic analysis and quantification of language similarity. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Methodological Soundness (with caveats - see weaknesses):** The use of MDL provides a statistically grounded framework for model selection. The authors correctly emphasize the benefit of avoiding arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distributions, a common issue in existing approaches. The deterministic nature of the algorithm is also a positive attribute, promoting reproducibility.
2. **Multi-lingual Extension:** The ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a genuine advancement. Existing methods often struggle with scaling to larger numbers of languages due to combinatorial complexity. The authors’ approach seems to offer a viable solution, demonstrated through the four-language experiment.
3. **Clear Presentation of the Algorithm:** The paper provides a detailed description of the algorithm, including the Expectation-Maximization steps and the MDL code design. The inclusion of equations and algorithmic explanations allows for a thorough understanding of the method.
4. **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments, while limited in scope, provide some evidence of the method's effectiveness. The phylogenetic tree generated from pairwise NCDs aligns with established linguistic classifications, and the four-language analysis reveals interesting similarities between language subsets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Empirical Validation:** The experiments are restricted to a single language family (Slavic) and a relatively small dataset. While the results are encouraging, they don't demonstrate the generalizability of the approach to other language families or larger datasets. This is a *significant* weakness. The claims about efficiency and detailed quantification of similarity need more rigorous testing on diverse data.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** A major flaw is the absence of a comprehensive comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods. The paper mentions several related approaches in the introduction but doesn’t present any comparative results.  It’s crucial to demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms or offers advantages over established techniques like Bayesian phylogenetic methods or alignment-based approaches. Without such comparison, the novelty and utility of the approach remain questionable. Simply stating that existing methods have limitations is not sufficient justification.
3. **Data Dependency and Overfitting Concerns:** While MDL is designed to prevent overfitting, the algorithm’s reliance on a specific dataset and the potential for data over-weighting (as discussed in Section 4.5) raise concerns about sensitivity to data quality and potential for artificially inflating results. The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough investigation of the robustness of the method to data variations is needed. The concept of "data over-weighting" feels ad-hoc and lacks strong theoretical justification; it's essentially introducing a prior *through* the data.
4. **Complexity and Scalability:** While the authors claim efficiency, the description of the alignment procedure highlights potential computational bottlenecks. The statement that processing five languages takes “hours” on a relatively modest machine suggests limited scalability. A more detailed analysis of the algorithm's time and space complexity is necessary.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity of your algorithm with that of existing methods for multi-lingual alignment and correspondence discovery?
2.  What steps have you taken to address the potential for overfitting, particularly in light of the data over-weighting strategy?
3.  Are you planning to evaluate the method on language families *other* than Slavic? If so, could you outline your plans and the datasets you intend to use?
4.  Can you provide a more rigorous justification for the data over-weighting strategy, beyond its ability to uncover more detailed correspondences? What statistical properties does it exploit?
5. How sensitive is the choice of the over-weighting parameter (α) to the resulting inferred correspondences?  



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly explains a complex method. However, the lack of comparative evaluation and the limited scope of the experiments significantly weaken its impact. The approach shows promise, but requires substantially more validation before its claims can be fully substantiated. The discussion of data over-weighting is interesting but needs more justification and analysis.
