## Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

**Summary:** This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors propose a deterministic algorithm to infer correspondence rules directly from data, avoiding assumptions about the shape or distribution of these rules. They demonstrate the method on Slavic languages, performing both pairwise analysis and a four-language experiment, concluding that the approach effectively captures linguistic relationships and quantifies similarities.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **MDL Application to Multi-lingual Correspondence:** The core contribution is the application of MDL to the problem of discovering language correspondences *without* imposing prior assumptions about rule structure. This is a potentially significant shift from existing statistical approaches.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The development of a deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules is a valuable asset.  Deterministic approaches enhance reproducibility and allow for easier interpretation of results, unlike probabilistic methods prone to stochastic variations.
3. **Beyond Pairwise Analysis:** The demonstration of the method’s capability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously, and the development of metrics to quantify similarity across multiple languages (shared description length), is a notable step forward.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of leveraging MDL to discover correspondences *without* strong prior assumptions is genuinely novel.  The field has been dominated by approaches with inherent biases, and this work offers a compelling alternative. The lack of assumption allows the data to "speak for itself".
2. **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding of the method in MDL provides a strong theoretical justification. The paper clearly explains the MDL principle and how it’s applied in the context of language correspondence. The two-part MDL formulation is well-described.
3. **Clear Algorithm Description:** The paper provides a relatively clear and detailed description of the algorithm, including the Expectation-Maximization (EM) steps and the alignment procedure. The mention of partial order reduction for graph size optimization is a practical detail.
4. **Demonstrated Effectiveness:** The experiments on Slavic languages provide evidence that the method can identify plausible linguistic relationships. The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values aligns with established linguistic classifications.  The examples of learned correspondences (Table 3) are convincing.
5. **Quantifiable Similarity Measures:** The introduction of shared Description Length (sDL) is a useful addition. It provides a quantifiable metric for comparing the structural similarity between different language subsets and identifying nuanced relationships.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:** While the authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks, the limitation to "up to five languages" on a standard desktop is a significant practical constraint. This restricts the method’s applicability to larger-scale analyses or datasets. A more thorough discussion of scalability, including potential optimization strategies (beyond partial order reduction), is needed.
2. **Data Dependency & Limited Dataset:** The experiments rely on Swadesh lists, which, while useful, represent a narrow slice of language data.  The robustness of the approach to different types of data (e.g., parallel corpora, historical texts) is not demonstrated. The limited size of the dataset (Table 1) also raises questions about the statistical significance of some findings.  The results need to be validated on more diverse and larger datasets.
3. **Over-Weighting Subjectivity:** While the introduction of data over-weighting provides flexibility, it introduces a subjective element controlled by the user.  The paper doesn't sufficiently discuss the potential impact of this parameter on the results, or provide guidelines for selecting an appropriate weighting value.  A more rigorous analysis of the sensitivity of the results to this parameter is required.
4. **Evaluation Primarily Intrinsic:** The evaluation primarily focuses on comparing the learned tree to known linguistic classifications.  While this is a good start, the evaluation would be strengthened by comparing the performance of the learned correspondences to other state-of-the-art methods on tasks like cognate identification or machine translation.
5. **Presentation could be tighter:** The paper feels somewhat verbose in places, and could benefit from more concise explanations of concepts.  For example, the detailed explanation of MDL (Section 3) feels slightly lengthy given its established nature.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What are the primary bottlenecks in the algorithm’s scalability? Are there avenues for parallelization or approximation techniques that could improve performance on larger datasets?
2.  How sensitive are the learned correspondences and the resulting phylogenetic tree to the choice of the over-weighting parameter (α)? Can you provide an analysis of the impact of varying α on the results?
3.  Have you considered applying your method to datasets beyond Swadesh lists? If so, what challenges did you encounter?
4.  How does the performance of your method compare to existing state-of-the-art approaches for cognate identification or machine translation, using a standard evaluation metric?
5.  You mention the possibility of randomized alignments.  Can you elaborate on how simulated annealing (or other stochastic search techniques) could be integrated into the algorithm, and what benefits it might provide?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are well-cited.
*   The formatting is generally good, though some equations could benefit from clearer presentation.
*   The paper’s strength lies in its innovative application of MDL and the potential for discovering truly unbiased language correspondences. Addressing the scalability and evaluation concerns will significantly strengthen the contribution.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper has the potential to be a significant contribution to the field of computational linguistics. While there are weaknesses that need to be addressed, the novelty of the approach, the sound theoretical foundation, and the promising experimental results warrant acceptance. I recommend **acceptance** contingent upon the authors addressing the weaknesses outlined above in their response and, ideally, through revisions to the paper.
