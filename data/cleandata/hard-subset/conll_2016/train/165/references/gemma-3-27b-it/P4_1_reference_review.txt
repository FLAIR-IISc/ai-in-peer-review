This paper presents an approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to identify the set of correspondence rules that most succinctly describe the observed data, avoiding prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of these rules. The authors propose a deterministic algorithm to infer these rules and demonstrate its efficacy through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages.

The primary contribution of this work is the application of MDL to the problem of multi-language correspondence discovery without imposing strong prior assumptions. While MDL has been previously used in related areas (e.g., Wettig et al., 2011 cited in the paper), extending it to handle more than two languages in a deterministic and assumption-minimal way represents a significant step. A second contribution lies in the detailed experimental evaluation, including both pairwise analysis with comparison to linguistic classifications and a novel four-language analysis, providing insights into shared linguistic structures. Finally, the introduction of the concept of “shared description length” offers a new way to quantify similarities between different subsets of languages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The use of MDL in this context, specifically aiming for assumption-minimal correspondence discovery across multiple languages, is a valuable contribution. Existing statistical methods often rely on predefined distributions or alignments, which this approach avoids. This allows the data to "speak for itself" and potentially uncover unexpected patterns.
2. **Rigorous Methodology:** The paper provides a clear and well-defined algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) and leverages coding theory through MDL. The detailed explanation of the model code (L(M)) and data code (L(D|M)) demonstrates a strong technical foundation. The emphasis on deterministic inference is also a strength, promoting reproducibility and interpretability.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and present a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed method. The use of both pairwise analysis (with comparison to established linguistic classifications) and four-language analysis is commendable. The inclusion of a phylogenetic tree visualization further strengthens the evaluation.
4. **Informative Results:** The results presented are insightful. The NCD-based phylogenetic tree largely aligns with established linguistic classifications. The examples of learned correspondences are illustrative and demonstrate the algorithm's ability to identify various types of rules. The introduction of shared description lengths provides a novel metric for quantifying linguistic similarity.
5. **Discussion of Data Over-Weighting:** The introduction of data over-weighting as a mechanism for incorporating linguistic knowledge and controlling the granularity of the learned rules is a thoughtful addition. This provides a useful parameter for tuning the algorithm to specific research questions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:** While the authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks, the stated limitations (handling up to five languages on a specific machine) raise concerns about the scalability of the algorithm to larger datasets or more languages. The time complexity analysis provided is helpful, but a discussion of potential optimizations (beyond partial order reduction) would be valuable.
2. **Data Dependency and Representativeness:** The results are based on Swadesh lists and a specific corpus of Slavic cognates. The limited size and specific nature of these datasets raise questions about the generalizability of the findings. A more extensive evaluation with diverse data sources would be desirable.
3. **Limited Comparison to Existing Methods:** While the introduction mentions existing statistical approaches, the paper lacks a direct, quantitative comparison to these methods. Demonstrating that the proposed approach outperforms or provides unique insights compared to established techniques would strengthen the argument for its value.
4. **Presentation of Rules:** The presented example correspondences (Table 3) are difficult to interpret without more context. Providing a broader range of examples and a more detailed explanation of the linguistic significance of the discovered rules would improve clarity. The distinction between rules found with and without data over-weighting could be more clearly explained in the context of linguistic interpretation.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper does not include any error analysis or discussion of cases where the algorithm fails to identify correct correspondences. A critical assessment of the algorithm’s limitations would enhance the credibility of the work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What specific optimizations are being considered to address the scalability concerns? Are there plans to explore parallelization or alternative data structures to improve performance?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of initial conditions (e.g., null alignment vs. greedy alignment)?
3. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the linguistic significance of the discovered rules? For example, what specific sound changes or morphological processes do these rules reflect?
4. How does the performance of your algorithm compare to existing methods (e.g., those cited in the introduction) in terms of accuracy, precision, and recall?
5.  Can you elaborate on the rationale for selecting the specific data weighting factor used in the experiments? How does the choice of this factor impact the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of the limitations of the data (e.g., the influence of Czech diacritics) is appreciated.
*   The notation used throughout the paper is generally clear and consistent.
*   The references are appropriately cited.
*   The paper is well-written and logically organized.
