This paper proposes a Minimum Description Length (MDL) approach for discovering correspondences between multiple languages, aiming to circumvent the limitations of existing statistical methods that often rely on restrictive assumptions. The core contribution is an algorithm that deterministically infers correspondence rules directly from data without pre-defined distributions or shapes, and applying it to Slavic languages to quantify similarity. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of a method to incorporate linguistic knowledge via data weighting to fine-tune the granularity of the discovered correspondences.

**Strengths:**

1. **Methodological Rigor (to a degree):** The application of MDL provides a statistically grounded framework for model selection, which is a positive aspect. The authors explicitly aim to avoid arbitrary assumptions, which is commendable. The algorithm's deterministic nature is also a strength, as it provides reproducibility and avoids stochastic fluctuations that can plague other approaches.
2. **Multi-Language Capability:** Existing methods often struggle with more than two languages. This work directly addresses that limitation, offering a framework for analyzing multiple languages simultaneously. The demonstration with four Slavic languages is a tangible benefit.
3. **Quantifiable Similarity:** The NCD-based analysis provides a quantifiable measure of linguistic similarity, offering a more objective and potentially insightful alternative to traditional linguistic comparison. The visualization through phylogenetic trees is helpful.
4. **Data Weighting for Control:** The introduction of data weighting offers a practical way to incorporate linguistic knowledge or tune the algorithm's sensitivity, allowing users to influence the level of detail in the discovered correspondences.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Baseline Comparison:** The most significant weakness is the almost complete absence of comparison to established methods. While the authors critique existing statistical approaches, they don't demonstrate superior performance *against* them. The claim of avoiding arbitrary assumptions is weakened without showing that those assumptions in other methods demonstrably harm results. A proper evaluation would involve implementing or adapting existing state-of-the-art techniques and comparing their performance (e.g., cognate identification accuracy, reconstruction quality) to the proposed MDL approach.
2. **Limited Data & Scope:** The experiments are limited to Slavic languages and relatively small datasets (Swadesh lists). While a good starting point, it raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings. The performance on a broader range of language families and data sizes needs to be established. The reliance on Swadesh lists, while common, is known to have limitations and may not capture the full complexity of linguistic relationships.
3. **Computational Scalability:** The authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks and limitations in handling larger datasets. While they mention plans for improvement, the current scalability constraints are a significant concern, particularly for analyzing large-scale corpora.
4. **Black Box Nature of Rule Discovery:** While the deterministic nature is a strength, the process of rule discovery feels somewhat opaque. It’s unclear *why* certain rules are favored over others, beyond their MDL score. More insight into the internal workings of the algorithm and the factors influencing rule selection would be valuable.
5. **Over-reliance on Orthography:** The method appears heavily focused on orthographic similarity. While this is a reasonable starting point, linguistic similarity extends beyond surface form. The algorithm doesn't seem to explicitly incorporate phonological or morphological information, which could lead to spurious correspondences.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a quantitative comparison of your method against established techniques (e.g., Levenshtein distance-based methods, Bayesian phylogenetic methods) on the Slavic language dataset, specifically in terms of cognate identification accuracy or reconstruction quality?
2. How does the performance of your algorithm scale with the number of languages and the size of the dataset? What are the main computational bottlenecks, and what specific optimizations are planned to address them?
3. Could you elaborate on how linguistic knowledge, beyond simple data weighting, could be integrated into the model? For example, could phonological rules or morphological constraints be incorporated as priors or constraints on the rule discovery process?
4. What strategies are employed to mitigate the influence of orthographic variation and noise in the data?
5. The paper mentions a potential for overfitting even with MDL. Can you describe the practical implications of this and how you’ve addressed it in your experiments?



**Additional Comments:**

The notation is generally clear, but more precise definitions of key terms (e.g., "complexity," "description length") would be helpful. The references are somewhat limited and could be expanded to include more relevant work in computational linguistics and phylogenetic methods. While the paper presents a conceptually interesting approach, the lack of thorough evaluation and comparison to existing methods significantly weakens its impact.
