This paper presents an approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to find the set of correspondence rules that most succinctly describes the given data, avoiding assumptions about the shape or distribution of these rules. The authors provide an efficient, deterministic algorithm for inferring these rules and demonstrate its efficacy through analyses of Slavic languages, including pairwise comparisons and a four-language experiment.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **MDL-based Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The paper introduces a principled, statistically grounded framework for identifying language correspondences without relying on pre-defined distributions or assumptions. This is a significant contribution as it addresses limitations of existing statistical approaches highlighted in the introduction.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm for Rule Inference:** The authors propose and implement a deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) to infer correspondence rules directly from data. The deterministic nature ensures reproducibility and reduces the influence of chance, offering an advantage over stochastic methods.
3. **Empirical Validation on Slavic Languages:** The comprehensive experiments on Slavic languages, including phylogenetic analysis and detailed four-way comparisons, provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The evaluation includes comparison with linguistic classifications and insightful analysis of shared description lengths.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The application of MDL to multi-language correspondence discovery, coupled with a deterministic algorithm, is novel. While MDL has been used in related areas (e.g., Wettig et al., 2011, cited in the paper), the authors extend its use to a more general setting and offer a specific algorithmic solution.
2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The paper builds upon a solid theoretical foundation (MDL principle) and provides a clear mathematical formulation of the problem and the proposed solution. The connection to information theory is well-articulated.
3. **Detailed Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The use of Swadesh lists provides a standardized dataset, and the comparison with linguistic classifications lends credibility to the results. The four-way analysis goes beyond standard pairwise comparisons and demonstrates the method's ability to handle multiple languages simultaneously.
4. **Clear Explanation of the Algorithm:** The paper clearly explains the EM algorithm and the rationale behind its design choices, such as the initialization with singleton rules and the merging criterion. The discussion of time complexity provides valuable insight into the algorithm's scalability.
5. **Insightful Analysis of Results:** The authors provide insightful interpretations of the experimental results, connecting the discovered correspondences to linguistic phenomena and highlighting the benefits of data over-weighting.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Dependency and Limited Generalizability:** The experiments are limited to Slavic languages. While this allows for in-depth analysis, it is unclear how well the approach would generalize to languages with different typological characteristics or writing systems. The reliance on Swadesh lists, while standardized, might also limit the richness of the data and the types of correspondences that can be discovered.
2. **Scalability Concerns:** While the authors mention the ability to process up to five languages, the description of bottlenecks in memory consumption and runtime suggests that scaling to a larger number of languages or larger datasets could be challenging. Further investigation into optimization techniques is needed.
3. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Methods:** The paper primarily positions itself against the limitations of existing statistical approaches without directly comparing its performance to them on the same datasets. A quantitative comparison with other methods (e.g., those based on cognate identification or alignment) would strengthen the claim of superiority.
4. **Over-Weighting as a Subjective Parameter:** The introduction of data over-weighting, while potentially useful for incorporating linguistic knowledge, introduces a subjective parameter (α) that can influence the results. The paper does not provide clear guidelines for choosing an appropriate value for α.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses on presenting successful results but lacks a detailed discussion of cases where the algorithm fails to identify correct correspondences or produces spurious ones. A thorough error analysis would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How well does the proposed approach generalize to languages with different typological characteristics (e.g., isolating languages, agglutinative languages)? Are there specific language features that pose challenges for the algorithm?
2. What optimization techniques have been explored to address the scalability concerns, and what are the current limitations in terms of the number of languages and dataset size that can be handled?
3. Could you provide a quantitative comparison of the proposed method with state-of-the-art approaches on a common dataset?
4. What criteria can be used to determine an appropriate value for the data over-weighting parameter α? Are there any guidelines or heuristics that can be provided to users?
5. Could you provide examples of cases where the algorithm fails to identify correct correspondences or produces spurious ones, and explain the reasons for these failures?

**Additional Comments:**

* The discussion of the connection to coding theory and the use of two-part MDL is well-explained.
* The figures and tables are clear and informative.
* The writing style is generally clear and concise. However, some sections could benefit from further elaboration and simplification. For example, the description of the EM algorithm could be more accessible to readers who are not familiar with the technique.
