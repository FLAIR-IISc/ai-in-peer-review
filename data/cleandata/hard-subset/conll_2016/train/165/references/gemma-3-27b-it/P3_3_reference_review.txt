## ConLL Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

**Summary:** This paper presents a novel approach to discovering systematic correspondences between multiple languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors propose a deterministic algorithm that avoids imposing prior assumptions on the shape or distribution of correspondences, a common limitation of existing statistical methods. They demonstrate the efficacy of their approach through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages, showcasing its ability to quantify string-level similarities and identify linguistic subgroups.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of MDL to Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The core contribution is the successful application of MDL – a principle well-suited for model selection – to the challenging problem of discovering linguistic correspondences across *multiple* languages simultaneously. This is a significant departure from the dominant paradigm of pairwise comparison.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm & Avoidance of Priors:** The development of a deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules is commendable. By avoiding distributional assumptions, the method offers a more objective and unbiased approach to uncovering inherent relationships within the data.
3. **Demonstration on Slavic Languages & Detailed Quantification:** The experiments on Slavic languages serve as a convincing demonstration of the method's capabilities.  The quantification of similarity, including the analysis of shared description lengths between language subsets, provides valuable insights beyond traditional phylogenetic trees.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Theoretical Soundness:** The application of MDL to this problem is genuinely novel and theoretically well-grounded.  The paper clearly articulates the benefits of MDL – specifically its avoidance of arbitrary assumptions – and establishes a strong rationale for its use.  The formalization of the problem within the MDL framework is rigorous.
2. **Deterministic Nature & Interpretability:**  The deterministic nature of the algorithm is a major strength.  It fosters reproducibility and allows for easier interpretation of the results. Unlike stochastic methods, the resulting correspondence rules are not influenced by random initialization or sampling.
3. **Scalability & Multi-Language Capability:** The method’s ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a substantial advantage.  This capability opens up new avenues for investigating complex linguistic relationships that are inaccessible to pairwise methods.  While computational limitations are acknowledged, the authors demonstrate successful application to four languages.
4. **Informative Experiments & Analysis:** The experiments are well-designed and provide compelling evidence of the method's effectiveness. The inclusion of both pairwise NCD analysis and the four-way analysis with shared description length quantification provides a comprehensive evaluation.  The discussion of the results, especially the identification of linguistic subgroups and subtle differences between languages, is insightful.
5. **Clear and Well-Written Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the technical details of the approach. The inclusion of examples (e.g., Table 3) helps to illustrate the discovered correspondences and the effect of data weighting.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Dependency & Limited Data Size:** The performance of the algorithm is heavily dependent on the quality and quantity of the input data. The use of Swadesh lists, while common, is a relatively limited dataset. The authors acknowledge this limitation, but the impact on the generalizability of the results should be further discussed.  The analysis of the effects of data weighting attempts to address this, but it introduces a subjective element.
2. **Computational Complexity:** While the authors address computational challenges, the scaling limitations remain a concern. The current implementation can only process up to five languages. Exploring more efficient data structures and algorithms (e.g., approximation algorithms or parallelization strategies) is crucial for wider applicability.  A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity would be valuable.
3. **Evaluation Primarily Based on Internal Consistency:** The evaluation primarily focuses on demonstrating the internal consistency of the method (e.g., alignment with known linguistic classifications). While this is important, it would be strengthened by comparing the discovered correspondences to *external* linguistic knowledge or established cognate databases.  A benchmark against existing methods would also be beneficial.
4. **Over-Weighting as a Subjective Parameter:**  The introduction of data over-weighting, while providing flexibility, introduces a subjective parameter that requires expert judgment. While the authors acknowledge this, a more systematic exploration of the impact of different weighting schemes and guidance on how to choose appropriate values would improve the robustness of the method.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed discussion of potential errors or limitations of the algorithm. Identifying common types of errors and explaining why they occur would enhance the credibility of the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm, particularly in relation to the number of languages (N) and the length of the cognate tuples?
2.  How sensitive is the algorithm to errors or inconsistencies in the input data?  Have you investigated the impact of noisy data on the discovered correspondences?
3.  Can you elaborate on the criteria used to determine the appropriate level of data over-weighting?  Is there a principled way to select the weighting factor, or is it solely based on expert judgment?
4.  You mention comparing the results to external linguistic knowledge in the conclusion.  Have you begun such a comparison, and if so, what preliminary findings have emerged?
5.  Regarding the four-way analysis (Section 5.3), could you provide more examples of rules that link only two or three languages, and explain the linguistic significance of these partial correspondences?




**Additional Comments:**

* The references are appropriately cited.
* The paper's structure is logical and easy to follow.
* The inclusion of code or data would greatly enhance the reproducibility of the research.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong and innovative paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of computational linguistics. Despite some limitations regarding data dependency and computational complexity, the proposed approach offers a promising new direction for discovering and quantifying linguistic correspondences. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above. The strengths of the paper – particularly its novelty, theoretical soundness, and clear presentation – outweigh its limitations.
