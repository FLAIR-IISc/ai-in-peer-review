The paper proposes a Minimum Description Length (MDL) approach for discovering correspondences between multiple languages, aiming to overcome limitations of existing statistical methods that often rely on strong assumptions or are limited to pairwise comparisons. The core contribution is an efficient, deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) to infer correspondence rules directly from data without prior assumptions about their shape or distribution. The authors demonstrate their approach on Slavic languages, showing its ability to quantify string-level similarities and identify linguistic subgroups.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Methodological Components:** While the application of MDL to this specific problem is presented as novel, the individual components—EM algorithm, string alignment, and MDL principle itself—are well-established techniques. The paper doesn't demonstrate significant innovation in *how* these techniques are applied or modified; it's more of a thoughtful combination. The algorithmic description, particularly the alignment procedure leveraging Dijkstra’s, is standard. This diminishes the paper's overall impact.

2. **Insufficient Comparative Evaluation:** The paper claims to overcome limitations of existing methods, but lacks a robust comparative evaluation against these methods. While the authors mention existing work in the introduction, there’s no direct comparison of the results produced by their method with the results from, for example, Schulz et al. (2004), Snyder et al. (2010), or Wettig et al. (2011). Simply stating the limitations of other methods isn't sufficient; demonstrating superior performance is crucial. The NCD-based phylogenetic tree comparison to “linguistic classifications” is vague and lacks quantitative metrics. What constitutes "linguistic classification"? Is this expert-defined, or derived from another source?

3. **Overly Detailed and Convoluted Presentation:** The paper is burdened by excessive detail in the MDL formulation. The detailed derivations of code lengths (L(M), L(π), etc.) feel unnecessarily verbose and obscure the core idea. While mathematical rigor is appreciated, the level of detail hinders readability and makes it difficult to grasp the high-level contribution. The extensive explanation of seemingly minor implementation details (e.g., LN encoding, partial order reduction) feels like padding. A more concise and focused presentation would significantly improve the paper’s clarity. The use of unnecessarily complex notation (e.g., the numerous subscripts and summations) further exacerbates this problem.

4. **Limited Data and Scope:** The experiments are limited to Slavic languages and Swadesh lists. While this provides a controlled environment, it raises questions about the generalizability of the approach.  Swadesh lists are known to be problematic due to semantic shifts and borrowing, potentially influencing the results. Extending the evaluation to a more diverse set of languages and data types (e.g., parallel corpora, cognate databases) would strengthen the findings.

5. **Inconsistent Handling of Data Weighting:** The introduction of data over-weighting as a mechanism to address insufficient statistical evidence feels ad-hoc and compromises the objectivity of the MDL approach. While acknowledging the subjective nature of this adjustment, the paper doesn't sufficiently justify *when* and *how* to apply it. The explanation is vague, and its impact isn’t thoroughly analyzed.  The assertion that the rules are still discovered "in the correct order" is unsubstantiated.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of discovering multilingual correspondences without relying on prior assumptions. This is a well-defined and important challenge in computational linguistics.

2. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The emphasis on a deterministic algorithm is a positive aspect, promoting reproducibility and transparency. This contrasts with many statistical methods that involve randomness and can yield different results on different runs.

3. **Potential for Detailed Analysis:** The MDL framework, when appropriately applied, provides a statistically sound foundation for quantifying linguistic similarity. The capability to analyze all subsets of languages simultaneously offers a level of granularity not readily available with pairwise comparison methods.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a quantitative comparison of your method’s performance against existing state-of-the-art approaches on a standardized dataset? Specifically, how do your results compare to those reported by Schulz et al. (2004), Snyder et al. (2010), or Wettig et al. (2011)?

2. What criteria were used to define the "linguistic classifications" against which the phylogenetic tree was compared? Can you provide a more rigorous evaluation of the tree’s accuracy?

3. Could you provide a justification for the level of detail included in the MDL formulation? Is this level of detail necessary for understanding the core contribution, or could the paper be made more accessible by presenting a more concise explanation?

4. How sensitive are the results to the choice of data weighting factor (α) in Section 4.5? Can you provide a quantitative analysis of the impact of different α values on the inferred correspondences?

5. How does the computational complexity of the algorithm scale with the number of languages? What are the practical limitations of applying this method to a very large number of languages?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper’s formatting is inconsistent. For example, some equations are not centered.
*   The references are not consistently formatted.
*   The use of abbreviations (e.g., NCD, EM) should be defined upon first mention.
*   The phrasing "the true correspondences" is problematic. Correspondence is a matter of degree and interpretation, not an objective truth.



**Recommendation:**

Reject. While the paper presents a potentially interesting application of MDL, the lack of novelty, insufficient comparative evaluation, overly detailed presentation, and limited scope outweigh the strengths. A major revision addressing these weaknesses would be necessary for reconsideration. The paper feels like a well-executed implementation of existing ideas rather than a significant advancement in the field.
