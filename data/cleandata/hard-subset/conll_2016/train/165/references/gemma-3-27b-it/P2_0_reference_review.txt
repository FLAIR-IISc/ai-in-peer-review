## Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a method for discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to treat correspondences as simply associated strings without pre-defined distributional assumptions, aiming for objective string-level similarity measures. The authors introduce an efficient, deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) and demonstrate its effectiveness through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages, showcasing both pairwise comparisons and a four-language experiment. They also explore the impact of data over-weighting on the granularity of discovered correspondences.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A principled, data-driven approach to multi-way linguistic correspondence discovery:** The use of MDL provides a solid theoretical foundation for identifying statistically significant correspondences without imposing potentially biasing assumptions about their structure. This is a valuable contribution, as many existing methods rely on such assumptions.
2. **An efficient and deterministic algorithm for correspondence inference:** The EM-based algorithm, coupled with the described optimization techniques (partial order reduction, leveraging singleton rules for initialization), appears to be a practical solution for discovering correspondence rules, even for a moderate number of languages.
3. **Demonstration of multi-way analysis & quantification of linguistic similarity:**  The paper goes beyond simple pairwise comparisons, offering a way to quantify relationships within a group of languages and identify sub-groupings based on shared correspondence patterns – a capability lacking in many prior methods.



**Strengths:**

1. **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding in MDL is a major strength. It provides a clear justification for the approach and a principled way to balance model complexity with data fit. The authors appropriately link their work to the broader MDL literature.
2. **Novelty in Multi-way Analysis:** The ability to directly analyze correspondences across more than two languages is a significant advantage over existing methods that typically focus on pairwise comparisons. The four-way analysis and the ability to quantify similarities between different language subsets is a key differentiator.
3. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The deterministic nature of the algorithm is beneficial for reproducibility and interpretability. It also facilitates a more direct understanding of *why* certain correspondences are identified.  The authors’ emphasis on avoiding chance influence is commendable.
4. **Practicality & Scalability (to a degree):** While limitations are acknowledged (see weaknesses), the authors demonstrate the feasibility of their approach on a dataset of several languages. The reported runtime on a standard desktop machine suggests a reasonable level of scalability.
5. **Informative Experimental Results:**  The experiments are well-designed and presented. The comparison of the inferred phylogeny with known linguistic classifications provides strong validation. The examples of discovered correspondences are insightful and highlight the method’s capabilities. The demonstration of data over-weighting as a means of controlling granularity is valuable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scalability (and lack of detailed complexity analysis):**  The authors acknowledge the runtime and memory bottlenecks when dealing with more languages or larger datasets.  The lack of a more detailed analysis of the algorithm’s computational complexity (beyond stating O(|D| * |R|^2) and O(|D|*A^2/2) ) is a concern.  How quickly do runtime and memory usage grow with the number of languages *and* the size of the lexicon?  This is crucial for assessing the method’s applicability to larger-scale problems.
2. **Data Dependency & Sensitivity to Data Quality:** Like any statistical method, the results are heavily dependent on the quality and representativeness of the input data. The authors use Swadesh lists, which are known to have limitations.  The impact of noise or inaccuracies in the cognate data isn’t adequately addressed. The discussion of data over-weighting hints at a sensitivity issue that needs further investigation.  Is the algorithm robust to errors in the data, or will even small inaccuracies lead to significantly different results?
3. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art (specifically for multi-way analysis):** While the paper references related work, it lacks a direct comparative evaluation against other methods capable of handling multi-way analysis, even if those methods have limitations.  How does this MDL-based approach perform *relative* to existing techniques (e.g., phylogenetic methods adapted for linguistic data) in terms of accuracy, efficiency, and interpretability?  This omission weakens the claim of novelty and superiority.
4. **Orthographic Focus & Limited Linguistic Integration:**  The method operates solely on orthographic form, ignoring phonetic or morphological information. While the authors state they aim for objective measures, the lack of linguistic knowledge integration is a limitation.  Could incorporating phonetic or morphological features improve the accuracy or robustness of the correspondences discovered?




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Scalability:**  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the algorithm’s computational complexity, specifically how runtime and memory usage scale with the number of languages and the size of the lexicon?  What are the main bottlenecks, and are there any potential optimizations to address them?
2. **Data Robustness:** How sensitive is the algorithm to noise or inaccuracies in the cognate data?  Have you experimented with data augmentation or error correction techniques to improve robustness?
3. **Comparative Evaluation:**  Could you compare your method to other approaches capable of handling multi-way analysis, even if they have limitations? What are the advantages and disadvantages of your method relative to these alternatives?
4. **Linguistic Feature Integration:** Have you considered incorporating phonetic or morphological features into the model?  If so, what challenges did you encounter?  If not, could you discuss the potential benefits and drawbacks of doing so?
5. **Over-weighting Justification:** What criteria did you use to determine the level of data over-weighting?  Is there a principled way to choose the optimal weighting factor?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is generally well-written and clearly presented.
* The notation could be slightly improved for readability.
*  The figures are helpful and well-labeled.
* The inclusion of sample correspondences (Table 3) is excellent for illustrating the method’s capabilities.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics. The MDL-based approach, the efficient algorithm, and the demonstration of multi-way analysis are all significant strengths. While there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding scalability and comparison to other methods, the strengths outweigh the weaknesses.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and concerns raised above.  The authors should focus on providing more detailed analysis of the algorithm's scalability, demonstrating robustness to data noise, and conducting a comparative evaluation with other relevant approaches.
