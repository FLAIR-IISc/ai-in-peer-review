- Proposes an MDL-based approach to discover correspondences between multiple languages without strong prior assumptions.
- Introduces a deterministic EM-based algorithm to infer correspondence rules and demonstrates experiments on Slavic languages.
- Claims ability to quantify linguistic similarity beyond pairwise comparisons, enabling subset analyses of languages.
- The MDL formulation provides a principled, information-theoretic foundation that avoids bias from arbitrary priors.
- Method is novel and deterministic, offering more objectivity than many existing historical-linguistics approaches.
- Empirical results (phylogenetic trees from NCD values) align with established linguistic classifications, supporting validity.
- Data over-weighting is proposed to trade statistical rigor for more detailed, linguistically useful correspondences.
- Paper is generally well-written and includes illustrative examples of learned correspondences.
- Scalability is limited: current implementation handles only up to ~5 languages on a desktop and complexity analysis lacks concrete real-world parameter values.
- Method depends on high-quality cognate lists; preprocessing and robustness to noisy or inconsistent orthography are insufficiently addressed.
- Evaluation is narrow, focused on phylogeny; lacks comparisons to gold standards or downstream-task evaluations (e.g., MT).
- No detailed error analysis is provided to characterize or explain algorithmic failures and their causes.
- MDL exposition may be hard to follow for readers unfamiliar with the principle and could use a simpler introduction.
- Requests clarification on computational bottlenecks and typical sizes of |R| and |A| for used datasets.
- Asks what preprocessing was done for cognate quality, and whether automatic cognate detection or noise-handling was explored.
- Suggests expanding evaluation metrics and testing usefulness of correspondences in downstream tasks like machine translation.
- Seeks examples of incorrect or suboptimal correspondences and explanations for such errors.
- Wants guidance and concrete examples on choosing the over-weighting parameter α and its linguistic rationale.
- Notes the reference list appears incomplete and recommends discussing related work in unsupervised morphological segmentation.
- Recommends improving figure readability (Figures 1–3 are small).
- Overall assessment: solid, innovative paper with strong theoretical grounding and promising results, recommended for acceptance with minor revisions addressing scalability, evaluation, and clarity.
