This paper presents an interesting approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea – treating correspondences as associated strings without imposing prior distributional assumptions – is a valuable contribution, offering a potentially more objective method for analyzing linguistic similarity compared to existing statistical approaches. The authors demonstrate the method’s efficacy through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages, confirming the link between string-level similarity and linguistic classification, and successfully identifying sub-groups within the analyzed languages. The inclusion of experimental results with varying degrees of data over-weighting offers a practical consideration for handling data limitations and controlling the granularity of the discovered correspondences.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of MDL:** The application of the MDL principle to the problem of discovering language correspondences is a strong point. This provides a statistically grounded framework for identifying the "best" set of rules based on succinctness, circumventing the need for potentially arbitrary assumptions often found in other methods. The explanation of how the MDL principle is applied to this specific problem is clear and well-motivated.

2. **Multi-Language Capability:**  Unlike many existing methods limited to pairwise language comparison, this approach inherently supports analyzing more than two languages simultaneously. This is a significant advantage, enabling more nuanced and comprehensive quantification of linguistic relationships, as demonstrated by the four-language experiment. The demonstration of the algorithm's ability to handle more than two languages is a major strength.

3. **Objective and Deterministic Approach:** The emphasis on deterministic algorithms and avoiding prior assumptions is commendable. This contributes to the objectivity of the results and provides a solid foundation for linguistic analysis. The discussion of data over-weighting provides a controlled way to incorporate linguistic expertise without sacrificing the core principles of the method.

4. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments, including pairwise analysis and the four-language analysis, provide convincing evidence of the approach's effectiveness. The comparison of the generated phylogenetic tree with established linguistic classifications is a good validation strategy. The presentation of example correspondences and the shared description length analysis further enrich the evaluation.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Complexity:** The authors acknowledge the computational bottlenecks related to the algorithm’s complexity, particularly concerning memory consumption and runtime with increasing numbers of languages. While they mention future work to address this, the current limitations restrict the applicability of the method to relatively small datasets and a limited number of languages.  A more detailed discussion of the scalability challenges, and potential strategies beyond mentioning future work (e.g., approximation algorithms, parallelization), would be helpful.

2. **Data Dependency and Cognate Selection:** The reliance on cognates (Swadesh lists and compiled pan-Slavic words) as input data introduces a potential bias. The quality and representativeness of these cognates directly impact the results. A discussion on the sensitivity of the method to cognate selection, and potential strategies to mitigate bias from imperfect data, is missing. A clear statement regarding the criteria used to select cognates is needed.

3. **Limited Explanation of Rule Interpretation:**  While the authors present example correspondences, the linguistic interpretation of these rules is not fully explored. It would be beneficial to provide a more detailed discussion of *why* certain rules are discovered and their potential implications for linguistic theory.  Connecting the discovered rules to established linguistic phenomena (e.g., sound changes, morphological processes) would strengthen the paper. The paper mentions the possibility of exploring this in future work, but some preliminary discussion would be valuable.

4. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper primarily focuses on presenting the novel approach without a thorough comparison to established methods for identifying language correspondences (e.g., those based on probabilistic models or alignment algorithms).  A benchmark comparison, even on a small scale, would demonstrate the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method relative to the state-of-the-art.




**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the criteria used for selecting cognates in your datasets? How sensitive are your results to different cognate sets?

2.  What specific strategies are you planning to implement to address the computational complexity limitations, particularly for scaling to a larger number of languages? Could you provide a rough estimate of the expected performance improvement with these strategies?

3. Can you provide a more in-depth linguistic interpretation of the discovered correspondences (e.g., Table 3)? Are there any surprising or unexpected rules that challenge existing linguistic theories?

4. Have you considered comparing your method to existing approaches for identifying language correspondences, such as those based on probabilistic models or alignment algorithms? If so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation is generally clear, but the definition of sDL could be made more concise.
*  The discussion of data over-weighting is interesting, but it could be further clarified with examples illustrating how different weighting schemes affect the resulting correspondences.
* Figure 1 is somewhat difficult to interpret due to the dense layout of the tree. Consider alternative visualizations.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and innovative paper that introduces a promising approach to discovering language correspondences. The strengths of the paper – the novel application of MDL, the multi-language capability, and the objective nature of the method – outweigh the weaknesses. While the computational limitations and lack of comparison to existing methods are concerns, they do not fundamentally invalidate the contributions of the work. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses and questions. The paper has the potential to significantly impact the field of computational linguistics and historical linguistics.
