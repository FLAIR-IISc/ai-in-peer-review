## Peer Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors propose a deterministic algorithm that aims to identify systematic correspondences without imposing pre-defined assumptions about their shape or distribution. They demonstrate the method's efficacy through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages, confirming its ability to reflect linguistic classification and quantify similarity between language subsets. The core idea is to find the most succinct set of correspondence rules that describe the data, leveraging an Expectation-Maximization (EM) approach.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **MDL-based Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence:** The application of the MDL principle to the problem of discovering multi-language correspondences is a solid contribution.  It offers a theoretically grounded and potentially less biased method compared to approaches relying on distributional assumptions.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm & Scalability:** The development of a deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules is valuable. While the current implementation has limitations (discussed in weaknesses), the approach appears scalable and avoids the randomness inherent in many statistical methods, offering reproducibility.
3. **Demonstration on Slavic Languages & Quantitative Analysis:**  The experiments on Slavic languages provide a tangible demonstration of the method's capabilities.  The quantitative analysis through NCDs and shared description lengths offers a new perspective on linguistic relationships.



**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Soundness:** The grounding of the approach in MDL provides a strong theoretical foundation. The avoidance of arbitrary assumptions about the shape or distribution of correspondences is a significant strength, aiming for more objective results.
2. **Novelty of Approach:**  While the use of MDL isn't entirely novel in linguistics (as the authors acknowledge), applying it specifically to discovering correspondences *between multiple languages simultaneously*, and in a deterministic manner, represents a novel contribution.  The focus on finding the most *succinct* description is a compelling goal.
3. **Detailed Algorithm Description:** The paper provides a reasonably detailed description of the algorithm, including the EM steps and the encoding scheme. The explanation of the alignment procedure and the rationale behind initialization are clear.
4. **Compelling Experimental Results:** The experimental results, particularly the phylogenetic tree reconstruction and the NCD values, support the claim that the method can capture meaningful linguistic relationships. The demonstration of nuanced similarity quantification through shared description lengths is interesting.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' line of reasoning. The inclusion of examples (Table 3 and 4) helps illustrate the types of correspondences discovered.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scalability (Current Implementation):** The authors themselves acknowledge limitations in scalability, stating processing of up to five languages takes hours on a standard machine. This restricts the applicability of the method to larger language families and more complex datasets. While the theoretical approach might scale, the current implementation is a bottleneck.
2. **Data Dependency & Limited Data Size:** The analysis is based on relatively small Swadesh lists and a limited set of cognates.  The results are heavily dependent on the quality and coverage of this data. Expanding the dataset would be crucial to assess the robustness of the method.  The reliance on cognate tuples already known to exist presents a potential bias—the algorithm isn’t *discovering* cognates; it’s analyzing correspondences between pre-selected ones.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Baseline Methods:**  The paper lacks a thorough comparison to existing approaches for identifying language correspondences. While the authors critique some of these approaches, they don’t demonstrate a direct performance comparison (e.g., accuracy in identifying known correspondences, computational efficiency).  A comparison to simpler methods (e.g., edit distance) would also be valuable.
4. **Over-Weighting as a Subjective Parameter:** The introduction of data over-weighting, while potentially useful for incorporating linguistic knowledge, re-introduces a degree of subjectivity into what is presented as an objective method. The criteria for choosing the over-weighting factor are not clearly defined.  This diminishes the purely data-driven nature of the approach.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:**  The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the types of errors the algorithm makes and the reasons behind those errors. Investigating cases where the inferred correspondences are linguistically implausible would provide valuable insights.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Scalability Roadmap:**  What specific steps are planned to address the scalability limitations of the current implementation? Could parallelization or algorithmic optimizations significantly improve performance?
2. **Baseline Comparison:**  Could you provide a comparison of your method to at least one relevant baseline approach, such as a method based on edit distance or a probabilistic model for cognate identification? What metrics would be used for comparison?
3. **Data Expansion Plans:** Do you have plans to expand the dataset used in your experiments? If so, what are the sources of new data, and how will you ensure its quality and consistency?
4. **Over-Weighting Justification:** What objective criteria could be used to determine the appropriate level of data over-weighting? Could you provide examples of how linguistic knowledge influences the choice of over-weighting factors?
5. **Error Analysis Examples:**  Could you provide specific examples of instances where the algorithm produced incorrect or linguistically implausible correspondences, and discuss the potential reasons for these errors?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference to Likomanova (2004) and Angelov (2004) could be expanded to provide more detail about the data source.
*   The explanation of the phylogenetic tree generation process (using neighbor joining) could be slightly more detailed.
*   Consider adding a table showing some example alignments for the four-way analysis (CS-PL-RU-BG).




Overall, this is a promising paper with a novel and theoretically sound approach. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly the scalability concerns and the lack of baseline comparison, would significantly strengthen the submission.
