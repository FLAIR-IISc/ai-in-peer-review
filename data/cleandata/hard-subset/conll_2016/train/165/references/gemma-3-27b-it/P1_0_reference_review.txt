## Peer Review: Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering systematic correspondences between languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors argue that existing statistical methods often impose limiting assumptions, and propose a deterministic algorithm that learns correspondence rules directly from data without prior assumptions about their shape or distribution. The method is evaluated on Slavic languages, demonstrating its ability to quantify string-level similarities and identify linguistic subgroups.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of MDL to multilingual correspondence discovery:** The core contribution lies in framing the problem of finding language correspondences as an MDL problem. This provides a principled and theoretically sound foundation for the approach, moving away from ad-hoc heuristics.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm for Correspondence Learning:** The proposed EM-based algorithm offers a practical and deterministic way to infer correspondence rules. This contrasts with many statistical approaches that rely on probabilistic models and sampling.
3. **Demonstration of Scalability Beyond Pairwise Comparisons:** The paper successfully demonstrates the ability to analyze similarities between more than two languages simultaneously, opening up possibilities for more comprehensive linguistic analysis which are not readily available with existing techniques.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Grounding:**  The use of MDL provides a solid statistical foundation for the proposed method.  The explanation of how MDL is applied – particularly the two-part code and the encoding scheme – is well-articulated and demonstrates a clear understanding of the principle.  This is a significant advantage over methods relying on arbitrary similarity metrics.
2. **Deterministic Nature and Interpretability:**  The deterministic algorithm is a major strength.  It allows for repeatable results and facilitates the examination of *why* certain correspondences are discovered. This interpretability is crucial for linguistic analysis, where understanding the rationale behind observed patterns is paramount.
3. **Scalability to Multiple Languages:** The method’s ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a significant advancement.  Pairwise comparisons often fail to capture the full complexity of linguistic relationships, and this work addresses that limitation. The results on the four-way CS-PL-RU-BG analysis are particularly compelling.
4. **Clear Presentation of Results:**  The presentation of results, including the NCD-based phylogenetic tree and example correspondences, is clear and informative. The inclusion of results with and without data over-weighting is also valuable for demonstrating the flexibility of the approach.
5. **Well-Defined Problem Formulation:** The authors clearly articulate the problem they are addressing and the limitations of existing approaches.  This sets the stage for a compelling justification of their work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Evaluation Data:**  The experiments are primarily based on Swadesh lists. While a good starting point, these lists are known to have limitations and may not be representative of the full complexity of natural language.  The reliance on orthographic form only, without phonetic or morphological analysis, could also limit the accuracy and scope of the discovered correspondences.
2. **Computational Complexity Concerns:** While the authors mention the ability to process up to five languages, the description of the computational bottlenecks (memory consumption) suggests that scaling to larger datasets or more languages could be a significant challenge. A more detailed analysis of the algorithm's time and space complexity would be beneficial.  The comment regarding needing a few hours on a single core machine is concerning.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper lacks a direct comparison to existing statistical approaches for correspondence discovery. While the authors motivate their work by highlighting the limitations of these methods, a quantitative comparison would strengthen the claims of superiority. How does this approach perform against, for example, the methods mentioned in the introduction (Schulz et al., Wettig et al., Snyder & Barzilay)?
4. **Subjectivity in Data Over-Weighting:** The introduction of data over-weighting, while potentially useful for incorporating linguistic knowledge, introduces a degree of subjectivity that is at odds with the goal of a purely objective, data-driven approach. The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs involved and guidelines for choosing appropriate over-weighting parameters.
5. **Limited Discussion of False Positives:** The paper doesn’t explicitly address the potential for discovering spurious correspondences. While MDL aims to minimize description length, there is always a risk of overfitting or identifying patterns that are purely coincidental.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Scalability:** Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the algorithm’s time and space complexity as a function of the number of languages (N) and the size of the data (D)? What optimizations are planned to address the computational bottlenecks?
2. **Comparison to Existing Methods:** Could you provide a brief comparison of the performance of your method to other relevant approaches (e.g., Schulz et al., Wettig et al., Snyder & Barzilay) on the same dataset? Even a qualitative comparison would be helpful.
3. **Data Over-Weighting Justification:**  What criteria or guidelines can be used to determine an appropriate level of data over-weighting? Is there a way to automate this process or to evaluate the impact of different over-weighting parameters?
4. **False Positive Control:** How does the MDL framework help to mitigate the risk of discovering spurious correspondences? Are there any strategies for identifying and filtering out potentially false positives?
5. **Phonetic/Morphological Features:**  Have you considered incorporating phonetic or morphological features into the model?  How might this improve the accuracy and interpretability of the discovered correspondences?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly structured.
*   The references are relevant and up-to-date.
*   The use of figures and tables is effective in presenting the results.
*   Consider expanding the discussion of related work to include more recent advances in computational historical linguistics.
*  It would be helpful to provide a publicly available implementation of the algorithm to facilitate reproducibility and further research.



Overall, this is a promising and well-motivated work that presents a novel approach to a challenging problem. While there are some weaknesses that need to be addressed, the strengths of the paper – particularly the theoretical grounding, deterministic nature, and scalability – make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
