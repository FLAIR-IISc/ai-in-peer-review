This paper presents an interesting approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors aim to overcome limitations of existing statistical methods by avoiding arbitrary assumptions about the shape or distribution of correspondences and by allowing for the analysis of more than two languages simultaneously. The core contribution lies in the development of a deterministic algorithm based on MDL to infer these correspondences directly from data. The application to Slavic languages, coupled with the quantitative analysis of similarity, demonstrates the potential of the approach.

**Contributions:**

1. **MDL-based Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The most significant contribution is the novel application of the MDL principle to the problem of discovering correspondences between multiple languages. This provides a statistically grounded, unbiased approach that avoids imposing prior assumptions on the form of the correspondences.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm for Correspondence Inference:**  The authors present an efficient, deterministic algorithm that leverages Expectation-Maximization (EM) to infer sets of correspondence rules. This is a substantial achievement, offering a clear and reproducible methodology.
3. **Empirical Validation on Slavic Languages & Quantification of Similarity:**  The application of the method to Slavic languages, including the generation of a phylogenetic tree and detailed quantification of similarity using Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) and shared Description Lengths (sDL), provides strong empirical evidence of the approach’s efficacy. The analysis reveals insightful linguistic patterns and demonstrates the utility of the sDL metric.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The use of MDL for this specific task is genuinely novel.  Many existing approaches rely on probabilistic models or pre-defined alignment algorithms. The MDL framework offers a theoretically sound alternative.
2. **Determinism and Reproducibility:** The deterministic nature of the algorithm is a major strength.  It ensures reproducibility and facilitates rigorous evaluation, contrasting with stochastic methods where results can vary.
3. **Scalability to Multiple Languages:** The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously is a significant advantage over many existing methods. This allows for a more nuanced understanding of linguistic relationships. The demonstration with four languages is promising.
4. **Informative Quantitative Analysis:** The use of NCD and the newly introduced sDL provides valuable quantitative measures of linguistic similarity. The sDL metric, in particular, offers an insightful way to quantify shared structures among languages.
5. **Clear Presentation and Well-Structured Experimentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The experiments are clearly described, and the results are presented in a logical manner, accompanied by helpful tables and figures.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Data Size & Representativeness:** The experiments rely on Swadesh lists and a relatively small set of cognates. While this provides a starting point, the limited data size may affect the robustness and generalizability of the findings. It is unclear how well this method would perform on larger, more diverse datasets, or on languages with more complex morphological structures.
2. **Data Preprocessing & Orthographic Variations:** The use of raw orthographic forms without transcription might introduce noise and inconsistencies.  Different orthographic conventions across languages can lead to spurious discrepancies in the analysis. The impact of these variations is not adequately addressed.
3. **Complexity of the Alignment Procedure:** While the algorithm is deterministic, the description of the alignment procedure as a shortest-path problem in a weighted graph suggests potential computational complexity issues.  The authors acknowledge limitations in processing power but further details regarding scalability bottlenecks would be beneficial. A more detailed discussion of the graph size and algorithmic efficiency would strengthen the paper.
4. **Interpretation of Results & Linguistic Significance:** While the results are presented clearly, the paper could benefit from a more in-depth discussion of the *linguistic* significance of the discovered correspondences.  Connecting the observed patterns to established linguistic theories would increase the impact of the work. For example, relating specific correspondences to sound changes or historical linguistic relationships.
5. **Over-Weighting Data – Subjectivity:**  The introduction of data over-weighting, while presented as a way to incorporate linguistic expertise, introduces a degree of subjectivity. The criteria for choosing the over-weighting factor are not fully explained, and the potential bias introduced by this process should be discussed more thoroughly.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. How does the performance of the algorithm scale with the number of languages and the size of the dataset? Could you provide more detailed benchmarks on computational resources (memory, runtime) for larger datasets?
2. What is the sensitivity of the results to variations in the input data?  Have you investigated the impact of different data sources or preprocessing steps (e.g., stemming, lemmatization, phonetic transcription)?
3. Could you elaborate on the linguistic rationale behind the data over-weighting technique? What specific linguistic insights or expert knowledge inform the choice of the over-weighting factor?
4.  How does the approach compare to existing methods (e.g., those based on cognate identification or probabilistic models) in terms of accuracy, efficiency, and interpretability, particularly on languages *beyond* the Slavic family?
5. What mechanisms are in place to deal with cognates that have undergone significant sound changes or morphological alterations between languages?  Does the algorithm incorporate any form of sound correspondence rules or morphological analysis to account for these variations?

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list appears incomplete.
* Figure 1 could benefit from a more informative caption explaining the tree construction method and the interpretation of branch lengths.
* Consider providing a brief example illustrating the application of the algorithm to a specific linguistic problem beyond the general assessment of language similarity.



Overall, this is a promising and well-executed paper that presents a novel and valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics. The strengths of the approach outweigh the weaknesses, and the authors have demonstrated its potential through a compelling set of experiments. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance contingent upon addressing the reviewer’s questions and concerns.
