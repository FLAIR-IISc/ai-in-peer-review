The paper proposes a Minimum Description Length (MDL) approach for discovering correspondences between multiple languages, aiming to overcome limitations of existing statistical methods that often rely on pre-defined assumptions. The core contribution is an efficient, deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) to infer these correspondences directly from data, applied to Slavic languages. A secondary contribution is the application of this method to phylogenetic analysis and quantification of string-level similarity across multiple languages, demonstrating its practical utility. A tertiary, though less substantial, contribution is the demonstration of data over-weighting as a mechanism to control the granularity of discovered correspondences.

**Strengths:**

1. **Formal Rigor:** The paper provides a detailed, mathematically grounded formulation of the problem using the MDL principle. The two-part MDL code is well-defined and attempts a comprehensive description length calculation, addressing both model and data complexity. This provides a solid theoretical basis for the approach.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The development of a deterministic algorithm is commendable. Deterministic approaches offer reproducibility and can be easier to interpret than stochastic methods, especially in linguistic analyses where transparency is crucial. The EM framework is a standard approach for this type of problem, and the described updates seem logically sound.
3. **Multi-Language Capability:** The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously is a genuine advantage over many existing methods. This enables a more nuanced understanding of linguistic relationships and the identification of subgroups, as demonstrated in the experiments.
4. **Clear Presentation of Concepts:** The introduction and the sections on approach and MDL are reasonably well-written and explain the core ideas effectively. The explanations of the EM steps and the encoding scheme are detailed and show a clear understanding of the underlying principles.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Comparative Evaluation:** While the paper demonstrates the method on Slavic languages and provides a phylogenetic tree, it lacks a robust comparative evaluation against existing state-of-the-art methods for cognate identification or language relatedness. The authors mention limitations of previous approaches but do not present a head-to-head comparison, making it difficult to assess the actual improvements offered by their method. Simply stating that their method "confirms" results obtained through other methods is insufficient.
2. **Computational Scalability Concerns:** The authors acknowledge that the algorithm's performance is limited to a small number of languages (up to five) due to computational complexity, specifically memory consumption. This severely restricts the practical applicability of the method to larger language families or datasets. While future work is mentioned, the current limitations are substantial. The bottleneck in memory consumption should be more thoroughly investigated and addressed.
3. **Overly Detailed and Sometimes Redundant Presentation:** The paper is excessively verbose and contains numerous details that detract from the core message. For example, the extensive description of the code lengths for individual symbols (Section 4.1 and 4.2) feels overly pedantic and doesn’t significantly contribute to understanding the overall approach. The repetitive justifications for design choices feel like padding.
4. **Insufficient Exploration of Parameter Sensitivity:** The effect of data over-weighting is shown, but the paper does not provide a systematic exploration of how different weighting schemes impact the discovered correspondences. The choice of the weighting parameter appears arbitrary and lacks justification. A more thorough sensitivity analysis is needed to understand the robustness of the method.
5. **Data Set Limitations:** The use of Swadesh lists, while common, introduces biases. Swadesh lists represent a limited vocabulary and may not accurately capture the full complexity of linguistic relationships. Furthermore, the small size of the dataset (even after filtering for complete cognates) might limit the statistical power of the analysis.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a quantitative comparison of your method's performance (e.g., precision, recall, F1-score) against established methods for cognate identification or language relatedness on a standard benchmark dataset?
2.  What specific optimizations are planned to address the computational scalability issues, particularly the memory consumption bottleneck? Can you provide estimated scalability improvements based on planned changes?
3.  What criteria were used to select the data over-weighting parameter? Can you demonstrate how different weighting values affect the discovered correspondences and the resulting linguistic analysis?
4.  Have you considered using more diverse and larger datasets beyond Swadesh lists to evaluate the robustness of your approach? If so, what were the results?
5.  Given the deterministic nature of the algorithm, how do you address the potential for getting stuck in local optima during the EM process? What strategies are employed to ensure convergence to a meaningful solution?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting is inconsistent in places (e.g., some references are abbreviated differently).
*   Several phrases feel unnecessarily convoluted or repetitive. Streamlining the writing would improve clarity.
*   The figure captions could be more descriptive.




Overall, while the paper presents a theoretically sound and deterministic approach to discovering linguistic correspondences, its lack of comparative evaluation, computational limitations, and overly detailed presentation raise significant concerns. The potential contribution is diminished by these issues. **Recommendation: Reject.** The authors should significantly revise the paper, addressing the weaknesses outlined above, before resubmitting. A crucial aspect will be demonstrating the method’s superiority over existing approaches with a rigorous and quantitative evaluation.
