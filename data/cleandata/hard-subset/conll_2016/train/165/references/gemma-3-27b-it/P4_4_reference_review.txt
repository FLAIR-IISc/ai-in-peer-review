This paper presents an approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to identify the set of correspondence rules that most succinctly describes the given data, without imposing prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of these rules. The authors propose a deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) to infer these rules and apply it to Slavic languages, demonstrating its efficacy in phylogenetic analysis and quantifying linguistic similarity.

The main contributions of the work, as I see them, are:

1. **A principled, data-driven approach to multi-lingual correspondence discovery:** The use of MDL provides a statistically sound framework for identifying correspondences without relying on predefined assumptions, a significant advantage over existing statistical methods. This is a strong contribution, addressing a known limitation of prior work.
2. **An efficient, deterministic algorithm for correspondence inference:** The EM-based algorithm, coupled with partial order reduction for computational efficiency, allows the practical application of MDL to this problem. The authors explicitly address computational bottlenecks and outline avenues for scalability.
3. **Demonstration of the method on Slavic languages with a novel analysis of similarity:** The experiments on Slavic languages provide a compelling demonstration of the approach’s ability to uncover linguistically meaningful correspondences and quantify similarities, including the identification of language subgroups and a more nuanced assessment of relationships than pairwise comparisons allow.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding of the approach in MDL is a strong point. It provides a clear and justifiable criterion for model selection, avoiding arbitrary choices. The authors correctly position MDL as a statistically well-founded approach and draw connections to coding theory.
2. **Novelty in Multi-lingual Analysis:** The ability to simultaneously analyze correspondences across multiple languages (more than two) is a clear novelty.  Most existing methods are limited to pairwise comparisons. This is particularly valuable for languages with complex historical relationships.
3. **Practical Algorithm and Implementation:** The authors don’t just propose a theoretical framework; they provide a concrete algorithm and discuss implementation details, including optimization strategies. The reported performance on the tested data sets is encouraging. The focus on deterministic algorithms also avoids issues related to randomness.
4. **Detailed Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and provide valuable insights. The use of NCD for phylogenetic tree construction and the comparison with linguistic classifications are strong validation points. The analysis of shared description lengths offers a novel way to quantify similarity between language subsets.
5. **Clear Presentation and Motivation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates the motivation for the work, the limitations of existing approaches, and the contributions of the proposed method. The inclusion of example correspondences further clarifies the findings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Data Size and Scope:** The experiments are currently limited to Slavic languages. While this provides a focused case study, it doesn't demonstrate the generalizability of the approach to other language families with different characteristics. A broader evaluation with more diverse languages is needed.
2. **Data Preprocessing and Representation:** The use of raw orthographic form without any phonetic transcription or normalization might limit the accuracy of the discovered correspondences. Orthographic variations can obscure underlying phonetic similarities. The reliance on Swadesh lists, while standard, might introduce biases related to the types of words selected.
3. **Over-weighting Parameter Tuning:** While the concept of data over-weighting is interesting, the paper doesn't provide clear guidelines on how to choose the optimal weighting factor. A more systematic investigation of the impact of different weighting factors on the results would be beneficial.
4. **Computational Complexity and Scalability:** While the authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks, the reported scalability (up to five languages) is still relatively limited. More detailed analysis of the computational complexity and exploration of further optimization techniques would strengthen the paper.
5. **Limited Discussion of Linguistic Significance:** While the authors present example correspondences, the paper lacks a deeper linguistic analysis of the discovered rules. A more in-depth discussion of the linguistic motivations and implications of the findings would enhance the value of the work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the choice of data set (e.g., different Swadesh list versions, inclusion of additional cognates)?
2.  Could you provide more detail on the limitations of the current implementation regarding the number of languages that can be processed and the data set size? What specific steps are being taken to address these limitations?
3.  What criteria were used to determine the appropriate data over-weighting factor in the experiments? Can you provide a sensitivity analysis of the results to different weighting values?
4. Could you elaborate on the potential for incorporating phonetic information into the model to improve the accuracy of the discovered correspondences?
5. Do you have plans to apply the algorithm to other language families? What challenges do you anticipate in adapting the approach to languages with different linguistic properties (e.g., tonal languages, languages with different writing systems)?

**Additional Comments:**

* The table and figure references throughout the text are well-maintained.
* The discussion of related work is adequate but could be expanded to include more recent advances in computational historical linguistics.
* Consider adding a section discussing potential applications of the discovered correspondences, such as machine translation or language learning.
