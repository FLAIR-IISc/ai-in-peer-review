This paper presents an approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to identify the set of correspondence rules that most succinctly describes the data, avoiding arbitrary assumptions about the shape or distribution of these rules. The authors introduce a deterministic algorithm to infer these rules and demonstrate its efficacy through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages, confirming the relationship between string-level similarity and linguistic classification, and successfully identifying linguistic subgroups.

**Contributions:**

1. **MDL-Based Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution lies in framing the problem of finding language correspondences within the MDL framework. This provides a statistically grounded and objective approach, avoiding the need for pre-defined distributions or assumptions about the nature of the correspondences. This is a significant shift from many existing statistical methods.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm for Rule Inference:** The authors present a deterministic algorithm, based on Expectation-Maximization (EM), for inferring the correspondence rules. This determinism is a key feature, allowing for reproducibility and potentially easier integration of linguistic knowledge. The algorithm’s design, leveraging partial order reduction for efficiency, is also a noteworthy aspect.
3. **Scalability to Multiple Languages and Detailed Quantification of Similarity:** Unlike many existing methods limited to pairwise comparisons, this approach naturally extends to more than two languages. This enables a more nuanced and detailed quantification of linguistic similarity, illustrated through the analysis of four Slavic languages and the insightful use of Shared Description Length (sDL) to represent relationships between language subsets.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of MDL:** Applying the MDL principle to the problem of language correspondence discovery is a novel and potentially powerful approach. The justification for using MDL – aiming for the simplest explanation of the data – is well-articulated and logically sound.
2. **Clear and Well-Defined Algorithm:** The proposed EM-based algorithm is described clearly and comprehensively. The explanation of the expectation and maximization steps, along with the use of a null alignment for initialization, is easy to follow. The discussion of computational complexity provides valuable insight into the algorithm's scalability.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments presented are well-designed and provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the approach. The use of both pairwise phylogenetic analysis and the four-way analysis demonstrates the versatility of the method.  The comparison to linguistic classifications adds external validation.
4. **Insightful Analysis with sDL:** The introduction and use of the Shared Description Length (sDL) metric is a strong point. This metric provides a quantifiable measure of shared structure between languages, allowing for a nuanced understanding of linguistic relationships beyond simple pairwise comparisons. The visualizations of sDL values are particularly helpful.
5. **Deterministic Approach:** The emphasis on a deterministic approach distinguishes this work. This is valuable for reproducibility and can provide a stable foundation for further research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Dependency and Limited Dataset Size:** The results are heavily dependent on the quality and quantity of the input data. The use of Swadesh lists, while common, has limitations. The relatively small size of the datasets (even for the four-way analysis) might limit the generalizability of the findings. While the authors acknowledge this, it warrants further investigation with larger and more diverse datasets.
2. **Orthographic Focus & Lack of Phonetic Information:** The approach relies solely on orthographic forms, ignoring phonetic information. This could lead to overlooking subtle but important correspondences that are not apparent in the written form. Including phonetic transcriptions could potentially improve the accuracy and robustness of the method.
3. **Computational Bottlenecks:** While the algorithm is designed to be efficient, the authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks, particularly with larger numbers of languages. This limits the practical applicability of the method to significantly larger language families without further optimization.
4. **Over-Weighting Subjectivity:** The introduction of data over-weighting, while providing flexibility, re-introduces subjectivity into the process, undermining the initial claim of a fully objective approach. The criteria for determining the appropriate level of over-weighting are not clearly defined, and could influence the results.
5. **Limited Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper lacks a detailed comparison to other existing methods for language correspondence discovery. A more thorough comparison, including quantitative benchmarks, would strengthen the claim of novelty and demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the initial null alignment? Have you experimented with different initialization strategies?
2. Can you elaborate on the specific criteria used for determining the level of data over-weighting?  What guidelines would you provide to a user applying this technique?
3. What are the primary computational bottlenecks in the algorithm, and what strategies are being considered to address them (e.g., parallelization, approximation algorithms)?
4. How would the approach be affected by incorporating phonetic information alongside the orthographic forms? Could you provide a preliminary analysis or discussion of this possibility?
5. Could you provide a more detailed comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods in language correspondence discovery, perhaps including some quantitative benchmarks on a common dataset?

**Additional Comments:**

* The notation is generally clear, but could benefit from more consistent use of subscripts and superscripts.
* The description of the encoding scheme in Section 4 is quite detailed, but could be slightly more concise.
* Providing examples of alignments with more complex linguistic phenomena (e.g., irregular correspondences) could further illustrate the capabilities of the approach.
