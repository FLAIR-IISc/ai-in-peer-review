- Paper applies the MDL principle to discover correspondences across more than two languages, avoiding strong prior assumptions about rule shapes.
- Authors present a deterministic, reproducible algorithm to infer correspondence rules directly from data.
- Experiments provide detailed, information-theoretic quantification of linguistic similarity, including four-way analyses and phylogenetic reconstruction.
- Novel use of MDL for multi-language correspondence discovery distinguishes this work from statistical or heuristic approaches.
- Strong theoretical grounding in MDL, coding theory, and information theory with clear explanations.
- Promising empirical results: plausible correspondences, a phylogenetic tree aligning with known classifications, and insightful sDL analyses.
- Determinism and transparency of the algorithm facilitate interpretability and controlled weighting for linguistic refinement.
- Method handles more than two languages simultaneously, allowing richer linguistic relationship modeling.
- Scalability is limited (current implementation up to ~5 languages) due to computational complexity in the alignment/shortest-path search.
- Results rely on small Swadesh lists, raising concerns about dataset size, scope, and generalizability to broader textual data.
- Lack of direct comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods (e.g., ASDiT, Bayesian phylogenetics) limits assessment of relative performance.
- Large number and variable granularity of learned rules can hinder interpretation; more systematic analysis of rule types is needed.
- Question: How will scalability be improved and what performance gains are expected, specifically for the alignment step?
- Question: Are there plans to evaluate on larger, more diverse datasets beyond Swadesh lists, and how might results change?
- Question: Can you provide comparative evaluations against established methods to clarify strengths and weaknesses?
- Question: How will you facilitate interpretation of learned rules—metrics, visualizations, or summaries?
- Additional: Paper is well-written, examples effectively illustrate results, and discussion of data over-weighting is insightful.
- Overall: Promising, well-founded contribution with encouraging results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing scalability, data, comparisons, and interpretability.
