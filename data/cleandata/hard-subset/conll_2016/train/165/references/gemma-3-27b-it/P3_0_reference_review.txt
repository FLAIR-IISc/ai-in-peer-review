## Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors argue that existing methods often rely on assumptions about the distribution of correspondences and propose a deterministic algorithm that learns rules directly from data without such assumptions. They demonstrate their approach on Slavic languages, performing both pairwise and four-way analyses, showing its ability to identify linguistic subgroups and quantify similarities.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A principled, assumption-minimal framework for multi-way correspondence discovery:** The core contribution is the application of MDL to the problem of linguistic correspondence, moving away from methods reliant on pre-defined distributions or alignment models. This allows for the potentially unbiased discovery of relationships.
2. **A deterministic and efficient algorithm:** The authors provide a concrete algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) to implement the MDL approach. The focus on determinism is a key feature, offering reproducibility and interpretability.
3. **Demonstration on Slavic languages with detailed analysis:** The experiments on Slavic languages are not merely illustrative; they provide a substantive analysis, including phylogenetic trees and detailed examples of learned correspondences, showing the practical utility of the method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of the MDL approach:** Applying MDL to this problem space is genuinely novel. While MDL has been used in related areas (etymological reconstruction), its application to *discovering* correspondences across multiple languages without prior assumptions is a significant contribution. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches in this regard.
2. **Sound theoretical foundation:** The paper is well-grounded in information theory and provides a clear and mathematically rigorous explanation of the MDL framework and its application to the problem. The connection to coding theory is explicitly made, and the two-part MDL formulation is appropriate.
3. **Practical Algorithm and Implementation:** The EM algorithm proposed is a clever solution to the problem of finding optimal alignments and rule costs. The authors acknowledge computational limitations but demonstrate its feasibility on reasonably sized datasets.  The implementation details, including the handling of model complexity and alignment procedures, are clearly explained.
4. **Detailed Experimental Validation:** The experiments are thorough and provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the approach. The pairwise analysis with NCD calculation and phylogenetic tree construction are well-executed. The four-way analysis is particularly valuable, as it demonstrates the method’s scalability beyond pairwise comparisons and provides a richer picture of linguistic relationships. The example correspondences are insightful.
5. **Clear Presentation and Structure:**  The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The authors clearly define their terminology and methodology, making the paper accessible to readers with a background in linguistics and/or information theory.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Data:** While the Slavic language dataset is a good starting point, the relatively limited size (even in the four-way analysis) and the use of Swadesh lists (which are known to be problematic due to semantic issues and potential for borrowing) raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.  The reliance on cognates rather than broader textual data limits the richness of potential correspondences discovered.
2. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper doesn’t provide a direct, quantitative comparison to existing statistical approaches for discovering correspondences. While the authors criticize these approaches in the introduction, a head-to-head comparison would strengthen their claims of superiority.  Demonstrating improved performance on the same dataset would be highly impactful.
3. **Interpretation of “Objective” Correspondences:** The authors emphasize the objectivity of the MDL approach. However, the choice of data (cognates) itself introduces a level of subjectivity. While unavoidable, acknowledging this and discussing its potential impact on the results is important. The data over-weighting technique, while presented as a tool for linguistic expertise, also introduces a subjective element.
4. **Computational Scalability:** The authors acknowledge the computational bottlenecks, but this remains a significant limitation. Processing only up to five languages limits the applicability of the method to larger-scale linguistic analyses.  Further optimization or parallelization strategies would be needed for broader application.
5. **Over-reliance on Orthography:** The method, as presented, focuses solely on orthographic similarity. While orthography reflects underlying phonetic and morphological relationships, it can also be misleading due to historical spelling reforms or inconsistencies. Incorporating phonetic or morphological information could improve the accuracy and robustness of the approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Comparison to existing methods:** Could you provide a quantitative comparison of your method to at least one relevant existing approach (e.g., a cognate identification method followed by statistical alignment) on the same Slavic language dataset?  Specifically, how does your method perform in terms of precision, recall, and F1-score for identifying correct correspondences?
2. **Data Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the learned correspondences to variations in the input data? Have you experimented with different data sources (e.g., parallel corpora, historical texts) or different methods of cognate selection?
3. **Morphological Information:** Have you considered incorporating morphological information (e.g., stems, affixes) into the correspondence rules? How would this affect the complexity of the model and the computational efficiency of the algorithm?
4. **Data Over-weighting:** What criteria or guidelines do you recommend for selecting the appropriate level of data over-weighting? How can a linguist determine the optimal balance between objectivity and desired level of detail?
5. **Scalability:** What specific optimizations or parallelization strategies are you planning to implement to address the computational scalability limitations of the algorithm?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation is generally clear, but a glossary of symbols would be helpful.
*   The reference to “internationalisms and pan-Slavic words” in the data sets could be more specific – detailing the source and selection criteria would strengthen the reproducibility of the experiments.
*   Figure 1 could benefit from a clearer visual representation of branch lengths, reflecting the NCD distances.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a novel and theoretically sound approach to a challenging problem in computational linguistics. While there are some weaknesses related to data scope, comparisons to existing methods, and computational scalability, the contributions are significant and the experimental results are compelling. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses and questions. The potential of this work to advance our understanding of linguistic relationships and improve multilingual NLP applications is substantial.
