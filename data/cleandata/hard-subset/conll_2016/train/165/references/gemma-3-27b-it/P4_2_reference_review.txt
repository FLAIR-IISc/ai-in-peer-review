This paper presents an approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to identify the set of correspondence rules that most succinctly describes the observed data, avoiding distributional assumptions common in existing statistical approaches. The authors propose a deterministic algorithm for inferring these rules and demonstrate its effectiveness through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages, showcasing both pairwise comparisons and analysis of four languages simultaneously.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **MDL-based Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The most significant contribution is the application of the MDL principle to the problem of discovering language correspondences without imposing prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of those correspondences. This provides a theoretically sound and objective approach to a long-standing linguistic challenge.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm for Rule Inference:** The development of a deterministic algorithm based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) to infer correspondence rules is a substantial contribution. This algorithm addresses the combinatorial challenges of finding optimal alignments and offers a reproducible and interpretable method.
3. **Scalable Analysis Beyond Pairwise Comparisons:** The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously, and to quantify similarities between all subsets of those languages, expands the capabilities of existing approaches and opens up new avenues for linguistic investigation. This is particularly highlighted in the four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian.

**Strengths:**

1. **Theoretical Foundation:** The use of MDL provides a strong theoretical underpinning for the proposed approach, offering a principled way to balance model complexity and data fit. This addresses a key weakness of many existing methods that rely on arbitrary assumptions.
2. **Novelty of the Approach:**  Applying MDL to this problem, coupled with the deterministic algorithm, distinguishes this work from existing statistical methods. The focus on discovering *actual* distributions of correspondence rules, rather than imposing them, is a noteworthy departure.
3. **Clear Experimental Validation:** The experiments with Slavic languages are well-designed and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The comparison to linguistic classifications via phylogenetic trees provides compelling evidence of the approach's validity.
4. **Detailed Analysis and Insights:** The paper provides a thorough analysis of the learned correspondences, including examples and discussion of their linguistic implications. The exploration of shared description lengths offers a nuanced understanding of relationships between languages.
5. **Practical Algorithm:** The proposed algorithm appears to be computationally feasible, capable of processing up to five languages within a reasonable timeframe, as reported. The discussion of potential optimizations further strengthens this point.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Limitations:** The reliance on Swadesh lists, while a common starting point, limits the scope of the analysis. Swadesh lists are known to be susceptible to semantic and phonetic shifts and might not capture the full complexity of language relationships. The authors acknowledge this implicitly, but its impact on the generalizability of the findings should be more explicitly addressed.
2. **Orthographic Focus:** The exclusive use of raw orthographic forms, without phonetic transcriptions, might obscure underlying phonetic correspondences. While the authors state this is a deliberate choice, it could limit the depth of the analysis and potentially lead to spurious results.
3. **Limited Comparative Evaluation:** The paper primarily focuses on demonstrating the internal consistency and plausibility of the results. A more comprehensive comparison against established methods, even those with limitations, would strengthen the claims of superiority.  Quantitative metrics beyond NCD (e.g., accuracy against a gold standard if available) would be beneficial.
4. **Complexity of Interpretation:** The large number of learned rules and the detailed nature of the analysis can make it challenging to extract high-level insights. A more streamlined presentation of the key findings and a clearer explanation of their linguistic significance would improve the accessibility of the work.
5. **Over-Weighting Subjectivity:** While the introduction of data over-weighting provides flexibility, it also introduces a degree of subjectivity. The authors acknowledge this, but a more rigorous justification for the choice of weighting parameters would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of cognate data?  Did you explore using different datasets or expanding the Swadesh lists with additional cognates?
2.  Could you elaborate on the potential benefits and drawbacks of incorporating phonetic transcriptions into the analysis?  Would it significantly alter the learned correspondences or the overall results?
3.  What specific challenges prevented a more direct comparison with existing statistical methods, and what steps could be taken to address those challenges in future work?
4.  How was the choice of over-weighting factor determined? Was this driven by linguistic intuition, or were there other optimization criteria involved?
5.  The paper mentions future work involving simulated annealing. What specific aspects of the algorithm do you believe would benefit most from a non-deterministic approach?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation is generally clear, but defining the key variables and symbols in a dedicated glossary would improve readability.
*   Figures 1 and 2 could benefit from clearer labeling and more concise captions.
*   The phrase "objective" is used repeatedly. While the MDL approach minimizes assumptions, it's important to acknowledge that data selection and parameter choices still involve subjective decisions.
