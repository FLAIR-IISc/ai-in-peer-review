This paper presents an interesting approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea – treating correspondences as simply associated strings without imposing prior assumptions – is a valuable contribution, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness through experiments on Slavic languages. The work is well-motivated, clearly written, and the results are presented in a compelling manner.

**Contributions:**

1. **An MDL-based Framework for Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is the application of the MDL principle to the problem of discovering string-level correspondences across multiple languages. This provides a statistically grounded and unbiased method compared to approaches that rely on pre-defined assumptions about correspondence distributions.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm for Correspondence Inference:** The development of an efficient, deterministic algorithm to infer correspondence rules from data is a significant achievement. This determinism is a desirable property, particularly for linguistic analyses where reproducibility and interpretability are crucial. The authors explicitly address computational complexity and offer potential avenues for improvement.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Slavic Languages & Quantification of Similarity:** The experimental application to Slavic languages and the resulting quantification of linguistic similarity—going beyond pairwise comparisons—represent a solid validation of the approach. The insights gained regarding the relationships between the languages (e.g., identifying the specific groupings, highlighting differences between Czech and other West Slavic languages) are valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Soundness of Approach:** The use of MDL is a well-justified and theoretically sound approach. The authors clearly articulate why it’s advantageous over methods imposing restrictive assumptions. The explicit avoidance of priors and the focus on data-driven discovery are commendable.
2. **Algorithm Design and Efficiency:** The proposed algorithm, based on Expectation-Maximization, is well-explained, and the discussion of computational complexity demonstrates awareness of practical limitations. The use of partial order reduction and Dijkstra's algorithm are appropriate choices for the problem.
3. **Comprehensive Experiments and Results:** The experiments are well-designed, utilizing both pairwise and four-way analyses. The inclusion of phylogenetic trees and the analysis of shared description lengths provide a rich and insightful view of the relationships between the languages. The demonstration of how to control granularity through data over-weighting adds flexibility to the method.
4. **Clear and Thorough Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The terminology is clearly defined, and the explanations of the concepts are accessible. The inclusion of example alignments is particularly helpful for understanding the results.
5. **Focus on Determinism and Linguistic Interpretability:** The emphasis on a deterministic algorithm and the potential for linguistic interpretation of the discovered rules are strong points, making this work particularly relevant to the linguistic community.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Data Limitations & Representativeness:** The reliance on Swadesh lists, while a standard practice, is a potential limitation. Swadesh lists are known to be sensitive to semantic and cultural factors, and may not fully capture the nuances of linguistic relationships. A broader range of text data might reveal different patterns. It's unclear how well these results generalize to other language families.
2. **Limited Comparison to Existing Methods:** While the paper positions the approach against existing statistical methods, there isn’t a direct empirical comparison to other state-of-the-art techniques for correspondence discovery, particularly those focusing on cognate identification or reconstruction. Demonstrating superior performance against such methods would strengthen the claims.
3. **Over-Weighting Subjectivity:** While the introduction of data over-weighting provides flexibility, it also introduces a degree of subjectivity into the process. The paper acknowledges this, but further discussion on how to systematically choose the over-weighting factor would be beneficial.  A more quantitative justification for the chosen levels of over-weighting would add rigor.
4. **Scalability Concerns:** Despite the efficiency improvements, the algorithm still faces scalability challenges, as noted by the authors. Processing only up to five languages is a limitation, and further optimization will be crucial for applying this method to larger language families. While future work is mentioned, a more concrete discussion of scalability strategies would be helpful.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on showcasing successful correspondences. A more thorough analysis of cases where the algorithm *fails* to identify expected correspondences—or generates incorrect ones—would provide valuable insights and guide future improvements.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of data source (e.g., different types of texts beyond Swadesh lists)? Have you considered using parallel corpora or other forms of linguistic data?
2.  Could you provide a more quantitative justification for the choice of over-weighting factors in your experiments? What criteria did you use to determine the appropriate level of over-weighting?
3.  What specific optimization strategies are you considering to improve the scalability of the algorithm?  Are there any opportunities for parallelization or approximation techniques?
4.  Can you elaborate on the limitations of the MDL approach in cases where data is sparse or noisy? How might these limitations be addressed?
5.  What strategies could be implemented to automatically detect and handle potential errors in the input data (e.g., incorrect cognate assignments)?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures are well-labeled and informative.
*   The references are comprehensive.
*   The clear distinction between the objective MDL approach and the data-overweighting modification is appreciated.
*   The concluding remarks regarding the potential for future linguistic applications are encouraging.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to multi-language correspondence discovery. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work—particularly the theoretical soundness, algorithmic efficiency, and comprehensive experimental validation—outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the questions and suggestions outlined above. The paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the fields of computational linguistics and historical linguistics.
