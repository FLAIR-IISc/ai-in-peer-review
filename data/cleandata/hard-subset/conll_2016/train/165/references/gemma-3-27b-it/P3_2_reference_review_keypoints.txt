- Proposed MDL-based, assumption-minimal method for discovering multi-way language correspondences directly from data.  
- Deterministic EM-based algorithm enabling automated correspondence learning without stochastic search or pre-defined parameters.  
- Demonstrated scalability beyond pairwise comparisons via a four-language experiment showing subgroup discovery and similarity quantification.  

- MDL framework provides strong theoretical justification and connections to compression distance/Bayesian inference.  
- Novel application of MDL to multi-language correspondence discovery, distinct from prior cognate-identification/reconstruction work.  
- Clear, well-detailed algorithmic description including EM steps, initialization, model/data code construction, and complexity discussion.  
- Empirical results on Slavic languages show meaningful linguistic relationships and useful visualizations (NCD-based trees).  
- Data over-weighting introduced as a tunable control for granularity of discovered correspondences.  

- Evaluation limited to small Swadesh lists and a single Slavic family, restricting generalizability.  
- No comparisons to existing baselines or adapted pairwise methods to benchmark performance.  
- Implementation faces memory and runtime bottlenecks, raising scalability concerns for more languages.  
- Data preparation choice (requiring cognates present in all languages) may bias results and exclude useful data.  
- Paper lacks deeper linguistic analysis linking discovered correspondences to known sound changes or historical theory.  

- Request for details on implementation bottlenecks and possible optimizations/parallelization.  
- Ask how results change if cognate inclusion criteria are relaxed or varied.  
- Question why baseline methods were not adapted/compared for multi-way discovery.  
- Seek elaboration on linguistic significance of learned rules and alignment with Slavic historical changes.  
- Inquire about criteria and systematic selection of the data over-weighting factor.  

- Paper is well-written, organized, figures informative; a glossary of symbols would help.  
- References are relevant and up-to-date.  

- Overall recommendation: strong, promising work; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address limited evaluation, baseline comparisons, scalability, and deeper linguistic interpretation.
