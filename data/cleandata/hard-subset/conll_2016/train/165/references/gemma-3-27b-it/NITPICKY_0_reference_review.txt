This paper proposes a Minimum Description Length (MDL) approach for discovering correspondences between multiple languages, aiming to overcome limitations of existing statistical methods that often rely on prior assumptions. The authors present a deterministic algorithm and demonstrate its efficacy through phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages, confirming links between language similarity and linguistic classification, and identifying subgroups within the languages analyzed.

The primary contribution is the application of MDL to multi-lingual correspondence discovery *without* pre-defined assumptions about the shape or distribution of correspondences. While MDL has been used in related areas (etymological modeling), its application to simultaneously analyzing more than two languages and doing so in a deterministic, assumption-free manner is novel. A secondary contribution lies in the presented algorithm itself, particularly the EM-based approach coupled with a merging strategy for refining correspondence rules. Finally, the empirical evaluation using Slavic languages provides a concrete demonstration of the method's ability to uncover linguistic relationships and quantify similarities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The core idea of using MDL to discover correspondences without strong prior assumptions is genuinely interesting and addresses a clear gap in the field. Existing methods often impose constraints that can bias results; this approach attempts to avoid that.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The determinism of the algorithm is a valuable characteristic. It allows for reproducibility and simplifies interpretation of the results. Many statistical methods introduce randomness, making it harder to pinpoint the underlying causes of observed patterns.
3. **Multi-lingual Capability:** The ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously is a significant advantage. This unlocks the possibility of uncovering complex relationships that would be difficult or impossible to detect with pairwise comparisons alone. The detailed quantification of similarity between all subsets of languages is a compelling outcome.
4. **Clear Presentation of Methodology:** The paper lays out the MDL framework and the algorithm in a reasonably clear and detailed manner. The explanation of the alignment procedure and the EM steps are helpful for understanding the technical aspects of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Robustness Evaluation:** The experiments focus exclusively on Slavic languages, which are relatively closely related. The robustness of the method to languages with vastly different structures and origins remains untested. Results on a more diverse language family are crucial to establish the generalizability of the approach.
2. **Computational Scalability Concerns:** The authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks, stating the current implementation can only process up to five languages. This severely limits the applicability of the method to larger language datasets and real-world scenarios. While they mention plans for improvement, this is a significant limitation in its current form.
3. **Overly Detailed Description, Insufficient Critical Analysis:** The paper is *excessively* detailed in its mathematical formalization, bordering on tedious. While rigor is appreciated, a more concise presentation focusing on the core concepts would improve readability. More critically, there's a lack of critical self-assessment. The authors consistently present the results as conclusive without acknowledging potential limitations or alternative interpretations. The discussion around data weighting, for example, feels glossed over, despite acknowledging its subjective nature.
4. **Ambiguous Evaluation Metrics:** While NCD provides a quantitative measure of distance, its interpretation in the context of linguistic similarity is not fully clear. The phylogenetic tree is visually helpful, but a more rigorous comparison against established linguistic classifications (e.g., using standard metrics like precision, recall, or F1-score) would strengthen the evaluation.
5. **Minor Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper contains several minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., variations in code formatting, inconsistent use of italics) and grammatical errors, suggesting a lack of careful proofreading. While not critical, these detract from the overall professionalism of the submission.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What is the computational complexity of the algorithm as a function of the number of languages (N) and the size of the cognate list (D)? Can you provide more details on the specific bottlenecks limiting scalability, and a concrete plan for addressing them?
2. How sensitive is the algorithm to the quality of the input data? For example, how does the presence of cognates with uncertain etymologies or erroneous alignments affect the results?
3. The introduction of data weighting seems to introduce a subjective element. What criteria would you recommend for determining the appropriate weighting factor, and how can the impact of this choice be minimized?
4. Can you provide a more rigorous evaluation of the results, comparing the inferred linguistic relationships against established classifications using quantitative metrics?
5. The phrase "objective string-level measures of linguistic similarity" is used frequently. Is it truly "objective" if data weighting is employed, thereby introducing user bias?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are not formatted consistently.
*   The use of bullet points within paragraphs is unconventional.
*   The discussion of related work is somewhat superficial. A more in-depth comparison of the proposed approach to existing methods would be beneficial.
*   The description of the EM algorithm could be more concise and focused on the key steps relevant to this specific application.



Despite the novelty of the approach and the deterministic nature of the algorithm, the limitations regarding scalability, robustness, and critical analysis, coupled with the excessive detail and minor presentation flaws, lead me to recommend **rejection**. While the core idea holds promise, substantial revisions are needed to address these weaknesses and demonstrate the practical applicability of the method.
