## Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL"

**Summary:**

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors argue that existing statistical methods often rely on limiting assumptions and propose a deterministic algorithm that avoids these by seeking the most succinct description of the data without pre-defined distributions of correspondences. They apply this approach to Slavic languages, demonstrating its ability to identify linguistic subgroups and quantify similarities between languages.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **A principled, assumption-free framework for multi-way linguistic comparison:** The application of MDL to this problem, explicitly avoiding prior assumptions about correspondence shapes or distributions, is a valuable contribution. This opens up the possibility of discovering previously unseen patterns.
2. **A deterministic algorithm for correspondence discovery:** The development of an efficient, deterministic algorithm for implementing the MDL approach is significant. Determinism facilitates reproducibility and allows for easier integration of linguistic expertise.
3. **Demonstration on Slavic languages:** The application of the method to a challenging set of languages (Slavic) and the presentation of insightful results, including phylogenetic tree reconstruction and quantification of similarity, validates the approach and provides a strong empirical basis for further development.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Theoretical Soundness:** The core idea of using MDL to discover correspondences, rather than assuming their form, is a refreshing departure from many existing methods. The grounding in information theory provides a strong theoretical foundation. The use of two-part MDL is appropriate for the task.
2. **Determinism & Interpretability:**  The deterministic nature of the algorithm is a major strength. This makes the results easier to understand and verify, and allows linguists to directly inspect the learned correspondences and potentially refine the process with their knowledge. The EM approach and the initialization with singleton rules are elegant and logically sound.
3. **Scalability to Multiple Languages:** A key advantage of this work is its ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously. Existing methods often struggle with this, making this a significant advance. The experimental results clearly demonstrate this capability, providing a more nuanced understanding of linguistic relationships than pairwise comparisons alone.
4. **Insightful Results:** The phylogenetic tree constructed using the NCDs is largely consistent with established linguistic classifications. The discovered correspondences (examples in Table 3) are linguistically plausible and demonstrate the algorithm’s ability to capture both phonological and orthographic patterns. The exploration of Shared Description Lengths is a clever way to quantify the degree of shared structure between language subsets.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors’ reasoning and methodology. The detailed explanation of the MDL code (Sections 3 & 4) is particularly commendable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Complexity:** While the authors acknowledge the computational challenges, the runtime (hours on a relatively modest machine for a limited dataset) is a significant limitation.  This restricts the applicability of the method to larger datasets or more complex linguistic scenarios. A more detailed analysis of the algorithmic complexity and potential optimization strategies would be beneficial.
2. **Data Dependency & Limited Data Size:** The method's performance is heavily dependent on the quality and quantity of the input data. The use of Swadesh lists, while a common starting point, is known to have limitations. Expanding the dataset with additional cognates and potentially incorporating external linguistic resources could improve the robustness of the results. The use of cognates *defined* for all languages is very restrictive.  Analyzing data with gaps could be valuable, although more complex.
3. **Lack of Comparison with Existing Methods:** The paper would be strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison with existing statistical approaches to correspondence discovery. While the authors mention the limitations of these approaches, a direct comparison on the same dataset would provide a clearer assessment of the advantages of the proposed method.  Specifically, comparing performance (accuracy and runtime) would be very useful.
4. **Subjectivity in Over-Weighting:** While the introduction of data over-weighting provides a mechanism for incorporating linguistic intuition, it also introduces a degree of subjectivity. A more rigorous evaluation of the impact of different weighting schemes and a discussion of best practices for selecting appropriate weights would be helpful.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:**  A deeper analysis of the errors made by the algorithm – i.e., incorrect or spurious correspondences – would be valuable. This could provide insights into the limitations of the method and guide future improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the algorithmic complexity of your approach? What are the bottlenecks in the current implementation, and what potential optimizations could be explored to improve scalability?
2.  How sensitive is the algorithm to the choice of the initial alignment (null alignment vs. greedy alignment)? Have you explored other initialization strategies?
3.  You mention the limitations of Swadesh lists.  Have you considered incorporating other types of linguistic data, such as parallel corpora or etymological databases, to improve the robustness of the results?
4.  Could you elaborate on how you determined the optimal over-weighting factor in your experiments?  What criteria did you use to balance objectivity and linguistic intuition?
5.  Can you provide examples of incorrect or spurious correspondences discovered by the algorithm, and discuss the potential reasons for these errors?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The figure numbers are slightly out of order (Fig. 2 before Fig. 1).
*   A discussion of potential applications beyond phylogenetic analysis (e.g., machine translation, language learning) would be valuable.




Overall, this is a strong and innovative paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of computational linguistics.  While there are some weaknesses that could be addressed, the strengths of the approach – particularly its principled foundation, deterministic nature, and scalability to multiple languages – outweigh these concerns. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
