## Review of “Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL”

**Summary:** This paper presents a method for discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors propose a deterministic algorithm to infer correspondence rules directly from data, avoiding assumptions about the shape or distribution of these rules. They demonstrate the approach on Slavic languages, showing its ability to quantify string-level similarities and identify linguistic subgroups.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Application of MDL to Multi-Language Correspondence Discovery:** The core contribution is the successful application of the MDL principle to a challenging problem – discovering linguistic correspondences across *more than two* languages. This is a significant step beyond existing methods often limited to pairwise comparisons.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The authors provide a deterministic algorithm for this task, offering transparency and reproducibility, which is valuable in linguistics where interpretability is crucial.
3. **Detailed Quantitative Analysis:**  The experiments, particularly the four-way analysis, provide a granular and information-theoretic quantification of linguistic similarity, going beyond traditional phylogenetic tree representations.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Approach:** The use of MDL for multi-language correspondence discovery is a genuinely novel approach.  Most existing work relies on statistical modeling with pre-defined distributions or heuristics.  The MDL framework's emphasis on finding the most succinct description of the data is well-suited to this task.
2. **Strong Theoretical Foundation:**  The paper grounds its method in a solid theoretical framework – MDL – and clearly explains the relevant concepts and equations. The connection to coding theory and information theory is well established.
3. **Promising Results:** The results presented, particularly the phylogenetic tree reconstruction and the four-way analysis, are compelling. The tree aligns well with known linguistic classifications, and the sDL analysis provides insightful quantitative data about language relationships.  The examples of discovered correspondences (Table 3) are plausible and demonstrate the method’s ability to identify meaningful patterns.
4. **Determinism & Transparency:** The algorithm's deterministic nature is a considerable strength.  This makes the process transparent and allows for careful examination of the learned rules.  The ability to control for over-weighting adds another layer of flexibility and potential for linguistically informed refinement.
5. **Handles Multiple Languages:**  The method’s ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a clear advantage over many existing approaches. This allows for a more nuanced and complete picture of linguistic relationships.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:**  The authors acknowledge scalability issues, stating that the current implementation can handle up to five languages.  This limitation is significant.  While the paper suggests future work to address this, it’s a substantial barrier to applying the method to larger language families or comprehensive datasets. The bottleneck appears to be the computational complexity of the alignment procedure, specifically the shortest-path search.
2. **Data Dependency & Limited Dataset:** The results are based on Swadesh lists, which, while standard, are known to be limited in scope and can sometimes lead to misleading results. The dataset size (Table 1) is relatively small, raising concerns about the robustness and generalizability of the findings. More extensive data, including texts beyond basic word lists, would strengthen the evaluation.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Existing Methods:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods for cognate identification and language relatedness.  A direct comparison with methods like ASDiT or Bayesian phylogenetic methods would help to establish the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed approach.  The authors mention existing methods in the introduction but don’t demonstrate performance relative to them.
4. **Interpretability of Rules:** While the algorithm is deterministic, the sheer number of rules generated and the varying granularity of correspondences (as seen in Table 3) can make it challenging to interpret the overall findings.  A more systematic analysis of the types of rules learned and their linguistic significance would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Scalability:** Can you elaborate on the specific steps you are taking to address the scalability issues? What are the expected performance improvements with these changes? Specifically, how do you intend to address the computational complexity of the alignment procedure?
2. **Data Expansion:** Do you have plans to evaluate the method on larger and more diverse datasets, including non-Swadesh list data? How do you anticipate the performance might change with different types of data?
3. **Comparative Evaluation:**  Could you provide a comparison of your method to existing approaches (e.g., ASDiT, Bayesian phylogenetic methods) on the same datasets?  What are the relative strengths and weaknesses of your method compared to these alternatives?
4. **Rule Interpretation:**  How do you plan to facilitate the interpretation of the learned rules?  Are there any metrics or visualizations you could use to summarize the types of correspondences being discovered?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly structured.
*   The use of examples (Table 3 and Figure 4) is effective in illustrating the method and its results.
*   The discussion of data over-weighting (Section 4.5) is insightful and highlights the potential for incorporating linguistic expertise into the analysis.



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper presents a promising new approach to the problem of multi-language correspondence discovery. The application of MDL is novel, the algorithm is well-founded, and the results are encouraging. While there are concerns regarding scalability, data limitations, and the lack of a comprehensive comparative evaluation, the strengths of the work outweigh these weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised in the weaknesses section and the questions for the authors.
