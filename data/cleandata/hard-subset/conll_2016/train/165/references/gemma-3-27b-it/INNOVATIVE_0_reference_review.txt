This paper presents a novel application of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to discover correspondences between multiple languages simultaneously. The primary contribution is a deterministic algorithm for inferring these correspondences without imposing prior assumptions about their shape or distribution, a significant departure from many existing statistical approaches. A second, valuable contribution is demonstrating the applicability of this approach to phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages and quantifying inter-language similarity in a nuanced way, going beyond simple pairwise comparisons. Finally, the introduction of data over-weighting provides a flexible mechanism to incorporate linguistic expertise and control the granularity of the discovered rules.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Conceptual Leap:** The core idea of applying MDL to this problem – treating correspondences as simply associated strings without pre-defined distributions – is genuinely novel. This sidesteps the limitations of methods constrained by distributional assumptions, and opens up a more data-driven approach. The authors correctly identify the weaknesses of existing methods and position their work as a direct response.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The deterministic nature of the algorithm is a strong point. While non-deterministic approaches can be valuable, a deterministic algorithm facilitates reproducibility and allows for a clearer understanding of the discovered relationships. This is particularly appealing in a field like linguistics, where interpretability is crucial.
3. **Multi-Language Capability:** The algorithm's ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a significant advantage. This allows for a more holistic view of linguistic relationships and enables the discovery of patterns that would be missed in pairwise analyses. The four-way analysis and subsequent quantification of shared description lengths provide a compelling demonstration of this capability.
4. **Practical Demonstration & Insightful Results:** The experiments with Slavic languages showcase the approach's potential. The phylogenetic tree aligns with established linguistic classifications, and the discovered correspondences, even with the simplified dataset, appear linguistically plausible. The use of data over-weighting as a tool for controlling the level of detail in the learned rules is a smart addition.
5. **Clear Theoretical Foundation:** The paper presents a solid grounding in MDL principles and explains the encoding scheme in a relatively accessible manner. The connection to coding theory and the emphasis on concise descriptions are well-articulated.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Concerns:** The authors acknowledge limitations in scalability, stating that processing five languages on their hardware takes "a few hours." While this is reasonable for a proof-of-concept, it raises concerns about applying this method to larger language families or more extensive datasets. A more detailed discussion of computational complexity and potential optimizations would be beneficial.
2. **Data Dependency & Limited Dataset:** The results are based on Swadesh lists, which are a relatively limited form of linguistic data. While sufficient for demonstration, the generalizability of the findings to more complex linguistic phenomena is unclear. Expanding the dataset with more diverse texts would strengthen the conclusions. The authors do acknowledge this in discussion of over-weighting, but more exploration would be beneficial.
3. **Evaluation Focus:** While the NCD-based phylogenetic tree is a reasonable evaluation metric, the paper lacks a more direct comparison to existing methods on the same task. Showing that the MDL approach achieves comparable or superior performance to state-of-the-art cognate identification or reconstruction methods would bolster the claims of its effectiveness.
4. **Presentation of Correspondence Rules:** The presentation of the discovered correspondences (Table 3) is somewhat limited. Providing more context – the frequency of the rules in the corpus, their statistical significance – would enhance the interpretability of the results. It's also not clear *why* certain alignments were chosen for presentation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the algorithm, particularly with respect to the number of languages and the size of the vocabulary?
2.  What strategies are being considered to address the scalability limitations, such as parallelization or approximation algorithms?
3.  Have you explored the use of other evaluation metrics beyond NCD, such as manual validation by linguists or comparison with established etymological databases?
4.  Can you elaborate on the criteria used for selecting the correspondences presented in Table 3? Were these chosen based on frequency, statistical significance, or other factors?
5.  What is the role of data cleaning and pre-processing in your pipeline? Are you dealing with issues like spelling variations, orthographic normalization, or dialectal differences?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. While the current experiments are limited in scope, the novelty of the approach and the potential for future development are substantial. The idea of leveraging MDL for this task is exciting, and the authors have provided a solid foundation for further research. I am inclined to accept this paper despite the acknowledged limitations, due to the significant novelty and potential impact of the work.
