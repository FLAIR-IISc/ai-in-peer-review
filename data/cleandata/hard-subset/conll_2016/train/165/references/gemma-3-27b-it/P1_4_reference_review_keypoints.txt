- Proposes an MDL-based deterministic algorithm to discover correspondences across multiple languages without pre-defined shape/distribution assumptions.  
- Uses an EM approach to find the most succinct set of correspondence rules describing the data.  
- Demonstrates method on Slavic languages, producing phylogenetic trees and similarity measures (NCDs, shared description lengths).  
- Contribution: MDL framework offers a theoretically grounded, less biased method for multi-language correspondence discovery.  
- Contribution: Deterministic algorithm improves reproducibility and appears scalable in principle despite current implementation limits.  
- Contribution: Quantitative experiments on Slavic data show the method can reflect linguistic classification and nuanced similarity.  
- Strength: The MDL grounding and avoidance of arbitrary distributional assumptions strengthen theoretical soundness.  
- Strength: Applying MDL to multi-language, simultaneous correspondence discovery is a novel angle.  
- Strength: Paper gives a detailed algorithmic description (EM steps, encoding, alignment, initialization).  
- Strength: Experimental results (phylogenetic reconstruction, NCDs) are compelling and illustrative.  
- Strength: Clear writing and useful example tables aid understanding.  
- Weakness: Current implementation scales poorly (e.g., up to five languages takes hours), limiting applicability.  
- Weakness: Experiments rely on small Swadesh lists and pre-selected cognate tuples, risking data-dependence and bias.  
- Weakness: Lacks direct comparison to baseline methods (e.g., edit distance, probabilistic cognate ID) and quantitative benchmarks.  
- Weakness: Introduces data over-weighting as a subjective parameter without clear criteria, undermining full objectivity.  
- Weakness: Insufficient error analysis and discussion of linguistically implausible correspondences.  
- Questions: What specific scalability improvements (parallelization/optimizations) are planned?  
- Questions: Can the method be benchmarked against baseline approaches and which metrics would be used?  
- Questions: Are there plans to expand the dataset and ensure data quality/consistency?  
- Questions: How should the over-weighting factor be chosen objectively and how does linguistic knowledge inform it?  
- Questions: Can the authors provide concrete examples of algorithmic errors and their causes?  
- Additional comments: Expand references/details for Likomanova (2004) and Angelov (2004).  
- Additional comments: Provide more detail on phylogenetic tree generation (neighbor joining) and a table of example four-way alignments (CS-PL-RU-BG).  
- Overall: Promising, theoretically sound work that would be stronger with scalability fixes, baseline comparisons, larger datasets, and deeper error analysis.
