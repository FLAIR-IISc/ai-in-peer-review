- Proposes an MDL-based framework to discover systematic correspondences between languages without prior assumptions.  
- Introduces a deterministic EM-based algorithm to learn correspondence rules directly from data.  
- Demonstrates scalability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously, evaluated on Slavic languages.  

- Strength: Strong theoretical grounding via MDL and clear two-part code encoding explanation.  
- Strength: Deterministic method yields repeatable, interpretable correspondences useful for linguistic analysis.  
- Strength: Handles multi-language (beyond pairwise) comparisons, shown in a 4-way CS-PL-RU-BG analysis.  
- Strength: Clear presentation of results, including NCD-based phylogenetic tree and examples with/without data over-weighting.  
- Strength: Well-defined problem formulation and motivation against ad-hoc similarity metrics.  

- Weakness: Evaluation relies primarily on Swadesh lists and orthographic forms, limiting representativeness and ignoring phonetics/morphology.  
- Weakness: Potential computational and memory bottlenecks; unclear time/space complexity and scalability beyond ~5 languages.  
- Weakness: No direct quantitative comparison to existing statistical methods (e.g., Schulz, Wettig, Snyder & Barzilay).  
- Weakness: Data over-weighting introduces subjectivity that conflicts with a purely objective, data-driven goal.  
- Weakness: Insufficient discussion of false positives and potential overfitting despite MDL’s regularization role.  

- Question: Provide detailed time and space complexity as functions of number of languages (N) and data size (D), and planned optimizations.  
- Question: Include a (even qualitative) comparison to existing correspondence-discovery methods on the same dataset.  
- Question: Offer criteria or automated strategies for choosing data over-weighting parameters and assessing their impact.  
- Question: Explain how the MDL framework controls spurious correspondences and propose strategies for false positive filtering.  
- Question: Consider incorporating phonetic and morphological features and discuss expected benefits for accuracy and interpretability.  

- Recommend: Paper is well-written, clearly structured, and uses effective figures/tables.  
- Recommend: Expand related work to include recent advances in computational historical linguistics.  
- Recommend: Provide a publicly available implementation to aid reproducibility and further research.  
- Overall: Promising, theoretically grounded, and interpretable contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
