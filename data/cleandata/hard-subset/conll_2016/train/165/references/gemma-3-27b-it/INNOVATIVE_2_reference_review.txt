This paper introduces a novel application of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to discover correspondences between multiple languages simultaneously. The core contribution lies in framing the problem not as one requiring pre-defined distributional assumptions about correspondences, but as a succinct data description problem. The deterministic algorithm developed for this purpose, alongside the demonstration of its effectiveness on Slavic languages, represents a significant departure from existing approaches. A secondary, but notable, contribution is the ability to quantify linguistic similarity across subsets of languages, offering a more nuanced view than pairwise comparisons allow.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Approach:** The use of MDL to discover multi-lingual correspondences *without* distributional assumptions is genuinely novel. The field is saturated with methods imposing constraints (probabilistic models, alignment algorithms with built-in biases). This work’s commitment to a purely data-driven approach is refreshing and potentially groundbreaking.
2. **Scalability to Multiple Languages:** Existing methods largely focus on pairs of languages. This paper demonstrably extends the analysis to four languages, opening possibilities for investigating broader linguistic relationships. The algorithm's efficiency, even with current limitations, is commendable. The authors acknowledge bottlenecks but present a clear path toward improvement.
3. **Informative Experiments & Results:** The experiments are well-designed, leveraging readily available data (Swadesh lists). The phylogenetic tree construction and comparison to established linguistic classifications provide a strong validation of the approach. The demonstration of its ability to identify linguistic subgroups is particularly compelling. The examples of inferred correspondences, even preliminary, are insightful.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically structured. The introduction effectively motivates the problem and situates the work within the existing literature. The mathematical formulation of the MDL code is clear and the explanation of the EM-based alignment procedure is intuitive.
5. **Data Over-weighting as a Flexible Control:** The introduction of data over-weighting as a way to incorporate linguistic knowledge and control the granularity of the discovered rules is a clever addition. It provides a practical means for researchers to tailor the algorithm’s output to their specific needs and expertise.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Validation Beyond Slavic Languages:** While the results on Slavic languages are promising, the generalizability of the approach to other language families remains unproven. Testing with a more diverse set of languages (e.g., Indo-European, Sino-Tibetan, Afro-Asiatic) is crucial.
2. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art (Specifically on Four+ Languages):** While the paper references related work, it doesn’t offer a direct comparison to existing methods on the *multi-lingual* task. The focus is largely on validating against linguistic intuition. Demonstrating superior performance against baselines (even if adapted for this setting) would strengthen the claims.
3. **Computational Bottlenecks and Scalability Concerns:** The authors acknowledge limitations in scalability. The current implementation only handles up to five languages. While they plan future work to address this, it's a significant practical constraint. A discussion of algorithmic complexity and potential optimizations would be beneficial.
4. **Subjectivity in Data Over-weighting:** While the data over-weighting technique is clever, it introduces a degree of subjectivity. The authors acknowledge this, but a more rigorous analysis of its impact and guidelines for its application would be helpful. The “linguistic expert” reliance could be a barrier to adoption.
5. **Reliance on Orthographic Form:** The exclusive reliance on orthographic form might limit the discovery of deeper, phonological or morphological correspondences. Incorporating phonetic or morphological features could potentially improve the accuracy and robustness of the algorithm.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What is the computational complexity of the alignment procedure with respect to the number of languages (N)? Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the factors limiting scalability, and your plans to address them?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of data set (e.g., different Swadesh lists, different cognate databases)? Have you explored the impact of data quality and coverage on the performance of the algorithm?
3.  Could you elaborate on the guidelines for using the data over-weighting technique? What criteria should a user consider when determining the appropriate weight to assign to different data entries?
4.  Do you have preliminary results on language families beyond Slavic, even if limited? Any insights into the potential challenges and adaptations required for different language structures would be appreciated.



**Additional Comments:**

The paper's boldness and originality outweigh its current limitations. The approach has the potential to significantly advance the field of historical linguistics and computational philology. The exploration of the shared description length concept is particularly exciting. The claim that this approach eschews “assumptions about the shape or distribution” of correspondences is strong, and the experiments support this. The authors’ willingness to embrace a deterministic approach, even with its limitations, is commendable. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
