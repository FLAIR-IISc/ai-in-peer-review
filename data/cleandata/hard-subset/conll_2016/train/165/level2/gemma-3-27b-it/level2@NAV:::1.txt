## Review of “Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL”

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors tackle the problem of identifying statistically significant relationships between words across languages, avoiding the limitations of pairwise comparisons and pre-defined distributional assumptions. The core contribution lies in the development of an efficient algorithm for inferring these correspondences directly from data, demonstrated through analysis of Slavic languages. A secondary contribution is the successful application of this method to generate a phylogenetic analysis, confirming expected linguistic classifications and quantifying similarity levels within and between language groups. Finally, the paper highlights the utility of the approach in identifying nuanced similarities between language subsets, offering a richer, more detailed analysis than existing methods.

The strengths of this paper are significant. The proposed MDL-based approach is theoretically sound and offers a compelling alternative to traditional methods. The algorithm’s ability to handle multiple languages simultaneously is a clear advantage. The empirical evaluation on Slavic languages is well-executed, and the results align with established linguistic knowledge, particularly in the generated phylogenetic tree. The presentation of example correspondences offers valuable insight into the types of patterns discovered, while the discussion of shared description lengths provides a quantitative measure of linguistic relatedness. The acknowledgement and potential utilization of data over-weighting adds a pragmatic dimension, allowing for control over the level of detail in the analysis.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. While the algorithm is described, the complexity and scalability limitations aren't fully addressed; the statement that processing five languages takes hours on limited hardware is concerning. The discussion of the Expectation-Maximization (EM) step lacks sufficient detail to fully understand its implementation and computational cost. Furthermore, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods beyond the qualitative assessment of generated trees. A clearer explanation of how the algorithm handles ambiguous alignments (as illustrated in the example in Section 2) would also strengthen the work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational complexity of the EM algorithm, specifically how it scales with the number of languages and the size of the dataset?
2.  How does your approach compare quantitatively to other methods for cognate identification or language relatedness assessment, such as those based on Bayesian inference or Levenshtein distance?
3.  The over-weighting technique is mentioned as allowing control over the level of detail. Can you provide more concrete guidelines or heuristics for choosing an appropriate over-weighting factor?
