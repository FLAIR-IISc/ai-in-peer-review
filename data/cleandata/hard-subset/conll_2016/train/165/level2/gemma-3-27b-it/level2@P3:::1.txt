This paper introduces a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The central contribution lies in the development of an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules without imposing prior assumptions about their shape or distribution. A secondary contribution is the application of this method to a set of Slavic languages, providing both quantitative (NCD-based phylogenetic tree) and qualitative (learned correspondences) insights. The demonstrated ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously, and to quantify similarity across all subsets, represents a further, though less substantial, contribution.

Strengths:

1. **Novelty of the Approach:** The use of MDL to discover multi-lingual correspondences, particularly without pre-defined assumptions, is a significant departure from existing statistical approaches. Many current methods rely on cognate identification or pre-defined etymological relationships, which can introduce bias. The paper convincingly argues for the benefits of an unbiased, data-driven approach.
2. **Efficiency of the Algorithm:** The described algorithm, utilizing an Expectation-Maximization (EM) framework and deterministic optimization, appears computationally feasible for a reasonable number of languages. The reported processing time for five languages on modest hardware suggests scalability, and the authors acknowledge potential bottlenecks and future optimization directions.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experiments presented are well-designed. The use of both pairwise analyses (NCD, phylogenetic tree) and four-way analysis provides a robust evaluation of the method. The comparison of the generated phylogenetic tree with known linguistic classifications strengthens the validity of the approach.
4. **Detailed Analysis & Insights:** The presentation of learned correspondences offers valuable insights into the relationships between the Slavic languages. The examples given in Table 3 are illustrative and demonstrate the algorithm’s ability to identify both phonological and orthographic patterns. The analysis of shared description lengths (sDL) provides a novel way to quantify linguistic similarity between language subsets. 
5. **Flexibility through Data Over-Weighting:** The introduction of data over-weighting as a mechanism for controlling the level of detail in the learned correspondences provides a useful degree of control for the user. It allows a linguistic expert to incorporate their knowledge and influence the output of the algorithm without fundamentally altering the underlying statistical framework.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Data Scope:** The experiments are primarily focused on Slavic languages. While this allows for a thorough analysis within a well-defined linguistic family, it limits the generalizability of the findings. Evaluating the method on languages from different families, with varying degrees of relatedness, would be crucial for demonstrating its broader applicability.
2. **Data Pre-processing:** The data used consists of Swadesh lists, which, while commonly used, represent a limited vocabulary. The results might not generalize well to other types of text or broader linguistic phenomena. The choice of using raw orthographic forms is generally good for avoiding transcription biases, but the paper doesn't discuss potential challenges arising from orthographic variations within each language.
3. **Interpretation of sDL:** While the concept of shared description length (sDL) is intriguing, the explanation of its interpretation could be more thorough.  The paper states that higher sDL values indicate greater similarity, but it doesn't fully explain *why* this is the case in intuitive linguistic terms. Additional clarification could improve understanding.
4. **Missing Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the learned correspondences.  Identifying cases where the algorithm produces incorrect or misleading correspondences, and investigating the underlying causes, would significantly strengthen the evaluation.
5. **Limited Comparison to Existing Methods:** While the paper critiques existing statistical approaches, it lacks a direct quantitative comparison with state-of-the-art methods for cognate identification or multilingual alignment. Providing such a comparison would demonstrate the relative advantages of the proposed MDL-based approach.

Questions to Authors:

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational complexity of the algorithm as the number of languages increases? Are there strategies for mitigating the bottlenecks identified in Section 4.3 for very large N?
2.  How sensitive is the algorithm to noise or errors in the input data? Could you provide an example of how an incorrect cognate pair might affect the learned correspondences?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the data over-weighting parameter (α) was chosen in the experiments? Is there a principled way to determine an optimal value for α based on the characteristics of the data?
4.  Regarding the sDL metric, can you provide examples of linguistic phenomena that would lead to a higher or lower sDL value? How can this metric be used to identify subgroupings within a language family beyond the obvious classifications?

Additional Comments:

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the potential limitations of relying solely on word form similarity. Factors such as semantic change and borrowing could influence the results and should be acknowledged.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the number of rules learned for different language combinations and data weighting schemes.
*   The reference formatting is slightly inconsistent. Ensure adherence to CONLL guidelines.
