- Proposes an MDL-based method for discovering correspondences between multiple languages.  
- Avoids pairwise comparisons and pre-defined distributional assumptions.  
- Core contribution: an algorithm that infers correspondences directly from data, demonstrated on Slavic languages.  
- Secondary contribution: uses the method to generate a phylogenetic analysis that confirms expected classifications and quantifies similarities.  
- Capable of identifying nuanced similarities between language subsets, offering richer analyses than existing methods.  
- The MDL approach is theoretically sound and a compelling alternative to traditional methods.  
- Algorithm handles multiple languages simultaneously, a clear practical advantage.  
- Empirical evaluation on Slavic languages is well-executed and aligns with linguistic knowledge (phylogenetic tree).  
- Presentation of example correspondences provides insight into discovered patterns.  
- Shared description lengths offer a quantitative measure of linguistic relatedness.  
- Acknowledges and leverages data over-weighting to control analysis detail.  
- Weakness: algorithmic complexity and scalability are insufficiently addressed; five-language runs taking hours is concerning.  
- Weakness: EM step is under-described, leaving implementation and computational cost unclear.  
- Weakness: lacks thorough quantitative comparison to state-of-the-art methods beyond qualitative tree assessment.  
- Weakness: handling of ambiguous alignments (e.g., Section 2 example) is not clearly explained.  
- Question: clarify computational complexity of the EM algorithm and its scaling with number of languages and dataset size.  
- Question: provide quantitative comparisons to other methods (Bayesian inference, Levenshtein distance, etc.).  
- Question: offer concrete guidelines or heuristics for selecting an appropriate over-weighting factor.
