- Central contribution: MDL-based algorithm for discovering multilingual correspondences without prior assumptions on rule shape or distribution.
- Secondary contribution: Application to Slavic languages with NCD-based phylogenetic tree and qualitative learned correspondences.
- Additional contribution: Ability to analyze more than two languages simultaneously and quantify similarity across all subsets.

- Strength: Novel, unbiased MDL approach avoids cognate/etymology biases common in prior methods.
- Strength: Efficient EM-based deterministic algorithm appears computationally feasible and scalable; authors note bottlenecks and optimizations.
- Strength: Comprehensive evaluation using pairwise NCD, phylogenetic comparison, and four-way analyses aligned with known classifications.
- Strength: Learned correspondences and shared description length (sDL) provide meaningful phonological and orthographic insights.
- Strength: Data over-weighting (α) offers flexible control to tailor detail and incorporate expert knowledge.

- Weakness: Experiments focus primarily on Slavic languages, limiting generalizability to other families.
- Weakness: Use of Swadesh lists and raw orthography may not generalize; orthographic variation within languages is under-discussed.
- Weakness: sDL interpretation is underexplained; intuitive linguistic rationale for higher/lower sDL is lacking.
- Weakness: No detailed error analysis identifying incorrect or misleading correspondences and their causes.
- Weakness: Lacks direct quantitative comparison with state-of-the-art cognate identification or multilingual alignment methods.

- Question: What is the algorithm’s computational complexity as number of languages grows, and how can identified bottlenecks be mitigated for large N?
- Question: How sensitive is the algorithm to noise or incorrect input (e.g., wrong cognate pairs), and how does that affect learned correspondences?
- Question: How was the data over-weighting parameter (α) chosen, and is there a principled way to select an optimal α for given data?
- Question: Can authors give linguistic examples that produce higher or lower sDL and show how sDL can reveal subgroupings beyond obvious classifications?

- Additional comment: Discuss limitations of relying solely on word form similarity, including effects of semantic change and borrowing.
- Additional comment: Add a table summarizing number of rules learned for different language combinations and data weighting schemes.
- Additional comment: Fix inconsistent reference formatting to comply with CONLL guidelines.
