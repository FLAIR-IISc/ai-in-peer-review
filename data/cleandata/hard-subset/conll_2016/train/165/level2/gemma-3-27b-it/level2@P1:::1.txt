This paper presents an interesting application of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to the problem of discovering correspondences between multiple languages. The core idea – treating correspondences as simply associated strings without pre-defined distributional assumptions – is a strong point, and the authors successfully demonstrate that their algorithm can uncover meaningful relationships, particularly within the Slavic language family. The experiments, utilizing both pairwise analysis and a four-language setting, provide compelling evidence of the method’s efficacy.

**Contributions:**

1. **Novel Application of MDL:** The primary contribution is the application of MDL to the problem of multi-lingual correspondence discovery. While MDL has been used in related areas (etymological reconstruction), applying it directly to identifying string-level correspondences without strong prior assumptions is a notable advancement.
2. **Efficient Algorithm:** The authors present a deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules based on the MDL principle. The efficiency claim, especially for handling more than two languages, is valuable, as many existing approaches struggle with scalability.
3. **Quantitative & Qualitative Analysis:** The paper goes beyond simply presenting rules; it uses the discovered correspondences to perform both quantitative (NCD-based phylogenetic tree) and qualitative analyses, demonstrating the practical utility of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the challenges of discovering language correspondences and positions its approach as a solution to limitations of existing methods. The introduction effectively highlights the need for a method that avoids arbitrary assumptions and scales to multiple languages.
2. **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The grounding of the method in MDL provides a solid statistical foundation. The explanation of the two-part MDL code and the associated optimization problem is relatively clear and well-motivated.
3. **Empirically Supported Claims:** The experimental results on Slavic languages are convincing. The NCD-based phylogenetic tree aligns well with established linguistic classifications, and the identified correspondences appear linguistically plausible (as illustrated in Table 3).
4. **Practical Insights & Data Over-Weighting:** The introduction of data over-weighting is a clever addition, allowing users to tune the granularity of discovered correspondences. This demonstrates an understanding of the practical challenges of working with real-world data and provides a mechanism for incorporating linguistic expertise.
5. **Detailed Explanation of Algorithm:** The description of the alignment procedure (as a shortest-path problem) and the training procedure (Expectation-Maximization) is sufficiently detailed to understand the core mechanics of the algorithm.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Limitations:** While the authors claim efficiency, the statement about processing "up to five languages" on a standard desktop machine suggests scalability is still a significant constraint. More detailed discussion of computational complexity and potential optimizations would be beneficial. It’s unclear how well this would scale with larger language sets or more complex data.
2. **Data Dependency:** The quality of the results is heavily dependent on the quality and coverage of the input data (Swadesh lists). The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough discussion of the limitations imposed by this data source would be helpful. What happens when dealing with languages with limited lexical resources?
3. **Limited Comparison to Baselines:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to other state-of-the-art methods for language comparison or cognate identification. While the authors critique existing approaches in the introduction, a direct empirical comparison would strengthen the claim of superiority.
4. **Clarity of MDL Explanation:** While the authors attempt to explain MDL, it remains a complex concept. A more intuitive explanation, or perhaps a worked example of the MDL calculation for a simple case, would improve accessibility. Specifically, the jump from the general MDL formula to the specific implementation details in Section 4 feels abrupt.
5. **Presentation of Results:** Table 3 is helpful, but could be expanded to include more examples. Also, the discussion of Figures 2 and 3 could be more detailed, offering stronger interpretations of the observed shared description lengths.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What is the computational complexity of the algorithm as a function of the number of languages (N) and the size of the data (D)?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the initial alignment (null alignment vs. greedy alignment)?
3. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your approach to existing methods like cognate identification algorithms based on probabilistic models (e.g., those using Bayesian inference)?
4. How does the algorithm perform when applied to languages with significantly different typological features (e.g., a Slavic language and a tonal language)?
5.  The data over-weighting parameter α is introduced but not systematically explored. How does varying α affect the discovered correspondences and the overall performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "objective statistical analysis" in the conclusion feels a bit strong, given the subjective element introduced by data over-weighting.
*   The references are listed without full details (e.g., journal/conference name, page numbers). Please complete these.
*   The repeated mention of the "raw orthographic form" of the data is important, but could be mentioned less frequently.



Overall, this is a well-written and promising paper. The authors have developed a novel and effective approach to a challenging problem, and the results are compelling. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the contribution and increase the impact of this work. I recommend acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the reviewer’s concerns.
