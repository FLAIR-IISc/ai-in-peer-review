This paper presents an interesting approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea of identifying statistically significant correspondences without imposing prior assumptions on their shape or distribution is valuable, and the authors demonstrate its efficacy on a set of Slavic languages. The provided experiments show promising results in both pairwise and four-way analyses, confirming expected linguistic classifications and revealing detailed similarity patterns.

**Contributions:**

1. **MDL-based Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is the formulation of multi-language correspondence discovery as an MDL problem and the development of an efficient, deterministic algorithm to solve it. This provides a principled statistical framework for identifying linguistic relationships.
2. **Scalability to Multiple Languages:**  Unlike many existing methods limited to pairwise comparisons, the proposed approach extends naturally to more than two languages, enabling a more holistic analysis of linguistic relationships. This is demonstrated in the four-way analysis with Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian.
3. **Detailed Quantification of Similarity:** The method allows for a fine-grained quantification of linguistic similarity, going beyond simple pairwise distances to identify shared structures and patterns within subsets of languages. The Shared Description Length (sDL) metric provides a useful way to analyze these similarities.

**Strengths:**

1. **Principled Statistical Approach:** Using MDL provides a strong theoretical foundation for the method, offering a well-defined criterion for model selection and avoiding arbitrary assumptions.  This addresses a key weakness of many existing statistical approaches as highlighted in the introduction.
2. **Deterministic Algorithm:** The deterministic nature of the algorithm is a significant strength, ensuring reproducibility and facilitating interpretability. It avoids the potential for randomness affecting the discovered correspondences.
3. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on Slavic languages provide compelling evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The alignment of the learned phylogenetic tree with established linguistic classifications and the insightful analysis of four-way correspondences demonstrate the practical value of the approach.
4. **Clear Explanation of the Algorithm:** The paper does a good job of explaining the core concepts of MDL and the specific implementation of the algorithm, including the Expectation-Maximization (EM) steps and the alignment procedure. The introduction of data over-weighting is also clearly presented.
5. **Novel Quantification via sDL:** The introduction of the Shared Description Length (sDL) metric offers a novel and insightful way to quantify the amount of shared structure between different subsets of languages, beyond simple pairwise comparisons.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Complexity:** The authors acknowledge computational bottlenecks, specifically mentioning limitations in processing higher numbers of languages. While an efficient algorithm is claimed, the scaling challenges could hinder the applicability of the method to very large datasets or a greater number of languages. More detail on the exact scaling properties (e.g., how runtime increases with the number of languages and the size of the data) would be beneficial.
2. **Data Dependence and Limited Data Size:** The results are based on Swadesh lists and a relatively small set of cognates. The robustness of the method to different data sources and the impact of data size on the discovered correspondences are not fully explored.  A larger, more diverse dataset could provide further validation.
3. **Evaluation Metric Limitations:** While NCD is a standard metric, it might not fully capture the nuanced linguistic relationships being discovered. Exploring other evaluation metrics, perhaps incorporating linguistic expertise, could strengthen the assessment of the method’s performance.
4. **Lack of Comparison with Existing Methods:** The paper focuses primarily on presenting the new method without extensive comparison to existing approaches for correspondence discovery (e.g., those mentioned in the introduction). A more thorough comparative evaluation would help to establish the advantages and disadvantages of the proposed method relative to the state-of-the-art.
5. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates successful examples, a discussion of cases where the method fails to identify expected correspondences or produces spurious results would be valuable. This would provide insights into the limitations of the approach and potential areas for improvement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What is the computational complexity of the algorithm as a function of the number of languages (N) and the size of the dataset (D)? Could you provide more detailed runtime measurements for different dataset sizes and N?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the initial null alignment? Have you explored other initialization strategies?
3. How does the performance of the method change with the size and quality of the input data? Have you experimented with different data sources or data augmentation techniques?
4. Could you elaborate on the linguistic significance of the specific correspondences identified in the four-way analysis, beyond the general observations about vowel similarities? Are there any unexpected or surprising findings?
5.  Have you considered using or adapting existing methods for assessing the statistical significance of the discovered correspondences, beyond the MDL framework?
