- Novel application of MDL to multi-lingual correspondence discovery, avoiding strong prior distributional assumptions.  
- Deterministic, reportedly efficient algorithm for inferring correspondence rules, claimed to scale beyond pairwise comparisons.  
- Paper includes both quantitative (NCD-based phylogenetic tree) and qualitative analyses demonstrating practical utility.  
- Clear motivation and problem definition emphasizing avoidance of arbitrary assumptions and multi-language scaling.  
- Method has a sound theoretical foundation grounded in two-part MDL coding and a well-motivated optimization objective.  
- Experimental results on Slavic languages are convincing and produce linguistically plausible correspondences.  
- Introduction of data over-weighting as a tunable mechanism for adjusting correspondence granularity.  
- Detailed explanation of the alignment-as-shortest-path formulation and the EM-based training procedure.  
- Scalability concerns remain: reported processing “up to five languages” suggests limits and missing complexity analysis.  
- Strong dependence on input data quality/coverage (Swadesh lists); unclear behavior with scarce lexical resources.  
- Limited empirical comparison to state-of-the-art cognate-identification or probabilistic methods.  
- MDL explanation is still hard to follow; a more intuitive walkthrough or worked example is needed.  
- Presentation could be improved: expand Table 3 and provide deeper discussion of Figures 2 and 3.  
- Open questions about computational complexity as a function of language count and data size.  
- Sensitivity to initial alignment choices (null vs greedy) is not evaluated.  
- Request for direct empirical comparison to Bayesian/probabilistic cognate-identification methods.  
- Unclear performance on typologically distant languages (e.g., Slavic vs tonal languages).  
- The effect of the data over-weighting parameter α is introduced but not systematically explored.  
- Minor issues: conclusion’s “objective statistical analysis” phrasing is overstated, references are incomplete, and repetition about “raw orthographic form” is excessive.  
- Overall verdict: promising and well-written; recommend acceptance conditional on addressing the above concerns.
