### Review

**Summary of the paper and its main contributions:**
This paper introduces a novel method for automatically discovering orthographic correspondences among multiple languages simultaneously. The authors frame the problem using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, seeking the set of correspondence rules that most concisely describes a given dataset of cognate words. A deterministic, EM-like algorithm is proposed to find a good set of rules by starting with single-character correspondences and iteratively merging them based on which merge provides the greatest reduction in the total description length (model + data). The method's effectiveness is demonstrated on a set of 13 Slavic languages. A key feature is the ability to handle more than two languages at once, discovering correspondences that may exist only between a subset of the languages under consideration.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A principled, non-parametric framework for multi-language correspondence discovery. By using MDL, the method avoids strong prior assumptions about the nature of correspondences and naturally handles rules spanning any subset of the input languages, a significant advance over methods limited to pairwise analysis.
2.  The introduction of Shared Description Length (sDL), a novel quantitative metric for fine-grained analysis of multi-language similarity. This metric, derived directly from the model, allows for the quantification and visualization of shared structural complexity across different language subgroups, offering a richer view than traditional phylogenetic trees.
3.  A practical algorithm that includes a "data over-weighting" mechanism. This feature provides a useful way for users (e.g., linguists) to control the granularity of the discovered rules, allowing them to explore patterns that may not be statistically dominant but are nevertheless of interest.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Novel and theoretically sound methodology:** The application of the MDL principle to the problem of multi-language correspondence discovery is elegant and well-motivated. It provides a formal, objective function for balancing model complexity against data fit without requiring parameter tuning. The ability to model correspondences between arbitrary subsets of languages (e.g., finding a rule that links Czech and Polish, but is separate from one linking Russian and Bulgarian, all within a single 4-way analysis) is a powerful and novel feature.
2.  **Compelling multi-way analysis and results:** The 4-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian is the paper's most compelling section. The Shared Description Length (sDL) metric and its visualization in Figure 3 effectively showcase the method's unique capabilities. It successfully identifies the known West Slavic (CS-PL) and East/South Slavic (RU-BG) subgroups while also quantifying the complexity shared by all four languages, by triplets, and by individual languages. This provides a far more detailed and nuanced picture of linguistic similarity than is possible with purely pairwise methods.
3.  **Clear and reproducible experiments:** The experiments are well-designed and clearly presented. The pairwise analysis, which produces a standard phylogenetic tree (Figure 1) that aligns well with established linguistic classifications of Slavic languages, serves as an effective validation of the underlying distance metric. This gives the reader confidence in the method before the more novel multi-way analysis is presented. The examples of learned rules in Tables 3 and 4 are illustrative and give a good intuition for what the model discovers.
4.  **Practicality and potential for expert interaction:** The authors have developed not just a theoretical model but a practical tool. The deterministic nature of the algorithm makes results reproducible. The "data over-weighting" technique is a thoughtful inclusion, acknowledging that the most statistically significant patterns are not always the only ones of interest. It provides a simple knob for a linguistic expert to steer the model towards a desired level of detail, making the tool more useful for exploratory analysis.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Scalability of the approach is a major concern:** The paper states that the current implementation can handle "up to five languages" and notes that runtime and memory are bottlenecks. This is a significant limitation for a method whose main selling point is multi-language analysis. The complexity appears to be exponential in the number of languages `N`, both in the alignment step (combinatorial search space) and in the model itself (2^N - 1 rule subsets). The paper would be much stronger with a more detailed complexity analysis and a discussion of potential optimizations or approximations that could make the method viable for larger language groups (e.g., N > 7).
2.  **Evaluation of the discovered rules is limited:** The quality of the learned correspondence rules is evaluated primarily through anecdotal examples. While the examples shown are plausible, the paper lacks a systematic, quantitative evaluation against a gold standard. For instance, comparing the discovered rules to a set of known phonological or orthographic changes documented by historical linguists would provide much stronger evidence of the model's correctness. Without this, it is difficult to assess whether the model is truly discovering linguistically meaningful correspondences or simply common, superficial string patterns.
3.  **Potential impact of the greedy search is under-explored:** The M-step of the training algorithm uses a greedy strategy, iteratively merging the single best pair of patterns. This is computationally efficient but offers no guarantee of finding a globally optimal solution. The paper mentions this briefly but does not analyze how sensitive the final model is to this choice. An ablation study or comparison with a non-greedy search method (even on a smaller dataset) would help characterize the robustness of the results and the severity of the local optima problem.
4.  **Lack of comparison to other computational methods:** The paper positions itself against the manual "comparative method" but provides little context regarding other computational approaches in historical linguistics or cognate analysis. How does the NCD metric derived from this model compare to other established measures of linguistic distance? How do the discovered rules compare in form or quality to those produced by other unsupervised alignment or morphological analysis methods, even if those are limited to pairwise comparison? Situating the work more firmly within the existing computational literature would clarify its specific advantages.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Could you provide more detail on the computational complexity of the alignment procedure as a function of the number of languages `N`? What is the primary bottleneck that limits the analysis to five languages, and do you have concrete ideas for improving the scalability to larger language families?
2.  The "data over-weighting" parameter `α` is a pragmatic tool for controlling rule granularity. Is the selection of `α` purely subjective based on the user's goal, or are there heuristics you can suggest for choosing a value that balances statistical significance with descriptive detail? For the results in Table 3, how was the final value of `α` chosen?
3.  The greedy merging strategy is deterministic and efficient. Have you explored how the final set of rules differs when using a stochastic search process, such as simulated annealing, which might escape local optima? Does the greedy approach consistently find solutions that are qualitatively similar to those found by more exhaustive methods?
