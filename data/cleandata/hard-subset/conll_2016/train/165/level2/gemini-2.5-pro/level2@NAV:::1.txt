**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper introduces an unsupervised method based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to automatically discover orthographic correspondences from cognate lists that span multiple languages. The primary contributions of this work are:

1.  A principled, assumption-free framework for discovering correspondence rules simultaneously across more than two languages. This represents a significant and valuable advance over traditional methods that are limited to pairwise comparisons.
2.  The introduction of "Shared Description Length" (sDL), a novel metric that enables a fine-grained, quantitative analysis of the structural similarity between any subset of languages, offering deeper insights than a simple phylogenetic tree.

**Strengths**

The paper’s greatest strengths lie in its novelty and rigorous evaluation.

1.  The application of MDL is well-motivated, providing an objective, data-driven method for identifying statistically significant patterns without imposing prior linguistic assumptions. The extension of this robust theoretical framework to the multi-language setting is the key contribution.
2.  The experimental validation is compelling. The pairwise analysis on 13 Slavic languages produces a phylogenetic tree that aligns remarkably well with established linguistic classifications, providing strong evidence for the method's validity.
3.  The four-way analysis using the sDL metric is highly insightful. It successfully demonstrates the model's ability to uncover nuanced relationships—such as quantifying the shared structure specific to the CS-PL and RU-BG subgroups—that a simple hierarchical clustering would miss.

**Weaknesses**

1.  The primary weakness is the method's scalability. The authors acknowledge a significant computational bottleneck that limits the current implementation to approximately five languages. This is a major constraint that hinders its applicability to larger language families, and the paper offers no concrete technical plan to overcome this issue.
2.  The proposed "data over-weighting" mechanism feels like an ad-hoc departure from the principled MDL framework. While presented as a pragmatic tool for experts to find less-obvious rules, the selection of the weighting parameter α is subjective and lacks theoretical justification, which undermines the method’s core claim of objectivity.
3.  The deterministic nature of the training procedure is a notable trade-off. While it ensures reproducibility, its potential to converge to local optima and thus miss subtle but valid correspondence rules is a significant limitation that is acknowledged but not thoroughly investigated or mitigated.
