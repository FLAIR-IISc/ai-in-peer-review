**Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper presents a novel framework based on the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle for automatically discovering string-level correspondences among multiple languages simultaneously. The core idea is to find a set of correspondence rules that provides the most succinct description of a given parallel corpus of cognates. An efficient, deterministic, EM-like algorithm is proposed to infer these rules. The framework is notable for its ability to handle more than two languages and to discover rules that apply to any subset of those languages.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A principled MDL-based method for multi-language correspondence discovery. The primary innovation is moving beyond pairwise analysis to a model that can identify and quantify regularities across any subset of the input languages. This is operationalized through the proposed "Shared Description Length" (sDL) metric, which provides a fine-grained quantitative analysis of language similarities.
2.  An efficient, deterministic greedy algorithm for inferring a good set of correspondence rules from data. The training procedure starts from a simple initial state and iteratively merges patterns in a way that maximally compresses the data, effectively learning rules in order of statistical importance.
3.  A practical extension called "data over-weighting," which allows a user to adjust a parameter to encourage the discovery of more granular, longer correspondence rules that may lack sufficient statistical evidence in the original data, thus enabling controllable, exploratory analysis.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Novelty and Significance:** The paper tackles a significant limitation of many existing methods for correspondence analysis, which are often restricted to language pairs. The proposed multi-language framework is a substantial conceptual advance. The ability to quantify the "shared information" or complexity for every subset of languages (e.g., CS-PL, RU-BG, CS-RU, and CS-PL-RU-BG in the four-way experiment) offers a much richer and more nuanced view of linguistic relationships than a single phylogenetic tree can provide. This has strong potential for applications in historical linguistics, typology, and multilingual NLP.

2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The choice of the MDL principle is well-motivated for this problem, as it provides a formal, assumption-light criterion for model selection that naturally balances model complexity with goodness of fit. The formulation of the two-part code for the model and the data is detailed and appears sound. The EM-like optimization procedure is a sensible and practical approach to a computationally hard search problem.

3.  **Convincing Experimental Validation:** The experiments on a set of 13 Slavic languages are well-designed and the results are compelling. The pairwise analysis, using the derived Normalized Compression Distance (NCD), successfully reconstructs a phylogenetic tree that aligns with established linguistic classifications, which serves as an effective sanity check and validation of the method's core distance metric. The four-way analysis and the accompanying sDL visualization (Figure 3) powerfully demonstrate the unique analytical capabilities of the proposed approach, revealing subtle relationships between language subgroups.

4.  **Practical Considerations for Linguists:** The work shows a keen awareness of its potential users. The deterministic nature of the core algorithm ensures reproducibility, which is a valuable feature for scientific inquiry. Furthermore, the introduction of the data over-weighting mechanism is a pragmatic and useful feature, allowing an expert user to guide the analysis towards a desired level of detail, effectively trading statistical objectivity for linguistic interpretability when needed.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Scalability Concerns:** The paper notes that the current implementation is limited to approximately five languages due to bottlenecks in the alignment procedure. Given that the number of language subsets grows exponentially (2^N - 1), the scalability of both the alignment graph construction and the sDL analysis is a significant concern. The paper would be strengthened by a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity with respect to N and a discussion of potential heuristics or pruning strategies that might be necessary to apply the method to a larger number of languages.

2.  **Limitations of Greedy Optimization:** The maximization step employs a greedy strategy, merging the single best pair of rules at each iteration. While efficient and deterministic, this approach is susceptible to finding local optima and may fail to discover complex rules that require multiple non-optimal intermediate merges. The paper acknowledges this possibility briefly ("occasionally we may miss some of the more subtle correspondence rules"), but a more thorough discussion of the potential impact of this search strategy would be welcome.

3.  **Clarity of the MDL Formulation:** While generally well-explained, parts of Section 4.1 describing the code length L(Π) are quite dense. For instance, the final two terms involving the data-to-model code (`LN(TΠ) + log(...)`) are introduced without much intuition. A brief, more accessible explanation of what these terms represent (e.g., encoding the usage counts of the rules in an optimal way) could significantly improve the paper's readability for a broader audience not deeply versed in information theory.

4.  **Lack of Comparative Evaluation:** The work is well-positioned against the conceptual limitations of prior art, but it lacks a direct experimental comparison. While a multi-way comparison is difficult, a pairwise comparison could still be insightful. For example, it would be valuable to see how the correspondences learned for a language pair (e.g., CS-PL) within the four-way model compare to those learned by the pairwise-only model, or to those produced by another established pairwise alignment method. This could help quantify the benefits of the joint multi-language modeling.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the greedy M-step: Have alternative optimization strategies been considered to mitigate the risk of local optima? For instance, would a beam search approach, where the top-k candidate merges are maintained at each step, be computationally feasible and potentially yield better results?

2.  The paper concludes by mentioning that the deterministic approach is promising for linguists. Could the authors elaborate on how the data over-weighting parameter α could be set in a principled way by a linguist, beyond simple trial and error? Is there a way to interpret the value of α, or to observe a point of diminishing returns as it is increased?
