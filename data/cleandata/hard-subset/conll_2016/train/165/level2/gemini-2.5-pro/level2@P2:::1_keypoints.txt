- MDL-based method for automatically discovering orthographic correspondences across multiple languages simultaneously.
- Deterministic EM-like algorithm that starts with single-character correspondences and iteratively merges to minimize total description length.
- Demonstrated on 13 Slavic languages and can discover correspondences that apply to arbitrary subsets of languages.

- Principled, non-parametric MDL framework that models correspondences across any subset of input languages.
- Introduces Shared Description Length (sDL), a novel metric for quantifying and visualizing multi-language similarity.
- Practical algorithm with a "data over-weighting" knob to control rule granularity for expert-driven exploration.

- Novel and theoretically well-motivated application of MDL that avoids strong priors and supports subset correspondences.
- Effective multi-way analysis (e.g., CS/PL vs RU/BG) with sDL visualizations recovering known subgroupings and richer similarity structure.
- Clear, reproducible experiments and pairwise validation that align with established linguistic classifications.
- Deterministic, reproducible tool design with practical features for linguist interaction.

- Scalability is limited (claims up to five languages); complexity appears exponential in N with runtime and memory bottlenecks.
- Evaluation of discovered rules is anecdotal and lacks systematic quantitative comparison to linguistic gold standards.
- Greedy merging strategy may suffer from local optima; no analysis of sensitivity or comparison with non-greedy search methods.
- Insufficient comparison to other computational approaches in historical linguistics and cognate analysis.
