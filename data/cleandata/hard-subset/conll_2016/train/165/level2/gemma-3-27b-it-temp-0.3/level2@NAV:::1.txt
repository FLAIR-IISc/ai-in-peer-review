## Review of “Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL”

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between words across multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea – treating correspondences as statistically significant patterns without pre-defined assumptions – is compelling, and the authors demonstrate its efficacy on a set of Slavic languages. The paper’s primary contribution lies in providing a deterministic algorithm for identifying these correspondences and extending the applicability of such analyses beyond pairwise language comparisons. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation of the method, showing its ability to reflect linguistic classifications and quantify similarity across language subsets. Finally, the exploration of data over-weighting as a mechanism for controlling the granularity of discovered rules is a valuable addition.

The strengths of this work are significant. The MDL framework provides a solid theoretical foundation, and the algorithm appears efficient enough to handle a reasonable number of languages. The experimental results, particularly the phylogenetic tree reconstruction aligning with established linguistic groupings, are convincing. The inclusion of both objective and over-weighted analyses demonstrates a nuanced understanding of the trade-offs between statistical rigor and linguistic interpretability. The detailed presentation of example correspondences and the analysis of shared description lengths offer valuable insights into the method’s behavior.

However, the paper also has weaknesses. The presentation could be more accessible; the initial sections are dense and assume a strong background in information theory and linguistics. While the algorithm's efficiency is mentioned, a more detailed discussion of its computational complexity and scalability would be beneficial. The choice of Slavic languages, while convenient, limits the generalizability claims. Testing on languages with more diverse typological features would strengthen the findings. Furthermore, the paper lacks a thorough comparison to existing methods beyond a brief mention in the introduction. A more direct comparison, perhaps using the same datasets, would better position the contribution. Finally, the discussion of the limitations is brief; a more in-depth exploration of potential biases or failure modes would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the algorithm's computational complexity, particularly as the number of languages increases?
2.  How does the performance of your method compare to existing statistical approaches (e.g., those mentioned in the introduction) on the same datasets?
3.  What are the potential limitations of applying this method to languages with significantly different typological characteristics (e.g., isolating languages vs. fusional languages)?
4.  The data over-weighting technique seems promising. Could you elaborate on how the weighting factor is chosen and whether there is a principled way to determine an optimal value?

**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written overall, but the notation could be clarified in some instances. The figures are helpful and well-presented. Despite the weaknesses, the work presents a novel and promising approach to a challenging problem, and I believe it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points raised above.
