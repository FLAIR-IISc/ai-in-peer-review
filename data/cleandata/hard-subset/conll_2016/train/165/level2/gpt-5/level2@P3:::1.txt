Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based framework for discovering correspondences between strings across two or more languages, without prespecifying the shape or distribution of rules and allowing rules to apply to any subset of languages. The approach encodes alphabets and a correspondence rule table in a two-part code, aligns cognate tuples by shortest paths in a lattice (Dijkstra), and learns rules via an EM-style procedure with greedy rule merging. Experiments on Slavic languages demonstrate pairwise phylogeny via NCD and a four-way analysis using a proposed shared Description Length (sDL) measure.

As seen by this reviewer, the main contributions are:
1) An MDL formulation and deterministic EM-plus-merge algorithm for discovering multi-language, subset-specific correspondence rules directly from orthographic data, extending serial-episode coding ideas to multilingual string correspondences.
2) Information-theoretic analyses of inter-language similarity beyond pairs: NCD-based distances yielding a phylogeny consistent with linguistic groupings, and sDL to quantify structure shared by all subsets among four languages.
3) A practical alignment and training instantiation (shortest-path alignment, partial order reduction, Laplace smoothing) that recovers a range of orthographic/phonological patterns and enables controlled granularity via data overweighting.

Strengths
- Methodological clarity and principled objective: The two-part MDL code is carefully designed, separating model and data costs, with explicit treatment of alphabet coding, rule counts via data-to-model codes, and rule sparsity (encoding only defined entries). The EM-like procedure with greedy merges is well motivated by MDL and frequency arguments.
- Multilingual, subset-aware correspondences: Rules can involve any subset of languages, enabling discovery of patterns that are shared by some but not all languages. The four-way examples show the model learning mixed two-/three-way rules alongside singletons, which is difficult for pairwise-only approaches.
- Empirical signal matches linguistic knowledge: The NCD-based tree over 13 Slavic languages recovers expected groupings and plausible outliers (e.g., BG/MK due to morphological simplification; Polish and Sorbian differences), and script effects are sensibly captured (Serbo-Croatian Latin vs Cyrillic).
- Insightful measures beyond pairwise: The sDL analysis surfaces how much model complexity is shared across all four languages versus pairs and singletons, highlighting RU–BG and CS–PL relationships and quantifying individual overheads (e.g., Czech diacritics).
- Usable without heavy linguistic resources: Operating directly on orthography and supporting deterministic initialization from singletons or seed rules provides accessibility and a path for integrating expert knowledge.

Weaknesses
- Limited quantitative evaluation and baselines: The evaluation is primarily qualitative (examples) and indirect (phylogeny). There is no precision/recall or coverage assessment of discovered correspondences against expert-curated Slavic sound correspondences, nor comparisons to strong baselines (e.g., PMI-based substring alignment, normalized Levenshtein for phylogeny, unsupervised morphological alignment such as Snyder & Barzilay 2008, or pairwise probabilistic models). It is also unclear whether the multilingual setting yields better rules than pairwise training on the same data.
- Scalability constraints: Although the paper states an efficient, deterministic algorithm, the reported practical limit is five languages with hours of computation on modest hardware, with E-step O(|D|·|R|^2) and M-step O(|D|·A^2/2). No pruning/approximation strategies, parallelization, or empirical scaling curves are provided, leaving applicability to larger language sets or vocabularies uncertain.
- Orthography confounds: Modeling raw orthography conflates script and phonology; the Serbo-Croatian script split illustrates sensitivity to encoding. There is no ablation with transliteration or phonemic normalization, and diacritics/digraphs may dominate code lengths and bias correspondences and sDL.
- Ambiguities in derived measures and reproducibility: sDL sums model code lengths L(π) rather than usage-weighted contributions, which may not reflect the magnitude of shared evidence. No uncertainty estimates (e.g., bootstrap supports) are reported for the tree or sDL. Some implementation details are underspecified for replication (e.g., precise merge-compatibility criterion, partial order reduction specifics, stopping condition).
- Overweighting introduces subjectivity without guidance: The α > 1 knob can force inclusion of larger patterns (e.g., eliminating singletons), but the paper lacks principled selection criteria or sensitivity analysis; heavy overweighting risks overfitting and complicates cross-setting comparisons.

Questions to Authors
- How are merge candidates defined and tested for “compatibility” in the M-step? Is there an explicit algorithmic criterion and bound on the number of candidates considered per iteration?
- For NCD, are L(X,Y) models trained jointly on concatenated data with re-optimized codes, and are L(X,X), L(Y,Y) trained separately with identical coding conventions? Please clarify training/setup to ensure valid NCD computation.
- What Laplace correction magnitude is used, and how sensitive are alignments and learned rules to this smoothing?
- What is the stopping criterion (no further MDL decrease, max merges, or convergence in counts)? Are multiple random restarts or alternative initializations considered?
- Could sDL be redefined or complemented with a usage-weighted variant (e.g., ∑ count(π)·L(code(π)))? If tested, how do conclusions change?

Suggestions for Improvement
- Add quantitative validation: (a) compare NCD phylogeny to baselines (normalized Levenshtein/ASJP) with correlation metrics; (b) evaluate discovered correspondences against expert gold sets on a subset with precision/recall; (c) demonstrate benefits of multilingual over pairwise training.
- Provide robustness analyses: bootstrap resampling for tree support and sDL stability; sensitivity to α, initialization (null vs seeded), and smoothing.
- Address orthography vs phonology: include experiments with transliteration to a common script and/or coarse phonemic normalization; report impact on correspondences and NCD.
- Discuss scalability and engineering: empirical runtime/memory as functions of |D|, |R|, and N; explore admissible heuristics (A*, beam search with optimality guarantees), caching, or parallelization; consider pruning strategies for candidate merges.
- Improve reproducibility: release code and data; add pseudocode for alignment and training; specify all hyperparameters and implementation choices.

Overall, the paper offers a principled MDL formulation for multilingual correspondence discovery with compelling qualitative evidence and informative analyses. Strengthening empirical validation, clarifying technical details, and addressing scalability and orthography confounds would substantially improve the submission.
