- Proposes an MDL-based model and deterministic EM/greedy-merge algorithm to discover string-level multi-language correspondence rules without parametric assumptions.
- Represents correspondences as string tuples, learns a code table, and aligns data via shortest-path search; introduces a data-overweighting knob for granularity control.
- Evaluates with NCD-based pairwise distances and a new shared description length (sDL) measure for subset-level similarity, demonstrated on Slavic data.

- MDL formulation is carefully specified, encourages sparsity, and supports rules on arbitrary language subsets.
- Alignment-as-shortest-path plus EM/merge scales conceptually from 2 to N languages and can reveal subset-specific regularities via sDL.
- Learned correspondences and phylogeny are linguistically plausible and interpretable, matching expected Slavic groupings and patterns.
- Deterministic initialization and greedy merges allow reproducibility and incorporation of expert priors.
- Paper addresses runtime/memory costs, algorithmic details (Dijkstra with partial order reduction), and contrasts MDL vs overweighted solutions.

- Lacks comparisons to alternative methods, gold-standard evaluation of correspondences, and quantitative tree-accuracy metrics.
- Operating on orthography conflates script/spelling with phonological correspondences; no phonemic normalization or transliteration experiments reported.
- Scalability is unclear: E-step and alignment complexities are high, experiments are on small word lists and few languages, and detailed profiling/pruning strategies are absent.
- Data-overweighting (α) is subjective with no principled selection, sensitivity analysis, or demonstration of downstream benefit.
- Several modeling details are under-specified (definition of “compatible” merges, safeguards against memorizing whole words, tie-breaking, Laplace correction, encoding of Π).

- Can you provide quantitative evaluation of correspondences against expert annotations or tree comparison to a reference (e.g., RF distance)?
- How do you prevent degenerate long rules (boundary markers, length caps, penalties) and what is the stopping criterion?
- How are “compatible” rules defined for merging, does merging consider context, and how are ties broken?
- How sensitive are results to alphabet/tokenization choices and did you try phonemic transliteration?
- Please report typical alignment graph sizes and runtimes, and detail the partial order reduction and any pruning heuristics.
- What Laplace smoothing value is used for rule counts, and how does smoothing affect results?
- How does α quantitatively impact sDL/NCD and is there a stable range for results?

- Clarify notation in L(Π) (define TΠ, |Π|, ΠCi) and add a short derivation for data-to-model code choices; fix typos and references.
- Include ablations (null vs seeded initialization, with/without overweighting/merging) and sensitivity analyses to strengthen empirical claims.
- Releasing code and processed datasets would improve reproducibility and adoption.
