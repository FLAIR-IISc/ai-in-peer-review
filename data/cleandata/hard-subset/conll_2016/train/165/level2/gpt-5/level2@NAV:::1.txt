Summary: The paper proposes an MDL-based framework (PS-2 code) and a deterministic EM-style algorithm to discover statistically significant string correspondences among two or more languages, allowing rules to apply to any subset of languages. Alignments are found via shortest paths in a weighted graph; model selection is driven by two-part MDL. The method is evaluated on Slavic languages: pairwise analyses yield NCD-based distances and a phylogeny consistent with linguistic classifications; a four-language experiment introduces shared description length (sDL) to quantify structure shared across subsets. An “over-weighting” knob (α) controls granularity of discovered rules.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) A principled, subset-aware MDL formulation and deterministic learning procedure for multi-language string correspondences with graph-based optimal alignment.
2) Information-theoretic analyses for similarity beyond pairs (NCD across pairs; sDL across all subsets), yielding interpretable, quantitative insights.
3) Empirical demonstration on Slavic data showing non-trivial multi-character correspondences and a plausible phylogeny.

Strengths:
- Principled and interpretable: The MDL objective regularizes model complexity without ad hoc priors and naturally suppresses redundant rules; subset-specific rules support fine-grained linguistic interpretations. The examples (e.g., (cz,č), (ла,оло), stem-level correspondences) illustrate meaningful structure.
- Beyond pairwise: The four-way experiment and sDL metric provide a clear, quantitative view of structure shared by arbitrary subsets—something pairwise-only methods miss. The RU–BG and CS–PL groupings emerge coherently.
- Empirical plausibility: The NCD matrix produces a phylogeny consistent with known Slavic groupings and even script effects (Latin vs Cyrillic Serbo-Croatian), supporting usefulness for exploratory comparative linguistics.
- Deterministic learning with optional prior knowledge: The merge-based M-step and ability to seed rules encourage reproducibility and expert steering.

Weaknesses:
- Limited quantitative validation: No comparison to baselines (e.g., Bayesian sound correspondences, alignment-based cognate models) or intrinsic metrics (precision/recall against curated correspondences), making it hard to assess gains.
- Scalability constraints: The E-step O(|D|·|R|^2) and memory bottlenecks limit N to ≲5; guidance on scaling (pruning, beam search) is absent.
- Data and representation issues: Experiments rely on orthography and cognate lists, conflating script/orthographic idiosyncrasies with phonology; no normalization or robustness tests to borrowing/noise.
- Subjective over-weighting: The α parameter lacks principled selection; risks overfitting and undermines MDL’s objectivity without diagnostics or ablations.

Questions to authors:
- How should α be chosen in practice, and can you provide criteria or validation curves to prevent overfitting?
- Can you report comparisons to at least one established baseline and an intrinsic evaluation against a small gold set of correspondences?
- What pruning/approximation strategies could improve scalability in N and rule length?
- How sensitive are results to orthographic normalization (e.g., phonemic transcriptions) and to missing entries in tuples?
