Summary and claimed contributions
- An MDL-based framework (PS-2 code) for discovering correspondences between strings across two or more languages, allowing rules that cover arbitrary subsets of languages. The method jointly optimizes a two-part code for model and data, with rule usages determining code lengths.
- A deterministic EM-like learning procedure that alternates between shortest-path alignments (via Dijkstra) and greedy rule merges that reduce total description length; plus a practical “data overweighting” knob to control granularity of discovered rules.
- Empirical demonstration on Slavic languages: (i) pairwise NCD-based phylogeny broadly consistent with accepted groupings; (ii) qualitative correspondences that capture orthographic/phonological patterns; (iii) a four-way analysis introducing “shared Description Length” (sDL) to quantify structure shared by language subsets.

Strengths
- Generality beyond pairwise: The model naturally handles rules spanning any subset of languages and yields interpretable multiway correspondences. The sDL analysis is a novel, informative way to quantify similarities at different subset granularities.
- Sound MDL framing: Clear two-part code with explicit accounting for model cost (alphabets, rule table, symbol codes) and data cost (alignments via rule usages). The approach follows established MDL pattern-mining practices and avoids strong parametric assumptions.
- Qualitative linguistic plausibility: Examples recover well-known correspondences (e.g., cz–č, g–h, palatalizations, liquid metathesis) and reflect known groupings (CS–PL, RU–BG). The NCD-derived tree broadly matches Slavic classifications, including plausible outliers.
- Determinism and extensibility: Starting from null alignment with deterministic updates is attractive for reproducibility and for injecting linguistic priors via initialization.
- Transparent discussion of trade-offs: The paper is upfront about scalability limits and motivates overweighting as a way to elicit larger, lower-frequency patterns when data are scarce.

Weaknesses
- Limited empirical validation and lack of baselines: Beyond the phylogeny and examples, there is no quantitative evaluation of rule quality (e.g., precision/recall against curated correspondences), held-out codelengths, or performance on an extrinsic task (e.g., cognate identification, prediction/reconstruction). No comparison to prior statistical models for sound correspondence/alignment (e.g., PHMMs/Pair HMMs, ALINE, Bayesian models for sound change, Snyder & Barzilay 2008). This makes it hard to assess substantive gains due to the MDL approach.
- Overweighting undermines the “objective MDL” claim: While practical, optimizing L(M)+αL(D|M) or per-tuple weights departs from MDL’s principled balance and risks cherry-picking larger patterns. The paper uses overweighted alignments to illustrate results but does not quantify how α affects rule length distributions, alignments, or downstream similarity measures.
- Technical clarity and justification gaps: The code specification (especially the L(Π) terms) is not fully justified. The role of the “data-to-model” components and combinatorial terms (e.g., LN(TΠ)+log((TΠ−1 choose |Π|−1))) needs a clearer derivation and relation to standard MDL/KT coding. There is a risk of double-counting between model and data terms via code(π). The NCD computation with a learned compressor needs more detail (e.g., how L(X,X) and L(X,Y) are computed; whether models are trained jointly or separately and then reused).
- Scalability and convergence: The E-step O(|D|·|R|^2) and greedy M-step O(|D|·A^2/2) suggest rapid growth with rule inventory and string lengths. Experiments cap at 4 languages for detailed analysis, with “few hours” runtime for up to 5. There is no study of convergence behavior, number of iterations, or strategies (beam search/annealing) to escape local optima induced by greedy merges.
- Data and preprocessing choices: Using raw orthography across scripts (Latin vs Cyrillic) conflates script with linguistic similarity (as the authors note for Serbo-Croatian). No phonemic normalization/transliteration or control analyses are presented. This may inflate NCD distances and influence learned symbol codes. Also, Swadesh lists from Wiktionary and internationalisms may bias results; the datasets and their noise characteristics are not analyzed.

Questions to authors
- NCD details: Precisely how are L(X,X), L(Y,Y), and L(X,Y) computed? Is a single joint model trained on the concatenated data or are separate models trained and then used to encode? How are alphabets handled across scripts in joint encodings?
- Coding derivations: Can you provide a more formal derivation/justification for L(Π), especially the data-to-model code terms and why they are appropriate here? How do you ensure no double counting between model and data code lengths?
- Model capacity control: What prevents the model from memorizing entire words as rules when α is large or data are small? Can you report the learned rule-length distributions under different α, and the impact on held-out codelength?
- Convergence: How many EM iterations are typically required? Do you ever reverse merges or perform multiple candidate merges per iteration? Have you tried annealing or beam search, and with what effect?
- Sensitivity to preprocessing: How do results change with transliteration to a common phonemic/phonetic inventory (e.g., IPA or language-specific grapheme-to-phoneme rules)? Does the SC Latin/Cyrillic duplication persist after transliteration?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and processed datasets? If not, can you detail hyperparameters (Laplace smoothing constants, stopping criteria) and tie-breaking in merges/alignments?

Additional comments
- Consider a quantitative evaluation: (i) annotate a subset of correspondences and measure precision/recall; (ii) leave-one-language-out reconstruction and report accuracy; (iii) compare held-out codelengths across α values; (iv) compare against pairwise PHMM/ALINE and a simple baseline (character n-gram edit models).
- Present ablations: without data-to-model code; without subset-specific rule counts; without Laplace smoothing; with/without overweighting.
- Normalize scripts or use phonemic transcription to reduce orthographic confounds; report results both ways.
- Clarify complexity and memory usage with empirical scaling plots (|D|, N, |Σ|).
- Improve references and formatting (several entries are incomplete/misformatted). Cite additional related work on cognate alignment and sound change induction where relevant.
- Minor editorial issues: typos (e.g., spacing, punctuation), inconsistent variable naming, and figure/table captions could be more self-contained.
