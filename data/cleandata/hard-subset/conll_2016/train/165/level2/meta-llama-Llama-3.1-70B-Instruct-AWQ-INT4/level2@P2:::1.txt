**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes an approach to discover correspondences between words from multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors present an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules and demonstrate its efficacy through experiments on Slavic languages. The approach is based on a two-part MDL code, which consists of a stack of two-part codes, and uses a deterministic algorithm to learn correspondence rules.

**Main Contributions**

1. **MDL-based approach for correspondence discovery**: The paper introduces an MDL-based approach for discovering correspondences between words from multiple languages, which is a novel application of the MDL principle.
2. **Efficient algorithm for inferring correspondence rules**: The authors present an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules, which is based on a two-part MDL code and uses a deterministic approach.
3. **Experiments on Slavic languages**: The paper demonstrates the efficacy of the approach through experiments on Slavic languages, including pairwise and four-way analyses.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel application of MDL principle**: The paper introduces a novel application of the MDL principle to correspondence discovery, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. **Efficient algorithm**: The algorithm presented in the paper is efficient and can handle large datasets, making it a practical solution for correspondence discovery.
3. **Experiments on Slavic languages**: The experiments on Slavic languages demonstrate the efficacy of the approach and provide insights into the linguistic structure of these languages.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited evaluation**: The paper only evaluates the approach on Slavic languages, and it is unclear how well it would perform on other language families.
2. **No comparison to state-of-the-art methods**: The paper does not compare the proposed approach to state-of-the-art methods for correspondence discovery, making it difficult to assess its performance relative to other approaches.
3. **No discussion of limitations**: The paper does not discuss the limitations of the approach, such as its ability to handle noisy or incomplete data.
