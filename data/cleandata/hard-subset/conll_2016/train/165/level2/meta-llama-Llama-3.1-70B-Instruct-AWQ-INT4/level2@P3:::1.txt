**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors propose an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules from data, which can be applied to any subset of languages without imposing arbitrary assumptions about their shape or distribution. The approach is evaluated on a dataset of Slavic languages, demonstrating its ability to automatically discover non-trivial associations and quantify linguistic similarity between languages.

**Main Contributions**

1. **MDL-based approach**: The paper introduces a novel application of the MDL principle to discover correspondences between multiple languages, providing a statistically well-founded approach to identifying the best model for given data.
2. **Efficient algorithm**: The authors propose an efficient, deterministic algorithm for inferring correspondence rules from data, which can be applied to any subset of languages.
3. **Quantification of linguistic similarity**: The approach allows for the quantification of linguistic similarity between languages, providing a more detailed analysis of linguistic relationships than pairwise comparisons.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel application of MDL**: The paper presents a new application of the MDL principle to a complex problem in natural language processing, demonstrating its potential for discovering correspondences between multiple languages.
2. **Efficient algorithm**: The proposed algorithm is efficient and deterministic, making it suitable for large-scale applications.
3. **Quantification of linguistic similarity**: The approach provides a more detailed analysis of linguistic relationships than pairwise comparisons, allowing for a better understanding of linguistic similarities and differences.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Computational complexity**: The algorithm's computational complexity may limit its applicability to very large datasets or high-dimensional data.
2. **Data quality**: The quality of the input data may significantly impact the accuracy of the results, and the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the data quality.
3. **Limited evaluation**: The evaluation of the approach is limited to a single dataset of Slavic languages, and further evaluation on other languages and datasets is necessary to fully assess its generalizability.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the computational complexity of the algorithm to make it more scalable to large datasets?
2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the data quality and its impact on the results?
3. How do you plan to evaluate the approach on other languages and datasets to assess its generalizability?

**Additional Comments**

The paper presents a novel and promising approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages. However, further evaluation and refinement of the approach are necessary to fully assess its potential and limitations.
