**Summary of the Paper**

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors propose an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules from data, which can be used to analyze and quantify linguistic similarity between languages. The approach is based on a two-part MDL code that balances the complexity of the model with that of the data, allowing for the discovery of objective string-level correspondences. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach through experiments on Slavic languages, showing that it can be used to construct a sensible phylogeny, discover correspondences, and quantify similarity not only between pairs but also between all subsets of analyzed languages.

**Main Contributions**

1. **MDL-based approach**: The authors propose a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the MDL principle, which provides a statistically well-founded method for identifying the best model for given data.
2. **Efficient algorithm**: The authors develop an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules from data, which can be used to analyze and quantify linguistic similarity between languages.
3. **Experimental results**: The authors demonstrate the efficacy of their approach through experiments on Slavic languages, showing that it can be used to construct a sensible phylogeny, discover correspondences, and quantify similarity not only between pairs but also between all subsets of analyzed languages.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach**: The authors propose a novel approach to discovering correspondences between multiple languages, which provides a statistically well-founded method for identifying the best model for given data.
2. **Efficient algorithm**: The algorithm developed by the authors is efficient and can be used to analyze and quantify linguistic similarity between languages.
3. **Experimental results**: The authors provide experimental results that demonstrate the efficacy of their approach, showing that it can be used to construct a sensible phylogeny, discover correspondences, and quantify similarity not only between pairs but also between all subsets of analyzed languages.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited scope**: The authors focus on Slavic languages, and it is unclear whether their approach can be applied to other language families.
2. **Assumptions**: The authors assume that the data is in raw orthographic form, without transcriptions of any kind, which may not always be the case.
3. **Computational complexity**: The authors note that their algorithm has high computational complexity, which may limit its applicability to large datasets.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How can the authors' approach be extended to other language families?
2. How can the authors' approach be modified to handle data that is not in raw orthographic form?
3. How can the authors' approach be optimized to reduce computational complexity and make it more applicable to large datasets?
