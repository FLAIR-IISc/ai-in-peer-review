

**Summary and Contributions**
This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering statistically significant correspondences between multiple languages without making arbitrary assumptions about their distribution. The main contributions are: 1) A novel MDL framework for correspondence discovery that treats correspondences as associated character strings without underlying distribution assumptions; 2) An efficient, deterministic algorithm for learning correspondence rules directly from data; 3) A practical method for controlling the level of detail in discovered correspondences through data over-weighting.

**Strengths**
1. The paper provides a theoretically sound application of MDL to the problem of cross-lingual correspondence discovery. The two-part MDL code design is well-motivated and properly accounts for model complexity and data description, avoiding the pitfalls of prior methods that impose arbitrary distributional assumptions. The algorithm's deterministic nature is particularly valuable for linguistic analysis where reproducibility is essential.

2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-structured, demonstrating the method's effectiveness in both pairwise and multi-language analyses. The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values aligns well with established linguistic classifications of Slavic languages, providing strong empirical validation of the approach. The analysis of shared description lengths (sDL) for language subsets offers a novel, information-theoretic way to quantify linguistic similarities beyond pairwise comparisons.

3. The paper's introduction of "data over-weighting" is a particularly valuable practical contribution. It allows linguists to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences by adjusting data weights, making the approach more adaptable to different linguistic analysis needs. The examples in Table 3 effectively demonstrate how this technique reveals correspondences at different levels of granularity.

**Weaknesses**
1. The paper fails to sufficiently establish novelty relative to prior work. While the authors cite Wettig et al. (2011) who also used MDL for linguistic analysis, they don't clearly differentiate their approach from this prior work or from other statistical approaches to correspondence discovery. The paper claims their method "does not impose arbitrary assumptions," but the paper itself uses the assumption that correspondences can be represented as string alignments, which is not explicitly compared to other approaches.

2. The evaluation lacks proper baselines for comparison. The paper demonstrates that their method produces linguistically meaningful correspondences, but doesn't show that these correspondences are superior to those found by simpler methods (e.g., string alignment with edit distance or standard NCD). Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess whether the added complexity of the MDL approach provides meaningful advantages.

3. The paper claims the method can handle "any subset of languages" but provides insufficient evidence for scalability. The four-language experiment is the most complex shown, and the paper doesn't address how the algorithm's computational complexity scales with the number of languages (N). The claim that it can "efficiently analyze more than two languages at a time" is unsupported by data showing runtime or memory usage for N > 4.

4. The paper doesn't provide a clear metric for "statistical significance" of discovered correspondences. While the MDL framework theoretically provides a measure of significance, the paper doesn't explain how the authors determine which correspondences are "important" versus "redundant" in their analyses. The examples in Table 3 show interesting patterns, but it's unclear how these were selected or validated.

**Questions to Authors**
1. How does your method specifically differ from Wettig et al.'s (2011) MDL-based approach to linguistic analysis, and what advantages does your approach offer over theirs?

2. Could you provide a quantitative comparison between your method and simpler string alignment approaches (e.g., using edit distance or standard NCD) on the same Slavic language data, to demonstrate the added value of your MDL approach?

3. The paper claims the method can handle "any subset of languages" but shows experiments only with pairs and four languages. Could you provide estimates of computational complexity and runtime as a function of the number of languages (N), to better assess scalability?
