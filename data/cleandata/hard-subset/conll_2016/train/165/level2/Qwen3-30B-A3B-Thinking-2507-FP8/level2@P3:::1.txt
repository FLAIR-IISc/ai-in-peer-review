

Summary of the paper: The work introduces an MDL-based approach for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without imposing arbitrary assumptions about their distribution or shape. It proposes an efficient algorithm that can handle any subset of languages simultaneously, allowing for both quantitative and qualitative analysis of linguistic similarity. The method is evaluated on Slavic languages, demonstrating the ability to recover linguistically meaningful correspondences and construct phylogenetic trees consistent with established linguistic classifications.

Main contributions: The primary contribution is an MDL-based framework for discovering linguistic correspondences between multiple languages without distributional assumptions. A secondary contribution is an efficient algorithm that can handle multiple languages simultaneously and control the level of detail through data over-weighting. A third contribution is the demonstration of the method's ability to quantify similarity between all subsets of analyzed languages.

Strengths: The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature by providing a method for discovering correspondences across multiple languages without restrictive assumptions. The MDL approach is well-motivated and theoretically sound for this task. The evaluation on Slavic languages is comprehensive, showing that the method recovers linguistically meaningful correspondences and produces phylogenetic trees that align with established linguistic classifications. The data over-weighting technique provides valuable flexibility for controlling the granularity of discovered correspondences, which is particularly useful for linguistic analysis. The paper demonstrates the method's ability to quantify similarities between all language subsets simultaneously, providing richer insights than pairwise approaches.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks a direct comparison with state-of-the-art methods for multi-language correspondence discovery. While previous work is cited, the paper does not quantify how the proposed method improves upon existing approaches, making it difficult to assess the novelty and significance of the contributions. The computational complexity analysis of the algorithm is insufficient; the paper mentions processing up to five languages on a modest machine but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of scalability with respect to the number of languages or dataset size. The evaluation is limited to Slavic languages, and the paper doesn't discuss potential challenges when applying the method to other language families or languages with significantly different structures.

Questions to Authors: How does the proposed method compare quantitatively to existing approaches for multi-language correspondence discovery? What are the computational bottlenecks when scaling the algorithm to more than five languages, and how might these be addressed? Could the authors provide examples of correspondence discovery in non-Slavic language families to demonstrate broader applicability?

Additional comments: The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the method handles languages with very different writing systems or linguistic structures. While the data over-weighting technique is a useful addition, the paper could better explain the theoretical justification for this approach, as it moves away from the strict MDL framework. The connection between the discovered correspondences and linguistic theory could be strengthened with more specific examples of how these correspondences might inform linguistic hypotheses. The evaluation of the method's ability to handle noisy or incomplete data could be expanded to address a common challenge in linguistic data collection.
