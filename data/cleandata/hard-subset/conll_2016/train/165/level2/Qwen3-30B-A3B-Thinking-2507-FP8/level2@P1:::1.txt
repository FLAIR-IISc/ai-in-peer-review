

**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

The paper introduces an MDL-based approach for discovering string-level correspondences between multiple languages without making assumptions about their distribution or shape. The main contributions are: (1) a principled MDL framework for discovering linguistically meaningful correspondences across multiple languages; (2) an efficient algorithm that grows correspondence rules according to their statistical significance; and (3) experimental validation showing the method's ability to quantify linguistic similarities across language subsets, including identifying linguistic subgroups.

**Strengths**

1. The paper addresses a significant gap in computational linguistics: the lack of methods for discovering correspondences across more than two languages without restrictive assumptions. The MDL approach is particularly well-suited to this problem, providing an objective, statistically grounded method that avoids the pitfalls of prior approaches.

2. The algorithmic development is thorough and well-justified. The paper provides clear explanations of the MDL code design, the training procedure, and the alignment strategy. The inclusion of the data over-weighting technique is particularly valuable, as it gives linguists control over the level of detail in the discovered correspondences.

3. The experimental validation is strong and well-executed. The pairwise analysis successfully reconstructs known linguistic groupings, and the four-way analysis demonstrates the method's unique ability to quantify similarities across multiple language subsets simultaneously. The phylogenetic tree constructed from NCD values aligns well with established linguistic classifications.

4. The paper provides rich qualitative insights through example correspondences, showing that the method discovers both phonological and orthographic patterns at varying levels of granularity. The comparison between objective and over-weighted models effectively demonstrates the flexibility of the approach.

5. The paper's technical presentation is clear and accessible. The mathematical formulations are well-motivated, and the authors provide sufficient detail for others to implement and build upon their work.

**Weaknesses**

1. The evaluation is limited to Slavic languages, which are relatively closely related. It would strengthen the paper to include experiments with more diverse language families (e.g., Indo-European vs. Sino-Tibetan) to better demonstrate the method's generalizability.

2. The paper does not provide a detailed comparison with existing methods for discovering language correspondences. A thorough comparison with state-of-the-art approaches would better establish the novelty and advantages of the proposed method, particularly regarding its ability to handle multiple languages simultaneously.

3. The computational complexity analysis is limited. While the paper mentions the algorithm can handle up to five languages within a few hours, it would be valuable to provide a more detailed analysis of scalability with respect to the number of languages and data size, including potential bottlenecks.

4. The paper does not adequately address how the method handles languages with very different writing systems or morphological structures. For example, how would the method perform with languages using Arabic script versus Latin script, or with agglutinative languages versus fusional languages?

**Questions to Authors**

1. How would you expect your method to perform on languages with significantly different writing systems (e.g., Arabic and English) or with very different morphological structures? Could you provide insights into potential challenges and how your method might be adapted to address them?

2. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your approach with existing methods for discovering language correspondences, particularly those that handle multiple languages? How does your method specifically improve upon these approaches?

3. The paper mentions that the algorithm can process up to five languages within a few hours on a standard desktop. Could you provide more details about the scalability of your approach to larger numbers of languages (e.g., 10+ languages) and larger datasets? What are the main computational bottlenecks?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and makes a valuable contribution to computational linguistics. The method has clear potential for applications in historical linguistics, language teaching, and multilingual NLP. I would encourage the authors to address the scalability concerns and include more diverse language experiments in future work. The data over-weighting technique is particularly promising and deserves further exploration in linguistic applications.
