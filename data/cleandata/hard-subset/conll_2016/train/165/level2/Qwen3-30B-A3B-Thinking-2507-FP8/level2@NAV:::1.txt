

Summary: This paper proposes an MDL-based approach for discovering statistically significant correspondences between words from multiple languages, without making arbitrary assumptions about the distribution or shape of correspondences. The authors claim their method can automatically discover non-trivial associations between any subset of languages, allowing for both quantitative and qualitative analysis.

Main Contributions: 1) A novel MDL-based algorithm for discovering correspondence rules between multiple languages without distributional assumptions; 2) An efficient implementation that handles up to five languages with reasonable computational resources; 3) A demonstration of how the method quantifies linguistic similarity across multiple language subsets simultaneously.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling case for using MDL to avoid arbitrary assumptions about correspondence patterns, which is a significant limitation in existing approaches. The method's ability to discover correspondences at different granularities through data over-weighting is particularly valuable for linguistic analysis. The experimental results with Slavic languages demonstrate the method's ability to recover expected linguistic groupings and produce meaningful correspondence rules that align with linguistic knowledge (e.g., identifying Czech-Polish and Russian-Bulgarian subgroups).

Weaknesses: The paper lacks a thorough comparison with state-of-the-art methods for multilingual correspondence discovery, making it difficult to assess the relative performance gains. The experimental validation is limited to Slavic languages, with no demonstration of applicability to other language families or non-linguistic domains. The paper does not sufficiently address the computational complexity limitations (only handling up to five languages) or provide clear guidance on how to determine appropriate data over-weighting parameters for different language pairs.

Questions to Authors: How does your method compare quantitatively to other recent approaches for multilingual correspondence discovery? What are the specific computational bottlenecks that prevent scaling beyond five languages, and how might these be addressed? Can you provide more concrete examples of how linguists would apply the discovered correspondence rules in practical linguistic research?
