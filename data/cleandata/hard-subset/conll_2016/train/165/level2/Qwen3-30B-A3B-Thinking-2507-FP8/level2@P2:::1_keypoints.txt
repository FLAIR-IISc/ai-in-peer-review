1) Proposes an MDL framework treating correspondences as character strings without assuming underlying distributions.  
2) Introduces an efficient, deterministic algorithm to learn correspondence rules directly from data.  
3) Offers data over-weighting to control the level of detail in discovered correspondences.  
4) The MDL two-part code is theoretically sound, accounting for model complexity and data description; determinism aids reproducibility.  
5) Experiments are comprehensive; NCD-based phylogeny matches known Slavic classifications and sDL provides a novel subset similarity metric.  
6) Data over-weighting is a practical contribution, demonstrated to reveal correspondences at different granularities.  
7) Novelty is unclear relative to prior MDL work (e.g., Wettig et al. 2011) and other statistical approaches; paper still assumes string-alignment representation.  
8) Lacks proper baselines—no quantitative comparison to simpler methods like edit-distance alignments or standard NCD.  
9) Scalability claims are unsupported: only up to four-language experiments and no runtime or memory scaling analysis.  
10) No clear metric or procedure for declaring correspondences statistically significant or distinguishing important vs. redundant correspondences.  
11) How exactly does your method differ from Wettig et al. (2011) and what are the concrete advantages?  
12) Can you provide quantitative comparisons to simpler string-alignment baselines on the same Slavic data?  
13) Please supply computational complexity estimates or empirical runtime/memory results as a function of number of languages (N).
