- Summary: MDL-based method to discover statistically significant word correspondences across multiple languages without assuming correspondence distributions, enabling quantitative and qualitative analysis.
- Contribution: Novel MDL-based algorithm for multilingual correspondence discovery without distributional assumptions.
- Contribution: Efficient implementation that handles up to five languages with reasonable computational resources.
- Contribution: Demonstration that the method quantifies linguistic similarity across multiple language subsets simultaneously.
- Strength: MDL formulation avoids arbitrary assumptions about correspondence patterns, addressing a limitation of prior work.
- Strength: Can discover correspondences at different granularities via data over-weighting, useful for linguistic analysis.
- Strength: Experiments on Slavic languages recover expected groupings and produce linguistically meaningful rules (e.g., Czech–Polish, Russian–Bulgarian).
- Weakness: No thorough comparison with state-of-the-art multilingual correspondence discovery methods.
- Weakness: Experimental validation limited to Slavic languages; no demonstrations on other families or non-linguistic domains.
- Weakness: Insufficient treatment of computational complexity/scalability (only up to five languages) and lack of guidance on choosing data over-weighting parameters.
- Question: How does the method compare quantitatively to recent approaches for multilingual correspondence discovery?
- Question: What are the specific computational bottlenecks preventing scaling beyond five languages, and how might they be addressed?
- Question: Can the authors provide concrete examples of how linguists would apply the discovered correspondence rules in practical research?
