- Proposes an MDL-based method that models substring-matching rules to discover cross-language word correspondences without strong prior assumptions.  
- Claims capability to discover correspondences across any subset of languages, enabling multi-language (not just pairwise) similarity analysis.  
- Empirically validated on 13 Slavic languages, producing linguistically interpretable correspondences and phylogenetic groupings.

- Strength: Strong theoretical foundation—MDL balances model complexity and data fit and avoids arbitrary assumptions about correspondence shapes.  
- Strength: Novel ability to analyze similarity across all language subsets simultaneously, offering information-theoretic measures beyond pairwise comparisons.  
- Strength: Empirical results (phylogeny and example rules) align with established linguistic classifications and reveal interpretable phonological/orthographic patterns.

- Weakness: Fails to compare to established cognate-detection/borrowing methods (e.g., List & Moran 2013; Kitchen et al. 2009; Bouchard-Côté et al.), weakening evaluation.  
- Weakness: Severe memory/runtime bottleneck limiting experiments to ≤5 languages, undermining claims about handling “multiple languages.”  
- Weakness: Uses raw orthographic data without phonetic transcription, risking conflation of script changes with true linguistic correspondences.  
- Weakness: Several technical details are unclear (e.g., “optimal unigram for symbol usages,” the merging step, EM convergence/stopping), harming reproducibility.  
- Weakness: Swadesh lists mix cognate and non-cognate words, which may introduce noise and affect the validity of discovered correspondences.

- Question: What are the specific computational bottlenecks, and how can the method be scaled to more than 5 languages?  
- Question: How do you address conflation of script changes when using orthography, and would phonetic transcriptions yield different results?  
- Question: Please clarify the meaning and role of the “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules.”  
- Question: How does your approach differ from and improve upon List & Moran 2013 (and related work), and would their method yield similar results?  
- Question: How were mixed cognate/non-cognate Swadesh lists handled, and what impact does this noise have on discovered correspondences?
