

This paper proposes an MDL-based method for discovering cross-language word correspondences, modeling substring-matching rules and optimizing the trade-off between model complexity and data description length. The authors employ Expectation-Maximization to train the model and evaluate it on 13 Slavic languages using distance measures, phylogenetic trees, and example correspondences.

The primary contribution is the principled MDL approach that discovers correspondences without imposing arbitrary assumptions about their distribution, allowing for objective, unbiased string-level linguistic analysis. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of quantifying similarity across all language subsets simultaneously, not just pairwise. The third contribution is the experimental validation showing the method's ability to discover meaningful linguistic patterns and construct phylogenetic trees that align with linguistic classifications.

Strengths include the well-motivated use of MDL for correspondence discovery, the thorough experimental evaluation on Slavic languages showing the method's ability to identify meaningful linguistic patterns, and the demonstration of quantifying similarity across all language subsets rather than just pairwise comparisons.

Major weaknesses include the lack of comparison to related work on borrowing and cognate detection (e.g., List & Moran 2013, Kitchen et al. 2009), which significantly weakens the novelty claim. The alignment procedure has a severe memory/runtime bottleneck limiting analysis to at most five languages, which restricts the method's practical applicability. Using orthographic data instead of phonetic transcriptions risks conflating script changes with linguistic correspondences. Additionally, the phylogenetic trees are built with neighbor joining, a method with known disadvantages (e.g., manual rooting), and the paper fails to clarify whether EM runs to convergence or uses another stopping criterion. Two technical points remain unclear: the meaning of "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" and the merging step in the maximization update.

The paper would benefit from comparing with Bayesian phylogenetic inference tools (e.g., MrBayes) due to the resemblance to Bayesian objectives, and addressing the mixing of cognate and non-cognate Swadesh lists in the dataset. The editorial error "focus in on" (line 440) should be corrected to "focus on."
