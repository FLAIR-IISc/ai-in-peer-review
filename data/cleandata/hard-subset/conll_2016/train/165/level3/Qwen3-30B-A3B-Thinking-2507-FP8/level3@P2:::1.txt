

**Summary and Contributions**

The paper proposes an MDL-based approach to discover cross-language word correspondences by modeling substring-matching rules and optimizing the trade-off between model complexity and data description length. The main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. A novel method for discovering statistically significant correspondence rules between any subset of multiple languages simultaneously, without assuming a specific distribution of correspondences.
2. An efficient algorithm using Expectation-Maximization to learn correspondence rules from raw orthographic data, with an implementation that scales to five languages.
3. A demonstration of the method's efficacy through detailed linguistic analyses of Slavic languages, including phylogenetic tree construction and quantification of similarities across all language subsets.

**Strengths**

1. The paper's core methodology is well-motivated and well-executed. The use of MDL provides a theoretically sound foundation for discovering correspondences without imposing arbitrary assumptions about their distribution, which is a significant improvement over previous approaches. The authors clearly articulate why MDL is appropriate for this problem, and the implementation of the two-part MDL code is technically sound.

2. The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-structured. The pairwise analysis of 13 Slavic languages produces a phylogenetic tree that aligns well with linguistic classifications, demonstrating the method's ability to capture meaningful linguistic relationships. The four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian languages reveals nuanced patterns of similarity that would be difficult to capture with pairwise methods alone, showcasing the method's unique capability.

3. The approach's flexibility is a major strength. The authors demonstrate how data over-weighting can be used to control the level of detail in the discovered correspondences, allowing linguistic experts to tailor the analysis to their specific needs. This practical consideration makes the method more usable for real linguistic applications than approaches that produce only one fixed level of detail.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper lacks critical comparison to related work in computational historical linguistics. The authors fail to compare their approach with established methods for cognate detection and phylogenetic inference (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen et al., List & Moran 2013), which would help contextualize their contribution and demonstrate its novelty. The paper mentions Bayesian phylogenetic inference tools as a potential comparison point but doesn't provide actual comparisons.

2. The computational limitations of the approach are significant. The alignment procedure has major memory/runtime bottlenecks, currently restricting the method to at most five languages. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't provide clear justification for why this constraint is acceptable for the intended applications or how they plan to address it in future work.

3. The use of raw orthographic data rather than phonetic transcriptions risks conflating script-related changes with genuine linguistic correspondences. For example, the transition from Cyrillic to Latin script in Serbo-Croatian would likely produce spurious correspondences that aren't linguistically meaningful. This is a fundamental methodological weakness that undermines the validity of the results.

4. Several technical aspects of the method are unclear. The paper doesn't adequately explain key components such as "the optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" or the exact mechanics of the merging step in the maximization update. These omissions make it difficult to fully assess the method's validity and reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How does your method specifically address the problem of linguistic borrowing, which can produce spurious correspondences that are not indicative of true historical relationships? How do you plan to distinguish between inherited and borrowed correspondences?

2. The paper uses a mixture of Swadesh lists (which contain cognates) and internationalisms (which may not be cognate). How did you ensure that the data set doesn't contain non-cognate entries that could distort your results? Have you conducted any analysis to verify the cognate status of your data?

3. Could you elaborate on the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" mentioned in Section 4.1.1? Specifically, what is the exact procedure for computing this unigram, and how does it differ from standard unigram models?

4. The paper mentions that the EM algorithm may not run to convergence. What stopping criterion did you use, and how did you validate that the results are stable with respect to this criterion?
