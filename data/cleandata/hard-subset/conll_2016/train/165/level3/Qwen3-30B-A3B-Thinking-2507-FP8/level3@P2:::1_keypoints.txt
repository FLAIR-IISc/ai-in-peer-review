- Proposes an MDL-based method to discover cross-language substring-matching correspondence rules, optimizing model complexity versus data description length.
- Novel method discovers statistically significant correspondence rules among any subset of languages without assuming a specific correspondence distribution.
- Presents an EM-based algorithm to learn correspondence rules from raw orthographic data with an implementation scaling up to five languages.
- Demonstrates efficacy with detailed analyses of Slavic languages, including phylogenetic tree construction and similarity quantification across language subsets.
- Strength: MDL formulation is well-motivated and technically sound, avoiding arbitrary distributional assumptions.
- Strength: Comprehensive empirical evaluation; pairwise and four-way analyses capture meaningful and nuanced linguistic relationships.
- Strength: Method is flexible—data over-weighting controls detail level, making results tunable for linguistic use.
- Weakness: Lacks critical comparisons to established computational historical linguistics methods (cognate detection, Bayesian phylogenetics).
- Weakness: Computational bottlenecks (memory/runtime) restrict applicability to at most five languages, with no clear mitigation plan.
- Weakness: Using raw orthography risks conflating script-induced changes with genuine linguistic correspondences.
- Weakness: Key technical details are unclear (e.g., "optimal unigram" computation and merging step mechanics), hindering reproducibility.
- Question: How does the method address linguistic borrowing versus inherited correspondences?
- Question: How were mixed-data issues (Swadesh cognates vs internationalisms) handled to avoid non-cognate distortion?
- Question: Please clarify the exact procedure for computing the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" and how it differs from standard unigram models.
- Question: What EM stopping criterion was used, and how was stability of results with respect to convergence assessed?
