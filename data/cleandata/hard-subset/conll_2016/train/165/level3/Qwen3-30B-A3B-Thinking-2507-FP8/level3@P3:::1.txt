

# Review of "Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL" for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes an MDL-based method for discovering cross-language word correspondences, modeling substring-matching rules and optimizing model vs. data description length. The primary contribution is a principled approach to discovering linguistic correspondences without making strong assumptions about their distribution, which is a significant improvement over existing pairwise methods. The second contribution is the demonstration of how this approach enables fine-grained analysis of language similarity across multiple subsets of languages simultaneously, not just pairwise comparisons. The third contribution is the empirical validation on 13 Slavic languages, showing the method's ability to reveal meaningful linguistic groupings through quantitative measures and phylogenetic analysis.

## Strengths
1. **Well-motivated theoretical foundation**: The paper's use of MDL is particularly strong, as it provides a principled way to balance model complexity against data fit without requiring strong prior assumptions about correspondence distributions. The authors convincingly argue that this approach avoids the pitfalls of previous methods that impose arbitrary assumptions on correspondence shapes.

2. **Novel multi-language analysis capability**: The paper successfully demonstrates how the method can analyze similarity across all subsets of languages simultaneously, providing information-theoretic quantification of linguistic similarity that goes beyond pairwise comparisons. This is a significant advancement over existing approaches limited to language pairs.

3. **Effective empirical validation**: The phylogenetic analysis of Slavic languages reveals meaningful linguistic groupings that align with established linguistic classifications. The example correspondences (Table 3) show that the method discovers linguistically interpretable rules at different granularities, including both phonological and orthographic patterns.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of comparison to relevant linguistic literature**: The paper fails to compare with established methods for cognate detection and borrowing analysis (e.g., List & Moran 2013; Kitchen et al. 2009; Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh). This omission significantly weakens the evaluation, as it's unclear whether the method outperforms or complements existing approaches in the field.

2. **Major computational limitations**: The alignment procedure has a severe memory/runtime bottleneck, limiting comparisons to at most 5 languages. This severely restricts the method's applicability to larger language groups or more complex linguistic analyses, which is a significant practical limitation for a method claiming to handle "multiple languages."

3. **Orthographic data concerns**: The paper uses raw orthographic data without phonetic transcriptions, which risks conflating script changes with linguistic correspondences. As the authors note, "Using orthographic data risks conflating script changes with linguistic correspondences; phonetic transcriptions would be preferable." This is a serious methodological flaw that affects the validity of the results.

4. **Unclear technical details**: Several technical aspects are poorly explained, including the meaning of "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" and the merging step in the maximization update. Additionally, it's unclear whether EM is run to convergence or uses another stopping criterion, which impacts reproducibility.

5. **Data quality concerns**: The Swadesh lists mix cognate and non-cognate words, which may affect results. The paper should address how this mixture impacts the discovered correspondences, as non-cognate words could introduce noise that the method might mistakenly treat as systematic correspondences.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper claims to "discover correspondences between any subset of languages," yet the evaluation is limited to at most 5 languages due to computational constraints. Could you elaborate on the specific computational bottlenecks and potential strategies to scale the method to more languages?

2. The paper uses orthographic data without phonetic transcriptions. How do you address the risk of conflating script changes (e.g., Latin vs. Cyrillic) with linguistic correspondences? Would you expect different results with phonetic transcriptions?

3. In Section 4.4, the authors mention "the optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" but don't clearly explain this concept. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of this component?

4. The paper cites List & Moran 2013 as relevant literature but doesn't compare with their method. How does your approach differ from and improve upon their work? Would you expect similar results with their method?

5. The Swadesh lists used in the experiments mix cognate and non-cognate words. How did the authors handle this potential source of noise, and what impact might it have on the discovered correspondences?
