

This paper proposes an MDL-based method for discovering cross-language word correspondences by modeling substring-matching rules and optimizing model vs data description length. The main contributions are: 1) an MDL-based framework for objective, assumption-free discovery of string-level correspondences between multiple languages; 2) an efficient EM-based algorithm for learning these correspondences; and 3) empirical validation demonstrating the method's ability to capture linguistic structure across multiple Slavic languages.

Strengths: The MDL approach is well-motivated for discovering objective correspondences without imposing arbitrary assumptions about correspondence distribution. The paper successfully demonstrates how the method can automatically discover non-trivial linguistic patterns without prior assumptions, as evidenced by the phylogenetic tree that correctly identifies Slavic language groupings. The four-way analysis provides a novel, highly detailed quantification of linguistic similarity across language subsets that surpasses traditional pairwise approaches, offering new insights into language relationships.

The method's ability to handle multiple languages simultaneously is particularly valuable for linguistic analysis. The paper's clear explanation of the MDL framework and its application to language correspondences makes the approach accessible to linguists. The authors' empirical analysis of Slavic languages provides concrete evidence of the method's efficacy, with discovered correspondences aligning with known linguistic patterns.

Weaknesses: The paper lacks comparison to related work on cognate detection and borrowing, such as the approaches by List & Moran (2013) and Kitchen et al. (2009), which would help position this work within the existing literature. The alignment procedure has a major memory/runtime bottleneck, currently limiting analysis to at most five languages, which significantly restricts the method's applicability to larger language sets or more complex analyses.

The use of orthographic data rather than phonetic transcriptions risks conflating script changes with linguistic correspondences, potentially introducing noise into the analysis. The paper does not sufficiently address how this choice affects the discovered correspondences. Additionally, the paper mixes cognate datasets with Swadesh lists, which may affect results as these datasets serve different linguistic purposes.

Technical details about the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" and the merging step in the maximization update are unclear, making it difficult to fully assess the algorithm's mechanics. The stopping criterion for the EM algorithm is also unspecified, raising questions about whether the method runs to convergence or uses a heuristic stopping rule.

Questions to Authors: How does your MDL approach compare to Bayesian phylogenetic inference tools like MrBayes, given the resemblance to Bayesian objectives? What specific advantages does your method offer over existing cognate detection approaches? How would using phonetic transcriptions instead of orthographic data affect your results, particularly for languages with different writing systems? What is the precise stopping criterion for your EM algorithm, and how do you ensure sufficient convergence?
