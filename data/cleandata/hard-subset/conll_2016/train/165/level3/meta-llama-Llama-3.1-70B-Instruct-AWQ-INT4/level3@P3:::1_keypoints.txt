- Proposes a novel MDL-based method to discover cross-language word correspondences by modeling substring-matching rules and optimizing model vs data description length.
- Develops an efficient EM-based algorithm and two-part MDL code to infer correspondence rules directly from data.
- Evaluates the method on 13 Slavic languages, showing it discovers non-trivial associations with quantitative and qualitative analyses.
- MDL provides a statistically principled framework and the authors’ extension of Tatti & Vreeken (2012) is a notable contribution.
- Large-scale evaluation across Slavic languages offers valuable insight into linguistic similarities and differences.
- The paper responsibly discusses limitations like alignment bottlenecks and potential overfitting.
- Lacks comparison to related work on borrowing and cognate detection (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen, List & Moran).
- Alignment procedure has a major memory/runtime bottleneck, currently limiting multi-language comparisons to about 5 languages.
- Phylogenetic trees built with neighbor joining have known drawbacks (e.g., manual rooting); more advanced phylogenetic methods should be considered.
- It is unclear whether EM is run to convergence or stopped by another criterion, and this affects reproducibility and results.
- Mixing cognate datasets with non-cognate Swadesh lists may bias or confound the findings.
- Relying on orthographic data risks conflating script differences with true linguistic correspondences; phonetic transcriptions would be preferable.
- Requests clarification on the EM stopping criterion and its effect on results.
- Asks how the authors will address the alignment memory/runtime bottleneck to scale beyond 5 languages.
- Suggests discussing the benefits and limitations of more sophisticated phylogenetic methods (e.g., Bayesian inference).
- Requests elaboration on planned extensions to incorporate linguistic knowledge and non-determinism.
- Recommends a more detailed related-work discussion on borrowing and cognate detection.
- Notes that using orthographic data may limit generalizability across different scripts.
- Finds the authors’ plan to explore practical applications of the learned rules promising and worth expanding in future work.
