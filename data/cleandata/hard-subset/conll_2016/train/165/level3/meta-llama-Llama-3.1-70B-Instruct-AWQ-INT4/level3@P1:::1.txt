This paper proposes a novel approach to discovering cross-language word correspondences using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors introduce an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules between multiple languages, which is a crucial task in linguistic research and natural language processing.

**Summary of the Paper**

The paper addresses the problem of automatically discovering correspondences between words from two or more languages. The authors employ the MDL principle to identify the best set of correspondence rules that describe the data most succinctly. They propose an efficient algorithm for discovering these rules, which is based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) and uses a two-part code to encode the model and the data. The algorithm is tested on a set of Slavic languages, and the results show that it can automatically discover non-trivial associations between languages, allowing for both quantitative and qualitative analysis of multiple languages.

**Main Contributions**

1. **MDL-based approach**: The paper proposes a novel MDL-based approach to discovering cross-language word correspondences, which is a significant contribution to the field of linguistic research and natural language processing.
2. **Efficient algorithm**: The authors introduce an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules between multiple languages, which is a crucial task in linguistic research and natural language processing.
3. **Experimental results**: The paper presents experimental results on a set of Slavic languages, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach in discovering non-trivial associations between languages.

**Strengths**

1. **Well-motivated approach**: The MDL-based approach is well-motivated and sensible for discovering cross-language word correspondences.
2. **Efficient algorithm**: The proposed algorithm is efficient and can be used to analyze multiple languages simultaneously.
3. **Experimental results**: The paper presents experimental results on a set of Slavic languages, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited comparison to related work**: The paper lacks a comparison to related work on borrowing and cognate detection, which is a significant limitation.
2. **Alignment procedure bottleneck**: The alignment procedure has a major memory/runtime bottleneck, which currently limits comparisons to at most 5 languages.
3. **Unclear derivations**: Some derivations in the paper are unclear, which may make it difficult for readers to follow the authors' arguments.
4. **Minor editorial issues**: There are minor editorial issues, such as the use of "focus in on" instead of "focus on".

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the merging step in the maximization update?
2. How do you ensure that the EM algorithm is run to convergence or uses another stopping criterion?
3. Can you discuss the potential effects of mixing cognate datasets with non-cognate Swadesh lists on the results?
4. Would using phonetic transcriptions instead of orthographic data improve the results, and if so, why?

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their approach and algorithm. However, there are some minor editorial issues that need to be addressed. Additionally, the authors should consider comparing their approach to related work on borrowing and cognate detection to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their method.
