This paper proposes a novel approach to discovering cross-language word correspondences using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors present a well-motivated method that models substring-matching rules and optimizes model vs data description length. The use of MDL is particularly suitable for this task, as it allows for the discovery of statistically significant correspondences without imposing arbitrary assumptions on their distribution or shape.

The paper's main contributions are:

1. The proposal of an MDL-based method for discovering cross-language word correspondences, which is a novel and well-motivated approach to this problem.
2. The development of an efficient algorithm for inferring good sets of correspondence rules directly from data, using Expectation-Maximization (EM) and a two-part MDL code.
3. The evaluation of the method on 13 Slavic languages, demonstrating its efficacy in discovering non-trivial associations and providing a quantitative and qualitative analysis of multiple languages.

The strengths of this paper are:

1. The use of MDL provides a statistically well-founded approach to identifying the best model for given data, and the authors' extension of the encoding introduced by Tatti and Vreeken (2012) is a significant contribution.
2. The evaluation of the method on a large dataset of Slavic languages demonstrates its efficacy and provides valuable insights into the linguistic similarities and differences between these languages.
3. The authors' discussion of the limitations of their approach, including the alignment procedure's memory/runtime bottleneck and the potential for overfitting, is thorough and well-motivated.

However, there are also some weaknesses and areas for improvement:

1. The paper lacks comparison to related work on borrowing and cognate detection, such as Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen, and List & Moran (2013).
2. The alignment procedure has a major memory/runtime bottleneck, currently limiting comparisons to at most 5 languages.
3. The phylogenetic trees built with neighbor joining have known disadvantages, such as manual rooting, and more sophisticated phylogenetic methods could be explored.
4. It is unclear whether EM is run to convergence or uses another stopping criterion.
5. Mixing cognate dataset with non-cognate Swadesh lists may affect results and should be considered.
6. Using orthographic data risks conflating script changes with linguistic correspondences, and phonetic transcriptions would be preferable.

Questions to authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the EM algorithm's stopping criterion and how it affects the results?
2. How do you plan to address the alignment procedure's memory/runtime bottleneck to enable comparisons with more than 5 languages?
3. Can you discuss the potential benefits and limitations of using more sophisticated phylogenetic methods, such as Bayesian phylogenetic inference tools?
4. How do you plan to extend your approach to incorporate linguistic knowledge and non-determinism, as mentioned in the conclusion?

Additional comments:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work on borrowing and cognate detection.
* The use of orthographic data may limit the generalizability of the results to languages with different scripts.
* The authors' plan to discuss potential applications of the different resulting rules in future work is promising, and it would be interesting to see how these rules can be used in practical applications.
