**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a method for automatically discovering correspondences between words from multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors present an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules and demonstrate its efficacy on a set of Slavic languages. The approach is based on modeling substring-matching rules and optimizing the model versus data description length. The authors evaluate their method using distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and example correspondences.

**Main Contributions**

1. **MDL-based approach for cross-language word correspondences**: The authors propose a novel approach for discovering correspondences between multiple languages using the MDL principle. This approach is well-motivated and provides a statistically well-founded method for identifying the best model for given data.
2. **Efficient algorithm for inferring correspondence rules**: The authors present an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules, which is based on Expectation-Maximization (EM) and optimizes the model versus data description length.
3. **Evaluation on 13 Slavic languages**: The authors evaluate their method on a set of 13 Slavic languages, demonstrating its efficacy in discovering non-trivial associations and providing a quantitative and qualitative analysis of multiple languages.

**Strengths**

1. **Well-motivated approach**: The authors provide a clear motivation for their approach, and the MDL principle is well-suited for the problem of discovering correspondences between multiple languages.
2. **Efficient algorithm**: The authors present an efficient algorithm for inferring statistically significant correspondence rules, which is based on EM and optimizes the model versus data description length.
3. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors evaluate their method on a set of 13 Slavic languages, demonstrating its efficacy in discovering non-trivial associations and providing a quantitative and qualitative analysis of multiple languages.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited comparison to related work**: The authors do not provide a comprehensive comparison to related work on borrowing and cognate detection, which would be useful for understanding the strengths and limitations of their approach.
2. **Major memory/runtime bottleneck**: The alignment procedure has a major memory/runtime bottleneck, currently limiting comparisons to at most 5 languages.
3. **Unclear derivations and technical points**: Some derivations and technical points, such as the meaning of “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” and the merging step in the maximization update, are unclear and require further clarification.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the major memory/runtime bottleneck in the alignment procedure to enable comparisons with more than 5 languages?
2. Can the authors provide a more comprehensive comparison to related work on borrowing and cognate detection to understand the strengths and limitations of their approach?
3. How do the authors plan to clarify the unclear derivations and technical points, such as the meaning of “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” and the merging step in the maximization update?

**Additional Comments**

The authors should consider providing more details on the EM algorithm, such as whether it is run to convergence or uses another stopping criterion. Additionally, the authors should consider using phonetic transcriptions instead of orthographic data to avoid conflating script changes with linguistic correspondences.
