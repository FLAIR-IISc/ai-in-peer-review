## Review of “Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL”

This paper presents a novel approach to discovering cross-language word correspondences using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors propose a method that models substring-matching rules and optimizes for model complexity versus data description length, implemented via an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The method is evaluated on 13 Slavic languages using distance measures, phylogenetic tree construction, and qualitative analysis of learned correspondences.

The primary contribution of this work lies in its formulation of a multi-language correspondence discovery problem within an MDL framework. This allows for a principled, data-driven approach that avoids many of the arbitrary assumptions present in existing methods. The implementation, while computationally challenging, demonstrates the feasibility of applying MDL to this problem and provides a valuable tool for exploring linguistic relationships beyond pairwise comparisons. A secondary contribution is the application of this method to a relatively large set of Slavic languages, providing a rich dataset for evaluation and demonstrating the method’s ability to capture both expected and nuanced linguistic patterns.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The use of MDL provides a strong statistical justification for the approach. The motivation for choosing MDL over Bayesian methods is clearly articulated, and the formulation of the problem in terms of description length is well-reasoned.
2. **Novelty of Approach:** Applying MDL to multi-language correspondence discovery is a novel contribution. The method’s ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a significant advantage over many existing techniques.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation using NCD, phylogenetic trees, and example correspondences provides a multi-faceted assessment of the method’s performance. The inclusion of both quantitative and qualitative analyses strengthens the findings.
4. **Demonstrated Scalability (to a point):** While limited by computational constraints, the authors demonstrate the method’s applicability to a reasonable number of languages (13) and highlight areas for future optimization.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Computational Bottleneck:** The alignment procedure’s memory and runtime limitations, currently restricting comparisons to at most five languages, severely hinders broader applicability and comparison to other methods. This is a critical issue that needs to be addressed.
2. **Limited Comparison to Existing Work:** The paper lacks a thorough comparison to related work in cognate detection and borrowing (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen; List & Moran 2013). Establishing a clear benchmark against existing methods is crucial for demonstrating the method’s advantages.
3. **Phylogenetic Tree Methodology:** The use of neighbor joining for phylogenetic tree construction is a weakness, given its known limitations (manual rooting, sensitivity to long-branch attraction). More sophisticated phylogenetic methods should be considered.
4. **Data Considerations:** The mixing of cognate datasets with non-cognate Swadesh lists and the use of orthographic data (rather than phonetic transcriptions) raise concerns about the validity of the results. Orthographic variation can obscure true linguistic correspondences.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. What specific optimizations are planned to address the computational bottleneck in the alignment procedure?
2. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of your method’s performance against established cognate detection tools (e.g., those by List & Moran, Kitchen et al.)?
3. How sensitive are the results to the inclusion of non-cognate Swadesh lists? Have you considered performing the analysis using only cognate data?
4. Would using phonetic transcriptions instead of orthographic data significantly alter the learned correspondences and the resulting phylogenetic tree?

**Additional Comments:**

* The editorial suggestion to change “focus in on” to “focus on” (line 440) is correct.
* Please cite List & Moran 2013 and Kitchen et al. 2009 as suggested.
* Clarify the meaning of “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” and the merging step in the maximization update for better understanding.
