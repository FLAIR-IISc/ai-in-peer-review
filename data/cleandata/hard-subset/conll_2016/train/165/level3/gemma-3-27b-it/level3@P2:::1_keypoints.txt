- Paper proposes a novel MDL+EM-based deterministic algorithm to discover cross-language word correspondences directly from data.  
- Secondary contribution: uses shared description lengths to produce quantitative linguistic-similarity measures (phylogenies, subset similarities).  
- Provides experiments on Slavic languages and a publicly available implementation.

Strengths:
- Innovative application of MDL gives a principled, assumption-light framework balancing model complexity and data fit.  
- Method scales to more than two languages, enabling analysis of all language subsets rather than just pairwise comparisons.  
- Deterministic algorithm yields reproducible, interpretable correspondence rules.  
- Thorough evaluation combining NCDs, phylogenetic trees, and qualitative rule analysis; introduces shared description length as a useful metric.

Weaknesses:
- Lacks comprehensive comparison and empirical baselines against established work in cognate detection and historical linguistics.  
- Alignment procedure has memory/runtime bottlenecks limiting scalability beyond a few languages.  
- Use of raw orthography and mixed datasets risks confounding script effects and noisy data; choice of neighbor-joining phylogeny is debatable.  
- Some technical details (e.g., “optimal unigram,” merging in maximization, EM convergence/stopping) are unclear.

Questions to authors:
- Provide detailed empirical comparisons with existing cognate-detection and historical-reconstruction methods on shared datasets.  
- What concrete steps will address the alignment computational bottleneck to scale to larger families?  
- How sensitive are results to orthographic vs. phonetic input; have experiments with transcriptions been considered?  
- Clarify “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” and the merging step in the maximization update.  
- What convergence/stopping criterion is used for the EM algorithm?

Additional comments:
- Discuss limitations and potential biases of the MDL approach in this application.  
- Minor edit: change “focus in on” to “focus on” (line 440).  
- Consider citing and comparing to Kitchen et al. (2009).
