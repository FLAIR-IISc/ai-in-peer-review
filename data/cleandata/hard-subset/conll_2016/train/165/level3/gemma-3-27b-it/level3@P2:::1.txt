This paper presents a novel approach to discovering cross-language word correspondences using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The authors tackle the challenge of identifying statistically significant relationships between words across multiple languages without imposing restrictive assumptions or limiting analysis to pairwise comparisons. The primary contribution lies in formulating a statistically grounded, deterministic algorithm based on MDL and Expectation-Maximization (EM) for inferring these correspondences directly from data, demonstrated through analysis of Slavic languages. A secondary contribution is the application of this method to generate quantitative measures of linguistic similarity, including phylogenetic trees and shared description lengths across language subsets, offering a more nuanced analysis than traditional pairwise approaches. Finally, the work provides a publicly available implementation and interesting experimental results on a set of Slavic languages.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel MDL Formulation for Correspondence Discovery:** The application of the MDL principle to the problem of cross-lingual correspondence discovery is a strong and innovative aspect of this work. Unlike many existing approaches that rely on predefined assumptions about the distribution of correspondences, the MDL framework provides a principled way to balance model complexity and data fit, leading to objective and unbiased results. The authors successfully translate this principle into a concrete algorithmic framework.

2. **Scalability to Multiple Languages:** A significant strength is the method’s ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously. This is a notable improvement over many existing techniques that are limited to pairwise comparisons. The demonstration of the method’s ability to quantify similarities between all subsets of languages is compelling and allows for a more holistic view of linguistic relationships. The experiments with four Slavic languages highlight this capability effectively.

3. **Deterministic Algorithm & Interpretability:** The deterministic nature of the algorithm, coupled with the inherent interpretability of the discovered correspondence rules, is a valuable asset. Unlike probabilistic models, the deterministic approach avoids the need for multiple runs and provides reproducible results. The specific rules learned (demonstrated in Table 3) provide insights into the underlying linguistic processes and similarities between languages.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation & Information-Theoretic Analysis:** The paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed method, using a combination of quantitative metrics (NCDs), phylogenetic tree construction, and qualitative analysis of learned correspondences. The introduction of shared description length as a measure of linguistic similarity is a particularly insightful contribution, allowing for a granular assessment of relationships between different language subsets.




**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Existing Work:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to related work in the fields of historical linguistics, cognate detection, and borrowing detection. While the introduction briefly mentions existing approaches, a more detailed discussion and empirical comparison with methods like those developed by Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen, or List & Moran (2013), is crucial to establish the novelty and superiority of the proposed method. Addressing these works would contextualize the contribution more effectively.

2. **Computational Bottleneck & Scalability Issues:** The reported memory and runtime bottleneck in the alignment procedure limits the applicability of the method to a small number of languages (currently up to five). While the authors acknowledge this limitation and propose future work to address it, it is a significant concern. Expanding the scalability of the algorithm is essential for practical applications involving larger language families.

3. **Methodological Concerns Regarding Data & Analysis:** Using raw orthographic data introduces the risk of confounding script changes with actual linguistic correspondences. Phonetic transcriptions would be more robust and would mitigate this issue. Furthermore, mixing cognate datasets (internationalisms and pan-Slavic words) with potentially less reliable Swadesh lists may introduce noise and affect the results. The effect of this mixture should be explicitly addressed. The choice of neighbor joining for phylogenetic tree construction is also questionable, given its known limitations. More sophisticated phylogenetic methods should be considered.

4. **Unclear Technical Details & Convergence:** Certain technical aspects of the algorithm, such as the meaning of “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” and the merging step in the maximization update, are not fully clarified. Additionally, it is unclear whether the EM algorithm is run to convergence, or if a different stopping criterion is employed, which could influence the quality of the results.





**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the proposed method with existing approaches for cognate detection and historical linguistic reconstruction, including empirical results on shared datasets?
2.  What steps are being taken to address the computational bottleneck in the alignment procedure and improve the scalability of the algorithm to handle larger language families?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the use of raw orthographic data versus phonetic transcriptions? Have you considered performing experiments with transcribed data?
4.  Please clarify the meaning of “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” and provide a more detailed explanation of the merging step in the maximization update.
5.  What convergence criterion is used for the EM algorithm?



**Additional Comments:**
