## Review of “Discovering Correspondences between Multiple Languages by MDL”

This paper presents an interesting approach to discovering cross-language word correspondences using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, coupled with an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The core idea of modeling substring-matching rules and optimizing for model complexity versus data description length is sensible and potentially valuable for linguistic analysis and NLP tasks. The application to 13 Slavic languages provides a reasonably substantial evaluation, utilizing distance measures, phylogenetic trees, and illustrative example correspondences.

The primary contribution of this work lies in its novel application of MDL and EM to the multi-lingual word correspondence problem *without* strong prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of correspondences. While related work exists in cognate detection and linguistic reconstruction, this approach offers a deterministic, data-driven alternative that allows for flexible exploration of patterns across multiple languages simultaneously. A second contribution is the demonstration of the method’s ability to reveal meaningful linguistic relationships, evidenced by the generated phylogenetic tree aligning with known language classifications and the insightful examples of learned correspondences. A third, albeit less prominent, contribution is the introduction of a method to modulate the granularity of discovered rules through data over-weighting, providing users with control over the level of detail in the analysis.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The most significant limitation is the scalability of the alignment procedure, currently restricted to a maximum of five languages due to memory and runtime bottlenecks. This severely limits the scope of potentially impactful analyses. Furthermore, the phylogenetic tree construction relies on neighbor-joining, a method known to have disadvantages, such as sensitivity to rooting. While the authors acknowledge this, more sophisticated phylogenetic methods should be considered. The description of the EM algorithm is also incomplete; it is unclear whether convergence criteria are used, raising concerns about the reliability of the learned models. 

Crucially, the paper lacks a thorough comparison to existing work on borrowing and cognate detection methods (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen; List & Moran 2013), as noted by the authors themselves. The choice of orthographic data, while convenient, introduces potential confounding factors from script changes, and the use of phonetic transcriptions would be preferable. Finally, some technical details remain unclear, such as the precise meaning of "optimal unigram for symbol usages" and the merging step in the maximization update.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  What specific convergence criteria (if any) are used for the EM algorithm, and how is termination determined?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the “optimal unigram” calculation and the merging step within the maximization update?
3.  What steps are being considered to address the scalability limitations of the alignment procedure, and what is the projected timeline for implementing these improvements?
4.  What is the justification for including both cognate datasets and Swadesh lists, given the potential for affecting the results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   Please cite List & Moran 2013 and Kitchen et al. 2009, as suggested.
*   The editorial issue on line 440 (“focus in on” to “focus on”) should be addressed.
*   A discussion of the limitations of using orthographic data and the potential benefits of phonetic transcriptions would strengthen the analysis.
