This paper proposes a Minimum Description Length (MDL)-based method for discovering cross-language word correspondences. The core idea is to model relationships between substrings of words across multiple languages, optimizing a trade-off between model complexity and the description length of the data it explains. The approach utilizes an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm for training and is evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, employing distance measures, phylogenetic tree construction, and illustrative example correspondences. The primary contribution lies in providing a deterministic and statistically grounded approach to identifying linguistic similarities across multiple languages without imposing strong prior assumptions about the shape or distribution of these correspondences. A second contribution is the extension of MDL-based modeling to scenarios involving more than two languages, enabling a more detailed quantification of linguistic similarity than previously possible. Finally, the method's ability to reveal similarities at different levels of granularity—through data over-weighting—provides a valuable tool for linguistic analysis.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Application of MDL:** The application of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle to the problem of discovering cross-lingual correspondences is a strong point. MDL provides a theoretically sound framework for model selection, offering a principled way to balance model complexity with its ability to explain the observed data. The method’s deterministic nature, stemming from the MDL approach, is valuable for reproducibility and interpretability, setting it apart from probabilistic methods.

2. **Multi-lingual Capability:** The ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a significant advancement.  Existing methods often focus on pairwise comparisons, which can become computationally intractable or fail to capture higher-order relationships when scaling to multiple languages. This approach efficiently allows for the exploration of similarities among subsets of languages, providing a more holistic view of linguistic relationships. The experiment with Czech, Polish, Russian and Bulgarian successfully demonstrates this capability.

3. **Granularity Control:** The introduction of data over-weighting provides a mechanism for controlling the level of detail in the discovered correspondences. This allows users to trade off between statistical rigor and the inclusion of more nuanced, but potentially less statistically significant, patterns. This is a particularly valuable feature for linguists who may have prior knowledge or specific hypotheses they wish to investigate. The demonstration of this feature with the example alignments is compelling.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Comparison to Related Work:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to existing methods in the field of cognate detection and language relatedness. While related work is briefly mentioned, the paper does not adequately position its approach against alternatives such as Bayesian phylogenetic inference tools (e.g., MrBayes) or methods based on borrowing detection (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen; List & Moran 2013). A more thorough comparison would help establish the novelty and advantages of the proposed method.

2. **Computational Bottleneck:** The alignment procedure presents a significant computational bottleneck, currently limiting the application of the method to a maximum of five languages. This severely restricts the scalability of the approach and its applicability to larger language families. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, further optimization or alternative algorithmic strategies are needed to address it.

3. **Methodological Concerns:** The use of orthographic data, rather than phonetic transcriptions, introduces potential confounding factors. Script changes can be mistaken for linguistic correspondences, potentially leading to inaccurate results. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, employing phonetic transcriptions would significantly improve the robustness of the method. Additionally, the reliance on a neighbor-joining method for phylogenetic tree construction—a method known to have disadvantages—and the lack of clarity regarding the EM algorithm’s convergence criteria raise concerns about the reliability of the results. The meaning of “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” and the merging step in the maximization update require further clarification.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the convergence criterion used for the EM algorithm? Was it based on a fixed number of iterations, a change in log-likelihood, or another metric?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the over-weighting parameter?  Is there a principled way to select an appropriate value for this parameter, or is it purely a matter of experimentation?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational complexity of the alignment procedure and discuss potential strategies for mitigating the bottleneck?
4.  How would the results change if phonetic transcriptions were used instead of orthographic data? Have you conducted any preliminary experiments using phonetic data?
