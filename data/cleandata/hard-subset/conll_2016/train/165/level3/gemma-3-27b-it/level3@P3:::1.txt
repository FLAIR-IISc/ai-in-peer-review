This paper presents a novel approach to discovering cross-language word correspondences using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The method aims to identify statistically significant associations between strings across multiple languages without strong prior assumptions, modeling these correspondences as substring-matching rules and optimizing for model and data description length. The authors demonstrate the method’s efficacy on a set of 13 Slavic languages, evaluating it using distance measures, a phylogenetic tree, and illustrative examples.

The primary contribution of this work lies in formulating the problem of cross-language correspondence discovery within an MDL framework, coupled with an EM algorithm for efficient implementation. This allows for a systematic and data-driven approach that avoids many of the limitations of traditional, manually-driven comparative methods or those relying on strong distributional assumptions. The ability to simultaneously analyze multiple languages (beyond pairwise comparisons) is a significant advantage, enabling the quantification of linguistic similarity across language subsets in a way previously difficult to achieve. A secondary contribution is the application of this framework to Slavic languages, providing a concrete demonstration of its capabilities and generating interesting linguistic insights.

**Strengths:**

1. **Sound Theoretical Foundation:** The use of MDL provides a strong theoretical justification for the approach.  The principle’s inherent focus on model simplicity and data fidelity is well-suited to the task of discovering underlying linguistic correspondences. The authors adequately explain the motivation behind MDL and its relevance to this problem, and the connection to Bayesian inference is insightful.
2. **Multi-Language Capability:**  The method’s ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a significant advantage over many existing approaches. This unlocks the potential for more nuanced analyses of linguistic relationships and the quantification of similarity between arbitrary subsets of languages. The demonstration with four languages (CS, PL, RU, BG) is compelling.
3. **Deterministic Approach & Controllable Detail:** The authors' emphasis on a deterministic algorithm is a notable strength. This allows for reproducibility and facilitates linguistic interpretation. The introduction of data over-weighting as a mechanism to control the level of detail in the discovered correspondences provides a valuable degree of flexibility and user control.
4. **Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments on 13 Slavic languages provide a reasonable empirical evaluation of the method. The use of NCD to construct a phylogenetic tree and the comparison to expected linguistic classifications are relevant and provide some validation of the approach. The examples of learned correspondences (Table 3) are illustrative and suggest the algorithm captures meaningful patterns.
5. **Clear Presentation of the MDL Framework:** The authors present the MDL code and its components (model code and data code) in a clear and well-structured manner. The explanation of the encoding process, including the use of universal codes and Laplace correction, is detailed and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Scalability Bottleneck:** The major limitation is the alignment procedure’s memory and runtime bottleneck, currently restricting comparisons to at most five languages. This severely limits the method's applicability to larger language families or more complex datasets. This is a critical issue that needs to be addressed before the method can be widely adopted.
2. **Phylogenetic Tree Construction:**  The use of neighbor joining for phylogenetic tree construction is a potential weakness. This method is known to be sensitive to long-branch attraction and requires manual rooting, introducing subjective elements into the analysis. More sophisticated phylogenetic methods (e.g., Bayesian inference as suggested by the authors) would provide a more robust and reliable assessment of the language relationships.
3. **Data Considerations:** The mixing of cognate datasets with non-cognate Swadesh lists is a valid concern.  The authors should thoroughly investigate the potential impact of this mixture on the results. A more careful curation of the data, ensuring a consistent definition of cognacy, is recommended.
4. **Orthographic Data:** The reliance on orthographic data, as the authors acknowledge, risks conflating script changes with genuine linguistic correspondences. While pragmatic for readily available data, the use of phonetic transcriptions would significantly improve the accuracy and reliability of the results.
5. **Implementation Details & Convergence:** The lack of clarity regarding the EM algorithm’s stopping criterion (convergence vs. fixed iterations) is a concern. It is important to demonstrate that the algorithm reaches a stable state and that the results are not significantly affected by the choice of stopping criterion.
6. **Limited Comparison to Existing Work:** The paper lacks a comprehensive comparison to related work on borrowing and cognate detection, particularly those employing Bayesian phylogenetic inference tools (e.g., MrBayes) or methods by Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh, and Kitchen, as well as List & Moran (2013). A thorough comparative analysis would help to position this work within the broader field and highlight its unique contributions.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. What specific efforts are being made to address the scalability bottleneck in the alignment procedure? Are there any planned optimizations or alternative algorithms that could allow for the analysis of larger datasets?
2. Have you considered using more sophisticated phylogenetic methods, such as Bayesian inference, to construct the language trees? If so, what were the reasons for choosing neighbor joining?
3. Could you provide more details on the impact of including non-cognate Swadesh lists in your dataset? Have you conducted any sensitivity analyses to assess how the results change when using only cognate data?
4. Regarding the EM algorithm, what stopping criterion is used? Is it based on convergence (e.g., change in log-likelihood below a threshold), or is it a fixed number of iterations?
5. Can you elaborate on the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules"? Is this a frequency-based unigram model, and how is it updated during the EM iterations?
6. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the merging step in the maximization update? What criteria are used to determine which rules to merge, and how is the decrease in description length calculated?
