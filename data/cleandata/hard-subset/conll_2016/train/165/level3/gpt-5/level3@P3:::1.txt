Summary
The paper introduces an MDL-based framework for discovering substring-based correspondence rules across two or more languages. Rules are learned with an EM-style procedure that alternates between optimal alignments (via shortest-path search) and model updates that merge patterns to reduce total description length. The approach supports multi-language correspondences (not only pairwise) and proposes an “over-weighting” trick to control the granularity of discovered rules. The method is evaluated on 13 Slavic languages using pairwise distances (NCD), a neighbor-joining tree, and qualitative inspection of learned correspondences. A four-language case study (Czech–Polish–Russian–Bulgarian) demonstrates correspondences among all subsets via a “shared description length” analysis.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) An MDL formulation for multi-language substring correspondence discovery with a deterministic EM-style training procedure and a Dijkstra-based exact aligner.
2) An empirical case study on Slavic languages showing that MDL-derived distances recover known subgroupings and that the framework can quantify shared structure among any subset of languages.
3) A practical mechanism (“data over-weighting”) to steer the level of correspondence granularity when purely MDL-optimal rules are too coarse.

Strengths
- Sound, well-motivated objective: Casting correspondence discovery as two-part MDL is principled, balances model parsimony against fit, and naturally extends beyond pairwise settings. The connection to regularized maximum likelihood is clear, and the EM rationale (alignment vs. code updates) is sensible.
- Interpretable, linguistically meaningful rules: The learned correspondences include phonological and orthographic regularities (e.g., cz–č, g–h), endings, and stem-level patterns; the four-way examples capture subset-specific patterns. These are easy to inspect and potentially useful for downstream historical/typological analyses.
- Multi-language analysis beyond pairwise: The shared description length view for all subsets is novel and informative, revealing where structure is shared vs. language-specific. This goes beyond standard pairwise cognate-based approaches.
- Empirical results largely concordant with known classifications: NCD-based distances and the derived neighbor-joining tree broadly recover Slavic subgroupings and sensible outliers (e.g., Bulgarian/Macedonian phenomena), lending face validity to the method.
- Deterministic alignment and rule growth: Using shortest-path alignment and a deterministic merge criterion reduces run-to-run variance, which is attractive for linguistic reproducibility and expert inspection.

Weaknesses
- Missing comparisons to established phylogenetic and cognate methods: The work resembles Bayesian objectives but does not compare against Bayesian phylogenetic inference (e.g., MrBayes, BEAST; see Kitchen et al. 2009) or modern cognate/borrowing detection pipelines (e.g., Bouchard-Côté et al.; Warnow; Nakhleh; List & Moran 2013). Even a small-scale quantitative comparison (tree topology, quartet concordance, distance correlations) would strengthen claims.
- Computational bottleneck limits scope: The alignment graph leads to severe memory/runtime constraints, reportedly capping practical runs at five languages for the “most complicated” dataset. This undercuts the multi-language promise and prevents broader comparative evaluations. A discussion of complexity with respect to N, vocabulary size, and rule table size, and any pruning/approximation strategies, is needed.
- Phylogeny via neighbor joining and manual rooting: NJ is distance-based, sensitive to noise and long-branch artifacts, and manual rooting can bias interpretation. More robust alternatives (e.g., maximum likelihood or Bayesian character-based inference, or even NJ with objective rooting) would provide stronger evidence for phylogenetic claims and allow uncertainty quantification.
- Data and representation issues: Combining curated cognate sets with general Swadesh orthographic lists risks conflating cognacy with surface similarity and borrowing. Orthographic data further confounds script changes with linguistic correspondences; IPA or phonemic transcriptions would reduce spurious orthographic effects (e.g., Serbo-Croatian Latin vs. Cyrillic differences).
- Unclear technical details: Two points need clarification: (i) “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” (Section 4.1) — is this a global ML estimate over all σ across all Π, and how are counts obtained (from current E-step counts only)? (ii) The M-step “merging” operation — precisely what constitutes a merge, how candidate pairs are enumerated, and how costs and counts are updated to ensure L(M)+L(D|M) decreases. Also, the EM stopping criterion (to convergence? delta threshold? fixed iterations?) and monotonicity guarantees are not specified.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Stopping criterion and guarantees: Do you run EM to convergence in L(M)+L(D|M)? If not, what is the criterion, and do you observe monotone decrease each iteration?
- M-step merging: Please formalize the merge operation. What are “compatible” rules, how do you generate candidate merges, and how do you update usage counts and code tables after a merge?
- “Optimal unigram” code: How exactly are symbol code lengths estimated (data-to-model code vs. ML), and over which counts? Are these re-estimated every M-step?
- Scalability: What are the memory and time complexities in terms of N (languages), average word length, and |Π|? Can you incorporate beam search, A* with admissible heuristics, lattice pruning, or suffix automata to scale alignment? Any parallelization options?
- Over-weighting: How sensitive are results to α and to per-item weighting? Can you report stability plots (e.g., sDL contributions vs. α) and clarify when over-weighting is recommended?
- Data and representation: Have you tried phonemic/IPA transcriptions to control for orthographic confounds? What is the effect of mixing cognate lists with Swadesh items; can you report analyses run separately to quantify impact?
- Baselines and phylogeny: Can you compare NJ trees against Bayesian or ML trees (e.g., MrBayes, BEAST), and report topological distances or quartet scores? At minimum, correlate MDL-based NCDs with distances from established tools or with published Slavic trees (Kitchen et al. 2009; List & Moran 2013).

Additional comments
- Related work to cite/compare: List & Moran (2013) on cognate detection and phylogenies; Kitchen et al. (2009) on Indo-European phylogenies; Bayesian cognate/phonological change models (e.g., Bouchard-Côté et al.); work by Warnow and Nakhleh on phylogenetic inference with borrowing/homoplasy; modern string-similarity/cognate pipelines (e.g., LexStat/ASJP-style approaches).
- Consider more sophisticated phylogenetic inference with uncertainty estimates (posterior clade support) and principled rooting (e.g., outgroup selection).
- Editorial: change “focus in on” to “focus on” (Section 4.5, around line 440). Clarify derivations in Section 4.1, including the L(Π) terms and the data-to-model code rationale. Provide pseudo-code for EM steps and release code/data to enhance reproducibility.

Overall, this is a promising, well-motivated MDL approach with interpretable outputs and intriguing multi-language analyses. Its impact would be significantly increased by stronger empirical comparisons, clearer technical specification, and attention to scalability and data representation.
