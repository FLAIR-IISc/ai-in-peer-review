- Paper introduces an MDL-based framework to discover substring correspondence rules across multiple languages using an EM-style procedure alternating shortest-path alignment and model merges.
- Evaluated on 13 Slavic languages with NCD distances, neighbor-joining tree, qualitative inspection, and a four-language shared description length case study.

- Contribution: MDL formulation for multi-language substring correspondence discovery with deterministic EM and Dijkstra-based exact aligner.
- Contribution: Empirical Slavic case study showing MDL-derived distances recover known subgroupings and quantify shared structure for any language subset.
- Contribution: Practical “data over-weighting” mechanism to control correspondence granularity when MDL-optimal rules are too coarse.

- Strength: Principled two-part MDL objective that balances parsimony and fit and extends naturally beyond pairwise settings.
- Strength: Interpretable, linguistically meaningful learned rules (phonological/orthographic patterns, endings, stems).
- Strength: Multi-language shared-description analysis reveals subset-specific vs. shared structure beyond pairwise approaches.
- Strength: Empirical results largely concordant with known Slavic classifications, lending face validity.
- Strength: Deterministic alignment and merge decisions reduce run-to-run variance and aid reproducibility.

- Weakness: Missing quantitative comparisons to established phylogenetic and cognate methods (Bayesian phylogenetics, modern cognate/borrowing pipelines).
- Weakness: Computational bottleneck (alignment graph) limits scalability, reportedly capping practical runs around five languages for complex data.
- Weakness: Phylogenetic analysis relies on NJ and manual rooting, which are sensitive to noise and can bias interpretation.
- Weakness: Data/representation confounds from mixing curated cognate sets with Swadesh orthographic lists and using orthography instead of phonemic/IPA transcriptions.
- Weakness: Unclear technical details on “optimal unigram” estimation, the precise M-step merge operation, EM stopping criterion, and monotonicity guarantees.

- Question: Do you run EM to convergence on L(M)+L(D|M), and do you observe monotone decrease each iteration or use another stopping criterion?
- Question: Please formalize the M-step merge: definition of a merge, how compatible rules are determined, candidate enumeration, and cost/count updates.
- Question: How exactly are unigram code lengths estimated, over which counts, and are these re-estimated each M-step?
- Question: What are memory/time complexities in terms of N (languages), average word length, and |Π|, and can you use beam search, A*, pruning, suffix automata, or parallelization to scale?
- Question: How sensitive are results to over-weighting parameter α and per-item weighting; can you provide stability plots and guidance?
- Question: Have you tried phonemic/IPA transcriptions to control orthographic confounds, and what is the effect of mixing cognate lists with Swadesh items?
- Question: Can you compare NJ trees against Bayesian/ML trees (MrBayes/BEAST) and report topology/distance correlations or quartet scores?

- Related work to cite/compare: List & Moran (2013), Kitchen et al. (2009), Bouchard-Côté et al., Warnow, Nakhleh, and modern string-similarity/cognate pipelines (LexStat/ASJP).
- Suggestion: Use more robust phylogenetic inference with uncertainty estimates and principled rooting (outgroup selection).
- Reproducibility/editorial: Clarify derivations (Section 4.1), provide pseudocode for EM, fix minor editorial issues, and release code/data.
