Summary and contributions
- Primary: An MDL-based formulation for discovering cross-language substring correspondences that can apply to any subset of languages, trained via an EM procedure that alternates optimal alignments with rule-table updates.
- Secondary: A quantitative evaluation on Slavic data (13 languages pairwise; 4-way jointly) using NCD-derived distances, an NJ tree, and illustrative rules; introduces shared-description-length analyses for subsets.
- Tertiary: A deterministic learning setup with a tunable data overweighting mechanism to control granularity of learned rules.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and motivation: Casting correspondence discovery as two-part MDL is well-justified and avoids strong parametric assumptions; the EM coupling of alignment and rule-cost estimation is natural and compelling.
- Beyond pairwise: The capacity to learn rules defined on arbitrary language subsets is novel and valuable for fine-grained comparative analysis; the shared description length metric is a useful diagnostic.
- Empirical plausibility: Pairwise NCDs and the resulting tree broadly recover known Slavic subgroupings; examples capture phonological, orthographic, and morphological regularities (e.g., cz~č, liquid metathesis).
- Interpretability and determinism: Rule lists with costs are inspectable; the framework allows seeding with linguistic knowledge and analyzing contribution by subset.

Weaknesses
- Scalability: The alignment graph leads to memory/runtime bottlenecks, limiting practical comparisons to ≤5 languages, undermining the central multi-language promise; no pruning/approximation strategy is offered.
- Data representation: Using raw orthography risks conflating script/orthographic conventions with linguistic correspondences; mixed scripts (Latin/Cyrillic) and lack of phonetic normalization likely bias rules and NCDs.
- Methodological comparisons: No empirical comparison to Bayesian phylogenetic inference (e.g., MrBayes; Kitchen et al. 2009) despite the MDL objective’s Bayesian analogue; missing related work on borrowing/cognate detection (e.g., Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, Nakhleh; List & Moran 2013).
- Experimental design: Mixing curated cognates with Swadesh lists may distort distance estimates; neighbor joining with manual rooting is suboptimal—more principled phylogenetic methods (Bayesian/maximum likelihood, clock models) are warranted.
- Clarity of derivations: Unclear what “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules” precisely estimates; the maximization “merging” step lacks detail on candidate generation, constraints, and complexity; EM stopping criterion (convergence vs. fixed iterations) is unspecified.

Questions to authors
- How is EM terminated? What convergence criterion and initialization(s) are used; are results stable across runs?
- Define and estimate “optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules.” Are counts pooled over Π or usage in D; how is the data-to-model code applied?
- Describe the merge operation: which pairs are eligible (adjacent in alignments? across languages?), how are candidates enumerated/pruned, and what is its complexity?
- How do you control for script effects (e.g., transliteration or IPA)? Any results with phonetic transcriptions?
- Have you compared your distances/trees to MrBayes or other Bayesian tools, and to cognate/borrowing models (e.g., List & Moran 2013)? Please add these baselines.
- How sensitive are results to overweighting α and to mixing Swadesh with cognate sets?
