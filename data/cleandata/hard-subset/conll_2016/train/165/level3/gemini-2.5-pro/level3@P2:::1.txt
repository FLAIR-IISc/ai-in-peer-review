### Summary and Contributions

This paper introduces a method for automatically discovering string-level correspondences between words from multiple languages. The approach is framed under the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle, which seeks the set of correspondence rules that most succinctly describes the given data (cognate tuples). The model, which consists of a set of multi-way substring matching rules, is trained using an Expectation-Maximization (EM) like procedure. The method is evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The authors demonstrate its utility through pairwise language distance calculation, the construction of a phylogenetic tree, and a novel four-way analysis that quantifies the "shared description length" between different subsets of languages.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel MDL-based formulation for multi-language correspondence discovery:** The primary contribution is the principled, non-parametric framework for learning substring correspondences simultaneously across more than two languages. By optimizing the trade-off between model complexity and data description length, the method avoids strong prior assumptions about the nature of the correspondences. The proposed "shared Description Length" (sDL) is an interesting information-theoretic metric for quantifying n-way language similarities.
2.  **An empirical demonstration on Slavic languages:** The paper provides a multi-faceted analysis of 13 Slavic languages. The results, including the generated phylogenetic tree and the qualitative examples of learned rules, are largely consistent with known linguistic classifications and demonstrate the potential of the method for computational historical linguistics.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated theoretical framework:** The choice of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle is a strong fit for this problem. It provides a formal basis for balancing model complexity against data fit, naturally guarding against overfitting and discovering statistically significant patterns without requiring arbitrary assumptions about their distribution. The formulation of the problem as an optimization task solvable with an EM-like algorithm is logical and well-explained.
2.  **Goes beyond pairwise comparison:** A key strength of the proposed method is its ability to analyze correspondences across an arbitrary number of languages simultaneously. The four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian, and the subsequent breakdown of shared description length (sDL), offers a much more nuanced view of language relationships than what is possible with purely pairwise methods. This is a promising direction for multi-language comparative analysis.
3.  **Linguistically plausible results:** The empirical results are compelling. The pairwise Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) measures successfully group the Slavic languages into their known west, east, and south branches in the phylogenetic tree. The qualitative examples of learned correspondences (e.g., phonological rules like (g,h), orthographic rules like (cz,č), and morphological endings like (eć,et)) are intuitive and demonstrate that the model captures meaningful linguistic phenomena.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient comparison with related work:** The paper positions itself in the domain of computational historical linguistics but fails to engage with or compare against a large body of highly relevant work. The task is closely related to automatic cognate detection, borrowing detection, and phylogenetic inference. There is no comparison to established Bayesian methods for phylogenetic inference (e.g., using tools like MrBayes, or work by Bouchard-Côté et al.), which also perform model selection and are standard in the field. Furthermore, the paper overlooks significant work in computational cognate detection (e.g., List & Moran 2013, "Loanword detection in historical linguistics") and phylogenetic reconstruction (e.g., Kitchen et al. 2009, "Bayesian phylogenetic analysis of Semitic languages"). This lack of context makes it difficult to assess the novelty and performance of the proposed method relative to the state of the art.
2.  **Significant methodological limitations:**
    *   **Scalability:** The authors state that the alignment procedure is a major bottleneck in memory and runtime, limiting the current implementation to a maximum of five languages. This is a severe limitation that undermines the main selling point of the paper—the ability to perform multi-way analysis. The utility for larger language families is therefore questionable.
    *   **Use of orthographic data:** The experiments rely exclusively on raw orthographic forms. This is a major weakness, as it risks conflating superficial script-level differences with deeper linguistic correspondences. For instance, the analysis finds Serbo-Croatian in Latin script to be closer to other Latin-script languages, which is an artifact of the writing system, not the language itself. The use of phonetic or phonemic transcriptions is standard practice in this field and would provide a much more linguistically sound basis for comparison.
    *   **Phylogenetic method:** The paper uses the neighbor-joining algorithm to construct its tree, which is a relatively simple method with known disadvantages (e.g., it is sensitive to the order of data and requires manual rooting). More sophisticated and standard phylogenetic methods should have been used for a more robust evaluation of the learned distance matrix.
3.  **Lack of clarity in technical details:** Several key components of the model and training procedure are insufficiently described, hindering reproducibility.
    *   It is unclear what stopping criterion is used for the EM algorithm. Do the authors run it for a fixed number of iterations or until convergence of the description length?
    *   The maximization step is described as merging the two patterns that yield the highest decrease in description length. However, the paper does not explain how the candidate pairs for merging are generated or how this search is performed efficiently. A naive search would be computationally intractable.
    *   The definition of L(π) relies on "the optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules," which is ambiguous. Clarification is needed on what this unigram model is defined over and how its costs are calculated.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The maximization step involves finding the best pair of rules to merge. Could you please elaborate on the search strategy used here? How do you efficiently identify the candidate merge that leads to the greatest reduction in the total description length?
2.  Could you clarify the meaning of "the optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" used to encode the characters within a correspondence rule? What exactly are the events this unigram model is defined over?
3.  What was the stopping criterion for your EM training procedure? Was convergence of the objective function tracked and achieved?
4.  Your method resembles Bayesian inference, where L(M) is akin to a prior and L(D|M) to a likelihood. Could you discuss the conceptual and potential empirical differences between your MDL approach and established Bayesian phylogenetic inference methods (e.g., as implemented in MrBayes)?
5.  Have you considered the impact of mixing a core vocabulary list (Swadesh) with a list containing internationalisms and pan-Slavic words, which may have different etymological histories and include more borrowings?
