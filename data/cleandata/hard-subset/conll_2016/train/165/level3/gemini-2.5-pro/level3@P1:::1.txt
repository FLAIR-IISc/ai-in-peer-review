### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a method for automatically discovering string-level correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The core idea is to find a set of correspondence rules (i.e., aligned substrings across languages) that provides the most succinct description of a given corpus of cognates, balancing the complexity of the model (the rules) and the complexity of the data encoded with the model. The authors formulate this as a two-part MDL problem and propose an iterative, EM-like algorithm for optimization. The E-step involves finding the optimal alignment of the data given the current rules, while the M-step improves the rule set by merging existing rules. The method is evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages. The authors perform pairwise comparisons using Normalized Compression Distance (NCD) to build a phylogenetic tree and conduct a novel four-way analysis to quantify the "shared Description Length" (sDL) between different subsets of languages.

### Main Contributions

1.  **A Principled, Multi-Language Framework for Correspondence Discovery:** The primary contribution is the formulation of multi-language correspondence discovery as an MDL problem. This provides a non-parametric, data-driven approach that avoids strong assumptions about the shape or distribution of correspondence rules. The ability to handle more than two languages simultaneously is a significant advantage over many existing methods, and the proposed "shared Description Length" metric offers a novel way to quantify the complex similarities among subsets of languages.
2.  **Empirical Analysis of Slavic Languages:** The paper presents a detailed case study on 13 Slavic languages. The experiments, which include building a plausible phylogenetic tree from pairwise distances and a more fine-grained four-language analysis, successfully demonstrate the method's potential for both quantitative and qualitative linguistic analysis. The discovery of various types of correspondences—from single characters to complex stems—highlights the flexibility of the approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated Theoretical Framework:** The choice of the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle is well-justified and provides a solid theoretical foundation for the problem of model selection. It elegantly balances model complexity with data fit, naturally guarding against overfitting without requiring arbitrary regularization parameters or prior distributions. This allows the algorithm to discover statistically significant patterns directly from the data.
2.  **Generalization to Multiple Languages:** A key strength of this work is its ability to move beyond pairwise analysis. The model is inherently designed to operate on an arbitrary number of languages, enabling a more holistic and detailed analysis of language families. The four-way analysis and the concept of shared Description Length (sDL) are particularly compelling, as they offer a way to dissect and quantify similarities that are missed by traditional pairwise phylogenetic methods.
3.  **Intuitive Optimization Procedure:** The EM-like training algorithm is a sensible and practical approach to a difficult, non-convex optimization problem. The procedure of iteratively aligning the data with current rules (E-step) and then greedily merging rules to improve the overall description length (M-step) is intuitive and deterministic, which makes the results reproducible and easier to interpret.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Comparison with Related Work:** The paper's positioning and claims of novelty are weakened by a lack of engagement with highly relevant prior work.
    *   The objective function, which balances a model cost and a data likelihood term (− log p(M) − log p(D|M)), is formally very similar to Bayesian inference. The paper should discuss and compare its approach to established Bayesian methods for phylogenetic inference, such as those implemented in tools like MrBayes. These methods also infer models of linguistic change and have been extensively used in historical linguistics (e.g., Kitchen et al., 2009, *PNAS*).
    *   Within computational linguistics, there is a large body of work on automatic cognate and borrowing detection that also relies on identifying regular sound correspondences (e.g., List & Moran, 2013, "On the limits of automatic detection of borrowings"). The paper does not compare its discovered correspondences or its language similarity measures to those produced by these alternative methods.
2.  **Significant Practical and Methodological Limitations:** The proposed method has several limitations that affect its practical utility and the reliability of its results.
    *   The alignment procedure is described as a major computational bottleneck, limiting the analysis to a maximum of five languages. This severely constrains the method's main selling point—the ability to analyze multiple languages simultaneously.
    *   The phylogenetic tree is constructed using the neighbor-joining algorithm, which has known disadvantages compared to more modern phylogenetic methods. For instance, it requires manual rooting and can be less accurate under varying rates of evolution.
    *   The "data over-weighting" technique (Section 4.5), while presented as a way to control the level of detail, moves the method away from its "objective" MDL foundation and introduces a subjective, user-defined parameter without clear guidance on how to set it.
3.  **Suboptimal Data Representation:** The experiments are based on orthographic forms rather than phonetic transcriptions. This is a major simplification that risks conflating differences in writing systems (e.g., Cyrillic vs. Latin scripts) with genuine linguistic divergence. For instance, the finding that the Cyrillic version of Serbo-Croatian is "more similar" to other Cyrillic-based languages may simply be an artifact of shared characters, not deeper linguistic similarity. Using a standardized phonetic representation like IPA would provide a much more linguistically sound basis for comparison. Furthermore, the data mixes Swadesh lists with a set of internationalisms and pan-Slavic words, which may bias the discovered correspondences toward particular types of words.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The description of the model code L(M) and the maximization step contains some unclear points. Could you please clarify:
    *   What is meant by "the optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" (Section 4.1, Encoding a Correspondence Rule)? How is this unigram distribution derived and encoded?
    *   In the Maximization step, how are pairs of patterns selected for merging? Is it an exhaustive search over all pairs of existing rules? Could you provide more detail on the merging operation itself?
2.  What is the stopping criterion for the EM training procedure? Is the model trained for a fixed number of iterations, or until the total description length converges?
3.  How do you see your MDL-based approach comparing conceptually to Bayesian phylogenetic inference methods? Both seem to optimize a similar objective (model evidence/posterior probability vs. description length). What are the key advantages of the MDL formulation in this specific linguistic context?
4.  Have you considered applying your method to phonetically transcribed data (e.g., using IPA)? How do you expect this would change the results, particularly in disentangling orthographic artifacts from genuine linguistic correspondences?
