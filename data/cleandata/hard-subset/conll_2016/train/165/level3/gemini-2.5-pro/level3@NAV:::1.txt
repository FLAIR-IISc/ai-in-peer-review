**Summary:**
This paper proposes a novel method for discovering cross-linguistic word correspondences using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The approach models correspondences as a set of substring-matching rules, optimized via an Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to find the most compact representation of the model and the data. The primary contribution is this unsupervised, non-parametric formulation for discovering correspondences in multiple languages simultaneously. A secondary contribution is the application of the method to 13 Slavic languages, including a "shared Description Length" metric for quantifying subgroup similarities.

**Strengths:**
1.  The core idea of framing correspondence discovery as an MDL problem is elegant and well-motivated. It provides a principled, data-driven way to infer significant patterns without imposing strong prior assumptions on their structure, which is a sound approach for this linguistic task.
2.  The model’s theoretical ability to operate on more than two languages at once is a key advantage over traditional pairwise methods. This allows for more nuanced quantitative analyses, as demonstrated by the shared description length calculations for language subgroups.
3.  The qualitative results are compelling. The example correspondences learned for Slavic languages demonstrate the model’s capacity to capture a range of linguistically plausible patterns, from simple phonetic alternations to more complex morphological features.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  The paper suffers from a critical lack of engagement with related work. The problem is central to computational historical linguistics, yet there is no comparison to established methods for cognate detection or phylogenetic inference (e.g., work by Bouchard-Côté, Warnow, List & Moran 2013) or to widely-used Bayesian phylogenetic tools like MrBayes, despite the acknowledged similarity of the MDL objective to a Bayesian one. This makes it impossible to situate the contribution or evaluate its performance against the state of the art.
2.  The method’s scalability is a major concern. The authors report a bottleneck in the alignment procedure that limits analysis to at most five languages. This severely undermines the primary claim of multi-language applicability and restricts the method’s utility for the very problems it aims to solve.
3.  Several methodological choices are questionable. The use of raw orthography risks conflating superficial script differences with deeper linguistic relationships; phonetic data would be more appropriate. The choice of neighbor-joining for tree building is outdated, and the potential confounding effect of mixing cognate and non-cognate data (Swadesh lists) is not discussed. Further, key details of the EM algorithm, such as the stopping criterion and the merging step in the M-update, are unclear.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Can you elaborate on the computational complexity of the M-step, specifically how the optimal pair of rules to merge is found efficiently?
2.  Given the scalability bottleneck, what are your concrete proposals for making the alignment procedure tractable for a larger number of languages (e.g., >10), which is common in phylogenetic studies?
