### Summary

This paper proposes a method for automatically discovering substring correspondences between multiple languages using the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle. The goal is to find a set of correspondence rules that provides the most succinct description of the data (cognate lists) by balancing the complexity of the model (the rules) and the length of the data encoded with that model. The authors frame this as a non-convex optimization problem and propose an efficient Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm to find a good solution. The E-step involves aligning the data using the current set of rules, while the M-step improves the model by merging rules that yield the largest decrease in total description length. The method is evaluated on a dataset of 13 Slavic languages, where it is used to compute pairwise language distances, construct a phylogenetic tree, and provide qualitative examples of learned multi-language correspondences.

### Contributions

1.  **A novel MDL-based formulation for multi-language correspondence discovery:** The primary contribution is a well-motivated, non-parametric framework for identifying significant substring patterns across more than two languages simultaneously. By avoiding prior assumptions about the distribution of rules, the method offers an objective way to model linguistic data.

2.  **A method for quantifying n-way linguistic similarity:** A key strength of the proposed approach is its ability to move beyond simple pairwise comparisons. The concept of "shared Description Length" (sDL) provides a principled, information-theoretic measure to quantify the amount of shared structure among any subset of the languages under analysis, offering a more fine-grained view of linguistic relationships than traditional phylogenetic trees alone.

### Strengths

1.  **Sensible and well-motivated framework:** The choice of MDL is well-justified for this discovery task, as it naturally balances model complexity with goodness-of-fit, thus preventing overfitting to spurious patterns. The formulation as an optimization problem to be solved with EM is a standard and appropriate choice for a model with latent alignments.

2.  **Promising empirical results on Slavic languages:** The evaluation demonstrates the method's potential. The pairwise Normalized Compression Distances (NCDs) produce a phylogenetic tree that largely corresponds to the established linguistic classification of Slavic languages (West, East, South). This provides strong evidence that the learned model captures genuine linguistic similarities.

3.  **Detailed n-way analysis:** The four-way analysis of Czech, Polish, Russian, and Bulgarian is a compelling demonstration of the method's main advantage over pairwise approaches. The ability to identify and quantify similarities specific to subgroups (e.g., CS-PL vs. RU-BG) and even across groups (e.g., similarities involving Russian and another subset) is a powerful analytical tool.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Critical lack of comparison to related work:** The paper positions itself as a novel approach to finding correspondences but fails to compare with or even cite the extensive literature in computational historical linguistics on cognate detection, borrowing detection, and phylogenetic inference. Methods by Bouchard-Côté et al., Warnow, Nakhleh, Kitchen et al. (2009), and comprehensive surveys like List & Moran (2013) are highly relevant and represent the state of the art. Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the novelty and performance of the proposed method relative to existing techniques.

2.  **Major scalability limitations:** The paper reports a significant memory and runtime bottleneck in the alignment procedure, limiting experiments to a maximum of five languages. This is a severe practical limitation that undermines the primary claim of being able to analyze multiple languages simultaneously. For a method whose main contribution is n-way analysis, being unable to scale beyond small `n` is a major drawback.

3.  **Oversimplified phylogenetic evaluation:** The use of neighbor-joining to build a tree from a distance matrix is a simple heuristic with known disadvantages (e.g., requiring manual rooting and sensitivity to rate variation). A more convincing evaluation would involve comparing the MDL-based model and its objective function to established probabilistic models for phylogenetic inference, such as those used in Bayesian tools like MrBayes or BEAST. The resemblance of the MDL objective to a Bayesian MAP objective makes this comparison particularly relevant and necessary.

4.  **Use of orthographic data:** The experiments rely on orthographic representations of words. This is a significant methodological weakness, as the model may learn superficial correspondences based on shared writing systems (e.g., Cyrillic vs. Latin scripts) rather than true phonological correspondences that reflect linguistic history. Using phonetic transcriptions (e.g., IPA) would provide a much more linguistically sound basis for evaluation.

5.  **Lack of clarity on technical details:** Several key aspects of the method are insufficiently explained. It is unclear what is meant by "the optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules." The description of the maximization step is also vague, particularly regarding how candidate rules are selected for merging. Finally, the paper does not state the stopping criterion for the EM algorithm (e.g., convergence of the objective function or a fixed number of iterations).

### Questions to Authors

1.  The scalability of the alignment procedure is a major bottleneck. Do you have concrete plans or ideas for improving its efficiency to allow for the analysis of larger sets of languages (e.g., >10)?

2.  Could you elaborate on the maximization step of your algorithm? Specifically, how are pairs of patterns selected for a potential merge, and how is the "optimal unigram for symbol usages in all rules" computed and used?

3.  What was the stopping criterion for your EM training procedure? Did the algorithm typically run to convergence, and if so, how many iterations did this require?

4.  The similarity between the MDL objective and a Bayesian MAP objective is noted. How do you see your approach comparing to established Bayesian phylogenetic inference models that also jointly infer alignments and trees? An empirical comparison, even on a small scale, would significantly strengthen the paper.

5.  Could you comment on the potential confounding effect of using orthographic data? Do you believe the core findings would hold if the method were applied to phonetic transcriptions, and would this be a straightforward adaptation?

### Additional Comments

-   The discussion of data "over-weighting" (Sec 4.5) is interesting but moves the method away from its objective MDL foundation. This should be framed more clearly as a heuristic for exploratory analysis rather than a core part of the statistical model.
-   Mixing a pan-Slavic cognate set with Swadesh lists (which may contain non-cognates or borrowings) could influence the results. It would be useful to analyze these datasets separately to understand their impact.
-   Minor editorial suggestion (line 440): change "focus in on" to "focus on".
