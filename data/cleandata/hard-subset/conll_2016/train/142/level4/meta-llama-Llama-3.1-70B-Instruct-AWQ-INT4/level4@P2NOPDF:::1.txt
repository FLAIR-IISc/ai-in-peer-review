The paper explores the application of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian processes, to predict post-editing time/rates for quality estimation of machine translation (MT) output. The authors present a well-structured paper with a clear introduction that highlights the limitations of point estimates in quality estimation for real-world applications. The description of asymmetric risk scenarios and their corresponding estimators is particularly noteworthy.

The paper dedicates sufficient space to explaining Gaussian processes, which may be beneficial for readers unfamiliar with the concept. The authors clearly describe their GP approach, including kernel choices and the use of warping. The research questions posed by the paper are interesting and well-formulated.

However, I have some questions and suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion sections, particularly for Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation:

* The authors choose to predict post-editing rates instead of prediction (H)TER or TER, a common metric in quality estimation research. It would be beneficial to justify this choice in the paper.
* In Section 3.2, the first paragraph is unclear. The authors mention a trend observed for fr-en and en-de datasets that is not present in the en-es dataset. However, the text states that NLL and NLPD decrease drastically with warped GPs for all three datasets, which seems contradictory.
* Although the authors acknowledge that their work does not aim to advance the state-of-the-art, it would be helpful to include a comparison with another point estimate model from existing research in the result tables. This would provide a better understanding of the overall quality of the models.
* The paper emphasizes the limitations of point estimates, but the results are presented in terms of NLL and NLPD, which can be difficult to interpret. Including a qualitative analysis with actual examples would help to illustrate the significance of the reductions in NLL and NLPD. Alternatively, providing a notion of what constitutes a drastic reduction in these values would be beneficial.

Section 4 is well-written and effectively explains the results in an intuitive manner.

Overall, I appreciate the paper's contribution to highlighting the problematic use of point estimates in quality estimation. The research questions and goals are clearly stated and executed. Although the submission does not advance the state-of-the-art or provide significant novelty in terms of modeling, its focus on the importance of confidence information in quality estimation is valuable.

Minor issues:

* In Section 4, "over and underestimates" should be "over- and underestimates."
* The caption for Figure 1 incorrectly states that the lines are blue and red, when they are actually blue and green.
* If a specific toolkit was used for GP modeling, it would be helpful to include a reference to it in the final paper.
