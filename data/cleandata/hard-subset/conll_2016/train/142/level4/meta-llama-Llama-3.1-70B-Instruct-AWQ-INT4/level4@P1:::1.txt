The paper explores the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian processes (GPs), to regress on the target variable of post-editing time/rates for quality estimation of machine translation (MT) output. The paper is well-structured with a clear introduction that highlights the problem of quality estimation (QE) point estimates in real-world applications. I particularly appreciated the description of the different asymmetric risk scenarios and how they entail different estimators.

For readers familiar with GPs, the paper spends considerable space reflecting on these concepts, but I believe it is worth the effort to introduce these concepts to the reader. The GP approach and the choices for kernels and using warping are explained clearly and are easy to follow. In general, the research questions that are to be answered by this paper are interesting and well-phrased.

However, I do have some questions/suggestions about the Results and Discussion sections for Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation:

- Why were post-editing rates chosen over prediction (H)TER? TER is a common value to predict in QE research, and it would have been nice to justify the choice made in the paper.
- Section 3.2: I don't understand the first paragraph at all: What exactly is the trend you see for fr-en & en-de that you do not see for en-es? NLL and NLPD 'drastically' decrease with warped GPs for all three datasets.
- The paper indeed states that it does not want to advance state-of-the-art (given that they use only the standard 17 baseline features), but it would have been nice to show another point estimate model from existing work in the result tables, to get a sense of the overall quality of the models.
- Related to this, it is hard to interpret NLL and NLPD values, so one is always tempted to look at MAE in the tables to get a sense of 'how different the predictions are'. Since the whole point of the paper is to say that this is not the right thing to do, it would be great to provide some notion of what is a drastic reduce in NLL/NLPD worth: A qualitative analysis with actual examples.

Section 4 is very nicely written and explains results very intuitively!

Overall, I like the paper since it points out the problematic use of point estimates in QE. A difficult task in general where additional information such as confidence arguably are very important. The submission does not advance state-of-the-art and does not provide a lot of novelty in terms of modeling (since GPs have been used before), but its research questions and goals are clearly stated and nicely executed.

Minor problems:

- Section 4: "over and underestimates" -> "over- and underestimates"
- Figure 1 caption: Lines are actually blue and green, not blue and red as stated in the caption.
- If a certain toolkit was used for GP modeling, it would be great to refer to this in the final paper.

**Summary of the paper:**

The paper explores probabilistic models for machine translation quality estimation (QE), focusing on Gaussian processes (GPs) to regress on post-editing time/rates. The authors argue that point estimates are insufficient for real-world applications and propose using well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. They introduce new evaluation metrics, such as Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), to capture the full posterior predictive distributions. The paper also discusses asymmetric risk scenarios and proposes Bayes risk estimators for these scenarios.

**Main contributions:**

1. The paper proposes the use of probabilistic models, specifically GPs, for QE, which allows for well-calibrated uncertainty estimates.
2. The authors introduce new evaluation metrics, such as NLPD, to capture the full posterior predictive distributions.
3. The paper discusses asymmetric risk scenarios and proposes Bayes risk estimators for these scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper is well-structured and easy to follow, with a clear introduction and well-explained concepts.
2. The authors provide a thorough discussion of the research questions and goals.
3. The paper proposes new evaluation metrics and discusses asymmetric risk scenarios, which are important contributions to the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper does not advance state-of-the-art in terms of modeling, as GPs have been used before in QE research.
2. The authors do not provide a lot of novelty in terms of modeling.
3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the results and a qualitative analysis with actual examples.

**Questions to authors:**

1. Can you provide more justification for choosing post-editing rates over prediction (H)TER?
2. Can you clarify the trend you see in Section 3.2 for fr-en & en-de that you do not see for en-es?
3. Would you consider including another point estimate model from existing work in the result tables to provide a sense of the overall quality of the models?
4. Can you provide more information on the toolkit used for GP modeling?
