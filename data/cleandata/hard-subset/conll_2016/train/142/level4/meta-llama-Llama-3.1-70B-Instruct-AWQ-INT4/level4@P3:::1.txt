The paper explores the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors propose a novel approach to QE by modeling uncertainty in the predictions, which is essential for real-world applications. The paper is well-structured, and the introduction effectively highlights the limitations of traditional point estimate-based QE methods.

The authors provide a clear explanation of GPs, including their underlying theory, and discuss the choices of kernels and warping functions. The research questions are well-phrased, and the use of asymmetric risk scenarios is a valuable contribution to the field.

However, there are some concerns and suggestions for improvement:

1. **Choice of evaluation metric**: The authors use post-editing rates as the target variable, but it would be beneficial to justify this choice over other common metrics like prediction (H)TER.
2. **Clarity in results**: The first paragraph of Section 3.2 is unclear, and it would be helpful to rephrase it for better understanding. The trend observed in the results for fr-en and en-de datasets is not apparent, and the drastic decrease in NLL and NLPD values for all three datasets using warped GPs is not fully explained.
3. **Comparison to existing work**: Although the authors state that they do not aim to advance the state-of-the-art, including a point estimate model from existing work in the result tables would provide a better understanding of the overall quality of the models.
4. **Interpretability of NLL and NLPD values**: As the paper argues against the use of point estimates, it would be helpful to provide a qualitative analysis with actual examples to illustrate the significance of the reductions in NLL and NLPD values.

The paper's strengths include:

1. **Clear research questions**: The authors effectively identify the limitations of traditional QE methods and propose a novel approach to address these issues.
2. **Well-structured paper**: The paper is easy to follow, and the introduction, methodology, and results are well-organized.
3. **Valuable contribution**: The use of asymmetric risk scenarios and the proposal of a novel approach to QE are significant contributions to the field.

Weaknesses:

1. **Lack of novelty in modeling**: Although the authors apply GPs to QE, the use of GPs is not new, and the paper does not provide significant novelty in terms of modeling.
2. **Limited comparison to existing work**: The paper does not include a comprehensive comparison to existing point estimate-based QE methods, which would have strengthened the argument for the proposed approach.

Questions to authors:

1. Can you provide a more detailed justification for choosing post-editing rates over other common metrics like prediction (H)TER?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of interpretability of NLL and NLPD values in future work?
3. Can you include a point estimate model from existing work in the result tables to provide a better understanding of the overall quality of the models?
