Summary
This paper investigates probabilistic approaches to machine translation quality estimation (QE) using Gaussian Processes (GPs), with a focus on obtaining calibrated uncertainty estimates rather than just point predictions. The authors evaluate posterior predictive distributions using NLPD, explore kernel and warping choices, and demonstrate how posterior information supports decision-making under asymmetric risk (optimistic/pessimistic) scenarios.

Main contributions (as I see them)
- Demonstration that probabilistic evaluation with NLPD reveals differences among QE models that are not captured by point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r), highlighting the importance of calibrated uncertainty in QE.
- Application and assessment of Warped GPs and Matérn kernels for QE, showing consistent gains in NLL/NLPD and analyzing the impact of warping choices on predictive uncertainty.
- A practical framework for asymmetric risk scenarios using Bayes estimators (quantiles for asymmetric linear loss; closed-form estimator for linex), illustrating how predictive distributions can tailor decisions to real-world workflows.

Strengths
- Strong motivation for uncertainty-aware QE: The paper clearly articulates why point estimates are insufficient in production workflows and how uncertainty can improve decisions (e.g., post-editing, gisting).
- Clear methodology and exposition: The presentation of GPs, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs is accessible and well-structured; the asymmetric loss scenarios and corresponding Bayes estimators are explained intuitively.
- Empirical results support the thesis: Warping substantially improves NLL/NLPD across datasets, underscoring the value of modeling the non-Gaussian nature of QE targets and of evaluating full predictive distributions.
- Practical relevance: The asymmetric risk analysis convincingly connects predictive uncertainty to operational decisions, showing how to adapt predictions without retraining when the loss or business constraints change.

Weaknesses
- Target choice not justified: The paper predicts post-editing rates rather than (H)TER, a standard QE target. The rationale for this choice (e.g., availability, reliability, application fit) is not provided, limiting comparability to prior work.
- Interpretation of Section 3.2: The first paragraph claims a discrepancy between fr-en/en-de and en-es trends, but all three datasets show large NLL/NLPD reductions with warping. The argument about overfitting in en-es needs clearer evidence and phrasing.
- Missing baseline context: Although the paper does not aim to advance state-of-the-art, including at least one competitive point-estimate baseline from prior QE work (e.g., SVR or a recent neural baseline) would contextualize the absolute difficulty of the task and the relative performance of the GP models.
- Limited interpretability of NLL/NLPD: Readers may struggle to gauge what constitutes a “large” improvement in NLL/NLPD. Without qualitative examples (e.g., predictive intervals, reliability plots, or case studies), it is hard to internalize the practical impact beyond table scores.
- Modeling novelty is modest: GPs (including Warped GPs) have been applied to QE before; the novelty lies more in the evaluation emphasis (NLPD, asymmetric risk) than in new modeling techniques. This is acceptable but should be more explicitly acknowledged.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Why were post-editing rates chosen over (H)TER? Please clarify the choice, its implications for generality, and how it affects comparability with prior QE research.
- Section 3.2: What “trend” differs in en-es versus fr-en/en-de? If the claim is overfitting in en-es, can you provide supporting evidence (e.g., train vs. test NLL gap, calibration plots, predictive variance analysis)?
- Can you provide a qualitative analysis illustrating NLPD improvements? For example: coverage of predictive intervals, reliability diagrams, or a few sentence-level examples showing how models with similar MAE differ markedly in uncertainty/calibration.
- Could you include at least one strong point-estimate baseline (e.g., SVR with RBF, or a previously published QE system) to situate the GP performance in absolute terms?
- For the pessimistic linex setting, you hypothesize that variance drives the behavior. Can you show how varying predictive variance (or truncating at zero) affects linex loss, and whether calibration/sharpness trade-offs differ across models?

Minor comments
- Section 4: use “over- and underestimates.”
- Figure 1 caption: line colors appear blue and green (not blue and red); please fix.
- If you used a specific GP toolkit (e.g., GPML, GPy), please cite it and provide details for reproducibility (hyperparameter optimization settings, warping implementation, quadrature settings).
- Consider adding brief guidance on the practical scale of NLL/NLPD changes (e.g., typical ranges in your datasets) to improve interpretability.
