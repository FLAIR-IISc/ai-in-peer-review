The paper investigates probabilistic approaches—specifically Gaussian Processes—for predicting post-editing time/rates as the target variable for machine translation quality estimation.

The manuscript is well organized, with a clear introduction that motivates the limitations of point estimates in real-world QE workflows. I particularly appreciated the treatment of asymmetric risk scenarios and the way these lead to different optimal estimators.

For readers already familiar with GPs, a sizable portion recaps known material, but I think this background is justified to make the work accessible.

The GP setup, kernel choices, and use of output warping are explained cleanly and are straightforward to follow. Overall, the research questions are interesting and articulated well.

However, I have a few questions/suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion for Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation:
- Why were post-editing rates selected over predicting (H)TER? TER is a standard target in QE, and it would help to justify this design choice.
- Section 3.2: I find the first paragraph unclear. What precise trend is observed for fr-en and en-de that does not appear for en-es? The NLL and NLPD appear to drop substantially with warped GPs for all three datasets.
- While the paper states it does not aim to push the state of the art (given the use of the standard 17 baseline features), including a representative point-estimate model from prior work in the results would help contextualize overall performance.
- NLL and NLPD are not very interpretable in isolation, so readers may default to MAE to gauge how different the predictions are. Since the paper’s thesis is that this is misleading, it would strengthen the work to include a qualitative analysis with concrete examples illustrating what a large reduction in NLL/NLPD “looks like” in practice.

Section 4 is excellently written and offers intuitive explanations of the results.

Overall, I like the paper for highlighting the pitfalls of relying on point estimates in QE—a challenging task where additional signals such as confidence are arguably crucial. The submission does not advance the state of the art and offers limited modeling novelty (since GPs have prior use in QE), but its aims are clearly defined and executed well.

Minor problems:
- Section 4: “over and underestimates” → “over- and underestimates”
- Figure 1 caption: the lines are blue and green, not blue and red as stated.
- If a specific toolkit was used for GP modelling, please cite it.
