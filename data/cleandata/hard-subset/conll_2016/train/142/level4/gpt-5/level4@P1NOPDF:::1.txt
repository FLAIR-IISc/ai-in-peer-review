The paper investigates probabilistic modeling—specifically Gaussian processes (GPs)—to regress post-editing time/rates for MT quality estimation. The paper is well structured, with a clear introduction that highlights the limitations of point estimates in real-world QE applications. I especially liked the discussion of asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications for different estimators.

While readers familiar with GPs may find the background material extensive, I think the space devoted to introducing these concepts is justified and helpful. The GP setup, kernel choices, and the use of output warping are clearly explained and easy to follow. Overall, the research questions are interesting and well posed.

That said, I have several questions and suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion for the Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation:

- Why were post-editing rates chosen over (H)TER as the prediction target? TER is a common outcome in QE research; please justify this choice and discuss any trade-offs relative to (H)TER.

- Section 3.2: I do not understand the first paragraph. What specific trend do you observe for fr–en and en–de that you do not observe for en–es? As presented, NLL and NLPD “drastically” decrease with warped GPs for all three datasets. If there is a qualitative or quantitative difference for en–es, please spell it out explicitly and explain the cause.

- Although the paper does not aim to advance the state of the art (given the use of the standard 17 baseline features), it would still be helpful to include at least one strong point-estimate baseline from prior work in the tables. This would better contextualize the absolute quality of your models, even if the focus is on uncertainty.

- NLL and NLPD are hard to interpret in isolation, so readers will be tempted to look at MAE to gauge “how different the predictions are.” Since the core argument of the paper is that this is not the right lens, please provide guidance on what constitutes a practically meaningful reduction in NLL/NLPD. A small qualitative analysis with concrete examples—e.g., predictions with uncertainty intervals before/after warping, and how these would affect decisions under asymmetric loss—would make the gains much more tangible.

Section 4 is very clear and explains the results intuitively.

Overall, I like the paper. It effectively highlights the pitfalls of relying on point estimates in QE—a challenging setting where calibrated confidence is arguably crucial. While the submission does not push the state of the art and offers limited modeling novelty (GPs have been used before), the goals are clearly articulated and the study is carefully executed.

Minor issues:
- Section 4: “over and underestimates” → “over- and underestimates.”
- Figure 1 caption: The lines are blue and green, not blue and red.
- If a specific toolkit was used for GP modeling, please cite or reference it in the final paper.
