Summary and contributions
The paper investigates probabilistic modeling for machine translation quality estimation (QE) using Gaussian Processes (GPs) to predict post-editing time/rates. It argues for evaluating models via their full posterior predictive distributions rather than point estimates, and explores modeling choices (Matérn kernels and Warped GPs) to improve uncertainty calibration. It further demonstrates how predictive posteriors can be exploited under asymmetric risk using asymmetric linear (AL) and linex losses with Bayes estimators.

As I see them, the primary contributions are:
1) A careful, uncertainty-aware evaluation of QE models using NLPD, showing that conventional point metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling.
2) A clear formulation and empirical demonstration of asymmetric risk scenarios in QE (pessimistic vs. optimistic), leveraging the predictive distribution with appropriate Bayes estimators.
3) Practical modeling guidance for QE: Warped GPs to handle positive-valued targets and Matérn kernels to relax smoothness assumptions, with evidence that warping substantially improves NLL/NLPD.

Strengths
- Motivation and clarity: The paper is well structured, with a clear motivation for moving beyond point estimates in real-world QE workflows. The exposition of kernels, warping, and GP fundamentals is accessible and justified, even for readers less familiar with GPs.
- Asymmetric risk treatment: Section 4 is particularly strong. The scenarios are well motivated, the estimators are derived cleanly, and the empirical results are explained intuitively.
- Modeling choices and transparency: The kernel/warping design choices are sensible and clearly argued. The experimental protocol (three language pairs, 10-fold cross-validation, and Bayes estimators without retraining) is sound and easy to follow.
- Empirical insight: The results highlight that Warped GPs markedly reduce NLL/NLPD versus standard GPs, illustrating the value of calibrated uncertainty. The discussion of why linex in the pessimistic regime may favor higher-variance models is thoughtful and helps interpret the mixed results.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and scope: The modeling techniques (GPs, Warped GPs, Matérn kernels) are established in QE and broader ML, and the feature set is restricted to 17 baseline features. While the paper does not aim to push SOTA, including a stronger point-estimate baseline from prior QE work in the tables would help readers calibrate absolute performance.
- Choice of target variable: The paper predicts post-editing rates rather than (H)TER, which is common in QE. The choice is not justified. Given the community’s frequent use of (H)TER, readers need a rationale for predicting post-editing rates instead.
- Clarity in Section 3.2: The first paragraph is confusing. It claims a trend seen for fr-en and en-de but not for en-es, yet Warped GPs “drastically” reduce both NLL and NLPD across all datasets. The precise difference in trends (NLL vs. NLPD alignment) and the evidence for overfitting on en-es need clearer explanation.
- Interpreting NLL/NLPD: These values are hard to interpret in isolation. Readers will gravitate to MAE to understand “how different” predictions are, which runs counter to the paper’s message. Please add a qualitative analysis with concrete examples to illustrate what a “drastic” reduction in NLL/NLPD looks like in practice (e.g., how decisions would change for specific segments under different models).
- Result context: Because the goal is to advocate for uncertainty-aware evaluation, showing at least one strong point-estimate baseline (e.g., SVR) alongside GP variants would contextualize the gains in uncertainty metrics versus conventional performance.

Questions to authors
- Why did you choose post-editing rates over (H)TER as the target? Please justify this choice and discuss implications for generality and comparability to prior QE work.
- In Section 3.2, what exactly is the trend seen for fr-en and en-de that is absent for en-es? If the claim is that NLL tracks NLPD for the larger datasets but not for en-es due to overfitting, please clarify the evidence.
- Could you include a representative point-estimate baseline from prior work in the result tables to help contextualize your models’ absolute performance?
- Which GP toolkit or implementation did you use? Providing this information would aid reproducibility.

Additional comments and minor issues
- Section 4: “over and underestimates” should be “over- and underestimates.”
- Figure 1 caption: The lines appear blue and green, not blue and red as stated.
- If a particular GP toolkit was used, please cite or mention it explicitly.

Overall assessment
I like the paper’s focus on uncertainty in QE, a difficult task where confidence estimates are valuable for decision-making. While the modeling novelty is limited and the work does not advance SOTA in point prediction, the research questions are clear and the execution is solid. Addressing the clarity issues in Section 3.2, justifying the target variable, contextualizing results with a standard baseline, and adding qualitative examples would substantially strengthen the submission.
