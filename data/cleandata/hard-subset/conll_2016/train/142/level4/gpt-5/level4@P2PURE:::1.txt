The paper investigates using probabilistic models (Gaussian Processes) to regress post-editing time/rates for MT quality estimation.

The paper is well structured, with a clear introduction that frames the problem of relying on QE point estimates in real-world applications. I particularly appreciated the discussion of asymmetric risk scenarios and how they imply different estimators. Although readers already familiar with GPs may find the background somewhat lengthy, the exposition is worthwhile to introduce these concepts. The GP methodology—kernel choices and output warping—is explained clearly and is easy to follow. Overall, the research questions are interesting and well posed.

However, I have several questions and suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion for the Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation:
- Why were post-editing rates chosen over predicting TER/HTER? TER is a common target in QE research; please justify this choice.
- Section 3.2: The first paragraph is unclear. What trend do you observe for fr–en and en–de that you do not observe for en–es? NLL and NLPD “drastically” decrease with warped GPs for all three datasets.
- While the paper explicitly does not seek to advance the state of the art (given the use of only the standard 17 baseline features), it would be helpful to include at least one point-estimate model from prior work in the results tables to contextualize overall model quality.
- Relatedly, NLL and NLPD are difficult to interpret, which tempts readers to rely on MAE to gauge “how different the predictions are.” Since the paper argues this is not the right focus, please provide guidance on what constitutes a “drastic” reduction in NLL/NLPD—ideally via a qualitative analysis with concrete examples.

Section 4 is very well written and explains the results intuitively.

Overall, I like the paper because it highlights the problematic use of point estimates in QE—a challenging task where additional information such as confidence is arguably very important. The submission does not advance the state of the art and offers limited modeling novelty (GPs have been used before), but the research questions and goals are clearly stated and well executed.

Minor issues:
- Section 4: “over and underestimates” -> “over- and underestimates”
- Figure 1 caption: the lines are blue and green, not blue and red as stated.
- If a specific toolkit was used for GP modeling, please cite it in the final paper.
