The manuscript investigates using probabilistic models (Gaussian Processes) to regress the target variable of post-editing time/rates for quality estimation of MT output.
The paper is well organized, with an introduction that clearly frames the limitations of QE point estimates in practical settings. I particularly appreciated the discussion of asymmetric risk scenarios and how they motivate different estimators.
Readers already familiar with GPs may find the background somewhat extensive, but I think the space devoted to introducing these concepts is justified.
The GP methodology, including kernel choices and the use of warping, is presented very clearly and is easy to follow. Overall, the research questions are interesting and well formulated.
However, I have several questions/suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion sections for Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation:
- Why was post-editing rate chosen instead of predicting (H)TER? TER is a common target in QE research, and a justification for this decision would be helpful.
- Section 3.2: I find the first paragraph unclear. What specific trend do you observe for fr–en and en–de that is not present for en–es? NLL and NLPD appear to decrease “drastically” with warped GPs for all three datasets.
- The paper notes it does not aim to advance the state of the art (given the reliance on the standard 17 baseline features), but it would still be useful to include a point-estimate model from prior work in the result tables to contextualize overall model quality.
- Relatedly, NLL and NLPD are hard to interpret, which tempts readers to consult MAE to gauge “how different the predictions are.” Since the paper argues this is not appropriate, it would help to calibrate what a substantial reduction in NLL/NLPD means—for example, via a qualitative analysis with concrete examples.
Section 4 is very well written and explains the results intuitively.
Overall, I like the paper for highlighting the issues with relying on point estimates in QE—a challenging problem where additional information such as confidence is arguably crucial. The submission does not advance the state of the art and offers limited modeling novelty (GPs have been used previously), but its questions and objectives are clearly stated and executed.
Minor issues:
- Section 4: “over and underestimates” -> “over- and underestimates”
- Figure 1 caption: the lines are blue and green, not blue and red as stated.
- If a specific toolkit was used for GP modeling, please reference it in the final paper.
