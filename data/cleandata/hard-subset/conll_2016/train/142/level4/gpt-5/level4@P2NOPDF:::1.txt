This paper investigates probabilistic modeling with Gaussian processes (GPs) to regress post-editing time/rates for quality estimation (QE) of MT output.

The paper is well organized, with a clear introduction that frames the limitations of point estimates in real-world QE applications. I especially appreciated the discussion of asymmetric risk scenarios and how they imply different optimal estimators. While the exposition on GPs may feel lengthy to readers already familiar with the topic, it is a useful and accessible introduction for the broader audience. The modeling choices—kernel selection and target warping—are explained clearly and are easy to follow. Overall, the research questions are well posed and interesting.

That said, I have several questions and suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion, specifically for Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation:
- Why focus on post-editing rates instead of predicting (H)TER? TER is a common target in QE, and the paper should justify prioritizing time/rates over TER, particularly in light of comparability with prior work.
- Section 3.2: The first paragraph is unclear. What specific trend is observed for fr–en and en–de that is absent for en–es? As reported, NLL and NLPD decrease substantially with warped GPs across all three datasets, so the stated difference in trends is hard to interpret.
- Although the paper states it does not aim to advance the state of the art (given the use of the standard 17 baseline features), it would still help to include at least one strong point-estimate baseline from prior work in the results tables, to contextualize the overall predictive quality.
- NLL and NLPD values are difficult to interpret in isolation, which encourages readers to rely on MAE to gauge predictive differences. Since the paper’s central argument is that this is inappropriate, please add a qualitative analysis with concrete examples that illustrates what constitutes a substantial reduction in NLL/NLPD in practical terms (e.g., how uncertainty calibration changes decision outcomes under asymmetric costs).

Section 4 is very well written and provides an intuitive explanation of the results.

Overall, I like the paper. It convincingly highlights the pitfalls of relying solely on point estimates for QE—a challenging task where uncertainty information is indeed valuable. The submission does not claim SOTA performance and offers limited novelty in modeling per se (GPs have been used before), but the goals and research questions are clearly articulated and executed thoughtfully.

Minor issues:
- Section 4: “over and underestimates” → “over- and underestimates.”
- Figure 1 caption: The lines are blue and green, not blue and red as stated.
- If a particular toolkit was used for GP modeling, please cite or reference it in the final version.
