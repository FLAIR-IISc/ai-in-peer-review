This paper investigates Gaussian Process (GP) regression for MT quality estimation (QE), targeting post-editing time/rates and explicitly modeling predictive uncertainty. The motivation—moving beyond point estimates to support asymmetric risk scenarios—is clearly articulated, and the decision-theoretic framing is compelling. The exposition of GP modeling choices (kernels, warping) is clear and accessible, and the research questions are well-posed. Section 4, in particular, offers an intuitive explanation of results and their practical implications.

Strengths
- Timely and well-motivated argument for probabilistic QE, with concrete asymmetric risk scenarios that directly inform decision-making.
- Clear presentation of the GP approach, including kernel choices and output warping, with good pedagogical value for readers unfamiliar with GPs.
- Nicely structured paper with a coherent narrative and well-defined research questions.
- Empirical results suggest meaningful improvements in NLL/NLPD with warped GPs, supporting the value of modeling heteroscedasticity and non-Gaussianity.

Questions and suggestions
- Target variable choice: Please justify the choice of post-editing time/rates over (H)TER. TER is commonly predicted in QE, and a brief rationale (e.g., closer link to real editing cost, noisier labels, alignment with risk functions) would help readers situate your work. If applicable, a short comparison or discussion of transferability to (H)TER would be valuable.
- Clarification of Section 3.2: The first paragraph is unclear. You mention a trend for fr-en and en-de that is not present for en-es, yet NLL/NLPD decrease substantially with warped GPs for all datasets. Please make explicit:
  - What trend you are referring to (e.g., relative gains of warping across kernels, behavior under specific heteroscedasticity patterns).
  - Whether the en-es dataset has different label distributions, noise characteristics, or feature-label relationships that might explain the discrepancy.
  - Any dataset statistics (e.g., variance, skewness) that correlate with the observed behavior.
- Contextualizing performance: Although advancing SOTA is not the goal, including at least one strong point-estimate baseline from prior work in your result tables would help readers interpret the absolute quality of predictions. Even a simple, well-tuned regression baseline (e.g., SVR or tree-based) would provide a useful anchor.
- Interpreting NLL/NLPD: Since these metrics are less intuitive, please offer guidance on what constitutes a “large” reduction. Suggestions:
  - Include a qualitative analysis with a few example sentences showing predicted means, predictive intervals, and the realized outcomes, contrasting unwarped vs. warped GPs.
  - Report calibration analyses (e.g., empirical coverage of predictive intervals at 50/80/95%), which directly assess whether uncertainty estimates are reliable.
  - Where feasible, add a proper scoring rule complementary to NLL/NLPD (e.g., CRPS) that is often easier to interpret.
- Scope and novelty: While the modeling novelty is limited (GPs in QE are not new), the paper’s contribution lies in the principled framing of risk and the careful treatment of uncertainty. Emphasize this positioning explicitly and consider a brief discussion of when probabilistic QE should be preferred operationally (e.g., triaging human effort, dynamic pricing), and any limits (e.g., data scarcity, heavy tails).

Additional clarifications that would strengthen the paper
- Provide a brief ablation on kernel and warping choices, noting when warping helps most (e.g., skewed or heavy-tailed labels).
- If possible, comment on computational cost and scalability (training/inference time, dataset sizes), since deployability is central to the paper’s motivation.

Minor issues
- Section 4: “over and underestimates” → “over- and underestimates.”
- Figure 1 caption: The lines appear blue and green, not blue and red; please correct.
- If you used a specific GP toolkit, please cite it (and version) for reproducibility.

Overall assessment
This is a well-written paper that makes a strong case for probabilistic QE, highlights the limitations of point estimates in real-world scenarios, and demonstrates clear benefits from modeling uncertainty with warped GPs. While it does not push the state of the art or introduce radically new models, it offers a valuable, well-executed contribution: framing QE as risk-sensitive decision-making and showing how better uncertainty estimates can materially improve it. With clarifications to Section 3.2, justification for the target variable, improved interpretability of NLL/NLPD (ideally via qualitative examples and calibration), and inclusion of a point-estimate baseline for context, the paper would be a solid and practically relevant addition to the literature.
