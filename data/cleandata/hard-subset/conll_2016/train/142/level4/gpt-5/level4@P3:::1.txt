Summary and contributions
This paper argues that Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) should move beyond point predictions to calibrated predictive distributions, and explores Gaussian Processes (GPs) for this purpose. The authors (i) evaluate QE models using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to account for uncertainty, showing that point-metrics can miss important differences; (ii) apply and assess Warped GPs and Matérn kernels to improve uncertainty modeling for strictly positive targets (post‑editing rates); and (iii) demonstrate how posterior predictive distributions enable principled decision-making under asymmetric risk via Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses (asymmetric linear and linex losses), reflecting realistic translation workflows.

Strengths
- Clear, practically motivated problem framing: The paper convincingly argues that point estimates are often insufficient in real-world QE, where downstream decisions depend on uncertainty. The asymmetric risk scenarios are well chosen and clearly explained, illustrating how uncertainty-aware predictions can change operational decisions.
- Solid probabilistic treatment and model choices: The use of GPs, especially Warped GPs for positive-valued targets and Matérn kernels to relax smoothness, is well motivated and clearly presented. The explanation of warping and its consequences for mean vs median predictions is precise and accessible.
- Appropriate evaluation with proper scoring rules: Using NLPD and test likelihood is apt for assessing both accuracy and calibration. The analysis that differences in uncertainty are not captured by MAE/Pearson’s r is important for the QE community and well supported by results across three language pairs.
- Thoughtful asymmetric-risk evaluation: The Bayes risk estimators for AL and linex losses are correctly derived and applied post hoc to the predictive distributions, avoiding retraining. The empirical findings (e.g., Warped GPs performing well in optimistic settings; linex pessimistic behavior) are insightful and of practical interest.
- Transparent experimental setup: The use of standard QuEst features, cross-validation, and a two-pass hyperparameter optimization procedure is sensible and clearly described, facilitating reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and baselines: While the uncertainty-centric evaluation is valuable, the modeling components (GPs for QE, warping, Matérn kernels) have precedents. The study would be stronger with comparative baselines beyond GP variants (e.g., SVR/Random Forest point-estimate methods for reference; quantile regression for AL; or alternative probabilistic models such as Bayesian neural networks), to contextualize absolute performance and uncertainty quality.
- Unclear explanation in Section 3.2: The first paragraph claims NLL and NLPD “follow similar trends” for fr-en and en-de but not for en-es, yet reports “drastic” improvements with warping on all datasets. The discrepancy and the specific “trend” are not clearly articulated. If the point is that NLL indicates overfitting on small data (en-es), this should be explicitly evidenced (e.g., train vs test likelihoods).
- Interpretability of NLPD/NLL: The paper highlights that MAE/Pearson’s r can be misleading, but it is still difficult for practitioners to interpret the magnitude of NLPD/NLL improvements. The work would benefit from calibration diagnostics (e.g., probability integral transform histograms, coverage of posterior intervals) and/or qualitative examples where two models have similar MAE but distinct predictive distributions leading to different decisions.
- Target choice not justified: The choice of post‑editing rate instead of more standard QE targets such as (H)TER is not discussed. A brief justification (e.g., alignment with the decision scenarios; distributional properties favoring warping) would help readers situate the contribution relative to prior QE work.
- Asymmetric loss analysis incomplete: The observation that Warped GPs underperform standard GPs under pessimistic linex seems important but is only hypothesized (variance-driven effect). This section would be stronger with a deeper analysis: variance comparisons, sensitivity to w, or discussion of whether linex is inherently biased toward higher-variance models. Including a quantile regression baseline for AL would also clarify the benefits of probabilistic posteriors versus direct asymmetric training.

Questions to authors
- Why were post-editing rates chosen over (H)TER as the response variable? How do you expect the conclusions to transfer to (H)TER?
- In Section 3.2, please clarify the precise mismatch between NLL and NLPD on en-es. Can you provide training vs test likelihoods or additional evidence of overfitting?
- Can you include calibration diagnostics (e.g., interval coverage, PIT histograms) to substantiate claims of “well-calibrated” uncertainty?
- For the asymmetric scenarios, could you report sensitivity analyses across more w values, and provide intuition/examples where posteriors change the decision relative to medians?
- Which GP implementation/toolkit was used? Are code and hyperparameters (including warping terms) available for reproduction?

Additional comments (minor)
- Section 4: “over and underestimates” → “over- and underestimates.”
- Figure 1 caption: Lines are blue and green, not blue and red.
- Please state the GP software library used, and any non-default settings.
- Consider reporting coverage of, e.g., 50% and 90% predictive intervals alongside NLPD to aid interpretability.
- If feasible, include a small qualitative case study showing two segments where models have similar MAE but different posterior spreads, and how AL/linex estimators would act differently.

Overall recommendation
This is a well-written and timely paper that effectively advocates for uncertainty-aware evaluation and decision-making in QE. While the modeling novelty is limited, the study is careful, the scenarios are realistic, and the conclusions are useful to the community. Addressing the clarifications above, adding calibration diagnostics, and situating results with broader baselines would substantially strengthen the paper.
