This paper explores the use of probability models (Gaussian processes) to regress on the target variable of post-editing time/rate to determine the quality of MT output.

The paper has a well-structured introduction that highlights the problem of QE point estimation in real-world applications. I particularly liked the different asymmetric risk scenarios and how they involve different estimators.

For those familiar with GP, the paper spends quite a bit of space reflecting them, but I think it's worth trying to present these concepts to the reader.

The GP method and the choice of kernels and warping are very clearly explained and easy to follow. In general, the research questions addressed by this paper are interesting and well-formulated.

However, I have some questions/suggestions regarding the results of the intrinsic uncertainty assessment and the discussion section:

- Why were the post-editing rates chosen over the predicted (H)TER? TER is a common standard for predicting in QE research, and it would have been good to justify the choice made in the paper.
- Section 3.2: I don't understand the first paragraph at all: what exactly are the trends you see for fr-en and en-de that you don't see for en-es? NLL and NLPD 'drastically' decrease with warped GP for the three datasets.
- The paper actually says that it doesn't want to push the state-of-the-art (since they only use the standard 17 baseline features), but it would have been good to show another point estimate model from existing work in the results table to get an idea of the overall quality of the models.
- Related to this, the NLL and NLPD values are difficult to interpret, so one might always be tempted to look at the MAE in the table to see 'how different the predictions are'. Since the main purpose of the paper is that this shouldn't be done, what makes the drastic reduction of NLL/NLPD worthwhile: providing a qualitative analysis with actual examples would be great.

Chapter 4 is very well written and explains the results very intuitively!

Overall, I like the paper because it highlights the problematic use of point estimation in QE. Generally a difficult task where additional information such as confidence is probably very important. This submission doesn't push the state-of-the-art and doesn't offer much novelty in the field of modeling (since GP has been used before), but its research questions and goals are clearly stated and beautifully executed.

Small problems:

- Section 4: "over and underestimates" -> "over- and underestimates"
- Caption of Figure 1: The lines are actually blue and green, not blue and red as stated in the caption.
- If a specific toolkit was used for GP modeling, it would be good to mention this in the final paper.
