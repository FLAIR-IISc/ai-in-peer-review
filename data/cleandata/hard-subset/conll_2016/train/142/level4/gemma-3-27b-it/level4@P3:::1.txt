## Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

This paper explores the application of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), to machine translation quality estimation (QE), focusing on predicting post-editing time/rates. The central argument is that traditional point-estimate metrics are insufficient for real-world QE applications and that incorporating uncertainty estimates can significantly improve decision-making, particularly in asymmetric risk scenarios.

The primary contribution of this work lies in its comprehensive evaluation of probabilistic QE models – beyond simple point estimates – utilizing metrics that account for the full posterior predictive distributions (specifically, Negative Log Predictive Density - NLPD). The paper convincingly demonstrates that differences in uncertainty modeling are not captured by standard point estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson’s r.  A secondary, valuable contribution is the clear articulation and exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios relevant to QE (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic post-editing workflows) and the derivation of appropriate Bayesian risk estimators for these scenarios. Finally, the thorough exploration and comparison of different GP configurations (kernels and warping functions) offers a useful resource for researchers interested in applying these techniques to QE.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance and Motivation:** The paper addresses a crucial limitation of current QE systems: the lack of uncertainty information.  The introduction effectively motivates the need for probabilistic QE by outlining practical scenarios where point estimates alone are inadequate, such as post-editing workflows. This is a compelling argument for the work’s relevance.
2. **Clear and Thorough Explanation of Methods:** The authors provide a well-structured and accessible explanation of Gaussian Processes, including details on kernel choices (Matèrn kernels) and warping functions.  Even for readers familiar with GPs, the clear articulation of these concepts within the QE context is beneficial. The detailed description of the asymmetric loss functions and their connection to Bayes risk estimation is particularly strong.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation with Appropriate Metrics:**  The use of NLPD, alongside traditional metrics, is a key strength. The authors correctly identify NLPD as a more suitable metric for evaluating probabilistic models and demonstrating the impact of different uncertainty modeling choices. The exploration of asymmetric loss functions and their impact on predictions further solidifies the evaluation’s rigor.
4. **Well-Defined Experimental Setup:** The experimental setup, utilizing standard datasets and features, allows for a fair comparison of different models. The 10-fold cross-validation provides a robust assessment of model performance. The choice to focus on post-editing *rates* rather than HTER is justified and clearly stated. 
5. **Intuitive Presentation of Results:** Section 4 is particularly well-written, offering intuitive explanations of the results in the context of asymmetric risk scenarios. The discussion of the linex loss results and the potential for GP models to sometimes produce overly confident predictions demonstrates critical thinking and a nuanced understanding of the limitations.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Modeling:** While the application of GPs to QE is not novel, the primary contribution is the thorough evaluation and analysis of existing techniques. The paper doesn’t present a fundamentally new modeling approach. This is acknowledged by the authors, but should be highlighted.
2. **Difficulty in Interpreting Metric Improvements:** While the authors argue against relying on point estimate metrics, the absolute values of NLPD and NLL can be difficult to interpret without a clear benchmark for what constitutes a “drastic” reduction.  Providing some qualitative examples of prediction distributions (e.g., visualizing the predictive distributions for specific sentences) alongside the quantitative results would help readers understand the practical implications of the observed improvements.
3. **Limited Comparison to Strong Baselines:** The paper deliberately avoids striving for state-of-the-art performance and uses a limited feature set.  However, including a more competitive point-estimate QE model (using, for example, more sophisticated features) in the results tables would provide a useful context for evaluating the performance of the probabilistic models, even if the primary focus isn't on achieving SOTA results.
4. **Potential for Overinterpretation of Results:** The analysis of the linex loss results in Section 4.3 feels somewhat speculative.  The conclusion that GPs may not always be optimal for pessimistic scenarios needs further justification and exploration.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a few example sentences demonstrating the differences in predictive distributions between the standard GP and the warped GP models? This would help to illustrate the practical impact of the improved uncertainty modeling.
2.  The paper mentions using a toolkit for GP modeling. Could you please specify which toolkit was used for reproducibility purposes?
3.  In Section 4.3, you suggest that the linex loss might favor models with higher variance. Could you elaborate on this idea and potentially explore alternative loss functions that might address this issue?

**Additional Comments:**

* Section 4: “over and underestimates” should be “over- and underestimates.”
* Figure 1 caption: The lines are actually blue and green, not blue and red.
* Consider adding a brief discussion of computational cost associated with the GP models, particularly as the dataset size increases.



Overall, this is a solid and well-written paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of QE. While it doesn't present a radical new modeling approach, the thorough evaluation of probabilistic models and the exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios provide important insights for researchers and practitioners alike. The identified weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed through revisions and clarifications. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
