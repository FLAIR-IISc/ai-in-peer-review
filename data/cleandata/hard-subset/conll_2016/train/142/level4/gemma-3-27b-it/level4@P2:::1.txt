This paper investigates probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on predicting post-editing time/rates rather than traditional metrics like (H)TER. The core argument is that relying solely on point estimates in QE is insufficient for real-world applications, and incorporating uncertainty estimates is crucial for decision-making, particularly in scenarios with asymmetric risk. The authors clearly articulate this problem and explore how well-calibrated uncertainty, provided by GPs with different kernel and warping function configurations, can be leveraged.

The paper’s strengths lie in its clear structure and thorough explanation of the methodology. The introduction effectively motivates the need for probabilistic QE, and the detailed description of GPs, while potentially familiar to some readers, is valuable for context and accessibility. The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios and their corresponding estimators is a particularly strong aspect, providing a practical application for the developed probabilistic models. The research questions are well-defined and addressed logically throughout the paper.

The primary contribution of this work is a compelling argument for the necessity of evaluating QE models beyond point-estimate metrics, coupled with a practical demonstration using GPs. While the use of GPs in QE isn’t novel, the paper’s emphasis on *calibrated* uncertainty estimation and its application to asymmetric risk scenarios offers a valuable perspective. A secondary contribution lies in the empirical evaluation of different GP configurations (kernels, warping functions) using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric, highlighting its usefulness in capturing uncertainty characteristics that are missed by standard metrics like MAE and Pearson’s r.

However, several points require clarification and could strengthen the paper. 

Firstly, the rationale for using post-editing *rates* rather than the more common (H)TER as the target variable is not sufficiently explained. Justifying this choice would enhance the paper’s clarity.

Secondly, the discussion in Section 3.2 regarding the observed trends in NLL and NLPD is unclear. The statement about differing trends between datasets needs to be more precisely articulated. While NLL and NLPD generally decrease with warped GPs, specifying *what* constitutes a “drastic” decrease and providing a clearer comparison across datasets would be beneficial. 

Thirdly, while the authors intentionally avoid striving for state-of-the-art results, including a comparison to a representative point-estimate model from existing QE literature would provide valuable context for interpreting the observed NLL and NLPD improvements. This would help readers assess the practical significance of the presented results.

Finally, the paper would benefit from a qualitative analysis illustrating the impact of the observed NLL/NLPD reductions. Providing concrete examples of predictions with varying uncertainty levels and their potential implications in a post-editing scenario would help readers appreciate the value of probabilistic QE.

In summary, this paper presents a well-reasoned and clearly articulated argument for embracing probabilistic modeling in QE. Despite lacking significant modeling novelty, its focus on uncertainty calibration and asymmetric risk scenarios makes it a valuable contribution to the field. Addressing the points raised above would further enhance the paper’s clarity and impact.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please provide a justification for the choice of post-editing rates over (H)TER as the target variable.
2.  Could you elaborate on the specific trends observed in Section 3.2 regarding NLL and NLPD, providing a more precise comparison between the datasets?
3.  Would you consider including a comparison to a representative point-estimate QE model from existing literature to provide context for the reported NLL and NLPD values?
4.  Could you provide qualitative examples demonstrating how the observed improvements in NLL/NLPD translate into tangible benefits in a post-editing scenario?
