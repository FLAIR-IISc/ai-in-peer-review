This paper investigates the application of Gaussian Processes (GPs) to model post-editing time and rates, aiming to improve quality estimation (QE) by providing more informative uncertainty estimates than traditional point predictions. 

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. The introduction effectively highlights the limitations of relying solely on point estimates in real-world QE applications, and the discussion of asymmetric risk scenarios and their corresponding optimal estimators is particularly insightful. While the paper dedicates considerable space to reviewing GPs – potentially redundant for those familiar with the method – this thoroughness is justified by the need to make the work accessible to a broader audience. The explanation of the GP implementation, including kernel choices and the use of warping, is lucid and easy to follow. The research questions are interesting and well-defined.

I do, however, have some questions and suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion, specifically within the Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation section. First, the choice of post-editing rates as the target variable, rather than the more commonly predicted (H)TER, warrants further justification. While not necessarily incorrect, explaining this decision would strengthen the paper.

Second, the first paragraph of Section 3.2 is unclear. The described trend observed for fr-en and en-de, and its absence for en-es, requires clarification. Although NLL and NLPD demonstrably decrease with warped GPs across all three datasets – and are even described as ‘drastically’ decreasing – the specific contrast implied in the opening sentences is not readily apparent.

Third, the authors acknowledge that this work does not aim to advance the state-of-the-art, given the use of a standard feature set. Nevertheless, including results from a comparable point estimate model from existing QE literature would offer valuable context and facilitate a better understanding of the models’ overall performance. 

Finally, interpreting NLL and NLPD values can be challenging. Readers naturally gravitate towards MAE to gauge prediction differences, which the paper rightly cautions against. To reinforce the argument that point estimates are insufficient, providing a qualitative analysis demonstrating the practical implications of reductions in NLL/NLPD – perhaps with illustrative examples of predictions and their associated uncertainties – would be beneficial.

Section 4, which discusses the results, is exceptionally well-written and presents the findings in an intuitive manner. 

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the QE field. The paper effectively critiques the prevalent reliance on point estimates and emphasizes the importance of incorporating uncertainty information, a crucial aspect of QE in practical scenarios. While the modeling approach itself isn’t novel – GPs have been previously applied in this domain – the research questions are clearly articulated and rigorously addressed. 

Minor points to address:

*   Section 4: Change "over and underestimates" to "over- and underestimates."
*   Figure 1 caption: Correct the color description from “blue and red” to “blue and green.”
*   Consider mentioning the specific toolkit used for GP modeling to enhance reproducibility.
