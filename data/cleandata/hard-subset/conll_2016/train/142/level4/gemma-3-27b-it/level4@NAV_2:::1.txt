The paper explores the use of probabilistic models (Gaussian processes) to regress on the target variable of post-editing time/rates for quality estimation of MT output.

The paper is well structured with a clear introduction that highlights the problem of QE point estimates in real-world applications. I especially liked the description of the different asymmetric risk scenarios and how they entail different estimators. 

For readers familiar with GPs the paper spends considerable space revisiting them, but I think this is worthwhile to ensure accessibility and introduce these concepts to the reader. 

The GP approach and the choices for kernels and using warping are explained very clearly and are easy to follow. In general, the research questions addressed by this paper are interesting and well-phrased.

However, I do have some questions/suggestions about the Results and Discussion sections for Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation:

- Why were post-editing rates chosen over prediction (H)TER? TER is a common value to predict in QE research and it would have been nice to justify this choice.
- Section 3.2: The first paragraph is unclear: What exactly is the trend observed for fr-en & en-de that differs from en-es? NLL and NLPD decrease with warped GPs for all three datasets.
- The paper states it does not aim to advance state-of-the-art (given the use of only the standard 17 baseline features), but including another point estimate model from existing work in the result tables would provide context for the overall quality of the models.
- Related to this, NLL and NLPD values are difficult to interpret; providing a qualitative analysis with actual examples to illustrate what constitutes a substantial reduction in NLL/NLPD would be helpful, especially given the paper's argument against relying on point estimates like MAE.
Section 4 is very nicely written and explains results intuitively!

Overall, I like the paper because it highlights the problematic use of point estimates in QE. This is a challenging task where additional information, such as confidence, is arguably very important. The submission does not advance state-of-the-art and does not present significant modeling novelty (since GPs have been used previously), but its research questions and goals are clearly stated and well-executed.

Minor problems:

- Section 4: “over and underestimates” should be “over- and underestimates.”
- Figure 1 caption: Lines are actually blue and green, not blue and red as stated.
- If a specific toolkit was used for GP modeling, please refer to it in the final paper.
