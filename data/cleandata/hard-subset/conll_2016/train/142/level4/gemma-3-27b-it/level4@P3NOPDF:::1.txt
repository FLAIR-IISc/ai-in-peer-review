This paper explores the application of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), to the task of quality estimation (QE) by regressing on post-editing time/rates. The work is well-structured, beginning with a clear introduction that effectively highlights the limitations of relying on point estimates in real-world QE applications. I particularly appreciated the nuanced description of asymmetric risk scenarios and how these different scenarios necessitate distinct estimators. 

While readers already familiar with GPs may find the initial exposition somewhat lengthy, I believe the thorough review of these concepts is justified and valuable for accessibility. The GP approach, including the rationale behind kernel choices and the implementation of warping, is explained in a clear and easily understandable manner. More broadly, the research questions posed by the paper are both interesting and well-defined.

However, I have several questions and suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion sections, specifically concerning the Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation.

First, the choice of post-editing rates as the target variable, rather than (H)TER, warrants further justification. Predicting TER is a common practice within the QE research community, and the paper would benefit from a clear explanation of the rationale behind prioritizing post-editing rates.

Second, the first paragraph of Section 3.2 is unclear. The described trend for fr-en and en-de, which is seemingly absent for en-es, is not readily apparent. While the paper notes that NLL and NLPD decrease “drastically” with warped GPs across all three datasets, this claim requires more substantiation. The quantitative improvement should be contextualized for better understanding.

Third, the authors acknowledge that this work does not aim to advance the state-of-the-art, given the use of only the standard 17 baseline features. Nevertheless, including results from another point-estimate model from recent QE literature would provide a useful benchmark and allow readers to better assess the overall quality of the proposed models. 

Finally, interpreting NLL and NLPD values can be challenging. While the paper rightfully argues against relying solely on metrics like MAE, which focus on point predictions, readers will inevitably gravitate towards these values for a sense of the predictive differences. To strengthen this argument, it would be beneficial to provide a qualitative analysis, illustrating what constitutes a “drastic” reduction in NLL/NLPD with concrete examples. Demonstrating this through real-world scenarios would significantly enhance the impact of the paper’s central claim.

Section 4 is exceptionally well-written, presenting the results in an intuitive and readily digestible manner.

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the field of QE. The paper effectively points out the problematic reliance on point estimates and highlights the importance of incorporating uncertainty information, a crucial consideration for practical applications. Although the submission does not represent a significant advancement in modeling techniques—GPs have been employed in related areas previously—its research questions are clearly articulated, and the execution is well-considered.

I have identified a few minor issues:

*   In Section 4, “over and underestimates” should be corrected to “over- and underestimates”.
*   The caption for Figure 1 incorrectly states that the lines are blue and red; they are actually blue and green.
*   Specifying the toolkit used for GP modeling would improve reproducibility and provide a helpful resource for other researchers.
