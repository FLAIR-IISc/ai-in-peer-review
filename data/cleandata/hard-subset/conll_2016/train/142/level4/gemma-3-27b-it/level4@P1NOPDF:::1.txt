The paper explores the use of probabilistic models – specifically Gaussian processes – to regress on post-editing time/rates for quality estimation (QE) of machine translation output.

The paper is well structured, with a clear introduction that effectively highlights the limitations of point estimates in real-world QE applications. I particularly appreciated the description of different asymmetric risk scenarios and how these necessitate different estimators. 

While readers familiar with Gaussian processes may find the background material somewhat extensive, I believe it is a worthwhile investment to make the concepts accessible to a broader audience. The GP approach, including the choices for kernels and the use of warping, is explained very clearly and is easy to follow. In general, the research questions addressed by this paper are interesting and well-formulated.

However, I have some questions and suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion sections, specifically concerning the Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation:

*   Why were post-editing rates chosen as the target variable over (H)TER? TER is a commonly predicted metric in QE research, and the paper would benefit from a justification for this alternative choice.
*   Section 3.2: The first paragraph is unclear. What specific trend is observed for fr-en and en-de that is not present for en-es? NLL and NLPD demonstrably decrease with warped GPs across all three datasets, so the claim of a drastic decrease requires clarification.
*   The paper acknowledges that it does not aim to advance state-of-the-art results (given the use of only standard 17 baseline features). However, including a point estimate model from existing work in the result tables would provide valuable context for assessing the overall quality of the presented models.
*   Related to this, NLL and NLPD values can be difficult to interpret. While the paper rightly argues against relying solely on MAE, readers will naturally gravitate towards it for gauging the magnitude of prediction differences. To strengthen this argument, it would be helpful to provide a qualitative analysis illustrating what constitutes a ‘drastic’ reduction in NLL/NLPD, perhaps with specific examples.

Section 4 is very well written and explains the results in an intuitive manner.

Overall, I find this paper to be a valuable contribution, as it rightly points out the problematic reliance on point estimates in QE. This is a particularly important consideration in a field where information about confidence and uncertainty is crucial. While the submission does not represent a significant advance in state-of-the-art performance or modeling novelty (given the existing use of GPs), its research questions are clearly stated and effectively addressed.

Minor issues:

*   Section 4: “over and underestimates” should be “over- and underestimates”.
*   Figure 1 caption: The lines are actually blue and green, not blue and red as stated.
*   Mentioning the specific toolkit used for GP modeling would be beneficial in the final version of the paper.
