This paper determines the quality of MT output using a probability model (Gaussian process) to regress on the target variable of post-editing time/rate.

The paper has a well-structured introduction that highlights the problem of QE point estimation in real-world applications. I particularly liked the different asymmetric risk scenarios and how they involve different estimators.

For those familiar with GP, the paper spends quite a bit of space reflecting them, but I think it's worth trying to present these concepts to the reader.

The GP method and the choice of kernel and warping are very clearly explained and easy to follow. In general, the research questions addressed by this paper are interesting and well-formulated.

However, I have some questions/suggestions regarding the results of the underlying uncertainty assessment and discussion section:

- Why were the post-editing rates chosen over the predicted (H) rate? Rate is a common standard for prediction in QE research, and it would have been good to justify the choice made in the paper.
- Section 3.2: I don't understand the first paragraph at all: What exactly are the trends you see for fr-en and en-de that you don't see for en-es? NLL and NLPD 'drastically' decrease with warped GP for all three datasets.
- The paper actually says it doesn't want to push the state-of-the-art (since they only use the standard 17 baseline features), but it would have been good to show another point estimation model from existing work in the results table to get an idea of the overall quality of the models.
- Related to this, the NLL and NLPD values are difficult to interpret, so it's always tempting to look at the MAE in the table to see 'how different the predictions are'. Since the main purpose of the paper is that it shouldn't be done, what makes the drastic reduction of NLL/NLPD valuable: providing a qualitative analysis with real examples would be great.

Chapter 4 is very well written and explains the results very intuitively!

Overall, I like the paper because it highlights the problematic use of point estimation in QE. Usually a difficult task where additional information such as confidence is probably very important. This submission doesn't push the state-of-the-art and doesn't offer much novelty in the field of modeling (since GP has been used before), but its research questions and goals are clearly stated and beautifully executed.

Small problems:

- Section 4: "Over and UnderEstimates" -> "Over- and UnderEstimates"
- Caption of Figure 1: The lines are actually blue and green, the caption says blue and red.
- If a specific toolkit had been used for GP modeling, it would have been good to mention it in the final paper.
