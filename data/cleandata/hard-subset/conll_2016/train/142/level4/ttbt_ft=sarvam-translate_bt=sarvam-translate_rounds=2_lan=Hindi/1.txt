This research paper explores the use of a probability model (Gaussian process) to regress the target variable of post-editing time/rates for MT output quality estimation.

The paper is well-structured and has a clear introduction that highlights the problem of QE point estimation in real-world applications. I particularly liked the description of various asymmetric risk scenarios and how it incorporates different estimators.

For readers familiar with GP, this paper provides enough space to illustrate them, but I think it's worthwhile to try to familiarize the reader with these concepts.

The GP approach and the use of kernels, options, and warping are explained very clearly and are easy to understand. Generally, the research questions addressed by this paper are interesting and well-stated.

However, I have some questions/suggestions about the results and discussion sections for internal uncertainty estimation:

- Why were post-editing rates chosen at (H)TER? TER is a common value to be predicted for QE research, and it would have been good to justify the choice made in the paper.
- Section 3.2: I didn't understand the first paragraph at all: What trend do you see for fr-en and en-de that you don't see for en-es? All three datasets, NLL and NLPD, decrease with 'sufficient' deformed GP.
- The paper actually says it doesn't want to push cutting-edge technology (given that they only use the standard 17 basic features), but it would have been good to show another point estimation model from existing work in the results tables, so that the overall quality of the model could be estimated.
- Relatedly, it's difficult to interpret the NLL and NLPD values, so one is always tempted to look at the MAE in the tables to understand 'how different the predictions are'. Since the whole purpose of the paper is to say that doing so is not right, it would be great to explain what the 'sufficient' reduction in NLL/NLPD is: a qualitative analysis with real examples.

Section 4 is very well written and explains the results very intuitively!

Overall, I liked this paper because it points out the problematic use of point estimation in QE. Generally a difficult task where additional information such as confidence is arguably very important. The presentation does not push cutting-edge technology and does not offer much novelty in terms of modeling (as GP has been used before), but its research questions and goals are clearly stated and well-executed.

Minor problems:

- Section 4: "Over and Underestimates" -> "Over- and Underestimates"
- Figure 1 Caption: The lines are actually blue and green, not blue and red as stated in the caption.
- If a specific toolkit was used for GP modeling, it would have been great to mention it in the final paper.
