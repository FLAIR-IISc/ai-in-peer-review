

**Summary and Contributions**  
This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate approaches are insufficient for real-world translation workflows where uncertainty matters. The authors focus on Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a framework for generating well-calibrated uncertainty estimates, extending the GP literature with Matèrn kernels (for handling noisy data) and Warped GPs (for modeling strictly positive quality scores). The main contributions, ordered by strength, are: (1) demonstrating that NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) is a more appropriate evaluation metric than point-estimate metrics (e.g., MAE) for capturing uncertainty differences, (2) showing how predictive uncertainty enables better decision-making in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., post-editing vs. gisting), and (3) providing empirical evidence that Warped GPs with Matèrn kernels improve uncertainty calibration over standard GPs.

**Strengths**  
1. **Compelling motivation for uncertainty in QE**: The paper effectively argues that point estimates alone are inadequate for real-world translation workflows. The asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic) are well-motivated and directly address practical translation challenges, such as balancing post-editor workload and translation quality. The demonstration of how predictive uncertainty can guide filtering decisions (e.g., only forwarding high-confidence segments for post-editing) is particularly strong.

2. **Clear demonstration of NLPD's superiority**: The paper convincingly shows that traditional metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) fail to distinguish between models with different uncertainty profiles, while NLPD captures these differences. For example, in Table 1, Warped GPs with tanh-based warping improve NLPD by 15-20% over standard GPs on fr-en and en-de, but MAE shows only marginal gains. This is a critical contribution, as it highlights a fundamental flaw in current QE evaluation practices.

3. **Practical application in asymmetric risk**: The paper successfully demonstrates how probabilistic models can be leveraged for decision-making under asymmetric loss (e.g., penalizing underestimates more heavily in pessimistic scenarios). The results in Table 2 show consistent improvements with Warped GPs in optimistic settings (e.g., 5-10% better AL loss with w=1/3), which directly supports the paper’s thesis that uncertainty estimates enable better workflow optimization.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Lack of justification for post-editing rates vs. standard QE metrics**: The authors use normalized post-editing rates as the target variable but do not justify why this is preferable to standard QE metrics like TER or BLEU. This omission makes it difficult to contextualize their results against prior work, which predominantly uses TER. A brief comparison would strengthen the paper’s relevance.

2. **Unclear explanation of dataset-specific trends**: Section 3.2 claims that NLL and NLPD trends differ between the en-es dataset (smaller, n=858) and the larger fr-en/en-de datasets, but the explanation is opaque. The authors note that NLL is unreliable for small datasets due to overfitting, yet the text states that "NLL and NLPD follow similar trends" for fr-en/en-de. This contradiction needs clarification to support their argument about NLPD’s reliability.

3. **Insufficient baseline comparison**: While the authors rightly state they do not aim to advance state-of-the-art, the absence of a strong point-estimate baseline (e.g., SVR from WMT shared tasks) in the results tables makes it hard to gauge the practical significance of their probabilistic improvements. Including even one high-performing point-estimate model would provide essential context for readers.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. Could the authors clarify why post-editing rates were chosen over standard QE metrics (e.g., TER) and how this choice affects the generalizability of their findings?  
2. In Section 3.2, the paper states that "NLL and NLPD follow similar trends" for fr-en/en-de but not en-es. Given that the en-es dataset is smaller, could the authors elaborate on why NLL is unreliable here and how this impacts the interpretation of NLPD as a general metric?  
3. To help readers interpret the NLPD improvements, could the authors provide qualitative examples showing how Warped GPs' uncertainty estimates lead to better filtering decisions in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., a specific en-es segment where the model’s low uncertainty prevented a poor translation from being post-edited)?
