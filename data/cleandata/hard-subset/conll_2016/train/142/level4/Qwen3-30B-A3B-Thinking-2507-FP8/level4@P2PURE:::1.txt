

The paper investigates the application of probabilistic models (Gaussian processes) to regress on the target variable of post-editing time/rates for quality estimation of machine translation output.  

The paper is well-structured, with a clear introduction that effectively highlights the limitations of point estimates in quality estimation (QE) for real-world applications. The description of asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications for estimator selection is particularly strong. While the paper dedicates substantial space to introducing Gaussian processes (GPs) for readers unfamiliar with the technique, this effort is justified and enhances accessibility.  

The GP approach, kernel choices, and use of warping are explained clearly and coherently. Overall, the research questions are well-defined and compelling.  

However, the Results and Discussion section for Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation raises several points for clarification:  
- The choice of post-editing rates over prediction (H)TER warrants justification, as TER is a standard target in QE research.  
- Section 3.2’s first paragraph is unclear: the described trend for fr-en and en-de (which differs from en-es) is not evident, as NLL and NLPD values "drastically" decrease with warped GPs across all three datasets.  
- Although the paper explicitly states it does not aim to advance the state-of-the-art (using only standard 17 baseline features), including a point-estimate model from prior work in the results tables would contextualize the overall model performance.  
- The difficulty in interpreting NLL and NLPD values necessitates relying on MAE for intuition—a practice the paper argues against. To reinforce this, a qualitative analysis with concrete examples demonstrating the significance of drastic NLL/NLPD reductions would be valuable.  

Section 4 is exceptionally well-written and intuitively explains the results.  

Overall, the paper makes a meaningful contribution by highlighting the pitfalls of point estimates in QE, a critical consideration where confidence information is essential. While it does not advance the state-of-the-art or introduce novel modeling (as GPs have been applied previously), the research questions are clearly articulated and effectively addressed.  

Minor issues:  
- Section 4: "over and underestimates" → "over- and underestimates"  
- Figure 1 caption: Lines are blue and green, not blue and red as stated.  
- If a specific GP modeling toolkit was used, it should be referenced in the final paper.
