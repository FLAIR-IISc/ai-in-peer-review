

Summary: The paper investigates probabilistic models for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs) to provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The primary contributions are: (1) demonstrating that traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling, while the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric effectively evaluates probabilistic models; (2) showing how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios common in translation workflows; and (3) proposing model extensions (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) that better suit the noisy nature of QE datasets.

Strengths:
1. The paper compellingly argues that point estimate metrics are inadequate for QE evaluation in real-world decision-making contexts, effectively demonstrating this through their analysis of NLPD versus traditional metrics across multiple datasets.
2. The paper provides a clear and well-executed demonstration of how predictive uncertainty can be practically applied in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic/optimistic settings), showing tangible benefits of probabilistic models in these contexts.
3. The evaluation methodology is sound, using NLPD as the primary metric to assess uncertainty calibration, which aligns with established practices in probabilistic modeling literature.
4. The explanation of GP variants (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) and their relevance to QE is thorough and accessible, making the technical content approachable for the broader NLP community.

Weaknesses:
1. The choice of post-editing rates as the target variable lacks sufficient justification, particularly given that (H)TER is a standard metric in QE research, making it difficult to assess the practical relevance of the results.
2. The presentation of results in Section 3.2 is confusing, especially regarding the dataset-specific trends in NLL/NLPD behavior, which undermines the paper's main argument about the superiority of NLPD as an evaluation metric.
3. The paper does not provide adequate context for the NLPD values by comparing against established point estimate models, making it challenging to interpret the magnitude of the improvements claimed.
4. The paper fails to provide qualitative examples demonstrating what constitutes "drastic" improvements in NLPD, which would help readers understand the practical significance of the reported metric differences.

Questions to Authors:
1. Please justify the choice of post-editing rates over standard QE metrics like (H)TER, and explain how your approach generalizes to other quality estimation tasks.
2. Clarify the dataset-specific trends in NLL/NLPD behavior (particularly the en-es dataset anomaly) and explain why these trends matter for the paper's main argument.
3. Provide qualitative examples showing how the uncertainty estimates from your models would change decision-making in practical translation workflows.
4. Include a comparison of your models' point estimate performance against at least one strong baseline from recent QE literature to contextualize your results.
