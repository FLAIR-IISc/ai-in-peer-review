

The paper investigates probabilistic regression using Gaussian processes (GPs) to model post-editing time/rate for quality estimation (QE) of machine translation output. The work is well-structured, with a clear introduction that effectively frames the limitations of point-estimate approaches in real-world QE applications. The discussion of asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications for estimator choice is particularly strong and well-executed.

For readers unfamiliar with GPs, the paper appropriately dedicates sufficient space to explaining foundational concepts, which is valuable given the technical nature of the approach. The GP methodology, kernel selection, and warping techniques are described clearly and logically. The research questions are compelling and well-articulated, addressing a meaningful gap in QE practice.

However, several concerns regarding the Results and Discussion (Section 3) require clarification:

1.  **Target Variable Justification:** The choice of post-editing rates over standard QE metrics like (H)TER is unexplained. Given TER's prevalence in QE literature, the authors should justify why post-editing rates were prioritized as the target variable, especially since the paper aims to inform practical QE deployment where TER is often the primary concern.

2.  **Section 3.2 Ambiguity:** The first paragraph of Section 3.2 is unclear. The authors state a trend is observed for fr-en and en-de but not en-es regarding NLL/NLPD with warped GPs. However, the subsequent sentence states NLL/NLPD "drastically decrease" with warped GPs for *all* three datasets. This contradiction needs resolution to clarify the reported trends.

3.  **Baseline Comparison:** While the paper rightly avoids claiming SOTA advancement (using only standard 17 features), the results lack a meaningful point-estimate baseline for context. Including a representative point-estimate model (e.g., from a recent SOTA QE paper) in the results tables would help readers gauge the absolute performance level of both point and probabilistic approaches.

4.  **Interpreting NLL/NLPD:** The paper's core argument hinges on avoiding MAE-based interpretation of uncertainty. Yet, the presented NLL/NLPD values are difficult to interpret without context. Providing qualitative examples demonstrating *what a "drastic" reduction in NLL/NLPD signifies* (e.g., showing specific translations where the probabilistic model's uncertainty estimate aligns with actual post-editing difficulty) would significantly strengthen the argument and aid reader comprehension.

Section 4 is exceptionally well-written, offering intuitive and clear explanations of the results, particularly regarding the interpretation of uncertainty.

Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution by highlighting the pitfalls of relying solely on point estimates in QE, where confidence information is crucial for practical decision-making. While the modeling approach (GPs) is not novel and the work does not advance the SOTA, the research questions are clearly defined, the methodology is soundly executed, and the critique of current QE practice is well-founded and important.

**Minor Corrections Needed:**
*   Section 4: "over and underestimates" → "over- and underestimates"
*   Figure 1 Caption: Correct "red" to "green" (lines are blue and green, not blue and red).
*   Mention the specific GP modeling toolkit used in the final paper for reproducibility.

This is a strong, insightful paper that effectively advocates for a more nuanced view of uncertainty in QE. Addressing the points above would significantly enhance its clarity and impact.
