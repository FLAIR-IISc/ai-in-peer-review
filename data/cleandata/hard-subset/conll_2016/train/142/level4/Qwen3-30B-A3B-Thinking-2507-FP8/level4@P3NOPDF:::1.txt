

The paper investigates the application of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for probabilistic regression of post-editing time/rates in machine translation quality estimation (QE), addressing a critical gap in the field. The introduction is well-crafted, clearly articulating the limitations of point estimates in real-world QE applications and thoughtfully framing asymmetric risk scenarios that necessitate different estimators. This conceptual foundation is a significant strength. The paper's explanation of GP fundamentals, kernel choices, and warping techniques is exceptionally clear and accessible, even for readers less familiar with the methodology—justifying the space devoted to these concepts. The research questions are compelling and precisely formulated.

The primary contribution lies in advocating for probabilistic predictions over point estimates in QE, a timely and important perspective. However, several aspects of the Results and Discussion require clarification or enhancement:

1.  **Metric Choice Justification:** The focus on post-editing rates rather than standard QE metrics like (H)TER warrants explicit justification. Given TER's prevalence in QE literature, the authors should clarify why post-editing rates were selected as the target variable and how they align with the paper's core argument about risk-sensitive estimation. Without this, the choice appears arbitrary to readers familiar with QE benchmarks.

2.  **Section 3.2 Interpretation:** The description of trends in Section 3.2 (e.g., "the trend you see for fr-en & en-de that you do not see for en-es") is confusing. The subsequent statement that "NLL and NLPD 'drastically' decrease with warped GPs for all three datasets" contradicts the initial claim of a dataset-specific trend. The authors must clarify the specific pattern they observe across datasets and reconcile this with the reported NLL/NLPD results. A concise table or figure illustrating the comparison would significantly improve clarity.

3.  **Contextualizing Model Performance:** While the paper rightly avoids claiming SOTA advancement (using only standard 17 features), the results tables lack a meaningful benchmark. Including a representative point-estimate model (e.g., a strong linear regression or SVR baseline from recent QE work) would provide essential context for interpreting the GP results. This would help readers assess whether the probabilistic approach offers a *meaningful* improvement over existing point-estimate methods, beyond the theoretical argument.

4.  **Interpreting NLL/NLPD:** The paper's central thesis—that relying on MAE (as is common) is misleading—creates a challenge in interpreting the reported NLL/NLPD values. The current tables present these metrics without a clear benchmark for what constitutes a "drastic" improvement. A brief qualitative analysis (e.g., showing example predictions where a significant NLL/NLPD drop correlates with meaningful confidence adjustments in post-editing scenarios) would powerfully reinforce the argument and make the numerical results more tangible.

Section 4 is a model of clear and intuitive exposition, effectively translating the technical results into practical insights about uncertainty. The paper's core contribution—highlighting the pitfalls of point estimates in risk-sensitive QE applications—is highly valuable and well-executed. While the modeling approach (GPs) is not novel in isolation, the paper's clear articulation of the *why* (addressing asymmetric risk) and the *how* (probabilistic regression for QE) provides significant conceptual clarity to the field. It makes a strong case for moving beyond simple point estimates, which is crucial for practical QE deployment.

**Minor Corrections:**
*   Section 4: "over and underestimates" → "over- and underestimates".
*   Figure 1 Caption: Correct "blue and red" to "blue and green".
*   Consider adding a sentence acknowledging the GP library/toolkit used (e.g., "GP regression was implemented using [Library Name]") in the methodology for reproducibility.

**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong, well-written paper making a vital contribution to the discourse on QE. It successfully shifts the focus from *what* to predict (point estimates) to *how* to predict (with appropriate uncertainty), addressing a genuine practical need. The arguments are logically sound, the methodology is clearly explained, and the results support the core thesis. While it does not present a novel model architecture or claim SOTA performance, it advances the field by clarifying a critical methodological issue and providing a clear, executable approach (GP regression) to address it. The suggested clarifications and contextualizations would further strengthen the paper's impact. I recommend acceptance.
