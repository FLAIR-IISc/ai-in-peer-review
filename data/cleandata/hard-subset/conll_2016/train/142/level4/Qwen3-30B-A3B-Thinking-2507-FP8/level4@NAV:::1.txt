

The paper examines the application of probabilistic models (Gaussian processes) to regress on post-editing time/rates as a target variable for quality estimation of machine translation output.  
The paper is well-organized with a clear introduction that effectively identifies the limitations of point estimates in quality estimation (QE) for real-world deployment. I particularly appreciate the detailed discussion of asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications for estimator selection.  
While the paper dedicates substantial space to explaining Gaussian processes (GPs) for readers unfamiliar with them, this thoroughness is justified and beneficial. The GP methodology, kernel choices, and warping technique are described with exceptional clarity and accessibility. Overall, the research questions are compelling and precisely articulated.  

However, I have the following suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion (Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation):  
- The choice of post-editing rates over prediction (H)TER warrants justification, as TER is a standard target in QE research.  
- Section 3.2: The initial paragraph is unclear—specifically, the trend observed for fr-en and en-de that is absent in en-es is not evident, as NLL and NLPD values "drastically" decrease with warped GPs across all three datasets.  
- Although the paper acknowledges it does not aim to advance state-of-the-art (using only standard 17 baseline features), including a point-estimate model from prior work in the results tables would contextualize the models' performance.  
- Given the paper's central argument against relying on MAE, the interpretation of NLL/NLPD values remains challenging. Providing qualitative examples to illustrate what constitutes a "drastic" reduction in these metrics would strengthen the discussion.  

Section 4 is exceptionally well-written and conveys results with remarkable clarity.  
Overall, I appreciate the paper's critical perspective on the pitfalls of point estimates in QE—a crucial consideration where confidence intervals are highly valuable. While the work does not advance state-of-the-art or introduce significant modeling novelty (as GPs have been previously applied), its research goals are clearly defined and effectively executed.  

Minor issues:  
- Section 4: "over and underestimates" → "over- and underestimates"  
- Figure 1 caption: Lines are blue and green, not blue and red as stated.  
- If a specific GP modeling toolkit was used, please cite it in the final paper.
