

The paper explores the use of probabilistic models (Gaussian processes) to regress on the target variable of post-editing time/rates for quality estimation of machine translation output.  

The paper is well structured, with a clear introduction that highlights the limitations of point estimates in quality estimation (QE) for real-world applications. I particularly appreciate the description of asymmetric risk scenarios and how they necessitate different estimators. For readers familiar with Gaussian processes (GPs), the paper dedicates significant space to explaining these concepts, which is worthwhile for broader accessibility.  

The GP approach, including kernel choices and warping, is explained clearly and coherently. Overall, the research questions are interesting and well-formulated.  

However, I have several questions and suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion sections (Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation):  
- Why were post-editing rates selected over prediction (H)TER? TER is a standard target in QE research, and the paper should justify this choice.  
- Section 3.2: The first paragraph is unclear. Specifically, what trend is observed for fr-en and en-de that is absent for en-es? The text states that NLL and NLPD "drastically" decrease with warped GPs across all three datasets, yet the trend description is confusing.  
- While the paper explicitly states it does not aim to advance state-of-the-art (using only standard 17 baseline features), it would be valuable to include an additional point-estimate model from existing work in the results tables to contextualize model quality.  
- Interpreting NLL and NLPD values is challenging, making readers default to MAE in the tables. Given the paper’s central argument that MAE is misleading, providing qualitative examples to illustrate meaningful reductions in NLL/NLPD would strengthen the discussion.  

Section 4 is exceptionally well written and intuitively explains the results.  

Overall, I appreciate the paper’s emphasis on the pitfalls of point estimates in QE—a critical issue where confidence information is highly valuable. While the submission does not advance state-of-the-art or offer significant modeling novelty (as GPs have been used previously), its research questions and execution are clear and well executed.  

Minor issues:  
- Section 4: "over and underestimates" → "over- and underestimates"  
- Figure 1 caption: The lines are blue and green, not blue and red as stated.  
- If a specific GP modeling toolkit was used, it should be cited in the final paper.
