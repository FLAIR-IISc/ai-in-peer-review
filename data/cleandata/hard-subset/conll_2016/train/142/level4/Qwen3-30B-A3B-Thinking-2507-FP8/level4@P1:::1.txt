

This paper explores the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), to provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that traditional QE models, which provide only point estimates, are insufficient for real-world translation workflows where uncertainty information can improve decision making. They propose evaluating models using metrics that capture the full predictive distribution (such as Negative Log Predictive Density, NLPD) rather than relying solely on point estimate metrics (like MAE). The paper also demonstrates how these uncertainty estimates can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios common in translation workflows.

The primary contributions are:
1. Demonstrating that NLPD is a more appropriate evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models than traditional point estimate metrics, as it captures differences in uncertainty modeling that point estimate metrics fail to detect.
2. Providing a clear framework for applying uncertainty estimates to asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic settings) in translation workflows.
3. Proposing and evaluating GP model extensions (Matèrn kernels and warped GPs) that better suit the characteristics of QE data.

Strengths:
1. The paper effectively articulates the limitations of current point-estimate-based QE evaluation and demonstrates why uncertainty estimates matter in practical translation scenarios. The asymmetric risk scenarios are particularly well-motivated and provide a compelling practical application for uncertainty information.
2. The evaluation using NLPD clearly shows that uncertainty modeling differences are not captured by traditional metrics, which is the paper's central thesis. The comparison between standard GPs and warped GPs across multiple datasets provides strong evidence for this claim.
3. The paper provides clear and accessible explanations of GP concepts and model extensions, making the technical content approachable for readers unfamiliar with these methods. The careful development of the warped GP framework is especially well executed.
4. The application to asymmetric risk scenarios is both theoretically sound and practically relevant, showing how uncertainty information can directly improve decision making in translation workflows.

Weaknesses:
1. The choice of post-editing rate as the target variable instead of more common QE metrics like (H)TER is not justified. Given that (H)TER is standard in QE research, this decision significantly impacts the paper's relevance to the field and warrants explanation.
2. The results in Section 3.2 are confusing regarding the trend differences between datasets. The claim that NLL and NLPD trends don't hold for en-es despite the "drastic decrease" with warped GPs across all datasets needs clarification. The paper should explain why the smaller en-es dataset behaves differently from the larger datasets.
3. The paper does not include comparisons to other point estimate models beyond the baseline 17 features, making it difficult to assess the overall quality of the models. Adding a comparison to a strong point estimate model (e.g., SVR) would help contextualize the performance of the probabilistic models.
4. The NLPD and NLL values are presented without contextualization, making it hard to interpret their practical significance. The authors should provide qualitative examples showing what constitutes a "drastic reduction" in these metrics, given that readers will naturally compare them to MAE values.

Questions to Authors:
1. Why was post-editing rate chosen as the target variable instead of (H)TER, which is more commonly used in QE research? How would the results differ if (H)TER were used?
2. In Section 3.2, the authors claim that "NLL and NLPD follow similar trends" for fr-en and en-de but not for en-es. However, the tables show that both NLL and NLPD decrease dramatically with warped GPs across all datasets. Can the authors clarify this discrepancy and explain why the trend appears different for the smaller en-es dataset?
3. Could the authors provide some qualitative examples demonstrating what a "drastic reduction" in NLPD/NLL actually means in terms of prediction quality? This would help readers better understand the practical significance of these metrics.

Minor issues:
- Section 4: "over and underestimates" should be hyphenated as "over- and underestimates"
- Figure 1 caption: The lines are blue and green, not blue and red as stated
- If a specific GP toolkit was used for implementation, the authors should reference it in the final paper

Overall, this is a well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE. While the work doesn't advance the state-of-the-art in terms of prediction accuracy, it clearly articulates the limitations of current evaluation practices and provides a compelling case for using probabilistic models with appropriate evaluation metrics. The paper's research questions and goals are clearly stated and well-executed, making it a useful contribution to the field.
