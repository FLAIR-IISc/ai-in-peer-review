

Summary: The paper investigates probabilistic approaches for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs) to provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates alongside point predictions. The primary contribution is demonstrating that Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) is a superior evaluation metric to standard point-estimate metrics (e.g., MAE, Pearson’s r) for capturing predictive uncertainty in QE. The secondary contribution is showing that warping functions (particularly parametric tanh-based) and Matérn kernels improve uncertainty modeling, especially for noisy QE data. The tertiary contribution is illustrating practical utility through asymmetric risk scenarios where uncertainty-aware predictions outperform point estimates.

Strengths:  
1. **Strong motivation for uncertainty in QE**: The paper compellingly argues that real-world translation workflows (e.g., post-editing) require uncertainty estimates to avoid costly errors from overconfident point predictions. The asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic/optimistic) are well-defined and demonstrate tangible decision-making benefits beyond theoretical novelty.  
2. **NLPD as a critical evaluation metric**: The paper effectively shows that standard metrics fail to distinguish between models with identical point-estimate performance but divergent uncertainty properties (e.g., overconfident vs. underconfident models). This is a significant contribution to QE evaluation methodology, as prior work largely relied on point-estimate metrics.  
3. **Clear empirical validation of uncertainty modeling**: The results robustly demonstrate that warped GPs (with tanh warping) consistently improve NLPD across datasets (fr-en, en-de), while Matérn kernels show promise for specific language pairs. The analysis of why NLL fails as a general metric (due to overfitting in small datasets like en-es) is insightful and directly addresses a key gap in the literature.

Weaknesses:  
1. **Unjustified choice of response variable**: The paper uses normalized post-editing rates instead of standard QE metrics like (H)TER, without justification. Since TER is the dominant target in QE research (e.g., WMT shared tasks), this choice weakens the paper’s relevance to the field. A comparison or rationale for this deviation is needed.  
2. **Insufficient interpretation of NLPD**: While NLPD is championed as the key metric, the paper provides no qualitative examples to contextualize "drastic" improvements in NLPD values (e.g., what does a 0.5 NLPD reduction mean for real-world predictions?). Without this, readers cannot assess the practical significance of the reported gains.  
3. **Lack of baseline for point-estimate context**: The paper correctly states it does not aim to advance point-estimate SOTA, but omitting a comparison to strong point-estimate baselines (e.g., SVR from WMT tasks) makes it difficult to gauge the relative impact of uncertainty modeling. Including a single baseline (e.g., MAE for SVR) would strengthen the argument that uncertainty is the key contribution.

Questions to Authors:  
1. Why were post-editing rates (normalized by sentence length) chosen as the response variable instead of standard QE metrics like (H)TER? How would results differ if TER were used, given its prevalence in QE literature?  
2. Can you provide qualitative examples showing how NLPD improvements (e.g., 0.2–0.5 reduction) translate to tangible decision-making benefits in asymmetric risk scenarios?  
3. How do the proposed warping functions (tanh vs. log) interact with different language pairs? For instance, why does log outperform tanh on the small en-es dataset, while tanh excels on larger datasets (fr-en, en-de)?  

Minor Comments:  
- Section 4: "over and underestimates" should be hyphenated as "over- and underestimates."  
- Figure 1 caption: Correct "red" to "green" to match line colors.  
- Mention the GP library/toolkit used (e.g., GPy, GPflow) for reproducibility.
