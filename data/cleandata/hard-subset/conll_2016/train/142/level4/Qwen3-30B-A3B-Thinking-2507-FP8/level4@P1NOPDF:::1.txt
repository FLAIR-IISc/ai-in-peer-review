

The paper investigates the application of Gaussian processes (GPs) for probabilistic regression of post-editing time/rate targets in machine translation quality estimation (QE). It is well-structured, with a clear introduction that effectively highlights the limitations of point-estimate approaches in real-world QE applications. I particularly appreciate the nuanced discussion of asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications for estimator selection.

For readers familiar with GPs, the paper appropriately dedicates space to explaining these concepts, which is valuable for broader accessibility. The GP methodology, kernel choices, and warping techniques are described clearly and logically. Overall, the research questions are well-motivated and precisely articulated.

However, several points require clarification in the Results and Discussion (Section 3):

1.  **Target Variable Justification:** The choice of post-editing rates over standard QE metrics like (H)TER is not justified. Given TER's prevalence in QE literature, the authors should explain why rates were selected as the primary target, especially since the paper's core argument concerns point estimates.
2.  **Section 3.2 Clarity:** The first paragraph of Section 3.2 is unclear. Specifically, it states a trend for fr-en and en-de datasets that is absent in en-es, yet the subsequent data shows NLL and NLPD "drastically" decreasing with warped GPs across all three datasets. The authors need to reconcile this discrepancy.
3.  **Model Comparison Context:** While the paper correctly notes it does not aim for SOTA (using only standard 17 features), including a representative point-estimate baseline from existing work would provide crucial context for interpreting the results. This would help readers gauge the relative performance of the GP approach.
4.  **Interpreting Uncertainty Metrics:** The paper rightly emphasizes that MAE is misleading for evaluating uncertainty (NLL/NLPD). However, the magnitude of "drastic" reductions in NLL/NLPD remains abstract. Providing qualitative examples demonstrating what a meaningful reduction entails (e.g., comparing prediction intervals for specific MT outputs with high vs. low uncertainty) would significantly strengthen the argument.

Section 4 is exceptionally well-written, offering intuitive explanations of the results. Overall, the paper makes a valuable contribution by highlighting the pitfalls of relying solely on point estimates in QE—a critical consideration where confidence information is highly relevant. While the modeling approach (GPs) isn't novel, and the work doesn't advance SOTA, the research questions are clearly defined, the execution is sound, and the paper effectively advances the discussion on uncertainty in QE.

**Minor Issues:**
*   Section 4: "over and underestimates" → "over- and underestimates"
*   Figure 1 caption: Lines are blue and green, not blue and red as stated.
*   If a specific GP toolkit was used (e.g., GPy, GPflow), please cite it in the final paper.
