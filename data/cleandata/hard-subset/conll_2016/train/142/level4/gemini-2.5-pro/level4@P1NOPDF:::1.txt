This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs), a probabilistic modeling approach, to predict post-editing time/effort for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE).

The paper is well-structured, with a clear introduction that effectively highlights the limitations of point estimates for QE in real-world applications. I particularly appreciated the discussion of various asymmetric risk scenarios and how they necessitate different types of estimators. While readers familiar with GPs may find the background section extensive, I believe the detailed introduction to the core concepts is a valuable contribution, making the work more accessible to a broader audience. The authors' specific GP approach, including their choices of kernels and the use of warping, is explained with excellent clarity and is easy to follow. The research questions driving the study are both interesting and well-articulated.

However, I have some questions and suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion, particularly for the Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation:

*   The choice of post-editing rates as the target variable is not fully justified. Given that (H)TER is a more common target in QE research, it would be beneficial for the authors to explain the rationale behind their decision.

*   In Section 3.2, I found the first paragraph's analysis of the results confusing. The authors claim to observe a trend for fr-en and en-de that is absent for en-es. However, based on the tables, the NLL and NLPD values appear to decrease drastically with warped GPs for all three language pairs. I would ask the authors to clarify this point.

*   The authors state that their goal is not to advance the state-of-the-art, and they use a standard set of 17 baseline features. However, to better contextualize the performance of their models, it would be very helpful to include results from a standard point-estimate baseline (e.g., from prior work) in the comparison tables.

*   A related point concerns the interpretability of the NLL and NLPD metrics. Since a core argument of the paper is to move beyond simple error metrics like MAE, it is crucial to help the reader understand the practical implications of the probabilistic metrics. What does a "drastic" reduction in NLL/NLPD signify? I strongly suggest adding a qualitative analysis with concrete examples from the data. This would powerfully illustrate how improved NLL/NLPD corresponds to more useful or reliable predictions in practice, reinforcing the paper's central thesis.

Overall, this is a valuable paper that successfully highlights the shortcomings of using point estimates in QE. The authors convincingly argue for the importance of additional information, such as prediction confidence, for this difficult task. While the submission does not claim to advance the state-of-the-art or introduce significant modeling novelty (as GPs have been used in this space before), its strength lies in its clearly stated research questions and its focused and well-executed investigation. The extrinsic evaluation in Section 4 is particularly well-written and intuitively explains the practical benefits of the proposed approach.

Minor Issues:
*   In Section 4, "over and underestimates" should be "over- and underestimates".
*   The caption for Figure 1 mistakenly refers to red and blue lines, but the lines in the figure are green and blue.
*   For reproducibility, please consider citing the specific software toolkit used for the GP modeling in the final version of the paper.
