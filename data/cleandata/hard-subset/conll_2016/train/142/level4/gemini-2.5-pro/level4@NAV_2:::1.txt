**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The central thesis is that traditional point-estimate predictions and corresponding evaluation metrics (like MAE) are insufficient for real-world applications, which could benefit greatly from well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors evaluate several GP model variants, including different kernels and warping functions, using a more appropriate probabilistic metric, Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD). Furthermore, the paper demonstrates how the predictive distributions from these models can be leveraged in practical asymmetric risk scenarios, such as pessimistic post-editing versus optimistic gisting workflows.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  A compelling demonstration of the value of probabilistic modeling and uncertainty-aware evaluation in QE. The paper successfully argues that standard point-estimate metrics can be misleading and advocates for the adoption of metrics like NLPD that evaluate the entire predictive distribution.
2.  A practical application of uncertainty estimates in QE through the framing of asymmetric risk scenarios. By applying Bayes risk estimators for different loss functions, the paper shows how a single probabilistic model can be adapted to different user needs (e.g., prioritizing precision vs. recall) without retraining.
3.  A thorough empirical comparison of different GP model configurations for QE, showing that extensions like Warped GPs can significantly improve the quality of predictive distributions compared to standard GP models.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Strong Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of point estimates in QE for decision-making in real-world translation workflows. The introduction provides an excellent motivation for why uncertainty is a critical, yet often overlooked, component of a useful QE system. This work addresses a significant gap in the current QE literature and practice.
2.  **Novel and Practical Application to Asymmetric Risk:** The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios in Section 4 is a key strength. This section provides a concrete, well-motivated, and intuitive example of how predictive distributions can be directly applied to serve different operational needs. This significantly strengthens the paper's argument about the practical utility of its proposed approach.
3.  **Clarity of Methodology and Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The technical concepts behind Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs are explained in an accessible manner, making the methodology easy to follow. The research questions are well-defined, and the experiments are designed logically to answer them.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Lack of Standard Non-Probabilistic Baselines:** The experiments compare different GP variants against each other but omit a comparison to a strong, standard point-estimate model like Support Vector Regression (SVR), which the paper itself identifies as a popular method in QE. Including such a baseline would allow readers to better contextualize the performance of the GP models on standard metrics (e.g., MAE) and understand any potential performance trade-offs for gaining uncertainty estimates.
2.  **Limited Interpretability of NLPD Results:** While NLPD is the correct metric for the paper's goals, its absolute values are difficult to interpret. The paper shows that certain models reduce NLPD but fails to provide a qualitative analysis or examples that would give the reader an intuitive sense of what these improvements mean. For example, showing a few predictions with their associated distributions for high- and low-NLPD models would greatly enhance the reader's understanding of the practical impact.
3.  **Unclear Analysis of Overfitting:** The analysis in the first paragraph of Section 3.2 is difficult to follow. The authors claim that for the en-es dataset, the divergence between NLL and NLPD trends is evidence of overfitting, but this is not apparent from the results in Table 1, where both metrics seem to improve consistently with the use of warping functions across all datasets. This central argument in the results section needs significant clarification to be convincing.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  Could you please clarify the reasoning in the first paragraph of Section 3.2? Specifically, what is the trend in NLL versus NLPD that you observe for the fr-en and en-de datasets that is not present for the en-es dataset, which leads you to the conclusion about NLL being an unreliable metric and evidence of overfitting on smaller datasets?
2.  The paper focuses on post-editing time as the target variable. Could you justify this choice over other common QE labels like HTER? A brief discussion on this would help position your results more broadly within the QE field.
3.  Given that your goal is to compare modeling approaches rather than achieve state-of-the-art results, would it be possible to include results for a standard SVR baseline using the same feature set? This would provide a valuable point of reference for the community and strengthen your experimental evaluation.
