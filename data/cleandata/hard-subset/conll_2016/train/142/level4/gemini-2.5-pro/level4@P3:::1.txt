### Summary of the Paper

This paper argues that the standard practice of evaluating Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) models using point estimates (e.g., Mean Absolute Error) is insufficient for real-world applications where decision-making is involved. The authors advocate for a shift towards probabilistic models that provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates alongside predictions. The paper focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs) and explores extensions such as Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better model QE data, which often consists of noisy, strictly positive values like post-editing time.

The core of the work is twofold: first, it proposes using metrics that evaluate the entire predictive distribution, such as Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), to better distinguish between models. Second, it demonstrates the practical utility of these uncertainty-aware predictions in asymmetric risk scenarios, where the cost of overestimating quality differs from the cost of underestimating it. Experiments on three datasets show that Warped GPs offer better-calibrated uncertainty estimates (as measured by NLPD) and provide superior performance in these asymmetric scenarios, benefits that are not always apparent from traditional point-estimate metrics alone.

### Contributions

1.  **A compelling argument for evaluating QE models probabilistically.** The primary contribution is the clear articulation and empirical demonstration that evaluating QE models based on their full predictive distribution (using metrics like NLPD) provides more insight than traditional point-estimate metrics. This challenges a long-standing practice in the field and encourages a more nuanced approach to model evaluation and selection.
2.  **A practical framework for leveraging uncertainty in asymmetric risk scenarios.** The paper provides a valuable demonstration of how predictive distributions from a single probabilistic model can be used to derive optimal estimators for various asymmetric loss functions without retraining. This application is highly relevant for real-world translation workflows where the cost-benefit trade-offs can change dynamically.
3.  **An empirical investigation of advanced Gaussian Process models for QE.** The paper presents a useful comparison of different GP configurations, highlighting the effectiveness of Warped GPs for modeling non-Gaussian, positive-only target variables like post-editing time.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses an important and practical problem.** The paper's core motivation—that point estimates are insufficient for many real-world QE applications—is strong and highly relevant. By focusing on uncertainty, the work tackles a fundamental aspect of making QE useful in production environments, such as deciding whether to send a segment for post-editing. The introduction frames this motivation clearly with compelling examples.
2.  **Sound and well-executed methodology.** The choice of Gaussian Processes as the probabilistic framework is well-justified, and the exploration of model extensions (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) is systematic and appropriate for the task. The proposal to adopt NLPD for evaluation is principled and brings QE evaluation more in line with best practices from the machine learning community.
3.  **Novel and insightful application to asymmetric risk.** The analysis in Section 4 is a key strength. It elegantly connects the abstract concept of predictive uncertainty to concrete, practical scenarios using asymmetric loss functions. Showing how Bayes risk estimators can be derived on the fly for different scenarios from a single trained model is a powerful demonstration of the flexibility of the probabilistic approach.
4.  **Excellent clarity and presentation.** The paper is exceptionally well-written, structured, and easy to follow. Concepts like Gaussian Processes and asymmetric loss are explained clearly, making the work accessible to a broad audience within the NLP community who may not be experts in probabilistic modeling.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of comparison to a strong non-probabilistic baseline.** The authors explicitly state they do not aim to advance the state-of-the-art in point prediction, which is reasonable. However, the experiments feel somewhat isolated without a standard baseline like Support Vector Regression (SVR), which is a common and strong performer in WMT QE shared tasks. Including an SVR baseline in the results tables would help contextualize the GP models' performance on MAE and Pearson's r, clarifying whether the move to a probabilistic framework involves a trade-off in point-prediction accuracy.
2.  **The practical benefit of improved NLPD scores is not made tangible.** A central argument is that NLPD is a more informative metric than MAE. However, for readers unfamiliar with it, a "drastic decrease" in NLPD is difficult to interpret. The paper would be significantly more impactful if it included a qualitative analysis with a few concrete examples. For instance, showing a case where a standard GP and a Warped GP have a similar MAE, but the Warped GP (with its better NLPD) correctly expresses high uncertainty on a difficult-to-predict sentence, while the standard GP is confidently wrong. This would make the advantages of the proposed evaluation method much clearer.
3.  **The choice of target variable is not justified or discussed.** The experiments exclusively use post-editing time as the quality label. Much of the QE literature, however, focuses on predicting (H)TER. The paper does not justify why post-editing time was chosen or discuss how its findings might generalize to other targets. The benefits of Warped GPs, in particular, are tied to modeling strictly positive real numbers, a property not shared by HTER, which can be zero. A brief discussion of this would broaden the paper's applicability.

### Questions to Authors

1.  In Section 3.2, you state that for the small `en-es` dataset, the trends for NLL and NLPD differ, which you attribute to overfitting. However, from the results presented, the general pattern (e.g., Warped GPs are better than standard GPs) seems to hold for both NLL and NLPD across all datasets. Could you please clarify this point? Are you referring to a more subtle difference, such as the relative ranking of the top-performing models being different under NLL versus NLPD for `en-es`?
2.  Your work convincingly demonstrates the benefits of Warped GPs for modeling post-editing time. Could you comment on the applicability of this approach to other common QE targets like HTER? Since HTER is not strictly positive and has a different underlying distribution, would you expect the same advantages from the warping function?
3.  To help readers contextualize the performance of your models, would it be possible to add results for a standard SVR baseline to your tables? This would provide a valuable reference point for the MAE and Pearson's r metrics.
