This paper explores a probabilistic approach to Quality Estimation (QE) for machine translation, using Gaussian Processes (GPs) to model the distribution of post-editing time and rates, rather than providing a single point estimate.

**Overall Assessment and Strengths:**

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. The introduction effectively highlights the limitations of point estimates in real-world QE applications, where understanding model uncertainty is often crucial. A particularly strong aspect is the discussion of different asymmetric risk scenarios, which convincingly demonstrates how the choice of estimator should depend on the downstream task (e.g., whether over- or under-estimation is more costly).

The technical exposition is also a key strength. While the detailed introduction to Gaussian Processes may be familiar to some readers, it is a valuable inclusion that makes the paper self-contained and accessible to a broader audience. The GP approach, including the choice of kernels and the use of input warping, is explained with excellent clarity, making the methodology easy to follow. The research questions posed by the paper are interesting, relevant, and well-phrased. Finally, the qualitative discussion in Section 4 is very well-written and provides an intuitive explanation of the results.

In summary, this is a valuable paper that effectively argues for a probabilistic perspective on QE. While it does not aim to advance the state-of-the-art in terms of predictive accuracy, its contribution lies in its focused and well-executed demonstration of how to model and leverage uncertainty, which is a critical aspect of the QE task.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

While the paper is generally strong, there are several areas where additional clarification and analysis would significantly improve its impact.

1.  **Justification of Target Variable:** The experiments use post-editing rates as the target variable. However, a more common target in QE research is (H)TER. Given the prevalence of (H)TER, a brief rationale for choosing post-editing rates instead would strengthen the experimental design and help readers situate the results within the broader QE literature.

2.  **Clarity of Results in Section 3.2:** The first paragraph of this section claims a specific trend for the fr-en and en-de language pairs that is purportedly absent in en-es. However, based on the results presented in the tables, both Negative Log-Likelihood (NLL) and Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) appear to decrease substantially for all three language pairs when using warped GPs. This apparent contradiction needs clarification to make the analysis of the results more transparent.

3.  **Contextualizing Performance with a Baseline:** The authors explicitly state that advancing the state-of-the-art is not a primary goal, justifying the use of a standard set of 17 baseline features. However, the results would be significantly more grounded with the inclusion of a standard point-estimate baseline model from prior work (e.g., a simple SVR or feed-forward network). This would provide valuable context for interpreting the model's performance, even on metrics like MAE, and would help quantify the trade-offs of the GP approach.

4.  **Interpreting NLL/NLPD Improvements:** The paper's central argument is to move beyond simple error metrics like MAE towards probabilistic measures like NLL and NLPD. A key challenge, however, is that these metrics are not intuitively interpretable on their own. To better illustrate the practical benefits of the proposed model, a qualitative analysis with concrete examples would be highly beneficial. For instance, showing a few translation outputs where the GP provides a more useful (e.g., wider for an ambiguous case, or narrower for a high-confidence case) predictive distribution could powerfully demonstrate the value of a reduced NLL/NLPD score and make the paper's core message more compelling.

**Minor Comments:**

*   **Typo:** In Section 4, "over and underestimates" should be hyphenated as "over- and underestimates".
*   **Figure Caption:** The caption for Figure 1 incorrectly identifies the line colors as blue and red; they appear to be blue and green in the submission.
*   **Reproducibility:** For reproducibility, please consider citing the specific software toolkit or library used for the Gaussian Process modeling in the final version of the paper.
