This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). Instead of providing point estimates of post-editing effort, the proposed method regresses on post-editing time/rates to produce a full predictive distribution.

The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated. A particular strength is the introduction, which effectively highlights the limitations of point estimates in real-world QE applications. The discussion of asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications for different estimators is especially insightful. While the paper devotes considerable space to explaining GPs, this effort is worthwhile as it makes the concepts accessible to a broader audience. The description of the GP approach, including the choice of kernels and the use of warping, is very clear and easy to follow. The research questions are well-posed and provide a strong framework for the experimental work.

However, I have some questions and suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion sections.

**Weaknesses and Questions for the Author:**

*   The choice of post-editing rates as the target variable, rather than the more common (H)TER, could be better justified. Providing the rationale for this decision would help contextualize the results within the broader QE literature.
*   The first paragraph of Section 3.2 is unclear. The text claims a trend for fr-en and en-de that is not observed for en-es. However, looking at the results, NLL and NLPD appear to decrease drastically for all three language pairs when using warped GPs. Could the authors please clarify the specific trend they are referring to?
*   The authors state that their goal is not to advance the state-of-the-art, which is reflected in their use of only the standard 17 baseline features. However, the analysis would be strengthened by including at least one other point estimate model from prior work in the result tables. This would provide a valuable baseline to help gauge the overall quality of the proposed models.
*   Related to the previous point, interpreting the absolute values of NLL and NLPD is difficult. While the paper rightly argues against focusing on metrics like MAE, readers may still be tempted to use them to assess the magnitude of prediction errors. To better illustrate the practical benefit of a reduction in NLL/NLPD, a qualitative analysis with concrete examples would be highly beneficial. This could show how the improved uncertainty estimates manifest for specific translation instances.

**Conclusion:**

Overall, this is a valuable paper that addresses the important and often overlooked problem of using point estimates in QE, a task where understanding model confidence is crucial. While the submission may not present significant novelty in its modeling approach or push state-of-the-art performance, its research goals are clearly articulated and the execution is solid. The paper successfully makes a strong case for the utility of probabilistic predictions in QE.

**Minor Issues:**

*   Section 4: "over and underestimates" should be hyphenated as "over- and underestimates".
*   Figure 1 caption: The caption states the lines are blue and red, but they appear to be blue and green in the figure.
*   If a standard toolkit was used for the GP modeling, it would be helpful to cite it in the final version of the paper.
