This paper investigates the application of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes, to perform regression on post-editing time and rates for Machine Translation Quality Estimation.

The submission is well-organized, featuring a lucid introduction that effectively frames the limitations of point estimates in practical QE applications. The discussion of various asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications for estimator selection is particularly insightful. While the extensive background on Gaussian Processes may be familiar to experts, its inclusion is a worthwhile effort to make the concepts accessible to a broader audience. The methodology, including the GP implementation, kernel selection, and the use of warping, is explained with exceptional clarity and is straightforward to follow. The research questions guiding the study are both compelling and well-articulated.

However, I have several questions and suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion sections on Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation:

-   The authors chose to predict post-editing rates. Given that (H)TER is a standard prediction target in QE research, a justification for this choice would strengthen the paper.
-   Regarding Section 3.2, the first paragraph is unclear. The text mentions a trend observed for fr-en and en-de but not for en-es. However, based on the results, NLL and NLPD appear to decrease substantially for all three language pairs when using warped GPs. Clarification on this specific trend is needed.
-   While the authors explicitly state that advancing the state-of-the-art is not a primary goal (as evidenced by the use of the 17 standard baseline features), the results would be better contextualized by including a comparison with a standard point estimate model from the literature. This would offer a clearer baseline for the overall performance of the proposed models.
-   A related point is the interpretability of NLL and NLPD metrics. It is difficult to grasp the practical significance of changes in these values, which can lead readers to default to MAE for a sense of prediction error. Since the paper's core argument is to move beyond such metrics, a qualitative analysis with concrete examples would be highly beneficial. This could illustrate what a "drastic" reduction in NLL/NLPD signifies in terms of prediction quality.

The analysis in Section 4 is exceptionally well-written, presenting the results in a highly intuitive manner.

Overall, this is a valuable paper that effectively highlights the problematic use of point estimates for Quality Estimation—a challenging task where uncertainty information is critically important. While the work does not introduce novel modeling techniques or advance the state-of-the-art, its strength lies in its clearly articulated research goals and its meticulous execution.

Minor Corrections:
-   In Section 4, please correct "over and underestimates" to "over- and underestimates".
-   The caption for Figure 1 refers to blue and red lines, but the lines in the figure are blue and green.
-   It would be beneficial to cite the specific software toolkit used for the Gaussian Process modeling in the final version of the paper.
