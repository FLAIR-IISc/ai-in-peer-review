### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The central argument is that standard point-estimate predictions (e.g., a single quality score) are insufficient for real-world applications, which could benefit from well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors compare different GP configurations (standard vs. Warped, various kernels) and evaluate them not only on traditional metrics like Mean Absolute Error (MAE) but also on the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), which assesses the entire predictive distribution. Furthermore, the paper demonstrates the utility of these probabilistic predictions in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-estimation of quality carries different penalties.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Advocacy and Demonstration of Uncertainty-Aware Evaluation:** The primary contribution is highlighting the limitations of point-estimate metrics in QE and providing a clear, empirical demonstration of how to evaluate models based on their predictive uncertainty. The use of NLPD is shown to capture differences between models that are otherwise invisible to metrics like MAE and Pearson's r.
2.  **Application to Asymmetric Risk Scenarios:** The paper presents a compelling use case for probabilistic QE by framing it within asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic post-editing workflows). It effectively shows how Bayes risk estimators can leverage the predictive distribution to make optimal decisions under different loss functions without retraining the model, which is a valuable and practical insight for deploying QE systems.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper is built on a strong and well-articulated premise: point estimates are insufficient for many real-world QE applications. The introduction clearly explains why uncertainty is crucial for decision-making in translation workflows. This framing successfully identifies a significant gap in conventional QE research and practice.
2.  **Clear and Practical Application Showcase:** The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios in Section 4 is a major strength. It moves the discussion from a purely theoretical evaluation of uncertainty to a practical demonstration of its utility. The explanation of pessimistic and optimistic scenarios is intuitive and grounded in realistic use cases (e.g., gisting vs. post-editing for novice translators), making the potential impact of this work easy to grasp.
3.  **Well-Written and Accessible:** The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The authors do a commendable job of introducing Gaussian Processes, including concepts like Matern kernels and Warped GPs, in a way that is accessible to readers who may not be experts in probabilistic machine learning. This pedagogical clarity enhances the paper's potential to influence the broader QE community.
4.  **Sound Experimental Design for Core Claims:** The experimental setup effectively supports the paper's central arguments. By comparing different GP models and evaluating them with both point-estimate and distribution-aware metrics (NLPD), the authors convincingly show that traditional metrics fail to capture important differences in model quality related to uncertainty calibration.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Context for Results and Interpretability:** While the paper argues for using NLPD, the absolute values of NLPD and NLL are difficult for a reader to interpret without context. The argument would be significantly strengthened by:
    *   **A Qualitative Analysis:** Including a few concrete examples of translations where models with similar MAE but different NLPD scores produce different predictions. For instance, showing a case where a better-calibrated model correctly assigns high uncertainty to a difficult or ambiguous sentence would provide powerful, intuitive evidence for the paper's claims.
    *   **A Standard Baseline:** The authors explicitly state they do not aim to advance the state-of-the-art, which is acceptable. However, the inclusion of a widely-used, non-probabilistic baseline like Support Vector Regression (SVR) in the results tables would help ground the performance of the GP models. This would allow readers to gauge whether the move to a probabilistic framework involves a trade-off with point-estimate accuracy.
2.  **Unclear Analysis of Intrinsic Evaluation Results:** The discussion in the first paragraph of Section 3.2 is confusing. The authors claim that for the en-es dataset, unlike the others, the trend in NLL does not follow NLPD, suggesting overfitting. However, looking at Table 1, NLL and NLPD seem to decrease together for all datasets when moving from a standard GP to a Warped GP. This central piece of analysis is not clearly explained or supported by the provided results, weakening the conclusions drawn from it.
3.  **Missing Justification for Key Experimental Choices:** The experiments predict post-editing rates (normalized time). While this is a valid quality metric, much of the QE literature focuses on predicting metrics like HTER. The paper would be more impactful if it either justified the choice of post-editing time over HTER or demonstrated that its findings also hold for HTER prediction, thus showing the generality of the approach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the argument in the first paragraph of Section 3.2? Specifically, what trend do you observe in NLL and NLPD for the fr-en and en-de datasets that is absent in the en-es dataset, and how does this support the conclusion of overfitting on the smaller dataset?
2.  To help readers build intuition, could you provide a brief qualitative analysis of one or two prediction examples? For instance, an example where a Warped GP provides a more useful prediction (e.g., higher uncertainty for an obviously poor translation) than a standard GP, despite having a similar MAE.
3.  What was the reasoning for choosing post-editing time as the prediction target over a more common automated metric like HTER? Do you believe your conclusions regarding the benefits of probabilistic modeling and uncertainty-aware evaluation would generalize to an HTER prediction task?
