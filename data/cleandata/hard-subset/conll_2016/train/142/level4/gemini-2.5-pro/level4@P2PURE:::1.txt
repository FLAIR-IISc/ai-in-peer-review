This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs), a type of probabilistic model, to predict post-editing time/effort for machine translation quality estimation (QE).

The paper is well-structured, with a clear introduction that effectively frames the limitations of point estimates in real-world QE applications. A particular strength is the detailed description of asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications for selecting appropriate estimators. While the paper dedicates considerable space to introducing Gaussian Processes, which may be familiar to some readers, this detailed explanation is a worthwhile effort that makes the work accessible to a broader audience. The methodology, including the GP approach, the choice of kernels, and the use of warping, is explained with excellent clarity. The research questions are well-phrased and compelling.

However, I have several questions and suggestions regarding the Results and Discussion, particularly concerning the Intrinsic Uncertainty Evaluation:

*   The authors chose post-editing rates as the prediction target. Given that (H)TER is a standard target in QE research, the paper would benefit from a justification for this choice.
*   In Section 3.2, the authors claim to observe a trend for fr-en and en-de that is not present for en-es. This claim is unclear, as the provided results show that both NLL and NLPD decrease drastically with warped GPs for all three language pairs. The authors should clarify this discrepancy.
*   While the authors explicitly state their goal is not to advance the state-of-the-art (e.g., by using only standard baseline features), the results would be better contextualized by including a comparison with a standard point-estimate model from prior work. This would provide readers with a clearer sense of the models' overall performance.
*   Furthermore, metrics like NLL and NLPD can be difficult to interpret in isolation. To reinforce the paper's central argument against relying on point-estimate metrics like MAE, the authors could provide a qualitative analysis. Showing concrete examples would help illustrate what a "drastic" reduction in NLL/NLPD signifies in practice and provide a more intuitive understanding of the model's benefits.

The discussion in Section 4 is very well-written and explains the results intuitively.

In summary, this is a valuable paper that successfully highlights the limitations of using point estimates in QE, a task where uncertainty information is crucial. While the submission does not advance the state-of-the-art or introduce significant modeling novelty (as GPs have been used in this domain before), its contribution lies in its clearly stated research questions and well-executed analysis. The paper effectively argues for a shift in how QE systems are evaluated and utilized.

Minor issues:
*   Section 4: "over and underestimates" should be "over- and underestimates".
*   Figure 1 caption: The caption states the lines are blue and red, but they appear to be blue and green in the figure.
*   If a specific toolkit was used for GP modeling, it would be beneficial to cite it in the final version.
