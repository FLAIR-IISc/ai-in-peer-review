### Summary of the Paper and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for the task of Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The central argument is that conventional QE models, which provide only point estimates (e.g., a single quality score), are of limited use in real-world translation workflows. The authors advocate for models that produce a full predictive distribution, thereby providing a measure of uncertainty alongside the quality prediction. The work evaluates several GP variants using both standard point-estimate metrics and a probabilistic metric (Negative Log Predictive Density, NLPD) that considers the entire predictive distribution. Furthermore, the paper demonstrates the practical utility of these uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios, which are designed to mimic realistic decision-making processes in translation pipelines (e.g., deciding whether to post-edit or discard a translation).

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong, well-motivated argument for incorporating predictive uncertainty into QE.** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of point estimates and makes a compelling case for the practical value of probabilistic predictions. The framing of the problem in the context of asymmetric risk scenarios is particularly effective and relevant to the QE community.
2.  **A systematic evaluation of probabilistic QE models using an appropriate, uncertainty-aware metric (NLPD).** The study provides a clear comparison of different GP configurations (e.g., standard vs. Warped GPs, different kernel choices), offering valuable insights into how these modeling choices impact the quality of uncertainty estimates. This demonstrates that standard metrics like MAE can fail to capture important differences between probabilistic models, reinforcing the paper's central thesis.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Framing:** The paper's primary strength lies in its clear and persuasive motivation. It successfully highlights a key deficiency in current QE practices—the reliance on point estimates—and grounds its proposed solution in practical, real-world applications. The introduction of asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic) is a particularly strong feature, as it provides a concrete framework for understanding why uncertainty is crucial for decision-making.
2.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. It does an admirable job of introducing the theoretical background of Gaussian Processes to an audience that may be unfamiliar with them, without being overly dense. The research questions are clearly stated, and the experimental sections logically follow from the initial motivation. The discussion of the results for the asymmetric risk evaluation (Section 4) is especially intuitive.
3.  **Well-Executed and Focused Experiments:** While the authors state they are not aiming to advance the state-of-the-art in terms of point-prediction accuracy, the experimental design is sound and perfectly aligned with the paper's research goals. The use of NLPD to evaluate the quality of the predictive distributions is appropriate, and the experiments on asymmetric loss functions effectively demonstrate the utility of the proposed approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Unclear Interpretation of Key Results in Section 3.2:** The analysis in the first paragraph of Section 3.2 is difficult to follow. The authors state that for the en-es dataset, unlike the other two, NLL is not a reliable metric and that NLL/NLPD trends differ. However, looking at Table 1, both NLL and NLPD appear to decrease substantially when moving from a standard GP to a Warped GP for *all three* datasets. The specific trend that differentiates the fr-en/en-de results from the en-es results is not clear from the text or the table, which weakens one of the paper's central empirical findings.
2.  **Lack of Comparative Baselines:** The experiments exclusively compare variants of GP models. While the focus is on probabilistic modeling, the results would be much easier to interpret if a strong, standard point-estimate baseline (such as the SVR model mentioned in the introduction) were included in the tables. Providing MAE and Pearson's r for an SVR would help contextualize the GP models' performance and give readers a better sense of what, if anything, is sacrificed in terms of point-estimate accuracy to gain the benefits of a predictive distribution.
3.  **The Practical Value of NLPD Improvements is Not Made Concrete:** A core argument of the paper is that NLPD is a more revealing metric than MAE. However, the absolute values of NLL and NLPD are not intuitive, making it hard for the reader to appreciate the scale of the reported improvements. The paper's argument would be significantly strengthened by a short qualitative analysis with one or two concrete examples. For instance, showing a translation where two models achieve a similar MAE, but the model with better NLPD correctly expresses high uncertainty, would make the benefit of the metric and the modeling approach much more tangible.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the interpretation of the results in the first paragraph of Section 3.2? Specifically, what is the trend in NLL and NLPD that is observed for fr-en and en-de but not for en-es? From Table 1, it appears that both metrics improve substantially with Warped GPs across all datasets.
2.  Your experiments predict post-editing rates (normalized time), whereas much of the QE shared task literature has focused on predicting (H)TER. Could you briefly justify this choice and comment on whether you expect the paper's conclusions regarding the benefits of probabilistic modeling to hold for HTER prediction as well?

### Additional Comments

-   In Section 4, the phrase "over and underestimates" should be hyphenated as "over- and underestimates".
-   The caption for Figure 1 states the lines are blue and red, but they appear to be blue and green.
-   For reproducibility, it would be helpful to mention which software toolkit or library was used for the GP modeling in the final version of the paper.
