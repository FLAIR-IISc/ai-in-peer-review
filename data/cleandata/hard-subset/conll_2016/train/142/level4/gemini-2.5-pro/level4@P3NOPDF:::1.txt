This paper proposes using Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), shifting the focus from traditional point estimates of quality to a probabilistic approach that provides valuable uncertainty estimates. The model is trained to regress on post-editing time/rates, offering a distribution over possible outcomes rather than a single value.

**Strengths**

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. A key strength is its compelling introduction, which effectively highlights the limitations of point estimates in real-world QE applications. The discussion of different asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications for choosing an estimator is particularly insightful and provides strong motivation for the work.

While the explanation of GPs may be a review for experts, the authors have done an excellent job of introducing these concepts accessibly, making the paper self-contained and easy to follow for a broader audience. The GP methodology, including the choice of kernels and the use of input warping, is explained with admirable clarity. The research questions posed are interesting, well-formulated, and directly addressed by the experiments. Furthermore, the discussion of the results in Section 4 is intuitive and effectively demonstrates the practical utility of the model's uncertainty estimates.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement**

While the paper is strong overall, there are several areas where clarification and additions could significantly improve its impact.

1.  **Justification of Target Variable:** The choice of post-editing rates as the target variable is a departure from the more common (H)TER metric used in much of the QE literature. While perfectly valid, the paper would benefit from a brief justification for this choice to help readers contextualize the results with respect to previous work.

2.  **Clarity of Analysis in Section 3.2:** The analysis of the intrinsic uncertainty evaluation in the first paragraph of Section 3.2 could be clarified. The paper claims a different trend for fr-en & en-de compared to en-es, but the results in Table 1 seem to show that NLL and NLPD decrease substantially for all three language pairs when using warped GPs. The authors should elaborate on the specific trend they are referring to, as it was not immediately apparent from the presented data.

3.  **Inclusion of a Point-Estimate Baseline:** While the paper explicitly states its goal is not to advance the state-of-the-art in terms of predictive accuracy (as evidenced by the use of standard baseline features), the evaluation would be strengthened by the inclusion of at least one standard point-estimate baseline model from existing QE literature in the result tables. This would provide a valuable reference point for metrics like MAE and help readers better appreciate the trade-offs and benefits of the proposed probabilistic approach.

4.  **Interpretability of NLL/NLPD and Qualitative Analysis:** The primary evaluation metrics, NLL and NLPD, are statistically sound but can be difficult for readers to interpret intuitively. As the paper correctly argues against over-relying on metrics like MAE, it would be highly beneficial to supplement the quantitative results with a qualitative analysis. Showing a few concrete examples of MT outputs where the GP model provides high or low uncertainty, and explaining why this uncertainty is useful in a post-editing scenario, would powerfully illustrate the practical value of the reduced NLL/NLPD scores and reinforce the paper's central thesis.

**Conclusion**

Overall, this is a valuable contribution to the QE literature. The paper successfully highlights the critical, often-overlooked problem of relying solely on point estimates in a task where uncertainty is a key factor. While it may not present a novel modeling architecture or achieve new state-of-the-art results, its strength lies in its clear articulation of an important problem and its methodologically sound execution of a probabilistic solution. The paper is well-written, the experiments are well-designed, and it encourages a more nuanced view of what constitutes a "useful" QE system.

**Minor Comments**

*   Section 4: "over and underestimates" should be hyphenated as "over- and underestimates".
*   Figure 1 Caption: The caption states the lines are blue and red, but they appear to be blue and green in the figure.
*   Implementation Details: If a standard toolkit was used for the GP modeling, it would be helpful to cite it in the final version of the paper for reproducibility.
