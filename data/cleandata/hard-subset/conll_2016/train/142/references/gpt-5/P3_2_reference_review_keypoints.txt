- Argues for uncertainty-aware QE using Gaussian Processes to obtain calibrated predictive distributions instead of point estimates.
- Revisits GP modeling choices (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) for positive, noisy post-editing time per token.
- Advocates Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as an evaluation metric that captures predictive uncertainty where point metrics fail.
- Shows predictive distributions enable minimum Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses (AL, linex) without retraining, supporting optimistic/pessimistic policies.

- Empirical study: probabilistic QE with GPs and Warped GPs across three language pairs; warping substantially improves NLL/NLPD versus standard GPs.
- Demonstrates that NLPD reveals model differences missed by MAE and Pearson r.
- Practical framework for asymmetric risk: derive Bayes estimators from one predictive distribution; shows scenarios where warped models help and where linex favors higher-variance models.
- Analysis of kernel and likelihood choices (Matérn vs EQ; tanh warping vs log) tailored to QE’s noisy, positive response.

- Clear motivation and relevance: addresses real deployment needs for risk-aware QE and connects modeling to downstream utility.
- Solid probabilistic framing and sensible modeling choices (ARD GPs, Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) aligned with data characteristics.
- Proper scoring evaluation: NLPD is appropriate and shows train NLL can be misleading due to overfitting.
- Warping reduces probability mass on implausible negative values and improves NLPD.
- Demonstrates practicality of adapting decision policy without retraining.
- Transparent discussion of mixed results and nuanced behavior (e.g., pessimistic linex preferring higher-variance models).

- Limited baselines: missing non-GP probabilistic comparators (quantile regression, quantile forests, Bayesian linear regression, deep ensembles/MC-dropout, calibrated SVR/GBDT).
- Incomplete calibration analysis: lacks coverage tests, PIT histograms, reliability curves, or CRPS to fully assess uncertainty calibration.
- Restrictive noise modeling: assumes homoscedastic Gaussian noise; ignores heteroscedastic GPs, Student-t, or direct positive parametric likelihoods (log-normal/Gamma).
- Asymmetric-loss experiments stop short of decision-level evaluation (no accept/reject simulations, utility curves, or triage metrics).
- Limited feature/label scope: uses only 17 QuEst features and post-editing time, leaving generality to richer features/labels (HTER/DA) unclear.
- Kernel comparisons sometimes inconclusive and effect sizes small.

- How are μy and σy computed for the linex Bayes estimator under non-Gaussian warped predictive distributions—exact quadrature or approximation, and how sensitive are results?
- Can you report coverage for 50/80/95% prediction intervals and PIT histograms for standard vs warped GPs—do warped models over- or under-cover?
- Why not model positive responses with parametric likelihoods (log-normal, Gamma) instead of or alongside warping, and any comparisons?
- Were heteroscedastic GPs or Student-t likelihoods tried to address potential variance miscalibration implicated by linex results?
- How was the number of tanh warp terms selected, were warp parameters regularized or given priors, and are learned warpings stable across CV folds?
- Can you include a simple accept/reject simulation (e.g., thresholding median and variance) to quantify decision-level utility gains?
- Could you compare against quantile regression (linear and boosted trees) and a lightweight Bayesian NN (MC dropout) to contextualize GP performance?
