Summary and claimed contributions
The paper argues for modelling predictive uncertainty in machine translation quality estimation (QE), focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs) and, in particular, Warped GPs and Matérn kernels. It evaluates uncertainty via Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) in addition to standard point metrics and demonstrates how predictive distributions can be exploited under asymmetric loss scenarios without retraining, using Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear (AL) and linex losses. Experiments on three QE datasets (en-es, fr-en, en-de) with 17 QuEst features and 10-fold CV show: (i) Warped GPs substantially improve NLL/NLPD over standard GPs; (ii) Matérn kernels sometimes help; (iii) point-metrics often miss these differences; (iv) in optimistic asymmetric settings, Warped GPs outperform standard GPs, whereas linex pessimistic results are mixed.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that uncertainty-aware evaluation (NLPD) reveals important differences among QE models that point metrics (MAE, Pearson r) do not capture, and that Warped GPs yield better-calibrated predictive distributions for QE than standard GPs.

2) Practical demonstration of decision-making with asymmetric risk by deriving and using Bayes risk estimators from predictive posteriors (quantiles and linex estimator), avoiding retraining when the application loss changes.

3) Application-focused GP design choices for QE (Matérn kernels, monotonic output warping) with analysis of their effects on predictive uncertainty and performance.

Strengths
- Clear, relevant motivation grounded in real QE workflows: The paper convincingly articulates why point estimates are insufficient for post-editing and gisting scenarios and how uncertainty can improve downstream decisions. The asymmetric loss setups are realistic and well connected to the proposed methodology.

- Sound probabilistic treatment and appropriate metrics: Using NLPD to assess predictive distributions is standard in GP research and appropriate here. The observation that NLL can be misleading on small data (en-es) while NLPD reflects generalization is valuable, actionable guidance for the QE community.

- Sensible model choices and careful exposition: Warped GPs address positivity of post-editing rates and reduce spurious negative support; Matérn kernels relax smoothness in noisy text regression. The discussion of median vs mean predictions, ARD, and optimization protocol is clear and technically correct.

- Practical contribution on asymmetric decisions without retraining: Deriving AL (quantile) and linex Bayes estimators from the same posterior supports flexible, scenario-dependent deployment, a genuine advantage in production QE pipelines.

- Empirical breadth across three language pairs: Although feature-light, evaluating on three datasets with 10-fold CV and reporting both uncertainty and point metrics provides robustness and highlights dataset-dependent effects (e.g., sensitivity of NLL vs NLPD, log vs tanh warping).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior GP literature: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, and asymmetric Bayes estimators are established. The novelty here is largely their application and analysis in QE; methodologically the contribution is incremental. To strengthen impact, comparisons to other uncertainty-aware baselines common in NLP/ML (e.g., quantile regression, heteroscedastic GPs, Student-t likelihood GPs, ensembles/bagging, Bayesian neural networks) are missing.

- Insufficient calibration analysis beyond NLPD: While NLPD is informative, the paper does not report calibration diagnostics (e.g., probability integral transform histograms, predictive interval coverage and width, calibration error, CRPS). Given the central claim about “well-calibrated” uncertainty, such analyses would substantiate the calibration improvements attributed to warping.

- Narrow feature set and absence of competitive QE baselines: Using only 17 QuEst features eases cross-language comparison but understates what state-of-the-art QE systems achieve. Without stronger baselines (e.g., SVR with ensembling/quantile regression, modern neural QE with uncertainty), it is hard to assess practical gains in realistic settings.

- Asymmetric loss experiments are partly inconclusive and under-analyzed: The linex pessimistic scenario performs worse for Warped GPs; the explanation (lower variance leads to insufficient conservatism) is plausible but not empirically verified. There’s no sensitivity analysis to w, nor an examination of posterior variance distributions, coverage, or tails to diagnose the effect. The en-de linex parameter adjustment (to avoid huge losses) suggests instability that merits deeper exploration.

- Missing details and reproducibility concerns: Tables are referenced but not shown here; effect sizes, confidence intervals, or significance tests are not discussed. Important modelling choices (e.g., warping terms I selection and overfitting control, standardization of inputs/outputs, treatment of outliers, convergence criteria, initialization) could be more precise. No code or data splits are promised, which limits adoption.

Questions to authors
- Calibration: Can you report calibration diagnostics (PIT histograms, coverage of 80/90% intervals, CRPS) to support the claim that Warped GPs are better calibrated than standard GPs? How does calibration differ across datasets?

- Alternative likelihoods: Did you compare Warped GPs to a log-Gaussian (i.e., log-transformed outputs with standard GP) or Gamma/LogNormal likelihood GPs? How do these compare in NLPD and calibration?

- Heteroscedasticity: QE often exhibits input-dependent noise. Did you try heteroscedastic GPs or Warped GPs with input-dependent noise, and if so, how did uncertainty and asymmetric decision performance change?

- Asymmetric loss analysis: Can you provide sensitivity curves over w for AL and linex, and report posterior variance distributions for standard vs Warped GPs to validate the “variance-driven” explanation for linex pessimistic underperformance?

- Baselines: Can you include uncertainty-aware baselines such as quantile regression (pinball loss), bagged SVR with empirical predictive intervals, gradient boosting quantile regression, or Bayesian neural nets (e.g., PBP) to contextualize the gains?

- Practical utility: Do improvements in uncertainty translate into better downstream outcomes (e.g., reduced bad MT sent to post-editors at a fixed throughput, or improved gisting precision/recall)? Any simulation or case study?

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper offers a well-argued case for uncertainty-aware QE with a solid probabilistic framework, appropriate evaluation via NLPD, and a practically useful treatment of asymmetric risks. Methodological novelty is limited and empirical analysis could be deeper (calibration diagnostics, stronger baselines, more thorough analysis of asymmetric scenarios). Strengthening these points would make the contribution clearly above the bar.
