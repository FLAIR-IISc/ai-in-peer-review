Summary
The paper investigates prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). It argues that point estimates are insufficient for downstream decision-making (e.g., post-editing workflows) and studies Gaussian Processes (GPs) as probabilistic QE models. The work evaluates kernels (EQ vs. Matérn) and likelihood adaptations via Warped GPs to handle positive-valued targets (post-editing time/rate). It adopts Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to assess uncertainty quality and demonstrates that point-metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) do not capture important differences. Finally, it shows how calibrated predictive distributions support asymmetric-risk decisions via Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear and linex losses, without retraining the model.

Claimed contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical evidence that probabilistic modelling choices (especially Warped GPs) substantially improve uncertainty quality in QE, as captured by NLPD, and that NLL can be misleading on small datasets while NLPD provides a more reliable generalization measure for uncertainty.

2) A task-oriented treatment of asymmetric risk in QE using Bayes-risk estimators from predictive distributions (quantiles for asymmetric linear loss and a closed-form estimator for linex in the Gaussian case), showing practical gains in optimistic scenarios and highlighting failure modes.

3) A comparative study of kernels and warpings for QE (Matérn vs. EQ; tanh-based vs. log warping), with observations on when these choices matter, and an intrinsic evaluation protocol for uncertainty in text regression.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and task relevance of uncertainty estimates: The paper links uncertainty to concrete QE decisions (e.g., filtering for post-editing vs. gisting) and shows how distributional predictions enable decision thresholds that point estimates cannot support.

- Proper scoring rule–based evaluation: Using NLPD on test data to compare models focuses evaluation on both accuracy and calibration. The results demonstrate that Warped GPs reduce NLPD materially relative to standard GPs, while MAE/Pearson’s r often fail to distinguish models—supporting the claim that point-metrics miss critical uncertainty differences.

- Well-chosen GP extensions for QE: Warped GPs address positive-support targets; Matérn kernels relax smoothness assumptions suitable for noisy QE features. The experiments show consistent NLPD gains from warping and, in some cases, advantages for Matérn kernels.

- Asymmetric-risk demonstration without retraining: The use of Bayes estimators to adapt predictions to asymmetric losses post hoc is practically valuable. The study shows consistent improvements for optimistic scenarios (and asymmetric linear loss), underscoring how calibrated distributions can be leveraged when operational preferences change.

- Transparent discussion of limitations and phenomena: The work identifies that NLL on training can be misleading for small datasets, and analyzes the linex pessimistic case, relating performance to variance properties of predictive distributions.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in methodology: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, and NLPD are established in GP literature. The main novelty is applying them to QE and framing asymmetric-risk evaluation. The methodological contribution is incremental rather than introducing new models or inference techniques.

- Incomplete treatment of linex Bayes estimator under non-Gaussian posteriors: The stated closed-form estimator ŷ = μy − (w σy^2)/2 is exact for Gaussian predictive distributions. Warped GP predictive distributions on the observation scale are non-Gaussian. The paper does not clarify whether it approximates posteriors as Gaussian, computes exact Bayes estimators using E[exp(−wY)], or uses moment-matching; this affects the technical soundness of linex results.

- Evaluation breadth and baselines: Experiments use small, older QE feature sets (17 QuEst features) and do not compare against alternative uncertainty approaches (e.g., quantile regression, Bayesian neural nets/dropout, deep ensembles, bagged SVR). This limits the generality of conclusions about uncertainty quality across modelling families.

- Calibration analysis is limited: NLPD is appropriate, but additional calibration diagnostics (e.g., coverage of predictive intervals, reliability diagrams, PIT histograms, calibration error) would strengthen claims about “well-calibrated” uncertainty. No statistical significance tests are reported for observed differences.

- Risk of overfitting and model selection details: Warped GPs with multiple tanh terms may overfit small datasets. The paper notes overfitting via NLL vs NLPD on en-es but provides limited ablation on number of warping terms and no regularization/prior sensitivity analysis. The two-pass hyperparameter optimization lacks details on initialization stability and selection criteria across model classes.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Linex estimator: For Warped GPs, is the linex Bayes estimator computed assuming Gaussian predictive posteriors on the observation scale (thus using μ, σ^2), or via the exact formula a∗ = −(1/w) log E[exp(−wY)]? If approximations are used, please detail the approximation and its accuracy for non-Gaussian posteriors.

- Calibration diagnostics: Can you report predictive interval coverage, PIT histograms, or calibration error to complement NLPD and support claims of well-calibrated uncertainty?

- Warping complexity: What are the results for different numbers of tanh terms (I=1,2,3) across datasets? Any regularization or priors on warping parameters? How sensitive are results to initialization?

- Baselines: Can you include quantile regression (for asymmetric linear loss) and a simple bagged SVR or dropout-based neural baseline to contextualize the GP-based uncertainty gains?

- Reproducibility: Are code, hyperparameters (including priors/noise), and data splits available to reproduce the NLPD and asymmetric-loss results?
