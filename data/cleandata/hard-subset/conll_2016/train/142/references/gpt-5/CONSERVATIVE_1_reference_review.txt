Summary
This paper studies prediction uncertainty for sentence-level MT quality estimation (QE) using Gaussian Processes (GPs). It argues for evaluating models with negative log predictive density (NLPD) in addition to point-error metrics, and explores two GP variants: (i) Matérn kernels to relax smoothness, and (ii) Warped GPs to handle positive-valued targets via learned monotone transformations. It further examines decision-making under asymmetric risk by applying Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear (AL) and linex losses to the learned predictive distributions. Experiments on three small QE datasets (post-editing time per token with 17 QuEst features) indicate: (a) Warped GPs substantially improve NLL/NLPD over standard GPs (sometimes without point-metric gains), (b) Matérn vs RBF is mixed, and (c) under certain asymmetric losses, warped models can outperform standard GPs, though results for pessimistic linex are inconclusive.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that probabilistic evaluation (NLPD) reveals differences among QE models’ uncertainty quality that point metrics miss, with Warped GPs often better calibrated than standard GPs.
2) Application and careful exposition of established GP extensions (Warped GPs, Matérn kernels) to QE, including practical Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses.
3) A small-scale decision-theoretic analysis showing how predictive distributions can be post-hoc adapted to asymmetric risk without retraining.

Strengths
- Clear focus on uncertainty and decision-making: The paper tackles an important, under-addressed aspect of QE—how to assess and use predictive uncertainty—using proper scoring rules (NLPD) and principled Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses.
- Use of established, well-founded methodology: GPs, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs are theoretically sound choices for small-data regression, which is typical in sentence-level QE. The exposition is accurate and readable.
- Evidence that uncertainty-aware evaluation matters: The experiments convincingly show cases where NLL/NLPD differentiates models that look similar under MAE or Pearson r, supporting the central argument for uncertainty-aware evaluation.
- Practical insight on asymmetric decisions: The post-hoc Bayes estimator approach is attractive for production scenarios where the utility function changes. The analysis of optimistic vs pessimistic regimes is relevant to real QE workflows.
- Sensible experimental protocol: 10-fold CV across three language pairs, with attention to ARD and a two-pass hyperparameter optimization, is reasonable for the scale of the task.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and scope: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, and asymmetric loss Bayes estimators are established. The paper’s novelty is largely in applying known tools to QE. To justify publication, more thorough breadth (comparisons and diagnostics) is needed.
- Missing strong baselines for uncertainty: No comparisons to contemporary uncertainty-capable methods (e.g., quantile regression baselines, gradient-boosted quantile regression, random forest quantiles, deep ensembles/MC dropout/Bayesian NNs), nor to modern QE models (e.g., neural QE with uncertainty). This limits the significance of the empirical claims.
- Insufficient calibration analysis: NLPD is useful but does not characterize calibration comprehensively. There are no reliability diagrams, coverage of prediction intervals, PIT histograms, sharpness vs calibration trade-offs, or alternative proper scores (e.g., CRPS). The explanation for linex under pessimistic settings is speculative without such diagnostics (e.g., variance calibration, tail behavior).
- Narrow and dated feature setup: Using only 17 QuEst features yields an intentionally weak feature space. While the authors state they do not aim to reach SOTA point performance, the limited representational capacity may confound conclusions about uncertainty quality and decision impact. Including a stronger feature set or neural embeddings would increase external validity.
- Limited asymmetric-risk evaluation: Only a few hand-picked weights, no risk curves across a range of w, and no downstream utility metrics (e.g., accept/reject decision quality, coverage vs utility trade-offs). Results for linex are inconclusive without deeper analysis or ablations (e.g., effect of variance scaling/regularization).
- Statistical rigor and reproducibility: No statistical significance testing is reported. Implementation details for Warped GP inference (quadrature settings, numerical stability, convergence criteria, priors/regularization on warping parameters) and code availability are not specified, which hampers reproducibility.

Questions to Authors
1) Calibration diagnostics: Can you provide reliability diagrams, empirical coverage of central prediction intervals, and PIT histograms for standard vs warped GPs? How do these relate to the NLPD gains and to the linex behavior?
2) Stronger baselines: Why not include quantile regression (pinball loss), quantile GBMs/forests, or neural QE with uncertainty (e.g., MC dropout, deep ensembles) to contextualize the gains from Warped GPs?
3) Warped GP details: How is the predictive density computed for NLPD—quadrature resolution, numerical stability, and handling of the inverse warp? Any regularization or priors on the tanh parameters to prevent overfitting, especially on the smallest dataset?
4) Variance calibration: For the pessimistic linex setting, did you examine variance calibration explicitly (e.g., scaling factors, over/under-dispersion)? Would simple post-hoc variance scaling or temperature scaling change the observed trends?
5) Sensitivity analyses: Can you report performance as a function of w (curves) for both AL and linex, and include more kernels/warps (e.g., Box–Cox) and ablations (isotropic vs ARD) to assess robustness?
6) Practical decision tasks: Can you include an accept/reject simulation (e.g., maximize expected utility under a given loss) showing that better NLPD translates into better downstream decision quality? Runtime and scalability considerations for larger QE datasets would also help.

Overall, this paper highlights an important direction—uncertainty-aware QE—using sound, established models. However, to meet the bar for publication, it needs broader comparative baselines, deeper calibration analysis, and stronger evidence that the proposed probabilistic treatments materially improve downstream decisions.
