Summary
This paper argues for modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in sentence-level Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs). It motivates the use of full predictive distributions for downstream decision making (e.g., post-editing filters), proposes GP variants more suitable for QE—Matérn kernels and Warped GPs (monotonic tanh-sum and log warps)—and evaluates them using proper probabilistic metrics (Negative Log Predictive Density, NLPD), in addition to MAE and Pearson’s r. It further demonstrates how to adapt predictions to asymmetric risk scenarios via Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear (pinball) and LINEX losses without retraining. Experiments on three QE datasets (en-es, fr-en, en-de) with 17 QuEst features and 10-fold CV show: (i) warped models substantially improve NLL/NLPD while point metrics often fail to reflect differences; (ii) Matérn kernels help in some cases; (iii) for asymmetric settings, warped GPs help under AL loss, while LINEX pessimistic settings favor higher-variance standard GPs.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that probabilistic evaluation (NLPD) reveals meaningful differences among QE models that point estimates (MAE/r) often miss, and that warping to enforce positivity improves uncertainty quality.
2) Application of Warped GPs and Matérn kernels to QE with careful training and Bayes estimators to handle asymmetric losses without retraining, linking predictive distributions to actionable decision policies.
3) Analysis of optimistic/pessimistic use cases showing the interaction between predictive variance and asymmetric losses (AL vs LINEX).

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation aligned with real QE workflows: The paper convincingly argues why uncertainty matters (e.g., minimizing costly bad MT passes in post-editing) and connects uncertainty to tangible decisions with asymmetric risk. This framing helps the community think beyond point estimation.
- Proper probabilistic evaluation and insights: Using NLPD is appropriate and revealing. The finding that NLL can be misleading on small datasets (en-es) but NLPD remains informative strengthens the case for test-time proper scoring rules and discourages reliance on training marginal likelihood alone.
- Sensible GP modeling choices for QE: The paper justifies Matérn kernels (reduced smoothness) for noisy text features and Warped GPs to enforce positivity. Improvements in NLL/NLPD and sometimes MAE corroborate the modeling rationale.
- Practical Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses without retraining: Demonstrating how to generate decision-optimized predictions from a single probabilistic model is operationally valuable (e.g., switching w as business needs change).
- Multi-dataset evaluation and CV: Using three language pairs and 10-fold CV increases robustness of the observations and mitigates overfitting concerns.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in methods: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD evaluation, and Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses are well established. The contribution is largely an application to QE and an empirical study, not methodological innovation. Prior GP work in QE exists; the advance is incremental.
- Missing strong baselines for uncertainty and asymmetry: There is no comparison to competitive non-GP uncertainty estimators (e.g., quantile regression with pinball loss, quantile random forests, gradient boosting quantiles, Bayesian linear/NN models, deep ensembles). For asymmetric scenarios in particular, quantile regression is a direct baseline for AL. The absence weakens claims about practical superiority.
- Calibration analysis is underdeveloped: NLPD is a proper score, but the paper does not assess calibration quality directly (e.g., prediction interval coverage, reliability diagrams/PIT histograms, CRPS, standardized residual diagnostics). The central claim concerns uncertainty quality; calibration-focused evaluation would strengthen it.
- Asymmetric evaluation risks tautology: The asymmetric experiments optimize predictions with the same loss used for evaluation, potentially overstating practical benefit. More realistic task-level outcomes (e.g., coverage vs productivity in post-editing, or recall/precision tradeoffs in gisting) would better validate decision impact. The LINEX findings remain inconclusive and are not fully resolved.
- Experimental scope and ablations: Only 17 baseline features are used; modern QE systems use richer features or neural encoders. No ablations on warping complexity (number of tanh terms), monotonicity constraints, or heteroscedastic noise modeling. No statistical significance or uncertainty on metrics is reported. Some claims (e.g., Matérn benefits) appear dataset-specific and would benefit from deeper analysis.

Questions to authors
- Warping details: How did you enforce monotonicity of the tanh-sum warp (constraints on ai, bi)? Did you observe optimization instabilities or non-monotonic regions? How sensitive are results to the number of terms I?
- Calibration: Can you report empirical coverage of predictive intervals (e.g., 50/80/95%) and CRPS or calibration plots? Do warped models yield better calibrated intervals than standard GPs?
- Baselines: How do your models compare to quantile regression baselines (pinball loss) for the AL setting, and to quantile RF/GBMs? For uncertainty, how about Bayesian linear regression or simple deep ensembles?
- Heteroscedasticity: Did you try heteroscedastic GPs or Student-t likelihoods? Could input-dependent noise increase variance where appropriate and improve LINEX pessimistic performance without assigning probability to negatives?
- Practical decision metrics: For the post-editing filtering scenario, can you simulate task-level outcomes (e.g., proportion of segments sent to PE, bad-passage rate, estimated productivity) as a function of thresholds and w to demonstrate tangible gains?
- Mean vs median: You fix the median for point comparisons. Did you check whether using the posterior mean (for RMSE-like metrics) changes the conclusions on MAE/r or NLPD?
- Reproducibility: Will code, hyperparameters, and splits be released? Are the QuEst feature extraction pipelines standardized across the three datasets?

Overall, this is a well-motivated empirical study that usefully promotes uncertainty-aware evaluation and decision-making in QE, but it would benefit from stronger baselines, deeper calibration analysis, and more realistic decision metrics to bolster its impact.
