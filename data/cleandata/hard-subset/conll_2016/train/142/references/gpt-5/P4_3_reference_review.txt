Summary and main contributions
The paper studies prediction uncertainty for sentence-level machine translation quality estimation (QE). It argues that point estimates are insufficient for decision-making in MT workflows and focuses on probabilistic models—specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs)—to obtain calibrated predictive distributions. The work evaluates variants of GP regression (Matern vs. EQ kernels; standard vs. warped likelihoods), recommends Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) for uncertainty-aware evaluation, and illustrates how predictive distributions can be exploited under asymmetric risk via Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric loss functions.

Contributions (as assessed here), ordered by strength:
1) Systematic uncertainty-centric evaluation of QE models using NLPD, showing that uncertainty quality is not captured by standard point-metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) and that NLL can be misleading on small datasets.
2) Application of Warped GPs (log and tanh warpings) and Matern kernels to QE, demonstrating improved NLL/NLPD and offering better-behaved predictive distributions aligned with positive-only targets.
3) Demonstration of post-hoc minimum Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses (asymmetric linear and linex), enabling adaptation to pessimistic/optimistic scenarios without retraining.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and practical relevance: The paper makes a convincing case that uncertainty is critical for downstream decisions in QE (e.g., filtering for post-editing vs. gisting), where cost asymmetries exist. This framing is well aligned with real workflows and elevates the evaluation beyond conventional point metrics.

- Technically sound probabilistic modeling: Using GPs for small QE datasets is appropriate. Warped GPs address target support mismatch (strictly positive times), and Matern kernels relax smoothness assumptions likely more realistic for noisy QE features. The training (marginal likelihood maximization, ARD) and evaluation with NLPD are standard and suitable.

- Insightful evaluation protocol and findings: Cross-validation across three language pairs and reporting NLL vs. NLPD provides evidence that (i) warping reduces overconfident mass on negative values, improving NLL/NLPD; (ii) NLL can overfit small data (en–es), making NLPD on test data preferable; and (iii) point metrics often fail to reflect uncertainty differences, supporting the paper’s advocacy for NLPD.

- Asymmetric risk use-case with Bayes estimators: The post-hoc estimators for AL and linex losses demonstrate how predictive distributions can be tailored to changing application risk profiles without retraining, a practical deployment advantage. The observed behavior differences (e.g., warped models underperforming standard GP under pessimistic linex) raise useful insights about variance and loss sensitivity.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Warped GPs, Matern kernels, and NLPD are established. Prior work has used GPs in QE. The main novelty is the systematic uncertainty evaluation in QE and the asymmetric-risk demonstration. The methodological advances are incremental rather than introducing new models or uncertainty methods.

- Calibration assessment is incomplete: NLPD captures sharpness/calibration mix but does not diagnose calibration directly. The paper lacks reliability diagrams/PIT histograms, coverage of prediction intervals, or proper scoring rules like CRPS. Without these, the claim of “well-calibrated” predictive distributions is insufficiently validated.

- Asymmetric linear (AL) estimator formula appears incorrect: The paper states the Bayes estimator is the w/(w+1) quantile. For the defined AL loss L(ŷ, y) = w(ŷ − y) if ŷ > y, else (y − ŷ), the standard derivation gives the 1/(w+1) quantile (i.e., lower quantile when overestimation is penalized more), not w/(w+1). This affects the mapping of “pessimistic” vs. “optimistic” settings and interpretation of results.

- Linex estimator assumptions and implementation unclear: The closed-form estimator ŷ = μ − (w σ²)/2 is exact under a Normal predictive distribution. Warped GPs produce non-Gaussian predictive posteriors over y. It is unclear whether the paper assumes normality, approximates μ and σ² from the warped predictive, or computes the exact Bayes estimator by quadrature. This may explain the mixed linex results and should be clarified and, ideally, corrected.

- Experimental scope and baselines: The study uses 17 QuEst features and does not compare to other uncertainty-capable baselines (e.g., bootstrapped SVR/ensembles, quantile regression forests, Bayesian neural networks/MC dropout/deep ensembles). This limits the generality of conclusions about GP-based uncertainty. There is also no mention of statistical significance testing, ablation on warping complexity (beyond I ≤ 3), or monotonicity constraints enforcement for the tanh warp. Reproducibility details (software, hyperparameter priors/initialization, runtime) are minimal.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Please verify the AL Bayes estimator: for your loss definition, should the optimal quantile be 1/(1 + w) rather than w/(1 + w)? If not, provide the derivation and clarify the mapping between w and “pessimistic/optimistic” regimes.

- For the linex estimator with warped GPs: Are you assuming a Normal predictive distribution over y, or are μ and σ² computed from the true non-Gaussian predictive? Did you attempt direct minimization of expected linex loss via 1D quadrature over p(y|x) rather than using the Normal formula?

- Can you provide calibration diagnostics beyond NLPD (e.g., reliability diagrams, PIT histograms, coverage of nominal prediction intervals, CRPS) to substantiate calibration claims?

- How are monotonicity constraints enforced for the tanh-based warping parameters to ensure a valid monotone warp? What initialization/regularization do you use, and how sensitive are results to I (number of tanh terms)?

- Could you include comparisons to at least one non-GP uncertainty baseline (e.g., bagged SVR with variance estimates, quantile regression forests, deep ensembles) and report statistical significance of main results?

Overall, the paper is clear and technically competent, and it provides a useful uncertainty-centric perspective for QE with practical relevance. The main concerns are limited methodological novelty and gaps/possible errors in the asymmetric loss derivations and calibration assessment.
