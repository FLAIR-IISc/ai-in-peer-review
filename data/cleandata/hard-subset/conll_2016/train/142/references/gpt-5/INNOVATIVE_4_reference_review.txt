Summary
This paper argues for modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE). It instantiates this with Gaussian Processes (GPs), including Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better handle positive, skewed targets (post-editing rate). It proposes Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a core evaluation metric and demonstrates that uncertainty-aware evaluation distinguishes models that look similar under point-metrics. Finally, it explores asymmetric-risk settings (optimistic/pessimistic) and shows how to obtain minimum Bayes risk estimators from predictive posteriors without retraining, with empirical results on three QE datasets.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Conceptual/empirical case for uncertainty-aware QE: adopting proper scoring rules (NLPD) and predictive distributions, showing that point metrics (MAE, r) mask important differences.
2) Application of Warped GPs (and Matérn kernels) to QE to respect positivity and reduce overconfident mass on nonsensical values; evidence that these yield better calibrated predictive densities than standard GPs.
3) Framing QE under asymmetric loss and demonstrating how to adapt predictions on-the-fly via Bayes estimators (quantiles, linex), avoiding retraining and improving performance in optimistic scenarios.

Strengths
- Clear, timely problem framing: The paper convincingly motivates why point estimates are insufficient for real QE workflows (post-editing, gisting) and why calibrated uncertainty matters for decision-making.
- Proper scoring and uncertainty-centric evaluation: The use of NLPD on test sets is appropriate and informative; the experiments show concrete cases where MAE and r fail to reflect uncertainty quality, which is valuable guidance for the community.
- Sensible modeling choices: Warped GPs are a principled way to handle positive-valued, skewed targets; Matérn kernels relax smoothness assumptions appropriate for noisy QE features. The empirical finding that warping dramatically improves NLL/NLPD is compelling.
- Asymmetric risk treatment with Bayes estimators: The paper connects QE decisions to asymmetric losses and shows how to adapt predictions via quantiles/linex without retraining. Results in optimistic scenarios consistently favor warped models, illustrating practical benefit.
- Breadth over three language pairs with cross-validation: While not SOTA-oriented, the breadth and protocol lend credibility to the generality of the findings. The observation that training NLL can mislead on small data is a useful caution.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: GPs (including Warped GPs), Matérn kernels, NLPD, and asymmetric losses/Bayes estimators are established. The novelty lies mainly in porting these ideas to QE and tying them together. Readers seeking new modeling techniques may find this incremental.
- Calibration analysis is incomplete: NLPD is informative but alone does not fully characterize calibration. The paper lacks reliability diagnostics (e.g., coverage of prediction intervals, PIT histograms, calibration curves) and proper scores beyond log score (e.g., CRPS), which would substantiate claims about “well-calibrated” uncertainty.
- Sparse baselines for asymmetric settings: Since AL loss optimal estimators are quantiles, direct quantile regression (e.g., linear/GBM quantile models) is a natural baseline; similarly, simple heteroscedastic regression or bootstrap ensembles could offer competitive uncertainty without GP complexity. Comparisons are limited to GP variants.
- Extrinsic decision impact not demonstrated: The asymmetric experiments optimize losses but stop short of realistic downstream evaluations (e.g., deferral policies for post-editing with utility/cost curves, precision–recall/coverage trade-offs conditioned on uncertainty). This would strengthen the case that uncertainty-aware QE materially improves workflow outcomes.
- Linex pessimistic results underexplored: The explanation that warped models’ lower variance harms linex pessimistic performance is plausible, but remains speculative. Analyses of variance calibration, sharpness vs. calibration, and sensitivity to w would clarify whether the issue is model misspecification, loss bias, or miscalibration.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report calibration diagnostics (e.g., empirical coverage of central prediction intervals, reliability diagrams, or PIT histograms) for standard vs. warped GPs? Do the warped models improve both calibration and sharpness?
- How sensitive are results to the number of tanh terms and regularization in the warping? Do you use priors or constraints to mitigate overfitting, and how stable is optimization across folds?
- For asymmetric settings, how do GP-based Bayes estimators compare to direct quantile regression baselines on the same features? Do GPs offer consistent gains in optimistic/pessimistic scenarios?
- Can you provide an extrinsic decision evaluation (e.g., cost/benefit of routing segments to post-editing based on predictive mean and uncertainty thresholds) to quantify practical impact?
- Do you plan to release code and CV splits to facilitate reproducibility?

Overall, while the technical ingredients are established, the paper takes a useful and underexplored perspective in QE—treating predictive uncertainty as a first-class citizen and leveraging it for asymmetric decision-making. I find the direction worthwhile and, despite some missing analyses, lean positive given the conceptual contribution and empirical evidence.
