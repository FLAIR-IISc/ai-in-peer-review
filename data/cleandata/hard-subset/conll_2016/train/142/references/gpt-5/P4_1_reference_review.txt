Summary
The paper argues that point estimates in MT Quality Estimation (QE) are insufficient for downstream decision-making, and that well-calibrated predictive uncertainty is needed. It proposes using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with (i) Matérn kernels to relax smoothness assumptions and (ii) Warped GPs to better model strictly positive targets (post-editing time/rate). It adopts Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as an evaluation metric sensitive to uncertainty calibration and shows that standard point metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) miss important differences. Experiments on three QE datasets (en–es, fr–en, en–de) using 17 QuEst features and 10-fold CV compare EQ vs Matérn kernels and standard vs warped likelihoods. The paper also demonstrates a decision-theoretic use case: asymmetric risk settings (optimistic/pessimistic) addressed via Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses (asymmetric linear and linex), showing benefits from calibrated predictive distributions without retraining.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical study of predictive uncertainty for QE: evaluating GP variants (Matérn kernels and Warped GPs) with NLPD, showing that uncertainty-aware metrics reveal differences not captured by MAE/Pearson r, and that NLL on training may be misleading relative to test NLPD.
2) Demonstration of a practical decision-theoretic layer for QE: using predictive posteriors to implement Bayes-optimal estimators for asymmetric losses (quantiles for AL; closed-form estimator for linex), enabling scenario-specific predictions without retraining.
3) Evidence that Warped GPs are better suited to positive-valued QE targets, improving NLL/NLPD across datasets and enabling improved performance in asymmetric-risk scenarios (particularly optimistic settings).

Strengths
- Clear motivation and practical relevance: The paper grounds the need for uncertainty in realistic workflows (post-editing, gisting) where asymmetric costs matter. This aligns the modeling choices with actionable decisions (filtering MT segments).
- Sound probabilistic framing and technically standard, correct methods: GPs are appropriate for small, feature-based QE datasets; Matérn kernels address over-smoothness; Warped GPs match the positive support of labels. Bayes estimators for AL and linex are appropriate and computationally efficient.
- Appropriate evaluation with uncertainty-aware metrics: NLPD is a proper metric for predictive distributions. The study shows (i) warped models substantially improve NLL/NLPD by avoiding mass on negative targets; (ii) NLL vs NLPD divergence on small data (en–es) indicates overfitting, supporting the recommendation to prefer test NLPD for model selection.
- Decision-theoretic analysis with asymmetric losses: The paper operationalizes predictive posteriors by deriving estimators tailored to losses without retraining, a useful design for changing business constraints. Results highlight patterns (warped models better in optimistic settings; linex pessimistic penalties interacting with variance), surfacing practically relevant trade-offs.
- Experimental protocol is reasonable and transparent: 10-fold CV across three language pairs, fixed feature set for comparability, ARD tuning, and reporting of multiple metrics increase credibility and reproducibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, and NLPD are well-established; the contribution lies primarily in application to QE and in tying predictive uncertainty to asymmetric decision rules. This is incremental rather than algorithmically novel.
- Lack of broader baselines and calibration diagnostics: The study does not compare to alternative uncertainty estimation methods (e.g., ensemble SVR/bootstrapped models, Bayesian linear models, heteroskedastic GPs, or neural uncertainty methods). Beyond NLPD, calibration diagnostics (PIT histograms, CRPS, reliability diagrams) are absent, limiting a full assessment of calibration quality.
- Asymmetric loss evaluation is somewhat shallow: The pessimistic linex case is reported as inconclusive, with limited analysis of why and no mitigation (e.g., variance adjustment, transformation-aware estimators, or alternative losses such as asymmetric quadratic/expectiles). There is also no task-level decision simulation (e.g., segment filtering precision/recall, productivity impact) that would tie loss improvements to business outcomes.
- Some technical clarity issues: The predictive density for Warped GPs appears to omit the squared term and 1/2 in the exponent in the text (likely a typographical error). It is not entirely clear whether the linex estimator for warped models uses exact moments in observation space (via quadrature) or a Normal approximation; details on variance computation in the warped space are not provided. The role of ARD in Matérn vs EQ is not analyzed beyond aggregate metrics.
- Scope and scalability: The study uses 17 features and relatively small datasets; stronger feature sets or modern QE architectures (e.g., pretrained encoders) are not considered. Computational cost and scalability of warped models (e.g., optimization stability with multiple tanh terms) are not discussed.

Questions to authors
- For Warped GPs, how are μy and σy computed in observation space for the linex estimator—by exact quadrature/moment matching, sampling, or Normal approximation? If approximation is used, how sensitive are the results?
- Can you provide calibration diagnostics beyond NLPD (e.g., PIT histograms, CRPS) to substantiate claims of “well-calibrated” uncertainty?
- How sensitive are results to the number of tanh terms (I) in the warping? Did you observe overfitting or optimization instability as I increases?
- Did you evaluate heteroskedastic GPs or input-dependent noise models, given the likely heteroskedasticity in post-editing times?
- How do the uncertainty-aware GPs compare to strong non-GP baselines that can produce uncertainty (e.g., quantile regression SVR, ensembles, Bayesian linear models), both in NLPD and asymmetric losses?
- Can you relate asymmetric-loss improvements to an end-to-end decision metric (e.g., filtering precision/recall or cost savings) to better quantify practical impact?
