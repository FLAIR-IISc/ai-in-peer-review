- Paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in MT quality estimation (QE) using Gaussian Processes (including Matérn and Warped GPs) and evaluates with proper scoring rules (NLPD), applying Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses without retraining.

Contributions:
- Shows uncertainty-aware evaluation (NLPD) reveals model differences not captured by point metrics and that Warped GPs substantially improve predictive densities for QE.
- Frames QE decision-making via asymmetric losses and Bayes estimators on predictive posteriors, demonstrating practical benefits for optimistic/pessimistic scenarios.
- Explores GP design choices (Matérn vs RBF, tanh warping vs log transform) and reports dataset-size effects such as NLL overfitting on small data.

Strengths:
- Clear motivation: highlights risks of relying on point estimates in downstream QE workflows and the value of calibrated uncertainty.
- Principled methodology: appropriate use of GPs, ARD, Matérn kernels, Warped GPs, and NLPD as a proper scoring rule.
- Practical decision-theoretic contribution: Bayes estimators enable dynamic risk preferences without retraining.
- Credible empirical setup: experiments across three language pairs with 10-fold CV on public datasets.
- Nontrivial analysis: identifies nuanced behaviors (e.g., linex loss performing worse for warped models due to lower variance).

Weaknesses:
- Limited novelty: methods are established; contribution is mainly application and comparative study within QE.
- Narrow baselines: only 17 QuEst features and traditional GP baselines; lacks stronger modern QE baselines (boosting, neural, heteroscedastic/Student-t GPs).
- Missing calibration diagnostics: no PIT histograms, interval coverage, or reliability diagrams to support calibration claims.
- Weak demonstration of practical impact: asymmetric-risk evaluation reports loss values but doesn't simulate downstream decision outcomes or cost-benefit trade-offs.
- Reporting/clarity issues: missing tables, unclear statistical testing, some imprecise equations, typos, and thin reproducibility details (initializations, hyperparams, seeds, runtime).

Questions for authors:
- Can you provide calibration diagnostics (PIT, credible interval coverage, calibration curves) and show whether warped models improve calibration beyond NLPD?
- How do your models compare to quantile regression, heteroscedastic/Student-t GPs, and modern non-GP baselines (e.g., gradient boosting with quantile loss, Bayesian neural nets)?
- Can you include a decision simulation (e.g., threshold-based post-editing acceptance) to demonstrate cost/productivity trade-offs under different risks?
- How were tanh warping parameters initialized and constrained for monotonicity, how was the number of terms chosen, and were alternative monotone warpings tried?
- Can you verify the linex “variance-driven” explanation by matching predictive variances or using positive-support likelihoods to test robustness?
- Will you release code, preprocessed features, and exact hyperparameters (optimizer, learning rates, quadrature settings, restarts, seeds) for reproducibility?

Overall assessment:
- Originality: moderate (application of known methods to QE).
- Soundness/technical quality: good, but lacks calibration diagnostics and broader baselines.
- Significance: moderate for QE practitioners, limited broader methodological impact.
- Clarity: generally clear but marred by reporting/formatting and implementation detail gaps.
- Reproducibility: partial—public data and standard methods used, but code and exact settings not provided.
