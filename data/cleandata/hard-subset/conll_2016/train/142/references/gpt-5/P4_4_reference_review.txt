Summary and Claimed Contributions
This paper argues for modelling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in machine translation quality estimation (QE). It studies Gaussian Processes (GPs), including Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, and evaluates them with Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) in addition to standard point metrics. It further illustrates how predictive distributions can support asymmetric risk scenarios via Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear (AL) and linex losses.

Contributions (as assessed by the reviewer), in order of importance:
1) Empirical demonstration that evaluating QE models with NLPD reveals important differences in uncertainty quality not captured by point metrics, and that Warped GPs substantially improve NLPD by respecting the positive support of post-editing time.
2) Application of Warped GPs (with tanh-based warping and log warping) and Matérn kernels to QE, showing consistent gains in NLL/NLPD and occasional improvements in MAE over standard GPs with EQ kernels.
3) Illustration of decision-oriented evaluation for QE under asymmetric risk, deriving Bayes estimators from predictive posteriors and showing benefits in optimistic/pessimistic AL scenarios (with caveats for linex in pessimistic settings).

Strengths
- Sound and well-motivated shift toward uncertainty-aware QE: The paper clearly motivates why point estimates are insufficient in production workflows (e.g., post-editing triage), and adopts a proper scoring rule (NLPD) to assess full predictive distributions. The intrinsic analysis shows that models can differ meaningfully in uncertainty quality even when point metrics are similar.
- Appropriate probabilistic modelling choices for positive-valued labels: Warped GPs are a principled way to handle strictly positive response variables, avoiding the probability mass on negatives under a Gaussian likelihood. Empirically, warping drastically reduces both NLL and NLPD across datasets, supporting the modelling choice.
- Clear, technically correct treatment of asymmetric risk: The paper derives Bayes estimators for AL and linex losses from predictive posteriors and evaluates them without retraining, aligning with realistic scenarios where the loss may change. Using quantiles for AL and the analytic estimator for linex is standard and sound, and the study shows tangible gains in optimistic AL settings.
- Careful experimental protocol and transparent observations: The use of 10-fold cross-validation and per-fold hyperparameter optimization is appropriate. The discussion acknowledges overfitting risks (NLL vs NLPD on small data), the mixed impact of Matérn vs EQ kernels, and the puzzling linex pessimistic results, avoiding overclaims.
- Broad applicability beyond QE: While focused on QE, the methodology (warped posteriors, NLPD evaluation, Bayes decision under asymmetric loss) is applicable to other text regression tasks, making the study useful to a wider audience.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established methods: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, and Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses are well-known. The main novelty lies in their combined application to QE and in emphasizing NLPD/decision-centric evaluation. Algorithmic contributions are minimal.
- Narrow experimental scope and baselines: Experiments use small, older datasets (WMT14/16, Specia 2011) and a restricted 17-feature QuEst set. There is no comparison to alternative uncertainty-capable baselines (e.g., quantile regression SVR, Bayesian linear/NN models, quantile/random forest approaches, heteroscedastic GPs, log-normal likelihoods), limiting the strength of empirical conclusions.
- Insufficient uncertainty calibration analysis: NLPD is informative, but calibration diagnostics (e.g., PIT histograms, coverage of predictive intervals, reliability curves) are not reported. Given the focus on “well-calibrated uncertainty,” calibration evidence would substantively strengthen the claims.
- Decision-oriented evaluation remains abstract: Although asymmetric losses are explored, there is no evaluation of downstream decision metrics (e.g., risk-coverage curves, selective prediction, precision-recall trade-offs for acceptance/triage). This limits practical insight into how much uncertainty modelling improves real QE decisions.
- Unresolved behavior under pessimistic linex: The explanation for worse linex performance (variance-related and potential bias toward high-variance models) remains speculative. Alternative remedies (e.g., heteroscedastic GPs, heavier-tailed posteriors, explicitly constrained support via log-normal likelihood) are not explored, leaving an open gap in the proposed approach.

Questions to Authors (optional)
- Calibration: Can you report calibration metrics (e.g., empirical coverage of 50/80/95% credible intervals, PIT histograms)? Do Warped GPs improve calibration beyond NLPD?
- Likelihood choice: Did you compare Warped GPs to GPs with log-normal or other positive-support likelihoods (e.g., using Laplace/EP)? This could disentangle gains from warping vs an appropriate likelihood.
- Alternative baselines: How do your models compare to quantile regression (e.g., SVR quantiles), quantile forests, or heteroscedastic GPs for asymmetric losses?
- Decision metrics: Can you provide risk-coverage or selective prediction analyses showing productivity/quality trade-offs for post-editing triage?
- Computational aspects: What are training/inference times and scalability of Warped GPs vs standard GPs under your settings? Any stability issues with warping parameter initialization or number of tanh terms?
- Linex pessimistic regime: Did you try alternative priors, variance inflation, or heteroscedastic models to mitigate the variance-sensitivity? Any sensitivity analysis over w values beyond those reported?

Overall assessment
This is a clear, technically sound empirical study advocating uncertainty-aware QE. The main value is methodological: using Warped GPs and NLPD, and demonstrating decision-focused evaluation via asymmetric losses. However, algorithmic novelty is limited, and the empirical scope and calibration/decision analyses are not comprehensive. Strengthening baselines and adding calibration and decision metrics would significantly improve the paper’s impact.
