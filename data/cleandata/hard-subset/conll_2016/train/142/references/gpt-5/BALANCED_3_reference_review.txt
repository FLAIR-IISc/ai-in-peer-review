Summary and claimed contributions
The paper argues for moving beyond point estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) by modeling predictive uncertainty with Gaussian Processes (GPs), including Warped GPs and Matérn kernels. It evaluates models using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) in addition to MAE and Pearson’s r, and demonstrates how probabilistic predictions enable decision-making under asymmetric risks via Bayes-optimal estimators for asymmetric losses (AL and linex). Experiments cover three QE datasets using 17 QuEst features.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Evidence that uncertainty-aware evaluation (NLPD) distinguishes QE models that look similar under point metrics, and that Warped GPs improve predictive densities over standard GPs.
2) A decision-theoretic framing of QE with asymmetric loss and Bayes-risk estimators, showing benefits of uncertainty modeling in optimistic/pessimistic scenarios and surfacing a counterintuitive negative result for linex in pessimistic settings.
3) Application study of GP design choices (Warped vs standard, Matérn vs EQ) for QE.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and decision-theoretic framing. The paper convincingly argues why uncertainty matters in QE workflows (e.g., post-editing triage, gisting), and connects this to proper scoring rules (NLPD) and asymmetric losses. This framing is valuable and underrepresented in QE evaluations that rely solely on symmetric point metrics.
- Solid use of probabilistic modeling tools. The GP setup is appropriate for small QE datasets. Warped GPs are a principled way to respect positivity of post-editing rates, and the use of ARD and Matérn kernels is well motivated for noisy text regression. The two-pass hyperparameter optimization and 10-fold CV are reasonable practices.
- Empirical finding that warping matters. Across datasets, warping substantially improves NLL/NLPD while MAE and Pearson’s r often fail to reflect these differences. This supports the core claim that uncertainty-aware metrics provide different and actionable model rankings.
- Decision-centric evaluation with asymmetric loss. Deriving and applying Bayes-optimal estimators for AL and linex concretely illustrates how predictive distributions enable post-training adaptation to changing utility. The reported optimistic-scenario gains for Warped GPs under AL are practically relevant.
- Interesting negative result for linex. The observation that Warped GPs underperform standard GPs with linex loss in pessimistic settings, possibly due to lower predictive variance, is a useful caution and suggests directions for model-loss interplay analysis.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty beyond application. Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, and Bayes-risk estimators for asymmetric losses are established. The contribution is largely an application to QE with relatively small-scale experiments. Stronger novelty would require new modeling, calibration, or decision mechanisms specific to QE.
- Incomplete uncertainty evaluation. NLPD is a proper score, but the work lacks calibration diagnostics that would substantiate “well-calibrated” claims: e.g., predictive interval coverage (PICP), calibration curves or PIT histograms, CRPS/Winkler scores, and risk-coverage/selective prediction analyses. These would directly assess the suitability of predictive variances used in asymmetric decisions.
- Narrow baselines and model space. Comparisons are only among GP variants. Missing are competitive probabilistic and decision-oriented baselines: quantile regression (classic for AL), heteroscedastic GPs or Student-t likelihoods, Bayesian neural regressors/MC-dropout, deep kernel learning, bagged SVR with prediction intervals, and gradient-boosted quantile models. This limits the strength of the empirical claims.
- Dataset and feature limitations. The datasets are relatively small and dated, feature sets minimal (17 QuEst features), and each language pair uses a single MT system. This constrains generalizability to modern QE settings (e.g., segment-level DA/MQM, multilingual setups, strong neural features). The paper’s conclusions about uncertainty utility in production should be tempered accordingly.
- Insufficient statistical rigor and ablations. No significance tests are reported for NLPD or asymmetric-loss differences. There is limited analysis of overfitting (noted on en-es), no ablation on the number of warping terms, and minimal hyperparameter/prior details. The explanation for the linex behavior is plausible but speculative without variance/coverage diagnostics.

Questions to authors
- How are zeros (or near-zeros) in post-editing rates handled? Log warping is undefined at zero; do the datasets contain zeros and how are they treated?
- Did you assess calibration beyond NLPD (e.g., coverage of 50/80/95% intervals, PIT, CRPS)? If not, can you report coverage to support claims about well-calibrated uncertainty?
- What are the distributions of predictive variances for standard vs Warped GPs? Can you quantify how these differences relate to the linex results?
- Did you try heteroscedastic GPs or heavy-tailed likelihoods (e.g., Student-t) that might better capture noise and improve linex behavior in pessimistic scenarios?
- How sensitive are results to the number of tanh terms in the warping and to regularization/prior choices? Any signs of overfitting with I=3?
- What is the runtime and computational overhead for training and for producing Bayes estimators post hoc?

Suggestions for improvement
- Add calibration and decision-focused evaluations: PICP/ACE for credible intervals, PIT/CRPS, coverage risk vs fraction accepted (risk-coverage curves), and a simulated post-editing triage study (utility vs throughput under abstention).
- Broaden baselines: quantile regression (and forests/GBMs) for AL; heteroscedastic/Student-t GPs; Bayesian neural regressors with approximate inference; bagged SVR intervals. Include modern QE features (e.g., sentence embedding/LM-based representations) to ensure findings transfer.
- Provide ablations and statistical tests: vary warping complexity, compare log vs learned warps systematically, and report paired tests for NLPD/asymmetric losses.
- Analyze the linex discrepancy: decompose contributions of mean vs variance, examine calibration of upper tails, and consider calibrated variance scaling or loss-aware post-hoc calibration.
- Clarify reproducibility: release code, scripts, and splits; detail optimization settings, priors/constraints ensuring monotonic warping, and preprocessing (normalization).

Overall recommendation
Weak reject. The paper is thoughtful and well-motivated, and it demonstrates the value of uncertainty-aware evaluation and decision-making in QE. However, the methodological novelty is limited, the empirical scope is narrow, and crucial uncertainty calibration and baseline comparisons are missing. With expanded evaluation, stronger baselines, and deeper analysis of calibration and decision metrics, this could become a solid contribution.
