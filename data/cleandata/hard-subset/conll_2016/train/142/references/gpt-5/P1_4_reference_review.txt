Summary
This paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE) and evaluates Gaussian Process (GP) variants that yield full predictive distributions. The authors (i) motivate using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to assess predictive distributions beyond point metrics, (ii) explore kernels (EQ vs. Matérn) and Warped GPs to better fit positive response variables (post-editing time/rate), and (iii) show how predictive distributions enable minimum Bayes risk decision-making under asymmetric losses (AL and linex) without retraining. Experiments on three language pairs (en–es, fr–en, en–de) with QuEst features and 10-fold CV suggest warped GPs improve NLPD over standard GPs and can improve asymmetric decision performance, especially in “optimistic” settings.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical study demonstrating that in QE, uncertainty-aware evaluation (NLPD) distinguishes models that point metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) fail to differentiate, and that Warped GPs provide better-calibrated predictive densities than standard GPs.
2) Pragmatic demonstration that asymmetric decision costs in QE (optimistic/pessimistic scenarios) can be handled by Bayes estimators on predictive posteriors without model retraining, yielding improvements in optimistic scenarios.
3) Smaller methodological exploration for QE: enforcing positivity and relaxed smoothness via Warped GPs and Matérn kernels; analysis of when training NLL can be misleading relative to test NLPD.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and relevance: The paper addresses a practical gap in QE workflows—point predictions alone are insufficient for risk-sensitive decisions. It makes a convincing case for uncertainty estimates and connects them to concrete downstream decisions (post-editing, gisting).
- Proper probabilistic evaluation: Using NLPD as a proper scoring rule for regression is appropriate and highlights differences that MAE/r miss. The observation that training NLL can mislead on small datasets while NLPD is more reliable is useful guidance for practitioners.
- Sensible modeling choices for QE: Warped GPs to handle positive-valued targets and Matérn kernels to relax smoothness are well-motivated. The empirical gains of warping on NLPD (and sometimes MAE) substantiate the modeling rationale.
- Decision-theoretic treatment of asymmetric risk: The use of Bayes estimators for AL and linex losses on top of a single trained probabilistic model is practical and reduces operational burden when costs change. The analysis of optimistic vs. pessimistic scenarios provides actionable insight.
- Cross-dataset evaluation: Results across three language pairs with 10-fold CV add robustness and show trends generalize beyond a single corpus.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core techniques (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses) are established. The contribution is largely an application-focused study for QE, rather than new models, inference, or evaluation methodology.
- Incomplete baselines and ablations: The study compares only GP variants. Missing are strong uncertainty-oriented baselines such as quantile regression (SVR/GBM), Student-t likelihood GPs, heteroscedastic GPs, Bayesian NNs/ensembles/MC-dropout, or even bagged SVR as a practical uncertainty proxy. This makes it hard to assess whether GPs are indeed preferable for QE uncertainty.
- Calibration analysis is shallow: While NLPD is reported, there is no assessment of calibration/coverage (e.g., prediction interval coverage probabilities, PIT histograms, CRPS). Claims about “well-calibrated uncertainty” would be stronger with coverage diagnostics and proper scoring beyond NLPD.
- Linex estimator derivation and use under non-Gaussian posteriors: The stated Bayes estimator ŷ = µy − (w/2)σy^2 is exact for Gaussian predictive posteriors. Warped GPs produce non-Gaussian predictive distributions over y; using only mean/variance is at best a moment-matching approximation. This may explain the observed failures in pessimistic linex scenarios. The paper should either derive the general solution (via log-MGF) or justify the approximation empirically.
- Reporting, significance, and reproducibility: Key experimental details are underspecified (initialization and regularization of warping, number of tanh terms chosen per dataset, constraints to prevent overfitting, feature scaling, numerical methods for posterior means/quantiles). No statistical significance tests are reported, and code/data splits are not released. Some conclusions (e.g., Matérn > EQ) are weak/inconclusive and would benefit from ablations and uncertainty bands.

Questions to Authors
- Linex estimator: For Warped GPs, the predictive over y is non-Gaussian. Did you use the normal-approximation estimator ŷ = µy − (w/2)σy^2, or did you compute the exact Bayes estimator via the log-MGF E[exp(−wY)]? If the former, can you quantify approximation error (e.g., via simulation) and whether this explains the pessimistic linex results?
- Calibration diagnostics: Can you report interval coverage (e.g., 50%/90% predictive intervals), PIT histograms, and CRPS to substantiate calibration claims? Do Warped GPs yield better coverage than standard GPs?
- Quantile computation: For AL loss, did you compute quantiles of the predictive y via numerical inversion of the warped posterior? Please detail the numerical procedure and its stability across folds/datasets.
- Baselines: Can you include quantile regression (e.g., quantile SVR), Student-t likelihood GP, and a heteroscedastic GP (or a simple deep ensemble) as uncertainty baselines? This would contextualize the relative merits of Warped GPs for QE.
- Implementation details: How were the warping parameters initialized and regularized? Did you use priors or constraints to prevent overfitting with I=3 tanh terms? Were features standardized and were any transforms applied to the target prior to warping? Will you release code and splits?

Overall, this paper provides a useful and timely case for uncertainty-aware QE and offers practical guidance with GPs and asymmetric decision-making. Strengthening empirical baselines and calibration analysis, and clarifying the linex estimator under non-Gaussian posteriors, would significantly improve its impact.
