Summary and contributions
This paper argues for modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in machine translation quality estimation (QE). It instantiates Gaussian Processes (GPs) with Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better capture non-Gaussian, positive-valued targets (post-editing time rates), and evaluates models with proper probabilistic metrics (Negative Log Predictive Density, NLPD) in addition to point metrics. It further demonstrates how predictive distributions enable decision-theoretic predictions under asymmetric risk via Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear and linex losses.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) Empirical evidence that uncertainty-aware evaluation (NLPD) distinguishes QE models that look similar under point metrics, and that Warped GPs substantially improve predictive densities on QE datasets.
2) A decision-theoretic treatment of asymmetric application scenarios in QE (post-editing vs gisting), showing how to derive and use Bayes-optimal predictors from the same probabilistic model without retraining.
3) Modest modeling exploration for QE (Matérn vs EQ kernels; parametric vs log warping) with analysis of when choices matter.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and correct use of proper scoring rules: The paper convincingly motivates why point estimates are insufficient in QE workflows and employs NLPD, a proper scoring rule that rewards calibrated predictive densities. The finding that NLL can mislead on small datasets while NLPD on held-out data is more reliable is useful guidance.

- Sound, well-grounded methodology: The GP and Warped GP setups (ARD, marginal likelihood training, warping via sums of tanh or log) and Bayes estimators for AL and linex losses are standard and correct. The two-pass hyperparameter optimization and 10-fold cross-validation are reasonable for small QE datasets.

- Practical decision-making angle: Mapping realistic scenarios (pessimistic post-editing vs optimistic gisting) to asymmetric losses and showing that a single probabilistic model can support multiple operational policies without retraining is a valuable, actionable insight for QE deployment.

- Evidence that distributional modeling matters beyond point accuracy: The paper shows that while MAE/ρ change little, uncertainty-aware models (especially Warped GPs) materially improve NLPD and performance under asymmetric losses, supporting the central thesis.

- Applicability beyond QE: The approach generalizes to other text regression tasks with positive-valued or skewed targets, making the contribution relevant to a broader NLP audience.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling; contributions are mainly empirical/advocacy: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, and asymmetric Bayes risk are established. The paper’s novelty lies in applying and assessing them in QE; methodological innovation is minimal. This can be acceptable if the empirical study is thorough, but see next points.

- Incomplete comparative baselines: There is no comparison to alternative uncertainty methods commonly used in practice, e.g., quantile regression (pinball loss) for AL scenarios, Bayesian linear regression, bootstrapped SVR/ensembles, or modern neural uncertainty methods. Without these, it is hard to assess how competitive GPs are in uncertainty quality for QE.

- Calibration analysis is shallow: NLPD is informative but does not decompose sharpness vs calibration. There are no calibration diagnostics (reliability diagrams, PIT histograms, CRPS). The puzzling linex results in pessimistic settings are attributed to lower variance in warped models, but this is not backed by calibration analyses. This weakens the conclusion about when to prefer which model under asymmetric risk.

- Narrow feature/modeling setting and dated QE setup: Experiments use only 17 QuEst features and classical GP regressors. Stronger point-estimate baselines (larger feature sets, neural QE models) are not considered. While the focus is uncertainty, stronger baselines would test whether GP-based uncertainty gains persist when point prediction is competitive.

- Reporting and statistical rigor: The paper references Tables and Figures but does not present statistical significance tests, confidence intervals, or effect sizes. Claims such as overfitting deduced from NLL vs NLPD on en-es would benefit from quantitative evidence (e.g., learning curves, variance of estimates).

Questions to authors
- Can you report calibration diagnostics (e.g., calibration curves/PIT histograms, CRPS) to support the interpretation of the linex behavior and to assess whether improvements in NLPD come from better calibration, better sharpness, or both?

- How do the GP models compare to a quantile regression baseline for AL (e.g., gradient boosting quantile regressor) and to a simple Bayesian linear regression? This would isolate the benefits of GP function priors vs generic probabilistic baselines.

- What are the distributions of predictive variances across models/datasets? Do warped GPs consistently reduce variance, and does this correlate with linex outcomes?

- Could you provide sensitivity analyses for the warping complexity (number of tanh terms), and for kernel ARD vs isotropic settings, possibly with regularization to mitigate overfitting on small datasets?

- How robust are results to using more features or modern QE representations (e.g., pretrained sentence embeddings)? Does the uncertainty advantage persist?

Overall assessment
The paper is well-motivated, methodologically sound, and clearly written. Its primary value is to foreground uncertainty-aware evaluation and decision-making in QE, with concrete, reproducible GP-based models. However, the lack of broader baselines and deeper calibration analysis limits the strength of its empirical conclusions and generality. I view it as a solid, incremental contribution with practical relevance, more on the side of empirical study and guidance than methodological novelty.
