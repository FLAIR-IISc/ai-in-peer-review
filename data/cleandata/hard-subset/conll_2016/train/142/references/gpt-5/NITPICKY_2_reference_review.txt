Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper advocates for probabilistic quality estimation (QE) in MT, focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs) to obtain calibrated predictive distributions rather than point estimates. It evaluates: (i) kernel choices (EQ vs. Matérn), (ii) Warped GPs to model positive-valued labels, (iii) evaluation via Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), and (iv) decision-making under asymmetric losses using Bayes risk estimators (AL and linex).
- Main contributions as I see them:
  1) Empirical study showing that Warped GPs substantially improve probabilistic fit (NLL/NLPD) over standard GPs in sentence-level QE, and that point-metrics (MAE, Pearson r) can miss uncertainty differences.
  2) Demonstration of decision rules under asymmetric risk via Bayes estimators derived from predictive posteriors (quantile estimator for AL; closed-form estimator for linex), with qualitative observations about pessimistic vs optimistic settings.
  3) Modest exploration of kernel choice (Matérn vs EQ) and cross-lingual datasets using a standard QE feature set.

Strengths
- Focus on uncertainty and calibrated predictive distributions is timely and important for QE, where decisions (post-editing vs discard) are downstream and risk-sensitive. Using NLPD as an evaluation metric is appropriate and underused in QE.
- Warped GPs for positive-valued labels are a sensible, principled choice; the paper correctly notes distributional mismatch of Gaussian likelihoods over R for time/rates. The finding that warping materially improves NLL/NLPD is plausible and useful in practice.
- The decision-theoretic framing and Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses are well-motivated; showing how to adapt predictions without retraining is operationally valuable for changing business constraints.
- Multi-dataset evaluation (en–es, fr–en, en–de) with 10-fold CV and a consistent, widely-used feature set (QuEst 17) provides some robustness and comparability, decoupling the uncertainty study from feature engineering.
- The paper underscores that MAE/Pearson r do not capture probabilistic quality; advocating for NLPD in QE evaluation is a constructive message for the community.

Weaknesses
- Substantial mathematical inaccuracies/typos in core equations:
  • EQ and Matérn kernels: definitions misuse r vs r^2 and omit constants (e.g., standard EQ commonly exp(-0.5 r^2); Matérn 3/2 and 5/2 forms require r = ||x−x' ||/l, not r^2). The printed k_M52 expression is incorrect.
  • Warped GP predictive density: exponent lacks the square and negative sign; the formula as written is not a valid Gaussian log-density times Jacobian. These errors undermine technical soundness and reproducibility.
  • NLPD notation p(ŷi = yi|xi) is non-standard/confusing; it should be p(yi | xi, D_train).
- Conceptual inconsistency in asymmetric loss setup:
  • The AL loss is defined with weight w on overestimates only, but the text states “increase penalisation weight for underestimates” for the pessimistic scenario and then equates pessimistic with w>1, which contradicts the definition. This misalignment propagates to interpretation of results and scenarios.
  • Linex discussion partially addresses directionality (sign of w), but there is no consistent mapping between loss parameterization and scenario definitions across AL and linex.
- Limited novelty and incomplete baselines:
  • Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, and asymmetric Bayes estimators are established. The novelty is primarily applying them to QE; however, prior QE GP work exists. Missing comparisons to strong relevant baselines that can produce uncertainty/asymmetric decisions: quantile regression (e.g., SVR-quantile), calibrated gradient boosting (NGBoost), Bayesian NNs/probabilistic deep QE, ensemble bootstrap methods with calibration checks.
- Insufficient evaluation of calibration and decision quality:
  • NLPD is reported, but there are no calibration diagnostics (PIT histograms, reliability diagrams, coverage of central credible intervals, CRPS). Claims about “well-calibrated” are not substantiated.
  • The “asymmetric risk” section evaluates surrogate losses, not task-level utility. No operational metrics (accept/reject curves, cost–benefit trade-offs, coverage vs efficiency) are provided for the post-editing/gisting examples.
- Reporting and reproducibility gaps:
  • Tables and figures are referenced but not included; effect sizes and variances are unclear. No CIs or significance tests across folds.
  • Hyperparameter details (priors/constraints for warping to ensure monotonicity, optimization settings, stopping criteria), software, seeds, and code/data availability are missing.
  • The two-pass optimization description is vague; potential overfitting is discussed but not systematically controlled (no validation-based early stopping, no complexity penalties, no sensitivity analyses).
- Formatting and editorial quality:
  • Numerous typos/grammar issues (“remaining of this paper,” “predicitive,” inconsistent hyphenation), inconsistent notation (“Matèrn”), stray footnotes/URLs in body text, and many malformed references with missing years, escapes, and inconsistent styles. This does not meet the standard of a polished submission.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Please correct and clarify all kernel and Warped GP density equations with consistent notation (r vs r^2), and provide the exact forms used in code. How do you enforce monotonicity of the tanh-sum warp (parameter constraints)?
- Resolve the AL loss scenario inconsistency: which direction (over vs underestimates) is penalized more in your pessimistic and optimistic settings? Recompute any affected interpretations if needed.
- Provide calibration analyses: coverage of 50/80/95% predictive intervals, PIT/reliability plots, and CRPS. Are Warped GPs actually better calibrated, beyond lower NLPD?
- Baselines: Can you add quantile regression (SVR-quantile), NGBoost, and a calibrated ensemble baseline? For asymmetric losses, compare to models trained directly for the loss (e.g., quantile reg) rather than only Bayes estimators.
- Decision outcomes: For a post-editing filter, show utility curves (acceptance rate vs error cost) using your predictive distributions. Do your models improve utility at fixed risk?
- Report fold-wise means/SEs and significance. Confirm that hyperparameters were tuned only on training folds. Provide code or sufficient details for reproduction.

Overall, while the topic is important and the direction is valuable, the paper in its current form suffers from technical inaccuracies, conceptual inconsistencies, weak baselines, and incomplete evaluation of calibration and decision impact.
