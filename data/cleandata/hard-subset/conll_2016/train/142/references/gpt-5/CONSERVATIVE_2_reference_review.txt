Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in sentence-level QE via Gaussian Processes (GPs), contrasting with point-estimate models. It evaluates kernels (EQ vs Matérn) and output transformations (Warped GPs with tanh and log warpings).
- It promotes Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as an evaluation metric that captures uncertainty quality beyond MAE/Pearson’s r, and reports that warped GPs improve NLL/NLPD.
- It demonstrates how to use predictive distributions to make decisions under asymmetric loss (AL and linex) without retraining, via Bayes risk estimators.

In my view, the primary contribution is an empirical study applying established probabilistic tools (GPs, warping, Matérn kernels, NLPD, Bayes estimators) to QE and highlighting differences that point metrics miss. A secondary contribution is a small-scale exploration of asymmetric risk decision rules in this context.

Strengths
- Clear focus on uncertainty with proper scoring: The paper emphasizes NLPD on test data (a proper scoring rule) and shows that it can distinguish models with similar MAE/r. This is methodologically sound and relevant for QE where uncertainty matters in downstream decisions.
- Sensible modeling choices grounded in prior work: Comparing EQ vs Matérn and standard vs warped GPs is reasonable, with ARD lengthscales and 10-fold CV. The observation that warping reduces NLL/NLPD (due to positive support) is expected and useful to document in QE.
- Decision-theoretic framing for asymmetric risk: Using Bayes estimators for AL and linex to adapt predictions to optimistic/pessimistic scenarios without retraining is practical and well-motivated. This could be beneficial in production settings where risk preferences vary.
- Multi-dataset evaluation: Experiments on three language pairs with consistent protocol is a plus relative to single-dataset studies.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and incremental contribution: All techniques used (GPs, Matérn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, AL/linex losses and Bayes estimators) are standard and well-known. The work primarily repackages established methods for QE without introducing new models, algorithms, or evaluative methodology specific to QE. For a ConLL venue, either stronger novelty or substantially broader/stronger empirical evidence would be expected.
- Missing or weak baselines for uncertainty and decisions: There is no comparison against competitive non-GP baselines that can produce uncertainty or quantiles (e.g., SVR with quantile regression, quantile/random forests, gradient boosting with quantile loss, calibrated ensembles, Bayesian NNs/MC dropout). Likewise, no comparison to recent QE systems (even with the same 17 QuEst features) is reported. The stated goal is not to improve SOTA, but without strong baselines it is hard to judge the utility of the proposed approach.
- Insufficient calibration analysis: The paper asserts “well-calibrated” uncertainty but only reports NLPD. There are no calibration diagnostics (PIT histograms, reliability diagrams, interval coverage vs nominal, sharpness), no interval coverage metrics, nor CRPS. The pessimistic linex results are inconclusive, yet the explanation is speculative and not backed by calibration evidence (e.g., variance miscalibration).
- Incomplete empirical detail and statistical rigor: The manuscript references Tables 1 and 2 but (in the version provided) does not show the numbers. Effect sizes, variance across folds, and statistical significance are not reported. The asymmetric experiments rely on a few hand-picked w values, and linex results can explode, limiting interpretability. The choice of median as the fixed point estimator seems arbitrary given the discussion; a quantitative comparison of mean vs median vs MAP under different losses is absent.
- Limited practical validation and ablations: The asymmetric risk study is synthetic and not tied to a concrete decision protocol (e.g., selective prediction/abstention, risk–coverage curves, utility/cost curves in post-editing triage). No ablations disentangle support-compatibility (positive outputs) from warping flexibility (e.g., truncated Gaussian, log-GP baseline vs multi-tanh). The choice of small, hand-crafted 17-feature sets is understandable, but it weakens claims about general usefulness; results with stronger features (e.g., standard QE resources or modern sentence embeddings) would be more convincing. No analysis of computational cost/stability of warped GPs (non-convex optimization with restarts) is provided.

Questions to authors
- Calibration: Can you report calibration diagnostics beyond NLPD (e.g., prediction interval coverage at multiple nominal levels, PIT histograms, reliability diagrams, CRPS)? How do standard and warped GPs compare on calibration vs sharpness?
- Baselines: Can you include comparisons to (i) SVR/GBDT quantile regression, (ii) random/quantile forests, and (iii) a simple calibrated ensemble (bagging) to assess whether the GP advantages persist? For deep baselines, a modest MC-dropout regressor would be informative.
- Ablations: How much of the NLL/NLPD gain is simply due to enforcing positive support? Please include a log-GP baseline and, if feasible, a truncated Gaussian likelihood. Also compare mean vs median vs MAP predictions under different losses.
- Asymmetric risk in practice: Can you provide decision curves (e.g., risk–coverage or utility vs threshold) for a realistic triage task (accept for PE vs discard) rather than only average loss with fixed w? Do uncertainty-aware decisions improve downstream metrics (e.g., proportion of bad segments forwarded)?
- Optimization and cost: Please detail optimization settings (initializations, restarts, convergence criteria), runtime/memory, and robustness of warped GPs across folds/datasets. Did you observe overfitting with multi-tanh warpings on small data (en–es)? Any regularization used?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, processed features, splits, and scripts to compute NLPD and asymmetric estimators?

Overall, while the topic is worthwhile and the framing is solid, the paper in its current form feels incremental and empirically under-substantiated against established alternatives. Strengthening baselines, calibration analysis, and downstream decision-oriented validation would significantly improve its impact.
