Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper advocates for probabilistic quality estimation (QE) for MT, focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs), including Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, to produce calibrated predictive distributions and uncertainty estimates. It proposes evaluating models using negative log predictive density (NLPD) and explores decision-making with asymmetric loss functions via Bayes risk estimators.
- Contributions as I see them:
  1) An empirical study highlighting the utility of NLPD for QE and showing that uncertainty-aware evaluation can differentiate models with similar point estimates.
  2) An application of Warped GPs to strictly positive QE targets, and a (limited) comparison of Matérn vs RBF kernels in low-data QE settings.
  3) A demonstration of asymmetric-risk decoding (AL and linex losses) via Bayes estimators without retraining, as a way to tailor predictions to different application risks.

Strengths
- Timely motivation: Uncertainty is genuinely important for QE workflows (e.g., post-editing triage), and the paper makes a clear case for moving beyond point estimates.
- Use of proper scoring rules: Adopting NLPD (log predictive density) is appropriate for probabilistic regression and can reveal differences not captured by MAE or Pearson’s r.
- Warped GP rationale: Modeling positivity and non-Gaussian observation behavior via Warped GPs is well motivated for post-editing time/rate, and the discussion of median vs mean point predictions is correct for skewed posteriors.
- Low-data suitability: GPs (with ARD) are well matched to the dataset sizes typical in QE, and the two-pass ARD procedure is sensible.
- Asymmetric risk framing: Connecting QE decisions to AL/linex losses and Bayes-risk decoding is a useful perspective for operational settings.

Weaknesses
- Missing core empirical evidence: The manuscript references Table 1, Table 2, and Figure 1, but they are not provided. Without numerical results or plots, the central claims (NLPD gains, Matérn impact, warping benefits, asymmetric loss performance) cannot be verified. This alone prevents thorough assessment.
- Mathematical inaccuracies and notational errors:
  • EQ kernel is written ambiguously; typical form is σ² exp(−r²/2) or with length-scales; your expression omits constants and clarity on scaling.  
  • Matérn kernels appear mis-specified: r is usually distance, but you define r² and then plug r² into Matérn forms; the Matérn-5/2 polynomial terms are incorrect (standard form uses r and includes 5r²/3).  
  • Warped GP change-of-variables density lacks the correct Gaussian exponent: the exponent should be −(f(y*) − μ*)²/(2σ*²), with the negative sign and squared numerator; as written it is dimensionally and mathematically incorrect.  
  These are not minor typos; they cast doubt on implementation correctness and reproducibility.
- Bayes estimators under asymmetric loss for non-Gaussian posteriors: The closed-form linex estimator ŷ = μ − wσ²/2 is optimal for Gaussian predictive posteriors. Warped GP predictive posteriors in observation space are non-Gaussian; the claimed estimator is not generally optimal. The paper does not explain how μy and σy² are computed for the warped observation distribution, nor justify the Gaussian assumption. For AL, quantile decoding is correct, but details on computing quantiles from Warped GP posteriors (numerics, accuracy) are missing.
- Baselines and calibration analysis are insufficient:
  • No SVR baseline (a de facto QE standard) is reported, even for point accuracy. The argument that SVR lacks uncertainty does not obviate the need to show that probabilistic models do not harm point performance.  
  • No modern baselines (e.g., Bayesian linear models, heteroscedastic GPs, Student-t likelihoods) or simple ensembling approaches for uncertainty are included.  
  • Calibration is not assessed beyond NLPD. Reliability diagrams, PIT histograms, interval coverage, and sharpness/coverage trade-offs are standard diagnostics for predictive uncertainty.
- Experimental design gaps:
  • Targets: post-editing rate can be zero; log-warping is undefined at zero, but the paper does not mention any offset/epsilon or preprocessing.  
  • Loss scaling: Linex losses explode on en-de (10^7), suggesting output scales are not normalized. Without target rescaling, comparisons across datasets and weights are hard to interpret.  
  • The choice of linex/AL weights (w in {3, 1/3}, etc.) appears arbitrary and not tied to real utility or costs; sensitivity analyses are limited.  
  • Point estimates for Pearson’s r are based on medians (optimal for L1) rather than means (optimal for L2), confounding interpretation of r and RMSE-style behavior.  
  • No runtime/complexity comparisons or discussion of optimization stability for Warped GPs (number of parameters, initializations, convergence diagnostics).
- Writing and formatting issues:
  • Numerous typos and inconsistencies: “postediting” vs “post-editing,” “Matèrn,” dangling footnotes (“Also known as RBF”), missing parentheses/superscripts.  
  • References are poorly formatted with missing years, odd escapes, and inconsistent author names (e.g., “Callison.burch,” “Nguyen and O.Connor,” “Beck et al.,? \Q2014\E”).  
  • Several claims rely on “we believe” without quantitative support; and there is no code/appendix to ensure reproducibility.

Questions to authors
- Please provide the missing tables and figure. What are the exact numerical values (mean±std over folds) for NLL, NLPD, MAE, r in Table 1, and the asymmetric loss results in Table 2?
- Can you correct and clarify the kernel and Warped GP density equations, and confirm the implementation uses the correct forms (especially the Matérn-5/2 and the Gaussian exponent in the change-of-variables)?
- For linex decoding: how do you compute μy and σy² for Warped GP predictive posteriors in observation space? If the posterior is non-Gaussian, why is the Gaussian-optimal estimator appropriate?
- How did you handle zeros in post-editing rates for the log warping? Was an offset used?
- Why is there no SVR baseline? Can you add it (and possibly a heteroscedastic GP or Student-t likelihood) to contextualize point and uncertainty performance?
- Did you assess calibration (e.g., coverage of predictive intervals, reliability diagrams)? If not, can you add these diagnostics?
- How were I (number of tanh terms) and other hyperparameters selected? Any safeguards against overfitting given small n? What were run times and convergence criteria?

Overall recommendation: Reject
Rationale: The topic is important and the high-level idea (uncertainty-aware QE with proper scoring rules and asymmetric-risk decoding) is worthwhile. However, the submission has critical mathematical and formatting errors, missing results tables/figure, gaps in methodology (especially around linex decoding for non-Gaussian posteriors), weak baselines, and insufficient calibration analysis. These issues collectively undermine confidence in the correctness and significance of the findings. With corrected math, complete empirical results, stronger baselines, and thorough calibration evaluation, a future version could be much stronger.
