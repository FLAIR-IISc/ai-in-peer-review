Summary
This paper investigates predictive uncertainty for machine translation quality estimation (QE), arguing that point estimates (MAE/RMSE/r) are insufficient for downstream decisions. The authors adopt Gaussian Processes (GPs) as probabilistic models, propose using Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better handle non-smooth functions and positive-valued targets (post-editing time), and advocate Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) for evaluation. They further demonstrate how predictive distributions can support asymmetric risk scenarios via Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric linear (AL) and linex losses. Experiments on three QE datasets with 10-fold CV suggest Warped GPs improve NLL/NLPD (and sometimes MAE/r), and can be advantageous under certain asymmetric losses.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical study showing the value of predictive distributions in QE, with NLPD revealing differences that point metrics miss. This includes evidence that Warped GPs meaningfully reduce NLL/NLPD by avoiding probability mass on negative times.
2) Application of asymmetric decision-making for QE via Bayes risk estimators, demonstrating how to adapt predictions without retraining when preferences change (optimistic/pessimistic settings).
3) Evaluation of kernel/likelihood design choices (Matérn vs EQ; parametric warps vs log) for uncertainty-aware QE.

Strengths
- Focus on uncertainty is timely and relevant: The paper clearly motivates why uncertainty matters for QE workflows (e.g., post-editing triage), moving beyond MAE/r. The adoption of NLPD aligns with best practices for probabilistic evaluation, and the study convincingly shows that changes in uncertainty modelling are not captured by point metrics.
- Well-motivated modelling choices: Using Warped GPs for positive-valued targets is principled and reduces pathologies (negative mass). The paper discusses mean vs median predictors and the implications of non-Gaussian posteriors, showing understanding of loss-optimal decision rules.
- Decision-theoretic framing: The Bayes risk perspective is a practical contribution—allowing adaptation to changing operational losses without retraining. The link between AL/linex losses and posterior quantiles/moments is clearly leveraged.
- Experimental protocol and clarity: 10-fold CV across three language pairs; consistent feature set; succinct and readable exposition. The discussion of NLL vs NLPD (overfitting on small data) is insightful and honest.
- Useful qualitative insights: The observed variance effect under linex in pessimistic settings (warped models’ lower variance leading to harsher penalties) raises interesting points about interaction between loss and uncertainty calibration.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior GP work: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, and NLPD are well-established. The primary novelty lies in applying these to QE and framing asymmetric decision-making. For a conference venue, the methodological advance is incremental. The work would benefit from stronger algorithmic or analytical contributions (e.g., heteroscedastic or log-normal likelihoods tailored to QE; structured kernels capturing linguistic hierarchy; multi-task/shared structure across language pairs).
- Sparse baseline comparisons: No comparison to alternative uncertainty approaches beyond GPs (e.g., quantile regression with SVR/Gradient Boosting; Bayesian linear models; heteroscedastic or Student-t GPs; log-normal GPs; modern deep probabilistic baselines such as MC dropout or deep ensembles). This makes it difficult to assess whether GPs are indeed preferable for QE uncertainty, or whether the Warped GP advantage is specific to the chosen baseline.
- Incomplete uncertainty evaluation: NLPD is a proper scoring rule, but calibration diagnostics are missing. Suggested additions include: coverage of prediction intervals (e.g., 50/90% interval coverage vs expected), PIT histograms or calibration curves, sharpness vs calibration trade-offs, CRPS as a complementary proper score. These would substantiate claims about “well-calibrated” distributions and clarify the variance-related behavior observed with the linex loss.
- Asymmetric risk experiments feel limited: The scenarios are synthetic with fixed w values, and evidence is mixed (particularly for linex, pessimistic). There is no user-centered or utility-grounded evaluation (e.g., selective prediction/deferral curves for post-editing triage, cost–benefit analyses), nor a thorough sensitivity analysis across a range of w with statistical testing. Conclusions about preferring standard GPs under linex pessimism remain speculative without calibration analysis.
- Scope and generality: Only 17 QuEst features and post-editing time as the sole label are used; no experiments with other QE targets commonly used (e.g., HTER/DA), or larger/modern feature representations (e.g., pretrained sentence embeddings). While the paper positions itself as not chasing SOTA on point accuracy, broader coverage would improve generality. Also, using small datasets magnifies overfitting and makes the NLL vs NLPD discussion harder to generalize.

Questions to Authors
- Calibration: Can you report interval coverage, calibration curves/PIT histograms, and CRPS to assess calibration and sharpness? Do Warped GPs improve calibration relative to standard GPs/log-warp variants?
- Alternative likelihoods: Did you try a log-normal GP (i.e., modeling log-time with Gaussian noise) or a Student-t likelihood to address heavy tails, versus general warping? How do they compare on NLPD/CRPS and asymmetric risk?
- Heteroscedasticity: Post-editing time variance likely depends on features (e.g., length, complexity). Have you explored heteroscedastic GPs or input-dependent noise? Would that mitigate the variance issues seen under linex?
- Baselines: How do quantile regression baselines (e.g., gradient boosting quantile regression) and simple bootstrapped ensembles compare on NLPD/CRPS and asymmetric losses?
- Practical decision tasks: Can you add a selective prediction experiment (accept/route-to-post-edit vs reject) to show how uncertainty improves utility (e.g., productivity or error rates) as a function of coverage? This aligns directly with your motivating scenarios.
- Robustness: How sensitive are results to the number of warp terms, hyperpriors/regularization, and kernel ARD? Any evidence of overfitting in learned warps (visualizations)? Statistical significance across folds?

Recommendation
Weak reject. The paper makes a solid case for uncertainty-aware QE and provides a clear, careful empirical study with useful insights. However, methodological novelty is limited, baselines are narrow, and uncertainty calibration and decision-level evaluations are underdeveloped. Strengthening calibration analysis, adding competitive probabilistic baselines, and demonstrating concrete decision gains in a realistic triage/abstention setup would substantially improve the paper and could move it to acceptance.
