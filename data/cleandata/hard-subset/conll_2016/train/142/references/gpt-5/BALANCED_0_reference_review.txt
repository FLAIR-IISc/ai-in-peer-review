Summary
This paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs). It introduces two GP design choices for QE—Matérn kernels and Warped GPs—and proposes evaluating models with Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) in addition to standard point metrics. It further demonstrates how predictive distributions can support asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., optimistic vs. pessimistic decisions) via Bayes-risk optimal estimators without retraining. Experiments on three sentence-level post-editing time datasets (en-es, fr-en, en-de) with 10-fold CV suggest Warped GPs improve NLL/NLPD and that point metrics often fail to distinguish uncertainty quality.

Contributions (as I see them, in order of strength)
1) Empirical evidence and methodology for evaluating QE models with NLPD, showing that point metrics can miss important differences in uncertainty quality.
2) Application of Warped GPs (tanh-based and log warping) and Matérn kernels to QE, with analyses showing improved uncertainty handling (e.g., positivity support) and benefits on larger datasets.
3) Demonstration of using predictive posteriors for asymmetric losses (AL and linex), providing Bayes-risk estimators to adapt to changing risk profiles without retraining.

Strengths
- Important problem framing: The paper convincingly motivates why uncertainty estimates matter in QE workflows (post-editing and gisting), where wrong but confident predictions can be costly. The focus on probabilistic modeling and calibration is timely and relevant beyond QE.
- Sound probabilistic evaluation: Using NLPD as a primary metric is appropriate and well-argued. The results show substantial improvements for Warped GPs in NLL/NLPD, while MAE and Pearson’s r often fail to reflect differences in uncertainty modeling—supporting the central thesis.
- Sensible GP modeling choices: Warped GPs resolve positivity constraints on post-editing rates and reduce spurious probability mass on negatives. ARD and Matérn kernels are reasonable choices for noisy, low-data QE. The two-pass optimization with restarts is practical and reproducible.
- Risk-aware decision perspective: The Bayes-risk estimators for asymmetric losses are a practical contribution, allowing adaptation to changing operational requirements without retraining. The optimistic setting results are promising and illustrate how uncertainty informs actionable decisions.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, and asymmetric losses are established techniques. The contribution is largely an application and synthesis for QE. While valuable, methodological novelty is modest.
- Incomplete baselines: The paper primarily compares standard GP vs. Warped GP variants. Missing comparisons to (a) non-probabilistic strong QE baselines (e.g., SVR, recent neural regressors) for point performance, and (b) probabilistic alternatives (e.g., heteroscedastic GPs, Student-t likelihood GPs, quantile regression, quantile random forests, Bayesian linear models, Bayesian NNs/deep ensembles). Without these, it is hard to contextualize the gains in uncertainty quality.
- Calibration not directly assessed: While NLPD is a proper scoring rule, the paper does not analyze calibration explicitly (e.g., reliability diagrams/PIT histograms, predictive interval coverage, CRPS). Claims about “well-calibrated uncertainty estimates” would be stronger with formal calibration diagnostics and coverage vs. sharpness analyses.
- Mixed asymmetric-loss evidence: The pessimistic linex results underperform standard GPs, and the explanation (variance dependence) is plausible but speculative. This weakens the claim that distributional modeling consistently enables better risk-sensitive decisions; an end-to-end decision simulation (e.g., selective acceptance/rejection with utility curves) would better substantiate practical benefits.
- Statistical rigor and analysis depth: No significance testing across folds; kernel effects are inconclusive without deeper ablation; warping complexity (number of tanh terms) selection not validated beyond a recommendation; small-data overfitting is observed but not mitigated (e.g., priors/regularization). The evaluation uses a small, old feature set; this limits generality to current QE pipelines.

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- How did you ensure the warping function is monotonic? Were constraints on the tanh sum parameters enforced during optimization? Any regularization or priors to prevent pathological warps?
- How was the number of tanh terms selected (I=1..3)? Did you compare models by marginal likelihood or via a validation metric? Any evidence that I=3 avoids overfitting on the smaller en-es dataset?
- For AL estimators in the warped model, how were quantiles computed (numeric inversion details, tolerances, robustness)? Similarly, for the mean under the warped posterior, what quadrature settings were used, and how sensitive are results?
- Can you report calibration metrics: predictive interval coverage (e.g., 80%/95%), PIT histograms, or CRPS? This would directly test the “well-calibrated” claim.
- Did you try alternative likelihoods (Student-t), or heteroscedastic GPs? These could address heavy tails/noise and may clarify the pessimistic-linex behavior.
- How do results change with richer features (e.g., QuEst++ full set or simple neural representations)? Are the uncertainty improvements robust to feature changes?
- Can you include significance tests across folds and runtime/convergence details (number of restarts, failures, sensitivity to initialization)?

Overall assessment
The paper makes a clear, useful case for modeling and evaluating uncertainty in QE, and presents a coherent application of established probabilistic tools. However, novelty is limited and the empirical evaluation lacks broader baselines and explicit calibration analysis. I encourage a revision that adds stronger probabilistic baselines, calibration diagnostics, statistical tests, and a more realistic decision-theoretic evaluation. My current recommendation is weak reject, with the caveat that the work is promising and could be impactful with a more comprehensive empirical study.
