Summary and main contributions
This paper argues for modelling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE). It instantiates Gaussian Process (GP) regression with (i) Matérn kernels and (ii) Warped GPs to better handle non-Gaussian, positive targets (post-editing time/rate), and evaluates models with Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD). It further demonstrates a decision-theoretic use of predictive distributions under asymmetric risk via Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses (asymmetric linear and linex).

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A systematic uncertainty-centric evaluation of QE models using a proper scoring rule (NLPD), showing that point metrics (MAE, r) mask important differences in predictive distributions.
2) Application and empirical assessment of Warped GPs (and Matérn kernels) for QE, with evidence that learned monotone warpings materially improve log-scores and enable better handling of positive-valued targets.
3) A decision-theoretic framing for QE deployment via Bayes estimators under asymmetric losses, avoiding retraining when preferences change.

Strengths
- Clear motivation for uncertainty in QE and decision-making: The paper articulates concrete downstream scenarios (post-editing vs gisting) where uncertainty is necessary, not optional. The use of Bayes estimators to adapt decisions without retraining is practical and well-motivated.

- Sound probabilistic modelling choices for small-data QE: GPs with ARD and Matérn kernels are appropriate for noisy, low-data regimes typical in QE. Warped GPs are a principled way to put support on R+ without ad-hoc truncation, and the paper explains inference and predictions (median vs mean) clearly.

- Use of a proper scoring rule: NLPD is a standard, strictly proper score for continuous predictive distributions. The empirical finding that NLL (train) can be misleading and that NLPD (test) reveals differences unseen by MAE/Pearson’s r is important for the QE community.

- Decision-theoretic evaluation under asymmetric risk: Deriving and applying Bayes estimators for AL and linex losses is useful and shows how calibrated posteriors allow flexible post-hoc adaptation to changing utility/risk preferences. The observation that linex pessimistic settings penalize lower-variance warped models is interesting and actionable.

- Experimental protocol and breadth: Three language pairs, 10-fold CV, and a consistent feature set make trends more trustworthy. The two-pass kernel training with multiple restarts is a reasonable approach for GP hyperparameter optimization.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Warped GPs and Matérn kernels are standard in GP literature; applying them to QE and advocating NLPD is valuable but incremental. Related alternatives more tailored to R+ and heavy-tailed noise (e.g., log-normal or Gamma likelihoods with GP mean; Student-t likelihoods; heteroscedastic GPs) are not compared. This limits the strength of the modeling contribution.

- Calibration assessment is incomplete: The paper repeatedly appeals to “well-calibrated” uncertainty but evaluates primarily with NLPD. It lacks complementary calibration diagnostics (e.g., predictive interval coverage, sharpness vs calibration, PIT histograms, calibration plots for continuous outcomes) and proper scores like CRPS. Without these, claims about calibration are under-supported.

- Asymmetric loss experiments are self-referential and thin: Performance is reported under the same loss used to derive the estimator, which by construction favors the approach. There is no downstream, task-level evaluation (e.g., post-edit productivity proxy, filtering precision/recall trade-offs conditioned on uncertainty). The puzzling linex behavior is hypothesized but not investigated (e.g., via variance calibration, temperature scaling, or comparing to models with positive support but higher variance).

- Potential inconsistencies/typos and clarity issues: 
  - The AL loss definition places weight w on overestimates, but the text associates “pessimistic” with heavier penalization of underestimates. This needs clarification or a corrected definition/sign convention.
  - The Warped GP predictive density equation seems to omit the squared term in the Gaussian exponent; please check and correct.
  - Some kernel equations are presented with minor inconsistencies (e.g., EQ form), which could confuse readers.

- Baselines and scope: No comparison to quantile regression baselines (e.g., SVR-QR) for asymmetric loss, bagged SVR for uncertainty, Bayesian linear regression with monotone transforms, or modern Bayesian NN/ensemble baselines. Even within GPs, positive-support likelihoods (Gamma/log-normal with log link), heteroscedastic GPs, or Student-t likelihoods are conspicuously absent. Using only 17 QuEst features is understandable for portability, but exploring whether findings hold with stronger feature sets or learned representations would strengthen claims about generality.

Questions to authors
1) Clarify the asymmetric linear loss and the mapping to “pessimistic” vs “optimistic.” As defined, L(ŷ,y)=w(ŷ−y) if ŷ>y and y−ŷ otherwise weights overestimates. Yet the text says pessimistic increases penalization of underestimates. Which is intended? Please align definitions, scenarios, and w choices.

2) Calibration: Can you report predictive interval coverage (e.g., 50%/90% intervals), PIT histograms, and CRPS across models? Do Warped GPs actually calibrate better than standard GPs on these diagnostics?

3) Positive-support likelihoods: Did you try a GP with a log link and Gaussian noise in log space (equivalently, log-normal on y), Gamma likelihood with log link, or truncated Gaussian? How do they compare to Warped GPs in NLPD and calibration?

4) Robustness to heavy tails/heteroscedasticity: QE noise likely varies with sentence length/complexity. Did you try Student-t likelihoods or heteroscedastic GPs? Could this explain the linex pessimistic behavior?

5) Variance calibration and linex: If the issue is variance being too small for Warped GPs, can simple post-hoc variance scaling (temperature) or Bayesian marginalization over warping parameters mitigate the problem?

6) Model selection and priors: How was the number of tanh terms (I) chosen (fixed to 3, or tuned)? Did you regularize warping parameters with priors to reduce overfitting on en-es? Any sensitivity analysis?

7) Statistical significance: Are the improvements in NLPD/AL significant across folds? Please report mean±std and paired tests.

8) Reproducibility: Will you release code, hyperparameters, and CV splits? This would aid adoption of uncertainty-aware QE.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and timely paper that foregrounds predictive uncertainty in QE and demonstrates practical decision-theoretic benefits. The main limitations are the incremental novelty and the lack of deeper calibration analysis and stronger baselines. Addressing the calibration diagnostics, clarifying the asymmetric loss setup, and expanding baselines would substantially strengthen the work.
