Summary
The paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE) using Gaussian Processes (GPs). It studies GP variants (Matérn kernels and Warped GPs) and evaluates them with Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), claiming that uncertainty-aware metrics reveal differences not captured by MAE/Pearson r. It further shows how predictive posteriors enable decision-making under asymmetric losses (asymmetric linear and linex) via Bayes risk estimators, avoiding retraining when the loss changes.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical study of predictive uncertainty for QE using established GP variants, showing that NLPD can differentiate models with similar point metrics and that NLL can mislead on small data.
2) Practical decision-theoretic framing for QE: post-hoc Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses (AL and linex) using predictive posteriors, illustrating pessimistic/optimistic scenarios without retraining.
3) Application of Warped GPs (monotone tanh warping) and Matérn kernels to QE with sentence-level post-editing time rates.

Strengths
1) Clear motivation and use-case for uncertainty in QE. The paper convincingly explains why point estimates alone are insufficient in workflows (e.g., gating MT for post-editing) and why well-calibrated posteriors are valuable for downstream decisions and risk management.

2) Sensible probabilistic baselines and metrics. Using NLPD on held-out data to assess predictive densities is established and appropriate. The observation that NLL on training can diverge from NLPD on small datasets is a useful, cautionary empirical finding for the QE community.

3) Decision-theoretic treatment of asymmetric risk with Bayes estimators is practical. Showing how to map predictive posteriors to optimizers under AL and linex losses post-hoc is elegant and operationally meaningful, especially when loss preferences change over time and retraining is impractical.

4) Methodological choices are principled within the GP framework. The use of ARD kernels, Matérn vs. EQ, and warped likelihoods for positive response variables are all textbook-correct decisions for noisy, small QE datasets. The two-pass optimization and CV setup are reasonable.

5) Honest reporting of mixed results. The paper does not over-claim: Matérn gains are sometimes inconclusive; pessimistic linex performance is worse for warped models, with a plausible explanation related to variance and penalty structure. Such negative results are informative.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty relative to established literature. Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, and Bayes estimators under AL/linex are all known. The contribution is primarily an application to QE with modest empirical analysis. For a venue like CoNLL, a clearer methodological advance or stronger empirical impact is expected.

2) Missing comparisons to strong non-GP uncertainty baselines and current QE models. The study compares only GP variants to each other. It omits standard alternatives that provide uncertainty and/or asymmetric decisions, e.g., quantile regression (linear or boosted), quantile regression forests, bagged SVR/bootstrapped ensembles, Bayesian ridge/linear models, Student-t or heteroscedastic regression, conformal prediction intervals, and (even lightweight) neural baselines with MC-dropout or deep ensembles. Without such baselines, it is hard to judge the practical advantage of GPs.

3) Insufficient calibration analysis. The paper relies on NLPD but does not assess calibration directly (PIT histograms, reliability diagrams, coverage of prediction intervals, CRPS). Since the core claim is better uncertainty, calibration diagnostics are essential. The reported linex behavior suggests variance misalignment; calibration analysis could illuminate this.

4) Evaluation scope and statistical rigor. Datasets are small (≤2.8k), features are limited to 17 QuEst features, and gains are often modest or mixed. There is no statistical significance testing, no confidence intervals, and no ablations on warping complexity or kernel choices beyond brief remarks. No analysis of sensitivity to the number of tanh terms, priors/regularization for warping parameters, or hyperparameter initialization is provided.

5) Practical impact not validated end-to-end. The asymmetric risk scenarios are motivated, but there is no downstream decision evaluation (e.g., precision/recall for gating, cost curves, translator productivity proxies). Reporting only loss values leaves unclear whether the proposed approach improves real workflow outcomes.

6) Reproducibility and reporting details. Code and exact hyperparameters are not provided. Important choices (e.g., number of warping terms per dataset, priors, convergence criteria, runtime) are not fully specified. Given the risk of overfitting with warped models on small data, such details matter.

Questions to Authors
1) Calibration: Did you evaluate calibration (PIT histograms, reliability diagrams, prediction interval coverage) or CRPS? If not, can you provide these for the key models to substantiate the “well-calibrated” claim?

2) Baselines: Why were standard uncertainty baselines (quantile regression/forests, bagged SVR, Bayesian linear/ridge, Student-t likelihood, heteroscedastic GPs, conformal prediction) omitted? Can you add at least one non-GP uncertainty method and a strong SVR baseline with bagging or quantiles?

3) Warping sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the number of tanh terms and to regularization/priors on warping parameters? Do you observe overfitting or pathological warps on the smallest dataset? Please provide ablations and stability analysis.

4) Statistical significance: Are the NLPD/MAE differences statistically significant across folds? Please report CIs or paired tests and effect sizes.

5) Calibration vs variance for linex: Can you quantify the predictive variance across models and relate it to linex performance? Would a Student-t likelihood or explicit heteroscedastic noise help in the pessimistic regime?

6) Practical evaluation: Can you simulate a gating decision (e.g., thresholding by predictive quantiles) and report precision/recall/utility or cost curves, to demonstrate impact beyond surrogate losses?

7) Reproducibility: Will you release code, data splits, and full hyperparameter settings (including restarts, initializations, convergence, runtime)?

Overall, this is a careful, well-motivated application paper using established probabilistic tools. However, the novelty is limited and the empirical validation lacks strong baselines and calibration analysis. I lean toward rejection unless the authors can substantially strengthen comparisons and calibration/decision evaluations.
