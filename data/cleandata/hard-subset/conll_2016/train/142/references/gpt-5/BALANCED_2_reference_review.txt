Summary
This paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE) and evaluates Gaussian Process (GP) variants that yield full predictive posteriors. The authors (i) replace the standard EQ kernel with Matérn kernels and (ii) apply Warped GPs to respect the positivity and non-Gaussianity of post-editing time. They advocate proper probabilistic evaluation via Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), show that point metrics (MAE, r) miss important differences in uncertainty quality, and demonstrate how predictive posteriors can be exploited to adapt to asymmetric risk using Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear and linex losses, without retraining. Experiments on three small QE datasets with QuEst features show improved NLPD with warping, mixed gains for Matérn kernels, and scenario-dependent behavior under asymmetric losses.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that Warped GPs materially improve probabilistic fit (NLPD) for QE over standard GPs, and that training NLL can mislead on small data relative to test NLPD.
2) A decision-centric demonstration that asymmetric risk can be handled at inference time by applying Bayes risk estimators to the predictive distribution (AL quantiles, linex mean-variance), avoiding retraining.
3) A clear argument and supporting results that point metrics are inadequate for assessing QE models when uncertainty is required; NLPD (and proper scoring) should be used.

Strengths
1) Solid motivation and decision-centric framing. The paper connects QE to realistic workflows (post-editing, gisting), and shows how uncertainty can alter actions via asymmetric losses. The use of Bayes estimators to adapt to changing operational costs without retraining is practical and well-grounded.
2) Proper probabilistic evaluation. Using NLPD to compare calibrated predictive distributions is appropriate; the analysis highlighting discrepancies between NLL (train) and NLPD (test) on small data is useful evidence against relying on training objectives to judge uncertainty quality.
3) Warping for positivity and non-Gaussian targets. Applying Warped GPs to post-editing rates is well-justified; the observed improvements in NLL/NLPD over standard GPs across datasets reinforce the modeling choice.
4) Transparent modeling and experimental protocol. The paper clearly describes kernels, ARD, warping, and optimization, and uses 10-fold cross-validation across three language pairs while keeping features fixed to isolate the effects of uncertainty modeling.
5) Useful qualitative insights. The analysis of why linex with pessimistic weights may favor higher-variance models (standard GP) offers an interesting hypothesis about loss-model interactions that could guide future work.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty. GPs, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs are established; the main contribution is an application-oriented empirical study on uncertainty in QE. As a CoNLL contribution, the methodological novelty is modest, and the experimental scope is relatively narrow.
2) Incomplete baselines for uncertainty. There are no comparisons to alternative probabilistic/uncertainty-capable baselines (e.g., quantile regression via gradient boosting or random forests, heteroscedastic GPs, Bayesian linear/GP with input-dependent noise, deep ensembles/MC dropout). This makes it difficult to assess how much of the benefit is specific to Warped GPs versus any calibrated model.
3) Lack of calibration diagnostics beyond NLPD. While NLPD is a proper score, the paper does not report calibration-focused diagnostics (prediction interval coverage, PIT histograms, reliability curves, CRPS). The linex results suggest under/over-dispersion issues; without diagnostics, the explanation remains speculative.
4) Limited task and decision evaluations. The asymmetric-loss evaluation is simulated and does not connect to actionable QE policies (e.g., triage/forward-or-discard thresholds, coverage-risk curves, expected utility). There is no demonstration that uncertainty improves end-to-end outcomes in a realistic setting.
5) Technical clarity and rigor. Some equations (EQ kernel form) appear mistyped; reference formatting is inconsistent. Model selection and stability details are light (e.g., choice of warping terms I and regularization; numerical stability of inverse warping and quadrature; computational cost). No statistical significance tests are reported; Pearson’s r is sensitive to choice of point estimate (median was used), potentially disadvantaging correlation metrics.

Questions to Authors
- Calibration: Can you report interval coverage (e.g., 50/80/95%), PIT histograms, or CRPS to assess calibration and sharpness directly? Do your models tend to be under- or over-dispersed by these diagnostics?
- Heteroscedasticity: Post-editing time plausibly has input-dependent noise. Did you try heteroscedastic (input-dependent noise) GPs or Warped heteroscedastic GPs? If not, can you comment on expected effects?
- Baselines: How do Warped GPs compare to quantile regression (e.g., gradient boosting quantiles, quantile random forests) and to deep uncertainty methods (e.g., MC dropout, deep ensembles) on NLPD/CRPS?
- Warping details: How did you choose the number of tanh terms (I up to 3)? Any regularization to avoid overfitting on small datasets (en-es)? How many quadrature points were used for mean estimates; any issues with inverse warping numerical stability?
- Decision evaluation: Can you provide triage-oriented analyses (e.g., deferral rates vs expected loss, productivity proxies) to better match post-editing/gisting use cases?
- Significance and variance: Are the improvements in NLPD/AL losses statistically significant across folds? Can you report confidence intervals?
- Computational aspects: What are training/inference times and scaling behavior relative to standard GPs/SVR? Any issues with convergence or sensitivity to initialization?

Overall recommendation
Borderline reject. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and makes a timely case for probabilistic evaluation and asymmetric decision-making in QE. However, methodological novelty is limited, and the empirical study lacks strong uncertainty-aware baselines and calibration diagnostics. Strengthening the evaluation (calibration, heteroscedastic models, broader baselines, decision-centric metrics) and addressing technical clarity would make this a stronger contribution.
