Summary and claimed contributions
This paper argues that uncertainty-aware modeling is valuable for sentence-level Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), and studies Gaussian Process (GP) variants that produce predictive distributions rather than point estimates. It evaluates models with negative log predictive density (NLPD) in addition to standard point metrics and explores downstream decisions via asymmetric loss functions, deriving Bayes estimators from the predictive posterior.

Contributions as I see them:
1) Empirical study of uncertainty modeling for QE with GPs, showing that Warped GPs (positivity-respecting) substantially improve predictive densities (NLPD/NLL) over standard GPs, and that differences in uncertainty are not captured by MAE/Pearson r. This clarifies evaluation practice for uncertainty-aware QE.
2) Demonstration of using predictive posteriors to accommodate asymmetric risk via Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear and linex losses, avoiding retraining when the loss changes; evidence that uncertainty-aware models can yield better performance in optimistic scenarios, and analysis of failure modes in pessimistic linex settings.
3) Smaller methodological additions: investigating Matérn kernels vs EQ in QE; a two-pass ARD optimization; and practical implementation details for Warped GPs in text regression.

Strengths
- Importance and timeliness: The paper squarely addresses a real need in QE workflows—calibrated uncertainty for decision-making (e.g., routing for post-editing vs discard). The case for going beyond point estimates is clearly argued and grounded in realistic scenarios.
- Sound probabilistic framing and metrics: Using GPs to produce full predictive distributions and advocating NLPD as a proper scoring rule for regression is methodologically solid. The evidence that point metrics can miss uncertainty differences is valuable for the community and likely generalizes beyond QE.
- Warped GP modeling for positive targets: Adapting the likelihood support via warping is a principled way to handle strictly positive labels (post-editing rates). The reported consistent gains in NLL/NLPD for warped models over standard GPs are compelling and practically meaningful.
- Asymmetric decision-making: Mapping asymmetric costs to Bayes-optimal estimators from a single predictive posterior is a useful contribution to QE. The experiments, while limited, illustrate how one can tune predictions post hoc for optimistic vs pessimistic use cases without retraining.
- Careful and transparent protocol: The use of three language pairs, 10-fold CV, a controlled feature set, and an explicit two-pass hyperparameter optimization gives a clean testbed to isolate the uncertainty-modeling questions, with some introspection (e.g., NLL vs NLPD discrepancy on small data as overfitting signal).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD evaluation, and asymmetric losses are well-established. The main novelty is their application and analysis in QE. This is fine for a workshop, but borderline for a conference unless the empirical insights are exceptionally deep or the practical impact is demonstrated at scale.
- Calibration not directly assessed: The paper argues for “well-calibrated” uncertainty but evaluates mostly with NLPD. There is no analysis of calibration quality (e.g., coverage of prediction intervals, PIT histograms, reliability curves, CRPS/interval scores). Claims about calibration would be strengthened by such diagnostics.
- Experimental scope and baselines: Only 17 baseline QuEst features are used and compared among GP variants; no comparison to strong non-probabilistic baselines (SVR, gradient boosting) with bagging/ensembles for uncertainty, nor to modern neural QE methods. This limits external validity and makes it hard to claim practical superiority in QE pipelines.
- Asymmetric losses: Some definitions and interpretations appear inconsistent with the literature. The AL loss assigns weight w to overestimates; the optimal quantile is typically τ = 1/(1 + w), not w/(1 + w). For linex, w > 0 penalizes overestimation more than underestimation; the paper states the opposite (negative w is “pessimistic”). This needs correction and may affect the reported conclusions.
- Inconclusive linex results and speculative explanation: The pessimistic linex scenario degrades for warped GPs; the explanation (variance not “pushing” estimates into positive tails) is plausible but untested. No ablations on posterior variance, no heteroscedastic GP baseline, no truncated Gaussian likelihood comparison, and no analysis of tail behavior. This weakens the mechanistic claims.
- Practical impact not quantified: For the motivating post-editing and gisting scenarios, no downstream decision analysis is presented (e.g., acceptance rate vs bad-passage rate curves using uncertainty thresholds, cost-benefit simulations). Without such analysis, it’s hard to judge real-world utility.

Questions to authors
- Please clarify the asymmetric loss definitions and Bayes estimators. For AL with overestimation weight w, shouldn’t the optimal quantile be τ = 1/(1 + w)? For linex, why do you label w < 0 as “pessimistic,” given that w > 0 penalizes overestimation?
- Can you report calibration diagnostics (e.g., 80/90% coverage of predictive intervals, PIT histograms), and CRPS or interval scores, to substantiate calibration claims?
- How sensitive are Warped GP results to the number of tanh terms and regularization? Any evidence of overfitting with I=3 on small en-es?
- Did you consider heteroscedastic GPs or heavy-tailed likelihoods (Student-t) as alternatives to warping? These could address variance/tail issues raised in the linex experiments.
- Can you provide decision-analytic evaluations for post-editing (e.g., threshold rules using posterior mean/quantiles and uncertainty) showing trade-offs in throughput vs error?
- How would results change with stronger features (e.g., pretrained embeddings) or on standard QE labels (e.g., HTER/DA) to demonstrate broader applicability?

Additional comments
- Reproducibility would benefit from code release and precise optimization details (software, seeds, priors/bounds for hyperparameters).
- Consider adding truncated Gaussian likelihoods as a simpler alternative to warping for positive support.
- Some typos/formatting issues in references; please clean for camera-ready.

Overall recommendation
Borderline accept. The paper lacks strong methodological novelty but addresses an important gap in QE, and provides useful empirical guidance on uncertainty-aware modeling and evaluation. With clarifications on asymmetric losses and added calibration/decision analyses, the contribution would be stronger.
