Summary and claimed contributions
This paper argues for uncertainty-aware quality estimation (QE) for machine translation, focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs) to obtain calibrated predictive distributions instead of point estimates. The authors (i) revisit GP modeling choices (Matérn kernels and Warped GPs) for QE with positive, noisy responses (post-editing time per token), (ii) advocate Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as an evaluation metric capturing predictive uncertainty, showing that point metrics fail to distinguish models, and (iii) demonstrate how predictive distributions can be used to derive minimum Bayes risk predictors for asymmetric losses (AL and linex) without retraining, enabling optimistic/pessimistic decision policies.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical study of probabilistic QE using GPs, including Warped GPs, across three language pairs, demonstrating that warping substantially improves NLL/NLPD versus standard GPs and that NLPD surfaces differences missed by MAE/Pearson r.
2) Practical framework for handling asymmetric risk via Bayes estimators from a single predictive distribution, with experiments showing conditions where warped models help (and where linex pessimistic settings can prefer higher-variance models).
3) Analysis of kernel and likelihood choices (Matérn vs EQ; parametric tanh warping vs log warping) tailored to QE’s noisy, positive response.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and relevance: The paper squarely addresses a real pain point in QE deployments: point estimates do not support risk-aware decisions in post-editing/gisting workflows. The asymmetric-loss experiments connect modeling choices to concrete downstream utility.
- Solid probabilistic framing and sensible modeling choices: Using ARD GPs, Matérn kernels to relax smoothness, and Warped GPs for positive, skewed responses is methodologically sound and well aligned with the data characteristics (noisy, heteroskedastic text-derived features; positive time targets).
- Evaluation with a proper scoring rule: NLPD is appropriate for probabilistic regression and the paper convincingly shows that NLL on train can be misleading (overfitting on smaller sets) while NLPD on test captures useful differences. The finding that warping reduces probability mass on implausible negative values and improves NLPD is compelling.
- Practicality of Bayes risk without retraining: The demonstration that one can adapt to different risk profiles (pessimistic vs optimistic) by simply changing the estimator over a fixed predictive distribution is useful in production settings where loss preferences can change over time.
- Transparent discussion of mixed results: The authors candidly analyze why linex in the pessimistic regime can favor higher-variance standard GPs, pointing to an important nuance in loss choice and variance calibration.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines and related comparators: The study contrasts GP variants only. Missing are strong non-GP probabilistic baselines such as quantile regression (linear and gradient-boosted), quantile regression forests, Bayesian linear regression with ARD, deep ensembles/MC-dropout Bayesian NNs (cited), or even calibrated SVR/GBDT with bootstrap or conformal prediction. Including at least one non-GP baseline would strengthen the claim that the benefits stem from uncertainty modeling rather than GP specifics.
- Calibration analysis is incomplete: NLPD alone does not fully reveal calibration quality. The paper lacks coverage tests of prediction intervals, PIT histograms, reliability curves for regression, or proper scoring beyond NLPD (e.g., CRPS). Given the paper’s central thesis on uncertainty, explicit calibration diagnostics would be valuable.
- Likelihood/noise modeling is restrictive: All models assume homoscedastic Gaussian noise in the latent space. QE signals are plausibly heteroscedastic. Alternatives such as heteroscedastic GPs, Student-t likelihoods for robustness, or direct log-normal/Gamma likelihoods over positive targets would be natural comparators to warping. The argument that negative mass is the main issue could be addressed by simpler, interpretable likelihoods without learned warpings.
- Asymmetric-loss experiments stop short of decision-level evaluation: While AL/linex losses are analyzed, there is no simulation of actual filtering/triage decisions (e.g., accept/reject policies with utility curves, precision/recall vs risk trade-offs under uncertainty thresholds). Such a demonstration would better substantiate the practical gains for post-editing/gisting pipelines.
- Scope and representativeness of data/features: The study uses 17 QuEst features and post-editing time only. Contemporary QE often uses richer features or neural encoders and other targets (HTER/DA). Even if state-of-the-art point accuracy is not the goal, the generality of results beyond these datasets and this label is unclear. Additionally, kernel comparisons are inconclusive and effect sizes sometimes small.

Questions to authors
- For the linex Bayes estimator ŷ = μy − w σy^2/2: under Warped GPs the predictive over y is non-Gaussian. How are μy and σy computed? Are they exact moments via quadrature or moment-matched approximations, and how sensitive are results to this approximation?
- Calibration: Can you report coverage of nominal prediction intervals (e.g., 50/80/95%) and PIT histograms for standard vs warped GPs? Do warped models over- or under-cover?
- Why not model positive responses with a parametric likelihood (log-normal, Gamma) instead of — or in addition to — warping? Any comparative results?
- Noise assumptions: Did you try heteroscedastic GPs or Student-t likelihoods? Given the mixed linex results, is variance miscalibration a factor that could be addressed by a heteroscedastic model?
- Warping capacity/regularization: How was the number of tanh terms (I up to 3) selected? Any regularization on warp parameters or priors to prevent pathologies? How stable are learned warpings across CV folds?
- Decision-level evaluation: Can you include a simple accept/reject simulation (e.g., pass to PE if predictive median above threshold and predictive variance below threshold) to quantify utility gains under pessimistic/optimistic settings?
- Baselines: Could you compare against quantile regression (linear and boosted trees) and a lightweight Bayesian NN (e.g., MC dropout) to contextualize GP performance?

Overall, this is a well-motivated paper that advances uncertainty-aware QE with a clean probabilistic treatment and offers useful insights on asymmetric risk. Its impact would be strengthened by broader baselines, explicit calibration diagnostics, and at least a small-scale decision-level evaluation.
