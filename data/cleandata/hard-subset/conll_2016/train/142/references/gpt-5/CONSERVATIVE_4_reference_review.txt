Summary and contributions
This paper revisits Gaussian Process (GP) regression for MT Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on predictive uncertainty. It (i) argues for using probabilistic models and proper scoring rules (NLPD) in QE, (ii) applies established GP variants—Matérn kernels and Warped GPs—to better model non-Gaussian, positive targets (post-editing rates), and (iii) demonstrates how predictive distributions can support asymmetric risk scenarios via Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses (asymmetric linear and linex).

As I see it, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical study showing that Warped GPs substantially improve NLPD over standard GPs in sentence-level QE, and that point-estimate metrics (MAE, r) fail to reflect uncertainty differences.
2) A task-motivated demonstration of using predictive posteriors for asymmetric decision-making without retraining, via Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses.
3) A modest evaluation of kernel choice (EQ vs Matérn) and warping functions (log vs tanh-sum) on three QE datasets.

Strengths
- Clear focus on uncertainty and proper evaluation: The paper correctly emphasizes that MAE/r ignore uncertainty and adopts NLPD, a proper scoring rule widely used for probabilistic regression. The empirical finding that Warped GPs improve NLPD while MAE/r barely change is convincing and relevant to QE pipelines.
- Sensible probabilistic modeling choices grounded in established methods: Warped GPs for positive, skewed targets and Matérn kernels for reduced smoothness assumptions are appropriate, well-motivated choices for noisy QE data. The paper stays within well-understood GP methodology, which aids interpretability.
- Practical framing via asymmetric risks: The formulation of pessimistic/optimistic scenarios and use of Bayes estimators to avoid retraining is useful and task-relevant. The analysis that linex may penalize low-variance models in pessimistic settings is an insightful observation for practitioners.
- Multi-dataset evaluation with cross-validation: Using three language pairs and 10-fold CV is better than a single split and reduces variance in reported results. The consistent improvements from warping on NLPD across datasets support the main claim.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and narrow baselines: The modeling (Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD) is established; the primary novelty is application to QE and the asymmetric demonstration. However, the baselines omit strong, relevant alternatives for uncertainty and asymmetry: heteroscedastic GPs, Student-t likelihood GPs, quantile regression (e.g., quantile SVR/GBM), Bayesian linear/ensemble methods, and modern QE regressors with uncertainty (e.g., calibrated ensembles). Without these, it is hard to assess whether Warped GPs are preferred beyond the specific setup.
- Uncertainty quality not thoroughly validated: NLPD is necessary but not sufficient to establish calibration. There is no assessment of calibration/coverage (e.g., PIT histograms, interval coverage vs nominal, reliability curves), sharpness, or decomposition of over/under-confidence. Claims about “well-calibrated” uncertainty are not substantiated beyond NLPD.
- Method selection and overfitting risk insufficiently controlled: The paper varies kernel (EQ vs Matérn) and warping (log vs tanh with up to 3 terms) but provides little ablation on warping complexity (I=1,2,3), regularization, or nested model selection. With small datasets (n≈0.8–2.8k) and additional warping parameters, overfitting is a concern. The mismatch between NLL and NLPD on en-es is noted but not systematically addressed (e.g., via nested CV or Bayesian model comparison). No statistical significance testing is reported across folds.
- Asymmetric-loss evaluation lacks strong comparative baselines and clarity: The AL/linex experiments reuse the same models and apply Bayes estimators, which is fair for the stated goal. However, asymmetric baselines that directly optimize these losses (quantile regression for AL, expectile/asymmetric quadratic, or dedicated linex learners) are absent. This leaves unclear whether the benefits come from the GP posterior or are achievable with simpler, established asymmetric regressors. Also, the linex setup appears brittle (extreme losses, ad-hoc w changes) without sensitivity analysis.
- Missing practical details and reproducibility: Key details are underspecified: preprocessing/normalization, how NLPD is computed for Warped GPs (Jacobian accounted for), exact cross-validation protocol (nested or not), hyperparameter priors/regularization, optimization stability/restarts per model variant, and runtime. No code or configuration release is mentioned.

Questions to authors
- Calibration: Can you report calibration diagnostics (e.g., PIT histograms, empirical coverage of 50/80/90% predictive intervals, reliability curves) for standard vs Warped GPs? Do improvements in NLPD come with better calibration or mainly through support restriction on negatives?
- Warped GP inference details: How exactly is NLPD computed under the warping—do you include the Jacobian of the warping in the predictive density? How do you compute predictive means/variances for y (numerical quadrature specifics)? Any stability issues?
- Model selection and overfitting: Was kernel/warping choice (including number of tanh terms) selected within a nested CV, or by inspecting test-fold NLPD? Can you report performance as a function of warping complexity (I=1,2,3) and add significance tests across folds?
- Asymmetric baselines: Can you include quantile regression (e.g., quantile SVR or GBM) for AL and an expectile/asymmetric quadratic baseline, and compare against your Bayes estimators? This would help isolate the value of the predictive posterior.
- Heteroscedasticity and likelihood choice: Did you try heteroscedastic GPs or heavy-tailed likelihoods (e.g., Student-t) given the known noise in post-editing times? How do they compare in NLPD and calibration?
- Practical deployment: For a fixed throughput constraint in post-editing or gisting, can you demonstrate decision quality (e.g., rejection curves, risk-utility trade-offs) rather than only loss values, to better connect to the motivating scenarios?
