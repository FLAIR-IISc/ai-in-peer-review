Summary and contributions
This paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in machine translation quality estimation (QE) and evaluates Gaussian Processes (GPs), particularly Warped GPs and Matérn kernels, using proper probabilistic metrics. It further demonstrates how predictive distributions enable decision-making under asymmetric risks via Bayes estimators without retraining. Experiments on three sentence-level QE datasets (post-editing time per token, 17 QuEst features, 10-fold CV) show that (i) output warping to enforce positivity substantially improves negative log scores, and (ii) asymmetry-aware estimators can be derived directly from the predictive posterior.

Contributions as I see them (in order):
1) An empirical study demonstrating that uncertainty-aware evaluation (NLPD) reveals substantial differences between models that are invisible to point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson r), with evidence that output warping markedly improves predictive densities for QE.
2) A principled treatment of asymmetric risk for QE using Bayes decision theory, yielding practical estimators (quantiles for asymmetric linear loss, closed-form for linex) without retraining and exploring optimistic vs. pessimistic scenarios.
3) An application-oriented assessment of GP variants for QE (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs), including insights on when train NLL can be misleading (small data overfitting) and qualitative analysis of linex behavior.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and correct use of proper scoring: The paper convincingly motivates uncertainty for downstream QE decisions and adopts Negative Log Predictive Density (a strictly proper scoring rule) to evaluate full predictive distributions. The empirical finding that MAE/r miss substantive differences while NLPD captures them supports the central thesis.
- Sound methodology leveraging established tools: The use of ARD Matérn kernels and Warped GPs is technically appropriate for noisy, positive-valued responses like post-editing time. The two-pass hyperparameter optimization and cross-validation protocol are reasonable for small QE datasets.
- Empirical consistency across datasets: Output warping consistently reduces NLL/NLPD across language pairs, aligning with the positivity constraint. The observation that en-es train NLL diverges from test NLPD and likely reflects overfitting is plausible and useful guidance for practitioners.
- Decision-theoretic extension to asymmetric risk: Deriving estimators from the predictive posterior (quantiles for AL, closed-form for linex) is a practical contribution. The analysis of why warped models may underperform standard GPs under pessimistic linex (variance-dependent penalty) shows thoughtful interpretation rather than overclaiming.
- Relevance and generality: While focused on QE, the techniques and evaluation practices generalize to other text regression tasks where calibrated uncertainty matters (e.g., active learning, cost estimation).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: GPs (including multi-task) are established for QE, and both Warped GPs and NLPD are well-known in the GP literature. Applying warping and proper scoring to QE is a useful adaptation but conceptually incremental. The asymmetric loss section mainly applies standard Bayes estimators rather than introducing new methodology.
- Constrained evaluation and baselines: Experiments use older, relatively small datasets and only 17 baseline features. There is no comparison to competitive uncertainty-aware baselines such as quantile regression forests/gradient boosting, SVR ensembles, heteroscedastic GPs, or contemporary Bayesian neural approaches (e.g., MC dropout, Bayesian last layer). This limits claims about the proposed models’ practical superiority.
- Calibration analysis is incomplete: The paper repeatedly refers to “well-calibrated” uncertainty but only reports NLL/NLPD. There is no assessment of calibration quality (e.g., PIT histograms, coverage of predictive intervals vs. nominal, CRPS), making it hard to judge whether improvements in NLPD translate into better-calibrated uncertainty.
- Asymmetric risk setup lacks application grounding: Choice of loss weights appears ad hoc, sometimes causing numerical issues (exploding linex losses). The experiments are not tied to a concrete downstream decision (e.g., pass-to-PE filtering utility, productivity/UX trade-offs), nor do they provide cost–coverage curves or guidance for selecting weights in practice.
- Reproducibility and implementation details: Important details are missing or thin (initialization/regularization of the warping function, sensitivity to number of tanh terms, convergence criteria, computational cost, per-fold variance/CI, handling of outliers). No code or config release is mentioned, which hampers reproducibility. Statistical significance is not reported.

Questions to authors
- Calibration: Can you provide calibration diagnostics (e.g., PIT histograms, predictive interval coverage vs. nominal, CRPS) to substantiate claims of well-calibrated uncertainty?
- Warping sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the number of tanh terms and initialization? Did you use any priors/regularization to prevent overfitting, especially on en-es? Please include an ablation.
- Baselines: Why not compare against quantile regression forests/GBMs, SVR ensembles (bagging), heteroscedastic GPs, or simple Bayesian neural baselines? This would help position your approach among practical alternatives.
- Decision-making: Can you connect asymmetric losses to a realistic utility in QE (e.g., acceptance rate vs. post-editing productivity) and show cost curves? How should practitioners choose w?
- Scalability and cost: What are training/inference times and memory footprints for your models per fold? How do they scale with more features or larger datasets?
- Robustness and significance: Please report variability across folds and conduct statistical tests on key comparisons. Also consider reporting CRPS as an additional proper scoring rule.

Overall, the paper is clear, timely for uncertainty-aware NLP, and empirically supports its main message. Its novelty is modest and the evaluation breadth/calibration analysis could be strengthened, but the work is likely to be useful to the QE community and beyond.
