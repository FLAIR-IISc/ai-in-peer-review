Summary and contributions
This paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in sentence-level MT quality estimation (QE) using Gaussian Processes (GPs), with two main refinements: (i) adopting Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better fit positive, noisy targets (post-editing rates), and (ii) evaluating models with proper scoring rules (Negative Log Predictive Density; NLPD) and demonstrating how predictive distributions enable Bayes-optimal decisions under asymmetric loss. Experiments on three small QE datasets compare standard GPs vs. Warped GPs (tanh and log warp) and discuss optimistic/pessimistic scenarios via asymmetric linear and linex losses.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Bringing established probabilistic evaluation (NLPD) and Warped GPs to the QE setting and showing that uncertainty quality is not captured by MAE/r alone.
2) Demonstrating a simple, general mechanism to adapt predictions to asymmetric utility via Bayes estimators without retraining.
3) Empirical findings that warping provides consistent gains in NLL/NLPD and sometimes improves asymmetric-risk decisions; Matérn kernels show mixed benefit.

Strengths
- Sound motivation for uncertainty in QE workflows. The paper clearly articulates why point estimates alone are problematic in downstream decisions such as post-editing triage, and why calibrated predictive distributions are useful. This is a practical and important motivation.
- Uses established, principled probabilistic models and metrics. GPs, Warped GPs, and NLPD are well-known and suitable tools for uncertainty modeling. The paper correctly leverages Bayes risk to obtain decision rules for asymmetric loss without retraining, which is a clean and general approach.
- Empirical evidence that standard point-metrics miss uncertainty differences. The finding that MAE/Pearson r fail to reflect meaningful changes in uncertainty (captured by NLL/NLPD) is valuable for the QE community, which often optimizes point metrics.
- Careful discussion of warping vs. log transform. The paper contrasts a learned monotonic warp with the log transform and provides some evidence (especially on larger datasets) that a flexible warp can be beneficial.
- Honest analysis of negative results. The linex pessimistic scenario reveals counterintuitive behavior; the authors acknowledge and discuss possible causes (variance sensitivity), which improves credibility.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established GP literature; weak baselines. Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, and NLPD are standard. The contribution is primarily an application to QE with modest empirical analysis. Missing are strong, established uncertainty baselines outside GPs: quantile regression (pinball loss) with linear/SVR/GBM; quantile random forests; conformal prediction; Bayesian linear regression/Student-t likelihood; deep ensembles/dropout Bayesian NNs. Without these, it is hard to conclude GPs are preferable for uncertainty in QE.
- Evaluation of uncertainty quality is narrow. NLPD alone does not characterize calibration. There are no interval coverage assessments (e.g., 50/90% interval coverage and widths), PIT histograms, CRPS, or reliability diagrams for regression. The claim of “well-calibrated” uncertainty is not rigorously substantiated.
- Experimental setup is small-scale and dated. The datasets are small (≤2.8k), with a minimal 17-feature set. Modern QE systems use richer features/embeddings and larger datasets (e.g., later WMT QE). The paper argues the goal is not SOTA point accuracy, but the absence of competitive contemporary baselines limits external validity. It is unclear whether conclusions persist with stronger features or neural encoders.
- Asymmetric risk evaluation is somewhat ad hoc and disconnected from task-level decisions. The chosen w values for AL/linex are arbitrary; no mapping to actual user costs or triage tasks is shown. There is no decision-oriented evaluation (e.g., risk-coverage curves, utility under abstention, precision/recall at fixed risk). The linex instability raises concerns that the analysis is sensitive to arbitrary parameterizations rather than reflecting real application needs.
- Lack of statistical testing, ablations, and robustness checks. Differences among kernels/warpings are often small. There are no significance tests, no ablation on number of warp terms, no analysis of sensitivity to hyperparameters or training size, and no runtime/scalability discussion. The statement that NLL overfits on en-es is plausible but not quantitatively verified.
- Modeling choices for positive targets are underexplored. Alternatives such as log-normal/Gamma/Student-t likelihood GPs, truncated/one-sided noise, or heteroscedastic GPs are not compared. The improvement from warping may largely come from enforcing positivity; simpler likelihood changes might suffice.

Questions to authors
- Can you add uncertainty calibration diagnostics beyond NLPD (e.g., CRPS, interval coverage metrics, PIT histograms, reliability diagrams)? Do the models produce well-calibrated coverage across folds/datasets?
- How do your models compare against strong, established non-GP uncertainty baselines: quantile regression (linear, SVR, GBM), quantile random forests, conformal prediction, and deep ensembles/dropout BNNs? If not available now, can you justify why GPs are preferable in production?
- Have you tried alternative likelihoods for positive, heavy-tailed targets (log-normal, Gamma, Student-t) or heteroscedastic GPs? How do they compare to Warped GPs in NLPD and asymmetric decisions?
- Can you provide decision-centric evaluation (e.g., risk-coverage trade-offs, expected utility under triage constraints) to substantiate the practical benefit in post-editing/gisting workflows?
- Are the NLPD improvements statistically significant? Please report paired tests across folds and provide effect sizes.
- How stable are the learned warp parameters with small data (I=3 tanh terms) across folds? Any signs of overfitting? An ablation on I would help.
- What are training/prediction time and memory costs for Warped GPs vs. standard GPs? Any issues scaling beyond a few thousand instances?
- Can you release code and exact preprocessing/hyperparameters to ensure reproducibility?

Overall, this paper advances a sensible message—use probabilistic modeling and proper scoring rules for QE—but the methodological novelty is limited and the empirical validation is not yet strong enough against established uncertainty baselines, with insufficient calibration and decision-level evaluation.
