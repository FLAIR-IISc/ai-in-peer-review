Summary and main contributions
- This paper investigates predictive uncertainty for sentence-level Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) using Gaussian Processes (GPs). It argues that point estimates (e.g., MAE, RMSE, r) are insufficient for decision-making workflows and advocates evaluating and using full predictive distributions.
- Primary contribution: an empirical study showing that Warped GPs (with learned monotonic output transformations) and Matérn kernels improve negative log predictive density (NLPD) over standard GPs on three QE datasets, while point-metric gains are small, highlighting the importance of uncertainty-aware evaluation.
- Secondary contribution: a decision-theoretic case study demonstrating how to reuse predictive posteriors to obtain Bayes risk–optimal estimators for asymmetric losses (asymmetric linear and linex) without retraining, with evidence that warped models help in optimistic and AL settings and analysis of failure modes under pessimistic linex.
- Tertiary contribution: practical modeling and evaluation recommendations for QE (e.g., prefer NLPD over NLL for small data, use warping for positive targets, consider Matérn kernels, and adopt asymmetric losses to reflect application risk).

Strengths
- Clear motivation and relevance: The paper precisely articulates why uncertainty, not just point accuracy, matters in QE workflows (e.g., post-editing and gisting). The asymmetric-risk scenarios are realistic and well connected to decision needs where over/underestimation have unequal costs.
- Sound modeling choices: The use of GPs with ARD, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs is technically appropriate for small, noisy QE data with positive targets. The derivations and use of Bayes-optimal estimators for AL and linex losses are standard and correct, and the link to quantile regression is accurate.
- Appropriate evaluation protocol for uncertainty: Evaluating via NLPD on held-out data (10-fold CV) is a principled choice that penalizes miscalibration (over/underconfidence), and the paper correctly cautions against relying on training NLL in small data regimes due to overfitting.
- Empirical insights with practical implications: The results (as described) indicate that warping substantially improves NLL/NLPD and that differences in uncertainty quality are not reflected in MAE or r. The asymmetric-loss analysis demonstrates a practical way to adapt decisions to varying risk profiles without model retraining.
- Scope across datasets: Experiments on three language pairs with consistent feature sets and a fixed protocol enhance the generality of observed trends. The discussion of why en-es behaves differently (data size) is plausible and informative.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior GP literature: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, and NLPD are established. The main novelty is applying these to QE and illustrating asymmetric risk usage. Methodological advances are incremental; the contribution is primarily empirical/practical.
- Incomplete uncertainty diagnostics: NLPD alone may not fully characterize calibration. The paper does not report complementary diagnostics such as CRPS, PIT/reliability diagrams, coverage of predictive intervals, or calibration under quantiles. The linex pessimistic behavior suggests variance miscalibration; reporting these diagnostics would strengthen conclusions.
- Baselines and ablations are narrow: Comparisons do not include other probabilistic or uncertainty-aware baselines (e.g., heteroscedastic GPs, Bayesian linear regression, Bayesian neural networks/deep ensembles, quantile regression forests, or SVR bagging with empirical uncertainty). The study also lacks ablations on ARD vs. isotropic, number of warping terms, or priors/regularization on warping parameters.
- Overfitting and constraint handling in Warped GPs: The warping function can overfit in small data, and monotonicity requires derivative positivity; it is unclear whether constraints were enforced during optimization. The paper reports overfitting signals (NLL vs. NLPD) but does not explore Bayesian regularization (e.g., Bayesian Warped GPs) or sensitivity analyses.
- External validity and scalability: Experiments use 17 QuEst features and relatively small datasets; no results on more recent QE settings (e.g., DA/HTER, token-level QE, neural features) or larger-scale scenarios are provided. While GPs are appropriate for small data, practical pathways for scaling (sparse/variational GPs) are not empirically explored.

Questions to authors (for rebuttal)
- Calibration: Can you report additional uncertainty diagnostics (e.g., CRPS, PIT/reliability plots, interval coverage) to substantiate calibration claims and clarify the linex pessimistic behavior?
- Warping constraints: How is monotonicity enforced during optimization of f(y) = y + Σ a_i tanh(b_i(y + c_i))? Are there parameter constraints or penalties to ensure f′(y) > 0? How sensitive are results to initialization and the number of tanh terms (I)?
- Ablations: What is the effect of ARD vs. isotropic kernels on NLPD/MAE? Can you provide ablations on kernel choice (EQ vs. Matérn 3/2 vs. 5/2) across all datasets with effect sizes and statistical tests?
- Asymmetric losses: For AL, did you evaluate performance across a range of quantiles (τ curves) to show robustness of the benefits? For linex, can you diagnose the role of predictive variance by plotting loss vs. predicted variance or by calibrating variance (e.g., temperature scaling of σ)?
- Baselines: Can you include at least one non-GP uncertainty baseline (e.g., quantile regression, heteroscedastic regression, deep ensemble with predictive variance, or SVR bagging) to situate the gains in a broader context?
- Data and metrics: Are exact effect sizes (NLPD/MAE/r) and statistical significance reported for Tables 1–2? Can you clarify handling of zero post-editing rates (if present) under warping, and whether log1p was considered?
- Reproducibility: Will code, hyperparameter settings, and trained models be released? This would facilitate adoption of the uncertainty-aware pipeline in QE.

Overall, the work is technically sound and clearly written, with useful empirical insights about uncertainty-aware QE and decision-theoretic usage. Its main value lies in advocating and demonstrating proper uncertainty modeling and evaluation in QE rather than introducing new learning algorithms.
