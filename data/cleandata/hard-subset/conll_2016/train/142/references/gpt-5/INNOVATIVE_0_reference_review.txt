Summary and contributions
This paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) and explores Gaussian Processes (GPs), including Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, as a means to obtain calibrated predictive distributions. It evaluates models using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) in addition to standard point metrics, and proposes a decision-theoretic layer for asymmetric risk via Bayes estimators under asymmetric linear and linex losses.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) Framing and evaluation: a clear case for uncertainty-aware QE with proper scoring (NLPD) and evidence that point metrics miss important differences in uncertainty quality.
2) Decision-theoretic deployment: demonstration that calibrated predictive distributions can be mapped to asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic/optimistic) without retraining, via Bayes-optimal estimators.
3) Modeling choices: application of Warped GPs and Matérn kernels to QE, with empirical observations on their behavior across three datasets.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity and useful reframing: The paper convincingly argues why probabilistic modeling and proper scoring rules (NLPD) matter for QE workflows. The demonstration that models with similar MAE/r can differ substantially in NLPD is compelling and practically important for downstream decision making.
- Decision-theoretic angle for asymmetric risk: The section on Bayes risk and asymmetric losses is a strong, under-explored direction for QE. The decoupling of training from deployment loss is valuable for real-world systems where costs and risk preferences change. This provides a concrete path from predictive distributions to operational policies.
- Sensible GP modeling for small-data QE: Warped GPs address positivity of targets; ARD and Matérn kernels are appropriate for noisy text features and low-data regimes. The empirical finding that warped likelihoods substantially improve NLL/NLPD (especially by avoiding mass on negative times) is insightful.
- Methodologically careful evaluation setup: 10-fold CV across three language pairs with consistent features and reporting of multiple metrics helps isolate the effect of uncertainty modeling. The observation that NLL can be misleading on small datasets (en-es) versus NLPD is a useful takeaway.
- Practicality of a single model serving multiple scenarios: The demonstration that one calibrated model can serve different asymmetric losses by changing estimators (quantiles/linex) without retraining is operationally attractive.

Weaknesses
- Limited technical novelty: The methods (GPs, Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses) are all standard. The contribution is primarily an application and evaluation framing in QE rather than a novel model or learning algorithm. For a venue like CoNLL, this may be considered incremental.
- Narrow baselines and comparisons: The study omits modern uncertainty-capable baselines (e.g., deep ensembles, MC dropout for neural QE, quantile regression, gradient boosting with quantile/NGBoost, calibrated regression). Claiming that “other common models provide only point estimates” is no longer accurate. Without such comparisons, it is hard to conclude that GPs are the preferred vehicle for uncertainty in QE.
- Calibration analysis is underdeveloped: Beyond NLL/NLPD, there are no calibration diagnostics (e.g., reliability diagrams, PIT histograms, coverage of predictive intervals, CRPS). Given the paper’s emphasis on decision making, demonstrating calibration and coverage would substantively strengthen the claims.
- Asymmetric risk results are partially inconclusive: The linex loss performance degrades for warped models in pessimistic settings, and the explanation (variance interaction) is plausible but speculative. The choice of w values is ad hoc, and there is no task-grounded cost model or decision-curve analysis to show real utility (e.g., throughput vs quality trade-offs in post-editing or gisting).
- Outdated feature set and datasets: Using only 17 QuEst features and older datasets limits the relevance to current QE practice (now dominated by neural encoders and larger/modern corpora, including token-level QE). There is no evidence the conclusions carry over to contemporary neural QE systems or richer features.
- Missing details and ablations: Key implementation details are sparse (e.g., selection of number of tanh terms and regularization, hyperprior choices, how hyperparameters are selected within CV to avoid leakage). There is no ablation of kernel vs warping contributions, nor runtime/complexity discussion (important for deployment).

Questions to authors
- Can you provide calibration diagnostics (e.g., coverage of 50/90% intervals, reliability diagrams, PIT) and a proper scoring rule beyond NLPD (e.g., CRPS) to substantiate claims of well-calibrated uncertainty?
- How do your conclusions hold against modern uncertainty-capable baselines (deep ensembles, MC dropout on neural QE; quantile regression; NGBoost; quantile forests)? Even a small-scale comparison would clarify whether GPs are uniquely advantageous here.
- Can you ground the asymmetric risk study in a task-relevant objective (e.g., simulate a post-editing triage pipeline with throughput/quality constraints), and report decision-curve or cost-utility analyses? How should practitioners choose w?
- What is the effect of the warping function complexity (number of tanh terms) on calibration and NLPD? Any overfitting countermeasures (priors/regularization)?
- How were hyperparameters tuned within the CV protocol to avoid test leakage? Any statistical significance testing of the reported differences?

Overall, I find the direction—bringing calibrated uncertainty and decision-theoretic deployment into QE—valuable and underrepresented. However, the technical novelty is modest and the evaluation lacks modern baselines and deeper calibration/decision analyses. Strengthening these aspects could make this a compelling contribution.
