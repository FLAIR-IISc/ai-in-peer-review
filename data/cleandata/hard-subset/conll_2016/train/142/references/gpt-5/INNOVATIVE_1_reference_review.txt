Summary
This paper argues for moving machine translation quality estimation (QE) from point predictions to uncertainty-aware, decision-theoretic modeling. It applies Gaussian Processes (GPs), including Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, to obtain calibrated predictive distributions; proposes NLPD as an evaluation metric for QE; and demonstrates how predictive posteriors can be exploited with asymmetric loss functions (AL and linex) via Bayes risk estimators to match application-specific risk profiles (pessimistic vs. optimistic). Experiments on three QE datasets show that Warped GPs improve NLL/NLPD over standard GPs, that point metrics often miss differences captured by NLPD, and that Bayes estimators can adapt predictions to asymmetric scenarios without retraining.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Conceptual reframing of QE as uncertainty-aware and decision-theoretic: advocating predictive distributions and minimum Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses tailored to downstream use-cases. This is a valuable direction for QE practice.
2) Empirical evidence that uncertainty metrics (NLPD) meaningfully differentiate models that are indistinguishable under MAE/Pearson’s r, and that Warped GPs are beneficial when outputs are positive-valued.
3) Practical modeling guidance for QE with GPs (Matérn vs. EQ, warping choices), and analysis of pitfalls (e.g., NLL overfitting on small data; linex pessimistic regimes interacting with variance).

Strengths
- Novel direction for QE practice: The paper’s strongest contribution is the shift from point estimates to calibrated predictive distributions and decision-theoretic evaluation. While GPs and asymmetric losses are known, bringing them together for QE with a clear operational framing (optimistic/pessimistic scenarios) is timely and useful. It opens a path toward utility-aware QE deployment.
- Clear, actionable methodology: The use of Warped GPs to enforce positivity, Matérn kernels for relaxed smoothness, and Bayes risk estimators (quantiles for AL; closed-form for linex) is well-motivated. The proposal to decouple model training from changing cost profiles (apply new Bayes estimators without retraining) is practical for production.
- Evidence that point metrics hide important differences: The results convincingly show that MAE and Pearson’s r can mask meaningful uncertainty differences that NLPD captures, including highlighting NLL’s overfitting risk on small data versus NLPD on held-out sets.
- Sensible experimental protocol and analysis: Cross-validation across three language pairs, consistent feature sets, and a two-pass hyperparameter optimization make for a careful comparison. The discussion on when tanh-based warping outperforms log and when Matérn helps is informative.
- Honest analysis of failure modes: The observation that linex in pessimistic settings may favor high-variance (even misspecified) models and the hypothesis regarding variance “push” is insightful and flags an important caveat for loss-model interactions.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The components (GPs, Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, AL/linex Bayes estimators) are established. The novelty lies primarily in their integration and application to QE/decision-making rather than in new models or theory. Readers seeking algorithmic innovation may find this incremental.
- Narrow empirical scope and baselines: Experiments use small, older QE datasets with 17 handcrafted features and no comparison to stronger modern baselines (e.g., neural QE, deep ensembles, Bayesian NNs, quantile regression baselines). This limits the generality and contemporary relevance of the findings.
- Limited calibration assessment: The paper relies on NLPD but does not provide direct calibration diagnostics (e.g., PIT histograms, reliability diagrams, CRPS). Given the emphasis on uncertainty, a deeper calibration study would strengthen claims about “well-calibrated” uncertainty.
- Asymmetric loss evaluation lacks task-level utility: While optimistic/pessimistic scenarios are motivated, the evaluation remains loss scores rather than decision-centric metrics (e.g., accept/reject curves, coverage vs. error trade-offs, cost–benefit analyses). This weakens the link to real workflows.
- Sensitivity and robustness under-explored: The choice of linex/AL weights seems ad hoc; results for linex in pessimistic regimes are inconclusive; and the sensitivity to warping complexity (number of tanh terms), ARD priors/regularization, and kernel choice is not systematically studied. Overfitting concerns, especially with small data and flexible warping, are acknowledged but not mitigated (e.g., via priors or Bayesian treatment).

Questions to Authors (for rebuttal)
- Calibration: Did you assess calibration beyond NLPD (e.g., PIT histograms, reliability diagrams, CRPS)? How well-calibrated are standard vs. Warped GPs on these datasets?
- Baselines: Can you include or comment on comparisons with quantile regression (pinball loss), deep ensembles, and Bayesian neural baselines for uncertainty? Even a small-scale comparison would contextualize the GP choice.
- Asymmetric utilities: Can you provide decision-oriented analyses (e.g., accept/reject curves, utility vs. coverage) for the post-editing/gisting scenarios? How do different w values translate to operational gains?
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the number of tanh terms (I) and to priors/regularization on warping parameters? Did you try heteroscedastic GPs or log-normal likelihoods as alternatives to warping?
- Linex pessimistic regime: Can you empirically validate the variance-based explanation (e.g., by matching marginal variance across models or using tempered variance) to test whether high variance drives the advantage?
- Practicality: What are the computational costs (training/inference) and scalability limits? Will you release code/scripts to facilitate adoption in QE pipelines?

Overall, while the modeling toolkit is standard, the paper’s decision-theoretic framing and emphasis on predictive uncertainty in QE are valuable and likely to influence practice. Strengthening calibration analysis and baseline comparisons would significantly improve the work.
