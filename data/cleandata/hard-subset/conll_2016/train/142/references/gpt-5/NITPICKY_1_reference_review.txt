Summary and claimed contributions
This paper argues for uncertainty-aware QE by using Gaussian Processes (GPs), including Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, and evaluates models with Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD). It further demonstrates a use case with asymmetric losses (AL and LINEX) via Bayes estimators, claiming that predictive distributions enable post-hoc adaptation to changing risk profiles.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) An empirical study showing that uncertainty-aware evaluation (NLPD) can differentiate QE models that look similar under point metrics, and that Warped GPs often improve NLPD over standard GPs.
2) A demonstration of asymmetric decision-making using Bayes risk estimators from the predictive distribution, without retraining.
3) A small-scale exploration of kernel/warping choices (EQ vs. Matérn; tanh vs. log warping) on three QE datasets.

Strengths
- Focus on uncertainty in QE: The paper brings needed attention to calibrated predictive distributions in QE workflows, where risk-sensitive decisions (e.g., filtering for post-editing) matter; this is under-emphasized in prior QE work focused on point metrics.
- Practical decision-theoretic angle: The post-hoc Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses are a practical idea when operational costs change and retraining is undesirable. This is a clear, reusable insight for practitioners.
- Evidence that point metrics miss uncertainty differences: The observation that MAE/Pearson’s r may not reflect uncertainty quality is important for evaluation practice; recommending NLPD is reasonable.
- Cleaner handling of positive support via warping: Highlighting that standard GPs allocate mass to negative times whereas Warped GPs avoid this is pertinent and helps explain improved NLPD.
- Multi-dataset setup and cross-validation: Using three language pairs and 10-fold CV with fixed feature sets improves comparability across conditions.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: The components are largely established (GPs, Matérn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, Bayes estimators for AL/LINEX). The paper is primarily an application/positioning piece for QE, with modest empirical depth. There is no new modeling or clear methodological advance specific to QE beyond selecting known GP variants.
- Technical/formula issues and notation errors:
  • EQ kernel definition appears incorrect (missing the standard 1/2 factor and inconsistent use of r vs. r^2). You define r^2 as a squared distance and then use sqrt(3) r^2 in Matérn; dimensionally this should be sqrt(3) r with r the distance, not r^2.
  • Warped GP predictive density (Eq. in Sec. 2.3) omits the squared term and −1/2 in the exponent; it should be proportional to exp(−0.5 (f(y*)−μ*)^2 / σ*^2).
  • NLPD definition uses log p(ŷi = yi|xi), which is odd notation; it should be log p(yi|xi).
  • Monotonicity of the warping function is claimed but no parameter constraints/priors are specified to guarantee f′(y)>0; as written, ai, bi unconstrained can break monotonicity.
These issues undermine confidence in the correctness and reproducibility.
- Incomplete uncertainty evaluation: NLPD alone is insufficient to assess calibration. Missing are standard diagnostics such as coverage of predictive intervals, PIT histograms, reliability diagrams, CRPS, and interval width vs. error trade-offs. The claim that NLL overfits small data is asserted from one small dataset without statistical support or broader calibration evidence.
- Baselines and ablations are sparse: No comparisons to other uncertainty-capable baselines (heteroscedastic/Student‑t GPs, quantile regression, quantile GBMs/forests, deep ensembles, MC dropout BNNs). No analysis of ARD effects, number of tanh terms beyond “up to 3,” or sensitivity to priors/initialization. The asymmetric-loss section lacks a realistic cost model and only uses a few ad-hoc w values; no decision-curve analysis (e.g., coverage/recall vs. cost), and the LINEX “pessimistic” failure is not investigated beyond speculation.
- Presentation, formatting, and references: Numerous typos (“predicitive,” “postediting”), inconsistent hyphenation, accent/encoding issues (“Matèrn”), footnotes inline, and many malformed references with missing years and escaped characters (e.g., “Beck et al\.,? 2014b”). Figures/Tables are referenced (Fig. 1, Tables 1–2) but numeric results are not shown here; ensure they exist and include variances/CI and significance testing. These issues reduce readability and rigor.
- Reproducibility gaps: No code, seeds, or implementation details (optimizer, learning rates, priors/constraints for warping, jitter, feature normalization). The claim of “10 random restarts” is vague without initialization schemes. No outlier handling for post-edit times, which is critical for heavy-tailed human time data.

Questions to Authors
- Please correct and confirm the exact kernel and Warped GP density equations, with consistent r vs. r^2 usage and proper normalizing constants. Were these corrected in the implementation?
- How do you enforce monotonicity of the warping function in practice (constraints or priors on ai, bi)? Did any runs produce non-monotone warps?
- Can you report calibration diagnostics beyond NLPD: empirical coverage for 50%/80%/90% intervals, PIT, and CRPS? Are your predictive variances under/over-dispersed?
- Provide comparisons to at least one heteroscedastic GP or Student‑t likelihood GP, and one non-GP uncertainty baseline (e.g., quantile regression forest, gradient boosted quantile regression, or deep ensembles).
- For the asymmetric scenarios, can you ground w choices in an operational cost model and provide decision curves (e.g., cost vs. acceptance rate)? Also include a sensitivity analysis over w with statistical tests.
- Implementation details: software/library, optimization hyperparameters, priors/constraints, feature scaling, noise priors, handling of outliers, splits and seeds. Please release code and exact data folds.

Recommendation
Reject. The paper raises a useful perspective for QE, but the methodological novelty is limited and the submission contains several technical inaccuracies, insufficient uncertainty calibration analysis, weak baselines, and significant presentation/reference issues. With corrected formulas, stronger baselines, proper calibration diagnostics, principled asymmetric-cost evaluation, and improved writing/references, this work could be a solid empirical report.
