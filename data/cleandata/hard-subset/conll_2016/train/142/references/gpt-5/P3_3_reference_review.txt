Summary and main contributions
This paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE) and studies Gaussian Processes (GPs), with two extensions: (i) Matérn kernels to relax smoothness assumptions, and (ii) Warped GPs to handle positive-valued targets (post-editing time/rate). It evaluates uncertainty via Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) in addition to point metrics, and demonstrates decision-theoretic usage of predictive distributions under asymmetric losses (as a proxy for practical workflows).

As I see it, the primary contributions are:
1) Empirical demonstration that Warped GPs yield better-calibrated predictive distributions (lower NLL/NLPD) for QE than standard GPs, and that such improvements are invisible to standard point metrics.
2) A decision-theoretic analysis showing how predictive posteriors can be used to optimize under asymmetric losses (AL, linex), with evidence that different modeling choices interact with the choice of loss.
3) A careful discussion of kernel selection (Matérn vs EQ) and the impact of small-data regimes common in QE on uncertainty estimation.

Strengths
- Clear, decision-centric motivation and proper scoring: The paper makes a compelling case that QE is used for downstream decisions where uncertainty matters, and evaluates models with a proper scoring rule (NLPD) rather than only MAE/Pearson r. The analysis convincingly shows that differences in uncertainty quality are not captured by point metrics.
- Appropriate probabilistic baselines for small data: GPs are well suited to typical QE dataset sizes. Warped GPs are a principled way to respect positivity of post-editing time, and Matérn kernels are a sensible choice when the EQ smoothness is too strong. The training/inference descriptions are technically sound.
- Decision-theoretic usage under asymmetric risk: The Bayes estimators for AL and linex losses are correctly derived/used, and the experiments illustrate how the same trained model can be adapted to different risk profiles without retraining—practically important for dynamic workflows.
- Honest analysis of failure modes: The paper does not overclaim; it reports puzzling linex results under pessimistic settings and offers a plausible hypothesis (variance sensitivity), which is useful for the community and suggests future methodological work.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines for uncertainty and calibration: The comparison set omits strong and relevant probabilistic baselines that could contextualize the gains:
  - Heteroscedastic (input-dependent noise) GPs to separate aleatoric from epistemic uncertainty.
  - Heavy-tailed likelihoods (e.g., Student-t) for robustness to outliers in post-editing time.
  - Simple log-normal observation models (GP on log-scale) with exact predictive distributions, or Box-Cox/Yeo–Johnson transforms as competitive learned monotone mappings.
  - Bayesian linear/kernel ridge with ARD as a simpler uncertainty baseline.
  Without these, it is hard to attribute the benefits to warping vs the general move from non-probabilistic to probabilistic models.
- Incomplete uncertainty evaluation: NLPD is important, but complementary calibration assessments are missing:
  - Coverage of nominal prediction intervals (e.g., 50/90%) and calibration curves (PIT histograms).
  - Proper scoring rules beyond log score (e.g., CRPS), which are less sensitive to tails and provide a fuller picture.
  Given the variance-related linex behavior, reporting interval coverage/calibration would strengthen conclusions and potentially explain the pessimistic-linex anomaly.
- Asymmetric loss experiments lack grounding and comparisons: The chosen weights (e.g., w=3,1/3 for AL; w=±0.75 for linex) are not tied to realistic cost ratios derived from workflow economics. Moreover, quantile regression (pinball loss) or conformal quantile regression baselines should be included for AL; otherwise, it is unclear whether the GP uncertainty is genuinely superior to directly optimized quantile estimators. For linex, sensitivity to variance could be probed via variance calibration/temperature scaling to verify the hypothesis.
- Overfitting diagnosis and protocol: The discrepancy between NLL (train) and NLPD (test) is attributed to overfitting for en–es, but the evaluation uses a single-layer CV without a clear nested validation for model selection. While marginal likelihood helps, nested CV or a dedicated validation split is advisable for hyperparameter and model variant selection, especially with flexible warpings. Reporting variability (e.g., std across folds) and statistical significance would also help.
- Scope and representativeness of QE setting: The experiments use 17 QuEst features and older WMT14–16 datasets focusing on post-editing rate. While the paper’s goal is not SOTA point accuracy, more diverse targets (e.g., HTER, DA, MQM-based labels) and feature richer/neural encoders would increase confidence that the uncertainty findings generalize. At minimum, a discussion of expected transfer to modern QE setups would help.

Questions to authors
- Can you report calibration diagnostics: empirical coverage of predictive intervals (e.g., 50%/90%), PIT histograms, and CRPS? These would directly test whether the variance under/over-confidence explains the linex behavior in pessimistic settings.
- Did you try heteroscedastic noise models or Student-t likelihoods? If so, how did they compare to Warped GPs on NLPD and asymmetric losses?
- For AL loss, how do the GP-based ww/(w+1) quantiles compare to standard quantile regression (pinball loss) with the same features, and to conformalized quantile regression in terms of coverage and loss?
- For warping, did you evaluate simpler parametric transforms (Box-Cox/Yeo–Johnson) with learned parameters? They are monotone, low-variance, interpretable, and may mitigate overfitting on small datasets.
- Can you provide sensitivity analyses for the number of tanh terms and priors/regularization on warping parameters? Any evidence of overfitting (e.g., highly non-monotone regions or extreme slopes)?
- How robust are conclusions across different labels and feature representations (e.g., sentence encoders or COMET-QE features)? Any preliminary results?
- Would variance calibration (e.g., temperature scaling of predictive variance or isotonic calibration of predictive CDFs) reduce the pessimistic-linex degradation you observed?

Overall, this is a timely and well-motivated study that foregrounds uncertainty in QE and connects it to decision-making. Strengthening baselines, adding calibration diagnostics, and grounding the asymmetric-loss setup would substantially increase its impact.
