Summary
This paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs) with richer kernels (Matérn) and Warped GPs to better handle positive-valued labels (post-editing rates). The authors evaluate probabilistic models with Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) in addition to standard point metrics, and demonstrate how predictive distributions enable scenario-specific decision-making via asymmetric loss functions (asymmetric linear and linex), without retraining.

Claimed contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that probabilistic modeling (especially Warped GPs) yields better-calibrated predictive distributions for QE than standard GPs, reflected in improved NLPD, with point metrics largely insensitive to these differences.
2) Practical demonstration of decision-theoretic usage: deriving Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses to match pessimistic/optimistic application scenarios using a single probabilistic model.
3) Exploration of kernel and likelihood choices (Matérn vs. EQ; tanh-parameterized warp vs. log transform) and a discussion of overfitting signs via NLL vs. NLPD.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and framing: The paper convincingly explains why point estimates are insufficient in realistic QE workflows, and why calibrated uncertainty matters for accept/reject post-editing and gisting scenarios. The asymmetric loss framing is concrete and relatable.
- Sound probabilistic foundation and correct use of proper scoring: NLPD is an appropriate metric for evaluating predictive distributions, and the analysis (e.g., overfitting indicated by NLL vs. NLPD divergence on small en–es) is insightful. The distinction between point metrics and distributional metrics is well-articulated and demonstrated empirically.
- Appropriate modeling choices for positive, noisy labels: Warped GPs address support mismatch and non-Gaussianity; Matérn kernels relax smoothness assumptions compared to EQ. The finding that warp parameters materially improve NLPD is robust across datasets.
- Decision-theoretic utility without retraining: The Bayes estimator approach for asymmetric losses (quantile estimator for AL; closed-form estimator for linex) is practical and shows how one model can serve multiple changing cost scenarios—highly relevant for production QE.
- Reproducible experimental setup across three language pairs with cross-validation and controlled features: The use of standard QuEst features and 10-fold CV allows reasonably fair comparisons across models and datasets, focusing the study on uncertainty rather than feature engineering.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior GP/QE work: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, and NLPD are established; GPs have been applied to QE before. The paper’s primary novelty is applying known uncertainty-aware tools to QE with an asymmetric-loss demonstration. This is useful but incremental for a competitive venue.
- Sparse baselines and scope of comparison: The evaluation compares variants of GPs among themselves, but lacks non-GP probabilistic baselines (e.g., quantile regression, gradient boosting with quantile loss, quantile random forests, Bayesian linear models, heteroscedastic GPs, deep ensembles/MC dropout). Even a strong SVR baseline with bagging or conformal prediction would contextualize the gains and clarify whether GPs are uniquely advantageous.
- Calibration analysis is underdeveloped: NLPD alone does not exhaustively assess calibration. The paper lacks reliability diagnostics such as coverage of prediction intervals, PIT histograms, calibration curves for quantiles, or CRPS. Claims about “well-calibrated” uncertainty would be strengthened by these checks and by reporting sharpness vs. calibration trade-offs.
- Asymmetric loss results are partly inconclusive and under-analyzed: The pessimistic linex results are worse than standard GPs; the explanation invokes variance effects but is not substantiated with diagnostics (e.g., variance histograms, posterior tails, clipping behavior for negative predictions). Without deeper analysis or mitigation (e.g., robust likelihoods, heteroscedastic noise), the takeaways for practitioners remain unclear.
- Modeling and reproducibility gaps: Given the positivity and heavy-tailed nature of post-editing times, alternative likelihoods (Student-t), heteroscedastic GPs, or Box-Cox/Yeo-Johnson warps could be more appropriate. Details on optimization (initialization, convergence criteria), numerical inversion for warps, and computational costs are light. No significance testing is reported.

Questions to Authors
- Did you evaluate calibration beyond NLPD (e.g., coverage of nominal 90% intervals, PIT histograms, CRPS)? If not, can you provide these diagnostics, especially to understand the linex behavior?
- How did you handle negative predictions from standard GPs when computing asymmetric losses? Were predictions clipped at zero? If not, could that explain some discrepancies?
- Can you report uncertainty interval coverage and sharpness for standard vs. warped GPs? Do warped models consistently produce narrower but still well-calibrated intervals?
- For the warped models, how was the number of tanh terms chosen (I=1..3)? Any signs of overfitting across folds? Can you include ablations and significance tests?
- Could you compare against a non-GP quantile method (e.g., gradient boosting with quantile loss) and/or a heteroscedastic GP to assess whether variance mis-specification is driving the linex results?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Add baselines beyond GPs: quantile regression, quantile GBMs/forests, conformal prediction, deep ensembles/MC dropout on a simple neural model. This would strengthen the case that calibrated uncertainty (not just GPs) improves QE.
- Include calibration diagnostics (CRPS, interval coverage, PIT). NLPD can be overly sensitive to tail misspecification; CRPS offers a more robust proper scoring rule for regression.
- Evaluate decision-making utility more directly: e.g., simulate an accept/reject policy with uncertainty thresholds, measure expected utility or task-specific cost savings, not only loss values.
- Consider heteroscedastic likelihoods or Student-t to handle heavy tails; report whether this mitigates pessimistic linex issues.
- Report computational cost and scalability, and release code to improve reproducibility.
- Minor: References have formatting issues and some incomplete entries; please correct.

Recommendation: Weak Reject. The paper is well-motivated and clear, and the empirical findings about the value of predictive distributions in QE are useful. However, the methodological novelty is limited, comparisons are narrow, and calibration/decision analyses need to go deeper. With stronger baselines, calibration diagnostics, and more robust asymmetric-risk evaluation, this could be a solid contribution.
