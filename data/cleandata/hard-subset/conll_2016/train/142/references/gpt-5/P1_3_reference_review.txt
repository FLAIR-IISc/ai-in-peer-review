Summary and contributions
This paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in machine translation quality estimation (QE). It focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs), including Warped GPs and Matérn kernels, and evaluates models using proper probabilistic scoring (NLPD) in addition to point metrics. It also demonstrates how predictive posteriors can support asymmetric decision-making via Bayes risk estimators (asymmetric linear and linex losses), avoiding retraining across scenarios.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical study showing that probabilistic evaluation (NLPD) distinguishes QE models with differing uncertainty quality that point metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) fail to separate; Warped GPs notably improve log scores over standard GPs across three QE datasets.
2) Application of Bayes decision theory to QE: deriving and using Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses directly from predictive posteriors, enabling scenario-specific predictions (optimistic/pessimistic) without retraining; analysis reveals nontrivial behavior (e.g., linex loss may favor higher-variance models).
3) Practical modeling guidance: evidence that training NLL can mislead (overfitting on small datasets), Matérn kernels can help, and warping choice matters.

Strengths
- Clear, well-motivated case for uncertainty in QE: The paper articulates realistic workflows (post-editing, gisting) where confidence matters. Using calibrated predictive distributions is the right framing, and NLPD is an appropriate proper scoring rule. This is valuable for a community that often relies on point estimates.
- Sound probabilistic framing with actionable decision rules: Leveraging Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses is principled and practically useful. The idea to avoid retraining when costs change is important for production QE.
- Consistent, multi-dataset evaluation: Three language pairs, 10-fold CV, and consistent features provide a fair within-family comparison of GP variants. Findings (e.g., Warped GP reduces NLPD; training NLL can be misleading on small data) are robust and practically relevant.
- Useful empirical observations: The result that differences in uncertainty quality are not reflected in MAE or Pearson’s r is an important takeaway for evaluation practice. The asymmetry experiments illustrate that choice of loss interacts with model variance in non-obvious ways, a nuanced insight for deployment.
- Methodological clarity on Warped GPs for positive targets: The motivation to move beyond ad hoc log transforms and learn monotone warpings is apt for positive-only labels like post-editing time, and the paper describes inference details (median vs mean, quadrature) clearly.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in methods: The core modeling elements (GPs, Matérn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses) are established. The contribution is mainly an application-focused empirical study in QE. For a top-tier venue, stronger methodological innovation or broader empirical scope would help.
- Missing baselines and broader comparisons: The paper compares only among GP variants. It omits strong non-probabilistic baselines (e.g., SVR) and recent neural QE models (e.g., CNN/RNN/transformer-based QE), both for point accuracy and, where possible, uncertainty approximations (ensembles, MC dropout). Even if uncertainty calibration is the focus, reporting point accuracy relative to common baselines situates the results.
- Incomplete uncertainty evaluation: NLPD is necessary but not sufficient to assess calibration. The paper lacks calibration diagnostics such as PIT histograms, reliability diagrams for prediction intervals, coverage vs nominal, or CRPS. Given the emphasis on “well-calibrated” uncertainty, these diagnostics would substantiate claims beyond log scores.
- Questionable use of linex Bayes estimator with non-Gaussian posteriors: For linex loss, the Bayes estimator involves the log-moment generating function; the closed-form ŷ = μ − (w σ²)/2 holds for normal posteriors. Warped GP predictive posteriors in y are non-Gaussian; using only μ and σ² is inexact. The paper does not clarify whether it approximates the mgf (e.g., quadrature) or assumes normality in y. This may confound results in asymmetric experiments, particularly the reported behavior in pessimistic settings.
- Narrow feature set and labels: Experiments use only 17 QuEst features and only post-editing time rate. Modern QE uses richer features and labels (e.g., HTER, DA, MQM). This limits generality and undercuts claims that findings “apply to any text regression task.” At minimum, one additional label type would strengthen the case.
- Reporting and statistical rigor: The paper references tables/figures but does not present statistical significance tests or uncertainty of reported metrics (e.g., std across folds). Moreover, kernel equations include apparent typos (e.g., EQ kernel exponent typically uses −0.5 r²; Matérn 5/2 form uses r not r² in the linear term), which reduce clarity.

Questions to authors
- Linex estimator: How exactly did you compute the Bayes estimator for Warped GP predictive posteriors? Did you approximate the mgf E[exp(−wY)] via quadrature, or did you plug in μ and σ² using the normal closed form? If the latter, can you quantify the approximation error or assess sensitivity?
- Quantiles for AL: How were predictive quantiles computed for Warped GPs (numerical inversion, quadrature, sampling)? Any failure modes or stability issues?
- Calibration diagnostics: Can you report coverage of predictive intervals (e.g., 50%/90%) and PIT histograms for the compared models? Do Warped GPs show better calibration than standard GPs?
- Baselines: Can you add comparisons to a strong SVR baseline (and possibly a simple neural baseline) for MAE/r, and discuss how your models compare on point accuracy?
- Warping complexity: How was the number of tanh terms selected? Any overfitting evidence on the small en–es set? Would monotonic spline warpings (or Box-Cox) behave differently?
- Asymmetric costs: Can you tie the chosen w values to realistic application costs, or conduct a sensitivity sweep with utility curves to illustrate trade-offs more concretely?

Overall: This is a well-motivated, careful empirical study advocating an important evaluation and modeling perspective for QE. The main limitations are modest novelty, missing calibration diagnostics, and limited baselines/scope. With stronger empirical validation (calibration checks, broader baselines, clarification on the linex estimator), this work would make a solid contribution. I lean toward borderline accept if these issues can be addressed.
