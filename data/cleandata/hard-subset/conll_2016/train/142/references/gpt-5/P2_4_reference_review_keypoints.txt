- Paper goal: argue for modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in QE using GPs (Matérn, Warped GPs), NLPD, and Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses.

- Contribution 1: Empirical case that probabilistic (Warped GP) models yield better-calibrated uncertainty (NLPD) than standard GPs, which point metrics miss.

- Contribution 2: Shows how to derive Bayes-optimal estimators from predictive posteriors for asymmetric linear and linex losses to support risk-sensitive decisions without retraining.

- Contribution 3: Empirically evaluates modeling choices (Matérn kernels, tanh vs log warping) and their effect on uncertainty quality and performance.

- Strength: Clear and relevant motivation tying uncertainty to practical QE workflows (post-editing, gisting) and insufficiency of point estimates.

- Strength: Sound probabilistic methodology (GPs, ARD, Matérn, Warped GPs) and use of NLPD as a proper scoring rule for uncertainty quality.

- Strength: Empirical evidence that warping substantially improves NLL/NLPD while MAE/pearson r remain insensitive, supporting the central claim.

- Strength: Decision-theoretic framing is practical—Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses allow one model to support multiple operational scenarios.

- Strength: Paper is well organized and clearly explains modeling choices, evaluation metrics, and asymmetric loss framework.

- Weakness: Limited novelty—methods (Warped GPs, Matérn, NLPD, Bayes estimators for asymmetric loss) are established; mainly an application/empirical study.

- Weakness: Narrow, dated experimental scope—small feature-based datasets, limited 17-feature set, no comparisons to strong modern or non-GP baselines (neural QE, quantile regression, SVR).

- Weakness: Incomplete calibration analysis—NLPD used but no interval coverage, reliability diagrams, PIT histograms, or CRPS to substantiate calibration claims.

- Weakness: Asymmetric risk analysis underdeveloped—ad hoc weight choices, limited sensitivity analysis, unstable linex/pessimistic behavior not quantitatively explained, no decision-oriented metrics (risk-coverage, utility curves).

- Weakness: Reproducibility and ablation gaps—missing code, seeds, runtimes, optimization/prior details, selection criteria for tanh terms, robustness to initialization and overfitting.

- Question: Why choose warping over likelihoods tailored to positive targets (log-normal, Gamma, truncated Gaussian); can you compare to GP with log-normal likelihood?

- Question: How calibrated are predictive distributions? Please report predictive interval coverage (50/90%), reliability diagrams, PIT histograms, or CRPS.

- Question: How sensitive are results to number of tanh terms, initialization, and regularization? Any cross-validated selection and evidence of overfitting?

- Question: Did you try heteroscedastic GPs to model input-dependent noise and address the pessimistic linex variance issues?

- Question: Can you provide decision-oriented evaluations for keep/discard use-cases (risk-coverage, cost curves, realistic operating points)?

- Question: Please report runtimes, scalability, and provide code or detailed reproduction instructions.

- Question: How would conclusions change when compared to modern neural QE uncertainty methods (Bayesian/deep ensembles/MC dropout) or quantile regression baselines?

- Overall assessment: Solid, well-argued application demonstrating the value of uncertainty-aware QE, but limited novelty and scope—needs stronger baselines, calibration diagnostics, and decision-centric evaluation to be compelling.
