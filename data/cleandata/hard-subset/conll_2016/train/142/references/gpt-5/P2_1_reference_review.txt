Summary and contributions
This paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in machine translation quality estimation (QE) and focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs) (including Matérn kernels and Warped GPs) to produce calibrated predictive distributions. It evaluates models using proper scoring rules (negative log predictive density, NLPD) and demonstrates using predictive posteriors to make decisions under asymmetric risk via Bayes estimators (asymmetric linear and linex losses), without retraining.

Contributions (as I see them, strongest first):
1) Empirical evidence that uncertainty-aware evaluation (NLPD) reveals model differences not captured by point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r), and that Warped GPs substantially improve predictive densities for QE over standard GPs. This is a clear, well-justified message for the QE community.
2) Decision-theoretic framing of QE via asymmetric losses with Bayes risk estimators applied to predictive posteriors, showing practical benefits in optimistic/pessimistic scenarios and highlighting interesting behavior of linex loss vs model variance.
3) Modest exploration of GP design choices for QE (Matérn vs RBF; parametric tanh-based warping vs log transform) with insights on dataset-size effects (e.g., NLL overfitting on small data).

Strengths
- Clear motivation and significance for QE practice: The paper convincingly argues that relying on point estimates can be risky in downstream workflows (post-editing, gisting), and that calibrated uncertainty can improve decisions. The asymmetric risk scenarios are realistic and well-framed.
- Principled methodology: Using GPs (including ARD, Matérn kernels) and Warped GPs is technically sound and appropriate for small-data QE. Evaluating with NLPD (a proper scoring rule) is the right choice to assess uncertainty quality.
- Decision-theoretic contribution without retraining: Deriving and applying Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses on the predictive posterior is elegant and practically relevant (supports dynamic risk preferences and multiple simultaneous scenarios).
- Empirical setup spans three language pairs with cross-validation: The use of 10-fold CV and public datasets adds credibility. The observation that NLL can mislead on small data while NLPD on held-out data remains informative is a useful, generalizable insight.
- Analysis provides nontrivial findings: The linex loss performing worse for warped models in pessimistic settings due to lower variance is a nuanced, hypothesis-driven observation that may inform model selection under different risks.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, and Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses are established. The main novelty is their application and comparative study in QE. This is valuable for the QE community but incremental from a methodological standpoint for a general NLP venue.
- Evaluation scope and baselines: The experiments use only 17 QuEst baseline features and traditional GP baselines. There is no comparison against stronger modern QE baselines (e.g., feature-rich systems, gradient boosting, neural/Bayesian neural models, heteroscedastic or Student-t GPs). This limits claims about broader impact.
- Missing calibration diagnostics: While NLPD is informative, the paper does not report standard calibration analyses (e.g., PIT histograms, coverage of credible intervals, reliability diagrams). These would directly support claims about “well-calibrated” uncertainty and clarify the linex behavior.
- Practical decision impact not demonstrated: The asymmetric-risk section evaluates loss values but does not demonstrate downstream decision quality (e.g., acceptance/rejection curves, cost-benefit analysis, productivity/user experience proxies). Such simulations would strengthen the argument that uncertainty improves operational outcomes.
- Reporting and clarity issues: Tables are referenced but not shown here; statistical significance testing is not discussed. Some equations are imprecisely formatted (e.g., kernel definitions, missing factors), and there are minor typos and reference formatting problems. Reproducibility details are thin (e.g., initializations of warping parameters, number of tanh terms selection, quadrature points, optimizer settings, random seeds, runtime/compute).

Questions to authors
- Calibration: Can you report calibration diagnostics (PIT histograms, coverage of nominal credible intervals, calibration curves) for the predictive posteriors? Do warped models improve calibration beyond NLPD?
- Baselines: How do your models compare against (i) quantile regression baselines for AL loss, (ii) heteroscedastic or Student-t GPs, and (iii) a modern non-GP baseline (e.g., gradient boosting with quantile loss or a Bayesian neural net with variational inference)?
- Decision impact: Can you include a simple decision simulation (e.g., thresholding acceptance of MT segments for post-editing) to show cost curves and productivity trade-offs under different risk settings?
- Warping details: How were the tanh warping parameters initialized and constrained for monotonicity? How was the number of terms I chosen (evidence-based, validation)? Did you attempt alternative monotone warpings (e.g., splines) or a log-normal likelihood?
- Linex phenomenon: Can you verify the “variance-driven” explanation by matching predictive variances between models (e.g., temperature-scaling variance) or by using a truncated Gaussian likelihood to avoid negative support? Would linex results change under a positive-support likelihood?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, preprocessed features, and exact hyperparameters (optimizer, learning rates, quadrature settings, restarts, seeds) to reproduce folds and results?

Overall assessment
- Originality: Moderate (application of known methods to a new QE focus). 
- Soundness/technical quality: Good, but missing calibration diagnostics and broader baselines weaken claims.
- Significance: Moderate for QE practitioners; limited broader methodological impact.
- Clarity: Generally clear but with reporting/formatting issues; key implementation details are missing.
- Reproducibility: Partial (public data; methods standard) but code/hyperparameters not provided.

I view this as a solid, application-focused paper that advances uncertainty-aware evaluation and decision-making in QE, with actionable insights. To strengthen it, broaden baselines, add calibration analysis, and demonstrate downstream decision gains.
