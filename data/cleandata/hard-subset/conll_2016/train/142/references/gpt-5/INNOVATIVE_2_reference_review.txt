Summary and main contributions
- This paper reframes sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE) as a probabilistic prediction problem, arguing for uncertainty-aware evaluation and decision making. It instantiates this with Gaussian Processes (GPs), including Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, and evaluates with Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) in addition to point metrics. It further shows how predictive distributions can support asymmetric-risk decisions via Bayes estimators without retraining.
- Contributions as I see them:
  1) A clear, uncertainty-centric agenda for QE: advocating proper scoring (NLPD) and decision-theoretic evaluation under asymmetric losses, with concrete evidence that point-metric evaluations miss important differences.
  2) Empirical study of Warped GPs for QE, demonstrating large gains in probabilistic fit (NLL/NLPD) over standard GPs and mixed but useful effects of Matérn kernels, and showing that warping addresses support mismatch for positive-valued labels.
  3) Practical demonstration that a single probabilistic model can serve multiple application risk profiles (pessimistic/optimistic) via analytic Bayes estimators (quantiles and linex), avoiding retraining.

Strengths
- Directional novelty with practical relevance: The paper pushes the QE community beyond point estimates to calibrated predictive distributions and risk-aware decisions. This is a meaningful shift in how QE systems should be built and evaluated in production workflows (post-editing, gisting).
- Solid probabilistic framing and evaluation: Using NLPD to compare probabilistic models is appropriate and informative; the analysis showing divergence between training NLL and test NLPD on small data (en–es) is insightful and actionable.
- Elegant, low-overhead decision-making: The Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses show how to adapt predictions to changing business risk w without retraining, which is operationally valuable and often overlooked in NLP work.
- Sensible modelling choices: Warped GPs address the positive-support nature of post-editing time; the finding that warping drastically improves NLL/NLPD is well-motivated and connects model assumptions to the label distribution. The exploration of Matérn kernels for noisier QE signals is reasonable.
- Careful, cross-validated experiments across three language pairs: While not state-of-the-art in features, the study is controlled and focused on the main question (uncertainty quality vs point accuracy), and highlights that MAE/Pearson r often obscure differences in uncertainty modelling.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, and asymmetric loss Bayes estimators are established. The main novelty is their application and synthesis for QE. This is a directional/conceptual contribution rather than a new model/algorithm.
- Missing calibration diagnostics: Beyond NLPD, there is no assessment of calibration (e.g., probability integral transform histograms, coverage of credible intervals, calibration error for quantiles). Since the paper’s thesis is about uncertainty quality, adding these would strengthen claims, especially given the linex findings.
- Narrow baselines and comparisons: No comparison to strong non-GP uncertainty baselines (e.g., quantile regression with gradient boosting, Bayesian linear models, heteroscedastic/Student-t GPs, deep ensembles or Bayesian NNs recently used for uncertainty). Also no comparison to standard SVR/GBDT point baselines in the asymmetric decision scenarios to quantify practical gains over traditional QE.
- Asymmetric loss results are mixed and under-explained: The linex pessimistic scenario underperforms with warped models, and the explanation (variance differences) is plausible but unverified. A simple sensitivity analysis over variance calibration, or a check with heteroscedastic likelihoods or Student-t, would make this more convincing.
- Limited scope and ablations: Feature set is small by design; however, the paper could include quick ablations on warping complexity (number of tanh terms), kernel choice impact across datasets, and significance testing. Also, computational cost and scaling are not reported, even though O(n^3) GPs may be a concern beyond the current data sizes.

Questions to authors
- Calibration: Can you report calibration diagnostics (e.g., empirical coverage of 50/80/95% predictive intervals, PIT histograms, or calibration error for quantiles)? Do warped GPs improve calibration beyond supporting the positive domain?
- Linex pessimistic case: Can you test whether the underperformance stems from variance underestimation? For example, compare to (a) a Student-t likelihood GP, (b) a heteroscedastic GP, or (c) a simple variance inflation post hoc, and report linex performance.
- Baselines for asymmetric decisions: How do your Bayes estimators compare against (i) a tuned quantile regression baseline (e.g., gradient boosting with pinball loss), and (ii) a strong point-baseline (SVR/GBDT) with thresholding tuned for the same asymmetric loss?
- Warping complexity: How sensitive are results to the number of tanh terms? Do you observe overfitting signals (e.g., worse NLPD) for I>3? Any guidance on regularization or priors for warping parameters?
- Practical deployment: How would you recommend choosing w in realistic workflows? Can you provide a simple procedure (e.g., optimize w on a validation set reflecting business KPIs)? Also, what is the runtime footprint and feasibility for batch prediction in production?

Overall, despite limited methodological novelty, this paper advances an important and timely direction for QE: uncertainty-aware evaluation and decision-making. If the authors can bolster calibration evidence and provide at least one strong non-GP baseline for the asymmetric setting, this would make a compelling case for acceptance.
