Summary and contributions
- Positions uncertainty as a first-class citizen in MT Quality Estimation (QE), advocating evaluation with Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) and demonstrating that point metrics (MAE, r) miss important differences in predictive uncertainty. Implements Gaussian Processes (GPs) with Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, and shows improved predictive densities on three QE datasets.
- Introduces a decision-theoretic framing for QE with asymmetric risks and derives minimum Bayes risk estimators from predictive posteriors (quantiles for asymmetric linear; closed form for linex), enabling post-hoc adaptation to changing operational costs without retraining. Provides empirical evidence that warped GPs are advantageous in optimistic scenarios.
- Offers empirical insights relevant to QE practice: (i) training NLL can be misleading on small data (overfitting), (ii) warping helps when targets are positive and skewed, and (iii) linex pessimistic losses may perversely favor high-variance models.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty and relevance to QE: The paper reframes QE as uncertainty-aware decision-making rather than pure point prediction. Bringing calibrated predictive distributions and asymmetric risk into QE is a meaningful shift in emphasis with practical implications for post-editing workflows and gisting.
- Principled evaluation: Advocates NLPD to compare models via full predictive distributions, and convincingly shows that traditional point metrics fail to capture uncertainty quality. This is a valuable message for the community.
- Practical decision-theoretic utility: The post-hoc Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses are operationally attractive—one probabilistic model supports multiple risk profiles without retraining, enabling flexible deployments.
- Sensible and well-grounded modeling choices: Use of Warped GPs to respect positivity and skew, and Matérn kernels to relax smoothness, are appropriate and thoughtfully motivated. The two-stage ARD optimization is standard and reasonable for small QE datasets.
- Informative empirical findings: The negative result for linex in pessimistic settings is noteworthy; the analysis hints at the interplay between variance magnitudes and asymmetric penalties, cautioning against models that over-disperse or place mass on implausible regions.

Weaknesses
- Limited technical novelty: All modeling components (GPs, Matérn, Warped GPs) and metrics (NLPD) are established. The contribution lies primarily in application and framing rather than new algorithms or theory. This may feel incremental to a broader ML/NLP audience.
- Narrow experimental scope and baselines: Experiments use a small, feature-based QE setup (17 QuEst features) and compare only GP variants. Missing comparisons to alternative probabilistic baselines (e.g., quantile regression/pinball loss, heteroscedastic/Student-t GPs, Bayesian linear/NN regressors, deep ensembles, conformal methods) weaken the empirical case that the proposed approach is preferable beyond the GP family.
- Calibration analysis is absent: Claims about “well-calibrated” uncertainty are not substantiated with standard diagnostics (PIT/reliability diagrams, CRPS). This is especially important given the interpretive claims and the puzzling linex behavior.
- Downstream validation is simulated: Asymmetric risk is evaluated via offline losses rather than an end-to-end decision task (e.g., accept/reject MT segments with a rejection option and measuring productivity/UX impact). This leaves practical impact somewhat speculative.
- Reporting/detail gaps: Important implementation details are unclear (how NLPD is computed for Warped GPs; numerical stability and monotonicity enforcement for the tanh warps; treatment of zero post-editing rates when using log warp; any priors/regularization; statistical significance). Kernel effects are weak/inconclusive and explanations are largely post-hoc; sensitivity analyses over w are limited.

Questions to authors
- Calibration: Can you report calibration diagnostics (PIT histograms, reliability plots, CRPS) to support the “well-calibrated” claim and to explain the linex result? Do your models tend to be over- or under-confident?
- Warped GP density and implementation: How exactly is NLPD computed under the tanh-based warp? Do you use exact change-of-variables with the learned monotone warp, or approximations? How do you enforce monotonicity and ensure numerical stability? What values of I (number of tanh terms) were selected across datasets, and how sensitive are results to I?
- Handling zeros/near-zeros: For log warping, did you add an epsilon to y? If so, what value, and how sensitive are results to it? Are there many zero post-editing rates?
- Baselines: Can you add at least one non-GP probabilistic baseline (e.g., quantile regression trained with pinball loss; a Student-t likelihood GP/heteroscedastic GP; a conformalized SVR or NGBoost) to demonstrate that your conclusions are not specific to GP variants?
- Decision-level evaluation: Can you include a simple downstream simulation (e.g., thresholding by posterior quantiles with a reject option) to show gains in precision/recall/productivity vs point-estimate baselines?
- Linex pessimistic setting: Can you show the distribution of predictive variances for each model and test whether variance rescaling or variance calibration (e.g., temperature scaling of log variance) mitigates the linex failure? Does a Student-t likelihood help by accommodating heavy tails without assigning mass to negative times?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and scripts, including hyperparameter settings, random seeds, and cross-validation splits?
