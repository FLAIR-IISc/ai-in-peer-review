Summary
This paper argues for modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). It focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs) with Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better handle non-Gaussian, positive-valued targets (post-editing time per token). It proposes using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as an evaluation metric and demonstrates how predictive distributions enable decision-making under asymmetric risks via Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear and linex losses. Experiments on three QE datasets compare standard GPs, Matérn kernels, and different warpings.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical case for uncertainty-aware QE: Demonstrates that probabilistic modeling (Warped GPs) improves calibrated uncertainty (as measured by NLPD) over standard GPs and that point-estimate metrics fail to capture these differences.
2) Application of asymmetric risk decision-making in QE: Shows how to derive Bayes-optimal estimators from predictive posteriors for asymmetric losses without retraining and explores optimistic/pessimistic scenarios.
3) Modeling choices for QE: Evaluates the impact of Matérn kernels and parametric warping (tanh vs. log) on uncertainty quality and performance.

Strengths
- Clear and relevant motivation: The paper convincingly argues that uncertainty is critical in QE workflows (post-editing, gisting) and that point estimates are insufficient for risk-sensitive decisions. The asymmetric risk discussion is practical and directly tied to realistic scenarios.
- Sound probabilistic methodology: The use of GPs, ARD, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs is technically correct and appropriate for small data regimes. Employing NLPD (a proper scoring rule on test data) to evaluate uncertainty quality is methodologically solid and more informative than MAE/pearson r.
- Evidence that uncertainty matters: Results show substantial gains in NLL/NLPD with warping and that differences in uncertainty modeling are largely invisible to MAE and r, supporting the central claim about the value of probabilistic evaluation.
- Decision-theoretic framing: The Bayes risk estimators for AL and linex losses are correctly derived and demonstrate how a single probabilistic model can serve multiple operational scenarios without retraining, which is a practical takeaway for production settings.
- Clarity and exposition: The paper is well-organized, with accessible explanations of GP modeling choices (kernels, warping), evaluation metrics, and the asymmetric loss framework.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, and NLPD are established; asymmetric losses and their Bayes estimators are classic. The contribution is primarily an application to QE and an empirical case study, rather than a methodological advance. To strengthen impact, comparisons to alternative probabilistic approaches for positive targets (e.g., log-normal likelihoods, truncated Gaussians) or heteroscedastic GPs would be valuable.
- Dated and narrow experimental scope: The evaluation uses small, feature-based QE datasets with a modest 17-feature set. There are no comparisons to strong modern baselines (e.g., neural QE, quantile regression, probabilistic NNs) nor to standard non-GP baselines (e.g., SVR) to contextualize the benefits of probabilistic modeling. This limits claims about practical superiority.
- Incomplete uncertainty calibration assessment: NLPD is informative, but there are no calibration diagnostics (predictive interval coverage, reliability diagrams, PIT histograms) or proper scores like CRPS. Claims about over/under-confidence and variance effects (especially in the linex pessimistic case) remain speculative without such analyses.
- Asymmetric risk analysis is underdeveloped: The choice of weights is ad hoc and sensitivity analysis is limited. The linex pessimistic scenario yields unstable behavior on en-de and mixed results overall; explanations attribute this to variance differences but lack quantitative support. No task-relevant decision metrics (e.g., risk-coverage curves, utility at fixed recall/precision for keep/discard) are provided.
- Reproducibility and ablation detail: Important implementation details are missing (code availability, seeds, wall-clock times, optimization settings, prior choices/regularization on warping, criteria for selecting the number of tanh terms). The two-pass optimization is described, but robustness to initialization and the risk of overfitting with warping are not systematically assessed.

Questions to authors
- Why choose warping over likelihood modeling tailored to positive targets (e.g., log-normal, Gamma, or truncated Gaussian likelihoods in a GP)? Can you compare Warped GP to a GP with a log-normal likelihood?
- How calibrated are the predictive distributions? Please report coverage of predictive intervals (e.g., 50/90%), reliability diagrams, or CRPS, and include PIT histograms.
- How sensitive are results to the number of tanh terms (I), initialization, and regularization? Do you observe overfitting with larger I? Any cross-validated selection of I?
- Did you try heteroscedastic GPs to model input-dependent noise, which is plausible in QE? Would that address the pessimistic linex behavior you attribute to variance?
- Can you provide decision-oriented evaluations for the keep/discard use-case, such as risk-coverage or cost curves, and operating points that reflect realistic constraints?
- Please report run times and scalability considerations, and provide code or sufficient details to reproduce the experiments.
- How would your conclusions change when compared against modern neural QE systems with uncertainty (e.g., Bayesian/deep ensembles/MC dropout) or quantile regression baselines?

Overall assessment
The paper presents a well-argued, sound, and useful application of probabilistic modeling and decision theory to QE, with clear evidence that uncertainty-aware evaluation reveals differences missed by point metrics. However, the methodological novelty is limited, experiments are small-scale and dated, and the analysis of calibration and asymmetric risk is incomplete. I view this as borderline; with stronger baselines, calibration diagnostics, and decision-centric evaluation, it would make a compelling case.
