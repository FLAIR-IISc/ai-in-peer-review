Summary
This paper argues for modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE) via Gaussian Processes (GPs). It revisits GP regression with ARD Matérn kernels and extends to Warped GPs to respect positive-only targets (post-editing rates). The authors advocate Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a proper scoring rule to compare models’ uncertainty calibration, and demonstrate that point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) often fail to reveal differences in uncertainty quality. Finally, they show how well-calibrated predictive posteriors can be exploited under asymmetric risk via Bayes estimators for tilted absolute (quantile) and linex losses, illustrating optimistic versus pessimistic operational settings.

Contributions (as I see them)
- Empirical case for using Warped GPs and NLPD in QE: Warping substantially improves likelihood-based metrics over standard GPs; NLPD reveals differences unseen by MAE/r.
- Application-oriented framing of uncertainty for asymmetric decision-making: derive and evaluate Bayes estimators for AL and linex losses to emulate practical QE use-cases without retraining.
- Examination of kernel and warping choices for QE: comparison of EQ vs Matérn and log vs parametric tanh warps on three QE datasets.

Strengths
- Focus on proper probabilistic evaluation: Using NLPD on test data is appropriate and superior to MAE/r for assessing uncertainty quality. The discussion on NLL vs NLPD and overfitting is sound and aligns with GP best practice.
- Sensible model choices for positive targets: Warped GPs directly address the support mismatch for post-editing time; the change-of-variables predictive density and use of the posterior median are correctly derived and articulated.
- Practical relevance through asymmetric risk: Mapping QE use-cases to AL/linex losses and showing how to obtain Bayes estimators without retraining is valuable for deployment, where risk preferences can change.
- Clear experimental protocol and fairness: Cross-validation across three language pairs, controlled feature set (QuEst 17), and kernel/warp ablations help isolate the effect of uncertainty modeling rather than feature engineering.
- Insightful negative result: The linex pessimistic setting underperforms warped models due to variance dependence, a useful cautionary observation about the interaction between proper scores and predictive variance.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines for uncertainty: The comparison omits competitive probabilistic alternatives that also enforce positivity or heavy tails, e.g., GP with log link/log-normal likelihood, Gamma/Inverse-Gaussian likelihoods, Student-t likelihood, heteroscedastic GPs, and non-GP baselines with uncertainty (quantile regression, gradient-boosted quantiles, quantile random forests, Bayesian linear regression). Since warping and proper likelihoods address similar issues (support, skew), the gains attributed to warping may be achievable by simpler or more robust models.
- Insufficient calibration diagnostics: NLPD is necessary but not sufficient. The paper lacks coverage tests (e.g., empirical coverage of 50/90% intervals), PIT histograms, reliability diagrams, and sharpness-vs-calibration analysis. The observed “over/underconfidence” claims would be better supported with these diagnostics.
- Incomplete treatment of asymmetric losses: For AL, optimal estimators are quantiles of p(y|x). For Warped GPs, computing general quantiles requires numerical inversion or sampling; the paper provides the median but does not detail how non-median quantiles are computed. For linex, the estimator formula given assumes a normal predictive law; for non-Gaussian warped posteriors, the formula is approximate—this limitation should be acknowledged and alternatives (e.g., Monte Carlo) provided. The extreme linex behavior suggests numerical/robustness issues not fully addressed.
- Reporting and statistical rigor: The paper refers to tables but (in this version) does not show numeric results, variances, or statistical significance across folds. Claims like “drastically decreases both NLL and NLPD” need quantified deltas and variance. Also, results are mixed across datasets; more careful analysis of when Matérn/warp help (e.g., by data size or noise profile) would strengthen conclusions.
- Scope of datasets and features: Using only 17 QuEst features is understandable for comparability, but it limits external validity. It would help to (a) augment with stronger, modern QE features or representations (e.g., neural sentence encoders) to test whether conclusions about uncertainty hold; (b) demonstrate out-of-domain robustness; and (c) ablate dataset size to support the “low-data” GP motivation.

Questions to Authors
- How were non-median AL quantiles computed for Warped GPs? Did you use numerical inversion of the CDF, Gauss-Hermite quadrature with monotone mapping, or sampling? Please detail the algorithm and its computational cost.
- For the linex estimator, do you use the normal-form formula for warped posteriors? If so, this is an approximation. Did you assess the bias by Monte Carlo integration under the true (warped) predictive density?
- Why not compare to GP with log-normal or Gamma likelihoods (which also enforce positivity) and to Student-t likelihoods (robust to outliers)? These would be strong baselines against Warped GPs for NLPD and asymmetric losses.
- Can you report empirical coverage of predictive intervals and PIT histograms for each model? This would substantiate the calibration claims beyond NLPD.
- What optimizer and regularization were used for warping parameters (e.g., bounds on a_i, b_i)? How sensitive are results to number of tanh terms and random restarts? Any evidence of overfitting in small data (en–es) via learning curves?

Additional comments
- Consider adding CRPS as an additional proper scoring rule, less sensitive to tail mis-specification than log score.
- The observed linex instability suggests constraining or rescaling w and/or tempering predictive variances; discussing practical guidance would help practitioners.
- Minor: please ensure tables/figures and exact numeric results are included with standard errors; fix reference formatting issues.

Overall, the paper makes a useful, application-driven case for probabilistic QE with Warped GPs and proper scoring, but would benefit from stronger baselines, calibration diagnostics, and clearer treatment of asymmetric estimators.
