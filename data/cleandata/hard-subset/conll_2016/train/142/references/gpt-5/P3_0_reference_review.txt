Summary and claimed contributions
This paper argues for modeling predictive uncertainty in sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE) using Gaussian Processes (GPs). It revisits kernels (Matérn vs. RBF) and introduces Warped GPs to handle strictly positive targets (post-editing rates/times). It advocates Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) for evaluating uncertainty and demonstrates how predictive distributions enable minimum Bayes risk (MBR) decision-making under asymmetric losses (asymmetric linear and linex), without retraining. Experiments on three QE datasets (en–es, fr–en, en–de) with 17 QuEst features compare kernels and warpings, and analyze optimistic vs. pessimistic risk settings.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) An empirical study showing that predictive distributions (via GPs) and NLPD provide information not captured by MAE/Pearson r in QE, with Warped GPs yielding substantially better NLL/NLPD.
2) A demonstration of loss-aware, post-hoc MBR decision rules for asymmetric risk in QE using GP posteriors (quantiles for asymmetric L1, closed-form estimator for linex), avoiding retraining.
3) A practical analysis of kernel/warping choices for QE uncertainty, including observations about overfitting on small data (NLL vs NLPD divergence) and the behavior of linex under variance differences.

Strengths
- Clear and timely motivation: The paper convincingly argues that point estimates are insufficient in production QE workflows (post-editing and gisting). It connects uncertainty calibration to real decisions and shows how to turn calibrated posteriors into tailored decision rules without retraining as loss preferences change.

- Sound probabilistic methodology: The GP treatment is correct and well-explained. Using ARD, Matérn kernels for rougher functions, and monotone warpings for positive targets are principled choices. The connection between asymmetric linear loss and posterior quantiles is spot-on and useful for practitioners.

- Evaluation with proper scoring: Using test NLPD to evaluate predictive uncertainty is appropriate and highlights pitfalls of relying on training NLL (overfitting on small datasets). The finding that point metrics largely fail to reflect uncertainty differences is important for the QE community.

- Insightful analysis of asymmetric losses: The post-hoc MBR estimators are practical. The observation that linex can favor high-variance models (and penalize variance-suppressing warps) is nuanced and valuable for selecting losses aligned with operational goals.

- Simple, reusable protocol: Ten-fold CV, unified features across language pairs, and post-hoc application of estimators offer a clean comparison that others can replicate or extend to different losses.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to GP literature: The techniques (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, MBR under asymmetric losses) are well-established. The contribution is largely a domain application and empirical study for QE. The paper would be stronger with more novel modeling or diagnostic tools specific to QE uncertainty.

- Missing strong baselines for uncertainty: The study does not compare to other probabilistic or distributional forecasters beyond GPs. Quantile regression (linear/SVR), heteroscedastic regression, gamma/log-normal likelihood GPs, Bayesian neural nets (e.g., MC dropout/probabilistic backprop), deep ensembles, or quantile regression forests are relevant and would contextualize the gains and the linex behavior.

- Incomplete uncertainty calibration assessment: NLL/NLPD evaluate sharpness and calibration jointly but do not diagnose miscalibration. There are no predictive interval coverage rates, PIT histograms, or proper scoring rules like CRPS. Since the core claim is about uncertainty quality, calibration diagnostics are needed.

- Modeling limitations and under-exploration: The targets are positive and likely heavy-tailed/heteroscedastic. Warping addresses support, but the paper does not explore alternative likelihoods (log-normal, gamma) or heteroscedastic GPs, which are natural baselines for time/rate data. The kernel analysis is shallow (weak or inconclusive Matérn vs. RBF differences) and there is no discussion of ARD lengthscales/feature relevance.

- Evaluation design lacks decision-centric metrics: While asymmetric losses are used, there is no evaluation on a realistic accept/reject decision task (e.g., coverage/precision/recall of “forward for post-editing” at a cost curve), which would better ground the claimed utility in workflows. Statistical significance of improvements is not reported.

- Reproducibility and implementation details: Important details are missing or under-specified: warping initialization and monotonicity constraints, number of tanh terms selection, optimization settings and convergence criteria, computational cost, numerical stability of inverse warps, and whether results are averaged over multiple random seeds beyond restarts. Code and splits are not released.

Questions to authors
- Calibration: Can you report predictive interval coverage (e.g., 50/90%), PIT histograms, or CRPS to assess calibration beyond NLPD? Do the warped models yield better-calibrated intervals?

- Baselines: How do your models compare to (i) quantile regression (linear/SVR), (ii) GP with log-normal or gamma likelihood, (iii) heteroscedastic GP, (iv) Bayesian neural nets (e.g., MC dropout) or deep ensembles in terms of NLPD and calibration?

- Warping details: How do you enforce monotonicity of the tanh-sum warp in training? How are parameters initialized? Did you try Bayesian Warped GPs or regularization to mitigate overfitting? What is the runtime/complexity impact?

- Linex behavior: Can you quantify posterior variances across models and show how the linex estimator shifts predictions? Is the poorer performance under pessimistic linex driven by miscalibration of tails vs. variance magnitude?

- Decision evaluation: For a concrete post-editing gate, can you show utility curves (e.g., cost-sensitive accept/reject with asymmetric penalties) comparing warped vs. standard GP and a point-estimate baseline?

- Modern QE context: Although you focus on features, how do conclusions change with neural sentence representations (e.g., XLM-R) or modern QE datasets? Does the advantage of Warped GPs persist?

Overall evaluation
The paper is well-motivated and technically solid, and it brings useful uncertainty-centric evaluation and decision framing to QE. However, methodological novelty is modest and the empirical study omits key probabilistic baselines and calibration diagnostics. With stronger comparisons, calibration analysis, and decision-centric evaluation, this would be a compelling contribution. As is, I view it as a valuable but incremental empirical study.
