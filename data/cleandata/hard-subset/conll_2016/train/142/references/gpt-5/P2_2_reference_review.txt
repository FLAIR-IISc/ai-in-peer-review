Summary and main contributions
This paper argues for modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs). It contrasts standard GPs with Warped GPs and Matern kernels, evaluates models with negative log predictive density (NLPD) in addition to point metrics, and demonstrates the utility of well-calibrated predictive distributions under asymmetric risk via Bayes risk estimators.

Contributions (as I see them):
1) Demonstration that Warped GPs substantially improve probabilistic fit (NLL, NLPD) over standard GPs for QE, and that point metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) often fail to capture improvements in uncertainty modeling.
2) Practical framework for asymmetric decision settings (pessimistic/optimistic) in QE using Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear and linex losses without retraining, showing when uncertainty-aware models help and where they may fail.
3) Empirical study of kernel/likelihood choices (Matern vs EQ; tanh vs log warping) on three QE datasets, highlighting dataset-size effects and potential overfitting with NLL vs test NLPD.

Strengths
- Important problem framing and practical relevance: The paper clearly motivates why point estimates are insufficient for operational QE (post-editing and gisting workflows). The emphasis on predictive distributions and asymmetric risk is aligned with real-world needs and goes beyond the typical focus on MAE/r.
- Methodologically sound use of proper scoring: NLPD is an appropriate, widely used proper scoring rule for probabilistic regression. The experiments convincingly show that improvements in uncertainty (via warping) are not reflected in MAE/r, justifying a shift in evaluation practice for QE.
- Clear, principled approach to asymmetric risk: Using Bayes estimators for AL and linex losses is theoretically justified and practically appealing since it decouples training from scenario-specific utilities. The “no retraining” message is operationally valuable.
- Sensible modeling choices and analysis: Warped GPs address the positive-support nature of post-editing times; Matern kernels relax smoothness. The observation that log-transform is sometimes sufficient on smaller data vs tanh warping on larger data is insightful and grounded in the results.
- Honest discussion of failure modes: The analysis of linex pessimistic settings, where standard GP variance can outperform warped models due to variance-sensitive penalties, is candid and useful. It underscores that not all proper losses reward “nicer” support equally and points to future work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling; contribution is primarily empirical for QE: Warped GPs, Matern kernels, NLPD evaluation, and Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses are well-established. The novelty lies in applying these to QE and presenting empirical evidence. For a research venue, stronger methodological innovation or broader comparisons would help.
- Narrow set of baselines and missing calibration diagnostics: The study focuses only on GPs. Absent are uncertainty baselines such as quantile regression (e.g., gradient boosting or pinball loss), quantile random forests, bootstrap ensembles, Bayesian linear models, Student-t likelihood GPs, heteroscedastic GPs, and Bayesian neural networks/MC dropout. Also missing are standard calibration diagnostics for regression distributions (e.g., coverage of predictive intervals, PIT histograms, CRPS). This limits claims about “well-calibrated” uncertainty.
- Limited datasets, features, and scope: All datasets are relatively small and single-system/single-post-editor in two cases. Only 17 QuEst features are used, which is far from state-of-the-art QE feature sets or modern neural QE. The extent to which conclusions generalize to today’s QE settings (e.g., DA/HTER labels, contextual or neural features) is unclear.
- Evaluation design gaps: No statistical significance testing is reported; some effects look small in point metrics. Choice of asymmetric loss weights is ad hoc and very limited; numerical issues for linex suggest sensitivity that is not deeply investigated. There is no computational/latency analysis; Warped GPs can be more complex to train, and the impact on practical deployment is not addressed.
- Notational/clarity issues and minor inaccuracies: The EQ kernel form appears nonstandard (scale and 1/2 factor); the NLPD formula as p(ŷi=yi|xi) is unconventional (it is the predictive density at yi). Some equation typesetting is rough. While not fatal, these hinder reproducibility/clarity.

Questions to authors
- Calibration: Can you report additional calibration diagnostics (e.g., empirical coverage of 50/80/95% intervals, PIT histograms, CRPS) to substantiate the claim of better-calibrated uncertainty for Warped GPs?
- Baselines: Why not include quantile regression (pinball loss), quantile random forests, heteroscedastic or Student-t likelihood GPs, or simple bootstrap ensembles? These are common uncertainty baselines and would strengthen the empirical case.
- Practical decision-making: Can you demonstrate end-to-end decision outcomes (e.g., acceptance rates, recall/precision at a cost curve) for the post-editing and gisting scenarios, not just loss values?
- Sensitivity to w and robustness: How sensitive are conclusions to the choice of w for AL and linex? Could you provide a sweep or curves, and explain the numerical instability for linex in the pessimistic case?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and splits? What optimizer settings, initialization, and regularization were used to stabilize Warped GP training?

Overall assessment
This is a timely and practically motivated paper that makes a strong case for probabilistic evaluation and asymmetric risk in QE. The empirical findings are useful, especially the demonstration that point metrics can obscure meaningful differences in uncertainty. However, methodological novelty is limited, the baseline and calibration coverage is narrow, and modern QE settings are not represented. I view this as a solid, well-argued empirical study advocating better practices, with clear room for strengthening via broader baselines, calibration analysis, and decision-centric evaluation.
