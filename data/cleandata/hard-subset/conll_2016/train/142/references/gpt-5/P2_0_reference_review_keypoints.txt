- Paper advocates modeling predictive uncertainty in MT quality estimation (QE) and evaluates Gaussian Processes (GPs), especially Warped GPs and Matérn kernels, using proper probabilistic metrics.
- Demonstrates that output warping to enforce positivity substantially improves negative log predictive density (NLPD/NLL) on QE tasks.
- Shows asymmetric decision-making can be handled post-hoc via Bayes estimators from predictive posteriors (quantiles for asymmetric linear loss, closed-form for linex).
- Contribution 1: Empirical study that NLPD reveals differences between models invisible to MAE/Pearson r, with warping improving predictive densities.
- Contribution 2: Principled treatment of asymmetric risk for QE yielding practical, retraining-free estimators for different loss types.
- Contribution 3: Application-oriented assessment of GP variants (Matérn, Warped GPs) with insights on train NLL vs. test NLPD and linex behavior.
- Strength: Clear motivation and correct use of proper scoring (NLPD) to evaluate full predictive distributions.
- Strength: Methodology is sound—ARD Matérn kernels, Warped GPs, two-pass hyperparameter optimization, and cross-validation are appropriate for small QE datasets.
- Strength: Empirical consistency—output warping consistently reduces NLL/NLPD across language pairs.
- Strength: Decision-theoretic extension is practical and interpretable; authors analyze cases where warped models may underperform under pessimistic linex.
- Strength: Techniques and evaluation practices generalize to other text regression tasks requiring calibrated uncertainty.
- Weakness: Novelty is limited—GPs, Warped GPs, and NLPD are established; asymmetric loss application mainly uses standard Bayes estimators.
- Weakness: Evaluation is constrained—older small datasets, limited features, and no comparisons to competitive uncertainty-aware baselines (quantile forests/GBMs, SVR ensembles, heteroscedastic GPs, Bayesian NN).
- Weakness: Calibration analysis is incomplete—only NLL/NLPD reported, missing PIT, interval coverage, CRPS, etc.
- Weakness: Asymmetric risk experiments lack grounding—loss weights seem ad hoc, cause numerical issues (exploding linex), and aren’t tied to concrete downstream utilities or cost–coverage curves.
- Weakness: Reproducibility/details lacking—missing implementation specifics (warping init/regularization, tanh terms sensitivity, convergence, computational cost), no code, no statistical significance reporting.
- Question: Provide calibration diagnostics (PIT, predictive interval coverage, CRPS) to substantiate calibration claims?
- Question: How sensitive are results to number of tanh terms and initialization; any priors/regularization or ablation performed?
- Question: Why not compare against quantile regression forests/GBMs, SVR ensembles, heteroscedastic GPs, or Bayesian neural baselines to better position the approach?
- Question: Can asymmetric losses be connected to realistic QE utilities (e.g., pass-to-PE filtering) and can you provide cost curves or guidance for choosing loss weights?
- Question: What are training/inference times and memory footprints per fold, and how do models scale with features/dataset size?
- Question: Please report per-fold variability, conduct statistical tests, and consider reporting CRPS as an additional proper scoring rule.
- Overall: Clear, timely, and empirically supportive work for uncertainty-aware NLP, but with modest novelty and evaluation/calibration and reproducibility gaps that should be addressed.
