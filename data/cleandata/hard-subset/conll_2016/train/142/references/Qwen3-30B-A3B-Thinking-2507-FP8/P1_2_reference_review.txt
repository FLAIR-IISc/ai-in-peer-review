

# Peer Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a critical gap in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) by exploring the use of uncertainty estimation in addition to point predictions. Traditional QE models provide only point estimates (e.g., using MAE, RMSE, Pearson's r), which are insufficient for decision-making in practical workflows like post-editing. The authors argue that well-calibrated uncertainty estimates can significantly improve decision-making processes, particularly in scenarios with asymmetric risk (e.g., where underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating it).

The main contributions of this work, as I see them (in order of strength):

1. **Demonstrating the superiority of NLPD as an evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models**: The authors show that traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling, while NLPD effectively distinguishes between models with similar point predictions but different uncertainty characteristics.

2. **Proposing and validating Warped Gaussian Processes with parametric warping functions for QE**: The paper demonstrates that Warped GPs with tanh-based warping functions outperform standard GPs and log-transformed approaches for modeling strictly positive quality scores, particularly on larger datasets.

3. **Demonstrating practical applications of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios**: The authors show how predictive distributions can be leveraged for different operational contexts (pessimistic vs. optimistic post-editing scenarios), providing concrete evidence of the value of uncertainty estimation.

## Strengths

1. **Strong justification for a new evaluation metric**: The paper makes a compelling case that NLPD should be the primary metric for evaluating probabilistic QE models. The authors clearly demonstrate that traditional metrics like MAE and Pearson's r fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, while NLPD properly penalizes overconfident models. This is particularly important for practitioners who need to select models based on their uncertainty characteristics, not just point estimates. The experimental results showing NLPD's ability to distinguish between warping functions (where MAE cannot) are particularly convincing.

2. **Practical relevance of uncertainty in real-world scenarios**: The paper doesn't just propose theoretical improvements but demonstrates concrete applications of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic post-editing). This bridges the gap between theoretical model improvements and practical decision-making, showing exactly how uncertainty information can improve real-world translation workflows. The connection to business scenarios like gisting (where more published translations increase sales opportunities) is particularly compelling.

3. **Rigorous and well-designed experimental evaluation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments across three different language pairs with varying dataset sizes (en-es, fr-en, en-de), using standard QE features and 10-fold cross-validation. They systematically compare different kernel types (Matérn vs. EQ), warping functions (log vs. tanh), and evaluation metrics. The results are clearly presented with appropriate statistical analysis, and the authors thoughtfully discuss the impact of dataset size on model performance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the proposed methods**: While the application of Warped GPs and Matérn kernels to QE is novel for the QE community, these are well-established techniques in the GP literature. The paper doesn't sufficiently establish why these specific methods are particularly suited to QE beyond general claims about noise and positive response variables. The authors could have provided a more thorough theoretical justification for why these particular extensions are better for QE than alternatives, or why they should be preferred over other probabilistic approaches.

2. **Lack of comparison with other probabilistic models**: The paper focuses exclusively on Gaussian Processes, but there are other probabilistic models that could provide uncertainty estimates (e.g., Bayesian Neural Networks, Bayesian Ridge Regression). The authors don't compare their GP-based approaches to these alternatives, which weakens their claim that GPs are the "best" approach for uncertainty modeling in QE. This comparison would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.

3. **Inconsistent results across datasets**: The paper shows inconsistent performance across the three language pairs, particularly for the smallest dataset (en-es). While the authors attribute this to dataset size, they don't provide sufficient analysis to confirm this hypothesis or discuss how to handle such inconsistencies in practice. This raises questions about the robustness of their findings and the generalizability of their approach across different QE scenarios.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "Warped GPs provide an easy and elegant way to model response variables with non-Gaussian behaviour within the GP framework." Can the authors elaborate on why they believe the parametric tanh warping function is particularly suited for QE compared to other potential warping functions (e.g., polynomial, spline-based)? What specific characteristics of QE data make tanh particularly effective?

2. In the asymmetric risk scenarios, the authors note that "the results for linex are consistently worse than the standard GP baseline" in the pessimistic scenario. They hypothesize this is because "warped models tend to have less variance." Could the authors provide more evidence for this hypothesis, perhaps by examining the variance distributions of the predictive distributions across different models?

3. The paper mentions that "the gains from using a Matèrn kernel over EQ are less conclusive." Given that the Matérn kernel was motivated by the noisy nature of QE data, could the authors provide more detailed analysis on why the kernel choice doesn't seem to have a clear impact across datasets? Is there a specific characteristic of the en-es dataset that makes the Matérn kernel less effective?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written, well-structured, and makes a valuable contribution to the QE community by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation. The connection between theoretical uncertainty modeling and practical decision-making is particularly strong. I recommend this paper for acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the novelty of the methods and the comparison with alternative probabilistic models. The paper has clear practical relevance and would be valuable for both researchers and practitioners in the QE field.
