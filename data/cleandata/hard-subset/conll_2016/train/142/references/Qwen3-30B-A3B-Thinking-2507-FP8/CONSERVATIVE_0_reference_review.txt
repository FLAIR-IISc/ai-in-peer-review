

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of uncertainty estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate approaches are insufficient for practical decision-making scenarios. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with two extensions: Matérn kernels and warped GPs, to better model uncertainty. They introduce NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r), and demonstrate how these probabilistic models can be applied in asymmetric risk scenarios.

The main contributions I see (in order of strength):
1. The compelling argument for uncertainty estimation in practical QE applications and the demonstration that NLPD captures uncertainty information that standard metrics miss.
2. The careful theoretical development of GP extensions (Matérn kernels, warped GPs) for the QE task, with clear explanations of their advantages.
3. The application of these probabilistic models to asymmetric loss scenarios, showing how uncertainty information can be practically utilized in different decision contexts.

## Strengths

1. **Strong justification for uncertainty estimation**: The paper effectively argues why uncertainty estimates are crucial for practical QE applications (e.g., post-editing scenarios), providing concrete examples where point estimates alone would lead to suboptimal decisions. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it addresses a real-world limitation of current QE systems.

2. **Clear demonstration of NLPD's superiority**: The paper provides convincing evidence that NLPD captures uncertainty information that standard metrics like MAE and Pearson's r cannot. The analysis of the en-es dataset showing how NLL fails as a reliability metric for small datasets is particularly valuable and well-supported.

3. **Theoretical soundness of model extensions**: The authors provide thorough theoretical explanations for their proposed GP extensions (Matérn kernels, warped GPs), correctly identifying the limitations of standard GP assumptions for QE data (noisy, positive-valued quality scores). The discussion of how these extensions address specific problems in QE is methodologically rigorous.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with established baselines**: The paper focuses exclusively on comparing different GP variants but fails to compare with state-of-the-art non-probabilistic QE methods that might already provide competitive point estimates. The authors acknowledge that SVR is the "most popular method" but don't demonstrate how their probabilistic approach compares to it in terms of point estimate quality. Without this comparison, it's unclear whether the benefits of uncertainty estimation outweigh the potential loss in point estimate performance.

2. **Inadequate validation of claimed improvements**: The paper claims "gains" from using warped GPs and Matérn kernels but provides only marginal improvements (e.g., "the gains from using a Matérn kernel over EQ are less conclusive"). The results show inconsistent patterns across datasets, with different models performing best on different language pairs. This inconsistency suggests the improvements may be dataset-specific rather than generalizable, yet the paper doesn't provide sufficient statistical analysis to support the claimed significance of these differences.

3. **Overstated generalizability claims**: The paper states that "the methods we explore in this paper can be applied to any text regression task," but provides no evidence beyond the specific QE context. This is an overreach given the limited scope of their experiments (three small datasets, one specific application). The paper doesn't demonstrate that the proposed methods work well on other text regression tasks, making this claim unsupported.

4. **Insufficient discussion of practical implementation challenges**: While the paper presents a theoretically sound approach, it doesn't address practical implementation challenges that would affect adoption in real-world QE systems. For instance, GPs are computationally expensive compared to SVR or neural networks, which are commonly used in QE. The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of their proposed methods or how they scale to larger datasets.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do your probabilistic GP models compare to state-of-the-art non-probabilistic QE baselines (e.g., SVR or neural network-based approaches) in terms of point estimate performance? Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the added complexity for uncertainty estimation is justified.

2. The paper shows inconsistent results across datasets (e.g., different warping functions perform best on different language pairs). Can you provide statistical significance tests to support the claimed improvements, rather than just reporting average scores?

3. Given the computational cost of GPs, how would your approach scale to larger QE datasets (e.g., the full WMT datasets with tens of thousands of sentences)? What would be the trade-off between uncertainty estimation quality and computational resources?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the discussion about uncertainty estimation in QE, with strong theoretical grounding and practical motivation. However, the lack of comparison with established baselines, insufficient validation of claimed improvements, and overreaching generalizability claims prevent me from recommending acceptance in their current form. The authors should strengthen their paper by comparing with standard QE baselines, providing statistical significance tests for their improvements, and tempering their generalizability claims. With these revisions, the paper would make a more compelling contribution to the field.
