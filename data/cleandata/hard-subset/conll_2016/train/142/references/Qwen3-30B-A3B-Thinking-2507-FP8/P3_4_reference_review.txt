

# Peer Review: "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses a critical gap in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) by exploring prediction uncertainty through Gaussian Processes (GPs) with extensions. The authors argue that traditional QE models provide only point estimates, which are insufficient for decision-making processes in real-world translation workflows (e.g., post-editing). Their main contributions are:

1. Demonstrating that NLPD is a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models compared to traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r), as it captures uncertainty information that point-based metrics miss.
2. Proposing and evaluating extensions to GP models for QE, specifically Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs with tanh-based warping functions, showing improved performance on multiple language pairs.
3. Demonstrating practical applications of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., post-editing where underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating).

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation for Uncertainty Estimation**: The paper compellingly argues that point estimates alone are inadequate for real-world QE applications. The post-editing scenario is well-motivated and clearly demonstrates how uncertainty estimates can improve decision-making processes (e.g., "an MT segment could be forwarded for post-editing only if a QE model assigns a high quality score with low uncertainty"). This is a significant practical contribution to the field.

2. **Thorough Theoretical Foundation**: The paper provides a clear and accessible explanation of Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs, making it valuable for readers unfamiliar with these probabilistic modeling techniques. The authors correctly identify the limitations of standard GP assumptions (e.g., Gaussian likelihood for strictly positive quality scores) and provide appropriate extensions.

3. **Compelling Evidence for NLPD as a Better Metric**: The experiments convincingly show that traditional metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling between models. For example, "the differences in uncertainty modelling are by and large not captured by the point estimate metrics" (Table 1), with Warped GPs showing significant NLPD improvements that aren't reflected in MAE. This is a critical contribution to how QE models should be evaluated.

4. **Practical Application in Asymmetric Risk Scenarios**: The exploration of how uncertainty information can be leveraged in asymmetric loss scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic) is novel and highly relevant to real-world translation workflows. The paper provides a clear method for deriving Bayes risk estimators without retraining models, which is valuable for practitioners.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Alternative Probabilistic Models**: The paper exclusively focuses on Gaussian Processes but doesn't adequately address why other probabilistic models (e.g., Bayesian Neural Networks, Bayesian SVR) aren't considered. The introduction mentions "other common models used in QE can only provide point estimates," but this claim is too broad. Bayesian Neural Networks have been successfully applied to QE (e.g., Hernández-Lobato and Adams, 2015), and a comparative study would strengthen the paper's argument for GPs being uniquely suited for uncertainty estimation.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Dataset-Specific Behavior**: The paper notes that the en-es dataset (smaller size) behaves differently from the larger fr-en and en-de datasets, but doesn't thoroughly investigate why. For instance, the log warping function performs better for en-es while tanh-based warping is superior for larger datasets. Understanding this dataset-size dependency is crucial for practical application, but the paper only mentions it as a "belief" without empirical investigation.

3. **Superficial Treatment of Asymmetric Risk Results**: The paper's results on asymmetric risk scenarios show that Warped GPs perform well in optimistic settings but poorly in pessimistic settings with linex loss. The explanation ("warped models tend to have less variance") is plausible but lacks deeper investigation. The authors should explore whether this is a fundamental limitation of Warped GPs or if it can be addressed with model modifications.

4. **Lack of Practical Implementation Guidance**: While the paper demonstrates the theoretical benefits of uncertainty estimation, it doesn't provide sufficient guidance on implementing these models in real-world translation pipelines. For practitioners, knowing how to calibrate uncertainty estimates for different MT systems and translation domains would be valuable.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states "other common models used in QE can only provide point estimates" but acknowledges that Bayesian approaches exist. Could you clarify why you believe GPs are uniquely suited for uncertainty estimation in QE compared to other probabilistic models, and why you didn't include a comparison with Bayesian Neural Networks?

2. The en-es dataset (smaller size) behaves differently from the larger datasets. Can you provide more insight into why this might be the case, and how your findings might generalize to even smaller datasets (e.g., <500 instances)?

3. The paper shows that Warped GPs with tanh-based warping outperform standard GPs in optimistic scenarios but perform poorly with linex loss in pessimistic scenarios. Is this a fundamental limitation of Warped GPs, or could it be addressed by modifying the warping function or incorporating additional uncertainty calibration?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of Quality Estimation by demonstrating the importance of uncertainty estimation and providing a principled framework for evaluating probabilistic QE models. The work is well-written, theoretically sound, and addresses a practical problem in MT workflows. While the focus on GPs alone is somewhat narrow, the paper's core contribution—demonstrating that NLPD is a superior metric for uncertainty-aware QE models—has significant implications for the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above.
