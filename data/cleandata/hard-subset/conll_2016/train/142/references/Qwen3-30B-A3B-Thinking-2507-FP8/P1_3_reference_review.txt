

# Peer Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary
This paper investigates the importance of uncertainty estimation in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate approaches are insufficient for practical decision-making in translation workflows. The authors focus on Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a probabilistic modeling framework that can provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. They propose two model extensions: Matérn kernels to handle noisy data better than standard exponentiated quadratic kernels, and warped GPs to properly model strictly positive quality scores. The authors evaluate these models using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), demonstrating that this metric captures uncertainty information better than traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r). They further show how predictive distributions can be used in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic decision-making in post-editing).

## Main Contributions
1. The paper establishes NLPD as a superior evaluation metric for QE models that properly captures uncertainty information, demonstrating that it better distinguishes between models than traditional point-estimate metrics.
2. The authors propose and evaluate two model extensions (Matérn kernels and warped GPs) specifically tailored for QE's characteristics (noisy data, strictly positive scores).
3. The paper demonstrates practical applications of uncertainty estimation in asymmetric risk scenarios, showing how predictive distributions can improve decision-making in real-world translation workflows.

## Strengths
1. **Strong Practical Motivation**: The paper provides a compelling, well-structured argument for why uncertainty estimation matters in real-world QE applications. The examples of post-editing and gisting scenarios clearly illustrate how uncertainty information can lead to better decisions than point estimates alone. This practical relevance addresses a significant gap in the QE literature.

2. **Rigorous Evaluation of Uncertainty**: The authors effectively demonstrate that NLPD captures differences in uncertainty modeling that traditional metrics miss. Their results show that while MAE and Pearson's r don't distinguish between different uncertainty models, NLPD does, which is a crucial contribution to the field. This evaluation approach is both principled and well-executed.

3. **Novel Application of Asymmetric Losses**: The paper successfully applies asymmetric loss functions (AL and linex) to QE, showing how predictive distributions can be leveraged for different decision-making scenarios. This bridges a gap between econometric techniques and NLP applications, offering a valuable framework for future work in QE and potentially other NLP tasks.

## Weaknesses
1. **Inconsistent Performance of Proposed Models**: The results show that the proposed model extensions (particularly warped GPs) perform inconsistently across datasets. For the smallest dataset (en-es), log-based warping outperforms tanh-based warping, while the opposite is true for larger datasets. The paper fails to adequately explain this inconsistency or provide guidance on when to use which model, which weakens the practical contribution.

2. **Lack of Comparison with Other Probabilistic Models**: While the authors argue that other common QE models can't provide uncertainty estimates, they don't compare their GP-based approach with other probabilistic methods that might be applicable to QE (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, Bayesian linear regression). This omission makes it difficult to assess whether GPs are truly the best probabilistic approach for QE.

3. **Superficial Analysis of Asymmetric Scenarios**: The paper presents results for asymmetric loss scenarios but doesn't thoroughly analyze why warped GPs perform well in optimistic scenarios but poorly in pessimistic ones. The explanation that "warped models tend to have less variance" is too simplistic and doesn't provide actionable insights for practitioners. A deeper analysis would strengthen the paper's contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper shows that the choice between log-based and tanh-based warping depends on dataset size, with log working better for smaller datasets. Could you provide more insight into why this happens, and whether there are ways to automatically select the appropriate warping function based on dataset characteristics?

2. The paper mentions that Matérn kernels showed inconsistent gains over EQ kernels across datasets. Given that the authors argue for Matérn kernels' suitability for noisy data, could you provide more analysis on why they perform better for fr-en but not for other datasets? Is there a specific characteristic of the fr-en dataset that makes Matérn kernels particularly effective?

3. In the pessimistic scenario, warped GPs consistently underperform compared to standard GPs with linex loss. Given that warped GPs have less variance (which should theoretically help in pessimistic scenarios), could you elaborate on the exact mechanism by which this leads to worse performance? Is this a limitation of the warped GP approach, or could it be mitigated with model adjustments?

## Additional Comments
The paper is well-written and makes a clear case for the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE. The experimental setup is appropriate, and the results are presented clearly. The connection to asymmetric loss functions from econometrics is particularly valuable. However, the inconsistent performance of the proposed models across datasets and the lack of comparison with other probabilistic approaches somewhat limit the paper's impact. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the QE field by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation and providing practical methods for incorporating it. With some additional analysis addressing the weaknesses noted above, it would be an excellent contribution to the conference.
