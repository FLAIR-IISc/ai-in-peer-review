

# Peer Review: "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary
This paper investigates the importance of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that traditional QE models, which provide only point estimates, are insufficient for practical decision-making scenarios (e.g., determining whether to post-edit a translation). They propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with extensions like Matérn kernels and warped GPs to better model uncertainty, and introduce NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r). They demonstrate how uncertainty information can be leveraged in asymmetric loss scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic decision-making).

## Primary Contributions
1. **Demonstrating the inadequacy of standard QE evaluation metrics for capturing uncertainty differences** - The most significant contribution, showing that models with identical point estimates but different uncertainty estimates receive identical scores under MAE/Pearson's r but different scores under NLPD.
2. **Proposing and validating NLPD as a metric for evaluating uncertainty in QE** - Providing a practical framework for QE model evaluation that captures uncertainty information.
3. **Demonstrating the practical utility of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric loss scenarios** - Showing how uncertainty information can lead to better decision-making in real-world translation workflows.

## Strengths

1. **Critical Insight on Evaluation Metrics**: The paper makes a compelling case that standard QE evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling. The experiments clearly show that models with identical point estimates but different uncertainty distributions receive identical scores under traditional metrics, while NLPD correctly distinguishes between them. This is a fundamental contribution to the field, as it challenges the standard evaluation practices in QE.

2. **Practical Application of Uncertainty**: The paper successfully bridges the gap between theoretical uncertainty modeling and practical application. The asymmetric loss scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic) are well-motivated and directly relevant to real-world translation workflows. The results showing that warped GPs with tanh-based warping outperform standard GPs in optimistic scenarios provide concrete evidence of the practical value of uncertainty information.

3. **Rigorous Experimental Design**: The use of multiple datasets (en-es, fr-en, en-de) with different sizes and language pairs strengthens the validity of the findings. The 10-fold cross-validation approach and careful ablation study comparing different kernel types and warping functions demonstrate thorough evaluation. The paper also thoughtfully addresses the small dataset issue (en-es) and explains the observed differences in behavior.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with Recent Non-GP Approaches**: While the authors argue that GPs are the natural choice for uncertainty modeling in QE, they fail to compare their approach with recent neural network-based methods that can also provide uncertainty estimates (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, ensemble methods). The paper mentions Hernández-Lobato & Adams (2015) in the related work but doesn't incorporate their approach or compare with it. This is a significant gap given that neural network approaches have become increasingly popular in QE (e.g., Bitvai & Cohn, 2015; Non-Linear Text Regression with a Deep Convolutional Neural Network).

2. **Inconsistent Results with Linex Loss in Pessimistic Scenario**: The paper reports that warped GP models perform worse than standard GPs in the pessimistic scenario (using linex loss with w < 0), which contradicts their main claim about the benefits of their approach. The authors suggest this might be due to lower variance in warped models, but don't provide a thorough analysis of why this happens or whether it's a fundamental limitation. This undermines one of their main claims and requires deeper investigation.

3. **Limited Discussion of Computational Complexity**: The paper mentions that QE datasets are "usually small" (in the order of thousands of instances), but doesn't address the computational complexity of GPs (O(n^3) training complexity) or how the proposed methods scale to larger datasets. The authors reference sparse GPs (Hensman et al., 2013) but don't use them in their experiments, leaving a gap in practical applicability.

4. **Insufficient Analysis of the Small Dataset Problem**: While the paper notes that the en-es dataset (smaller than others) shows different behavior with NLL vs NLPD, they don't provide a thorough analysis of why this happens or how to mitigate it for small datasets. The authors mention "more experiments are needed" but don't offer practical advice for practitioners dealing with small datasets.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors claim that "the differences in uncertainty modelling are by and large not captured by the point estimate metrics" but Table 1 shows that MAE does show gains from standard to warped GPs. Can you clarify why you consider MAE insufficient for capturing uncertainty differences, especially when it does show some improvement?

2. In Section 4.3, you report that "the results for linex are consistently worse than the standard GP baseline" in the pessimistic scenario. You suggest this might be because warped models have less variance. However, in a pessimistic scenario (where underestimates are more costly), shouldn't models with higher variance be better (as they can better capture the risk of underestimation)? Can you provide more insight into this counterintuitive result?

3. The paper focuses on GPs, but recent QE work has moved toward neural network approaches (e.g., Bitvai & Cohn, 2015). How do your proposed methods compare to state-of-the-art non-probabilistic methods, and how would you extend these neural network approaches to provide uncertainty estimates?

4. You use a "standard set of 17 features from the QuEst framework" but don't discuss whether these features are optimal for uncertainty modeling. Could the choice of features impact the quality of uncertainty estimates? Have you considered feature selection specifically for uncertainty modeling?

## Overall Recommendation
The paper makes a strong contribution by highlighting the critical importance of uncertainty modeling in QE and demonstrating that standard evaluation metrics fail to capture this aspect. The proposed NLPD metric is well-motivated and validated, and the asymmetric loss application provides a compelling practical use case.

However, the lack of comparison with recent non-GP approaches and the inconsistent results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are significant weaknesses that need to be addressed. The paper would be substantially strengthened by comparing with recent neural network-based approaches that can also provide uncertainty estimates, and by providing a deeper analysis of the counterintuitive results with the linex loss.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors will need to address the weaknesses identified above in the camera-ready version. The paper has significant potential to influence how QE models are evaluated and applied in practice, but needs to be strengthened with additional comparisons and analysis.
