

# Review for "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper explores the importance of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate approaches are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better model uncertainty, introduce NLPD as a more appropriate evaluation metric that captures uncertainty information, and demonstrate how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios.

**Main Contributions (ordered by strength):**
1. Introducing NLPD as an evaluation metric that captures uncertainty information, which traditional QE metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to do.
2. Proposing and empirically validating the use of Matérn kernels and Warped GPs for improved uncertainty modeling in QE.
3. Demonstrating practical applications of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic decision-making).

## Strengths

1. **Compelling Motivation and Practical Relevance:** The paper effectively establishes why uncertainty matters in QE with concrete, real-world examples (post-editing and gisting scenarios). The argument that decision-making in translation workflows requires more than point estimates is well-justified and addresses a genuine gap in the field. This practical relevance elevates the work beyond theoretical contributions.

2. **Strong Case for NLPD as Evaluation Metric:** The authors correctly identify that traditional QE metrics fail to capture uncertainty information, which is crucial for probabilistic models. Their empirical demonstration that NLPD reflects differences in uncertainty modeling not captured by MAE or Pearson's r is convincing and well-supported. This contribution alone would be valuable to the QE community.

3. **Practical Demonstration of Uncertainty Applications:** The paper doesn't just propose uncertainty modeling but shows how it can be practically applied in asymmetric risk scenarios. The examples of pessimistic (post-editing) and optimistic (gisting) scenarios are well-chosen and demonstrate tangible benefits of the proposed approach. The derivation of Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses is technically sound and adds significant value.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Baseline Comparison:** The paper only compares Warped GPs to standard GPs, but doesn't evaluate against other probabilistic models that could be relevant for QE (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, which are mentioned in the related work). This limits the significance of the claimed improvements. A comparison against at least one strong probabilistic baseline would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Insufficient Theoretical Justification for Warped GPs:** While the authors show empirical improvements with Warped GPs, they lack a strong theoretical argument for why the specific warping functions they use (log, tanh with 3 terms) are particularly suitable for QE. The statement that "the warping function should be flexible enough" is insufficient justification for the observed improvements. A more thorough theoretical analysis would strengthen the contribution.

3. **Inadequate Analysis of Small Dataset Behavior:** The paper notes that the smaller en-es dataset (858 instances) shows different behavior, suggesting overfitting with NLL. However, they don't provide a systematic analysis of how dataset size affects the relative performance of their proposed models. This is a critical limitation for real-world applicability, as QE datasets are often small. A more thorough investigation of this issue would be valuable.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the warping function should be flexible enough to allow the learning of complex mappings." Could you provide more theoretical justification for why the specific parametric form (sum of tanh functions) is particularly appropriate for QE tasks, rather than other flexible functions?

2. How do you expect your approach to scale to larger QE datasets, which are becoming more common in the field? The paper mentions that scalability issues "do not tend to affect QE in practice since the datasets are usually small," but this may change as QE datasets grow.

3. The paper notes that the en-es dataset shows different behavior due to its smaller size. Could you elaborate on how you would adjust your modeling approach for very small datasets (e.g., <1000 instances) versus larger ones?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the QE field by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation and proposing a practical way to incorporate it. The motivation is strong, the empirical evidence for NLPD as a better evaluation metric is convincing, and the demonstration of applications in asymmetric risk scenarios is novel and useful.

However, the limited baseline comparison and insufficient theoretical justification for the warping functions prevent this from being a transformative contribution. The paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing against other probabilistic models and providing deeper theoretical insights into why their specific approach works well for QE.

I recommend *weak acceptance* with the understanding that the paper has significant potential but requires addressing the weaknesses noted above. The authors should provide a more comprehensive comparison against alternative probabilistic approaches and a deeper theoretical analysis of their warping function choice. Addressing these points would substantially increase the paper's impact on the field.
