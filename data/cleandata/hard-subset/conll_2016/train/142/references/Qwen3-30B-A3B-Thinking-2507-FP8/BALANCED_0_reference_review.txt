

# Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper investigates the importance of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point estimate approaches are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios. The authors propose extending Gaussian Process (GP) models with Matèrn kernels and warped GPs to better capture uncertainty. The main contributions are:

1. Demonstrating that standard point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, while NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) successfully distinguishes between models with similar point estimates but different uncertainty profiles.

2. Proposing and evaluating practical extensions to GP models for QE (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs with tanh functions) that better handle the specific characteristics of QE data (noisy, strictly positive quality scores).

3. Showing how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic/optimistic settings) using Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric loss functions, with empirical evidence that warped GPs outperform standard GPs in optimistic settings.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical foundation for uncertainty in QE**: The paper effectively argues why uncertainty is crucial for real-world QE applications (e.g., post-editing workflows), moving beyond the traditional focus on point estimates. The motivation for using probabilistic models over point estimate models is compelling and well-supported with practical examples.

2. **Compelling evidence for NLPD as an evaluation metric**: The authors provide clear empirical evidence that NLPD captures differences in uncertainty modeling that standard metrics miss. The comparison across datasets (en-es, fr-en, en-de) demonstrates that NLL/NLPD can be reliable for larger datasets but may suffer from overfitting in smaller ones, which is a valuable insight for the field.

3. **Novel application to asymmetric risk scenarios**: The paper successfully demonstrates how uncertainty information can be practically utilized in real-world decision-making contexts (pessimistic vs. optimistic scenarios). This goes beyond standard QE evaluation to show tangible benefits for end-users, making the work highly applicable.

4. **Clear and well-structured presentation**: The paper is well-organized, with logical flow from motivation to theory to experiments. The explanations of GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) are accessible and well-motivated for the target audience.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with recent state-of-the-art QE methods**: The paper focuses on GP extensions but doesn't adequately compare against recent probabilistic QE approaches (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, ensemble methods) that might already incorporate uncertainty modeling. This weakens the claim that the proposed methods represent a significant advancement.

2. **Insufficient explanation of Matèrn kernel benefits**: While the paper proposes using Matèrn kernels, it doesn't provide strong theoretical justification for why they're particularly well-suited for QE data beyond the general claim of "relaxed smoothness assumptions." A more detailed analysis of how QE data characteristics (e.g., noise patterns) relate to the Matèrn kernel properties would strengthen this contribution.

3. **Inconsistent results in asymmetric risk experiments**: The results for linex loss in the pessimistic scenario (Table 2) are inconsistent and not properly explained. The paper notes that "results for linex are consistently worse than the standard GP baseline" but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why this might be the case, particularly given that warped models have less variance.

4. **Lack of computational cost analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational complexity of the proposed methods compared to standard GPs or other QE approaches. For practical adoption in large-scale translation workflows, computational efficiency is crucial, and this omission limits the paper's applicability.

5. **Small dataset size for en-es**: The paper acknowledges that the en-es dataset is smaller and might explain some inconsistencies, but doesn't provide sufficient statistical analysis to determine if these differences are significant.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do the proposed warped GP models compare to recent probabilistic QE approaches (e.g., Bayesian neural networks or ensemble methods) in terms of both uncertainty calibration and point estimate performance?

2. The paper mentions that "the gains from using a Matèrn kernel over EQ are less conclusive" but doesn't provide statistical significance tests to support this claim. Could the authors provide more rigorous statistical analysis of these differences?

3. For the inconsistent linex loss results in pessimistic scenarios, could the authors provide more insight into why warped GPs with lower variance perform worse with this specific loss function? Is this a fundamental limitation of the warped GP approach for certain loss functions?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written paper that makes a valuable contribution by highlighting the importance of uncertainty in QE and demonstrating practical applications. The theoretical foundation is strong, and the empirical evidence for NLPD as a superior evaluation metric is compelling. However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the weaknesses noted above, particularly by providing a more comprehensive comparison with recent probabilistic QE methods and a deeper analysis of the Matèrn kernel's benefits for QE data.

While the paper's core contributions are significant, the limited scope of the evaluation (only three language pairs) and lack of comparison with state-of-the-art probabilistic approaches prevent it from being a groundbreaking contribution. With the suggested improvements, this paper could become an important reference for uncertainty-aware QE systems.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors would address the weaknesses in the revision. The paper has the potential to be a strong contribution to the field if the authors can provide more comprehensive experimental validation and comparison.
