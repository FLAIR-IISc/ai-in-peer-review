

# Peer Review: "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the importance of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) and proposes using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with specific extensions (Matérn kernels and warped GPs) to better model uncertainty. The authors argue that traditional QE models, which provide only point estimates, are insufficient for practical decision-making scenarios where uncertainty information is critical. They introduce the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric as a more appropriate evaluation measure for probabilistic models and demonstrate how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric loss scenarios (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic decision-making contexts).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Demonstration that NLPD captures uncertainty differences missed by traditional metrics**: The paper provides compelling evidence that NLPD can distinguish between models with similar point estimate performance but different uncertainty characteristics, which is a significant contribution to QE evaluation methodology.

2. **Systematic exploration of GP extensions for QE**: The authors provide a thorough empirical comparison of Matérn kernels versus EQ kernels and parametric warping functions versus log warping, offering valuable insights into model choices for QE applications.

3. **Practical application of predictive distributions to asymmetric risk scenarios**: The paper demonstrates how uncertainty information can be utilized for decision-making under different risk preferences (pessimistic vs. optimistic), showing concrete benefits of the proposed models in these contexts.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation for uncertainty estimation**: The paper provides an exceptionally clear and compelling argument for why uncertainty estimation is crucial in practical QE applications. The post-editing scenario examples (pessimistic vs. optimistic) are well-chosen and directly relevant to real-world translation workflows. This motivation alone elevates the paper beyond typical technical contributions.

2. **Appropriate focus on NLPD as an evaluation metric**: The authors correctly identify that traditional QE metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture uncertainty differences, and they demonstrate this empirically. The NLPD metric is well-justified and appropriately applied, making it a significant contribution to the evaluation methodology in QE.

3. **Thorough exploration of GP extensions**: The systematic comparison of Matérn kernels versus EQ kernels and the parametric tanh warping versus log warping provides valuable empirical insights. The paper doesn't just propose these extensions but demonstrates their practical impact on QE performance.

4. **Practical application to asymmetric loss scenarios**: The paper successfully demonstrates how predictive distributions can be leveraged for different decision-making contexts (pessimistic vs. optimistic) without requiring model retraining. This is a substantial practical contribution that extends beyond the QE domain.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison to alternative probabilistic models**: The paper exclusively focuses on GPs but doesn't compare to other probabilistic approaches that have been used in QE, such as Bayesian neural networks or Bayesian SVR. For instance, the recent work by Hernández-Lobato and Adams (2015) on probabilistic deep learning for regression would be a relevant comparison. Without this, it's difficult to assess whether GPs are indeed the optimal choice for this task.

2. **Insufficient analysis of dataset size effects**: The authors note that the en-es dataset is smaller and that this may explain inconsistent results, but they don't provide a systematic analysis of how model performance scales with dataset size. This is particularly important given that the paper's main claim about warped GPs outperforming log transformations is inconsistent for the smallest dataset (en-es).

3. **Inconsistent results for linex loss in pessimistic scenarios**: The paper reports that warped GPs perform worse than standard GPs in the pessimistic scenario with linex loss, but the explanation (reduced variance leading to "not conservative enough" predictions) is not thoroughly validated. This undermines the authors' claim that warped GPs are generally superior for uncertainty modeling.

4. **Lack of ablation study for warping complexity**: The paper uses up to 3 tanh terms in the warping function but doesn't provide an ablation study to determine the optimal complexity. Given the small dataset sizes, this is a significant omission as more complex warping functions could lead to overfitting.

5. **Limited discussion of computational costs**: The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of training and using the extended GP models (warped GPs, Matérn kernels), which is crucial for practical adoption in translation workflows.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do the proposed GP-based models compare to recent probabilistic approaches in QE, such as Bayesian neural networks (Hernández-Lobato & Adams, 2015) or Bayesian SVR? Have you considered these alternatives?

2. The paper mentions that the en-es dataset is smaller, but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of how model performance changes with dataset size. Could you provide additional experiments or analysis showing how the performance trends change as the dataset size increases?

3. For the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, why do warped GPs perform worse than standard GPs? The authors suggest it's due to reduced variance in warped GPs, but they don't provide evidence supporting this hypothesis. Could you provide additional analysis or experiments to validate this explanation?

4. The paper uses a two-pass hyperparameter optimization procedure (isotropic kernel first, then ARD). Is this necessary for the warped GP models, or would a single-pass ARD optimization work just as well? How does this affect the computational cost?

5. The authors report "mixed" results for log-based models in the linex loss for the optimistic scenario. Could you elaborate on why the log-based models perform well with AL loss but not with linex loss?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE and demonstrating how it can be practically leveraged for decision-making. The focus on NLPD as an evaluation metric is particularly significant, as it addresses a clear gap in current QE evaluation practices.

However, the paper has several weaknesses that need to be addressed before it can be accepted. The lack of comparison to alternative probabilistic models is a major oversight, and the inconsistent results for the linex loss require further explanation. The limited dataset size analysis and lack of ablation study for warping complexity also weaken the paper's claims.

I recommend **major revision** to address these weaknesses, particularly by adding comparisons to alternative probabilistic models and providing more thorough analysis of the inconsistent results with the linex loss.

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and makes a strong case for the importance of uncertainty in QE. The experimental design is generally sound, but the limited dataset diversity (only three language pairs, all with relatively small sizes) reduces the generalizability of the findings. The authors should acknowledge this limitation more explicitly in the discussion.

The paper's claim that "the methods we explore in this paper can be applied to any text regression task where modelling predictive uncertainty is useful" is somewhat overstated. While the methodology is generalizable, the specific findings about Matérn kernels and warped GPs may not transfer to all text regression tasks, especially those with different data characteristics.

The discussion of asymmetric loss scenarios is particularly strong and represents a significant practical contribution. This section alone would make the paper valuable even without the other contributions.
