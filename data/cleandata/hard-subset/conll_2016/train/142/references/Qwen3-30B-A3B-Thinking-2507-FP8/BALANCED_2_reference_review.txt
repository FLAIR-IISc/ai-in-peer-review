

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) in machine translation, with a focus on modeling prediction uncertainty rather than just point estimates. The authors propose two main extensions to standard GP models for QE: (1) Matérn kernels instead of the standard exponentiated quadratic kernel, and (2) warped GPs to handle the strictly positive nature of quality scores. The primary contribution is advocating for NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than standard point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r), which fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling. The second contribution demonstrates how probabilistic models can be beneficial in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic post-editing decisions).

## Strengths

1. **Strong case for NLPD as evaluation metric**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that standard QE metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling that NLPD can detect. The empirical evidence across multiple datasets shows that models with similar point estimate performance can have significantly different uncertainty characteristics that NLPD captures but standard metrics miss. This is a significant contribution to the field.

2. **Practical relevance and application**: The paper effectively connects theoretical advances to concrete decision-making scenarios (post-editing vs. gisting), demonstrating how uncertainty estimates can lead to better real-world decisions. The asymmetric loss function section provides a clear, practical application of the proposed framework.

3. **Solid theoretical foundation**: The paper provides a clear and well-structured explanation of why Matérn kernels and warped GPs are appropriate for QE tasks, with good justification for their use. The mathematical formulations are presented accessibly.

4. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The authors evaluate their models across three language pairs (en-es, fr-en, en-de) using multiple metrics, providing a comprehensive assessment of their approach. The 10-fold cross-validation adds reliability to the results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of core approach**: While the application to QE is interesting, the core techniques (Matérn kernels, warped GPs) are not novel to the machine learning literature. The paper doesn't sufficiently establish why these specific extensions are necessary for QE beyond what's been done before, and doesn't convincingly argue that standard GPs would fail for QE tasks that these extensions solve.

2. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art QE methods**: The paper focuses on probabilistic models but doesn't adequately compare with non-probabilistic state-of-the-art QE approaches (e.g., deep learning models). It's unclear whether the gains in uncertainty modeling justify any potential loss in point estimate performance, which is the primary metric used in most QE applications.

3. **Lack of uncertainty calibration analysis**: The paper discusses uncertainty but doesn't provide thorough analysis of how well-calibrated the uncertainty estimates are (e.g., using reliability diagrams or expected calibration error). This is crucial for the practical utility of the uncertainty estimates in decision-making.

4. **Inconsistent results across datasets**: The en-es dataset behaves differently from the others, but the paper doesn't deeply analyze why these differences occur or what they imply for the generalizability of the approach. This undermines the paper's claim of broader applicability.

5. **Computational considerations**: The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of the proposed models compared to standard approaches or other QE methods. For real-world deployment in translation workflows, this could be a significant limitation.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do your proposed probabilistic models compare to state-of-the-art non-probabilistic QE models (e.g., deep learning approaches) in terms of point estimate performance? If they perform worse on standard metrics, is the trade-off justified by the improved uncertainty estimates?

2. Could you provide more detailed analysis on why the en-es dataset shows different behavior compared to the other datasets, and what implications this has for the generalizability of your approach?

3. Have you evaluated the calibration of your uncertainty estimates using standard metrics (e.g., reliability diagrams)? If not, how confident are you that the uncertainty estimates are well-calibrated for decision-making?

## Recommendations

The paper makes a valuable contribution by highlighting the importance of uncertainty in QE and advocating for NLPD as a more appropriate evaluation metric. However, to strengthen the paper for publication, I recommend:

1. Adding a more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art non-probabilistic QE methods to demonstrate the practical benefits of the uncertainty modeling approach.

2. Providing a deeper analysis of uncertainty calibration to establish the reliability of the uncertainty estimates for decision-making.

3. Including a discussion of computational considerations and scalability for real-world deployment.

4. Addressing the inconsistent results across datasets with a more thorough analysis of the underlying causes.

5. More clearly articulating the specific scenarios where the proposed approach provides a clear advantage over standard point-estimate-based QE.

## Final Recommendation

This paper has strong potential to be valuable to the QE community by emphasizing the importance of uncertainty in quality estimation. However, the current version lacks sufficient novelty and practical justification compared to existing approaches. I recommend **major revisions** to address the weaknesses identified above. If the authors can provide a more compelling case for the practical benefits of their approach through additional comparisons, calibration analysis, and computational considerations, this could become a strong contribution to the field. As it stands, the paper is borderline for acceptance but would benefit from addressing the weaknesses before publication.
