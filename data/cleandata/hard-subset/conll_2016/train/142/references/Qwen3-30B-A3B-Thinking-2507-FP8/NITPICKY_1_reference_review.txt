

# Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary and Contributions

This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) with specific extensions (Matèrn kernels and warped GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) in Machine Translation, with a focus on prediction uncertainty rather than point estimates. The authors propose using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a superior evaluation metric that captures uncertainty information, which traditional point estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson's r fail to do. They further demonstrate how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios through asymmetric loss functions.

## Strengths

1. The paper addresses a meaningful gap in QE research by emphasizing the practical importance of uncertainty estimation for real-world decision-making scenarios, such as post-editing workflows and gisting applications. This is a significant contribution to the field.

2. The theoretical foundation for using NLPD as an evaluation metric is well-established, and the paper provides a compelling argument for why this metric is superior to traditional point-estimate metrics when uncertainty information is required.

3. The paper makes a strong case for extending GP-based approaches in QE with Matèrn kernels and warped GPs, providing both theoretical justification and empirical evidence for their benefits.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent and Incomplete Reference Formatting**: The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies in the reference list (e.g., "Beck et al\.,? 2013" vs. "Beck et al\.,? 2014a"). Some references lack proper punctuation or use inconsistent spacing (e.g., "f −1" instead of "f⁻¹"). This level of carelessness in academic writing is unacceptable for a publication venue like CONLL.

2. **Unsubstantiated Claims**: The paper states "the most successful probabilistic models are Gaussian Processes (GPs) since they considered the state-of-the-art for regression" without providing sufficient evidence. The cited works (Cohn and Specia, 2013; Hensman et al., 2013) are specific to QE tasks, not regression in general, making this claim misleading and unsupported.

3. **Omission of Potentially Significant Results**: The paper states "We also report results only for models using the Matèrn52 kernel. While we did experiment with different kernels and weighting schemes our findings showed similar trends so we omit them for the sake of clarity." This is a problematic justification for omitting results, as "similar trends" is vague and potentially misleading. The authors should have included all relevant results or provided a clear justification for omitting specific findings.

4. **Inconsistent Terminology and Notation**: The paper inconsistently uses "Matèrn" (with accent) and "Matern" (without accent) to refer to the kernel type. Additionally, the notation "f −1" (with space) is used instead of the standard "f⁻¹" for inverse functions. Such inconsistencies, while minor, suggest a lack of attention to detail that undermines the paper's credibility.

5. **Inadequate Dataset Citation**: The paper references "the WMT16 QE shared task" with a URL "www.statmt.org/wmt16" but fails to provide a proper academic citation or sufficient details about the dataset. This makes it difficult for readers to locate and verify the dataset, which is a fundamental requirement for reproducibility.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "the most successful probabilistic models are Gaussian Processes" but cites only QE-specific papers. Can the authors provide evidence that GPs are indeed the state-of-the-art for regression in general, not just for QE tasks?

2. The paper omits results for different kernels and weighting schemes, claiming "similar trends." Could the authors clarify what specific trends were observed and why they considered them unimportant enough to omit?

3. The paper states that "the log-based models also gives good results for AL but for Linex the results are mixed except for enes." The use of "enes" instead of "en-es" appears to be a typo. Is this a consistent typo throughout the paper?

## Overall Recommendation

While the paper addresses an important problem in QE and makes a compelling argument for the importance of uncertainty estimation, the numerous formatting inconsistencies, unsubstantiated claims, and questionable decision to omit results significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The attention to detail in academic writing is a fundamental requirement for publication in a top-tier conference like CONLL. These minor flaws, though individually small, collectively indicate a lack of rigor and care that outweighs the paper's contributions. I recommend rejection.

The paper has strong potential but requires substantial revision to address these basic issues before it could be considered for publication. The authors should carefully proofread the manuscript, verify all claims with appropriate citations, and include all relevant results to ensure the paper meets the high standards expected at CONLL.
