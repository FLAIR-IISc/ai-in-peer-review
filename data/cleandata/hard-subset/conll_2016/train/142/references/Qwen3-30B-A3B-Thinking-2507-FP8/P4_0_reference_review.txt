

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the need for uncertainty estimation in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that point estimates (e.g., MAE, Pearson's r) are insufficient for practical decision-making scenarios. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a probabilistic framework for QE, extending GP models with Matérn kernels and warped GPs to better handle the specific characteristics of QE data.

The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. **Demonstration that standard point estimate metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling**, with evidence that NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) is a more appropriate metric for evaluating uncertainty-aware QE models.

2. **Proposed extensions to GP models (Matérn kernels and warped GPs) that better address the specific challenges of QE data** (noisy, strictly positive quality scores), with empirical validation across multiple language pairs.

3. **Application of predictive distributions to asymmetric risk scenarios**, showing how uncertainty estimates can improve decision-making in post-editing workflows with different cost structures.

## Strengths

1. **Strong practical motivation**: The paper effectively demonstrates why uncertainty estimation matters in real-world QE applications (e.g., post-editing workflows), making a compelling case for the importance of the work beyond academic interest. The authors clearly explain how uncertainty estimates can lead to better decision-making than point estimates alone.

2. **Clear technical justification for model choices**: The authors provide sound theoretical reasoning for using Matérn kernels (relaxed smoothness assumptions for noisy data) and warped GPs (handling strictly positive quality scores), which are well-motivated extensions to standard GP approaches. The paper correctly identifies that standard GPs have support over the entire real line, which is inappropriate for quality scores.

3. **Compelling evidence for NLPD as a superior evaluation metric**: The experiments clearly show that point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to distinguish between models with different uncertainty properties, while NLPD captures these differences. The results on the en-es dataset (smaller size) particularly demonstrate how NLL can be misleading due to overfitting, reinforcing the need for NLPD.

4. **Practical demonstration of asymmetric risk scenarios**: The paper successfully shows how predictive distributions can be leveraged in real-world decision-making contexts with asymmetric costs (pessimistic vs. optimistic scenarios), providing a concrete application of uncertainty estimation beyond theoretical interest.

5. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The multi-dataset evaluation across three language pairs (en-es, fr-en, en-de) with consistent experimental setup provides strong evidence for the proposed methods' effectiveness, with careful analysis of differences across datasets.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art point-estimate QE models**: While the paper mentions SVR as a common approach, it doesn't directly compare the GP-based models with the best-performing point-estimate models from recent WMT QE shared tasks. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the improvements in uncertainty estimation come at the cost of point estimate performance.

2. **Limited explanation of inconsistent results with linex loss**: The paper reports that warped GPs perform worse than standard GPs with the linex loss in pessimistic scenarios, but doesn't provide a thorough explanation for this counterintuitive result. The authors note that "warped models tend to have less variance," but don't explore whether this is a fundamental limitation of warped GPs or a specific issue with the linex loss.

3. **Insufficient discussion of novelty**: While the paper uses established GP techniques (Matérn kernels, warped GPs), it doesn't clearly establish how these are novel for the specific context of QE. The paper would be strengthened by a more explicit comparison with prior work applying similar techniques to related NLP tasks.

4. **Lack of scalability discussion**: The paper doesn't address computational costs or scalability of the proposed methods, which is crucial for real-world QE applications where models need to process large volumes of translation segments.

5. **Limited analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity**: The paper doesn't discuss how sensitive the results are to hyperparameter choices, particularly for the warped GP warping functions (e.g., number of tanh terms). This is important for understanding the practical applicability of the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do the proposed GP-based models compare with the best-performing point-estimate QE models (e.g., those from recent WMT QE shared tasks) in terms of standard point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r)? If the GP models achieve competitive point estimates while providing uncertainty, this would be a stronger argument for their adoption.

2. The paper reports that warped GPs perform worse than standard GPs with linex loss in pessimistic scenarios. Could you elaborate on why the reduced variance in warped GPs leads to worse performance with this specific loss function, and whether this is a fundamental limitation of warped GPs or specific to the linex loss?

3. The paper mentions that Matérn kernels show inconsistent gains across datasets. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, and whether there are specific characteristics of language pairs that would determine which kernel is more appropriate?

4. How do the computational requirements of the proposed GP-based models compare to standard point-estimate QE models (e.g., SVR), particularly for large-scale translation workflows?
