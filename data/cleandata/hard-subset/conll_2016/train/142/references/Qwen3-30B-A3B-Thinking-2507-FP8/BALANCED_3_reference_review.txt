

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper investigates the importance of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate models are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios. The authors focus on Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a probabilistic framework for QE that can provide uncertainty estimates. Their main contributions are: (1) Proposing the use of Matérn kernels and warped GPs for QE, demonstrating they provide better uncertainty estimates than standard GPs; (2) Establishing that traditional evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, while the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric does; and (3) Showing how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric loss scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic settings) for better decision making in real-world applications.

## Strengths
1. **Significant Practical Motivation**: The paper convincingly argues that uncertainty estimation is crucial for real-world QE applications, particularly in post-editing workflows where decision-making requires more than just point estimates. The examples of pessimistic (high-confidence filtering) and optimistic (maximizing coverage) scenarios are well-motivated and relevant to practical translation workflows.

2. **Strong Evaluation Metric Proposal**: The paper makes a compelling case for using NLPD as a more appropriate evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models. The empirical demonstration that NLPD captures differences in uncertainty modeling that traditional metrics miss is well-supported by the experimental results, particularly for the smaller en-es dataset where overfitting was observed.

3. **Novel Application of Asymmetric Losses**: The exploration of asymmetric loss scenarios demonstrates a clear practical application of the proposed models. The paper effectively shows how different models can be beneficial in different real-world contexts (e.g., the tanh-based warped GP outperforming standard GPs in optimistic scenarios), providing a valuable framework for future QE research.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited Scope of Experimental Evaluation**: The paper evaluates only three language pairs (en-es, fr-en, en-de) with relatively small datasets (max 2,828 instances). This limited scope reduces the generalizability of the findings, especially since the authors acknowledge that "the best performing systems in the shared tasks use larger feature sets" but choose to use a standard set of 17 features. The results might not generalize to larger datasets or different MT systems.

2. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art QE Models**: The paper primarily compares different GP variants but doesn't adequately contrast them with the current state-of-the-art QE models (e.g., neural network approaches). The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't address how their probabilistic models would compare to non-probabilistic state-of-the-art systems in terms of point estimates, which is crucial for establishing their practical value.

3. **Insufficient Evidence of Real-World Impact**: While the paper demonstrates theoretical benefits in asymmetric loss scenarios, it lacks empirical evidence showing how these models would actually improve decision-making in real translation workflows. The paper doesn't provide a case study showing how uncertainty estimates would change post-editor behavior or productivity metrics in practice.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper mentions that "the best performing systems in the shared tasks use larger feature sets" but uses a standard set of 17 features. How would incorporating more advanced features (e.g., those used in state-of-the-art systems) impact your results, and why did you choose to focus on a limited feature set?

2. The paper shows that warped GPs with tanh functions perform better than log warping for fr-en and en-de, but log performs better for en-es. Given the small size of en-es, could you provide more analysis on how dataset size affects the optimal choice of warping function?

3. While the paper demonstrates that NLPD captures uncertainty differences missed by point-estimate metrics, how would you address the potential computational cost of using probabilistic models in production environments with large-scale translation workflows?

## Overall Assessment
This paper addresses an important gap in QE research by focusing on uncertainty estimation, which is crucial for practical decision-making. The motivation is strong, the methodology is sound, and the proposed NLPD metric is well-justified. However, the limited scope of the evaluation and lack of comparison with state-of-the-art QE models weakens the paper's contribution. The paper makes a valuable theoretical contribution to understanding uncertainty in QE but would benefit from more extensive empirical validation in larger, more realistic settings.

While the paper provides a solid foundation for future work on uncertainty in QE, I recommend rejection due to the limited scope of evaluation and insufficient comparison with current state-of-the-art approaches. The authors should extend their experiments to larger datasets, include more comprehensive comparisons with existing QE systems, and provide empirical evidence of real-world impact before the paper can be considered for publication.

The paper has significant potential, and with more thorough evaluation, it could make a substantial contribution to the field of QE. I encourage the authors to address these limitations in future work.
