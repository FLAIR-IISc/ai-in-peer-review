

# Peer Review: "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper argues that traditional Quality Estimation (QE) models for machine translation provide only point estimates, which are insufficient for decision-making scenarios where uncertainty matters. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) to better model prediction uncertainty. They introduce NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) when uncertainty information is important. The paper demonstrates how predictive distributions can be used in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic settings).

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1. **Demonstrating the inadequacy of traditional point-estimate metrics for capturing meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling**: The paper provides strong evidence that standard metrics like MAE and Pearson's r fail to distinguish between models with different uncertainty properties, while NLPD captures these differences.

2. **Practical demonstration of how uncertainty estimates can improve decision-making**: The authors go beyond theoretical discussion to show how predictive distributions can be leveraged in real-world asymmetric risk scenarios (post-editing vs. gisting), making the work highly applicable.

3. **Systematic exploration of GP extensions for QE**: The paper provides a thorough evaluation of Matèrn kernels and Warped GP extensions in the context of QE, offering practical guidance for implementing uncertainty-aware QE systems.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation for uncertainty estimation in QE**: The paper effectively argues why uncertainty matters in practical QE applications through compelling examples of post-editing and gisting scenarios. The distinction between "pessimistic" and "optimistic" settings is particularly well-justified and addresses a real gap in the field where most QE work assumes symmetric loss functions.

2. **Clear demonstration that traditional evaluation metrics are insufficient**: The paper provides convincing evidence that MAE and Pearson's r fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling. Table 1 clearly shows that while point estimate metrics show minor improvements from standard to Warped GPs, NLPD captures the differences more effectively, especially in the en-es dataset where overfitting is evident with NLL.

3. **Practical application of uncertainty information**: The asymmetric loss scenarios section is the strongest part of the paper. The authors don't just propose uncertainty estimation as an academic exercise, but show how it can be used to improve actual decision-making processes in translation workflows. The connection between Bayes risk estimators and real-world application scenarios is particularly well-executed.

4. **Thorough comparison of model extensions**: The paper systematically evaluates different kernel choices (Matèrn vs. EQ) and warping functions (log vs. tanh), providing valuable insights for practitioners. The observation that Warped GPs with tanh functions perform better on larger datasets (fr-en, en-de) but log works better on smaller datasets (en-es) is a useful practical insight.

## Weaknesses

1. **Marginal performance gains with limited practical significance**: While the paper demonstrates that NLPD can distinguish between models with different uncertainty properties, the actual performance gains in terms of NLPD are marginal (e.g., Table 1 shows NLPD improvements of only 0.1-0.2 points across datasets). The paper doesn't establish whether these marginal improvements translate to meaningful gains in real-world decision-making outcomes.

2. **Inadequate discussion of model calibration**: The paper claims "good uncertainty estimates can be acquired from well-calibrated probability distributions" but provides no evidence that the proposed models actually produce well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. Calibration is crucial for uncertainty to be useful in decision-making, and this is a significant oversight in the paper.

3. **Limited experimental scope**: The paper uses only three datasets with relatively small sizes (en-es: 858, fr-en: 2,525, en-de: 2,828). The small dataset sizes, especially for en-es, make it difficult to draw robust conclusions about model performance. The paper doesn't address how the proposed methods would scale to larger datasets or new language pairs.

4. **Lack of computational cost analysis**: The paper mentions that scalability issues with kernel methods don't typically affect QE due to small datasets, but doesn't provide any analysis of the computational cost of the proposed extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) compared to standard GPs. This is a significant omission for practical adoption.

5. **No discussion of uncertainty thresholding**: The paper demonstrates how to use uncertainty in decision-making but doesn't address the critical question of how to set uncertainty thresholds for practical application. For example, how should a system determine the cutoff for "low uncertainty" when deciding whether to forward a translation for post-editing?

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper demonstrates that Warped GPs with tanh functions outperform standard GPs in optimistic scenarios but the results for the pessimistic scenario with linex loss are inconsistent. Could you provide more insight into why Warped GPs with tanh perform worse in the pessimistic setting, and whether this is a limitation of the warping approach or a characteristic of the linex loss?

2. The paper claims that "good uncertainty estimates can be acquired from well-calibrated probability distributions" but doesn't provide any calibration analysis. Could you add a section showing calibration plots (e.g., reliability diagrams) for the different models to demonstrate whether the uncertainty estimates are well-calibrated?

3. The paper uses only three datasets, all with relatively small sizes. How do you expect the performance of these models to scale to larger datasets or new language pairs not included in the current experiments?

4. The paper shows marginal improvements in NLPD but doesn't establish whether these translate to meaningful improvements in downstream decision-making. Could you provide an analysis of how much the decision quality (e.g., number of translations correctly filtered for post-editing) improves with the proposed models compared to standard GPs?

5. In Section 4.3, you note that "the results for linex are consistently worse than the standard GP baseline" in the pessimistic scenario. Could you elaborate on whether this is due to the warping approach reducing variance too much, or if there's an issue with the linex loss function itself when applied to these datasets?
