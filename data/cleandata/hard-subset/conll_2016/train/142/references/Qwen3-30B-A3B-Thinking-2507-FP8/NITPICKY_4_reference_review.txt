

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper explores the use of probabilistic models for uncertainty estimation in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs) with Warped GPs and Matèrn kernels. The authors argue that traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, and propose NLPD as a better evaluation metric. They also demonstrate how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios.

As I see them, the paper's main contributions are:
1. Demonstrating that NLPD effectively captures differences in uncertainty modeling that traditional point estimate metrics miss
2. Proposing Warped GPs with parametric warping functions as a practical improvement for QE
3. Providing a concrete application of predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios

## Strengths

1. The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE applications, particularly for decision-making in post-editing workflows. The post-editing scenario example is well-motivated and clearly illustrates why uncertainty estimates matter beyond point predictions.

2. The authors correctly identify that traditional QE evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, which is a significant insight for the field. Their empirical demonstration that NLPD can distinguish between models with similar point estimates is valuable.

3. The application to asymmetric loss scenarios is well-justified and shows practical utility of the proposed uncertainty estimates. The authors successfully demonstrate how a single probabilistic model can handle different loss functions without retraining.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent and non-standard citation formatting**: The paper contains numerous citation formatting errors that undermine professionalism. References like "Beck et al\.,? 2014a" and "Cohn and Specia.,? 2013" use non-standard backslashes and question marks in the citation format. The reference to "www.statmt.org/wmt16" in Section 3.1 is not properly formatted as a citation. These errors suggest a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for a CONLL submission.

2. **Insufficient statistical justification for claims**: The paper states "the log function tends to perform better" for the en-es dataset without providing statistical significance testing. Similarly, the claim that "the gains from using a Matèrn kernel over EQ are less conclusive" lacks statistical evidence. The authors state "we believe this is evidence of overfitting" regarding the en-es dataset but provide no theoretical or empirical evidence for this claim beyond the NLL vs NLPD discrepancy.

3. **Lack of reproducibility details**: The paper mentions "we use a subset of 2,828 instances" for en-de but fails to explain the selection criteria or process. The use of 10-fold cross-validation is mentioned but without details about how the folds were generated or whether they were consistent across experiments. The paper also states they "report results only for models using the Matèrn52 kernel" without justifying this selective reporting.

4. **Minor technical errors**: The paper contains several minor technical inconsistencies. For example, in Section 2.2, the Matèrn kernel equations use "r2" for squared distance but later uses "r^2" in the explanation. The phrase "we focus our analysis in this paper on GPs" contains an incorrect preposition ("in" should be "on"). The phrase "the median can be easily obtained by applying the inverse warping function to the latent median" should be "the median can be easily obtained by applying the inverse warping function to the latent median."

5. **Inconsistent dataset naming**: The paper refers to "en-es" without defining what this abbreviation stands for (English-Spanish), which is confusing for readers unfamiliar with the QE community. This inconsistency appears throughout the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify why you selected the specific subset of 2,828 instances from the WMT16 QE dataset for en-de? What was the selection criteria?

2. For the claim that "the log function tends to perform better" for en-es, what statistical tests were performed to establish this difference as significant?

3. The paper states "we believe this is evidence of overfitting" regarding the en-es dataset. Could you provide more specific evidence for this claim, such as comparing training vs. validation performance?

4. Why did you selectively report results for only the Matèrn52 kernel in Section 4.2, omitting results for other kernels? Was this due to lack of significance, or was it a selection bias?

## Overall Assessment

While the paper presents a valuable exploration of uncertainty in QE with potentially useful insights, the numerous minor flaws in formatting, technical presentation, and methodological reporting are unacceptable for a CONLL submission. The citation inconsistencies, lack of statistical justification for key claims, and insufficient reproducibility details collectively indicate a lack of attention to detail that would be expected in a high-quality conference paper. The paper's core ideas are interesting, but these formatting and methodological inconsistencies undermine the authors' credibility and the paper's overall quality.

I recommend rejection due to the significant number of minor but persistent issues that collectively indicate a lack of professional care in preparing the manuscript for submission.
