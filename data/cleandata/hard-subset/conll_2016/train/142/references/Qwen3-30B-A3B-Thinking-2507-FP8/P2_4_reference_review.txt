

# Review: "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper explores the use of probabilistic modeling for Quality Estimation (QE) in machine translation, with a specific focus on prediction uncertainty. The authors argue that traditional QE models that provide only point estimates are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios, and propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with extensions like Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better capture uncertainty. They advocate for using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more informative evaluation metric than traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r). The paper demonstrates how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios, such as post-editing where underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating it.

**Main Contributions (in order of strength):**

1. **Compelling argument for NLPD as a superior evaluation metric** that captures uncertainty information, which traditional point estimate metrics fail to do. The authors demonstrate through experiments that models with similar point estimates can have very different uncertainty profiles that are not reflected in MAE or Pearson's r.

2. **Practical application of uncertainty estimation in asymmetric risk scenarios** - showing how the same probabilistic model can be used for different decision-making contexts without retraining, with clear examples in post-editing and gisting scenarios.

3. **Thorough analysis of GP modeling choices for QE** (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) and their specific relevance to the characteristics of QE datasets.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation for the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE applications.** The post-editing scenario example is particularly compelling and well-articulated, showing how uncertainty information directly impacts decision-making processes in real-world translation workflows. The paper effectively argues that "only very accurate prediction models can be useful in practice" when relying solely on point estimates, which is a significant insight for the field.

2. **Clear demonstration of the limitations of traditional evaluation metrics.** The paper provides strong evidence that MAE and Pearson's r fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling, which is a crucial contribution to the field of QE evaluation methodology. This is well-supported by their results showing that different warping functions perform similarly on MAE but differently on NLPD.

3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple language pairs.** The authors evaluate their models on three different language pairs (en-es, fr-en, en-de), providing a more robust analysis than single-dataset studies and demonstrating the generalizability of their findings.

4. **Effective presentation of the asymmetric risk application.** The paper clearly explains how Bayes risk estimators can be derived from predictive distributions for different loss functions, and provides concrete evidence of how the proposed models outperform standard approaches in specific scenarios.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in model extensions.** While the authors propose using Matérn kernels and Warped GPs for QE, these are standard techniques in the GP literature. The paper doesn't sufficiently establish why these particular extensions are novel for QE or provide compelling evidence that they offer significant advantages over existing approaches beyond marginal improvements.

2. **Lack of comparison to other probabilistic models.** The paper exclusively focuses on GPs without comparing to other probabilistic models (e.g., Bayesian neural networks) that could also provide uncertainty estimates. This limits the significance of their claims about the superiority of their approach, as they don't establish whether GPs are inherently better for this task or whether other methods could achieve similar results.

3. **Insufficient analysis of the overfitting issue with small datasets.** The authors note that NLL is unreliable for the small en-es dataset due to overfitting but don't provide any analysis or discussion of why this happens or how to mitigate it. This is a significant limitation for a paper that claims to be about uncertainty estimation, as small datasets are common in QE.

4. **Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges.** The paper doesn't address how these probabilistic models would scale to larger datasets or real-world production environments, which is crucial for assessing the practical value of the proposed approach.

## Recommendation

**Accept**

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE and providing a clear methodology for evaluating probabilistic models. The demonstration of how uncertainty information can be used in asymmetric risk scenarios is particularly strong and has clear practical implications. The paper is well-written, well-organized, and provides a thorough experimental analysis.

While the novelty of the specific model extensions is somewhat limited, the overall contribution to the field through the focus on uncertainty estimation and the NLPD evaluation metric is significant. The authors have provided a convincing argument for why this perspective matters in real-world applications, which is the most important contribution of the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do you justify focusing exclusively on GPs when other probabilistic models (e.g., Bayesian neural networks) could potentially provide similar or better uncertainty estimates for QE?

2. The paper mentions that NLL is unreliable for small datasets due to overfitting but doesn't provide strategies to address this. What approaches could be used to improve uncertainty estimation for small datasets like en-es?

3. The paper states that "the gains from using a Matérn kernel over EQ are less conclusive." Can you provide more insight into why this might be the case, particularly in relation to the characteristics of QE datasets?
