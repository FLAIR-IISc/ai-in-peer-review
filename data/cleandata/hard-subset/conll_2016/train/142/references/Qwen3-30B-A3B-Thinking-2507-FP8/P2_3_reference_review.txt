

# Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary and Contributions

This paper argues that prediction uncertainty is critical for practical applications of Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), where decisions (e.g., whether to post-edit or translate from scratch) depend on quality predictions. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with specific extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) to better model uncertainty, and introduce Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a superior evaluation metric compared to traditional point-estimate metrics like MAE.

The paper's primary contributions are:
1. Demonstrating that uncertainty modeling in QE matters for practical decision-making, and that standard evaluation metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling.
2. Proposing and validating specific GP extensions (Warped GPs with tanh warping, Matèrn kernels) that improve uncertainty estimation for QE.
3. Showing how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios without retraining (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic decision-making contexts).

## Strengths

1. **Compelling argument for uncertainty in QE**: The paper effectively demonstrates why uncertainty matters in real-world QE applications (e.g., post-editing workflows), showing how point estimates alone are insufficient for decision-making. The examples of pessimistic (post-editing) and optimistic (gisting) scenarios make a strong case for the practical importance of this work.

2. **Strong evidence for NLPD as a better evaluation metric**: The paper convincingly shows that traditional metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling. Their experiments across three language pairs clearly demonstrate that NLPD identifies performance differences that point-estimate metrics miss, particularly when comparing Warped GP models with different warping functions. This is a significant contribution to the QE evaluation landscape.

3. **Practical demonstration of asymmetric risk handling**: The paper successfully demonstrates how predictive distributions can be used for decision-making under asymmetric risk without retraining models, which is a major practical advantage. Their results showing Warped GP models outperforming standard GPs in optimistic scenarios provide concrete evidence of the approach's value.

4. **Thorough theoretical foundation**: The paper provides a clear and well-structured explanation of GP theory, Warped GPs, and Matèrn kernels, making it accessible to readers familiar with machine learning but not necessarily experts in GPs.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited justification for focusing solely on GPs**: The authors claim "other common models used in QE can only provide point estimates," but they don't adequately address why other probabilistic models (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, Bayesian linear regression) aren't suitable for QE. This weakens their argument for focusing exclusively on GP extensions.

2. **Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art QE models**: While the paper focuses on uncertainty modeling, it doesn't compare their proposed models to the current state-of-the-art in QE (e.g., deep learning models), making it difficult to assess whether the improvements in uncertainty estimation translate to better overall QE performance in practical applications.

3. **Limited discussion of computational costs**: The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of Warped GPs compared to standard GPs or other QE models. For real-world deployment, training time and inference speed are critical factors that could limit the practical adoption of these methods.

4. **Inconsistent results with small dataset (en-es)**: The en-es dataset shows different trends compared to the larger datasets, but the paper doesn't thoroughly investigate why this happens. Given that QE often operates in low-data regimes, understanding this inconsistency is crucial for the proposed approach's applicability.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "other common models used in QE can only provide point estimates," but doesn't provide evidence or comparison to probabilistic alternatives (e.g., Bayesian neural networks). Could you elaborate on why these alternatives aren't suitable for QE?

2. How do the proposed Warped GP models compare to state-of-the-art QE models (e.g., deep learning approaches) in terms of point estimate performance, and how do the uncertainty improvements affect overall QE utility?

3. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the inconsistent results with the smaller en-es dataset? Is this a fundamental limitation of the approach, or is it specific to the dataset characteristics?

4. What are the computational costs (training and inference) of Warped GPs compared to standard GPs and other QE models, and how would this impact deployment in production environments?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the QE field by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation and proposing practical methods to better model it. The demonstration that NLPD is a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models is particularly significant. While the paper has some weaknesses in terms of justification and comparison to state-of-the-art, the core argument about uncertainty in QE decision-making is compelling and well-supported by the experiments. The paper would be strengthened by addressing the computational aspects and providing a more thorough comparison to current QE approaches.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions raised above, particularly regarding the comparison to other probabilistic models and the computational implications of the proposed methods. The paper makes an important contribution to the field by shifting the focus from point estimates to uncertainty in QE, which is crucial for practical applications.
