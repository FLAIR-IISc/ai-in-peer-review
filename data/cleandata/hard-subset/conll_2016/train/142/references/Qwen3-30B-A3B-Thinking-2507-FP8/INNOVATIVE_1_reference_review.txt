

# Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary
This paper explores the application of probabilistic models for uncertainty estimation in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), specifically focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs) with extensions including Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs. The authors argue that traditional QE models, which provide only point estimates, are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios where uncertainty information is crucial. They propose using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a superior evaluation metric to traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) and demonstrate how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic settings).

## Main Contributions
1. **Novel Evaluation Metric for Probabilistic QE**: Proposing NLPD as a more comprehensive metric that captures uncertainty information, challenging the field's reliance on point estimate metrics.
2. **Practical Application of Uncertainty Estimation**: Demonstrating how uncertainty estimates can be used in real-world asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., post-editing quality control), moving beyond theoretical discussion.
3. **Model Extensions for QE**: Introducing Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs as more suitable probabilistic modeling choices for QE's noisy, non-Gaussian data.

## Strengths

1. **Bold Shift in Evaluation Paradigm**: The paper's most compelling contribution is its challenge to the field's evaluation practices. By demonstrating that NLPD captures uncertainty information that MAE and Pearson's r miss, the authors propose a fundamental shift in how probabilistic QE models should be evaluated. This is not merely a methodological tweak but a potential paradigm shift in the field, which aligns perfectly with CoNLL's preference for novel conceptual directions.

2. **Real-World Decision-Making Connection**: The paper successfully connects uncertainty estimation to concrete, practical applications in translation workflows (post-editing and gisting scenarios). This is a significant strength because it moves beyond theoretical uncertainty modeling to demonstrate tangible benefits for real-world translation systems, making the work immediately relevant to practitioners.

3. **Novel Application of Established Statistical Methods**: The application of asymmetric loss functions (AL and linex) to QE is a clever use of established statistical concepts in a new domain. The authors show how predictive distributions can be directly leveraged for different decision-making contexts without retraining models, which is both elegant and practically valuable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Practical Impact Demonstration**: While the paper demonstrates that uncertainty information can be used in asymmetric scenarios, it doesn't convincingly show that the proposed models lead to better *actual* decision outcomes. The paper shows that warped GPs perform better in asymmetric loss settings, but doesn't provide evidence that this translates to improved translation productivity or cost savings in real-world translation workflows.

2. **Insufficient Comparative Analysis**: The paper claims that "differences in uncertainty modelling are by and large not captured by the point estimate metrics" but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of when traditional metrics might still be sufficient. The comparison with existing probabilistic models in QE is limited, making it difficult to assess the true novelty and significance of their extensions.

3. **Scalability and Generalizability Concerns**: The experimental evaluation is limited to three language pairs with relatively small datasets (max 2,828 instances). This is a significant limitation for a field that increasingly deals with large-scale translation systems. The paper doesn't address how these methods would scale to industrial-strength QE systems with millions of data points.

## Questions to Authors

1. Can you provide evidence that the improved performance in asymmetric loss scenarios translates to tangible improvements in real-world translation productivity metrics (e.g., reduced post-editing time, higher translator satisfaction)?

2. How do your proposed models compare to other probabilistic approaches in QE that have been explored in the literature (e.g., Bayesian neural networks or ensemble methods)? What specific advantages do Warped GPs have over these alternatives?

3. Given that the en-es dataset is small (858 instances), how confident are you that the observed improvements for the log warping function aren't due to overfitting, and what strategies would you recommend for applying these methods to similarly small datasets?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a compelling and novel direction for Quality Estimation research, with a strong conceptual contribution that challenges the field's evaluation practices. While the experimental validation has some limitations in scope and practical impact demonstration, the paper's core contribution—highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation and proposing a new evaluation framework—represents a significant step forward for the field. The work aligns perfectly with CoNLL's interest in bold, novel approaches that shift paradigms rather than making incremental improvements.

Given the high novelty of the evaluation metric proposal and the practical application to decision-making scenarios, I recommend **acceptance**. The paper's conceptual contribution outweighs the methodological limitations, especially considering the field's current reliance on point estimates without proper uncertainty handling. The authors have identified a genuine gap in the field and proposed a clear path forward, which is exactly what CoNLL values most highly.
