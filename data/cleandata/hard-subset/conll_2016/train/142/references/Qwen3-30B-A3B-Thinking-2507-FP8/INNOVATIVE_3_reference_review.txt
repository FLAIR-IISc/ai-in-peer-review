

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper presents a compelling argument for shifting the paradigm of Quality Estimation (QE) from point estimates to uncertainty-aware predictions. The authors identify a critical gap in current QE practice: while QE models are used in real-world translation workflows where decisions must be made under uncertainty, the field has focused almost exclusively on point estimates evaluated using traditional metrics like MAE and Pearson's r. The paper's primary contribution is advocating for the adoption of uncertainty estimation as a core component of QE systems.

The authors make three key contributions:
1. **Theoretical and practical argument for uncertainty estimation in QE**: Demonstrating how uncertainty information enables better decision-making in translation workflows (e.g., post-editing scenarios where underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating).
2. **Introduction of NLPD as the appropriate evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models**: Showing that standard point-estimate metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, while NLPD properly evaluates the quality of predictive distributions.
3. **Demonstration of practical applications in asymmetric risk scenarios**: Showing how predictive distributions can be leveraged for decision-making under different risk profiles (pessimistic vs. optimistic) without retraining models.

## Strengths

1. **Fundamental paradigm shift**: This paper challenges the core assumption of QE as a point estimation task, which is a significant contribution to the field. The argument that QE models should be designed for decision-making, not just prediction, is compelling and novel in the QE literature. The post-editing scenario example effectively illustrates how uncertainty estimates can directly improve real-world translation workflows.

2. **Strong justification for NLPD as the evaluation metric**: The paper provides clear theoretical and empirical evidence that standard metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture uncertainty information, while NLPD properly evaluates predictive distributions. This is a crucial methodological contribution that addresses a real problem in the field's evaluation practices.

3. **Practical demonstration of uncertainty's value**: The paper doesn't just theorize about uncertainty; it shows concrete applications in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic/optimistic settings), demonstrating how the same model can serve different business needs without retraining. This bridges the gap between theoretical uncertainty estimation and practical implementation.

4. **Novel application of existing techniques**: While warped GPs and Matérn kernels aren't new to machine learning, their application to QE with a focus on uncertainty estimation is novel and well-justified. The paper provides a strong theoretical rationale for why these techniques are particularly suitable for QE's noisy, small-data setting.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of probabilistic models**: The paper focuses exclusively on Gaussian Processes without comparing with other probabilistic models (e.g., Bayesian neural networks) that could also provide uncertainty estimates. While the authors argue GPs are most appropriate for QE, a more comprehensive comparison would strengthen the argument.

2. **Limited empirical validation**: Experiments are conducted on only three language pairs with relatively small datasets (especially en-es). While the authors acknowledge dataset size limitations, more extensive validation across diverse datasets would better establish the generalizability of their findings.

3. **Minimal discussion of computational cost**: GPs are known to be computationally intensive, which could be a significant limitation for practical QE systems. The paper acknowledges that QE datasets are small, but doesn't address whether these methods would scale to larger, more realistic settings.

## Recommendation

This paper makes a strong case for a fundamental shift in how we approach Quality Estimation. While the technical contributions (warped GPs, Matérn kernels) aren't entirely novel to machine learning, their application to QE with a focus on uncertainty estimation is significant and well-motivated. The paper's greatest strength is the clear demonstration of why uncertainty matters in real-world translation workflows—a perspective that has been missing from the QE literature.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find this paper's central contribution—advocating for uncertainty-aware decision-making in QE—to be highly valuable. The paper doesn't just present a new model; it proposes a new way of thinking about QE systems that could fundamentally change how the field approaches evaluation and model selection.

The weaknesses noted are important but not deal-breakers for a paper of this caliber. The focus on a clear, novel perspective outweighs the limitations in scope and validation. The paper provides a compelling roadmap for how QE should evolve, and I believe it will be influential in shaping future research in the field.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper introduces a significant new concept that challenges the status quo in Quality Estimation. While the technical implementation is not groundbreaking, the perspective it offers is valuable and novel. The paper successfully demonstrates why uncertainty estimation should be a core component of QE systems rather than an afterthought, making a strong case for its adoption in the field.
