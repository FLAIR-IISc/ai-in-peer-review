

# Peer Review for "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates the importance of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate models are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a probabilistic framework that naturally provides uncertainty estimates, and they explore two model extensions: Matérn kernels (to relax smoothness assumptions) and Warped GPs (to handle strictly positive quality scores).

The main contributions of this work, ordered by strength, are:

1. **Proposing NLPD as a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, while NLPD effectively distinguishes between models with similar point-estimate performance but different uncertainty characteristics.

2. **Demonstrating practical utility of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios**: The authors show how predictive distributions can be leveraged for better decision-making in real-world applications (e.g., post-editing workflows) where the cost of overestimating or underestimating quality differs.

3. **Introducing practical model extensions for GPs in QE**: The authors propose and validate the use of Matérn kernels and Warped GPs as improvements over standard GP approaches for QE, providing a clear theoretical justification for these choices.

## Strengths

1. **Strong theoretical motivation for uncertainty in QE**: The paper provides a compelling argument for why uncertainty estimates matter in practical QE applications, using concrete examples like post-editing scenarios where decision-making is risk-sensitive. The distinction between "pessimistic" and "optimistic" scenarios is particularly well-motivated and relevant to real-world translation workflows, making the work highly applicable rather than purely theoretical.

2. **Novel evaluation metric focus with empirical validation**: The authors correctly identify a critical gap in how probabilistic models are evaluated in QE (relying on point-estimate metrics when uncertainty is the focus), and they provide strong empirical evidence that NLPD is a more appropriate metric. The analysis showing how NLPD penalizes overconfident models while rewarding underconfident ones is particularly convincing and addresses a fundamental issue in the field.

3. **Clear connection to practical applications**: Unlike many papers that focus solely on theoretical or metric improvements, this work directly demonstrates how uncertainty estimates can improve real decision-making processes (e.g., filtering MT segments for post-editing based on quality and confidence). The asymmetric risk scenarios provide a clear bridge between theoretical uncertainty modeling and practical implementation.

4. **Well-designed experimental evaluation**: The experiments across three language pairs (en-es, fr-en, en-de) with appropriate metrics (NLL, NLPD, MAE, Pearson's r) provide a comprehensive assessment of the proposed models. The 10-fold cross-validation approach adds robustness to the results, and the comparison of different kernel and warping function choices is well-justified.

5. **Practical model extensions with clear implementation**: The paper doesn't just propose theoretical improvements but provides concrete, implementable extensions to standard GP approaches (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) that can be readily adopted by the QE community. The detailed explanation of how these models work and how to implement them adds significant practical value.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited scope of experiments**: The paper only evaluates on three language pairs with a standard set of 17 features, which is relatively limited for a paper claiming general applicability to text regression tasks. The authors acknowledge the small size of the en-es dataset as a potential limitation but don't provide sufficient analysis to determine if the observed patterns (e.g., inconsistent results across datasets) are specific to dataset size or indicative of a more fundamental issue with the proposed models.

2. **Lack of comparison to alternative probabilistic models**: While the paper argues that GPs are the most appropriate choice for probabilistic QE due to their ability to provide well-calibrated uncertainty, it doesn't compare against other probabilistic models that could provide uncertainty estimates (e.g., Bayesian neural networks). This weakens the claim that GPs are uniquely suited for this task and leaves open the question of whether alternative approaches might achieve similar or better results.

3. **Inconsistent results across datasets, particularly for the en-es dataset**: The results show notable inconsistencies across the three language pairs, especially with the smaller en-es dataset. The authors attribute this to dataset size, but they don't provide sufficient evidence to confirm this is the primary factor. The paper would be stronger if it could identify why certain models work better for certain language pairs or provide a more systematic analysis of the dataset characteristics that influence model performance.

4. **Limited discussion of computational costs**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of the proposed probabilistic models compared to standard point-estimate models. For practical adoption in real-world translation workflows, it's important to know whether the improved uncertainty estimates come with a significant computational burden. This is a critical consideration for the target audience (practitioners in translation workflows).

5. **Theoretical justification for model choices is somewhat thin**: While the paper explains why Matérn kernels and Warped GPs might be useful, the theoretical justification for why these specific choices are better for QE (compared to other possible extensions) is not as strong as it could be. For instance, the paper doesn't explore the relationship between the smoothness properties of different kernels and the specific characteristics of QE data.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper uses a standard set of 17 features from the QuEst framework. How would the results change if more sophisticated feature sets (like those used in top-performing systems in WMT QE shared tasks) were used? Would the improvements from using uncertainty estimates still hold, or might they be overshadowed by better point-estimate features?

2. The paper shows that for the pessimistic scenario, the linex loss results for warped GPs were consistently worse than standard GPs. Could the authors provide more insight into why this might be happening, and whether this is a fundamental limitation of warped GPs or a specific issue with the implementation (e.g., related to the warping function's flexibility)?

3. The authors propose using Warped GPs with a parametric tanh function, but they don't discuss how the number of tanh terms (I) should be chosen. In practice, how would a user determine the appropriate value of I for their specific QE task, and how would this choice impact model performance and overfitting?

4. The paper identifies that the en-es dataset (smaller in size) shows inconsistent results compared to the other datasets. Could the authors provide more detailed analysis of the en-es dataset (e.g., feature distributions, quality score distributions) to better understand why the results differ and whether the proposed methods might require specific adaptations for smaller datasets?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and makes a strong case for the importance of uncertainty in QE. The connection between theoretical uncertainty modeling and practical decision-making is particularly compelling. The authors have clearly identified a gap in the field and provided a concrete way to address it. The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of computational costs and a broader experimental evaluation to strengthen the generalizability of their claims. The writing is clear and accessible, making the technical contributions understandable to a broad audience in the NLP community.
