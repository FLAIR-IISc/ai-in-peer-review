

# Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper makes a compelling argument for shifting the paradigm of Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) from point estimate predictions to uncertainty-aware models. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with specific extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) to better model predictive uncertainty, introduce NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) as a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic models, and demonstrate practical applications in asymmetric risk scenarios.

The primary contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. **The conceptual shift from point estimates to uncertainty-aware predictions in QE**, arguing that uncertainty information is crucial for real-world decision-making (e.g., post-editing workflows).
2. **Introducing NLPD as a primary evaluation metric** for probabilistic QE models, demonstrating that it captures uncertainty information that traditional metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) miss.
3. **Demonstrating practical applications of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios** (pessimistic/optimistic settings), showing how well-calibrated predictive distributions can improve decision-making.

## Strengths

1. **Novel conceptual framing**: The paper fundamentally challenges the field's focus on point estimates by arguing that uncertainty information is essential for real-world QE applications. This is a bold, paradigm-shifting contribution that could reshape how QE is approached in the community. The authors effectively illustrate this with concrete examples (post-editing workflows) where uncertainty estimates directly impact decision quality.

2. **Strong justification for NLPD as an evaluation metric**: The paper provides compelling evidence that NLPD captures information about predictive uncertainty that traditional metrics miss. Their experiments show that models with similar point estimate performance can have vastly different uncertainty characteristics, which NLPD captures while point estimate metrics fail to distinguish. This is a significant methodological contribution that could improve evaluation practices across probabilistic ML.

3. **Practical demonstration of uncertainty in asymmetric risk scenarios**: The paper's application to pessimistic (high-quality threshold) and optimistic (low-quality threshold) scenarios demonstrates the real-world value of uncertainty estimates. By showing how predictive distributions can be used to derive Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses, the authors provide a concrete path for implementing uncertainty-aware decision making in production systems.

4. **Thoughtful extension of GP literature to QE**: The authors make a strong case for why Matèrn kernels (for noisy data) and Warped GPs (for strictly positive response variables) are particularly appropriate for QE. While these are standard GP extensions, their application to QE is novel and well-justified by the characteristics of QE data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art point estimate models**: While the paper convincingly argues for the importance of uncertainty, it doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that their proposed models achieve competitive point estimate performance compared to current state-of-the-art QE systems. For QE to be adopted in practice, point estimate accuracy must be acceptable first, and the paper doesn't establish this baseline.

2. **Narrow scope of probabilistic models**: The paper exclusively focuses on GPs, without comparing to other probabilistic approaches like Bayesian neural networks or ensemble methods. While the authors justify this by stating other models "can only provide point estimates," this is not entirely accurate (e.g., Bayesian neural networks can provide uncertainty estimates). A broader comparison would strengthen the case for GPs.

3. **Insufficient evidence for the "uncertainty difference" claim**: The paper claims that uncertainty modeling differences aren't captured by point estimate metrics, but the evidence provided (Table 1) shows only modest differences. More rigorous statistical testing or additional examples would better support this key claim.

4. **Limited experimental scale**: The experiments are conducted on only three language pairs with relatively small datasets. While the authors acknowledge this as a limitation, more extensive evaluation across diverse language pairs and larger datasets would provide stronger evidence for the generalizability of their claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you elaborate on why you believe GP-based approaches are the only viable probabilistic option for QE, given that Bayesian neural networks and other methods also provide uncertainty estimates?

2. How would you recommend integrating these uncertainty-aware QE models into existing MT pipelines, particularly considering the computational overhead of GPs compared to traditional regression models?

3. Given the limited dataset sizes, could you discuss potential strategies for scaling these approaches to larger QE datasets or more complex language pairs?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a significant and novel contribution by fundamentally rethinking how QE should be approached through the lens of uncertainty. While the experimental validation could be strengthened, the conceptual shift and methodological innovation are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. The paper's focus on practical applications of uncertainty in decision-making scenarios is particularly compelling and aligns with the growing need for more transparent and reliable AI systems.

The paper represents a bold step forward in the field, moving beyond incremental improvements to propose a new paradigm for quality estimation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly by strengthening the point estimate performance comparison and expanding the scope of probabilistic model comparisons. The novelty and conceptual contribution of this work far outweigh the methodological limitations.
