

# Peer Review: "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates the importance of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate approaches are insufficient for practical decision-making. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with extensions (Matérn kernels and warped GPs) to better model uncertainty and introduce Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than traditional point-estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson's r.

The main contributions of the paper, in order of strength, are:

1. **Demonstrating the inadequacy of traditional evaluation metrics for probabilistic models in QE**: The paper convincingly shows that standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling that NLPD can detect.

2. **Proposing and validating model extensions for GPs in QE**: The authors provide a well-motivated case for using Matérn kernels (which relax the smoothness assumption of standard EQ kernels) and warped GPs (which handle strictly positive quality scores) specifically for QE tasks.

3. **Demonstrating practical utility of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios**: The paper shows how predictive distributions can be leveraged for different application contexts (pessimistic vs. optimistic scenarios) using asymmetric loss functions.

## Strengths

1. **Compelling practical motivation**: The paper effectively argues why uncertainty estimation matters in real-world QE applications through clear examples (e.g., post-editing scenarios where underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating). This addresses a genuine gap in the field and makes a strong case for the work's relevance beyond academic interest.

2. **Well-justified evaluation methodology**: The introduction and validation of NLPD as a proper metric for probabilistic models is well-supported. The paper demonstrates convincingly that NLPD captures uncertainty information that traditional metrics miss, which is a significant contribution to evaluation practices in QE.

3. **Thoughtful model extensions**: The use of Matérn kernels and warped GPs is well-motivated for QE's noisy data and strictly positive quality scores. The ablation studies comparing different warping functions and kernel types provide valuable insights for future work in the field.

4. **Practical demonstration of asymmetric risk handling**: The paper's exploration of how uncertainty estimates can be leveraged in different application contexts (pessimistic vs. optimistic scenarios) provides concrete evidence of the practical value of the proposed approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited dataset size and generalizability concerns**: The experiments use relatively small datasets (en-es has only 858 instances), which limits the robustness of the findings. The paper acknowledges this for the en-es dataset but doesn't adequately address how small dataset sizes might impact the generalizability of the results to larger-scale applications.

2. **Insufficient comparison with alternative probabilistic models**: While the authors claim "other common models used in QE can only provide point estimates," they don't provide a thorough comparison with other probabilistic approaches (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, Bayesian linear regression) that might be applicable to QE. This weakens the claim that GPs are the only viable probabilistic approach.

3. **Superficial analysis of asymmetric loss performance**: The paper notes that warped GPs perform worse with linex loss in pessimistic scenarios but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of *why* this occurs. The limited discussion of this finding (attributed to "less variance" in warped models) doesn't provide sufficient insight for practitioners.

4. **Lack of computational complexity analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss how the proposed model extensions (Matérn kernels, warped GPs) impact training and prediction time. For practical deployment in production QE systems, computational efficiency is as important as performance.

5. **Overstated generalizability claim**: The assertion that "the methods we explore in this paper can be applied to any text regression task" lacks evidence. The paper doesn't provide examples or analysis demonstrating how these methods would generalize to other text regression tasks beyond QE.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the main reason behind [warped GPs] decreasing both NLL and NLPD is that standard GPs distribute probability mass over negative values." Can you provide specific evidence from your results (e.g., histogram of predicted values) to support this claim?

2. For the asymmetric risk scenarios, why did you choose specific values for the weight parameters (w=3, 1/3 for AL loss; w=-0.75, 0.75 for linex loss)? Were these chosen based on empirical analysis or theoretical considerations?

3. The paper mentions that "the gains from using a Matèrn kernel over EQ are less conclusive." Given the authors' strong motivation for Matèrn kernels (relaxed smoothness assumptions), what specific characteristics of the QE datasets might explain this lack of consistent improvement?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-written paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of uncertainty in Quality Estimation. The practical motivation is strong, the evaluation methodology is sound, and the proposed extensions to Gaussian Processes are well-motivated. While the experimental scope has limitations, the paper provides important insights that will inform future work in QE. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the comparison with alternative probabilistic models and the analysis of asymmetric loss performance. The paper's core contribution—demonstrating the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE and providing a proper evaluation metric for it—makes it a valuable addition to the field.
