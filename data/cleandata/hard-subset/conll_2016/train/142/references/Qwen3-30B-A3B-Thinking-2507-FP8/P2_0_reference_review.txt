

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper explores the importance of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate models are insufficient for practical decision-making scenarios. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with specific extensions (Matérn kernels and warped GPs) to better model uncertainty, introduce Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric for uncertainty-aware models, and demonstrate how predictive distributions can be utilized in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic settings).

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1. A compelling argument for the importance of uncertainty estimation in practical QE applications, supported by concrete examples in post-editing and gisting scenarios.
2. The demonstration that standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling, making NLPD a necessary metric for evaluating uncertainty-aware QE models.
3. A practical application of predictive uncertainty in asymmetric loss scenarios, showing how models with better-calibrated uncertainty can improve decision-making in specific contexts.

## Strengths

1. **Strong Motivation for Uncertainty Estimation**: The paper provides a compelling real-world motivation for why uncertainty estimation matters in QE. The post-editing scenario example clearly illustrates how point estimates alone can lead to poor decision-making (allowing low-quality translations to go through for post-editing), while uncertainty estimates enable more informed filtering decisions. This is a significant contribution to the field, as most QE work focuses solely on improving point estimates without considering the uncertainty of those estimates.

2. **Novel Evaluation Metric with Strong Justification**: The authors correctly identify that standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling. Their introduction of NLPD as a metric that evaluates the entire predictive distribution is well-justified and addresses a genuine gap in the evaluation methodology for probabilistic models in QE. The experimental results showing that NLPD captures differences in uncertainty modeling that MAE and Pearson's r miss provide strong evidence for the necessity of this new metric.

3. **Practical Application in Asymmetric Risk Scenarios**: The paper's demonstration of how uncertainty estimates can be leveraged in asymmetric loss scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic settings) is particularly valuable. The authors successfully show that models with better-calibrated uncertainty can outperform point-estimate models in these scenarios, providing concrete evidence that uncertainty information is useful for real-world decision-making. This is not just theoretical but has clear practical implications for QE applications.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with State-of-the-Art Point-Estimate Models**: While the paper argues that uncertainty estimates are important, it fails to demonstrate that the proposed GP-based models actually outperform state-of-the-art point-estimate QE models in terms of point prediction quality. The paper focuses on uncertainty metrics, but without showing that the GP models achieve competitive point prediction performance (e.g., comparable MAE to SVR or neural network baselines), it's unclear whether the improved uncertainty comes at the cost of worse point predictions. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation but don't provide evidence that the trade-off is favorable.

2. **Insufficient Statistical Validation of Results**: The paper reports average metrics across 10-fold cross-validation but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the observed differences between models. For instance, the paper claims that warped GPs with tanh functions outperform standard GPs on fr-en and en-de datasets, but without statistical tests (e.g., paired t-tests), it's impossible to determine if these differences are meaningful or merely due to random variation in the folds.

3. **Limited Comparison with Other Probabilistic Models**: The paper dismisses other probabilistic models as not being applicable to QE, but this is an overstatement. Recent work has shown that Bayesian neural networks and other probabilistic deep learning approaches can also provide uncertainty estimates for QE. The authors don't compare their GP-based approach with these alternatives, making it difficult to assess whether GPs are truly the best choice for uncertainty modeling in QE or if other methods might be more effective.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a comparison between the point prediction performance (MAE, Pearson's r) of your proposed GP models and state-of-the-art point-estimate QE models (e.g., SVR, neural networks)? Without this, it's difficult to assess whether the improved uncertainty comes at the cost of worse point predictions.

2. The paper claims that "the differences in uncertainty modelling are by and large not captured by the point estimate metrics," but the results show some correlation between NLPD and MAE. Could you elaborate on why this correlation is limited and what specific scenarios would benefit from uncertainty estimation even when point predictions are similar?

3. The paper uses a small number of features (17 features from QuEst) for all language pairs. How would the results change if the authors used more comprehensive feature sets that are typically used in state-of-the-art QE systems? Would the improvements from uncertainty modeling be maintained with more complex feature engineering?

4. The paper mentions that the en-es dataset is smaller and shows different behavior, but doesn't discuss how the authors would handle the uncertainty estimation problem in very small data regimes (which is common in QE). Could you elaborate on how your approach might be adapted for such scenarios?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE and demonstrating how it can be leveraged in practical decision-making scenarios. The proposed NLPD metric is well-justified and addresses a genuine gap in evaluation methodology. However, the paper's claim that their approach provides "better" QE models is weakened by the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art point-estimate models and insufficient statistical validation of their results.

I recommend *weak acceptance* with the understanding that the paper would need to address the weaknesses mentioned above in a revision. The core idea is strong and the evaluation of uncertainty is important, but the authors need to provide more evidence that their approach actually improves the practical utility of QE systems beyond just providing better uncertainty estimates.
