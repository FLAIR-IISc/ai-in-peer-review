

# Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models for Quality Estimation (QE) in machine translation, with a specific focus on prediction uncertainty. The authors argue that traditional point estimate approaches are insufficient for practical decision-making in translation workflows, and propose Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a more suitable framework for capturing uncertainty. The main contributions are:
1. Demonstrating that standard point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, while Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) serves as a more appropriate evaluation metric for probabilistic models in QE.
2. Proposing and validating the use of Matèrn kernels and warped Gaussian Processes as more appropriate modeling choices for QE tasks.
3. Showing how predictive distributions can be effectively utilized in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., post-editing with different risk preferences) through asymmetric loss functions without requiring model retraining.

## Strengths

1. **Clear practical motivation for uncertainty estimation**: The paper effectively demonstrates why point estimates are insufficient for real-world QE applications through compelling examples (e.g., post-editing workflows where uncertainty information enables better filtering decisions). This addresses a significant gap in the QE literature, where most work focuses on point estimate accuracy rather than the practical utility of uncertainty information.

2. **Strong evidence for NLPD as a superior evaluation metric**: The paper provides clear experimental evidence across multiple datasets (en-es, fr-en, en-de) showing that NLPD consistently captures differences in uncertainty modeling that standard point estimate metrics fail to detect. The analysis demonstrates that NLPD properly penalizes overconfident models in cases of uncertainty mismatch, making it a theoretically sound metric for probabilistic QE models.

3. **Practical demonstration of asymmetric loss applications**: The paper successfully shows how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios without retraining models, using Bayes risk estimators. The results for optimistic scenarios (where warped GPs with tanh-based warping outperform standard GPs) provide concrete evidence of the practical benefits of uncertainty estimation in real-world translation workflows.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art QE methods**: The paper focuses on probabilistic modeling aspects but doesn't sufficiently compare the point estimate performance of the proposed GP models with the current state-of-the-art non-probabilistic QE systems. Without this comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the uncertainty benefits come at a significant cost to point estimate accuracy, which is a major consideration for practical adoption.

2. **Inadequate explanation of Matèrn kernel benefits**: While the paper proposes Matèrn kernels as a promising extension, the results show inconsistent performance across datasets (with improvements only for fr-en). The paper fails to provide a thorough analysis of why Matèrn kernels might be beneficial for certain QE datasets but not others, limiting the practical guidance for QE practitioners.

3. **Unexplained inconsistency with linex loss in pessimistic scenarios**: The paper notes that warped GPs perform worse than standard GPs with the linex loss in pessimistic scenarios, but doesn't adequately explain this phenomenon. The authors suggest this might be due to the warped models having less variance, but this is not thoroughly investigated or validated, weakening the claim about the general applicability of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper shows that warped GPs with tanh-based warping outperform log-based warping across most datasets, but doesn't provide a clear explanation for this pattern. Could the authors elaborate on why tanh-based warping might be more suitable for QE datasets specifically?

2. The paper mentions that Matèrn kernels show inconsistent improvements across datasets. Could the authors provide more insight into the characteristics of QE datasets that might make Matèrn kernels more appropriate (or less appropriate)?

3. The inconsistent results with linex loss in pessimistic scenarios (where warped GPs perform worse) require further explanation. Is this a fundamental limitation of warped GPs in certain risk scenarios, or could it be addressed through model modifications?

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE applications and demonstrating that standard evaluation metrics fail to capture this aspect. The introduction of NLPD as a more appropriate evaluation metric for probabilistic models is well-supported by the experimental evidence. The practical application of predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios also provides concrete evidence of the value of uncertainty information.

However, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art QE methods and a deeper analysis of when Matèrn kernels provide benefits. The inconsistent results with linex loss also need more thorough explanation. Despite these limitations, the paper makes a compelling case for considering uncertainty in QE and provides a clear methodology for evaluating probabilistic models in this domain.
