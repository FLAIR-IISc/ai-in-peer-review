

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with specific extensions (Matèrn kernels and warped GPs) for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that uncertainty estimates are crucial for decision-making in practical QE applications. The authors claim three main contributions: (1) demonstrating that standard point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, and NLPD should be preferred; (2) proposing specific GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) for better uncertainty modeling in QE; and (3) showing how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios.

## Strengths

1. **Methodological Rigor in Uncertainty Evaluation**: The paper correctly identifies that standard QE metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) do not capture uncertainty differences between models, and the authors appropriately propose NLPD as a more comprehensive evaluation metric. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it addresses a genuine limitation in current QE evaluation practices.

2. **Clear Practical Motivation**: The paper convincingly argues that uncertainty estimates are valuable for real-world QE applications, particularly in scenarios where decisions about which translations to post-edit should be made. The examples provided (pessimistic vs. optimistic scenarios) are well-chosen and illustrate the practical importance of uncertainty estimation.

3. **Thorough Evaluation of GP Extensions**: The authors carefully evaluate different kernel choices (Matèrn vs. EQ) and warping functions (log vs. parametric tanh), providing empirical evidence for their comparative performance. The findings that warped GPs consistently outperform standard GPs on most datasets are credible and well-supported.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison with Established Probabilistic Baselines**: The paper claims GPs are the "state-of-the-art for regression" in QE, yet fails to compare with other probabilistic models that have been successfully used in QE. For instance, Bayesian neural networks, which have been applied to QE (e.g., Beck et al., 2014b), could provide a more meaningful baseline for evaluating the proposed GP extensions. The absence of such comparisons makes the claimed superiority of the proposed GP extensions appear unsubstantiated.

2. **Insufficient Evidence for Novelty Claims**: The paper's strongest claim—that the proposed GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) are "more appropriate for the task" of QE—lacks rigorous validation. The experiments only evaluate these extensions on three small datasets (with the smallest containing only 858 instances), which is insufficient to establish generalizability. The authors observe that "different kernels might be more appropriate depending on the language pair but more experiments are needed," which directly undermines their claim of having identified superior approaches.

3. **Questionable Practical Relevance of Asymmetric Loss Experiments**: While the paper's discussion of asymmetric loss scenarios is theoretically interesting, the empirical evaluation lacks practical significance. The results for the pessimistic scenario (with linex loss) are described as "inconclusive" and even "consistently worse than the standard GP baseline," yet the authors still present these as a contribution. The paper fails to demonstrate that these asymmetric scenarios represent real-world problems where QE models are actually deployed.

4. **Overstated Claims about NLPD's Superiority**: The paper claims NLPD should "be preferred as an evaluation metric when proper prediction uncertainty estimates are required," but provides minimal evidence beyond the observation that point estimate metrics don't capture uncertainty differences. The authors don't address whether NLPD has been validated in the QE community or whether it correlates with practical decision-making outcomes. Without such validation, the recommendation to adopt NLPD as a standard metric is premature.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why did the authors choose to focus exclusively on GP extensions without comparing with other probabilistic models that have been successfully applied to QE, such as Bayesian neural networks or ensemble methods?

2. The paper notes that "the gains from using a Matèrn kernel over EQ are less conclusive" and that "different kernels might be more appropriate depending on the language pair." Given these observations, how can the authors justify the claim that their proposed models are "more appropriate for the task" without extensive validation across multiple language pairs and datasets?

3. The asymmetric loss experiments for the pessimistic scenario (linex loss) showed "inconclusive" results. Can the authors provide a clearer explanation for why these results were inconsistent with their theoretical expectations, and how this impacts the practical usefulness of their proposed models?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE and advocating for the use of NLPD as an evaluation metric. However, the paper's claims about the superiority of the proposed GP extensions lack sufficient evidence, and the experimental validation is limited to small datasets without comparison to relevant baselines. The paper's main contribution is methodological (the evaluation metric), but this is overshadowed by unsubstantiated claims about the novelty and superiority of the proposed models. I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence supporting the paper's primary claims about model improvements, though the evaluation metric discussion is noteworthy. The authors should conduct more rigorous experiments with appropriate baselines and larger datasets before making stronger claims about model improvements.
