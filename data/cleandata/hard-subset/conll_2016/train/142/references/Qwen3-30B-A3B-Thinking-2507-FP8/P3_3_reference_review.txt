

# Peer Review: "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary
This paper investigates the importance of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate QE models are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with Matérn kernels and warped GPs to better model uncertainty, introduce NLPD as a more appropriate evaluation metric that captures uncertainty information, and demonstrate how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., post-editing vs. gisting).

## Main Contributions
1. **NLPD as a superior evaluation metric**: The paper convincingly demonstrates that NLPD captures uncertainty modeling differences not reflected in traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r), which is crucial for selecting appropriate probabilistic QE models.
2. **Model extensions for QE**: The authors provide strong theoretical and empirical justification for using Matérn kernels (to handle noisy QE data) and warped GPs (to address non-Gaussian quality scores), showing significant improvements over standard GP approaches.
3. **Application to asymmetric risk scenarios**: The paper successfully demonstrates how calibrated uncertainty estimates can improve performance in practical decision-making contexts where underestimation and overestimation have asymmetric consequences.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation for uncertainty estimation**: The paper effectively argues why uncertainty estimation is critical for real-world QE applications, particularly in post-editing workflows where the cost of misjudging quality is asymmetric. The examples of pessimistic (post-editing) and optimistic (gisting) scenarios are well-chosen and highly relevant to practical translation workflows.

2. **Compelling evidence for NLPD as an evaluation metric**: The experimental results in Table 1 provide clear evidence that NLPD captures uncertainty information missed by traditional metrics. This is a significant methodological contribution that should influence future QE research and evaluation practices.

3. **Theoretical justification for model extensions**: The paper provides a thorough explanation of why Matérn kernels and warped GPs are better suited for QE than standard GP approaches, addressing specific challenges of the task (noisy data, non-Gaussian response variables). The empirical results support this theoretical argument.

4. **Practical application demonstration**: The asymmetric risk scenario experiments show that the proposed models can directly improve performance in real-world decision-making contexts, moving beyond theoretical discussion to practical demonstration.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited experimental scope**: The paper uses only three language pairs, with the English-Spanish dataset being significantly smaller (858 instances) than the others (2,525 and 2,828 instances). This limits the generalizability of findings, particularly regarding the performance differences between warping functions (where log performed better for the smaller dataset). More extensive experiments across multiple language pairs would strengthen the claims.

2. **Lack of comparison with alternative probabilistic models**: While the authors argue that other common QE models can only provide point estimates, they don't compare with other probabilistic approaches like Bayesian neural networks or ensemble methods. A more comprehensive comparison would better establish the relative advantages of their GP-based approach.

3. **Inadequate explanation of contradictory results**: The paper reports that warped GP models performed worse than standard GPs in the pessimistic linex loss scenario, which contradicts their own argument about the benefits of uncertainty modeling. The explanation provided ("warped models tend to have less variance") is insufficient to explain why this would lead to worse performance under exponential penalty, undermining the paper's main claim.

4. **Insufficient discussion of practical implementation challenges**: The paper doesn't address computational complexity or scalability concerns of warped GPs with multiple tanh terms, which is critical for real-world deployment. It also fails to provide guidance on how asymmetric loss parameters (w) should be determined or adapted in production environments.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you address the computational complexity of warped GPs with multiple tanh terms in large-scale QE applications, where efficiency is crucial?

2. Can you provide more insight into why warped GPs performed worse than standard GPs in the pessimistic linex scenario, given that the authors argue their approach should improve performance in asymmetric settings?

3. How would you recommend determining the asymmetric loss parameters (w) in a production environment, and what mechanisms would you propose for dynamically adjusting them based on real-world feedback?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written, theoretically sound paper that makes a compelling case for the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE. The demonstration of NLPD as a superior evaluation metric is particularly valuable and should influence future research in the field. The model extensions (Matérn kernels, warped GPs) are well-justified and show clear empirical benefits.

However, the limited experimental scope, lack of comparison with alternative probabilistic models, and unexplained contradictory results in the asymmetric risk scenario weaken the paper's impact. Addressing these limitations would significantly strengthen the contribution.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by shifting the focus from point-estimate accuracy to uncertainty modeling in QE, with clear practical implications for real-world translation workflows. With minor revisions addressing the weaknesses noted above, this paper would make an excellent contribution to CONLL.
