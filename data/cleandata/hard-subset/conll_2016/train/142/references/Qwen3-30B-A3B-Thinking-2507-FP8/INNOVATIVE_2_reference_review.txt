

# Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary and Contributions

This paper makes a compelling case for shifting the paradigm of Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) from point estimate prediction to uncertainty-aware prediction. The authors argue that in practical settings (e.g., post-editing workflows), having uncertainty estimates alongside quality predictions enables better decision-making. Their primary contributions are:

1. **Proposing NLPD as the appropriate evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models** - challenging the traditional use of MAE and Pearson's r, which fail to capture uncertainty information.

2. **Introducing novel probabilistic modeling techniques for QE** - specifically applying Matérn kernels and Warped Gaussian Processes to address the specific characteristics of QE data (noisy, positive response variables).

3. **Demonstrating practical utility of uncertainty in asymmetric risk scenarios** - showing how uncertainty estimates can be leveraged in realistic post-editing workflows with different risk preferences.

## Strengths

**1. Fundamental Shift in Perspective:** This paper offers a paradigm shift in how we evaluate QE models. The authors correctly identify that traditional QE evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) are fundamentally inadequate for probabilistic models that provide uncertainty estimates. By advocating for NLPD as the primary evaluation metric, they address a critical gap in the field. This conceptual contribution alone is significant and could influence future QE research.

**2. Practical Application of Uncertainty:** The paper doesn't just propose theoretical improvements but demonstrates concrete applications of uncertainty estimates in realistic scenarios. The asymmetric risk framework (pessimistic vs. optimistic scenarios) provides a compelling practical motivation for uncertainty estimation that resonates with real-world translation workflows. This bridges the gap between theoretical uncertainty modeling and practical decision-making.

**3. Novel Application of Advanced GP Techniques:** While Matérn kernels and Warped GPs exist in the broader ML literature, their application to QE is novel. The authors provide a thoughtful justification for why these extensions are particularly suitable for QE data characteristics (noisy, positive response variables), demonstrating a deep understanding of the task-specific challenges.

**4. Clear Experimental Validation:** The paper provides thorough experimental validation across three language pairs with multiple modeling variants. The results consistently show that uncertainty-aware models (when properly evaluated with NLPD) outperform traditional point estimate models, and the differences are often missed by conventional metrics.

## Weaknesses

**1. Limited Comparison to Alternative Probabilistic Models:** While the paper focuses on Gaussian Processes, it doesn't compare against other probabilistic approaches (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, Bayesian SVR) that could also provide uncertainty estimates. This limits the strength of their claim that their specific approach is superior.

**2. Limited Analysis of Uncertainty Calibration:** The paper focuses on NLPD as an evaluation metric but doesn't deeply analyze how well-calibrated the uncertainty estimates are (e.g., through reliability diagrams). Calibration is a critical aspect of uncertainty estimation that deserves more attention.

**3. Small Dataset Limitations:** The en-es dataset (858 sentences) is quite small, and the authors note potential overfitting issues. While they acknowledge this, the paper doesn't sufficiently address how these limitations affect the reliability of their conclusions for smaller QE datasets.

## Recommendation

I recommend **acceptance** for this paper. The core contribution—shifting the evaluation paradigm for QE from point estimates to uncertainty-aware models—is significant and timely. The authors have identified a meaningful gap in the field and provided a compelling framework for addressing it.

While the paper has some methodological limitations (primarily the narrow focus on GP models), these are outweighed by the novelty of their perspective. The field of QE has been largely focused on improving point estimates, and this paper correctly identifies that uncertainty is the next critical frontier. The practical demonstration of how uncertainty can be used in asymmetric risk scenarios is particularly strong.

The paper's novelty and conceptual contribution are substantial enough to justify acceptance, even with the minor methodological limitations. The authors have successfully demonstrated that uncertainty estimation is not just a theoretical curiosity but has practical value in real translation workflows.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do your Warped GP models compare to other probabilistic approaches (e.g., Bayesian neural networks) in terms of uncertainty estimation for QE? Have you considered these comparisons?

2. In Table 2, the Warped GP models perform better in optimistic scenarios but worse in pessimistic scenarios with linex loss. What are the specific characteristics of the predictive distributions that lead to this difference, and how might this be addressed?

3. The paper mentions that the log warping function works better for the small en-es dataset. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, and whether this suggests any insights about the relationship between dataset size and optimal warping function choice?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the QE field by shifting the focus from point estimates to uncertainty-aware prediction, which is a necessary step toward more robust and practical QE systems. The proposed evaluation metric (NLPD) and the demonstration of practical applications provide a strong foundation for future research in this direction.
