

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper investigates the role of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate-based QE models are insufficient for decision-making scenarios where uncertainty information is valuable. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with specific extensions for better uncertainty modeling, and advocate for the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r).

The main contributions of this work, ordered by strength, are:

1. **Demonstrating NLPD as a superior evaluation metric for uncertainty-aware QE models**: The paper provides empirical evidence that NLPD captures differences in uncertainty modeling that traditional metrics miss, showing that models with similar point-estimate performance can have very different uncertainty characteristics.

2. **Proposing and evaluating model extensions for better uncertainty modeling in QE**: The authors introduce Matèrn kernels and warped Gaussian Processes with parametric warping functions (tanh-based) as improvements over standard GP approaches for QE, showing improved uncertainty estimation on multiple datasets.

3. **Demonstrating practical utility of predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios**: The paper shows how uncertainty information can be leveraged in real-world applications with asymmetric risk (e.g., post-editing vs. gisting scenarios), demonstrating that the proposed models provide better performance under asymmetric loss functions.

## Strengths

1. **Strong justification for NLPD as an evaluation metric**: The paper provides a compelling theoretical and empirical case for NLPD as a more comprehensive metric that accounts for uncertainty. The evidence showing that NLPD captures differences in uncertainty modeling that MAE and Pearson's r miss is well-supported by the results in Table 1. This addresses a significant gap in how QE models are evaluated, which is a substantial contribution to the field.

2. **Clear demonstration of practical utility**: The paper effectively shows how predictive distributions can be used in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic settings), which directly addresses real-world applications of QE. The experimental results in Section 4 demonstrate that the proposed models outperform standard GPs in these scenarios, providing concrete evidence of the value of uncertainty information in decision-making.

3. **Technical soundness of model extensions**: The proposed extensions (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) are well-motivated and technically sound. The paper provides clear theoretical foundations for these extensions and demonstrates their empirical benefits across multiple language pairs. The careful experimental design with 10-fold cross-validation and comparison of multiple model variants adds credibility to the findings.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty in model extensions**: While the application of Matèrn kernels and warped GPs to QE is novel, these are well-established techniques in the GP literature. The paper doesn't sufficiently establish why these specific extensions are uniquely valuable for QE beyond what's already known in the GP community. The improvements reported are modest and inconsistent across datasets, which weakens the claim of significant methodological advancement.

2. **Inconsistent results across datasets**: The paper shows inconsistent results across the three language pairs, particularly with the choice of warping function (log vs. tanh). For the smallest dataset (en-es), the log warping performs better, while for larger datasets (fr-en, en-de), the tanh-based warping outperforms. The authors attribute this to dataset size, but this inconsistency undermines the generalizability of the findings and makes it difficult to recommend a single best approach.

3. **Insufficient comparison to state-of-the-art QE models**: The paper focuses on probabilistic models (GPs) but doesn't adequately compare to the current state-of-the-art in QE, which typically uses non-probabilistic models like SVR. While the authors acknowledge SVR lacks probabilistic interpretation, they don't demonstrate whether their probabilistic models outperform non-probabilistic models in terms of point estimates (the primary evaluation metric in most QE work). This omission makes it difficult to assess the practical value of their approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "differences in uncertainty modelling are not captured by the usual point estimate metrics," yet Table 1 shows MAE improves with warped GPs (except for en-es). Could the authors clarify why MAE shows some gains but doesn't reflect the differences between warping functions for fr-en, as stated in the paper?

2. The inconsistent results across datasets (particularly the dataset size effect on warping function choice) are notable. Could the authors provide more insight into why this effect occurs and whether it's possible to develop a principled approach to selecting the appropriate warping function based on dataset characteristics?

3. The paper focuses exclusively on Gaussian Process models but doesn't compare to other probabilistic approaches that could provide uncertainty estimates (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, Bayesian SVR). How do the authors' proposed GP extensions compare to these alternatives in terms of both uncertainty estimation and point estimate performance?

## Additional Comments

The paper makes a valuable contribution by highlighting the importance of uncertainty in QE and demonstrating a practical application in asymmetric risk scenarios. However, the incremental nature of the model extensions and the inconsistent results across datasets limit the paper's impact. The authors should more clearly articulate how their approach advances beyond existing work in QE and provide stronger evidence for the generalizability of their findings.

The writing is clear and well-organized, with a logical flow from motivation to experiments to applications. The experimental setup is sound, with appropriate datasets and careful evaluation across multiple language pairs. The paper's focus on the practical implications of uncertainty modeling is particularly strong and well-executed.

Overall, the paper makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of uncertainty in QE, but the technical novelty is somewhat limited and the results are not consistently strong across all datasets. The work would be strengthened by more comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art models and a clearer demonstration of the generalizability of the findings.
