

# Review for "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary
This paper explores the importance of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate approaches are insufficient for decision-making scenarios. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with specific extensions (Matérn kernels and warped GPs) to better model uncertainty, and demonstrate that the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric is more appropriate for evaluating such models than traditional metrics like MAE and Pearson's r. The paper also shows how uncertainty estimates can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic settings) where the cost of different types of errors is not symmetric.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Demonstrating the inadequacy of traditional QE evaluation metrics for capturing uncertainty information**: The paper effectively shows through experiments that standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to distinguish between models with different uncertainty characteristics, while NLPD successfully captures these differences.

2. **Proposing and empirically validating model extensions (Matérn kernels, warped GPs) specifically tailored for QE**: The authors make a compelling case for why these GP extensions are more appropriate for the noisy, non-Gaussian nature of QE data, and provide empirical evidence supporting their effectiveness.

3. **Demonstrating the practical utility of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric decision-making scenarios**: The paper provides concrete examples (post-editing vs. gisting scenarios) where uncertainty information leads to better decision-making, moving beyond theoretical discussion to practical application.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and clear problem statement**: The paper effectively establishes why uncertainty estimation matters in real-world QE applications with concrete examples (e.g., post-editing scenarios where underestimating quality would lead to poor translator experience). This makes the problem relevant and immediately understandable to practitioners.

2. **Novel evaluation perspective**: The paper correctly identifies that traditional QE evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) don't capture uncertainty information, and proposes NLPD as a more appropriate metric. This is a significant contribution to the field's evaluation practices, as it challenges the current standard.

3. **Practical demonstration of utility**: The asymmetric risk section goes beyond theoretical discussion to show how uncertainty estimates can directly inform decision-making in different application scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic settings), which is highly valuable for practitioners.

4. **Rigorous experimental design**: The authors use multiple language pairs (en-es, fr-en, en-de), multiple metrics (NLL, NLPD, MAE, Pearson's r), and 10-fold cross-validation to ensure their findings are robust across different datasets and evaluation criteria.

5. **Clear theoretical foundation**: The paper builds on well-established probabilistic modeling concepts (GPs, warped GPs, asymmetric loss functions) and applies them to QE in a principled way, with appropriate citations to the relevant literature.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with other probabilistic models**: The paper exclusively focuses on GP-based approaches without comparing them to other probabilistic models that could also provide uncertainty estimates (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, Bayesian linear regression). This limits the claim that GP extensions are the "best" approach for uncertainty estimation in QE.

2. **Insufficient discussion of computational complexity**: GPs have O(n³) complexity, which can be prohibitive for larger datasets. The paper doesn't address the computational cost of the proposed models compared to standard SVR-based QE models, which is crucial for practical adoption in industry settings.

3. **Inconsistent results in asymmetric loss scenarios**: The paper shows that warped GPs perform well for AL loss in optimistic scenarios but poorly for linex loss in pessimistic scenarios. The authors don't adequately explain why this inconsistency occurs or provide guidance on when to use which model.

4. **Limited discussion of practical implementation challenges**: While the paper demonstrates the theoretical benefits of uncertainty estimation, it doesn't address practical challenges in implementing these models in production systems (e.g., model selection, hyperparameter tuning, integration with existing QE pipelines).

5. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art QE systems**: The paper focuses on uncertainty estimation rather than improving point estimate performance. However, it doesn't compare the point estimate performance of the proposed models to state-of-the-art QE systems, making it difficult to assess whether the uncertainty improvements come at the cost of reduced point estimate accuracy.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do the computational requirements of your proposed GP-based models compare to standard SVR-based QE models (which are widely used in practice), and how would this impact scalability for larger datasets?

2. In Table 2, the warped GP models with tanh warping function perform poorly on the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario compared to standard GPs. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, and if there are specific conditions under which warped GPs would be preferable for pessimistic scenarios?

3. Have you considered exploring the impact of different feature sets on your results? The paper uses a fixed set of 17 features from QuEst, but it's unclear whether the benefits of your proposed models would hold with more comprehensive feature sets used in state-of-the-art QE systems.

4. The paper mentions that the en-es dataset is smaller and might be causing overfitting issues. Have you considered using techniques like data augmentation or regularization to address this issue, or would the proposed models need to be adapted for smaller datasets?

5. How do you envision integrating uncertainty estimation into existing QE pipelines in production environments, where the primary concern is often point estimate accuracy rather than uncertainty information?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written, clearly argued paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE. The paper's strongest contribution is demonstrating that traditional evaluation metrics fail to capture uncertainty information, and proposing NLPD as a more appropriate metric. The model extensions (Matérn kernels, warped GPs) are well-motivated and empirically validated. However, the lack of comparison with other probabilistic models and insufficient discussion of computational complexity are significant limitations that should be addressed.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, primarily to address the weaknesses related to model comparisons and computational considerations. The paper's core contribution regarding uncertainty estimation in QE is timely and valuable for both researchers and practitioners in the field.
