

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary
This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for uncertainty estimation in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that traditional QE models provide only point estimates, but in practical applications (e.g., post-editing workflows), uncertainty estimates are crucial for decision-making. They propose using NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) as an evaluation metric that accounts for uncertainty, and explore GP extensions including Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs. They demonstrate how these models can be beneficial in asymmetric risk scenarios.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. Proposing NLPD as a more appropriate evaluation metric for QE models that takes uncertainty into account, rather than relying solely on traditional metrics like MAE.
2. Exploring extensions to GP models (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) for QE with practical implementation details.
3. Demonstrating the practical utility of uncertainty estimates through asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic/optimistic settings).

## Strengths
1. **Relevant problem statement**: The paper correctly identifies a genuine limitation in current QE practice - the lack of uncertainty information for decision-making in real-world applications. The post-editing scenario example is well-chosen and clearly explains why uncertainty estimates matter.

2. **Theoretical foundation**: The paper provides a solid theoretical basis for using GPs in QE, including clear explanations of the extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) and their relevance to the task. The connection to Quiñonero-Candela et al.'s work is well-motivated.

3. **Practical demonstration**: The asymmetric risk scenario examples (pessimistic/optimistic) effectively demonstrate the practical value of uncertainty estimates. The implementation of Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses is well-executed.

## Weaknesses
1. **Methodological flaws in claiming improvements**: The paper states that "using a warping function drastically decreases both NLL and NLPD" without providing statistical significance tests. Without these tests, it's impossible to determine if the observed differences are meaningful or due to random variation. This is a critical methodological flaw in a machine learning paper.

2. **Inconsistent claims about model performance**: The paper claims "the results for linex are consistently worse than the standard GP baseline" but then states "This might be a case where a standard GP is preferred" (Section 4.3). This directly contradicts the main contribution of the paper, which is to show that their proposed models (warped GPs) outperform standard GPs. This contradiction undermines the paper's core argument.

3. **Formatting and technical inconsistencies**: The paper contains numerous formatting errors that undermine its professionalism:
   - Inconsistent spelling of "Matèrn" (sometimes "Matèrn", sometimes "Matern")
   - Inconsistent kernel formulas with improper spacing and formatting (e.g., "kM32 = σv(1 + √ 3r2) exp(− √ 3r2)")
   - Inconsistent terminology ("post-editing time" vs "post-editing rates" without clear explanation)
   - Inconsistent citation formatting (e.g., "Rasmussen and Williams, 2006" vs "Rasmussen and Williams,? 2006" in references)

4. **Lack of justification for metric choice**: The paper claims NLPD should be preferred without providing a theoretical justification for why it's superior to other uncertainty-aware metrics like Expected Calibration Error (ECE). This weakens the argument for adopting NLPD as the primary evaluation metric.

5. **Omission of important details**: The paper states they "report results only for models using the Matèrn52 kernel" without explaining why they omitted other kernel results. This selective reporting could bias the reader's perception of the results.

## Questions to Authors
1. Can you provide statistical significance tests for the observed performance differences between standard GPs and warped GPs to demonstrate that the improvements are meaningful?

2. The paper claims "NLPD should be preferred as a evaluation metric when proper prediction uncertainty estimates are required" - can you provide a theoretical justification for why NLPD is better than other uncertainty-aware metrics like ECE?

3. The paper states that "the results for linex are consistently worse than the standard GP baseline" but then concludes "This might be a case where a standard GP is preferred" - how do you reconcile this with your main contribution of demonstrating the superiority of your proposed models?

4. Why did you choose to report results only for Matèrn52 kernels, omitting other kernel results? How do you justify this selective reporting?

## Overall Recommendation

While the paper addresses a relevant problem in QE with a theoretically sound approach, the methodological flaws in claiming improvements without statistical significance tests, the contradictory claims about model performance, and the numerous formatting inconsistencies significantly undermine the paper's credibility. The contradiction in Section 4.3 particularly weakens the paper's main contribution, as it suggests that the proposed models may not actually outperform standard GPs in all scenarios.

The paper's contribution is not novel enough to overcome these significant methodological and presentation flaws. The main contribution (using probabilistic models for uncertainty estimation in QE) has been explored in previous work, and the paper fails to clearly demonstrate how their specific extensions provide meaningful improvements over existing approaches.

Given the stringent standards for CONLL and the paper's methodological weaknesses, I recommend rejection. The paper would benefit from substantial revisions, including statistical significance testing, resolving the contradictory claims, and fixing the numerous formatting inconsistencies.
