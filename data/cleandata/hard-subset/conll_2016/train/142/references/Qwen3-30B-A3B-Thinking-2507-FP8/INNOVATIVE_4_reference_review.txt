

# Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper addresses a critical gap in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) by shifting the focus from point estimates to uncertainty estimation. The authors argue that traditional QE models, which provide only point estimates, are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios where the cost of errors is asymmetric. The primary contributions are:

1. **Introducing NLPD as a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models** that captures uncertainty information missing in standard point estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson's r.
2. **Proposing specific GP extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs)** for better uncertainty modeling in QE, particularly for handling the non-Gaussian nature of quality scores.
3. **Demonstrating practical applications of uncertainty estimates** in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., post-editing where underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating).

## Strengths

1. **Fundamental shift in evaluation methodology**: The paper makes a compelling case that the field has been evaluating QE models incorrectly by focusing solely on point estimates. The demonstration that different models with similar point estimate performance can have vastly different uncertainty characteristics (captured by NLPD but not MAE) is a significant methodological contribution that could reshape how QE is evaluated.

2. **Practical relevance of uncertainty estimation**: The paper doesn't just propose a theoretical framework but demonstrates concrete applications where uncertainty information directly improves decision-making. The asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic) provide a clear, real-world justification for why uncertainty matters beyond academic interest.

3. **Novel application of established probabilistic concepts**: While GPs have been used in QE before, the paper's specific application of Warped GPs and Matèrn kernels for this domain is novel, and the authors provide strong theoretical justification for why these extensions are appropriate for the noisy, non-Gaussian nature of QE data.

4. **Clear articulation of a critical gap**: The paper effectively argues that the field has been overlooking a fundamental aspect of QE that is crucial for practical implementation. This insight alone would be valuable, but the paper goes further by providing concrete solutions.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with other probabilistic approaches**: The paper correctly states that most QE models provide only point estimates, but it doesn't thoroughly compare the proposed GP extensions with other probabilistic approaches (e.g., Bayesian neural networks or ensemble methods) that could also provide uncertainty estimates. This weakens the claim that GPs are the optimal solution.

2. **Lack of scalability discussion**: GPs are known to have computational limitations with larger datasets. The paper uses small datasets (858-2,828 instances) typical of QE, but doesn't address how the proposed methods would scale to larger, more realistic QE applications, which is a significant practical concern.

3. **Underdeveloped calibration analysis**: While the paper demonstrates that NLPD captures uncertainty information better than point estimate metrics, it doesn't provide a thorough calibration analysis to show how well the predicted uncertainty levels match actual prediction errors. This is a key requirement for uncertainty estimates to be useful in decision-making contexts.

4. **Limited state-of-the-art comparison**: The paper focuses on the novelty of uncertainty estimation rather than improving point estimate performance. While this is a valid research direction, the lack of comparison with the current state-of-the-art in point estimates (which is often the primary focus of QE) makes it harder to gauge the overall practical value.

## Questions to Authors

1. How do the computational costs of the proposed Warped GP models compare to standard GP models and to the state-of-the-art neural network-based QE models? This is crucial for practical adoption.

2. The paper mentions that the en-es dataset is smaller and may affect results. How would you expect the performance differences between model variants to change with larger datasets, and what implications does this have for real-world applications?

3. Could you provide more details on the calibration of the uncertainty estimates? Specifically, how do the predicted uncertainty levels correlate with actual prediction errors across different model variants?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper makes a strong case for the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE, which is a critical gap in the field. The proposal of NLPD as a more appropriate evaluation metric is particularly valuable and could significantly impact how QE models are evaluated in the future. While the experimental validation has some limitations, the core insight about the importance of uncertainty for practical decision-making is compelling and novel.

I recommend acceptance, as the paper introduces a significant new direction for QE research that goes beyond incremental improvements to point estimates. The focus on how uncertainty information can be practically applied to improve decision-making in real-world scenarios is exactly the kind of bold approach that the conference should encourage. The methodological contribution of NLPD as an evaluation metric alone is sufficient to warrant acceptance, even with some limitations in the experimental validation.

The paper's main contribution is not just in the specific model extensions proposed, but in shifting the paradigm of how we think about QE evaluation and application. This is the kind of conceptual leap that can guide the field for years to come.
