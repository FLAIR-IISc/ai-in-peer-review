

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a crucial gap in Quality Estimation (QE) for Machine Translation: the lack of uncertainty estimates in traditional point-estimate models. The authors argue that in practical applications (e.g., post-editing workflows), knowing the confidence in quality predictions is as important as the prediction itself. Their main contributions, as I see them, are:

1. **Demonstration that traditional QE evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling** - The authors provide empirical evidence that models with identical point-estimate performance can have vastly different uncertainty properties, which are not reflected in standard metrics but are captured by NLPD.

2. **Application of established GP techniques (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) to QE with clear justification** - The authors don't introduce new modeling techniques but thoughtfully adapt existing GP extensions to the QE context with appropriate justifications based on the nature of QE data.

3. **Practical demonstration of how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios** - The paper shows how uncertainty estimates can be used to make better decisions under different risk preferences (pessimistic vs. optimistic), providing a concrete application of their approach.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and clear practical relevance**: The paper excels at explaining why uncertainty matters in real-world QE applications. The post-editing scenario example is particularly compelling—showing how uncertainty estimates can prevent bad translations from reaching human translators, which directly impacts productivity and user experience. This is a significant contribution because it shifts the focus from purely improving point estimates to improving the reliability of those estimates, which is crucial for practical adoption.

2. **Compelling empirical evidence that traditional metrics are insufficient**: The paper provides clear, well-structured experiments showing that models with similar point-estimate performance (MAE, Pearson's r) can have dramatically different uncertainty properties (as measured by NLPD). For example, Table 1 demonstrates that warping functions significantly improve NLPD while only marginally improving MAE, proving that traditional metrics fail to capture uncertainty differences. This is a fundamental insight that should change how the field evaluates QE models.

3. **Effective demonstration of practical utility in asymmetric risk settings**: The paper's Section 4 is particularly strong, showing how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged to make better decisions under different risk preferences (e.g., "pessimistic" scenarios where you want to be very conservative about accepting translations, versus "optimistic" scenarios where you want to maximize throughput). The use of Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses (AL and linex) is well-explained and provides a concrete path for applying uncertainty estimates in production systems.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of technical contributions**: While the application to QE is valuable, the core modeling techniques (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) are not new to the GP literature. The paper doesn't introduce novel modeling approaches but rather applies existing techniques to a new domain. The novelty lies in the application and evaluation in the QE context, but this is not sufficiently emphasized as the primary contribution. The paper would benefit from more discussion of how these techniques specifically address the unique challenges of QE data (e.g., small dataset sizes, noisy quality scores) compared to other applications of GPs.

2. **Lack of comparison to alternative probabilistic models**: The paper focuses exclusively on GPs, but there are other probabilistic models that could provide uncertainty estimates (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, Bayesian linear models). The absence of such comparisons makes it difficult to assess whether GPs are truly the best approach for QE or if simpler probabilistic alternatives might suffice. A brief comparison to one or two alternative probabilistic models would strengthen the paper's claims.

3. **Unclear explanation of the pessimistic scenario results**: The paper reports that "the results for linex are consistently worse than the standard GP baseline" in the pessimistic scenario, but the explanation is not fully convincing. The authors suggest it might be because "warped models tend to have less variance," but this seems counterintuitive (less variance should be better for a pessimistic scenario). The discussion of this finding needs more careful analysis to clarify why the warped models underperform in this specific case.

4. **Insufficient discussion of computational limitations**: GPs are known to have O(n^3) computational complexity, which can be prohibitive for large-scale QE applications. The paper doesn't address this trade-off between uncertainty quality and computational cost, which is a significant practical concern for real-world deployment.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "the differences in uncertainty modelling are not captured by the usual point estimate metrics," but doesn't provide a theoretical explanation for why this is the case. Could you elaborate on the theoretical relationship between point-estimate metrics and uncertainty evaluation?

2. In Table 2, the log-based warped GP performs better than tanh-based for en-es in the optimistic scenario but worse for fr-en and en-de. Given that en-es is the smallest dataset, could you provide more insight into why the log warping works better for smaller datasets in this context?

3. The paper states that "the main reason behind [the improvement from warping] is that standard GPs distribute probability mass over negative values, while the warped models do not." However, the standard GP likelihood is Gaussian with support over the entire real line, but the authors use the median (not the mean) for point predictions. Could you clarify how the median prediction is affected by the Gaussian assumption and why this matters for the quality scores?

4. The paper mentions that "we believe this is evidence of overfitting" for the en-es dataset, but doesn't provide evidence of overfitting (e.g., comparison of training vs. test NLPD). Could you provide such evidence to strengthen this claim?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-written, clearly argued paper that makes a valuable contribution to the QE field by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation and demonstrating how it can be practically applied. The empirical evidence supporting the inadequacy of traditional metrics is strong and well-presented. While the technical contributions are not novel, the application to QE is timely and well-motivated.

The paper would be significantly stronger if it addressed the computational limitations of GPs for large-scale QE, compared to alternative probabilistic models, and provided a more thorough theoretical explanation for why traditional metrics fail to capture uncertainty differences. However, even with these limitations, the paper makes a compelling case for why uncertainty estimation should be a core part of QE evaluation and application.

**Recommendation: Accept** (with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly the computational discussion and comparison to alternative probabilistic models).
