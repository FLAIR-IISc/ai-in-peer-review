

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary
This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) in machine translation, arguing that uncertainty estimates are crucial for decision-making in practical QE applications. The authors propose extending standard GP models with Matèrn kernels and warped GPs, and advocate for using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more informative evaluation metric than traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r). They demonstrate applications in asymmetric risk scenarios.

## Main Contributions
1. Advocacy for NLPD as an evaluation metric that captures uncertainty information
2. Proposals of Matèrn kernels and warped GPs for improved uncertainty estimation in QE
3. Demonstration of how probabilistic models can be beneficial in asymmetric risk scenarios

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical justification for uncertainty in QE**: The paper correctly identifies a meaningful limitation of current QE practice (reliance on point estimates without uncertainty) and provides a compelling argument for why uncertainty estimation matters in real-world applications like post-editing workflows. This is a valid and important observation that aligns with broader trends in machine learning.

2. **Clear explanation of established GP extensions**: The paper provides a well-structured theoretical foundation for the proposed GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs), explaining their mathematical properties and rationale. This is valuable for the community, especially given the relative lack of probabilistic approaches in QE.

3. **Strong case for NLPD as an evaluation metric**: The paper makes a convincing argument that NLPD better captures uncertainty information than traditional metrics, and demonstrates that standard QE evaluation metrics miss important differences in uncertainty modeling. This is a valuable contribution that could improve evaluation practices in the QE community.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of convincing evidence for claimed improvements**: The paper claims that the proposed models are "more appropriate for the task" but provides only marginal improvements in NLPD (Table 1) without corresponding improvements in traditional QE metrics. The authors acknowledge the need for "more experiments" to validate their claims, which is a significant weakness for a paper claiming methodological advances.

2. **Inadequate comparison against state-of-the-art QE methods**: The paper only compares against "standard GPs" but fails to benchmark against established QE methods (like SVR or neural network approaches) that dominate the field. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine if the proposed methods are actually better than current practice.

3. **Unsubstantiated practical relevance**: The asymmetric risk scenarios (Section 4) are theoretically interesting but lack strong practical justification. The marginal improvements in Table 2 don't convincingly demonstrate that these scenarios are common or important in real-world QE applications. The paper doesn't provide evidence that these asymmetric scenarios are more relevant than standard symmetric evaluation.

4. **Overstated significance of NLPD**: While NLPD is a valid metric in probabilistic modeling, the paper overstates its importance for QE applications. The field primarily uses MAE and Pearson's r for evaluation, and the paper doesn't demonstrate that NLPD better predicts real-world QE performance or decision-making outcomes.

5. **Insufficient validation on small datasets**: The paper acknowledges that for the smallest dataset (en-es), the NLL metric appears unreliable due to overfitting. However, it doesn't provide adequate validation for the proposed methods on the larger datasets where the improvements are marginal at best.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide comparative results against at least one strong baseline QE method (e.g., SVR or neural network approaches) to demonstrate that your proposed GP extensions actually outperform current practice?

2. How do you justify the practical importance of asymmetric risk scenarios in QE applications, given that most QE evaluations in the literature use symmetric metrics?

3. The paper notes that "more experiments are needed to check if this is the case" regarding kernel selection. Could you clarify how you would conduct these additional experiments to validate the claimed improvements?

## Overall Recommendation

While the paper identifies an important limitation in current QE practice and provides a theoretically sound exploration of GP extensions, the evidence supporting the claimed improvements is insufficient. The marginal gains in NLPD without corresponding improvements in standard QE metrics, combined with the lack of comparison against established baselines, significantly weakens the contribution. The paper would be substantially stronger if it provided more convincing empirical evidence that these methods offer practical advantages over current state-of-the-art QE approaches.

The paper's main contribution lies in advocating for NLPD as an evaluation metric, but the proposed methodological improvements lack sufficient validation. I recommend rejection on grounds of insufficient evidence for the primary claims of the paper. The authors would need to provide more extensive validation against established baselines and demonstrate clear practical benefits before this work could be considered a significant contribution to the field.
