

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the role of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point estimate approaches are insufficient for decision-making in practical applications. The authors propose extending Gaussian Process (GP) models for QE with Matérn kernels and warped GPs, and introduce the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric that captures uncertainty information.

**Main Contributions (in order of strength):**

1. **Validation of NLPD as a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models:** The paper demonstrates that NLPD captures differences in uncertainty modeling that traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to detect, providing a more comprehensive evaluation framework.

2. **Systematic evaluation of GP extensions for QE:** The authors rigorously compare Matérn kernels versus exponentiated quadratic kernels, and parametric warping (with tanh functions) versus log warping, showing clear improvements in uncertainty modeling for most datasets.

3. **Demonstration of practical utility in asymmetric risk scenarios:** The paper shows how probabilistic models with proper uncertainty estimates can be effectively adapted to different real-world decision-making contexts (pessimistic and optimistic scenarios) without requiring model retraining.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a critical gap in QE practice:** The paper effectively argues that point estimates alone are insufficient for practical QE applications, particularly in decision-making contexts like post-editing workflows. The post-editing and gisting scenarios provide compelling, concrete motivations for considering uncertainty in QE, making a strong case for the paper's relevance.

2. **Strong validation of NLPD as the appropriate evaluation metric:** The experimental results across three language pairs consistently show that NLPD reveals meaningful differences between models that MAE and Pearson's r fail to capture. This is a significant contribution to the QE evaluation methodology, as it provides a principled way to assess uncertainty quality.

3. **Practical demonstration of probabilistic models' utility:** The asymmetric risk scenarios section provides a compelling real-world application of the authors' approach. The demonstration that the same probabilistic model can be adapted to different decision contexts (pessimistic vs. optimistic) without retraining represents a valuable practical contribution to the field.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited analysis of model complexity trade-offs:** While the paper shows that warped GPs with parametric warping generally outperform other approaches, it doesn't thoroughly analyze the trade-offs between model complexity (e.g., number of tanh terms) and performance. The authors note that "more experiments are needed" but don't provide sufficient analysis of this critical aspect.

2. **Inconsistent results for the smallest dataset (en-es):** The paper acknowledges that the en-es dataset (858 sentences) behaves differently from larger datasets (fr-en: 2,525; en-de: 2,828), attributing this to overfitting. However, the authors don't provide a deeper analysis of why the smallest dataset behaves differently, which limits the generalizability of their findings. This is particularly concerning since the paper claims to explore "prediction uncertainty" as a general approach.

3. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art point estimate QE models:** The paper compares different GP variants but doesn't benchmark against current state-of-the-art point estimate QE models (e.g., deep learning approaches). This omission makes it difficult to assess whether the uncertainty improvements come at the cost of worse point estimates, which is crucial for practical applications.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the gains from using a Matèrn kernel over EQ are less conclusive" but doesn't provide statistical significance tests for these differences. Could the authors provide statistical significance tests for the differences observed between kernel types across datasets?

2. For the asymmetric risk scenarios, the paper reports inconsistent results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario. The authors suggest this might be due to warped models having less variance. Could they elaborate on why the linex loss behaves differently than the AL loss in this context, and whether they believe this is a fundamental limitation of the warped GP approach?

3. The paper mentions that "we also report results using the f(y) = log(y) warping function" and "we tried w ∈ {1/9, 1/7, 1/5, 5, 7, 9} for the AL loss." Could the authors provide a brief summary of these additional experiments and explain why they were omitted from the main results?

## Conclusion

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the QE field by demonstrating the importance of uncertainty estimation and providing a rigorous evaluation framework using NLPD. The practical demonstration of how probabilistic models can be adapted to different decision contexts is particularly strong. However, the inconsistent results with the smallest dataset and lack of comparison with state-of-the-art point estimate models represent significant limitations that should be addressed. With these issues clarified, the paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
