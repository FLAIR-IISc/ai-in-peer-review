

# Peer Review: "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper explores the use of Gaussian Process (GP) models for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) with a specific focus on uncertainty estimation. The authors argue that traditional QE models, which provide only point estimates, are insufficient for practical applications where uncertainty information is crucial for decision-making (e.g., determining whether to post-edit a segment or translate from scratch). The paper proposes several extensions to standard GP models: (1) using Matérn kernels instead of the common exponentiated quadratic kernel to better handle noisy data, and (2) implementing Warped Gaussian Processes to handle the strictly positive nature of quality scores. The authors advocate for using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as an evaluation metric that captures uncertainty information, rather than relying solely on traditional point estimate metrics like MAE or Pearson's r. They further demonstrate how these probabilistic models can be applied in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic settings).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)

1. **Advocacy for NLPD as a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models**: The paper compellingly demonstrates that NLPD captures uncertainty information that traditional metrics miss, which is critical for practical applications where uncertainty matters.

2. **Practical model extensions for QE**: The authors successfully demonstrate that Matérn kernels and Warped GPs provide better uncertainty estimates for QE than standard GPs, with Warped GPs (particularly with tanh-based warping) showing consistent improvements across multiple datasets.

3. **Application of probabilistic models to asymmetric risk scenarios**: The paper provides a clear, practical demonstration of how predictive distributions can be leveraged in real-world decision-making contexts with asymmetric risk profiles, showing that the proposed models outperform standard approaches in these scenarios.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a critical gap in QE evaluation**: The paper correctly identifies that the standard metrics used in QE (MAE, Pearson's r) don't capture uncertainty information, which is essential for practical applications. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it provides a framework for evaluating probabilistic QE models that aligns with their intended use cases.

2. **Strong theoretical foundation with practical relevance**: The authors build on solid GP theory (Rasmussen and Williams, 2006) and make well-justified extensions (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) that address specific challenges in QE data (noisy, strictly positive response variables). The connection to asymmetric risk scenarios demonstrates clear practical relevance beyond theoretical interest.

3. **Thorough empirical validation**: The experiments across three language pairs (en-es, fr-en, en-de) with multiple metrics and model variants provide convincing evidence for the authors' claims. The results consistently show that NLPD captures differences in uncertainty modeling that traditional metrics miss.

4. **Clear demonstration of practical utility**: The paper effectively illustrates how uncertainty estimates can inform real-world decisions (e.g., post-editing vs. translating from scratch) and shows how the proposed models perform better in asymmetric risk scenarios. This bridges the gap between theoretical model improvements and practical application.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art QE models**: The paper exclusively focuses on GP-based models but doesn't compare them with the current state-of-the-art neural network-based QE approaches (e.g., those using CNNs, RNNs, or Transformers). Given that neural networks have largely replaced kernel methods in recent QE work (as evidenced by WMT QE shared task results), this omission significantly weakens the paper's contribution. The authors should address whether their probabilistic approaches can be incorporated into or compared with these more modern architectures.

2. **Inadequate justification for NLPD as the primary evaluation metric**: While the paper argues that NLPD is better for capturing uncertainty, it doesn't provide sufficient theoretical justification for why NLPD should be preferred over other uncertainty-aware metrics (e.g., Brier score, ECE). More importantly, the paper doesn't address how NLPD might be affected by model misspecification, particularly for the Warped GP models where the likelihood might not match the true data distribution.

3. **Insufficient exploration of computational limitations**: GPs are known for their O(n³) computational complexity, which makes them impractical for large-scale applications. The paper doesn't discuss how the proposed extensions (especially Warped GPs with tanh-based warping) affect computational efficiency. For real-world QE systems, scalability is a critical concern that the authors should address.

4. **Limited analysis of failure cases and dataset-specific limitations**: The paper notes that the en-es dataset (the smallest) behaves differently from the others, but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why this occurs or how these limitations might affect generalizability. Understanding why smaller datasets show different behavior would be valuable for future work.

5. **Overlooking recent developments in probabilistic deep learning**: The paper focuses solely on GP-based approaches but ignores recent advances in uncertainty estimation for deep learning models (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, deep ensembles, Monte Carlo dropout), which have shown promise for handling uncertainty in NLP tasks. This limits the paper's relevance to current trends in the field.

## Questions to Authors

1. Given that neural network-based approaches have become dominant in recent QE work (e.g., WMT 2020-2023 shared tasks), how would you expect your GP-based approaches to compare with these state-of-the-art models in terms of both point estimate performance and uncertainty estimation? Have you considered hybrid approaches that combine neural features with GP uncertainty modeling?

2. The paper states that Warped GPs with tanh-based warping outperformed log warping for most datasets. What is the theoretical explanation for this phenomenon, given that log is a natural transformation for positive variables? Is this specific to the QE task or would it generalize to other text regression tasks?

3. The paper mentions that Warped GPs with tanh-based warping performed better for fr-en and en-de (larger datasets) but log warping was better for en-es (smaller dataset). Could this be related to the different data distributions across language pairs, or is it primarily a function of dataset size? How would you expect these results to scale to even larger datasets?

4. Given the computational limitations of GPs, what strategies would you recommend for scaling these uncertainty-aware QE models to industrial settings with large-scale translation pipelines (e.g., millions of segments)?

5. The paper uses the same feature set (17 features from QuEst) across all language pairs. How would you expect the performance of your probabilistic models to change if you used more language-specific features or neural embeddings, which are commonly used in state-of-the-art QE systems?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper makes a valuable contribution by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE and providing a strong case for using NLPD as an evaluation metric. The proposed model extensions (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) are well-motivated and show consistent improvements in uncertainty estimation across multiple datasets. However, the lack of comparison with state-of-the-art neural network-based QE models significantly limits the paper's impact and relevance to current research trends.

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the understanding that the authors would need to address the weaknesses mentioned above, particularly regarding comparison with modern QE approaches and scalability considerations. The paper has strong theoretical foundations and practical relevance, but its contribution would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating how these ideas can be integrated with or compared to contemporary QE systems.
