

# Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary
This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) with Matérn kernels and warped GPs to model prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that traditional QE models, which provide only point estimates, are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios. They propose using NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) as an evaluation metric that captures uncertainty information, and demonstrate how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. The paper advocates for using NLPD as a more informative evaluation metric than traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) for probabilistic QE models, which is a significant methodological contribution.
2. The paper demonstrates a practical application of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios, showing how different loss functions can be used based on application-specific needs.
3. The paper proposes the use of Matérn kernels and warped GPs for uncertainty modeling in QE, which is a principled extension of existing GP literature.

## Strengths

1. **Correct Identification of Limitations in Current QE Practice**: The paper correctly identifies that point estimates alone are insufficient for real-world QE applications where uncertainty matters for decision-making. The post-editing scenario example effectively illustrates why uncertainty estimates are practically valuable.

2. **Strong Argument for NLPD as an Evaluation Metric**: The paper makes a compelling case that NLPD captures uncertainty information that point estimate metrics cannot. The demonstration that MAE and Pearson's r fail to distinguish between models with different uncertainty profiles is a significant contribution to the field.

3. **Practical Application in Asymmetric Risk Scenarios**: The paper provides a clear and useful demonstration of how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in different application contexts (pessimistic vs. optimistic scenarios), which is a strong practical contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines**: This is the paper's most significant weakness. The authors claim that GPs are the "state-of-the-art for regression" in QE, but fail to provide any comparison with the current state-of-the-art QE models (e.g., the strong baselines from WMT QE shared tasks). Without such comparisons, it's impossible to assess whether the proposed methods actually improve upon current practice in terms of point estimate quality, which is the primary goal of QE. The paper only compares different GP variants, not GP vs. established non-probabilistic methods.

2. **Inadequate Validation of Uncertainty Calibration**: While the paper discusses uncertainty, it doesn't provide evidence that the proposed models produce well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors claim "good uncertainty estimates can be acquired from well-calibrated probability distributions," but the paper doesn't evaluate calibration (e.g., using reliability diagrams or proper scoring rules beyond NLPD). Without calibration assessment, it's impossible to know if the uncertainty estimates are actually useful in practice.

3. **Insufficient Evidence of Practical Benefit**: The paper shows modest improvements in NLPD for some configurations, but fails to demonstrate that these improvements translate to meaningful practical benefits. For example, the paper doesn't show how the uncertainty estimates would actually improve post-editing productivity or reduce translation costs in real-world settings.

4. **Overstated Claims about General Applicability**: The paper claims that "the methods we explore in this paper can be applied to any text regression task," but provides no evidence beyond QE. The paper doesn't demonstrate the approach on other text regression tasks, making this claim unsubstantiated.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that GPs are the state-of-the-art for regression in QE, but doesn't compare against current QE baselines (e.g., the WMT QE shared task winners). Can you provide a comparison with these established baselines?

2. The paper uses NLPD as the main evaluation metric but doesn't assess calibration of the predictive distributions. How do you ensure that the uncertainty estimates are well-calibrated (e.g., 95% prediction intervals cover the true value 95% of the time)?

3. The paper claims that "the differences in uncertainty modelling are not captured by the usual point estimate metrics," but doesn't demonstrate how these differences translate to practical benefits in QE applications. Can you provide evidence that the improved uncertainty estimates lead to better decision-making in real-world scenarios?

## Overall Assessment

While the paper correctly identifies a real limitation in current QE practice and proposes a theoretically sound approach to addressing it, it falls short of demonstrating that the proposed methods actually improve upon current practice. The lack of comparison to established baselines and the absence of evidence for well-calibrated uncertainty estimates are significant weaknesses. The paper reads more like a theoretical exploration of GP extensions rather than a practical contribution to QE.

As a reviewer who prefers established methods and requires rigorous validation against baselines, I find the paper's claims unsupported by sufficient evidence. The paper's main contribution—using NLPD as an evaluation metric—is valuable, but the proposed model extensions lack sufficient validation to justify their use over existing methods.

I recommend rejection unless the authors can provide:
1. Direct comparisons with state-of-the-art QE baselines
2. Calibration assessments of the uncertainty estimates
3. Evidence that the uncertainty improvements translate to practical benefits in QE applications

The paper's theoretical ideas are interesting, but without rigorous validation against established baselines and evidence of practical utility, it fails to meet the standards of CoNLL.
