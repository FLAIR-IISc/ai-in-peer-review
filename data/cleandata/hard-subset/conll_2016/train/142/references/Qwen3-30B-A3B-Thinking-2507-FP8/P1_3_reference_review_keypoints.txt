- Paper argues uncertainty estimation is essential for practical QE, proposing Gaussian Processes (GPs) with Matérn kernels and warped GPs, and evaluates them using NLPD and asymmetric decision scenarios.  
- Main contribution: establishes Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a superior metric for QE that captures uncertainty differences missed by MAE and Pearson’s r.  
- Main contribution: proposes Matérn kernels to handle noisy QE data and warped GPs to model strictly positive quality scores.  
- Main contribution: demonstrates practical use of predictive distributions with asymmetric losses (AL and linex) for improved decision-making in translation workflows.  
- Strength: strong practical motivation with clear real-world scenarios (post-editing, gisting) showing why uncertainty matters.  
- Strength: rigorous evaluation showing NLPD distinguishes uncertainty models that point-estimate metrics cannot.  
- Strength: novel application of asymmetric loss functions to QE, bridging econometric techniques and NLP.  
- Weakness: proposed model extensions perform inconsistently across datasets (warping and Matérn gains vary), with inadequate explanation or guidance.  
- Weakness: lacks comparisons to other probabilistic approaches (e.g., Bayesian neural nets, Bayesian linear regression) to justify GPs as the best choice.  
- Weakness: superficial analysis of asymmetric scenarios—insufficient explanation for warped GPs’ poor performance in pessimistic settings.  
- Question: why does log-based vs. tanh-based warping depend on dataset size, and can warping be auto-selected based on dataset characteristics?  
- Question: why do Matérn kernels help for fr-en but not other datasets—what dataset properties drive this?  
- Question: why do warped GPs underperform in pessimistic scenarios despite lower variance—limitation of warping or fixable by model adjustments?  
- Additional comment: paper is well-written with appropriate experiments and valuable linkage to asymmetric loss functions, but inconsistent results and missing probabilistic baselines limit impact.
