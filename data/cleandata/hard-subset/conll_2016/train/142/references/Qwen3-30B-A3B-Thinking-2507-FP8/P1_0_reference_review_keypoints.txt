- Paper argues point-estimate QE is insufficient and proposes Gaussian Processes (GPs) with Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to model prediction uncertainty.

- Main contribution: advocating NLPD as a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models that captures uncertainty differences missed by MAE/Pearson.

- Demonstrates practical utility of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk decision scenarios (e.g., post-editing workflows).

- Introduces and validates Matérn kernels and Warped GPs as practical GP extensions for QE with theoretical motivation.

- Strength: strong theoretical motivation and concrete real-world examples motivating uncertainty-aware QE.

- Strength: novel evaluation focus with empirical validation showing NLPD penalizes overconfident models and rewards calibrated uncertainty.

- Strength: clear connection between uncertainty modeling and practical decision-making in translation workflows.

- Strength: well-designed experiments across three language pairs with 10-fold cross-validation and multiple evaluation metrics.

- Strength: provides implementable model extensions with clear explanations for adoption by practitioners.

- Weakness: limited experimental scope—only three language pairs and a standard 17-feature set, raising generalizability concerns.

- Weakness: no comparison to other probabilistic approaches (e.g., Bayesian neural networks), weakening the claim that GPs are uniquely appropriate.

- Weakness: inconsistent results across datasets, especially the small en-es set, with insufficient analysis of causes.

- Weakness: missing discussion of computational costs and practical runtime/scale implications for real-world adoption.

- Weakness: theoretical justification for choosing Matérn kernels and Warped GPs could be stronger and more closely tied to QE data properties.

- Question: how would results change with richer feature sets (e.g., top WMT QE features), and would uncertainty gains persist?

- Question: why do warped GPs perform worse on linex loss in pessimistic scenarios—is this fundamental or implementation-related?

- Question: how should the number of tanh terms (I) in the warping function be chosen in practice to avoid overfitting?

- Question: can the authors provide deeper analysis of the en-es dataset (feature/score distributions) to explain inconsistent results?

- Additional comment: paper is well-written and compelling but would benefit from discussion of computational costs and broader experimental evaluation for generalizability.
