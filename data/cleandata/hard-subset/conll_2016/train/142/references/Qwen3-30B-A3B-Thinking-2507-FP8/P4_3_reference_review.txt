

# Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary and Contributions
This paper explores the use of uncertainty estimation in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) using Gaussian Processes (GPs). The authors argue that traditional point estimate QE models are insufficient for practical decision-making scenarios, and propose probabilistic models that provide uncertainty estimates. They introduce the use of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more informative evaluation metric than traditional point estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson's r. The authors also demonstrate how these uncertainty estimates can be applied to asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic settings).

Main contributions as assessed:
1. Demonstrating that NLPD is a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models compared to standard point estimate metrics, as it captures uncertainty information.
2. Proposing and empirically validating the use of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs for better uncertainty modeling in QE tasks.
3. Showing how predictive distributions from these models can be effectively utilized in asymmetric risk scenarios, which are common in practical QE applications.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation and practical relevance**: The paper provides a compelling argument for why uncertainty estimation matters in QE applications, with clear examples of how it improves decision-making in post-editing workflows. The authors effectively demonstrate how uncertainty estimates enable more informed filtering decisions (e.g., only forwarding segments with high quality and low uncertainty), which is a significant practical contribution to the field.

2. **Thorough evaluation of uncertainty metrics**: The paper makes a strong technical case for using NLPD as an evaluation metric instead of traditional point estimate metrics. The experiments across three language pairs (en-es, fr-en, en-de) convincingly show that standard metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, while NLPD reliably reflects these differences. The analysis of why NLPD performs better (penalizing overconfident models more than underconfident ones) is technically sound.

3. **Demonstrated utility in asymmetric risk scenarios**: The paper successfully demonstrates how probabilistic models with uncertainty estimates can be applied to practical asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic settings), showing that Warped GPs with tanh-based warping functions outperform standard GPs in optimistic scenarios. The theoretical justification for using Bayes risk estimators with asymmetric losses is well-founded and the experimental results provide clear evidence of the practical benefits.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of technical contributions**: While the application to QE is novel, the specific GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) are well-established in the GP literature. The paper doesn't sufficiently establish how these specific extensions are novel for QE beyond stating they're "more appropriate for the task." The authors don't provide a comparative analysis showing why these specific extensions are better than others for QE, which weakens the contribution claim.

2. **Insufficient hyperparameter analysis**: The paper states they use "up to 3 terms" for the warping function based on Snelson et al. (2004), but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of how varying this parameter affects performance. The claim that "using the parametric tanh function with 3 terms performs better" lacks experimental validation of why 3 terms specifically is optimal, which weakens the contribution of the Warped GP experiments.

3. **Lack of computational complexity analysis**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of the proposed models, which is crucial for practical deployment. While the authors mention that kernel methods are scalable for QE due to small datasets, they don't discuss whether the proposed extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) significantly increase computational requirements compared to standard GPs. This is a significant omission for a paper claiming practical relevance.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that "differences in uncertainty modelling are not captured by the usual point estimate metrics," but only reports results for MAE and Pearson's r. Could the authors provide additional point estimate metrics (e.g., RMSE) to further support this claim?

2. For the en-es dataset (the smallest dataset), the log warping function outperforms the tanh-based warping function. Could the authors provide more detailed analysis explaining why this pattern emerges specifically for smaller datasets?

3. The paper demonstrates the utility of probabilistic models in asymmetric risk scenarios, but doesn't compare with non-probabilistic approaches adapted to handle asymmetric losses. Could the authors comment on whether traditional point estimate models could be adapted to handle asymmetric losses through post-processing, and how this would compare to the probabilistic approach?

## Overall Assessment

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the QE field by demonstrating the importance of uncertainty estimation and providing a clear framework for evaluating probabilistic models using NLPD. The practical demonstration of how uncertainty estimates can be applied to asymmetric risk scenarios is particularly strong. However, the technical novelty of the specific GP extensions is limited, and the paper would be significantly strengthened by addressing the computational complexity of the proposed models and providing more thorough hyperparameter analysis. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a compelling case for the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE, but the novelty of the specific technical contributions is somewhat limited.
