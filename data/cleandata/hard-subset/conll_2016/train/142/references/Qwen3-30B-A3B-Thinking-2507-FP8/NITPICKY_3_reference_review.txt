

# Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

## Summary and Contributions
The paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for uncertainty estimation in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that probabilistic models like GPs provide more valuable information for decision-making than point estimates. The main contributions I identify are:
1. Demonstrating that uncertainty estimation metrics (NLPD) capture differences in model behavior that standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) miss
2. Proposing and evaluating warping functions and Matérn kernels for improving GP-based QE models
3. Showing how predictive distributions can be used in asymmetric loss scenarios for practical QE applications

## Strengths

1. **Novel application of uncertainty metrics**: The paper correctly identifies that NLPD is a more informative metric than traditional point-estimate metrics for evaluating uncertainty-aware models, which is an important contribution to the QE community.

2. **Practical relevance**: The discussion of asymmetric loss scenarios (pessimistic/optimistic settings) provides concrete examples of how uncertainty estimates can improve real-world QE applications, particularly in post-editing and gisting scenarios.

3. **Clear methodology for uncertainty modeling**: The paper provides a thorough explanation of warping functions and Matérn kernels for GP-based QE, with sufficient technical detail to understand the modifications to standard GP approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent terminology and formatting**: The paper uses inconsistent terminology (e.g., "Matèrn" vs. "Matérn" with an accent), inconsistent citation styles ("Beck et al.,? 2014" vs "Beck et al., 2014"), and inconsistent mathematical notation (e.g., "kM32" vs "kM52"). These are not just minor typographical errors but represent a lack of attention to detail that undermines the paper's credibility.

2. **Insufficient justification for model selection**: The claim that "other common models used in QE can only provide point estimates" is inaccurate. The paper cites Beck et al. (2014b), which is a GP-based model, and fails to acknowledge that SVR with bootstrapping can provide uncertainty estimates (though with limitations). The paper doesn't adequately justify why they excluded other probabilistic models like Bayesian neural networks.

3. **Lack of statistical validation**: The paper claims "we believe this is evidence of overfitting" for the en-es dataset but provides no statistical evidence (e.g., no significance testing for differences in NLL/NLPD across models). The statement "gains from using a Matèrn kernel over EQ are less conclusive" is made without any statistical analysis to support this claim.

4. **Inconsistent reporting of experimental details**: The paper mentions using "a subset of 2,828 instances" for en-de but doesn't explain why they didn't use the full dataset. The reference to "w = −0.75 in this case resulted in loss values on the order of 107" is concerning but not properly addressed or explained.

5. **Incomplete treatment of uncertainty metrics**: The paper states "For the fr-en and en-de datasets, NLL and NLPD follow similar trends. This means that we can trust NLL as a measure of uncertainty for these datasets." This is a weak argument - correlation between two metrics doesn't imply that one is a reliable measure of uncertainty. The paper fails to provide any theoretical justification for this claim.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims "we believe this is evidence of overfitting" for the en-es dataset but doesn't provide any statistical evidence. Could you provide a statistical test showing that the NLL/NLPD discrepancy in en-es is significant?

2. The paper mentions "Using w = −0.75 in this case resulted in loss values on the order of 107" but doesn't explain why this happened or how the authors handled it. Could you clarify this issue and explain why the loss values became so large?

3. The paper states "the gains from using a Matèrn kernel over EQ are less conclusive" but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these differences. Could you provide p-values or confidence intervals for the reported differences?

4. The paper claims "other common models used in QE can only provide point estimates" but cites Beck et al. (2014b) which is a GP-based model. Could you clarify which specific models you're excluding and why they're not suitable for uncertainty estimation?

This paper presents a valuable discussion on uncertainty in QE, but the numerous technical inconsistencies and lack of statistical rigor in the evaluation undermine its contribution. The minor methodological flaws are significant enough to warrant rejection, particularly given the paper's focus on precision and detail. While the core idea of using uncertainty metrics for QE is sound, the execution fails to meet the standards expected for CONLL.
