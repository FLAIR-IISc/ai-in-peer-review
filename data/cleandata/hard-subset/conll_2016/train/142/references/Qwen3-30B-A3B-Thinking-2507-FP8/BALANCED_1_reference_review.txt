

# Review: "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper investigates the importance of uncertainty estimation in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture important aspects of predictive uncertainty. The authors propose using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models. They extend Gaussian Process (GP) models for QE with two key modifications: (1) Warped GPs with parametric tanh-based warping functions instead of log transformation, and (2) Matérn kernels (3/2 and 5/2) instead of the standard exponentiated quadratic kernel. Finally, they demonstrate how these probabilistic models can be beneficial in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic settings).

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. **NLPD as a superior evaluation metric for uncertainty-aware QE models** - The paper provides compelling evidence that NLPD captures uncertainty differences that traditional metrics miss.
2. **Practical demonstration of uncertainty utilization** - The asymmetric risk scenarios show how predictive distributions can be leveraged for better decision-making in real-world applications.
3. **Model extensions for GPs in QE** - The paper provides a clear rationale for using Matérn kernels and parametric warping functions in QE contexts.

## Strengths
1. **Strong practical motivation**: The paper effectively demonstrates why uncertainty matters in QE applications through concrete examples (post-editing and gisting scenarios), making a compelling case for the importance of this research direction. This is a significant contribution to the field as it bridges theoretical uncertainty modeling with practical decision-making.

2. **Compelling argument for NLPD**: The experiments clearly show that NLPD captures differences in uncertainty modeling that MAE and Pearson's r fail to detect. The authors provide a clear explanation of why this happens (overconfident vs. underconfident models), which is a valuable contribution to evaluation practices in QE.

3. **Practical demonstration of uncertainty usefulness**: The asymmetric loss experiments effectively show how the same probabilistic model can serve different application needs without retraining. This is particularly valuable as it demonstrates a practical workflow for incorporating uncertainty into real-world QE systems.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited experimental scope**: The paper evaluates on only three language pairs, with one dataset (en-es) being significantly smaller than the others. The inconsistent results on the smallest dataset undermine the generalizability of the findings. The authors don't provide sufficient evidence that their proposed methods work across diverse QE scenarios.

2. **Lack of comparison with alternative probabilistic models**: The paper exclusively focuses on GP extensions but doesn't compare with other probabilistic approaches that could provide uncertainty estimates (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, Bayesian linear regression). This limits the significance of their claims about the superiority of their GP extensions.

3. **Insufficient analysis of warping function performance**: While the paper shows that tanh-based warping performs better than log warping in most cases, it doesn't deeply analyze why this is the case or how the warping complexity affects performance. The inconsistent behavior on the en-es dataset isn't adequately explained.

## Questions to Authors
1. The en-es dataset is notably smaller than the others (858 vs. 2,525 and 2,828 instances). How would the authors expect their approach to scale to even smaller datasets, and what modifications might be needed for such scenarios?

2. The paper claims their methods can be applied to "any text regression task where modeling predictive uncertainty is useful." Could the authors provide specific examples beyond QE where their approach would be particularly advantageous compared to other uncertainty modeling techniques?

3. The paper states that "differences in uncertainty modeling are by and large not captured by point estimate metrics." However, Table 1 shows MAE does improve with warped GPs. Could the authors clarify exactly which differences are captured by NLPD but not by MAE, and why this matters for practical applications?

## Overall Assessment
This paper makes a valuable contribution to the QE literature by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation and demonstrating a practical way to evaluate and utilize it. The core ideas are sound and well-motivated, and the paper provides a clear roadmap for incorporating uncertainty into QE systems. However, the limited experimental scope and lack of comparison with alternative probabilistic models weaken the significance of the claims. With additional experiments on more datasets, comparisons with other probabilistic models, and deeper analysis of the warping function behavior, this work could become a strong contribution to the field.

Decision: **Weak Accept** (with the understanding that the authors would need to address the weaknesses in a revision). The paper's core ideas are important, but the current evidence base is insufficient to fully support the claims about the generality and superiority of the proposed approaches.
