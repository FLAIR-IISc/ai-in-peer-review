

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with Warped GP extensions and Matérn kernels for uncertainty estimation in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors claim three main contributions: (1) introducing NLPD as a superior evaluation metric for assessing uncertainty in QE models, (2) demonstrating that Warped GPs with tanh-based warping functions improve uncertainty estimation compared to standard GPs, and (3) showing how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios.

## Strengths

1. **Theoretical Justification for Uncertainty Estimation**: The paper correctly identifies the practical importance of uncertainty estimation in QE for real-world decision-making scenarios (e.g., post-editing workflows). The authors effectively argue that point estimates alone are insufficient for many practical applications, which is a valid and important point.

2. **Appropriate Evaluation Metric**: The authors correctly identify that NLPD is a more suitable metric for evaluating uncertainty-aware models than traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r). This is a well-established practice in the GP literature, and the paper properly situates its use within this context.

3. **Clear Demonstration of Asymmetric Risk Applications**: The paper provides a theoretically sound demonstration of how uncertainty estimates can be utilized in asymmetric loss scenarios (pessimistic/optimistic settings), showing how different loss functions can be applied to the same predictive distribution without retraining.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Rigorous Baseline Comparison**: The paper's most significant weakness is the failure to demonstrate meaningful improvements over established, strong baselines. The authors compare their methods only to standard GPs (with EQ kernel) and don't show results against state-of-the-art QE systems that use SVR, neural networks, or ensemble methods. The marginal improvements shown (e.g., in Table 1) are not convincingly larger than the noise in the evaluation metrics, especially given the small dataset sizes (particularly en-es with only 858 instances). Without comparison to the strong baselines used in recent WMT QE shared tasks, the paper fails to establish that these "improvements" are meaningful.

2. **Insufficient Validation of Novel Contributions**: The authors propose Warped GPs with tanh-based warping as superior to log warping and standard GPs, but provide no statistical significance testing for these claims. The results show inconsistent improvements across datasets (e.g., log warping performs better on en-es while tanh performs better on larger datasets), suggesting that the improvements may be dataset-specific rather than generalizable. The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that these methodological changes actually lead to more reliable uncertainty estimates in practical applications.

3. **Misleading Claims About Methodological Superiority**: The paper makes strong claims about the superiority of their proposed methods without adequate justification. For example, the claim that "Warped GPs provide an easy and elegant way to model response variables with non-Gaussian behaviour" is overstated—this is a standard technique in the GP literature, not a novel contribution. The paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that the proposed extensions (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) provide substantial benefits over well-established GP implementations used in previous QE work.

4. **Inadequate Discussion of Practical Limitations**: The paper fails to address critical practical limitations of using GPs for QE. GPs are computationally expensive (O(n³)), which makes them impractical for large-scale QE applications. The paper mentions that "scalability issues that arise in kernel methods do not tend to affect QE in practice since the datasets are usually small," but this is misleading—recent QE systems often use large datasets (e.g., millions of sentences), and the paper's datasets are all small (max 2,828 instances). Without addressing this scalability issue, the paper's proposed methods have limited practical relevance.

5. **Lack of Real-World Impact Demonstration**: The paper demonstrates theoretical benefits of uncertainty estimation in asymmetric loss scenarios but fails to show how these improvements translate to practical gains in real-world QE applications. The paper doesn't provide evidence that using these uncertainty estimates would actually improve translation productivity, reduce post-editing costs, or enhance user experience in actual translation workflows.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide a direct comparison of your proposed Warped GP approach against state-of-the-art QE systems (e.g., those from the WMT QE shared tasks) that use SVR, neural networks, or ensemble methods, rather than just comparing to standard GPs?

2. Given that the improvements shown are marginal and inconsistent across datasets, could you provide statistical significance testing to demonstrate that these differences are meaningful and not due to random variation in the small datasets used?

3. How would your proposed methods scale to larger QE datasets (e.g., with hundreds of thousands of instances), and what computational resources would be required for training and inference?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper addresses an important problem (uncertainty estimation in QE) but fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed methods offer meaningful improvements over established approaches. The marginal improvements shown are not convincingly validated against strong baselines, and the paper doesn't adequately address practical limitations of GP-based approaches for real-world QE applications. While the theoretical discussion of uncertainty estimation is sound, the lack of rigorous validation against established methods prevents this work from making a significant contribution to the field.

I recommend rejection due to insufficient evidence of novelty and practical significance. The paper would need substantial additional experiments against strong baselines, statistical validation of results, and discussion of practical limitations to be acceptable for publication.
