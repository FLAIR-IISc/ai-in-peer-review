This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on modeling prediction uncertainty rather than simply providing point estimates. The primary contribution is an investigation of different GP extensions – Matèrn kernels and warped GPs – and their impact on uncertainty quantification, assessed using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric. A secondary, applied contribution is demonstrating how these uncertainty estimates can be leveraged within asymmetric risk scenarios, using both the Absolute Loss and Linex loss functions. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Focus on Uncertainty is Valuable:** The paper addresses a practical gap in QE – the need for reliable uncertainty estimates for real-world deployment. While point estimates are useful for ranking, decision-making *requires* knowing how confident the model is in those rankings. This is a worthwhile research direction.
2. **Sound Methodological Foundation:** The use of GPs is a reasonable choice given their ability to model uncertainty natively. The exploration of Matèrn kernels and warped GPs, drawing from the established GP literature, demonstrates a familiarity with relevant methods. The mathematical formulations are generally clear and correctly presented.
3. **NLPD as Evaluation Metric:** Employing NLPD is a strong point. Unlike MAE or Pearson’s r, NLPD directly assesses the quality of the predictive *distribution*, making it a more appropriate metric for evaluating uncertainty modeling. The empirical results convincingly demonstrate that point estimate metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty.
4. **Asymmetric Loss Demonstration:** The application of asymmetric loss functions provides a concrete example of how uncertainty estimates can be practically utilized. Though the results are mixed, the exploration of both pessimistic and optimistic scenarios illustrates the potential benefits of this approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core methods (GPs, Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) are not novel. The paper’s contribution lies primarily in their *application* to QE and the demonstration of the importance of uncertainty, rather than a breakthrough in modeling techniques. The extensions are well-established in the GP literature and the application to QE feels incremental.
2. **Empirical Evaluation is Insufficient:** While the results using NLPD are convincing *within* the presented experiments, the datasets are relatively small, and the feature set is limited. The observed differences in performance between models may not generalize to larger datasets or more complex feature representations. More comprehensive evaluation on diverse datasets is needed.
3. **Inconclusive Results with Linex Loss:** The results with the Linex loss are particularly problematic. The paper observes poorer performance than the baseline in the pessimistic scenario and attributes it to the models’ lower variance. However, this explanation feels speculative and lacks thorough investigation. It raises concerns about the stability or appropriateness of the Linex loss in this context. This is a significant weakness that casts doubt on the claims regarding asymmetric risk scenarios.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Uncertainty Estimates:** The paper focuses solely on GPs. A crucial missing comparison is to simpler, more scalable methods for estimating uncertainty, such as bootstrapping SVR models (mentioned briefly but not evaluated) or using ensembles of point estimate QE models. These methods may provide a reasonable trade-off between accuracy and computational cost. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the observed overfitting on the en-es dataset affects the reliability of NLL. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of why this dataset is more prone to overfitting and how that impacts the interpretation of results?
2.  Regarding the Linex loss results, can you provide further analysis into why models with lower variance perform poorly in the pessimistic scenario? Have you explored any regularization techniques or modifications to the Linex loss to address this issue?
3.  Have you considered comparing the performance of your GP models to simpler uncertainty estimation methods, such as bootstrapping SVR or ensemble methods, in terms of both NLPD and performance in asymmetric risk scenarios?
4.  The feature set used is relatively limited. How sensitive are your results to the inclusion of more sophisticated features commonly used in QE, such as linguistic features or source-side features?

**Additional Comments:**

The writing is generally clear, though somewhat dense. The paper could benefit from more concise explanations of the core concepts and a more focused discussion of the results. The references are comprehensive. While the contributions are not groundbreaking, the paper makes a valid point about the importance of uncertainty in QE, and the investigation of NLPD as an evaluation metric is a positive step. However, the limited novelty and insufficient empirical evaluation prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance at this time.
