This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), with a particular focus on modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty. The core idea—that capturing uncertainty is crucial for real-world QE applications, such as post-editing workflows—is well-motivated. The paper investigates different GP extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) and proposes using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more suitable evaluation metric than standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r). Furthermore, it explores the benefits of well-calibrated predictive distributions in scenarios with asymmetric risk, using both the Absolute Loss and Linex loss functions.

**Contributions:**

1. **Comprehensive Exploration of GPs for QE Uncertainty:** The paper provides a thorough investigation of GP-based models for QE, extending beyond standard GP regression to include Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs. This exploration itself is valuable, offering a detailed overview of potential modeling choices.
2. **Advocacy for NLPD as a QE Evaluation Metric:** The authors convincingly argue for the use of NLPD, demonstrating through experiments that it captures differences in uncertainty modeling that are missed by traditional metrics. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it suggests a better way to evaluate QE systems designed for decision-making.
3. **Demonstration of Asymmetric Risk Benefits:** The application of asymmetric loss functions, coupled with the Bayes risk estimation framework, demonstrates a practical use case for probabilistic QE predictions. The results, while not universally positive for all loss functions, highlight the potential for tailoring QE systems to specific risk profiles.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of relying on point estimates in real-world QE applications and provides a compelling argument for the importance of uncertainty modeling. The post-editing scenario is a particularly effective example.
2. **Thorough Technical Details:** The paper provides a detailed explanation of the mathematical foundations of GPs, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs. This allows readers to understand the technical intricacies of the proposed methods.
3. **Extensive Experiments:** The experiments are conducted on multiple datasets with different language pairs, demonstrating the generalizability of the proposed methods. The cross-validation setup is also appropriate.
4. **Thoughtful Discussion of Results:** The authors engage in a thoughtful discussion of the results, acknowledging limitations and potential areas for future work. The analysis of the linex loss results in the pessimistic scenario is particularly insightful.
5. **Well-Referenced and Contextualized:** The paper appropriately cites related work in QE, Gaussian Processes, and asymmetric loss functions, situating the research within the broader literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Individual Components:** While the combination of GPs with NLPD and asymmetric losses is insightful, the individual components (GPs, NLPD, asymmetric loss functions) are not novel. The primary novelty lies in their application and evaluation within the QE context.
2. **Inconclusive Results with Linex Loss:** The results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and require further investigation. The authors correctly identify a potential issue with models having low variance, but a more thorough analysis is needed. It would be helpful to understand *why* the linex loss penalizes these models so heavily, and whether there are ways to mitigate this effect.
3. **Feature Set Limitations:** The use of a relatively limited feature set (17 features from the QuEst framework) might limit the overall performance of the models. While the authors acknowledge this choice, it's possible that more sophisticated features could lead to more pronounced differences between the models.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While SVR is mentioned, the paper could benefit from a comparison to more recent and stronger QE baselines, especially those incorporating neural network architectures. This would provide a clearer benchmark for evaluating the performance of the GP-based models.
5. **Discussion of Computational Cost:**  The paper doesn’t address the computational cost of training and using the proposed GP models, especially for larger datasets. GPs can be computationally expensive, and this is a practical consideration for real-world deployment.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the reasons for choosing the specific range of w values for the asymmetric losses? Was this range determined empirically, or based on prior knowledge of the application domain?
2. You mention the potential issue of GPs being computationally expensive. Could you provide some insights into the training and prediction times for your models on the datasets used in the experiments?
3. Did you experiment with different feature sets beyond the 17 QuEst features? If so, how did the performance of the models change?
4. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of why the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario penalizes models with low variance? Is it possible that the choice of the linex loss function itself is not well-suited for this scenario?
5.  The paper focuses on GPs due to their probabilistic nature. Have you considered exploring Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) as an alternative? They offer similar probabilistic capabilities and could potentially scale better to larger datasets.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The formatting of the equations could be improved for better readability.
*   A table summarizing the key hyperparameters used for each model would be helpful.
*   Consider adding a section discussing the limitations of the study and potential future research directions in more detail.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-written paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of QE. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the combination and evaluation within the context of uncertainty modeling is insightful. The advocacy for NLPD as an evaluation metric is particularly strong. Although there are some weaknesses, particularly regarding the inconclusive results with the linex loss and the limited comparison to stronger baselines, these do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend **acceptance with minor revisions**, addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above. The paper has the potential to influence the way QE systems are developed and evaluated in the future.
