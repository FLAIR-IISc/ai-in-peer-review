This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on the benefits of modeling predictive uncertainty rather than just point estimates. The authors motivate this by highlighting scenarios where confidence in a prediction is crucial for decision-making, such as post-editing workflows. They investigate different GP kernel extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) and evaluate them using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric, alongside standard point estimate metrics. They also demonstrate how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios using asymmetric loss functions.

The primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical investigation of different GP configurations for QE, specifically focusing on their ability to capture and represent predictive uncertainty. The authors convincingly argue that standard point estimate metrics are insufficient for evaluating these models and advocate for the use of NLPD, demonstrating its sensitivity to differences in uncertainty modeling. A second, valuable contribution is the exploration of asymmetric loss functions in the context of QE, providing a framework for tailoring predictions to specific risk profiles and decision-making needs. Finally, the comprehensive experimental setup, including multiple language pairs and a variety of model configurations, provides a solid foundation for their conclusions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Focus on Uncertainty Modeling:** The paper directly addresses a crucial, often overlooked aspect of QE: quantifying confidence in predictions. This is a significant strength, as it acknowledges the practical limitations of relying solely on point estimates in real-world applications. The motivation provided with the post-editing example is clear and compelling.
2. **Rigorous Evaluation with NLPD:** The authors rightly identify the inadequacy of traditional QE metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) for evaluating probabilistic models. The adoption of NLPD, a metric designed for assessing predictive distributions, is a strong methodological choice and enables a more nuanced comparison of the different GP configurations. The results clearly demonstrate the metric’s sensitivity to differences in uncertainty.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-designed, employing multiple datasets (English-Spanish, French-English, English-German) and a consistent feature set. This allows for more generalizable conclusions than would be possible with a single dataset. The 10-fold cross-validation provides robust performance estimates.
4. **Exploration of Asymmetric Risk:** The investigation of asymmetric loss functions and their application to QE is a valuable extension of the work. It demonstrates the potential for tailoring QE models to specific downstream tasks and risk tolerances, increasing their practical utility. The discussion of Bayes risk estimators provides a practical means of leveraging the predictive distributions.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The background on GPs and related concepts is concise and accessible, while the experimental details are sufficiently thorough. The results are presented clearly, and the discussion is insightful.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in GP Application:** While the application of GPs to QE is not *new* (as acknowledged in the introduction), the specific extensions explored (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) have been established in the GP literature. The novelty primarily lies in their systematic evaluation *within the QE context* rather than in the development of novel modeling techniques.
2. **Confusing Results with Linex Loss:** The results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and the explanation provided feels somewhat speculative. The authors acknowledge the potential issue of the loss being biased toward models with high variance, but further investigation is needed to clarify this behavior and understand the limitations of using linex in this context.
3. **Feature Set Limitations:** While the authors justify the use of a relatively small feature set for consistency across language pairs, it's possible that more expressive features could further improve performance and highlight differences between the models. The impact of feature engineering is not thoroughly investigated.
4. **Dataset Size Considerations:** The paper acknowledges the potential for overfitting in the smaller en-es dataset. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings, particularly those related to model selection and hyperparameter tuning. More extensive experiments with larger datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Modern QE Methods:** While the paper effectively evaluates different GP configurations *among themselves*, it lacks a comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art QE methods based on neural networks. This makes it difficult to assess the competitive advantage (or disadvantage) of the GP-based approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential reasons why the linex loss performs poorly in the pessimistic scenario? Are there specific characteristics of the warped GP outputs that contribute to this issue?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters for the tanh-based warping function (number of terms, learning rate)? Was any regularization employed to prevent overfitting?
3.  Would the authors consider investigating the performance of GP models with more sophisticated feature sets or feature selection techniques?
4.  Have the authors experimented with other methods for quantifying uncertainty in QE, such as ensembles of SVR models or Bayesian Neural Networks? If so, how do these approaches compare to the GP-based methods in terms of NLPD and performance in asymmetric risk scenarios?
5.  The paper mentions using the median as a point estimate. Have you explored using the mean or other central tendency measures and how does this impact results, particularly in the asymmetric risk scenarios?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase “wmt16” on page 8 should be properly formatted (e.g., WMT16).
*   A brief discussion of the computational cost of training and evaluating the different GP models would be helpful.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used for each model configuration.



Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Quality Estimation. The focus on uncertainty modeling is particularly commendable, and the authors provide a strong justification for using NLPD as an evaluation metric. While the novelty of the specific GP extensions is limited, the systematic evaluation and application to asymmetric risk scenarios are significant contributions. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above and answer the questions posed.
