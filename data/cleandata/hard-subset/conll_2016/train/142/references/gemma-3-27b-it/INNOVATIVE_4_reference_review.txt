This paper explores the application of Gaussian Processes (GPs) to Quality Estimation (QE), specifically focusing on modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty. The primary contribution is a thorough investigation of different GP configurations – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and a compelling argument for evaluating QE models using metrics that account for predictive distributions, namely the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD). A secondary contribution is a demonstration of how these uncertainty estimates can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios using asymmetric loss functions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Focus on Uncertainty:** The paper tackles a critical, yet often overlooked, aspect of QE: predictive uncertainty. Moving beyond point estimates is a genuinely valuable direction, particularly for real-world applications where informed decision-making is crucial. This is a significant shift in thinking for the QE community.
2. **Sound Methodology & Thorough Exploration:** The authors systematically explore relevant extensions of GPs, choosing reasonable configurations (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) and providing detailed explanations of their rationale. The experimental setup, while not extensive, covers multiple language pairs and provides a reasonable comparative analysis.
3. **Strong Argument for NLPD:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that standard point-estimate metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling. The adoption of NLPD as a more appropriate evaluation metric is a strong recommendation and a key takeaway for researchers in this field.
4. **Practical Application with Asymmetric Loss:** The exploration of asymmetric loss functions, tied to realistic application scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic risk), provides a compelling demonstration of the practical value of well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. Even the inconclusive results in the pessimistic scenario raise interesting questions that warrant further investigation.
5. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and easy to follow, making it accessible to researchers familiar with QE and Gaussian Processes.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scalability Discussion:** While the authors acknowledge that QE datasets are typically small, the potential limitations of GPs for larger datasets are not thoroughly discussed. Briefly addressing this could strengthen the paper.
2. **Over-Reliance on Specific Feature Set:** The choice to use a fixed feature set from QuEst, while practical, limits the exploration of how these uncertainty modeling techniques interact with more advanced feature engineering approaches.
3. **Inconclusive Linex Results:** The inconclusive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario feel somewhat unresolved. While the authors offer a plausible explanation, further investigation would be beneficial.
4. **Missing Error Analysis:** The paper focuses heavily on aggregate metrics. Some qualitative error analysis (e.g., examining cases where uncertainty estimates lead to better or worse decisions) would strengthen the practical relevance of the findings.
5. **Limited Comparison to Alternative Uncertainty Estimation Techniques:** The paper concentrates solely on GPs. A brief discussion of, and potential comparison to, other methods for estimating uncertainty in QE (e.g., ensemble methods, dropout in neural networks) would broaden the context.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the Warped GP models compared to standard GPs, especially as dataset size increases?
2.  Do you have any hypotheses as to why the linex loss performed poorly in the pessimistic scenario, beyond the variance explanation? Is there a potential interaction between the warping function and the sensitivity of the linex loss to variance?
3.  Were any attempts made to incorporate more sophisticated feature sets into the experiments, or were the limitations of the QuEst feature set a deliberate choice to isolate the impact of the uncertainty modeling techniques?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper demonstrates a bold and innovative approach to a crucial problem in QE. While not without minor flaws, the novelty of the approach, coupled with the compelling arguments for evaluating uncertainty, outweighs these concerns. I recommend acceptance, as this paper has the potential to significantly influence the direction of future research in QE.
