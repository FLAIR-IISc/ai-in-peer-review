This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for modeling uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors investigate different kernel functions (Matern kernels) and warping functions within the GP framework, aiming to improve calibration and provide better uncertainty estimates for decision-making in MT workflows. The primary contribution is an empirical evaluation of these different GP configurations, focusing on how well they capture uncertainty, as measured by the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD). A secondary contribution is demonstrating the potential benefits of probabilistic QE models in asymmetric risk scenarios through the use of asymmetric loss functions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Exploration of GP Configurations:** The paper systematically investigates several extensions to standard GPs—Matern kernels and warped GPs—that are plausible improvements for QE. This level of detail in exploring model variations within a well-established framework is commendable.
2. **Emphasis on NLPD as an Evaluation Metric:** The authors correctly identify the limitations of traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) for evaluating probabilistic models and advocate for the use of NLPD, a more appropriate metric for assessing calibration and predictive uncertainty. This is a significant point, often overlooked in QE research.
3. **Connection to Decision-Making:**  The paper clearly articulates the practical motivation for modeling uncertainty in QE – improving decision-making in scenarios like post-editing, where risk tolerance varies. The exploration of asymmetric loss functions strengthens this connection and demonstrates potential applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the exploration of GP extensions is thorough, the individual components (Matern kernels, warped GPs) are well-established in the GP literature and not novel to the QE field. The contribution lies in *applying* these techniques, rather than *developing* them. This application, while valuable, doesn’t represent a significant breakthrough.
2. **Insufficient Baseline Comparison:** The paper focuses almost exclusively on variations within the GP framework. A more compelling evaluation would include comparisons to state-of-the-art QE models that *aren't* based on GPs, such as neural network-based approaches.  Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess the true advantages of the proposed GP-based models. The authors acknowledge SVR but dismiss it due to the lack of probabilistic interpretation; more recent neural QE models *do* provide mechanisms for uncertainty estimation (e.g., via dropout or ensemble methods).
3. **Over-reliance on Cross-Validation:**  The use of 10-fold cross-validation is standard, but the results are presented as averages without reporting variance. This makes it difficult to assess the stability of the observed improvements, particularly for the smaller datasets. A single held-out test set would provide a more robust evaluation.
4. **Inconclusive Asymmetric Loss Results:** The results with asymmetric losses are somewhat unclear, particularly for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario. The authors acknowledge potential issues with variance and suggest the loss might be biased, but this requires further investigation. The lack of a clear benefit across all scenarios weakens the impact of this section.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison to recent neural QE models that also offer uncertainty estimates?  Specifically, how does the performance (in terms of NLPD and asymmetric loss) of your best GP model compare to these alternatives?
2.  Can you provide the variance of the reported metrics from the cross-validation experiments? This will help assess the statistical significance of the observed differences.
3.  Regarding the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario: what specific investigations have you conducted to understand why this loss appears to perform poorly with the warped GP models? Are there specific hyperparameters or model configurations that mitigate this issue?
4.  The paper mentions that the features used are standard for WMT QE tasks, but are relatively limited compared to state-of-the-art systems. How sensitive are the results to the feature set? Would including more advanced features (e.g., from pre-trained language models) alter the conclusions?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The motivation for exploring uncertainty modeling in QE is well-articulated.
*   The inclusion of related work provides context, but could be expanded to include more recent advances in neural QE.
