## Peer Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) in Machine Translation (MT), focusing on their ability to model prediction uncertainty. The authors argue that point estimates from traditional QE models are insufficient for decision-making in real-world MT workflows and propose exploring extensions to GPs (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) to improve uncertainty calibration. They introduce Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than standard point-estimate metrics and demonstrate how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios using different loss functions.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Advocacy for Uncertainty-Aware QE:** The paper effectively highlights the limitations of relying solely on point estimates in practical MT applications and convincingly argues for the importance of incorporating predictive uncertainty into QE models.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of GP Extensions for Uncertainty:** The systematic evaluation of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs within the QE context provides valuable insights into their impact on uncertainty calibration and performance.
3. **Demonstration of Asymmetric Loss Application:** The paper shows a clear application of probabilistic QE outputs using asymmetric loss functions, highlighting potential benefits in scenarios where misclassification costs are uneven.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Relevance:** The paper addresses a critical gap in QE research - the need for uncertainty estimation - and connects it directly to practical MT workflows. The post-editing scenario is a particularly compelling example.
2. **Thorough Exploration of GP Models:** The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of GPs and explore relevant extensions in a systematic manner, providing a good overview of the methodology.
3. **Strong Evaluation Framework:** The adoption of NLPD as an evaluation metric is a significant strength. It directly assesses the quality of the predictive distributions, which is crucial for evaluating uncertainty-aware models. The experimental setup with multiple language pairs and datasets is also commendable.
4. **Practical Application & Analysis:** The demonstration of how to apply probabilistic predictions with asymmetric loss functions is insightful and showcases the potential benefits of the proposed approach. The discussion of optimistic vs. pessimistic scenarios is well-reasoned.
5. **Well-Written and Structured:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and easy to follow. The inclusion of related work and conclusions strengthens the overall presentation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the application of GPs to QE isn't entirely new (as acknowledged by the authors), the specific extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) are well-established in the GP literature. The paper's primary novelty lies in the application *within* QE and the emphasis on uncertainty, rather than a novel algorithmic contribution.  This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw, but should be acknowledged.
2. **Inconclusive Results with Linex Loss:** The results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are problematic and not adequately explained.  The authors suggest that the lower variance of the warped GPs might be contributing, but a more in-depth investigation is needed. This weakens the practical demonstration of asymmetric loss application.
3. **Feature Set Limitations:** While the authors acknowledge the simplicity of the feature set, its limited scope may hinder the ability to fully assess the potential of the proposed models. More comprehensive feature engineering could potentially reveal further performance gains or highlight limitations.
4. **Over-reliance on Cross-Validation:** While 10-fold cross-validation is standard, a dedicated test set could provide a more robust evaluation of generalization performance, particularly given the risk of overfitting highlighted in the discussion of the en-es dataset.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Other Uncertainty Estimation Techniques:** The paper focuses exclusively on GPs. A brief comparison to other techniques for estimating uncertainty in QE (e.g., bootstrapping SVR, Bayesian Neural Networks) would strengthen the justification for focusing on GPs.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the reasons why the linex loss results are inconclusive in the pessimistic scenario? What further analysis could be conducted to understand this behavior?
2.  Given the limitations of the feature set, do you foresee significant performance improvements by incorporating more complex or language-specific features? If so, how might these features impact the uncertainty estimation capabilities of the proposed models?
3.  The paper highlights overfitting issues with the smaller en-es dataset. How sensitive are the results to the size of the training data? Would data augmentation or transfer learning strategies be beneficial in low-resource scenarios?
4.  What are the computational costs associated with the proposed models (particularly Warped GPs with tanh functions) compared to simpler QE approaches like SVR?  Scalability considerations are important for real-world deployment.
5.  Although the focus is on GPs, how do you think the arguments for uncertainty-aware QE and the use of NLPD apply to other machine learning methods for QE, like neural networks?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   The figures and tables are clear and well-labeled.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of potential ethical considerations related to the use of QE models in MT workflows (e.g., bias amplification, impact on translator workload).
