This paper explores the application of Gaussian Processes (GPs) to Quality Estimation (QE), with a focus on modelling and evaluating prediction uncertainty. The primary contribution lies in advocating for the use of probabilistic QE models, specifically GPs, and demonstrating the inadequacy of standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) for evaluating the quality of their uncertainty estimates. A secondary contribution is the investigation of different GP extensions—Matèrn kernels and warped GPs—and their impact on uncertainty modelling. Finally, the paper presents an application of these uncertainty estimates within asymmetric risk scenarios, proposing the use of Bayes risk estimators with asymmetric loss functions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty of Focus:** The paper tackles a significantly under-explored aspect of QE: prediction uncertainty. While QE has focused heavily on improving point estimates, this work rightly highlights the practical importance of knowing *how confident* a model is in its prediction, particularly in real-world applications like post-editing workflows. This shift in perspective is a substantial contribution.
2. **Sound Methodology:** The use of GPs is well-justified, and the exploration of Matèrn kernels and warped GPs represents a logical and informed investigation of appropriate model extensions. The authors appropriately leverage established GP theory and demonstrate a solid understanding of the relevant literature.
3. **Compelling Empirical Motivation:** The demonstration that standard metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modelling is a critical finding. This motivates the need for metrics like NLPD, and provides a strong argument for focusing on probabilistic approaches. The asymmetric risk scenario provides a convincing practical application and shows how uncertainty estimates can be directly beneficial.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The theoretical background is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is detailed enough to be reproducible.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Comparison:** While the paper comprehensively explores variations *within* the GP framework (different kernels, warped GPs), it lacks a comparative analysis against other probabilistic QE models beyond SVR (which is rightly dismissed for its lack of probabilistic output). Exploring other Bayesian approaches or even ensemble methods could strengthen the argument for GPs.
2. **Inconclusive Results for Linex Loss:** The observation that the linex loss performs poorly in the pessimistic scenario, and the hypothesis about variance, warrants further investigation. The current explanation feels somewhat speculative. A more thorough analysis of the posterior distributions, beyond just mean and variance, might be needed to understand this behaviour.
3. **Dataset Size Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the small size of the en-es dataset and its impact on NLL. While this is addressed, the conclusions drawn from experiments on this dataset are less reliable. Increasing the dataset size or incorporating data augmentation techniques could improve the robustness of the findings.
4. **Practicality of Hyperparameter Optimization:** While the paper mentions efficient hyperparameter optimization for GPs, it does not discuss the computational cost associated with it, particularly for larger datasets or more complex models. This is an important consideration for practical adoption.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why you chose not to compare your GP-based models with other probabilistic QE techniques (e.g., Bayesian Neural Networks, ensembles of GPs)?
2.  Regarding the poor performance of the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, can you provide more detailed analysis of the posterior distributions (e.g., skewness, kurtosis) to support your hypothesis about the role of variance?
3.  What strategies could be employed to address the limitations of small datasets, such as data augmentation or transfer learning?
4.  What is the approximate computational cost of hyperparameter optimization for the models presented, and how does this scale with dataset size and model complexity?



**Additional Comments:**

The formatting of the table captions could be improved for clarity. It might be helpful to include confidence intervals or standard deviations alongside the reported metric values. The exploration of different asymmetric loss functions is insightful, and further research into this area could be fruitful.
