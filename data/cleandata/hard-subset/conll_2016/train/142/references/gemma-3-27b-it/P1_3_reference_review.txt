## Peer Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) in Machine Translation (MT), focusing on the benefits of modeling prediction uncertainty rather than relying solely on point estimates. The authors argue that uncertainty estimates are crucial for decision-making in real-world MT workflows (e.g., post-editing), and explore several GP extensions – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – to improve calibration and predictive performance. They propose using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than standard point-estimate metrics and demonstrate the utility of probabilistic predictions in asymmetric risk scenarios using simulated losses.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Advocacy for Uncertainty-Aware QE:** The paper provides a strong and well-motivated argument for shifting the focus in QE from solely accurate point predictions to well-calibrated probabilistic predictions, outlining concrete benefits in practical MT applications. This is a valuable perspective often overlooked in the field.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of GP Extensions for QE:** The systematic comparison of different GP kernel choices (EQ, Matèrn) and likelihood models (Gaussian, Warped) within the QE context provides valuable insights into their relative performance and suitability for this specific task.
3. **Demonstration of Asymmetric Risk Benefits:** Illustrating how probabilistic QE predictions can be leveraged with asymmetric loss functions in scenarios like selective post-editing showcases the practical applicability of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of point-estimate QE in real-world MT workflows and convincingly argues for the importance of uncertainty estimation. The post-editing scenario is a particularly compelling example.
2. **Thorough Exploration of GP Models:** The authors demonstrate a solid understanding of GPs and their extensions, systematically exploring different kernel and likelihood options relevant to the QE task. The explanations are clear and well-referenced.
3. **Strong Emphasis on Proper Evaluation:** The introduction and utilization of NLPD as an evaluation metric is a key strength.  It provides a more nuanced and informative assessment of model performance than traditional metrics, especially for probabilistic models. The observation that point estimate metrics don’t capture the differences in uncertainty modeling is insightful.
4. **Practical Application Focus:**  The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios and the demonstration of how predictive distributions can be used with asymmetric loss functions add significant practical value to the work. The linex and AL losses are relevant and appropriately applied.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors use publicly available datasets (WMT) and clearly describe the features used, enhancing the potential for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Empirical Gains:** While the paper demonstrates the *importance* of uncertainty modeling, the *magnitude* of improvements achieved with the proposed GP extensions, particularly in the point-estimate metrics, is relatively modest. This makes it difficult to strongly advocate for the increased complexity of GPs over simpler models.
2. **Sensitivity to Dataset Size:** The results indicate that the performance differences observed are sensitive to the dataset size. The inconsistencies between datasets (particularly the en-es dataset) raise concerns about the generalizability of the findings. A more detailed analysis of the impact of data size on the results would be beneficial.
3. **Linex Loss Issues:** The inconclusive results and even degradation in performance when using the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are concerning and require further investigation. The authors attribute this to the lower variance of warped GPs, but this explanation feels somewhat speculative and needs more robust justification.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Uncertainty Estimation Methods:** The focus is exclusively on GPs. A comparison to simpler, perhaps less well-calibrated, uncertainty estimation methods (e.g., bootstrapping with SVR) would help establish a clearer baseline and demonstrate the added value of GPs.
5. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the authors mention scalability isn't a major issue for QE datasets, a brief discussion of the computational cost associated with training and using the various GP models (particularly Warped GPs) compared to SVR would be helpful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why you chose to focus solely on GPs for uncertainty modeling, and what considerations led you to exclude other potential approaches (e.g., Bayesian Neural Networks, ensembles)?
2.  The results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are unexpected. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of this phenomenon, perhaps exploring the relationship between predictive variance and the performance of the linex estimator more closely?
3.  The performance differences between models seem to be sensitive to dataset size. Could you perform additional experiments with larger datasets to assess the stability and generalizability of your findings?
4.  Regarding the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., the number of tanh terms in the Warped GP), what strategies were used for selecting these parameters, and how sensitive are the results to these choices?
5.  Could you provide some insights into the computational cost (training and prediction time) of the proposed models, and how they compare to a standard SVR model?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The reference list is comprehensive and up-to-date.
*   Consider including a visualization of the predictive distributions for different models and scenarios to better illustrate the differences in uncertainty estimation.



Overall, this is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of QE. While the empirical gains are not overwhelming, the paper effectively highlights the importance of uncertainty modeling and provides a strong foundation for future research in this area. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
