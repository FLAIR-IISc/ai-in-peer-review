## Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

**Summary:** This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on modeling and evaluating *uncertainty* in predictions, rather than just point estimates. The authors explore various GP extensions – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and propose using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than standard MAE/Pearson’s r. They also demonstrate how predictive distributions can be leveraged in scenarios with asymmetric risk, using asymmetric loss functions.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Investigation of GP Variations for QE Uncertainty:** The systematic evaluation of different GP configurations (kernels and warping functions) tailored for QE, and their impact on uncertainty modeling, is a valuable contribution. This provides practical guidance for researchers and practitioners interested in applying GPs to QE.
2. **Advocacy for NLPD in QE Evaluation:** The paper convincingly argues for the use of NLPD as a more informative evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models, highlighting its sensitivity to calibration and ability to differentiate models beyond point estimate performance.
3. **Demonstration of Asymmetric Risk Application:** The paper effectively illustrates how the predictive distributions obtained from GPs can be integrated into decision-making processes involving asymmetric costs, showcasing the practical benefits of modeling uncertainty.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of point estimate QE and the importance of uncertainty modeling for real-world applications like post-editing. The introduction effectively sets the stage and establishes the rationale for the research.
2. **Thorough Exploration of GP Techniques:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of Gaussian Processes and explore relevant extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) with appropriate justification.  The descriptions of these techniques are clear and concise.
3. **Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup, utilizing multiple datasets and a 10-fold cross-validation scheme, is robust. The comparison of different models and the inclusion of relevant baseline metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) provide a comprehensive assessment.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Results:** The authors provide a nuanced interpretation of the experimental results, acknowledging the limitations of certain metrics (like NLL) and offering explanations for observed trends. The discussion on the impact of dataset size is particularly valuable.
5. **Practical Relevance:** The application of asymmetric loss functions to the QE problem is a practical contribution, demonstrating how uncertainty estimates can directly improve decision-making in scenarios with varying costs.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the application of GPs to QE isn’t new (as acknowledged in the related work), and the extensions themselves are established techniques, the combination and specific adaptations for QE *lack significant methodological novelty*. The main contribution lies in the detailed empirical investigation and analysis rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic approach.
2. **Feature Set Restriction:** The use of a limited set of 17 features, while justified for comparability, might hinder the performance of the models and potentially obscure the benefits of the uncertainty modeling.  Exploring a wider range of features could further differentiate the models.
3. **Inconclusive Linex Loss Results:** The authors acknowledge the problematic results obtained with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, attributing it to the lower variance of the warped GP models. While this is a reasonable explanation, a deeper investigation into the behavior of the loss function and its interaction with the model variance is needed.  The fact that the loss seems to *penalize* well-calibrated uncertainty is concerning.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Modern Neural QE Approaches:** The paper focuses almost exclusively on GPs.  A comparison to more recent, state-of-the-art Neural Quality Estimation (NQE) models (which are rapidly advancing) would be beneficial to contextualize the performance of the GP-based models and demonstrate their competitive advantages (or disadvantages).  While the authors state NQE models don't easily provide uncertainty estimates, exploring Bayesian NQE variants would be a valuable addition.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training the Warped GP models compared to the standard GPs, especially as the number of tanh terms (I) increases?
2.  You mention the linex loss behaving unexpectedly in the pessimistic scenario. Have you explored other methods to encourage higher variance in the predictive distribution, such as modifying the kernel or likelihood?
3.  While you rightly focus on intrinsic evaluation with NLPD, do you have any preliminary results on *extrinsic* evaluation, i.e., how the uncertainty estimates from these models affect the performance of a downstream task like post-editing triage (as mentioned in the introduction)?
4.  Given the limitations of applying uncertainty estimation directly to NQE models, have you considered using techniques like Monte Carlo Dropout or Deep Ensembles to obtain predictive distributions from NQE models, and comparing them to your GP-based approach?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The references are comprehensive and relevant.
* Table 1 could benefit from showing standard deviations alongside the mean values to indicate the variability of the results.
*  A more visual representation of the predictive distributions (e.g., showing predicted distributions for a few example sentences) would enhance the understanding of the model's uncertainty.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of Quality Estimation. While the methodological novelty is somewhat limited, the thorough empirical investigation, insightful analysis, and compelling argument for using NLPD for evaluating probabilistic QE models are significant. The demonstration of asymmetric risk application further strengthens the practical relevance of the work.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
