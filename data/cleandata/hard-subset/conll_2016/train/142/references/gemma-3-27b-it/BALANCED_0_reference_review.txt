This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on modeling predictive uncertainty rather than just point estimates. The core idea is that uncertainty estimates are crucial for decision-making in MT workflows, such as deciding whether to post-edit or discard a translated segment. The authors explore different GP kernel extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) and evaluate their ability to capture uncertainty using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric, alongside traditional point estimate metrics. They also demonstrate how these predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios using asymmetric loss functions.

The main contribution of this work, in my view, is the *thorough empirical evaluation of different GP models for QE, specifically examining their calibration and usefulness in decision-making scenarios*. While GPs have been used in QE before, this paper provides a comprehensive comparison of various GP extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) using the NLPD metric, which is well-suited for evaluating probabilistic models. The investigation into asymmetric risk scenarios and the application of asymmetric loss functions add further value. A secondary contribution is the *demonstration of the limitations of standard point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) in capturing differences in model uncertainty*. The authors show these metrics are often insufficient for distinguishing between models with varying degrees of calibration.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance and Motivation:** The paper tackles a highly relevant problem in MT – the need for reliable uncertainty estimates in QE for informed decision-making. The motivation is clearly articulated with a compelling example of a post-editing scenario.
2. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation of different GP models and kernel extensions across multiple datasets and language pairs. The use of 10-fold cross-validation adds robustness to the results.
3. **Appropriate Evaluation Metric:** The use of NLPD is a significant strength. It’s a well-established metric for evaluating probabilistic models and provides a more nuanced assessment of model performance than traditional point estimate metrics.
4. **Exploration of Asymmetric Risk:** The investigation of asymmetric risk scenarios and the application of asymmetric loss functions are novel and demonstrate the practical benefits of modeling predictive uncertainty. This section adds significant value to the paper.
5. **Sound Methodology:** The paper builds upon a solid foundation of established methods (GPs, kernel methods) and provides clear explanations of the techniques used.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Method:** The use of GPs for QE is not novel in itself. The primary novelty lies in the comprehensive evaluation and application to asymmetric risk, rather than a fundamentally new modeling approach. This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw, but it's important to acknowledge.
2. **Results Sensitivity to Dataset Size:** The authors acknowledge that the performance differences observed depend on the dataset size. The en-es dataset, being smaller, exhibits different behavior, raising concerns about the generalizability of the findings. Further investigation into the impact of dataset size would be beneficial.
3. **Linex Loss Results:** The results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and somewhat problematic. The authors suggest this might be due to the model being penalized for having low variance, which could be a limitation of the loss function or the model itself. This requires further clarification.
4. **Feature Set:** The decision to use a fixed set of 17 features, while understandable for comparability, might limit the potential performance of the models. Exploring the impact of different feature sets could be valuable.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Neural Methods:** While the paper focuses on GPs, a brief comparison to more recent neural QE models that *do* provide uncertainty estimates (e.g., using dropout as a Bayesian approximation) would strengthen the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why the linex loss performs poorly in the pessimistic scenario? Is it possible that the low variance of the Warped GPs is genuinely undesirable in this context, and if so, how might this be addressed?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters for the Warped GPs, particularly the number of tanh terms?  Did you observe any overfitting issues when using a larger number of terms?
3.  You mention that the feature set was chosen for comparability.  Could you briefly discuss the potential impact of using a more comprehensive feature set, and whether this would likely change the relative performance of the different models?
4.  Given the increasing prevalence of neural QE models, how do you see the role of GPs in this field evolving? Are there specific scenarios where GPs might remain advantageous over neural approaches?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and generally easy to follow.
*   The inclusion of related work is comprehensive and relevant.
*   The tables and figures are clear and informative.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost associated with the different GP models.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of QE. While the core methodology isn't entirely novel, the comprehensive evaluation, insightful analysis, and exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios are significant strengths. The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed through further investigation and clarification. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions, specifically addressing the questions raised above and providing a more in-depth discussion of the linex loss results.
