## Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on modeling predictive uncertainty, rather than just point estimates. The authors argue that capturing uncertainty is crucial for real-world QE applications, such as post-editing scenarios. They explore several GP extensions – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and propose using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than traditional MAE or Pearson’s r. Finally, they demonstrate how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios using different loss functions.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Strong Advocacy for Uncertainty-Aware QE:** The paper provides a compelling argument for moving beyond point estimates in QE and highlights the practical benefits of incorporating uncertainty information into decision-making processes.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration of GP Extensions for QE:** The systematic investigation of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs within the QE context is valuable. While not entirely novel techniques, the application and analysis within this domain are insightful.
3. **Empirical Demonstration of NLPD’s Relevance:** Showing that standard point estimate metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, and advocating for NLPD, is a key contribution, particularly given the field's historical reliance on simpler metrics.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Definition:** The introduction clearly articulates the limitations of relying solely on point estimates in real-world QE workflows and establishes a compelling need for uncertainty modeling. The post-editing example is particularly effective.
2. **Thorough Technical Background:** The paper provides a well-written and comprehensive overview of Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs, making it accessible to readers familiar with machine learning but perhaps new to their application in QE.
3. **Rigorous Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments across multiple language pairs and datasets, using cross-validation, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approaches.  The inclusion of both intrinsic (NLPD) and extrinsic (asymmetric risk) evaluation is a strength.
4. **Practical Focus:** The discussion of asymmetric risk scenarios and the use of Bayes risk estimators demonstrate a clear understanding of the practical applications of QE and how uncertainty information can be directly leveraged to improve decision-making.
5. **Well-Structured and Readable:** The paper is generally well-organized and clearly written, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the *application* of GPs and their extensions to QE is valuable, the methods themselves are not new. The paper doesn’t present any novel algorithmic advancements within the GP framework. The contribution lies more in the analysis and demonstration of their suitability for the QE task.
2. **Over-reliance on Feature Set:** The choice to use a relatively limited feature set (17 features from QuEst) while acknowledging the existence of more complex and potentially better-performing feature sets feels like a constraint. This limits the potential for achieving state-of-the-art point estimate performance and potentially impacts the conclusions about the relative importance of uncertainty modeling. Justifying this choice solely based on language independence is somewhat weak.
3. **Inconclusive Results in Asymmetric Risk Scenarios:** The findings regarding the linex loss in pessimistic scenarios are confusing. The claim that the standard GP outperformed Warped GPs suggests a potential issue with the model or estimator in this specific configuration. This needs further investigation and clearer explanation.  The abrupt shift in results and the need to alter the w parameter range indicate underlying issues.
4. **Limited Analysis of Model Calibration:** While the paper focuses on NLPD, a more direct assessment of model calibration (e.g., using calibration curves) would have strengthened the argument for the benefits of uncertainty modeling. Showing *how well* the predicted probabilities align with actual outcomes would be highly persuasive.
5. **Lack of Comparison with Newer QE Approaches:** The related work section primarily focuses on earlier QE methods. A discussion of more recent advancements, particularly those based on neural networks and transformers, would provide a better context for the contributions of this work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario: Could you provide a more detailed analysis of why the standard GP outperformed the Warped GPs in this case? What specific properties of the linex loss or the predictive distributions led to this result?
2.  The paper advocates for NLPD as an evaluation metric.  However, it doesn’t explicitly discuss its limitations. What are the potential drawbacks of using NLPD, and under what circumstances might it be less informative?
3.  You mention that the chosen feature set was limited for cross-lingual consistency. Could you briefly discuss the potential impact of using a richer feature set on the reported results, especially concerning the gains observed from Warped GPs?
4.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost of training and evaluating the Warped GP models compared to standard GPs, particularly for larger datasets? Scalability is a practical concern.
5.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the number of tanh terms (I) in the Warped GP? Did you explore a wider range of values for I and perform sensitivity analysis?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The figures are generally clear and informative.
*   The reference list is comprehensive and well-formatted.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the potential for combining uncertainty estimates with other sources of information (e.g., human feedback) to further improve decision-making in QE workflows.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Quality Estimation. While the core methodology isn’t groundbreaking, the strong advocacy for uncertainty-aware QE, the comprehensive analysis of GP extensions, and the empirical demonstration of NLPD’s relevance are significant contributions. The weaknesses are relatively minor and can be addressed through the author response and potential revisions. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
