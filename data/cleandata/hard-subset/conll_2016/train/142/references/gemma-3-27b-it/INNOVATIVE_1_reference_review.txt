This paper explores the application of Gaussian Processes (GPs) to Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on leveraging the inherent uncertainty modeling capabilities of GPs, specifically through the use of Matern kernels and warped GPs. The primary contribution lies in a thorough empirical investigation of these probabilistic models in QE, demonstrating that differences in uncertainty modeling *are* detectable using appropriate metrics (NLPD) – a finding often overlooked in QE research which traditionally relies on point estimate metrics. A secondary contribution is the exploration of asymmetric loss functions within the context of QE, demonstrating how well-calibrated uncertainty estimates can be utilized for improved decision-making in risk-sensitive scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Focus on Uncertainty:** The paper tackles a significantly under-explored aspect of QE: modeling and utilizing *uncertainty*. While QE models are frequently deployed in real-world scenarios where imperfect predictions have consequences, the field has largely ignored the value of knowing *how confident* a model is in its prediction. This is the most compelling aspect of the work.
2. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple language pairs and datasets, systematically comparing different GP configurations. The use of NLPD as a key evaluation metric is appropriate and insightful, highlighting the limitations of traditional point-estimate metrics.
3. **Practical Relevance:**  The exploration of asymmetric loss functions and their connection to real-world QE applications (post-editing, gisting) demonstrates a clear understanding of the practical implications of uncertainty modeling. The connection to Bayes risk estimation is a valuable addition.
4. **Thorough GP Exploration:** The paper provides a good overview of relevant GP extensions (Matern kernels, warped GPs), explaining the rationale behind their adoption in the QE context and presenting a clear experimental comparison.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methods:** While the *application* to QE is valuable, the GP methods themselves (Matern kernels, warped GPs) are not novel contributions. The paper correctly acknowledges this, but the overall impact is somewhat lessened by this fact. The core innovation resides in the application and evaluation, not the methods themselves.
2. **Inconclusive Linex Results:** The findings regarding the linex loss in pessimistic scenarios are puzzling and require further investigation. The reported negative results raise questions about the applicability of this loss function and the interpretation of the results. The authors acknowledge this but don't provide strong insights.
3. **Dataset Size Sensitivity:** The results appear sensitive to dataset size, particularly regarding the choice between the tanh-based warping function and the log-based function. This suggests that the models may be overfitting to smaller datasets. While this is mentioned, a more detailed analysis of this sensitivity would be beneficial.
4. **Feature Set Limitation:** The choice of a relatively limited feature set, while justifiable for comparability, might restrict the overall performance of the models and potentially mask subtle differences in uncertainty estimation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the inconclusive linex loss results in the pessimistic scenario: Can you elaborate on the potential reasons for the poor performance? Is it possible that the high variance associated with standard GPs is, in some way, *beneficial* in this particular loss/scenario, despite being undesirable in general?
2.  The paper mentions that the results are sensitive to dataset size. Could you provide further analysis of this sensitivity, perhaps by examining learning curves or conducting ablation studies?
3.  Were any attempts made to regularize the warped GP models to mitigate potential overfitting, particularly on the smaller datasets? If so, what were the results?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the chosen evaluation metrics, particularly regarding their ability to capture all aspects of uncertainty modeling.
*   A brief exploration of the computational cost associated with the different GP configurations would be useful.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite the limited novelty in the underlying methods, this paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of QE by highlighting the importance of uncertainty modeling and providing a thorough empirical evaluation of different approaches. The focus on practical relevance and the exploration of asymmetric loss functions further strengthen the paper's impact. I believe the strengths outweigh the weaknesses and recommend **acceptance**, as the paper introduces a compelling new direction for QE research. It is a bold exploration of a neglected aspect of the field.
