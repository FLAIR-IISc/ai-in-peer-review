This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on the benefits of modeling predictive uncertainty rather than relying on point estimates. The authors motivate this by highlighting the practical applications of QE in workflows where confidence levels are crucial for decision-making, such as post-editing scenarios. They investigate different GP extensions – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and propose using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more informative evaluation metric than standard point estimate metrics. They also demonstrate how predictive distributions can be leveraged in scenarios with asymmetric risk using appropriate loss functions.

**Contributions:**

1. **Comprehensive Exploration of GP Extensions for QE:** The paper provides a thorough review and experimental comparison of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs within the context of QE, offering valuable insights into their strengths and weaknesses for modeling uncertainty in this specific task. This is a significant contribution as it goes beyond simply applying GPs and delves into nuanced model choices.
2. **Advocacy for NLPD as an Evaluation Metric:** The authors convincingly argue for the adoption of NLPD as a more appropriate metric for evaluating probabilistic QE models, demonstrating that it captures differences in uncertainty modeling that are missed by traditional metrics like MAE and Pearson’s r. This is a key contribution that could shift evaluation practices in the field.
3. **Demonstration of Asymmetric Risk Application:** The exploration of asymmetric loss functions and their application to QE scenarios with varying risk preferences (pessimistic vs. optimistic) is a practical and insightful addition. It highlights how the full predictive distribution can be utilized for better decision-making in real-world applications.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of relying solely on point estimates in QE and provides a compelling motivation for exploring uncertainty modeling. The connection to practical applications like post-editing is particularly strong.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct experiments on multiple datasets with different language pairs, providing a robust evaluation of the proposed models and the different evaluation metrics. The 10-fold cross-validation setup adds to the rigor of the evaluation.
3. **Detailed Technical Explanation:** The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the underlying theory of GPs, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs, making it accessible to readers with varying levels of expertise. The inclusion of equations and clear explanations of the mathematical concepts is commendable.
4. **Insightful Discussion of Results:** The authors provide a thoughtful discussion of the experimental results, highlighting the key findings and their implications. They acknowledge limitations and suggest avenues for future work.
5. **Well-Structured and Clearly Written:** The paper is well-organized and written in a clear and concise style, making it easy to follow the arguments and understand the technical details.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Feature Sets:** The use of a fixed set of 17 features from the QuEst framework, while justified for consistency, might limit the potential performance of the models. Exploring more advanced or language-specific features could potentially lead to further improvements.
2. **Inconclusive Results for Linex Loss in Pessimistic Scenario:** The authors acknowledge that the results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive. Further investigation is needed to understand why the model performs poorly in this setting and whether alternative loss functions or model configurations could address this issue. The presented explanation of variance being too low is a reasonable hypothesis, but requires further support.
3. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While SVR is mentioned, the paper does not compare the proposed GP models to more recent and potentially stronger QE baselines that incorporate deep learning techniques. Comparing against these state-of-the-art models would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed approach.
4. **Over-Reliance on Cross-Validation:** While 10-fold cross-validation is a standard practice, reporting results on a dedicated test set would provide a more reliable estimate of generalization performance.
5. **Limited Exploration of Hyperparameter Optimization:** The two-pass hyperparameter optimization procedure described is reasonable, but a more exhaustive search or the use of more sophisticated optimization techniques might yield better results.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on why you chose to focus solely on GPs and did not explore other probabilistic models that could provide uncertainty estimates, such as Bayesian Neural Networks?
2. Given the inconclusive results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, what specific experiments or analyses could be conducted to better understand the underlying reasons for this poor performance?
3. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the tanh warping function parameters (number of terms, initialisation, etc.)? Did you explore different parameter settings or optimization strategies?
4. What computational cost is associated with the proposed GP models compared to traditional SVR or more recent deep learning-based QE systems? Is scalability a concern for larger datasets?

**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper would benefit from a table summarizing the key differences between the different GP models (EQ kernel, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) in terms of their assumptions and computational complexity.
*  Consider adding a visualization of the predictive distributions for a few sample sentences to illustrate the differences between the different models.
*  The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advances in uncertainty modeling for machine learning and natural language processing.



Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of QE. While there are a few minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper outweigh them, and the authors have provided a strong justification for their work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions and suggestions raised above.
