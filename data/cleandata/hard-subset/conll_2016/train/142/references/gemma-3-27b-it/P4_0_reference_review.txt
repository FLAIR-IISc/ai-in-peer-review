This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on incorporating uncertainty estimates into the prediction process. The primary motivation is to improve decision-making in MT workflows, such as post-editing, where confidence in a translation is crucial. The authors investigate different GP kernel extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) and evaluate their performance not only through standard point estimate metrics but also through the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), which explicitly considers predictive distributions. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of these predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios using asymmetric loss functions.

The main contribution is a thorough investigation of GP models, including less commonly used extensions, for QE with a focus on *calibration* and *uncertainty quantification*. While GPs have been applied to QE before, this work systematically explores kernel choices (Matèrn) and likelihood modifications (Warped GPs) and demonstrates their impact on predictive uncertainty, as measured by NLPD. The second contribution lies in the empirical evaluation highlighting that standard point estimate metrics do *not* effectively capture differences in uncertainty modeling. The third contribution lies in showing how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios, illustrating the practical benefit of the probabilistic approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance and Motivation:** The paper addresses a practically relevant problem. In real-world MT applications, simply knowing the estimated quality isn't enough; understanding the *confidence* in that estimate is critical for informed decision-making. The post-editing scenario is a compelling example.
2. **Thorough Exploration of GP Models:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of GPs and their extensions, specifically tailored for the QE task. The exploration of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs goes beyond standard SVR-based QE approaches and provides valuable insights into potential improvements.
3. **Appropriate Evaluation Metrics:** The use of NLPD is a key strength. It directly assesses the quality of the probabilistic predictions, unlike MAE or Pearson's r, which only evaluate point estimates. This allows for a more nuanced comparison of different models.
4. **Demonstration of Practical Utility:** The application to asymmetric risk scenarios is well-motivated and demonstrates how the uncertainty estimates can be practically leveraged. The use of asymmetric loss functions and the analysis of their performance are insightful.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The technical details are explained clearly, and the experimental setup is well-defined.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in GP Application:** While the application of GPs to QE isn’t new, the extensions explored are established in the GP literature. The novelty primarily lies in applying these extensions *specifically* to the QE domain and demonstrating their impact. A stronger claim of novelty regarding the *combination* of these techniques or a novel adaptation of them would be beneficial.
2. **Dataset Size Limitations:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small datasets (thousands of instances). While this is typical for QE, it limits the generalizability of the findings. The observation that NLPD becomes unreliable for smaller datasets (en-es) highlights this concern.
3. **Inconclusive Results in Asymmetric Risk:** The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and require further investigation. The explanation provided—that the warped models have lower variance and are therefore not penalized enough—is plausible, but more analysis is needed.  The sensitivity of the linex loss to variance is a potential limitation not fully addressed.
4. **Feature Set Limitation:** The authors acknowledge the use of a limited feature set to ensure comparability across language pairs. While understandable, this might prevent the models from reaching their full potential. It's unclear how much the choice of features influenced the observed differences between models.
5. **Lack of Comparison with Modern Neural QE:** The work focuses on kernel methods and doesn’t compare performance against state-of-the-art neural QE models, which have recently shown strong performance. A comparison with at least one representative neural QE model would provide valuable context.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the inconclusive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario: Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the variance of the predictive distributions for the different models? How does the variance correlate with the loss values?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of features? Did you explore any feature selection techniques to identify the most informative features for each language pair?
3.  Would the results change if you employed a different method for uncertainty estimation (e.g., Monte Carlo dropout) alongside the GP models? How does the computational cost compare?
4. You mention the potential for active learning with the variance estimates. Can you elaborate on how this might be implemented in the context of QE, and what specific query strategies would be most effective?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity of the different GP models, particularly for larger datasets.
*   It would be helpful to provide a more intuitive explanation of why the Warped GPs improve performance, beyond simply stating that they avoid assigning probability mass to negative values.
*   The terminology "optimistic" and "pessimistic" scenarios could be clarified further.
