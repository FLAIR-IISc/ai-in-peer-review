This paper explores the application of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), specifically focusing on modeling prediction uncertainty. The primary contribution is a systematic investigation of different GP extensions – Matern kernels and Warped GPs – and a demonstration that these models, when evaluated using appropriate metrics like Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), offer improved uncertainty estimates compared to standard GPs. A secondary, and valuable, contribution lies in illustrating how these uncertainty estimates can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic/optimistic post-editing) using asymmetric loss functions, and showing that doing so yields performance gains. The authors also correctly point out the inadequacy of standard point-estimate metrics for evaluating uncertainty-aware models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in QE Perspective:** While GPs aren’t new to QE, the focused and systematic exploration of uncertainty modeling *within* QE, and the direct comparison of different GP extensions geared towards better calibrated probability distributions is genuinely novel. The paper moves beyond simply achieving good point estimates and directly addresses a critical gap in practical QE deployment: reliable uncertainty quantification.
2. **Sound Methodology & Thorough Experimentation:** The authors perform a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple language pairs and datasets. They appropriately utilize cross-validation and a diverse set of features, ensuring a robust evaluation of their proposed models. The comparison against standard GP implementations provides a necessary baseline.
3. **Clear Demonstration of NLPD's Superiority:** The authors convincingly demonstrate the limitations of conventional QE metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) in capturing differences in uncertainty modeling. The use of NLPD offers a significantly more informative evaluation, aligning better with the goal of assessing probabilistic models.
4. **Practical Application with Asymmetric Loss:** The application of the uncertainty estimates to asymmetric risk scenarios is insightful. Showing how to adapt predictions based on different loss functions (pessimistic/optimistic) and the subsequent performance improvements adds substantial practical value.
5. **Well-Written and Structured:** The paper is clearly written, well-organized, and logically progresses through the problem statement, methodology, experiments, and results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of GP Extensions:** While the explored GP extensions (Matern kernels, Warped GPs) are reasonable choices, the investigation feels somewhat limited. Exploring other relevant extensions, like those incorporating deep learning (e.g., Deep GPs) or other kernel learning methods, could have broadened the scope and potentially yielded more significant improvements.
2. **Over-reliance on Feature Set:** The choice of a fixed feature set, while simplifying the comparison, might mask the interplay between features and uncertainty modeling. Investigating feature selection strategies *within* the probabilistic framework could be a valuable extension. It’s not clear whether the observed improvements are due to better uncertainty estimation *given* the features, or if a more nuanced model would also benefit from different features.
3. **Linex Loss Results:** The results with the Linex loss in the pessimistic scenario seem inconclusive and even counterintuitive (worse performance than baseline). The authors acknowledge this, but a deeper investigation is warranted. The potential issue of high variance models being favored by the Linex loss is a key point that needs further exploration.
4. **Missing Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Specifically, examining *where* the models with better uncertainty estimates succeed or fail compared to standard GPs would provide more nuanced insights. Are they better at identifying truly low-quality segments, or are they simply more conservative overall?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on why the Linex loss performed poorly in the pessimistic scenario, and what potential remedies (e.g., regularization) might address this issue?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the feature set? Did you experiment with any feature selection methods within the probabilistic GP framework?
3. You mention the potential for applying these methods beyond QE. Could you provide a more concrete example of another NLP task where this approach to uncertainty modeling would be particularly beneficial, and briefly outline how it would be applied?
4. Could you provide an example of a prediction from both a standard GP and a warped GP along with their posterior distributions for the same segment? This would help illustrate the difference in uncertainty estimates.

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper’s focus on practical implications and its critique of standard evaluation metrics are commendable.
* The references are comprehensive and relevant.
* The experimental setup is clearly described, facilitating reproducibility.



I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. While not a groundbreaking methodological advance, the paper's strong focus on a practical problem – uncertainty quantification in QE – combined with its systematic investigation and clear presentation, makes it a valuable contribution to the field. The novelty lies not in *what* is done, but *how* it is approached and evaluated. The authors convincingly demonstrate the importance of considering uncertainty in QE, and their work opens up exciting avenues for future research.
