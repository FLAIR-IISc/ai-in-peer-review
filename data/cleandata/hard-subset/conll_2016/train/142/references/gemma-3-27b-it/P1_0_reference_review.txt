## Peer Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) in Machine Translation, focusing on the benefits of modeling prediction uncertainty rather than relying on point estimates. The authors explore various GP extensions – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and propose using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric. They further demonstrate how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios using asymmetric loss functions, showing potential benefits for practical applications like post-editing.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of NLPD's Utility in QE:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) fail to capture differences in the quality of uncertainty modeling produced by different GP configurations.  The use of NLPD highlights these differences and offers a more informative evaluation approach for probabilistic QE models.
2. **Exploration of GP Extensions for QE:** The work provides a practical exploration of relevant GP extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) within the context of QE, outlining their potential benefits and limitations. This serves as a valuable resource for researchers interested in applying GPs to this domain.
3. **Application of Asymmetric Loss Functions to QE:** The paper showcases how to incorporate asymmetric risk preferences into QE decision-making through the use of asymmetric loss functions and corresponding Bayes risk estimators, providing a potentially impactful direction for practical QE systems.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Clear Problem Statement:** The paper effectively motivates the need for uncertainty modeling in QE, particularly in real-world scenarios involving human-machine interaction. The introduction clearly explains why point estimates alone are insufficient for effective decision-making.
2. **Thorough Exploration of GP Variants:** The authors systematically investigate different GP configurations, justifying their choices and providing detailed explanations of the underlying theory.  The comparison of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs is well-structured and insightful.
3. **Rigorous Evaluation with NLPD:**  The adoption of NLPD as the primary evaluation metric is a significant strength. It’s a principled approach that directly assesses the quality of the predictive distributions, rather than just the point estimates.
4. **Practical Relevance of Asymmetric Loss Application:** The application of asymmetric loss functions demonstrates a clear path toward building QE systems that are tailored to specific application needs and risk preferences.  The examples provided are relatable and illustrate the potential benefits.
5. **Well-written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written, clearly organized, and easy to follow. The mathematical notation is appropriate and the explanations are concise and understandable.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Methods:** While the *application* of GPs to QE with a focus on uncertainty is valuable, the underlying methods (GPs, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) are well-established in the broader machine learning literature.  The novelty lies primarily in the comprehensive evaluation and application to QE, not in the development of fundamentally new techniques.
2. **Limited Scope of Datasets:**  The experiments are conducted on a relatively small number of datasets (3 language pairs).  While these datasets are commonly used in QE research, a more extensive evaluation across a wider range of language pairs and MT systems would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Inconclusive Results Regarding Kernel Choice:** The results regarding the benefit of Matèrn kernels over the EQ kernel are mixed.  The authors acknowledge this, but further investigation is needed to determine the conditions under which Matèrn kernels provide a significant advantage.
4. **Sensitivity to Loss Function Choice in Asymmetric Risk:** The results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are somewhat concerning, suggesting potential issues with the loss function or the model’s ability to leverage its output effectively. This needs further investigation and clarification.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While SVR is mentioned, a comparison to more recent and state-of-the-art QE models (e.g., those based on neural networks) would provide a better benchmark for evaluating the performance of the proposed GP-based models.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Regarding the pessimistic scenario with the linex loss:** You observed that the linex loss led to worse performance than the GP baseline. Can you elaborate on why you believe this occurred? Is it potentially related to the variance of the predictive distributions from the warped GPs, as you suggest, or are there other factors at play?
2. **Dataset Expansion:** Do you have plans to evaluate these models on additional language pairs and/or MT systems? If so, could you briefly describe them?
3. **Comparison to Neural QE Models:**  Have you considered comparing your GP-based models to more recent QE approaches based on neural networks? If not, why not?  Do you anticipate that the advantages of modeling uncertainty would be sufficient to overcome the potentially superior point-estimate performance of neural models?
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the hyperparameters of the Warped GP models, particularly the number of tanh terms in the warping function? Did you perform a systematic hyperparameter search, and if so, could you share some of the results?




**Additional Comments:**

*  The discussion of related work is reasonably thorough, but it could be expanded to include more recent developments in QE, especially those involving neural networks.
*  The figures (particularly Figure 1) are well-designed and informative.
*  The writing style is clear and concise throughout the paper.



Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Quality Estimation. While the underlying methods are not entirely novel, the thorough evaluation, application to practical scenarios, and focus on uncertainty modeling are significant strengths. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
