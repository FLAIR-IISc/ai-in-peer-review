## Review for "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) in Machine Translation, focusing on modeling predictive uncertainty rather than just point estimates. The authors argue that uncertainty estimates are crucial for real-world QE applications, particularly in scenarios involving human post-editing. They explore several GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) and propose using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than traditional point estimate metrics. They also demonstrate how predictive distributions can be used with asymmetric loss functions for decision-making under different risk profiles.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Advocacy for Uncertainty-Aware QE:** The paper makes a strong case for the importance of incorporating uncertainty estimates into QE models, tying it directly to practical applications and decision-making in MT workflows. This is a valuable shift in perspective.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of GP Extensions:** The paper provides a relatively thorough empirical comparison of different GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) for QE, offering insights into their performance and suitability for this task.
3. **Demonstration of Asymmetric Loss Application:** The exploration of how predictive distributions can be leveraged with asymmetric loss functions to handle different risk scenarios provides a concrete example of the benefits of uncertainty modeling.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance to the Community:** QE is a vital area for MT research, and moving beyond point estimates is a logical and important step. The paper directly addresses a gap in current QE evaluation practices by highlighting the limitations of metrics like MAE and proposing NLPD.
2. **Solid Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a clear and concise overview of Gaussian Processes, including relevant extensions. The mathematical foundations are well-presented and accessible.
3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments across multiple language pairs and datasets are a strong point. The use of 10-fold cross-validation adds robustness to the results.
4. **Clear Motivation and Application:** The introduction clearly articulates the motivation for exploring uncertainty in QE, using a compelling post-editing scenario. The application to asymmetric risk scenarios is a practical and insightful demonstration of the benefits of the approach.
5. **Thorough Comparison:** The work presents a reasonable comparison to relevant baselines and explores various configurations of the proposed GP models, providing a nuanced understanding of their performance.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methods:** While the application of GPs to QE is not new, the specific GP extensions explored (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) are established techniques from the GP literature. The novelty lies more in the application *to* QE and the focus on uncertainty modeling than in fundamentally new algorithmic contributions. This doesn't necessarily disqualify the paper, but it lowers its impact.
2. **Inconclusive Results on Kernel Choice:** The results regarding the Matèrn kernel vs. the EQ kernel are somewhat inconclusive. The authors acknowledge this, but it suggests that the choice of kernel might not be as critical as the overall shift towards probabilistic modeling.
3. **Linex Loss Behavior:** The paper reports unexpected and problematic behavior with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, suggesting potential issues with the loss function or the models’ ability to effectively utilize it. This requires further investigation and a more detailed discussion.
4. **Feature Set Limitations:** The use of a fixed set of 17 features, while justified for comparability, may limit the potential performance of the models. More sophisticated feature engineering might yield better results and influence the observed differences between models.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Newer QE Models:** The paper primarily compares against SVR.  A comparison to more recent QE models based on neural networks (e.g., those using transformers) would strengthen the evaluation and provide a better benchmark for the proposed GP-based approaches.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the potential reasons for the unexpected behavior observed with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario? Do you have any hypotheses about why models with lower variance perform poorly with this loss?
2.  Given the limited impact of kernel choice in your experiments, do you believe the benefits of using GPs for QE primarily stem from the ability to model uncertainty rather than the specific kernel used?
3.  Have you considered exploring more recent QE models based on neural networks as baselines? If not, could you discuss the potential challenges and benefits of comparing your GP-based approach to these models?
4.  Could you provide more detail on the parameter tuning process for the warped GP models, specifically how you determined the number of tanh terms to use?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The writing is generally clear and well-organized.
*   The paper is well-referenced and provides a good overview of related work.
*   The presentation of results could be improved with more visual aids (e.g., plots showing predictive distributions).



**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of QE by advocating for and demonstrating the importance of uncertainty modeling. While the methodological novelty is limited, the empirical evaluation and practical application of the concepts are well-executed. The weaknesses are not severe enough to warrant rejection, but addressing the questions above in the author response period would significantly strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
