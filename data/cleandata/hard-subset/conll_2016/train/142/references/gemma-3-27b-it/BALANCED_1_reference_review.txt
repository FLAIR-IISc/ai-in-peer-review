This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty. The authors motivate the use of uncertainty estimates in QE, particularly for scenarios involving human post-editing, and investigate different GP extensions – Matern kernels and Warped GPs – to improve calibration and predictive performance. A key contribution is a thorough empirical evaluation, not only using standard QE metrics but also employing the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), which appropriately accounts for predictive distributions. The paper further examines how these uncertainty-aware predictions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios using asymmetric loss functions.

**Contributions:**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of NLPD's Utility in QE:** The authors convincingly demonstrate that standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling achieved by different GP configurations. This highlights the importance of metrics like NLPD for evaluating probabilistic QE models, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. **Systematic Investigation of GP Extensions for QE:** The paper provides a systematic exploration of different GP kernels (Matern) and likelihoods (Warped GPs) tailored for the QE task. This provides a practical guide for researchers and practitioners looking to implement probabilistic QE models.
3. **Application of Asymmetric Loss Functions:** The authors demonstrate how the predictive distributions generated by GPs can be effectively used in decision-making scenarios with asymmetric costs (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic risk tolerance) via asymmetric loss functions. This expands the potential applications of probabilistic QE beyond simple quality scoring.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of point-estimate QE and establishes a compelling motivation for exploring uncertainty modeling. The post-editing scenario is a particularly effective example.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough experimental evaluation across multiple datasets and compare several GP configurations, demonstrating a strong commitment to empirical validation. The use of 10-fold cross-validation adds to the robustness of the findings.
3. **Comprehensive Literature Review:** The paper provides a well-structured and comprehensive review of relevant literature, including work on GPs, QE, and asymmetric loss functions, properly contextualizing their work within the broader field.
4. **Insightful Discussion of Results:** The authors provide insightful discussions of their experimental results, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of different models and the implications of their findings. They appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the inconclusive results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario.
5. **Practical Relevance:** The findings have practical relevance for real-world QE applications, particularly those involving human-in-the-loop workflows where informed decision-making is crucial.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Feature Set:** While the authors justify using a relatively small feature set (17 features from QuEst), it is possible that exploring a more comprehensive set of features could further improve the performance of the models and potentially reveal different patterns in uncertainty modeling. This is especially relevant given that state-of-the-art QE systems often employ much larger feature sets.
2. **Dataset Size Limitations:** The en-es dataset is significantly smaller than the other two. The authors acknowledge that this might lead to overfitting and unreliable NLL measurements, but a larger dataset or more robust statistical analysis would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Hyperparameter Optimization Details:** While the paper mentions a two-pass hyperparameter optimization procedure, a more detailed description of the optimization process (e.g., search space, optimization algorithm) would be helpful for reproducibility.
4. **Lack of Comparison with Non-GP Models:** The paper focuses exclusively on GP models. Including a comparison with other probabilistic models (e.g., Bayesian neural networks) or even strong non-probabilistic QE systems (e.g., based on deep learning) would provide a broader benchmark and further demonstrate the value of the proposed approach.
5. **Linex Loss Interpretation:** The authors' interpretation of the poor performance of the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario is insightful but speculative. Further investigation, perhaps through analyzing the variance of the predictive distributions, would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the hyperparameter optimization, could you provide more details on the search space and optimization algorithm used? Were any regularization techniques employed to prevent overfitting, especially for the smaller en-es dataset?
2.  Have you considered exploring alternative asymmetric loss functions beyond AL and linex?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the number of tanh terms in the warped GP models? Did you experiment with different values beyond 3?
4.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of using standard QE metrics for evaluating probabilistic models, beyond what is already discussed? Would metrics based on calibration curves be relevant here?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not familiar with Gaussian Processes.
*   The figures and tables are clear and informative.
*   The inclusion of related work on asymmetric loss functions and their applications in other fields is a nice addition.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Quality Estimation. The authors effectively demonstrate the importance of modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty and provide practical guidance for implementing probabilistic QE models. While there are some minor weaknesses, the strengths of the paper significantly outweigh them. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions raised above.
