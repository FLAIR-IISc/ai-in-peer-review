This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on modeling predictive uncertainty rather than simply point estimates. The primary contribution is a thorough investigation of different GP configurations – specifically Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and their impact on uncertainty calibration, evaluated using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD). A secondary contribution is the application of these probabilistic models to asymmetric risk scenarios in post-editing, demonstrating potential benefits through the use of asymmetric loss functions.

**Strengths:**

1. **Focus on Uncertainty is Valuable:** The paper addresses a crucial gap in QE research. While point estimates are common, understanding *confidence* in those estimates is essential for real-world deployment, particularly in decision-making workflows. This is a well-motivated and practically relevant direction.
2. **Rigorous Exploration of GP Configurations:** The authors systematically investigate different GP kernel choices (Matèrn) and likelihood modifications (Warped GPs), providing a detailed explanation of their theoretical underpinnings and practical considerations. This level of detail is commendable and helps justify the experimentation.
3. **Appropriate Evaluation Metric (NLPD):**  The use of NLPD is a strong point. Unlike traditional QE metrics, NLPD directly assesses the quality of the predictive distributions, aligning with the paper’s core focus on uncertainty.  The authors correctly point out that point estimate metrics fail to capture the differences in uncertainty modelling.
4. **Demonstration of Practical Application:**  The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios adds practical weight to the work. Illustrating how probabilistic predictions can be leveraged for more informed decision-making (e.g., varying the risk tolerance) is a valuable contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Method:** While the application to QE is sound, the core methods—GPs, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs—are well-established in the machine learning literature. The paper doesn’t introduce fundamentally new algorithmic components; rather, it applies existing techniques to a specific domain and demonstrates their usefulness. This reduces the overall impact.
2. **Inconclusive and Limited Experimental Scope:** The results are mixed and sometimes dataset-dependent. The performance gains from Matèrn kernels are not consistently observed, and the linex loss experiment in the pessimistic scenario shows questionable results (even labeled as inconclusive by the authors). Furthermore, the experiments are limited to a small set of features and datasets.  A more extensive empirical evaluation across more diverse datasets and feature sets would strengthen the conclusions.
3. **Overstated Claims of Superiority:**  The authors sometimes present improvements as more significant than they appear. While NLPD is a better metric, the actual improvements over baseline GP models are often marginal. The claim that these models are “better” needs to be tempered with acknowledgment of the limited and sometimes inconsistent results.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Uncertainty Estimation Methods:** The paper focuses exclusively on GPs. A comparison to simpler uncertainty estimation techniques (e.g., bootstrapping with SVR) would provide valuable context. Demonstrating that the complexity of GPs is justified by a significant performance gain over these simpler alternatives is crucial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. The linex loss in the pessimistic scenario yielded problematic results. Can you elaborate on the potential reasons for this, and what steps, if any, could be taken to address this issue?
2. Given that the core methods are well-established, what specific challenges in QE motivated the selection of these particular GP configurations (Matèrn and Warped GPs) over other potential approaches?
3.  While you mention limitations regarding feature sets, could you provide more detail on the sensitivity of the results to different feature combinations? Would adding more features alter the conclusions?
4.  Can you provide some insights as to why the performance differences are observed between the datasets? Are there inherent characteristics of the en-es dataset that lead to the observed differences in results?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is well-written and clearly presented. The references are comprehensive. However, the overall contribution feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. While the application of probabilistic modeling to QE is valuable, the paper needs to better address the limitations of the experimental results and justify the added complexity of the chosen methods.
