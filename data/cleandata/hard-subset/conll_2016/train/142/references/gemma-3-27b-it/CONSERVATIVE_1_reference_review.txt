This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for modeling uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors propose extensions to standard GPs – specifically, Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and evaluate their performance in terms of both point estimate accuracy and calibration of predictive distributions, using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a key metric. They also demonstrate a potential application of these uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios using tailored loss functions.

The primary contribution is a thorough empirical investigation of different GP configurations for QE, specifically focusing on how different kernel choices and the inclusion of a warping function impact both prediction accuracy *and* the calibration of uncertainty estimates. A secondary contribution is the demonstration of how these calibrated uncertainty estimates can be leveraged within a Bayes risk framework to optimize performance in scenarios with asymmetric costs. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Exploration of GP Variants:** The paper provides a detailed and systematic examination of different GP configurations (EQ kernel vs. Matèrn kernels, standard GP vs. Warped GP) within the context of QE. The authors appropriately revisit the theoretical foundations of these methods and connect them to the specific challenges of QE.
2. **Emphasis on Calibration & NLPD:** The focus on evaluating models based on their calibration (via NLPD) is a key strength. Traditional QE evaluation relies heavily on point estimate metrics (MAE, r), which, as the authors convincingly demonstrate, fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling. The adoption of NLPD provides a more nuanced and informative evaluation.
3. **Connection to Asymmetric Risk:** The exploration of asymmetric loss functions and the application of Bayes risk estimation is a valuable addition. This section demonstrates a clear potential application of calibrated uncertainty estimates and highlights the practical benefits of moving beyond point predictions.
4. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The technical details are explained in a concise and accessible manner, making it relatively easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methods:** While the paper provides a comprehensive empirical analysis, the individual methods employed (GPs, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD) are well-established within the machine learning literature. The core technical innovation is limited. The application *to* QE is valuable, but not a breakthrough in GP methodology itself.
2. **Overstated Claims of Generality:** The authors repeatedly emphasize the applicability of their methods to "any text regression task." This feels overly broad. The specific characteristics of QE (small datasets, feature engineering) likely play a significant role in the observed results. Extrapolating these findings to other tasks without further validation is premature.
3. **Inconclusive Results in Asymmetric Risk:** The results in Section 4 regarding asymmetric loss functions are mixed and, in the case of the linex loss, somewhat inconclusive. The authors themselves acknowledge that the linex results may be affected by the lower variance of the warped GPs. A more thorough investigation of the interplay between uncertainty estimation and asymmetric loss functions is needed.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Uncertainty Estimation Techniques:** While the authors justify their focus on GPs, they do not compare their approach to simpler uncertainty estimation methods like bootstrapping or ensemble methods (e.g., bagging) applied to SVR, which could provide a baseline for assessing the added value of the GP-based approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper indicates that NLPD is not a reliable metric for small datasets (en-es). What specific characteristics of the dataset contribute to this observation?  Are there alternative metrics that might be more suitable for evaluating uncertainty estimation in such scenarios?
2. The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are questionable. Can you elaborate on why the warped GP models perform poorly in this specific setting, and what steps could be taken to address this issue?
3. Given the established nature of the underlying methods, what specific aspects of the QE problem necessitated the use of a full GP framework, as opposed to simpler uncertainty estimation techniques applied to established QE models like SVR?
4. Can you provide more details on the computational cost of training and evaluating the different GP models, especially the Warped GPs with tanh functions? How does this compare to the cost of training and evaluating SVR models?



**Additional Comments:**

* The repeated use of "arguably" feels unnecessarily hedging.
* The references are comprehensive.
* The paper would benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of the study and directions for future research. Specifically, exploring the sensitivity of the results to different feature sets and language pairs would be valuable.
