## Review for "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on modeling and evaluating *uncertainty* in predictions, rather than solely focusing on point estimate accuracy. The authors argue that uncertainty estimates are crucial for real-world QE applications, particularly in decision-making scenarios like post-editing. They explore several GP extensions – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and propose using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than standard point-estimate metrics. They also demonstrate how well-calibrated predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios using asymmetric loss functions.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Emphasis on Uncertainty Calibration in QE:**  The paper effectively highlights the often-overlooked importance of well-calibrated uncertainty estimates in QE for practical applications, presenting a compelling argument for why point estimates alone are insufficient. This is a valuable contribution, shifting the focus beyond just prediction accuracy.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of GP Extensions:** The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation of different GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) within the context of QE. The results demonstrate the impact of these modeling choices on uncertainty calibration, as measured by NLPD.
3. **Demonstration of Asymmetric Loss Application:**  The paper showcases how probabilistic predictions can be effectively used in scenarios with asymmetric risks (e.g., differing costs for false positives vs. false negatives) using asymmetric loss functions, offering a practical application for the developed models.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation:** The introduction clearly and convincingly motivates the need for uncertainty modeling in QE, grounding the work in real-world use cases like post-editing workflows.  The scenario described with the human translator needing confidence levels is very relatable and underscores the practical importance of the problem.
2. **Thorough Exploration of GP Models:** The paper provides a solid overview of relevant GP concepts and extensions, making it accessible to readers familiar with machine learning but potentially new to GPs. The justification for choosing GPs over other probabilistic models (like those offering only point estimates) is well-articulated.
3. **Appropriate Evaluation Metric:** The adoption of NLPD as the primary evaluation metric is a significant strength.  The paper convincingly argues why NLPD is superior to standard point-estimate metrics for assessing the quality of probabilistic predictions and shows empirical evidence supporting this claim. The results clearly demonstrate that standard metrics *do not* capture the differences in uncertainty modeling achieved by different GP configurations.
4. **Practical Application with Asymmetric Loss:**  Demonstrating the usefulness of the probabilistic predictions through asymmetric loss functions adds practical value to the work. The exploration of both optimistic and pessimistic scenarios, and the discussion of limitations with the linex loss, demonstrate a nuanced understanding of the application.
5. **Well-Structured and Clearly Written:** The paper is well-organized, logically structured, and clearly written, making it easy to follow the technical details and understand the main contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of QE Features:**  The authors intentionally chose a limited feature set (17 features from QuEst) to ensure fair comparison across language pairs. While understandable, this limits the potential performance gains achievable and might not fully represent the state-of-the-art in QE. The gains observed might be limited by the features themselves, not solely the uncertainty modeling.
2. **Dataset Size Concerns:** The results show inconsistencies across different datasets, particularly with the en-es dataset. The authors correctly identify this as potentially due to overfitting. The conclusions drawn about the reliability of NLL are thus somewhat tentative and warrant further investigation with larger datasets.
3. **Linex Loss Analysis:** The inconclusive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, and the authors’ speculation about the role of variance, suggest a more in-depth analysis is needed. The potential bias of the loss function towards models with higher variance is a significant finding that requires further investigation. It would be beneficial to explore other loss functions or regularization techniques to address this issue.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Modern QE Approaches:** While the focus on GPs is justified, the paper would benefit from a brief discussion of how the results compare to more recent QE approaches using neural networks, even if direct comparison isn't feasible.  Acknowledging the current landscape of QE research would provide valuable context.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the chosen feature set and discuss how the results might change with a more comprehensive feature set?
2.  Given the overfitting concerns with the smaller en-es dataset, do you have plans to evaluate the models on larger datasets to confirm the reliability of the NLL metric?
3.  Can you provide further insights into the potential reasons for the inconclusive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario? Are there specific strategies you considered or plan to explore to mitigate the bias towards high-variance models?
4.  How do you envision your approach being integrated with more recent QE systems based on neural networks? Are there any potential synergies or challenges in combining GP-based uncertainty estimation with neural QE models?

**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is a valuable contribution to the field of QE, emphasizing the importance of uncertainty calibration and providing practical guidance for building more robust and reliable QE systems.
* The code used for the experiments should be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The work is well-motivated, technically sound, and presents a compelling argument for the importance of uncertainty modeling in QE. While some weaknesses exist, they are acknowledged by the authors and do not significantly detract from the overall quality and contribution of the work. The paper’s focus on a critical yet often-overlooked aspect of QE makes it a valuable addition to the research community.
