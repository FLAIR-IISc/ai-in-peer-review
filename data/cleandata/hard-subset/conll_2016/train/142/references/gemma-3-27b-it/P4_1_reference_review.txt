This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on modeling predictive uncertainty. The authors motivate the use of uncertainty estimates for practical MT workflows, particularly in post-editing scenarios. They investigate different GP extensions – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and propose evaluating models using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric, alongside standard point estimate metrics. The paper also explores applying these models in asymmetric risk scenarios using asymmetric loss functions.

The primary contribution of this work is the empirical evaluation of different GP models and uncertainty estimation techniques within the QE task, showcasing the benefits of using NLPD as an evaluation metric. While GPs have been applied in QE previously, the systematic comparison of kernel choices (EQ vs. Matèrn) and the incorporation of Warped GPs, coupled with a strong emphasis on assessing uncertainty via NLPD, provides a valuable contribution. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating the applicability of asymmetric loss functions to QE, allowing for a more nuanced decision-making process based on different risk preferences. The extensive experimental setup with multiple language pairs and datasets adds to the robustness of the findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Focus on Uncertainty:** The paper correctly identifies the limitations of relying solely on point estimates in practical QE applications and persuasively argues for the importance of modeling uncertainty. This is a crucial aspect often overlooked in QE research.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive empirical evaluation, comparing different GP models (EQ, Matèrn, Warped GPs) across multiple datasets (en-es, fr-en, en-de). This provides strong evidence to support their claims about the relative performance of different approaches.
3. **NLPD as a Metric:** The advocacy for using NLPD as an evaluation metric is well-justified. The authors demonstrate that standard point estimate metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, highlighting the importance of evaluating probabilistic models using appropriate metrics.
4. **Asymmetric Risk Analysis:** The exploration of asymmetric loss functions and their application in QE scenarios is a valuable contribution, demonstrating the potential for tailoring QE models to specific practical needs and risk preferences.
5. **Detailed Technical Explanation:** The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the underlying theoretical concepts, including Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs. This allows readers to understand the technical details of the proposed approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methods:** While the application of GPs to QE is not new, the specific methods explored (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) are well-established within the GP literature. The novelty lies primarily in their application and comparison within the QE context, rather than introducing fundamentally new techniques.
2. **Overreliance on Existing Features:** The use of a fixed feature set (QuEst) limits the potential for improving QE performance. While the authors justify this choice by focusing on uncertainty modeling rather than state-of-the-art point estimate accuracy, it potentially restricts the overall impact of the study.
3. **Inconclusive Results with Linex Loss:** The results obtained with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and require further investigation. The authors acknowledge this but do not offer a clear explanation for the observed behavior. The explanation provided, linking to the lower variance of warped GPs, is plausible but requires further validation.
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper does not explicitly discuss the computational cost associated with different GP models, particularly Warped GPs with multiple tanh terms. This is an important consideration for practical applications.
5. **Dataset Size Sensitivity:** The observations regarding the differences in performance between warped functions depending on dataset size raises a concern about the generalizability of the findings. It suggests that the optimal model choice might be heavily influenced by the amount of available data.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the computational cost associated with training and predicting using the different GP models, particularly the Warped GPs?
2. You mention that the en-es dataset is smaller than the others. How did you determine the number of tanh terms to use in the Warped GP for each dataset, and did you perform any sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of this parameter on performance?
3. The linex loss results in the pessimistic scenario were inconclusive. Could you elaborate on any further analyses you conducted to investigate this phenomenon, and what potential explanations you considered beyond the variance argument?
4. How might the findings of this work generalize to other NLP tasks where modeling uncertainty is important, such as neural machine translation or text summarization?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are comprehensive and well-formatted.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise.
*   The figures and tables are informative and well-presented.
*   It would be beneficial to include a discussion of the limitations of GPs in the context of QE, such as their scalability to very large datasets.
