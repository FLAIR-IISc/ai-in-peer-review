1. Paper studies Gaussian Processes for QE to provide uncertainty estimates, comparing Matern kernels and Warped GPs using NLPD and asymmetric risk evaluation.  
2. Shows NLPD captures differences in probabilistic QE models that point metrics (MAE, Pearson) miss.  
3. Adapts Warped GPs to model non-Gaussian QE targets (post-editing time) for better-calibrated predictions.  
4. Examines asymmetric risk scenarios and demonstrates using GP predictive distributions to optimize under cost asymmetry.  
5. Strong motivation and clear problem framing tied to practical post-editing workflows.  
6. Rigorous experimental setup across en-es, fr-en, en-de datasets with 10-fold cross-validation.  
7. Thorough exploration and explanation of GP variants (Matern kernels, Warped GPs).  
8. Empirical results convincingly show the benefit of NLPD over point-estimate metrics.  
9. Asymmetric risk analysis is practically relevant and shows applicability to real-world QE decisions.  
10. Limited feature set (17 QuEst features) may cap achievable performance and weaken baseline comparisons.  
11. Potential overfitting and dataset-size issues (notably en-es) need more discussion and mitigation (e.g., regularization).  
12. Linex loss results in pessimistic scenarios are inconclusive and may behave counterintuitively with Warped GPs.  
13. Lack of comparison to other probabilistic approaches (e.g., ensembles, calibrated SVR) limits context.  
14. No qualitative error analysis showing when/why models fail and how uncertainty helps.  
15. Question: provide more detail on en-es dataset traits and whether regularization (e.g., increased GP noise) was tried.  
16. Question: clarify why Linex loss underperforms with Warped GPs and whether tuning asymmetry or other factors explain it.  
17. Question: consider calibrating non-probabilistic QE models (Platt scaling/isotonic) and compare them on NLPD and asymmetric risk.  
18. Question: give concrete examples of QE decisions where uncertainty estimates improve outcomes versus point predictions.  
19. Writing is generally clear and well-structured.  
20. Related work should be expanded to include recent advances in uncertainty quantification beyond GPs.  
21. Recommend releasing code and/or data to facilitate reproducibility.  
22. Overall: strong contribution advocating uncertainty estimation in QE; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
