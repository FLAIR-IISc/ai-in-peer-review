## Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) in Machine Translation (MT), with a focus on modeling predictive uncertainty. The authors argue that point estimates are insufficient for real-world MT workflows, especially those involving human post-editing, and that well-calibrated uncertainty estimates can improve decision-making. They explore different GP configurations (kernels, likelihoods, warping functions) and propose using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than standard point-estimate metrics.  They further demonstrate how these uncertainty estimates can be leveraged in scenarios with asymmetric risk using tailored loss functions.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of the Limitations of Standard QE Evaluation:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that commonly used point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in the quality of uncertainty modeling offered by different GP configurations. This is a valuable insight for the QE community.
2. **Comprehensive Exploration of GP Configurations for QE:** The authors systematically explore various GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) and provide a practical guide for selecting appropriate configurations for QE tasks.
3. **Application of Asymmetric Loss Functions to QE:** The work highlights the potential of leveraging predictive distributions in scenarios where the costs of over- and under-estimation are different, providing a concrete example of how uncertainty information can be practically applied.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of relying solely on point estimates in practical MT workflows and convincingly argues for the importance of modeling uncertainty. The post-editing scenario provides a compelling real-world example.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct extensive experiments across multiple language pairs, using well-established datasets and a reasonable feature set.  The 10-fold cross-validation setup enhances the robustness of the results.
3. **Sound Methodological Choices:** The selection of GPs as a probabilistic modeling framework is justified, given their strengths in regression and ability to provide uncertainty estimates. The exploration of different kernel functions and warping functions demonstrates a good understanding of the GP literature.
4. **Insightful Discussion of Evaluation Metrics:** The paper's advocacy for NLPD as a more informative metric for evaluating probabilistic QE models is a significant contribution. The empirical results support this claim, showing that NLPD *does* differentiate models where point-estimate metrics do not.
5. **Practical Relevance:** The application of asymmetric loss functions and the discussion of scenarios with different risk profiles demonstrate the potential for translating uncertainty estimates into practical improvements in MT workflows.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the application of GPs to QE isn't new (referenced correctly), the specific GP extensions explored (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) are established techniques. The novelty lies more in the systematic exploration *within the QE context* and the analysis of uncertainty modeling, rather than in the algorithms themselves.  This isn’t necessarily a fatal flaw, but needs acknowledgement.
2. **Inconclusive Results for Linex Loss in Pessimistic Scenario:** The paper acknowledges that the results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and potentially counterintuitive (penalizing models with lower variance).  This suggests a limitation in the applicability of this loss function or a need for further investigation into its behavior in the QE context. This weakness is identified by the authors, which is good.
3. **Potential for Overfitting in Small Datasets:** The authors acknowledge the possibility of overfitting in the smaller en-es dataset, and this is reflected in the inconsistent results across metrics. More thorough investigation into the robustness of the models on small datasets would be beneficial.
4. **Feature Set Limitation:** While the authors justify their choice of a standard feature set for comparability, it is worth acknowledging that the performance of QE models is heavily reliant on feature engineering. Exploring the impact of more sophisticated or language-specific features could potentially lead to further improvements.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the inconclusive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario: Do you have any hypotheses as to why models with lower variance are penalized?  Could this be related to the calibration of the uncertainty estimates themselves? 
2.  You mention the potential for overfitting in the en-es dataset. Have you explored techniques to mitigate this, such as regularization or data augmentation?
3.  While the focus is on GPs, could you briefly discuss the challenges of adapting other probabilistic models (e.g., Bayesian Neural Networks) to provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates for QE?
4.  The experiments focus on post-editing time as the target variable.  Would the findings generalize to other QE metrics, such as direct assessment (DA) scores?




**Additional Comments:**

* The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
* The references are comprehensive and up-to-date.
* The inclusion of example code or a link to a repository would further enhance the reproducibility of the results.




**Overall Assessment:**

I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of Quality Estimation. While the core methodological components may not be entirely novel, the thorough empirical evaluation, insightful analysis of uncertainty modeling, and demonstration of practical applications make it a worthwhile read for researchers and practitioners in the MT and QE communities. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
