This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), specifically focusing on modelling predictive uncertainty rather than just point estimates. The primary contribution is a systematic evaluation of different GP extensions – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – alongside a strong argument for using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate metric than standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) when assessing probabilistic QE models. A secondary, but interesting contribution, is the demonstration of how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios, exemplified with applications to post-editing workflows.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Focus on Uncertainty:** The paper tackles a crucial, yet often overlooked, aspect of QE: uncertainty estimation. While QE has matured in terms of point prediction accuracy, understanding *when* a prediction is reliable is vital for real-world applications, particularly those involving human-in-the-loop decision-making. This is a bold and important direction.
2. **Strong Methodological Exploration:** The authors don’t just apply a single GP variant; they systematically explore different kernel choices (Matèrn) and model extensions (Warped GPs), providing a comparative analysis. The choice to focus on GPs given their inherent probabilistic nature is well justified.
3. **Convincing Argument for NLPD:** The demonstration that standard QE metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modelling is compelling. The advocacy for NLPD is well-supported by the experimental results, and highlights a significant flaw in current QE evaluation practices.
4. **Practical Relevance through Asymmetric Risk:** The inclusion of asymmetric risk scenarios and the application of Bayes risk estimation elevates the work beyond purely theoretical exploration. This demonstrates a clear understanding of how QE models will be used in practice and grounds the research in real-world needs.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The background on GPs is concise yet sufficient, and the experimental setup is clearly described.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Asymmetric Loss Experiments:** The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and raise concerns. The authors acknowledge this, but a more thorough investigation of this phenomenon (perhaps with different parameter settings or losses) would strengthen the analysis.
2. **Dependence on Existing Feature Set:** The paper relies on a pre-defined feature set (QuEst). While reasonable given the focus on probabilistic modelling, it limits the exploration of potential feature interactions with the proposed GP models. It doesn’t explore whether new features could specifically help capture uncertainty.
3. **Incomplete Validation of Warping Function Benefits:** While Warped GPs consistently outperform standard GPs, the comparison of different warping functions (tanh vs. log) feels somewhat superficial. A more detailed analysis of *why* the tanh-based warping function performs better would be valuable. Is it capturing a specific characteristic of the QE data?
4. **Dataset Size Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the smaller size of the en-es dataset and its potential impact on overfitting. A larger dataset would provide more robust validation of the findings.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Modern Neural QE Models:** While the focus on GPs is understandable, a brief discussion of how the proposed methods compare to state-of-the-art neural QE models (which often incorporate attention mechanisms) would provide valuable context. A head-to-head comparison, even if limited, would be useful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you elaborate on the reasons why the linex loss performs poorly in the pessimistic scenario? Is it related to the variance of the predictive distributions, as suggested in the paper, and if so, can you explore alternative loss functions that are less sensitive to variance?
2. Did you experiment with feature selection or engineering specifically designed to improve the performance of the Warped GPs? If not, do you believe this would be a worthwhile avenue for future research?
3. How do you envision the integration of the proposed uncertainty estimates into a real-world post-editing workflow? What specific decision thresholds or policies would be appropriate based on the uncertainty estimates?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper demonstrates a clear understanding of the challenges and opportunities in QE. While the experimental evaluation could be further strengthened, the novelty of the approach, the strong arguments for using NLPD, and the practical relevance of the work make it a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, as the paper introduces a significant new perspective on QE and opens up exciting avenues for future research. The methodological imperfections are outweighed by the conceptual leap and potential impact.
