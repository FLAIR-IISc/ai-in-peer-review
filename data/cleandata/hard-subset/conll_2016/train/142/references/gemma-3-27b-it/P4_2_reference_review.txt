This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) in Machine Translation (MT), with a focus on modeling and evaluating prediction uncertainty. The authors argue that point estimates are insufficient for decision-making in MT workflows and propose leveraging the probabilistic outputs of GPs, specifically investigating kernel choices (Matèrn kernels) and likelihood transformations (Warped GPs). The primary contribution lies in the empirical evaluation demonstrating that differences in uncertainty modeling *are* detectable using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric, a result not apparent when using standard point-estimate metrics like MAE or Pearson’s r. A secondary contribution is the exploration of asymmetric loss functions in the context of QE, and how the well-calibrated predictive distributions from GPs can be used for Bayes risk estimation.

**Strengths:**

1. **Focus on Uncertainty Modeling is Timely and Important:** The paper addresses a crucial gap in QE research. While QE has traditionally focused on improving point prediction accuracy, this work rightly highlights the importance of quantifying uncertainty for real-world applications where decisions are made based on QE scores. This is particularly compelling given the increasing integration of MT into larger workflows.
2. **Thorough Investigation of GP Extensions:** The authors systematically investigate relevant extensions to standard GPs—Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs—and provide a clear rationale for their suitability to the QE task. The explanations of these concepts are accessible and well-referenced.
3. **Strong Empirical Validation with NLPD:** The use of NLPD as a primary evaluation metric is a significant strength. The authors convincingly demonstrate that NLPD can differentiate between models that appear similar based on point-estimate metrics, reinforcing the value of modeling predictive uncertainty. The cross-validation setup is also solid.
4. **Exploration of Asymmetric Risk Scenarios:** The application of asymmetric loss functions and Bayes risk estimation adds practical relevance to the work. This demonstrates a clear path for leveraging the probabilistic outputs of QE models in realistic scenarios with varying costs associated with different types of errors.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in GP Application:** While the application of GPs to QE isn’t entirely new (referenced in the paper), the core idea of employing and evaluating GPs *specifically* for their uncertainty modeling capabilities is not groundbreaking. The novelty resides more in the systematic empirical evaluation, rather than a fundamentally new algorithmic contribution.
2. **Inconclusive Results for Linex Loss:** The results related to the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and potentially misleading. The authors acknowledge this and attribute it to the lower variance of the warped GP models. This suggests a limitation in the applicability of this particular loss function within the proposed framework. Further investigation is needed to understand this behavior.
3. **Feature Set Limitations:** While the authors acknowledge the use of a standard feature set, it is somewhat limiting. The WMT QE shared tasks have shown substantial gains from using richer feature sets. The argument that these are "resource-intensive and language-dependent" could be strengthened with a brief exploration of the impact of adding a few key features.
4. **Presentation of Results:** Table 2 could benefit from clearer presentation. The lack of statistical significance testing (e.g., p-values) makes it difficult to assess the robustness of the observed differences.


**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why the linex loss performs poorly in the pessimistic scenario? Is it solely due to the lower variance of the warped GPs, or are there other factors at play? Would a different method for controlling the variance (e.g., scaling the predictive variance) improve performance with the linex loss?
2.  Given the limitations of the standard feature set, did you perform any preliminary experiments with a slightly expanded feature set (e.g., including source sentence length) to assess its impact on the results? If so, what were the findings?
3.  You mention that the dataset size might explain some of the discrepancies observed between the en-es dataset and the others. Could you provide more details on the effect of dataset size on the performance of different kernels and warping functions? Were any experiments performed to directly investigate this relationship?
4.  Regarding the use of NLPD: are there any known limitations or biases associated with this metric that might be relevant to the QE task?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized.
*   The references are comprehensive and up-to-date.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the different kernel and likelihood choices and their theoretical properties in a concise manner.
*   A visual representation of the different loss functions (AL and linex) would be helpful for readers unfamiliar with these concepts.
