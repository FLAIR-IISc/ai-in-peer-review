## Peer Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) in Machine Translation, focusing on modeling and evaluating *uncertainty* in predictions, rather than just point estimates. The authors argue that uncertainty estimation is crucial for real-world QE applications, particularly in scenarios like post-editing where decisions need to be made based on confidence levels. They explore variations of GPs – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and propose using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) for assessing the quality of probabilistic QE models. Finally, they demonstrate how predictive distributions can be used in asymmetric risk scenarios via Bayes risk estimation with asymmetric loss functions.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Highlighting the importance of uncertainty quantification in QE:** The paper convincingly argues that relying solely on point estimates is insufficient for practical QE applications and motivates a shift towards probabilistic modeling. This is a valuable perspective often overlooked in QE research.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation of GP variations for QE:** The paper provides a thorough exploration of different GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) within the QE context, offering a practical guide for researchers looking to employ these models.
3. **Demonstration of asymmetric loss application in QE:**  The authors successfully apply the concepts of asymmetric loss functions and Bayes risk estimation to QE scenarios, showcasing a potential pathway to improve decision-making in real-world translation workflows.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of point-estimate based QE and establishes a compelling need for uncertainty quantification, particularly within the context of human-in-the-loop translation workflows. The post-editing scenario is a well-chosen example.
2. **Thorough Exploration of GP Models:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of GPs and systematically explore relevant extensions for QE, providing a valuable resource for researchers in the field. The explanations of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs are clear and concise.
3. **Justification for NLPD as an Evaluation Metric:** The paper persuasively argues for the superiority of NLPD over traditional metrics when evaluating probabilistic models, backed up by experimental results showing that point estimate metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling.
4. **Practical Application to Asymmetric Risk:** The demonstration of how to leverage predictive distributions with asymmetric loss functions provides a concrete example of the benefits of uncertainty estimation in real-world QE applications, enhancing the paper’s practical relevance.
5. **Well-Written and Structured:** The paper is generally well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow, despite dealing with complex statistical concepts.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Experimental Scope:**  While the experiments cover three language pairs, they are limited to a single MT system and a fixed feature set.  The generalizability of the findings to other MT systems, data sources, and feature configurations remains unclear.  More comprehensive experiments are needed to strengthen the claims.
2. **Over-reliance on Cross-Validation:** The use of 10-fold cross-validation, while standard, doesn't provide a clear picture of out-of-sample performance on a held-out test set.  A dedicated test set would offer more robust evaluation.
3. **Inconclusive Results with Linex Loss:** The results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are described as inconclusive. The authors attribute this to the lower variance of the warped GP models, but this requires further investigation and justification.  The claim that the linex loss might be biased towards models with high variance is intriguing but needs more support.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Uncertainty Estimation Techniques:** The paper focuses solely on GPs. A comparison to simpler uncertainty estimation techniques (e.g., bootstrapping SVR models as mentioned in the introduction) would provide valuable context and demonstrate the added benefit of using GPs.
5. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper briefly mentions the scalability of kernel methods but does not provide any details regarding the computational cost of training and applying the different GP models.  This information would be useful for practitioners considering these models in real-world deployments.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the inconclusive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario: Could you provide more details on the variance analysis of the different models? Are there any diagnostic plots or statistical tests you can present to support your claim that the linex loss is biased towards models with higher variance?
2.  How sensitive are your results to the choice of the tanh function parameters in the Warped GP models? Did you perform any sensitivity analysis or hyperparameter optimization beyond what is described in the paper?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with training and applying the different GP models, particularly in terms of training time and memory usage?
4.  Would you expect the observed trends to remain the same if other features are used, or if different MT systems are considered? Is there a plan to evaluate the proposed method in more diverse scenarios?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are well-organized and comprehensive.
*   The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of the limitations of GPs (e.g., computational complexity for large datasets) and potential solutions (e.g., sparse GP approximations).
*   Consider including a table summarizing the key differences between the different GP variants explored in the paper.



Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of QE. While there are some limitations, the strengths of the work outweigh the weaknesses. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions and concerns raised above.
