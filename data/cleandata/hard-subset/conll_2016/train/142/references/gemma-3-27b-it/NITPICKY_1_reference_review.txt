This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on obtaining well-calibrated uncertainty estimates alongside point predictions. The authors explore various GP extensions – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and propose evaluating these models using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) alongside standard QE metrics. They further demonstrate the utility of these uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios via application of asymmetric loss functions.

The primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical evaluation of different GP configurations for QE, alongside a strong advocacy for the use of NLPD as a more informative evaluation metric than traditional point-estimate based measures. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating the practical relevance of probabilistic QE through the application of asymmetric loss functions, tailored to specific post-editing scenarios. The exploration of Warped GPs within the QE context also represents a worthwhile extension of existing work.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The paper presents a rigorous and well-structured experimental evaluation. Testing across multiple language pairs and employing cross-validation strengthens the findings. The consistent reporting of multiple metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r, NLL, NLPD) allows for a nuanced assessment of the models.
2. **Advocacy for NLPD:** The authors convincingly argue for the superiority of NLPD over traditional metrics in evaluating probabilistic QE models. The results clearly demonstrate that NLPD captures differences in uncertainty modeling that are missed by point-estimate metrics. This is a significant insight for the QE community.
3. **Practical Relevance:** Connecting uncertainty estimates to asymmetric risk scenarios is a valuable contribution. The demonstration of how predictive distributions can be leveraged with asymmetric loss functions provides a concrete application and highlights the potential benefits of this approach.
4. **Thorough Background & Methodology:** The paper provides a solid background on GPs, relevant kernels, and likelihoods. The methodological details are clearly explained, facilitating reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** While the experimental evaluation is thorough, the core methodological contributions (applying GPs and Warped GPs to QE) are not entirely novel. These techniques have been used in other regression contexts. The primary innovation lies in the specific application to QE and the emphasis on NLPD, but the fundamental ideas are well-established.
2. **Overly Detailed Discussion of Minor Implementation Choices:** The paper spends considerable space detailing choices regarding the number of tanh terms in the warping function and specific parameter settings. While thoroughness is appreciated, the significance of these specific choices isn't adequately justified, and the extensive discussion detracts from the broader message. It feels overly meticulous.
3. **Inconsistent Findings & Over-Interpretation:** The results regarding Matèrn kernels are inconclusive, yet the authors suggest they are “promising candidates”. The linex loss results in the pessimistic scenario are inconsistent and lead to an unsatisfying conclusion that the loss itself might be problematic rather than the models’ behavior. A more cautious interpretation of these results is needed.
4. **Formatting & Presentation Issues:** There are a number of minor formatting inconsistencies throughout the paper (e.g., inconsistent spacing after punctuation, non-uniform use of italics, equation numbering). While not critical, these detract from the professional presentation of the work.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates the benefits of NLPD, it lacks a detailed error analysis to understand *why* certain models perform better in specific scenarios.  Investigating the types of errors made by different models could provide valuable insights.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. You mention that standard QE datasets are usually small (thousands of instances). However, the computational cost of GP training can be significant, even for moderately sized datasets. Can you elaborate on the computational resources required for your experiments and any techniques used to mitigate computational challenges?
2. The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive. Could you provide further analysis of the predictive distributions generated by the different models in this setting? For example, are the predictive variances consistently low, preventing the loss function from effectively penalizing underestimates?
3. While you argue for the importance of NLPD, its interpretation can be less intuitive than traditional metrics like MAE. Could you provide guidance on how to interpret NLPD values in a practical QE context? What constitutes a "good" or "bad" NLPD score?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
*   The writing style is generally clear and concise, although the level of detail regarding implementation choices could be reduced.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between the different GP models evaluated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a solid contribution to the field of QE, particularly in advocating for a more nuanced evaluation of probabilistic models using NLPD. However, the incremental novelty, overly detailed discussion of minor points, and some inconsistencies in the results prevent me from strongly recommending acceptance. While the work is valuable, it falls short of making a truly groundbreaking contribution. I recommend **rejection** with encouragement to resubmit after addressing the weaknesses outlined above, specifically focusing on refining the interpretation of results and streamlining the presentation.
