## Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

**Summary:** This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on the ability of GPs to provide uncertainty estimates alongside point predictions. The authors explore different GP kernel choices (Matern kernels) and extensions (Warped GPs) and evaluate them both intrinsically (using Negative Log Predictive Density – NLPD) and extrinsically in an asymmetric risk scenario. They argue that well-calibrated uncertainty estimates are crucial for real-world QE applications, such as informing post-editing workflows.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of NLPD’s Value in QE:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) fail to capture differences in the quality of uncertainty estimates produced by different GP models.  The use of NLPD highlights the importance of evaluating probabilistic QE models based on their full predictive distributions, not just their point predictions.
2. **Application of Warped GPs to QE:** Adapting Warped GPs, previously less explored in QE, shows promise in modelling the non-Gaussian nature of QE targets (post-editing time). This opens up avenues for better calibrated predictions in a field often relying on assumptions about data distribution.
3. **Exploration of Asymmetric Risk Scenarios:** The paper provides a valuable framework for considering real-world QE applications where the cost of underestimation versus overestimation differs, and demonstrates how the predictive distributions from GPs can be leveraged to optimize performance under these constraints.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Clear Problem Definition:** The paper effectively motivates the need for uncertainty estimation in QE, grounding it in a practical post-editing scenario. The explanation of why point estimates are insufficient for decision-making is compelling.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The use of multiple datasets (en-es, fr-en, en-de) with established QE benchmarks allows for robust evaluation and generalizability claims. The 10-fold cross-validation ensures reliable results.
3. **Thorough Exploration of GP Variants:** The authors systematically investigate several GP extensions – Matern kernels and Warped GPs – providing a comprehensive comparative analysis.  The detailed explanations of these extensions are helpful for readers unfamiliar with the GP literature.
4. **Convincing Results Demonstrating NLPD's Benefit:**  The results consistently show NLPD revealing differences between models where point estimate metrics do not.  This strongly supports the authors’ argument that NLPD is a more suitable metric for evaluating probabilistic QE models.
5. **Practical Relevance of Asymmetric Risk Analysis:** The analysis of asymmetric risk scenarios and the use of Bayesian risk estimation provides a clear pathway for applying the developed models to real-world problems.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Feature Set:** While the authors acknowledge it, the use of a relatively small feature set (17 features from QuEst) might limit the potential performance gains achievable with the probabilistic models. Stronger baselines using more comprehensive features could provide a more challenging benchmark.
2. **Overfitting Concerns & Dataset Size:**  The discrepancies observed in the NLPD results across datasets (particularly en-es) suggest potential overfitting issues, especially with smaller datasets. The authors acknowledge this, but a more in-depth discussion of overfitting mitigation strategies (e.g., regularization) would be beneficial.
3. **Linex Loss Interpretation & Limitations:** The results using the Linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and even suggest potentially counterintuitive behaviour. The authors touch on this, but a deeper investigation into *why* the Linex loss performs poorly when combined with warped GPs is warranted. Is it a sensitivity to variance, as suggested, or is there a more fundamental issue?
4. **Limited Comparison to Other Probabilistic Models:** The paper focuses solely on GPs.  A brief comparison to other probabilistic models commonly used in QE, even if they require more effort to calibrate (e.g., ensemble methods with bootstrapping), could provide further context and justification for the focus on GPs.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are compelling, a qualitative error analysis of cases where the models perform poorly, and how uncertainty estimates can help identify these cases, would strengthen the paper's practical impact.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the discrepancies in NLPD results across datasets (especially en-es), could you provide further details about the characteristics of this dataset that might make it more prone to overfitting? Have you experimented with regularization techniques (e.g., increasing the noise variance in the GP) to mitigate this issue?
2.  Could you elaborate on the potential reasons why the Linex loss performed poorly in the pessimistic scenario with the warped GPs? Is the low variance of the predictive distributions a primary factor, or are there other contributing elements?  Would exploring different parameterizations of the Linex loss (e.g., adjusting the asymmetry parameter) be worthwhile?
3.  You mention that other common QE models (like SVR) cannot directly provide uncertainty estimates. Have you considered techniques for calibrating the outputs of these models to approximate probabilistic predictions (e.g., Platt scaling or isotonic regression)? How would the performance of these calibrated models compare to your GP-based approach in terms of NLPD and asymmetric risk scenarios?
4.  Could you provide examples of specific QE decisions (e.g., post-editing vs. translation from scratch) where the uncertainty estimates from your models demonstrably lead to better outcomes compared to using only point predictions?




**Additional Comments:**

* The writing is generally clear and well-structured.
* The related work section could be expanded to include more recent advances in uncertainty quantification in machine learning, beyond the cited GP literature.
* Consider providing code and/or data to facilitate reproducibility.



**Overall Assessment:** This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Quality Estimation. The authors convincingly demonstrate the importance of uncertainty estimation and provide a compelling case for using GPs and NLPD for evaluating probabilistic QE models. While there are some weaknesses, they are outweighed by the strengths of the work, and I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the questions and concerns raised above.
