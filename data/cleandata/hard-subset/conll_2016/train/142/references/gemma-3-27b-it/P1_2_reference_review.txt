## Peer Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE) in Machine Translation (MT), with a focus on leveraging the inherent uncertainty estimates provided by GPs. The authors argue that point estimates alone are insufficient for real-world MT workflows, and that well-calibrated uncertainty estimates are crucial for decision-making (e.g., determining whether to post-edit or discard a translation). They explore different GP kernel configurations (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) and evaluation metrics (Negative Log Predictive Density - NLPD) and demonstrate how these can be useful in scenarios with asymmetric risk.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Emphasis on Uncertainty Quantification in QE:** The paper makes a strong and timely argument for moving beyond point estimates in QE, highlighting the practical benefits of uncertainty awareness for downstream tasks. This framing is a valuable contribution to the field.
2. **Empirical Evaluation of GP Extensions for QE:** The paper provides a comprehensive empirical evaluation of different GP configurations (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) in the context of QE, using multiple datasets and comparing performance based on both traditional point estimate metrics and the more suitable NLPD metric.
3. **Demonstration of Asymmetric Risk Application:**  The authors effectively illustrate how probabilistic QE predictions can be utilized in scenarios with asymmetric risk, by applying asymmetric loss functions and showing improvements in performance.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Relevance:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of traditional QE approaches and convincingly motivates the need for uncertainty estimation, grounding the work in a practical application context (post-editing workflows).
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The use of multiple datasets (en-es, fr-en, en-de) and a consistent feature set provides a solid foundation for the empirical evaluation. The 10-fold cross-validation adds robustness to the results.
3. **Sound Methodological Choices:** Focusing on GPs is justified given their ability to provide uncertainty estimates and their suitability for low-resource QE scenarios. The exploration of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs represents reasonable extensions to the standard GP framework.
4. **Appropriate Evaluation Metrics:** The inclusion of NLPD alongside traditional metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) is a key strength. The paper correctly points out that NLPD is more sensitive to the quality of uncertainty estimates.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The inclusion of related work helps contextualize the contributions.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the application of GPs to QE is not new, the specific extensions explored (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) are well-established techniques in the GP literature. The novelty lies primarily in the application *to* QE, rather than in the development of new algorithmic components. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of this point.
2. **Inconclusive Results for Asymmetric Risk with Linex Loss:** The authors find that the linex loss performs poorly in the pessimistic scenario, and suggest this may be due to the warped GPs' reduced variance. This is a potentially significant finding, but the explanation is somewhat speculative and requires further investigation.  The results with linex loss in the pessimistic settings are not convincing.
3. **Feature Set Limitation:** While the authors justify using a standard feature set for cross-dataset comparison, it is acknowledged that more advanced feature sets exist.  Acknowledging the potential impact of this limitation on absolute performance would be beneficial. The focus on comparing uncertainty modeling approaches is appropriate, but it's important to recognize this constraint.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Alternative Uncertainty Estimation Techniques:** The paper focuses exclusively on GPs. A brief discussion of other uncertainty estimation techniques applicable to QE (e.g., bootstrapping with SVR, Bayesian Neural Networks) and a justification for why they were not considered would strengthen the work.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the potential reasons why the linex loss performs poorly in the pessimistic scenario with the warped GPs? Is the reduced variance the primary factor, or could other aspects of the warped posterior distribution be contributing?
2.  Given that the results with the log warping function sometimes perform comparably to the tanh-based warping function, especially on the smaller dataset, could you provide more insight into the trade-offs between model complexity and performance?
3.  The paper highlights the limitations of point estimate metrics for evaluating uncertainty estimates.  Are there other evaluation metrics, beyond NLPD, that you considered or believe might be useful for assessing the calibration of probabilistic QE models?
4.  You mention the potential for applying these methods to other text regression tasks. Could you briefly discuss the challenges and adaptations required for applying this approach to a different domain (e.g., sentiment analysis, readability assessment)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Figure 1 depicting the asymmetric loss functions is helpful.
*   The reference list is comprehensive.
*   Minor editing for clarity and conciseness would be beneficial. (e.g. removing redundant phrasing)



Overall, this is a solid and well-motivated paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Quality Estimation. While the core methodology may not be entirely novel, the focus on uncertainty quantification, the thorough empirical evaluation, and the demonstration of practical applications are commendable.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
