## Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

**Summary:**

This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on the benefits of modeling predictive uncertainty rather than relying solely on point estimates. The authors explore extensions to standard GPs, including Matern kernels and warped GPs, and evaluate their performance in terms of both intrinsic uncertainty metrics (NLL, NLPD) and downstream application in asymmetric risk scenarios (optimistic/pessimistic post-editing). The key argument is that well-calibrated uncertainty estimates are crucial for real-world QE applications, enabling more informed decision-making in MT workflows.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical demonstration of the limitations of point-estimate metrics:** The paper convincingly shows that standard QE evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling achieved through different GP configurations. This is a significant finding, highlighting a flaw in common QE evaluation practices.
2. **Comprehensive exploration of GP extensions for QE:** The work thoroughly investigates the application of Matern kernels and warped GPs to QE, providing valuable insights into their effectiveness and practical considerations.
3. **Illustrative application of uncertainty in asymmetric risk scenarios:**  The demonstration of how predictive distributions can be leveraged in optimistic/pessimistic post-editing scenarios provides a compelling practical motivation for pursuing uncertainty-aware QE models.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation and Clear Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the need for uncertainty estimates in QE, especially within the context of practical MT workflows. The post-editing scenario is a well-chosen example that effectively illustrates the benefits of uncertainty awareness.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The use of multiple datasets, 10-fold cross-validation, and a standardized feature set ensures a relatively robust and fair evaluation of the proposed models. 
3. **Insightful Analysis of Evaluation Metrics:**  The core argument about the inadequacy of point-estimate metrics is well-supported by the experimental results.  The focus on NLPD as a more appropriate metric for evaluating probabilistic models is crucial and timely.
4. **Thorough Exploration of GP Extensions:** The paper goes beyond simply applying standard GPs and dives into the nuances of Matern kernels and warped GPs, offering practical guidance for their use in QE.
5. **Practical Relevance:**  The application to asymmetric risk scenarios demonstrates the potential real-world impact of uncertainty-aware QE models and provides a strong justification for further research in this area.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques and the evaluation framework are valuable, the individual components (GPs, Matern kernels, warped GPs) are not novel in themselves. The paper would benefit from explicitly acknowledging this and focusing on the novelty of the application to QE and the resulting insights.
2. **Over-reliance on a Single Feature Set:** While a standardized feature set is good for comparability, it might limit the performance of the models and potentially mask the benefits of the uncertainty modeling. Investigating the impact of richer feature sets would strengthen the findings.
3. **Inconclusive Results in Pessimistic Scenario:**  The reported issues with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario and the suggestion that standard GPs might be preferred are concerning. This requires further investigation and a more thorough explanation in the paper.  The authors should either provide a convincing explanation for this behavior or acknowledge the limitations of their approach in this specific scenario.
4. **Lack of Comparison to Modern QE Approaches:** The paper primarily compares different GP configurations against each other.  A comparison to more recent QE models based on neural networks (which may not directly provide uncertainty estimates but achieve strong point prediction performance) would provide a better benchmark and contextualize the findings.
5. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:**  GPs can be computationally expensive, especially with larger datasets. The paper briefly mentions scalability but doesn’t provide detailed insights into the computational cost of the different models, which is crucial for practical adoption.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why the linex loss performed poorly in the pessimistic scenario? Is this related to the variance of the predictive distributions from the warped GPs, as suggested in the paper?
2.  You mention that the datasets used are relatively small. How do you anticipate the performance of these models scaling to larger datasets? Would techniques like sparse GPs be necessary to maintain computational feasibility?
3.  How do the proposed models compare to state-of-the-art neural QE models (e.g., those based on transformers) in terms of point estimate performance *and* uncertainty calibration?
4.  Could you provide more detail on the practical considerations for deploying these models in a real-world MT workflow, such as the cost of hyperparameter tuning and model retraining?
5.  Beyond the asymmetric risk scenarios presented, are there other potential applications of well-calibrated uncertainty estimates in QE that you envision?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is well-written and clearly organized. 
*   The figures and tables are informative and effectively support the analysis.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the computational cost (training and prediction time) of the different models.
*   A brief discussion of the limitations of GPs (e.g., sensitivity to kernel choice) would be beneficial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of QE. While the individual components are not groundbreaking, the combination of techniques, the insightful analysis of evaluation metrics, and the practical demonstration of the benefits of uncertainty awareness make this paper worthy of acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly regarding the pessimistic scenario and comparison to modern QE approaches, would further strengthen the work.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
