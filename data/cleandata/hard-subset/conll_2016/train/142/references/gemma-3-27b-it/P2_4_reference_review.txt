## Review for "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

**Summary:** This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on their ability to model uncertainty. The authors argue that point estimates are insufficient for real-world QE applications requiring decision-making, and propose exploring extensions to standard GPs – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – to improve uncertainty calibration. They evaluate these models using both intrinsic metrics (NLL, NLPD, MAE, Pearson’s r) and through a simulated application involving asymmetric risk scenarios.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Highlighting the importance of uncertainty in QE:** The paper effectively articulates why considering uncertainty is crucial for practical QE systems used in decision-making pipelines, moving beyond a sole focus on point estimate accuracy. This is a valuable framing of the problem.
2. **Systematic evaluation of GP extensions for QE:**  The authors conduct a relatively comprehensive evaluation of different GP configurations (kernels, warping functions) specifically within the context of QE, using standard datasets.
3. **Demonstration of asymmetric risk application:** The exploration of asymmetric loss functions and Bayes risk estimation provides a concrete example of how calibrated uncertainty estimates can be leveraged for improved decision-making.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Framing:** The paper starts with a compelling argument for why uncertainty estimation is essential in QE, linking it directly to practical MT workflows. This justification is well-explained and provides strong motivation for the work.
2. **Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The use of standard QE datasets, a consistent feature set, and 10-fold cross-validation lends credibility to the experimental results.  The inclusion of multiple language pairs is a good practice.
3. **Insightful Analysis with NLPD:** The authors correctly emphasize the importance of the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate metric than point-estimate metrics for evaluating probabilistic models and demonstrate its relevance in this context.  The finding that point estimate metrics don't fully capture uncertainty differences is important.
4. **Comprehensive GP Exploration:**  The paper systematically explores various GP extensions, providing a valuable comparative analysis. The inclusion of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs broadens the scope of the investigation beyond basic GP implementations.
5. **Practical Application & Asymmetric Risk:** The exploration of asymmetric loss functions and Bayes risk estimation illustrates a tangible benefit of well-calibrated uncertainty estimates, making the work more relevant to real-world applications.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the Methods:** While the application of GPs to QE isn't new, the specific GP extensions explored (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) are established techniques from the GP literature. The novelty lies primarily in their application *to* QE and the detailed evaluation within that domain, rather than in the methods themselves. The paper would benefit from more clearly acknowledging this.
2. **Overly Complex Experimental Details:** The detailed explanation of GP extensions (Sections 2.2 and 2.3) is dense and may be inaccessible to readers unfamiliar with GPs. Some streamlining or a more high-level overview would improve readability.  The benefit of each extension relative to a simpler baseline isn't always immediately clear.
3. **Inconclusive Results in Asymmetric Risk:** The results in Section 4 regarding the linex loss and pessimistic scenarios are inconclusive and potentially misleading.  The authors acknowledge this internally, but the explanation is somewhat hand-wavy (variance being too low). Further investigation or a more nuanced discussion is needed.  Why was w = -0.75 chosen initially?
4. **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Uncertainty Estimation Methods:** While the focus on GPs is justified, a brief comparison to simpler uncertainty estimation methods (e.g., bootstrapping SVR) could further strengthen the argument for using GPs, especially given their computational complexity.
5. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:**  GPs are computationally expensive, especially for larger datasets. The paper mentions scalability isn't a major issue for typical QE datasets, but a more detailed discussion of computational costs and trade-offs would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of using SVR with bootstrapping for uncertainty estimation in QE? Why is it considered less ideal than the GP-based approach, beyond just calibration concerns?
2.  Regarding the inconclusive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, what further investigations could be performed to better understand this behavior?  Specifically, could you explore different parameterizations of the linex loss or investigate the properties of the predictive distributions produced by the warped GPs?
3.  How does the computational cost of the proposed GP models scale with the size of the training dataset, and what strategies could be employed to improve scalability for larger datasets?
4.  The paper focuses on post-editing time as a quality metric. How sensitive are the results to using other quality metrics (e.g., human ratings, error tags)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key differences between the GP models explored (EQ kernel, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs).
*   The notation throughout the paper could be more consistent. For example, ‘w’ is used for both weight in the loss function and a hyperparameter.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-motivated paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of QE by highlighting the importance of uncertainty estimation and providing a thorough evaluation of GP-based models. While the methods themselves are not entirely novel, the systematic evaluation and application to asymmetric risk scenarios are strong points.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
