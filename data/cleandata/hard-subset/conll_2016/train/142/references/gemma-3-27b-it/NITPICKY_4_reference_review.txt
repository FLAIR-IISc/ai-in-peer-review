This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on modelling predictive uncertainty. The authors investigate various GP extensions – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and propose using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than standard point estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson's r. They further demonstrate how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios using asymmetric loss functions.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a comprehensive empirical evaluation of different GP configurations for QE, coupled with a strong advocacy for NLPD as a more informative evaluation metric when uncertainty estimation is crucial. The exploration of asymmetric loss functions, while building on existing work, offers a practical application of the probabilistic predictions. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a relatively thorough investigation of different GP configurations, including kernel choices and warping functions, across multiple datasets. The 10-fold cross-validation provides a robust evaluation framework.
2. **Justification for NLPD:** The authors convincingly argue for the superiority of NLPD over traditional metrics when evaluating probabilistic models. The results clearly demonstrate that point estimate metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modelling, solidifying the argument.
3. **Practical Application to Asymmetric Risk:** The demonstration of how predictive distributions can be used with asymmetric loss functions is a valuable contribution. It highlights the potential for real-world applications where the cost of overestimation and underestimation differ.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The introduction provides adequate motivation, and the technical details are explained reasonably clearly.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Incremental Novelty:** The individual components – GPs, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, and asymmetric loss functions – are not novel. The primary contribution lies in their *combination* and evaluation within the QE context, which feels incremental. The paper doesn't present a fundamentally new method.
2. **Limited Feature Set:** The authors deliberately chose a limited feature set to facilitate comparison. While understandable, this limits the potential performance of the models and might not reflect the state-of-the-art in QE. This choice somewhat undermines the practical relevance of the findings.
3. **Overstated Claims about Optimality:** While the results favour Warped GPs with the tanh kernel, the gains are not consistently significant across all datasets and metrics. The authors occasionally present these results as conclusive, without sufficiently acknowledging the limitations. The linex loss results, particularly in the pessimistic scenario, are quite poor and are glossed over.
4. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *where* the models fail and *why* would provide valuable insights for future work. For instance, investigating the characteristics of sentences where uncertainty estimates are particularly inaccurate would be beneficial.
5. **Minor Formatting and Typographical Issues:** There are numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent use of equation numbering) and typographical errors throughout the paper. While individually minor, they detract from the overall polish and professionalism. Specifically, the references are not formatted correctly, and the notation is inconsistent (e.g. inconsistent use of subscripts and superscripts).

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of the chosen feature set and how it might affect the generalizability of your findings?
2.  The linex loss results in the pessimistic scenario are considerably worse than the baseline. Can you elaborate on possible reasons for this and whether further investigation is warranted?
3.  What specific types of sentences or translation errors lead to high uncertainty estimates in your models? Would a more detailed error analysis provide additional insights?
4.  Regarding the choice of hyperparameters, specifically the number of tanh terms in the warped GP, did you perform a more extensive search or sensitivity analysis to justify the chosen value?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The introduction could benefit from a more concise and focused discussion of the motivation and background.
*   Please thoroughly proofread the paper to address the numerous formatting and typographical errors.
*   The reference section is not well formatted; please adhere to the conference style guidelines.



Overall, while the paper presents a solid empirical investigation and highlights the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE, the lack of substantial novelty and the presence of several minor flaws lead me to recommend **rejection**. A more compelling contribution would require a more original methodological advancement or a significantly more comprehensive analysis.
