This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on the modeling of prediction uncertainty and its application to asymmetric risk scenarios. The core idea is that providing uncertainty estimates alongside QE predictions can improve decision-making in MT workflows, particularly in post-editing scenarios. The authors investigate different GP kernels (Matern vs. Exponential Quadratic) and warping functions to better calibrate the predictive distributions. They also propose using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more suitable evaluation metric compared to standard point-estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson’s r, and demonstrate the benefits of using these predictive distributions within asymmetric loss functions.

The primary contribution of this work is the thorough empirical investigation of various GP models—specifically exploring kernel choices and warping functions—and their impact on the *calibration* of the predictive distributions for QE. While GPs have been previously used in QE, the systematic exploration of these techniques, and the advocacy for NLPD as a crucial evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models, constitutes a valuable contribution. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating the utility of these calibrated predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios via the use of asymmetric loss functions. The clear presentation of the theoretical background and experimental setup adds to the overall value.

**Strengths:**

1. **Focus on Calibration and Uncertainty:** The emphasis on modeling predictive uncertainty, rather than just point estimates, is a significant strength. This addresses a practical limitation of traditional QE models and highlights a crucial aspect for real-world deployment. The framing of QE as a decision-making problem, where uncertainty is paramount, is well motivated.
2. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The authors perform a comprehensive evaluation of different GP models across multiple datasets and languages. The exploration of Matern kernels and warped GPs, and the comparison of their performance using NLPD, provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of these techniques. The consistent experimental setup makes the results reliable.
3. **Advocacy for NLPD:** The argument for using NLPD as a more appropriate evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models is compelling. The results demonstrate that standard point-estimate metrics fail to capture the differences in uncertainty modeling achieved by different approaches. This will likely influence future work in QE evaluation.
4. **Practical Application with Asymmetric Loss:**  The application of the calibrated predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic settings) showcases the practical benefits of the proposed approach. Demonstrating the integration of probabilistic predictions into downstream decision-making is a strong point.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to follow the authors' reasoning and understand the experimental results. The related work section provides a good overview of relevant literature.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the application to QE is valuable, the underlying GP models and warping functions themselves are not novel. The core contribution is the *application* and *analysis* of existing techniques within the QE context, rather than the development of new algorithms.
2. **Overly Strong Claims Regarding NLPD Superiority:** While NLPD is demonstrably useful, the paper occasionally presents it as a universally superior metric without fully acknowledging potential drawbacks. For instance, NLPD is sensitive to outliers and relies on the correctness of the probabilistic model assumptions. A more nuanced discussion of its limitations would be beneficial.
3. **Inconclusive Results for Linex Loss:** The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and require further investigation, as acknowledged by the authors. This weakens the overall argument for the benefits of using asymmetric loss functions. The discussion regarding why the linex loss performs poorly when standard GPs have lower variance is not fully convincing.
4. **Feature Set Limitations:** The use of a standard feature set, while simplifying comparisons, may limit the potential performance of the models. It is unclear if the observed benefits of the probabilistic models are dependent on the feature set used.
5. **Computational Cost not Discussed:** The computational complexity of training and evaluating GPs, especially with warping functions, is not addressed. This is a practical consideration for real-world applications, and a discussion of scalability would be valuable.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of NLPD as an evaluation metric, particularly regarding its sensitivity to model misspecification and outliers?
2.  Regarding the inconclusive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, what further experiments could be conducted to better understand this phenomenon? Would exploring different parameterizations of the linex loss or investigating alternative asymmetric loss functions be helpful?
3.  How sensitive are the observed benefits of the probabilistic models to the choice of features? Did you experiment with other feature sets, and if so, how did the results change?
4.  What is the computational cost associated with training and evaluating the proposed GP models, particularly with the warping functions, and how does this compare to traditional SVR models? Are there any strategies for improving scalability?
5.   You mention exploring other kernels, would you be able to include the results of these explorations in the appendix?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a discussion of the potential for combining the probabilistic QE models with other machine learning techniques, such as neural networks.
*   Exploring the use of other calibration metrics (e.g., Expected Calibration Error) could provide a more comprehensive assessment of the model calibration.
*   Providing code and data availability would increase the reproducibility and impact of the research.
