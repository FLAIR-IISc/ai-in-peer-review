## Review of “Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation”

**Summary:** This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on modeling predictive uncertainty rather than just point estimates. The authors explore various GP extensions – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and propose using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than traditional MAE/Pearson’s r. They further demonstrate how the resulting predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios, using both the Absolute Loss and Linex loss functions.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of NLPD’s Value in QE:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that standard point-estimate metrics fail to capture differences in the quality of uncertainty modeling offered by different GP variants. This highlights the need for metrics like NLPD for evaluating probabilistic QE models.
2. **Application of Warped GPs to QE:**  The exploration of Warped GPs for QE, addressing the issue of predicting strictly positive quality scores, is a solid contribution. The results show promising gains over standard GPs.
3. **Framework for Asymmetric Risk Assessment:** The analysis of how predictive distributions can be utilized within asymmetric loss scenarios provides a valuable framework for applying QE in real-world, decision-making contexts.



**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation & Problem Definition:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of relying solely on point estimates in QE and provides a compelling motivation for exploring uncertainty modeling, linking it directly to practical applications like post-editing workflows.
2. **Thorough Exploration of GP Variants:** The authors systematically investigate relevant GP extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) and provide a good overview of their theoretical underpinnings.
3. **Strong Experimental Setup:** The use of multiple datasets with varying characteristics (language pairs) and the consistent feature set strengthens the robustness of the findings. The 10-fold cross-validation is a good practice.
4. **Insightful Analysis of Asymmetric Risk:**  The application of asymmetric loss functions and the resulting analysis (particularly the caution regarding Linex loss and variance) are well-reasoned and demonstrate a deep understanding of the implications of uncertainty modeling.
5. **Well-Written and Organized:** The paper is generally well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow.  The background on GPs is concise but sufficient for readers familiar with machine learning.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methodological Contributions:** While the *application* of GPs and Warped GPs to QE isn't entirely new (as acknowledged in the related work), the extensions themselves aren't novel.  The core contribution lies in the empirical analysis and demonstration of the benefits of these techniques within the QE context.  A stronger argument for novelty could be made by highlighting specific adaptations of these techniques tailored to QE.
2. **Over-reliance on a Single Feature Set:** The decision to use only the 17 features from the QuEst framework, while understandable for consistency, might limit the potential of the models. Exploring a wider range of features or feature engineering techniques could yield further improvements.  The authors acknowledge this, but it’s still a limitation.
3. **Inconclusive Results for Matèrn Kernels:** The results for Matèrn kernels are not consistently better than the EQ kernel. The paper acknowledges this, but a more detailed investigation into *why* this might be the case would be beneficial.  Perhaps different language pairs require different levels of smoothness, or the feature set isn’t suitable for exploiting the benefits of Matèrn kernels.
4. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the authors mention that scalability isn't a major issue for QE datasets, a brief discussion of the computational cost associated with the different GP variants (especially Warped GPs) would be helpful.
5. **Linex Loss Issue – Needs Further Investigation:** The finding that Linex loss performs poorly when used with the Warped GPs is intriguing but requires more in-depth analysis.  The authors’ explanation is reasonable, but more empirical evidence to support it would strengthen the argument.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Regarding the Linex loss performance with Warped GPs: Could you provide more detailed analysis (e.g., a breakdown of prediction errors) that supports your hypothesis about the relationship between the lower variance of Warped GP predictions and the Linex loss’s penalty structure?
2.  You mention the potential for applying these methods to other text regression tasks.  Could you elaborate on specific tasks where you believe the benefits of uncertainty modeling would be particularly significant?
3.  While you used a consistent feature set, did you explore any feature selection or dimensionality reduction techniques to potentially improve model performance? If so, what were the results?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters in the Warped GP model, specifically the number of tanh terms?  Did you perform a more extensive hyperparameter search?
5.  In the asymmetric risk experiments, you fixed the values of *w*. Did you experiment with adaptive values of *w*, perhaps based on observed prediction errors?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are comprehensive and well-formatted.
*   The paper would benefit from a visual representation of the predicted distributions (e.g., histograms) to illustrate the differences between the models more clearly.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the limitations of GPs (e.g., cubic complexity with dataset size) and potential alternatives for larger datasets.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of QE. While the methodological contributions are not groundbreaking, the empirical analysis and demonstration of the benefits of probabilistic modeling and the NLPD metric are significant. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and provides a strong foundation for future research in this area. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
