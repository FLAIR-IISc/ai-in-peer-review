- Paper investigates Gaussian Processes for QE, focusing on predictive uncertainty and using NLPD as an evaluation metric.
- Explores Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs and shows how predictive distributions can be used under asymmetric risk (Absolute and Linex losses).
- Empirically demonstrates that NLPD captures differences in uncertainty modeling that point-estimate metrics miss.
- Applies Warped GPs to QE to handle strictly positive quality scores, showing promising gains over standard GPs.
- Provides a framework for using predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios relevant to real-world decision-making.
- Clear motivation linking limits of point estimates in QE to practical applications like post-editing workflows.
- Thorough, systematic exploration of relevant GP variants with adequate theoretical background.
- Strong experimental setup: multiple language-pair datasets, consistent features, and 10-fold cross-validation.
- Insightful analysis of asymmetric risk, including cautions about Linex loss and variance effects.
- Well-written and well-organized paper with concise GP background.
- Limited novelty: techniques are known; main contribution is empirical analysis rather than new methods.
- Over-reliance on a single 17-feature QuEst set may limit model potential; broader feature exploration is lacking.
- Inconclusive results for Matèrn kernels; more investigation needed into why they don’t consistently outperform EQ kernels.
- Limited discussion of computational cost and scalability differences between GP variants, especially Warped GPs.
- Linex loss performing poorly with Warped GPs is intriguing but needs deeper empirical support.
- Request for more detailed analysis linking Warped GP variance to Linex loss penalties (e.g., error breakdowns).
- Ask for examples of other text regression tasks that would benefit from uncertainty modeling.
- Question whether feature selection or dimensionality reduction was tried and what the outcomes were.
- Ask about sensitivity to Warped GP hyperparameters (e.g., number of tanh terms) and extent of hyperparameter search.
- Question whether adaptive values of w were tried in asymmetric risk experiments instead of fixed values.
- Suggest adding visualizations (e.g., predicted distribution histograms) to illustrate model differences.
- Recommend brief discussion of GP limitations (cubic complexity) and alternatives for larger datasets.
- Overall: solid, well-executed paper with valuable empirical contributions; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
