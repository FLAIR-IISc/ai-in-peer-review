This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on modeling predictive uncertainty. The authors propose extensions to standard GPs – Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – and evaluate them using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric alongside traditional point-estimate metrics. A further contribution is the exploration of asymmetric loss functions within a Bayesian framework, demonstrating how predictive distributions can be leveraged for decision-making in scenarios with varying risks.

The primary contribution is a thorough empirical investigation into the impact of different GP configurations on uncertainty calibration in QE. While GPs aren’t novel to QE, the systematic comparison of kernel choices and warping functions, coupled with the emphasis on NLPD as a key evaluation metric, is valuable. A secondary contribution is the application of asymmetric loss functions to QE, offering a more nuanced approach to evaluating and utilizing QE predictions in real-world scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Evaluation with NLPD:** The authors rightly highlight the limitations of point-estimate metrics in evaluating probabilistic models. Employing NLPD provides a more comprehensive assessment of model calibration and predictive uncertainty, which is crucial for practical applications of QE.
2. **Systematic Exploration of GP Extensions:** The detailed examination of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs, including considerations for the warping function parameterization, demonstrates a thoughtful and thorough approach to improving GP-based QE models.
3. **Practical Application with Asymmetric Losses:** The application of asymmetric loss functions to QE offers a compelling argument for the benefits of uncertainty-aware predictions. Demonstrating how these losses can be integrated with the Bayesian framework is a valuable contribution.
4. **Well-Established Baseline:** The use of a standard feature set from the QuEst framework and established datasets provides a solid baseline for comparison and facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the application to QE is sensible, the individual components – GPs, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, asymmetric losses – are all well-established techniques in other fields. The paper lacks a truly novel methodological contribution.
2. **Overly Detailed Methodology Description:** The paper dedicates an extensive amount of space to describing the underlying mathematical details of GPs and their extensions. This level of detail is unnecessary for a conference audience and detracts from the focus on the empirical results and their implications. The introduction of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs feels like a textbook chapter rather than a focused adaptation to the QE task.
3. **Inconsistent Results & Overinterpretation:** The results are not consistently positive. The en-es dataset shows inconsistent behavior, suggesting potential overfitting or limitations of the models on smaller datasets. The authors overinterpret these results, claiming NLPD is unreliable for small datasets without providing sufficient justification. The discussion of linex loss in the pessimistic scenario is particularly weak, attributing inconclusive results to the models’ lower variance without a clear explanation.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** There's a surprising lack of detailed error analysis. While the paper demonstrates improvements in metrics, it fails to provide insights into *where* the models succeed and fail, or *why* certain configurations perform better. Understanding the types of errors made by each model would significantly strengthen the conclusions.
5. **Formatting & Redundancy:** The paper contains minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing, equation formatting). Furthermore, there is some redundancy in the presentation of concepts and results. For example, the description of the asymmetric loss functions is repeated across sections.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The results for the en-es dataset are inconsistent. Could you provide a more in-depth analysis of why this dataset behaves differently and whether the smaller size is the sole reason for this discrepancy?
2.  You mention that the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario resulted in inconclusive results. Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why a lower variance might be detrimental in this context, beyond simply stating that it doesn’t “push” the estimator enough?
3.  What specific types of errors (e.g., overestimating high-quality segments, underestimating low-quality segments) are made by each of the proposed models? Providing a more granular error analysis would strengthen the conclusions.
4.  Given the lack of substantial novelty in the core methodology, what is the key takeaway message you want the reader to remember from this work? Is it the importance of NLPD, the benefits of Warped GPs, or the application of asymmetric losses?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive but could benefit from formatting consistency.
*   Consider streamlining the methodology section to focus on the specific adaptations made for QE, rather than providing a comprehensive review of GPs.
*   A table summarizing the key findings for each dataset and configuration would improve readability.
