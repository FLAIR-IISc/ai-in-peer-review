This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty. The authors motivate the need for uncertainty estimates in QE, particularly for decision-making in MT workflows, and investigate various GP extensions – Matern kernels and warped GPs – evaluating them intrinsically using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) and in asymmetric risk scenarios using appropriate loss functions. 

The primary contribution is a thorough empirical investigation into different GP models and their ability to represent uncertainty in QE. While GPs have been previously used in QE, the paper systematically explores kernel choices (EQ vs. Matern) and warping functions, demonstrating their impact on uncertainty calibration as measured by NLPD. A second, related contribution is the application of asymmetric loss functions to QE, framing the problem as a decision-making process with varying costs for under- and over-estimation of quality. This showcases a practical application of the improved uncertainty estimates. Finally, the paper highlights the limitations of standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) in capturing differences in uncertainty modeling, advocating for NLPD as a more informative evaluation metric.

**Strengths:**

1. **Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation of different GP models across multiple language pairs and datasets. The use of 10-fold cross-validation adds robustness to the findings. The results clearly demonstrate the benefits of warped GPs and illustrate the inadequacy of traditional point-estimate metrics for evaluating uncertainty calibration.
2. **Relevance of Asymmetric Risk Scenarios:** The framing of QE within asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic/optimistic) is highly relevant and provides a compelling motivation for modeling uncertainty. The application of asymmetric loss functions and the associated analysis showcase the practical value of the proposed approach.
3. **Clear Presentation and Background:** The paper provides a well-structured and clear explanation of the underlying concepts, including GPs, Matern kernels, and warped GPs. The background on QE and the motivation for uncertainty modeling are also clearly presented, making the paper accessible to readers with varying levels of expertise.
4. **Sound Methodology:** The use of NLPD as a primary evaluation metric is justified and appropriate for assessing the quality of probabilistic models. The experimental setup is well-defined and the results are presented in a clear and concise manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Model Extensions:** While the application of Matern kernels and warped GPs to QE is valuable, these are established techniques from the GP literature. The novelty lies primarily in the systematic evaluation and application of these techniques to the QE task, rather than the introduction of new modeling approaches.
2. **Inconclusive Results with Linex Loss:** The findings related to the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and require further investigation. The authors acknowledge this and suggest that the reduced variance of the warped GPs may be a contributing factor. The lack of clear benefit in this scenario weakens the overall argument for asymmetric loss functions.
3. **Feature Set Limitation:** The authors acknowledge the use of a limited feature set for comparability across language pairs. While this is a reasonable approach, it may limit the potential performance gains of the proposed models. It is possible that more sophisticated features could further enhance the benefits of uncertainty modeling.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on quantitative results and lacks a detailed error analysis. Investigating specific examples where the proposed models outperform or underperform baseline models could provide valuable insights into their strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. The paper mentions potential overfitting for the en-es dataset based on the divergence between NLL and NLPD. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the en-es dataset and explain why it is more susceptible to overfitting compared to the other datasets?
2. Regarding the inconclusive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, could you explore alternative explanations beyond the reduced variance of the warped GPs? Perhaps the specific choice of the weighting parameter (w) or the characteristics of the QE task itself could be contributing factors.
3. While acknowledging the limitation of the feature set, could you briefly discuss the potential impact of incorporating more advanced features (e.g., contextual features, linguistic features) on the performance of the proposed models?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is adequate but could be expanded to include recent advances in uncertainty estimation for neural machine translation models.
*   The code and datasets used in the experiments should be made publicly available to facilitate reproducibility and further research.
