This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for modeling uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The primary contribution is an investigation of different GP kernel extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) and their impact on capturing predictive uncertainty, assessed via the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating the potential benefits of utilizing these uncertainty estimates within asymmetric risk scenarios, using the AL and linex loss functions. Finally, the paper provides a thorough revisiting of the theoretical foundations of GPs within the QE context.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Exploration of GP Extensions:** The paper systematically investigates relatively less-explored GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) within the QE domain. This is valuable as it goes beyond simply applying standard GPs and delves into more nuanced modeling choices. The theoretical grounding provided is comprehensive and well-presented.
2. **Focus on NLPD as an Evaluation Metric:** The emphasis on NLPD as a more appropriate metric for evaluating probabilistic QE models is a key strength. The authors correctly identify the limitations of point-estimate-based metrics in capturing uncertainty calibration, a crucial aspect for real-world applications. The empirical results supporting this claim are convincing, though limited in scope.
3. **Consideration of Asymmetric Risk:** The exploration of asymmetric loss functions and Bayesian risk minimization provides a practical demonstration of how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in downstream decision-making. This directly addresses the motivation presented in the introduction – using QE in real-world workflows.
4. **Well-Established Methodology:** The reliance on established methods like GPs and standard feature sets (QuEst) lends credibility to the work. The clear description of experimental settings and cross-validation protocols facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Empirical Scope & Dataset Size:** While the analysis is sound, the experiments are conducted on only three datasets, one of which (en-es) is considerably smaller than the others. The authors acknowledge the potential overfitting issues with the smaller dataset, but the generalizability of the findings remains questionable. More extensive evaluation across diverse language pairs and datasets is needed.
2. **Incremental Contribution:** The core idea of applying GPs for uncertainty estimation in QE is not entirely novel. Previous work (cited within the paper) has already explored this avenue. The contributions lie in the specific GP extensions investigated and the application to asymmetric risk, but these are incremental improvements rather than a breakthrough. The novelty claim feels overstated.
3. **Inconclusive Results with Linex Loss:** The results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are problematic and inconclusive. The authors acknowledge this and suggest the model’s lower variance might be a contributing factor, but this requires further investigation. The lack of clear improvement, or even a deterioration in performance, diminishes the impact of the asymmetric risk analysis. The abrupt cutoff of the pessimistic linex results is also unsettling.
4. **Insufficient Comparison to Simpler Uncertainty Estimation Methods:** The paper focuses exclusively on GPs, neglecting a comparison to simpler, potentially more efficient methods for uncertainty estimation, such as bootstrapping or ensemble methods applied to SVR. This makes it difficult to assess the added value of the more complex GP-based approaches.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The performance discrepancies observed between the en-es dataset and the other two are significant. Could you provide a more in-depth analysis of the characteristics of the en-es dataset that might explain this difference, and potentially mitigate its impact on the overall conclusions?
2.  You mention the potential issue of the linex loss being biased towards models with low variance. Have you explored any methods for mitigating this bias, such as scaling the variance or employing a different loss function?
3.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with training and evaluating the different GP models, particularly the Warped GPs with multiple tanh terms? This would help assess the practical feasibility of these models for large-scale QE applications.
4.  The analysis largely focuses on post-editing scenarios. Can you discuss the applicability of these uncertainty estimates and asymmetric loss functions in other QE use cases, such as human-in-the-loop MT or MT system selection?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential areas for future research.
*   While the writing is generally clear, some sections (e.g., the mathematical derivations) could be made more accessible to a broader audience.
*   Consider including error bars or confidence intervals in the tables to provide a better sense of the statistical significance of the results.
