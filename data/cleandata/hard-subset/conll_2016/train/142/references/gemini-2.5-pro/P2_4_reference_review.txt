### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper argues for a shift in how Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) models are built and evaluated. Instead of focusing solely on point estimates (like an MAE score), the authors advocate for probabilistic models that provide uncertainty estimates, which are more useful for real-world decision-making (e.g., in a post-editing workflow). The paper investigates Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a suitable framework. It proposes specific model extensions—namely Matérn kernels and Warped GPs—to better suit the noisy and strictly positive nature of QE data. The core of the paper is an empirical evaluation that (1) uses a probabilistic metric, Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), to show that standard metrics like MAE fail to capture important differences in model uncertainty, and (2) demonstrates the practical utility of these uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios without needing to retrain models.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A compelling argument and demonstration for evaluating QE models probabilistically.** The primary contribution is not a new algorithm, but a shift in perspective. The paper clearly shows that models with similar point-estimate performance can have vastly different predictive distributions, and it makes a strong, evidence-backed case for adopting metrics like NLPD that capture this uncertainty.
2.  **A practical application of advanced GP models to the QE task.** The paper successfully adapts and applies Warped GPs to handle the non-Gaussian nature of post-editing time data. This demonstrates a methodologically sound way to generate better-calibrated probabilistic predictions for this specific task.
3.  **A novel application of Bayes risk estimators to QE for handling asymmetric loss.** The demonstration in Section 4 is a valuable contribution, showing how a single, well-calibrated probabilistic model can be flexibly adapted to different business needs (e.g., "pessimistic" vs. "optimistic" scenarios) at prediction time, which is far more efficient than retraining models for each specific loss function.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a highly relevant and practical problem.** The paper's motivation is excellent. The critique of point estimates for decision-making is spot-on and addresses a genuine gap between academic QE evaluation and its practical deployment. Moving the focus from simply minimizing MAE to generating *useful*, reliable predictions is an important direction for the field.
2.  **Methodologically sound and well-explained.** The choice of Gaussian Processes is well-justified for this task. The proposed extensions are not arbitrary but are chosen for specific, well-argued reasons: Matérn kernels to handle noisy data by relaxing smoothness assumptions, and Warped GPs to correctly model the strictly positive domain of the response variable (post-editing time). The explanations are clear and grounded in the GP literature.
3.  **Strong and clear empirical results supporting the central thesis.** The results in Table 1 provide a powerful "aha" moment. The fact that Warped GP models achieve dramatically better NLPD scores while showing only modest or inconsistent gains in MAE or Pearson's r is clear evidence for the paper's main claim: standard metrics are hiding important differences in model quality. This finding alone is a significant contribution.
4.  **Insightful analysis of results, including limitations.** The authors provide a nuanced discussion of their findings. They are honest that the benefits of Matérn kernels are "less conclusive." More impressively, in Section 4.3, they investigate a surprising negative result (Warped GPs performing worse with the pessimistic Linex loss) and offer a plausible, technical explanation related to the model's variance. This kind of thoughtful analysis builds confidence in the authors' work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Scope is limited to a now-outdated QE paradigm.** The experiments rely exclusively on GPs with a small set (17) of hand-crafted features. While this is justified to focus on the probabilistic modeling aspect, the state-of-the-art in QE has largely moved to neural architectures using pre-trained representations (e.g., XLM-R). The paper would be significantly stronger if it discussed how these ideas of uncertainty estimation and probabilistic evaluation could be applied to modern neural QE models (e.g., via Monte Carlo dropout, deep ensembles, or conformal prediction). As it stands, it's unclear if the specific findings would generalize to SOTA systems.
2.  **Scalability concerns are not sufficiently addressed.** The paper dismisses the scalability issues of GPs by noting that QE datasets are "usually small." While true for the datasets used (all <3k instances), this is a fragile assumption. As QE becomes more integrated into production pipelines, datasets are growing. GPs scale cubically with the number of data points, which would make this approach infeasible for even moderately-sized datasets (e.g., 20k+ instances). A brief discussion of potential solutions, like sparse GPs (which the authors cite elsewhere), would have strengthened the paper's practical relevance.
3.  **The empirical evaluation could be broader.** The experiments are conducted on three datasets, which is reasonable. However, all of them use post-editing time as the quality label. The argument for better uncertainty modeling would be more generalizable if it were also demonstrated on a different type of QE label, such as HTER scores, which have different statistical properties. Furthermore, the smallest dataset (en-es) seems to cause overfitting issues, as noted by the authors, which somewhat weakens the conclusions drawn from it.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Your work compellingly demonstrates the value of probabilistic evaluation for classic, feature-based QE. How do you see this framework applying to modern, neural QE architectures? For instance, could the NLPD metric and asymmetric risk analysis be directly applied to uncertainty estimates generated from methods like deep ensembles or MC dropout, and would you expect to see similar benefits?
2.  Could you elaborate on the practical computational costs of your proposed Warped GP models? Given the O(n^3) complexity of standard GPs, at what dataset size would you anticipate needing to switch to a sparse approximation, and how might that affect the calibration of the uncertainty estimates that are central to your paper's contribution?
