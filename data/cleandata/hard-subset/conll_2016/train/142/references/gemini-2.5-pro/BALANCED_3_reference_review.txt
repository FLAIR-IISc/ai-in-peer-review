### Summary of the paper and main contributions

This paper argues for a shift in how Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) models are built and evaluated, moving from standard point estimates to probabilistic predictions that capture uncertainty. The authors explore Gaussian Process (GP) models, proposing extensions like Matérn kernels and Warped GPs as better suited for the noisy and non-Gaussian nature of QE data. The core of the work is demonstrating that traditional metrics like MAE fail to capture crucial differences in model quality, advocating instead for the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD). Finally, the paper showcases the practical utility of these probabilistic predictions in asymmetric risk scenarios, where the costs of over- and under-estimation are unequal.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A compelling and practical demonstration of applying uncertainty-aware modeling and evaluation to QE.** The paper effectively highlights the shortcomings of point estimates for real-world decision-making and provides a clear, principled framework for improvement. Shifting the focus from MAE to metrics like NLPD and evaluating models in application-grounded asymmetric loss scenarios is a significant conceptual contribution to the field.
2.  **A thorough empirical validation of advanced Gaussian Process models for QE.** The experiments clearly show that Warped GPs are a much more appropriate model class for this task than standard GPs. This finding is non-obvious when looking at traditional metrics but becomes starkly clear with NLPD, providing strong evidence for the authors' claims.
3.  **The introduction of asymmetric risk analysis as a novel extrinsic evaluation for QE.** Framing QE as a decision-making problem under varying "optimistic" and "pessimistic" losses is an insightful and practical way to assess a model's real-world utility. The demonstration that a single probabilistic model can be flexibly adapted to these different scenarios without retraining is a powerful argument for this approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent motivation and problem framing.** The paper opens with a clear and persuasive argument for why uncertainty is critical in practical QE workflows, such as post-editing. This strong motivation makes the subsequent technical explorations feel well-grounded and important. The critique of symmetric, point-based evaluation metrics is sharp and accurate, setting the stage perfectly for the proposed solutions.
2.  **Sound and appropriate methodology.** The choice of Gaussian Processes is well-justified for this task, and the exploration of specific, relevant extensions—Matérn kernels for noisy data and Warped GPs for strictly positive targets—is thoughtful and technically sound. The use of NLPD as the primary intrinsic metric is a major strength, as it provides a principled way to evaluate the entire predictive distribution, which is precisely the paper's focus.
3.  **Novel and impactful application in asymmetric risk scenarios.** The analysis in Section 4 is a standout contribution. It connects the abstract concept of a predictive distribution to concrete, plausible applications (e.g., gisting vs. cautious post-editing). Demonstrating how Bayes risk estimators allow a single model to serve different business needs is elegant and highlights a key practical advantage of the probabilistic modeling paradigm.
4.  **Thorough and insightful experimental analysis.** The experiments are well-designed, covering three language pairs, and the results are discussed with nuance. The direct comparison in Section 3.2 between what NLPD reveals and what MAE/Pearson's r obscures is highly effective. Furthermore, the authors' honest and thoughtful discussion of the unexpected negative result for the pessimistic linex loss (Section 4.3) adds to the paper's credibility and opens interesting avenues for future work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The focus on Gaussian Processes is somewhat narrow.** The paper justifies its focus on GPs by contrasting them with models like SVR that only provide point estimates. However, this overlooks other methods for uncertainty quantification. For instance, ensemble methods (which are briefly mentioned but quickly dismissed) or techniques like Monte Carlo Dropout with neural networks can also produce predictive distributions. The paper would be stronger if it situated the GP approach within this broader context, even if just through a brief discussion, to justify why GPs are a particularly suitable choice over other probabilistic alternatives.
2.  **The use of a basic feature set raises questions about generalizability.** The authors correctly note that their goal is model comparison, not achieving state-of-the-art point predictions, thus justifying the use of a standard 17-feature set. However, modern QE systems rely heavily on powerful pretrained representations (e.g., from XLM-R). It is unclear if the clear advantages of Warped GPs would persist or change when combined with these much richer, higher-dimensional features. An experiment on at least one dataset with a more modern feature set would significantly bolster the paper's claims.
3.  **The practical implications of the gains in the asymmetric scenarios are not fully elucidated.** While the results in Table 2 show that the proposed models consistently achieve lower loss values, the paper does not translate these numerical improvements into practical terms. For example, a reduction in the asymmetric linear loss from 0.082 to 0.076 is numerically better, but it is difficult for a reader to gauge the real-world impact. Providing some intuition, perhaps by analyzing how many decisions (e.g., "send to post-editor" vs. "translate from scratch") would change for the better, would make the benefits more tangible.
4.  **The analysis of the pessimistic linex loss result is inconclusive.** The authors hypothesize that Warped GPs perform poorly here because the linex loss's estimator is sensitive to variance, and the warped models have smaller (more realistic) variance. They suggest this might be a flaw in the loss function itself. This is an interesting but speculative conclusion. An alternative explanation could be that while the Warped GP's variance is better calibrated overall, it is underestimated in the distribution's tails, which is exactly where an exponential loss function exacts the highest penalty. This point warrants deeper investigation to be a fully convincing part of the analysis.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your work convincingly demonstrates that Warped GPs are superior to standard GPs for modeling strictly positive targets like post-editing time. As an alternative to learning a warping function, have you considered simply using a different likelihood within the GP framework, such as a Log-Normal likelihood, which naturally handles positive data? How would you expect such an approach to compare with the data-driven `tanh` warping function?
2.  The counter-intuitive result for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario is intriguing. You suggest it may be biased towards high-variance models. Could this phenomenon also suggest that the variance estimates from the Warped GP, while better calibrated overall (per NLPD), are systematically underestimated in the tails of the distribution? An exponential loss function would be exquisitely sensitive to such tail-end miscalibration, which could perhaps explain the poor performance. Do you have any further thoughts on this possibility?
