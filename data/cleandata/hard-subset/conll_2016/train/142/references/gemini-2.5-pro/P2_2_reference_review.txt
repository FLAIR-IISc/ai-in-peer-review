### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the importance of modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that traditional point-estimate predictions and their corresponding evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) are insufficient for real-world applications where decisions carry asymmetric risks (e.g., post-editing workflows). The paper proposes a framework centered around Gaussian Processes (GPs), exploring extensions like Matérn kernels and Warped GPs that are better suited to the properties of QE data. It advocates for the use of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more informative evaluation metric that accounts for the entire predictive distribution. Finally, it demonstrates the practical utility of these probabilistic models in asymmetric risk scenarios by deriving optimal predictions for different loss functions without retraining.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong, well-motivated argument for shifting QE evaluation from point estimates to probabilistic predictions.** The paper makes a compelling case for the use of NLPD as a primary evaluation metric, demonstrating empirically that it can distinguish between models that appear similar under MAE or Pearson's r. This is a valuable conceptual contribution to the QE field.
2.  **A practical demonstration of how well-calibrated uncertainty estimates can be leveraged in decision-making.** The section on asymmetric risk scenarios provides a concrete and useful application, showing how a single probabilistic model can flexibly serve different downstream needs (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic filtering) by calculating Bayes risk estimators. This is a significant step beyond simply reporting uncertainty.
3.  An empirical analysis of advanced GP models for QE. The paper shows that Warped GPs, which learn a non-linear mapping of the target variable, are particularly effective for modeling post-editing time, a common QE target that is strictly positive and non-Gaussian.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It starts with a clear, practical example of a post-editing scenario where uncertainty matters and follows through with a detailed exploration of asymmetric risk. This firmly grounds the work in real-world applicability, making the contributions feel significant and necessary for the field.
2.  **Methodologically Sound and Rigorous Evaluation:** The core thesis—that standard metrics are insufficient—is convincingly supported by the experiments. The introduction and use of NLPD is well-justified, and Table 1 clearly shows how NLPD captures differences in model quality (e.g., between log-warped and tanh-warped GPs) that MAE fails to reflect. The use of 10-fold cross-validation across three different datasets strengthens the reliability of the findings.
3.  **Novel Application of Probabilistic Methods to a Practical Problem:** The application of asymmetric loss functions (AL and linex) and their corresponding Bayes risk estimators is a key strength. It demonstrates a sophisticated and highly practical benefit of adopting a fully probabilistic approach. The ability to adjust predictions for different risk profiles without retraining the model is a powerful advantage for production systems, and the paper explains and executes this idea very clearly.
4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is very well-written. The theoretical background on Gaussian Processes, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs is presented concisely but with sufficient detail. The experimental setup and results are easy to follow, and the discussion provides insightful analysis, including an honest assessment of inconclusive results (e.g., for Matérn kernels and the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Proposed Methods:** The core machine learning methods—Gaussian Processes, Matérn kernels, Warped GPs, and the NLPD metric—are not new and are well-established in the ML literature. The primary novelty lies in their application, combination, and framing for the QE task. While this is a valid and important contribution, the work does not propose a new algorithm or model architecture.
2.  **Baselines Could Be Stronger:** The paper primarily compares variants of GP models against a standard GP baseline. While this is effective for showing the benefits of the proposed extensions (like warping), it doesn't compare against other common methods for generating uncertainty estimates. For instance, a comparison to a simple but strong baseline like a deep ensemble or a bootstrapped SVR model (which the authors mention as a popular QE method) would have provided a more comprehensive picture and strengthened the claims about the superiority of Warped GPs for this task.
3.  **Use of a Basic Feature Set:** The authors use a standard set of 17 hand-crafted features. While they justify this by stating their focus is on comparing models rather than achieving state-of-the-art performance, it leaves open the question of how these findings translate to modern QE systems, which increasingly rely on large, pre-trained neural representations (e.g., from XLM-R). The properties of the feature space can significantly impact kernel methods, and the conclusions drawn here might not fully generalize to high-dimensional, dense vector representations from end-to-end models.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario (Table 2) are quite interesting. You hypothesize this is because the linex estimator depends on variance, and the better-calibrated Warped GP models have lower (and more realistic) variance. This leads them to be "not conservative enough" compared to the standard GP, which gets "rewarded" for assigning mass to nonsensical negative values. Could this also suggest that the linex loss itself might be a flawed metric for bounded, non-Gaussian variables like post-editing time, as it may implicitly favor poorly-calibrated models with high variance?
2.  Your work makes a strong case for GP-based models. How do you see these methods fitting into the current landscape of large, pre-trained transformer models for QE? Could GP-based uncertainty estimation be combined with neural feature extractors, for example by placing a GP on top of the final embedding layer of a model like COMET or TransQuest? Do you foresee any scalability challenges with this approach, given that GPs are often difficult to scale to large datasets?
