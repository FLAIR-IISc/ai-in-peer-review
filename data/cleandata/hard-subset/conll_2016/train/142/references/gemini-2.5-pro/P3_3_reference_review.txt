### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the important but often overlooked issue of predictive uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that standard point estimates are insufficient for real-world applications where QE models guide decision-making. The core of the work is to advocate for and evaluate probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), that can provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The paper explores extensions to standard GPs, such as Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, to better model the noisy and non-Gaussian nature of QE data (specifically, post-editing time).

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A strong argument and demonstration for shifting QE evaluation beyond point metrics.** The paper convincingly shows that standard metrics like MAE fail to capture significant improvements in uncertainty modeling. It introduces and validates the use of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more holistic evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models.
2.  **A novel and practical application-driven evaluation using asymmetric risk scenarios.** The paper moves beyond intrinsic evaluation by demonstrating how predictive distributions can be used with asymmetric loss functions to optimize for different real-world goals (e.g., a "pessimistic" scenario minimizing risk for inexperienced post-editors vs. an "optimistic" one maximizing content for gisting). This provides a concrete framework for assessing the practical utility of uncertainty estimates.
3.  **An empirical validation of advanced GP models for QE.** The study shows that Warped GPs, which learn a non-linear transformation of the target variable, are particularly well-suited for QE tasks involving strictly positive labels like post-editing time, significantly outperforming standard GPs in terms of predictive likelihood.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Framing:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It correctly identifies a critical disconnect between the typical academic evaluation of QE (minimizing MAE) and its practical application in decision-making workflows. The introduction clearly lays out why uncertainty is crucial, using a compelling post-editing scenario. This framing makes the work relevant and timely.
2.  **Methodologically Sound and Rigorous:** The choice of GP models is well-justified for the low-data regimes common in QE. The paper provides clear, concise explanations of the different GP variants and the evaluation metrics. The experimental design is robust, using three distinct datasets and 10-fold cross-validation, which lends credibility to the findings. The progression from intrinsic evaluation (Section 3) to application-based evaluation (Section 4) is logical and compelling.
3.  **Clear and Convincing Results:** The empirical results strongly support the paper's central claims. Table 1 provides a stark illustration of how point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) can be insensitive to significant improvements in model fit, which are clearly captured by NLPD. The experiments in the asymmetric risk scenarios (Table 2) are a key strength, providing a tangible demonstration of how better uncertainty estimates from Warped GPs translate into better performance under specific, practical loss functions.
4.  **High-Quality Writing and Presentation:** The paper is very well-written, structured, and easy to follow. Complex concepts like Warped GPs and Bayes risk are explained with sufficient detail for the reader to understand their relevance without getting bogged down in unnecessary technicalities. This clarity significantly enhances the impact of the work.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art and Alternative Uncertainty Methods:** The authors explicitly state they do not aim to achieve SOTA point-prediction performance, using a small, standard 17-feature set. While this is acceptable for a focused study, it leaves open the question of how these findings generalize to modern QE systems that rely on features from large pretrained transformers (e.g., BERT, XLM-R). Furthermore, the paper focuses exclusively on GPs. A comparison with other, simpler methods for uncertainty estimation (e.g., deep ensembles or Monte Carlo dropout on a simple MLP, or even bootstrapping SVR) would have strengthened the claim that GPs are the superior choice for this task, rather than just *a* good choice.
2.  **Novelty is in Application, Not Method:** The core methods—GPs, Matérn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, and asymmetric loss—are all established techniques in the machine learning literature. The contribution is the insightful application, combination, and evaluation of these methods within the QE domain. While this is a valuable scientific contribution, the paper does not propose any new methodology.
3.  **Inconclusive Discussion of a Key Negative Result:** The finding that standard GPs outperform Warped GPs under the pessimistic Linex loss is one of the most interesting results, as it runs counter to the paper's main narrative. The authors' hypothesis—that the loss is biased toward the higher (and less realistic) variance of the standard GP—is plausible but not deeply investigated. This section feels somewhat rushed, concluding with "we leave further investigation... for future work." Providing more direct evidence, such as comparing the average predicted variances of the models or visualizing distributions for key examples, would have turned this from a puzzling loose end into a much deeper insight about the interaction between models and highly sensitive loss functions.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the counterintuitive results for the pessimistic Linex loss: your hypothesis is that this loss is biased towards models with high variance. Could you substantiate this claim further? For instance, what are the average predicted variances for the standard GP versus the Warped GP models on the test sets? Does the standard GP's ability to assign probability mass to non-physical negative values directly contribute to a higher variance that is then "rewarded" by this specific estimator?
2.  Your experiments use a classic feature set. How do you envision these methods and findings integrating with the current paradigm of QE, which is dominated by large, pretrained transformer models? Could a GP be used as a final calibrated output layer on top of a neural feature extractor, or do you believe methods like Monte Carlo dropout would be more suitable for estimating uncertainty in that context? A brief comment on the path forward would be valuable.
