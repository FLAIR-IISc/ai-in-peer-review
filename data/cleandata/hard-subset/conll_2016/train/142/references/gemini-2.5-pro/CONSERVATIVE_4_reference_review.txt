### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on modeling predictive uncertainty. The authors argue that standard point-estimate metrics like MAE are insufficient for evaluating models intended for decision-making tasks. They explore several extensions to the standard GP framework, namely Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs, and evaluate them using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) alongside traditional metrics. The paper also presents a case study on applying these models in hypothetical scenarios with asymmetric risk.

In my view, the contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical study comparing several variants of Gaussian Process models (standard, Matèrn kernel, Warped) for the QE task, advocating for the use of NLPD as a more appropriate evaluation metric for probabilistic forecasts.
2.  A demonstration of how predictive distributions from these models can be used to derive estimators for asymmetric loss functions, which may be relevant for certain downstream applications of QE.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Principled Problem Formulation:** The paper correctly points out a limitation of the standard QE paradigm, which is its over-reliance on point estimates. Framing QE as a probabilistic prediction problem and considering the uncertainty of predictions is a methodologically sound and important direction for the field, particularly as QE models are integrated into complex, real-world workflows.

2.  **Focus on Proper Evaluation:** The authors make a strong case for moving beyond metrics like MAE and Pearson's r when evaluating probabilistic models. The use of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) is well-motivated, as it evaluates the entire predictive distribution, rewarding well-calibrated uncertainty and penalizing overconfidence. This is a valuable message for the QE community.

3.  **Systematic Exploration of GP Models:** The paper provides a clear and systematic comparison of several sensible GP configurations. The motivation for using Matèrn kernels (to relax smoothness assumptions) and Warped GPs (to handle the non-Gaussian nature of quality scores) is well-explained and grounded in the established machine learning literature.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Novelty and Impact:** The methods explored—Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs—are all well-established techniques from the machine learning literature. Their application to QE is not entirely new either, as the authors' own citations show (e.g., Cohn and Specia, 2013). The paper reads more like an application note or a position paper advocating for existing ML best practices in QE, rather than presenting a novel contribution. The empirical results do not demonstrate a significant breakthrough that would justify a departure from current standard practices.

2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The experiments are conducted entirely within the GP framework, comparing different GP variants against a standard GP. However, the dominant method in QE for years has been Support Vector Regression (SVR). The authors dismiss SVR and bootstrapping approaches with a brief, unsubstantiated critique in Section 2, but provide no empirical comparison. Without a strong, competitive baseline like SVR (with uncertainty estimated via bootstrapping, for example), it is impossible to assess whether the complexity of the GP framework offers any real-world advantage. The claims about the superiority of GPs for uncertainty estimation remain unsubstantiated.

3.  **Unconvincing Empirical Gains and Setup:**
    *   The improvements in standard QE metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) are marginal at best and inconsistent across datasets (Table 1). This significantly weakens the paper's argument. If the new models do not improve, or barely improve, the primary point predictions, the justification for adopting them hinges entirely on the claimed benefits of "better uncertainty," which are not linked to a tangible downstream improvement.
    *   The experiments are performed on a small, 17-feature baseline system. State-of-the-art QE systems often use hundreds or thousands of features. It is unclear if the findings regarding different GP configurations would hold in a more competitive, high-dimensional setting. Justifying this setup by stating the goal is not to improve the state-of-the-art feels like an evasion of the need for robust evaluation.
    *   The claimed benefits of Matèrn kernels are "less conclusive" by the authors' own admission, making this part of the investigation feel superfluous.

4.  **Speculative Application Scenario:** The section on asymmetric risk is presented as a primary motivation, but the evaluation is entirely hypothetical. There is no user study or simulation based on real post-editing data to show that optimizing for these asymmetric losses actually improves translator productivity, reduces costs, or achieves a better outcome in a gisting scenario. Furthermore, the proposed Warped GP models perform *worse* than the standard GP baseline under the pessimistic linex loss, a significant negative result that undermines the authors' claims about their model's utility.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Given that SVR is a dominant and simpler baseline in QE, could you provide a comparison against an SVR model where uncertainty is estimated via a standard technique like bootstrapping? This would be crucial to justify the adoption of the more complex and computationally intensive GP framework.

2.  The paper's central argument rests on the superiority of the predictive distributions, as measured by NLPD, while point-prediction metrics (MAE) show little improvement. Can you provide a more concrete, quantitative link between the observed improvements in NLPD and a practical downstream benefit? For example, in a filtering scenario, how does the improved NLPD of a Warped GP translate into a higher F1-score for identifying sentences that require post-editing, compared to a standard GP or SVR?

3.  Your results in Table 2 for the pessimistic linex loss show that your proposed Warped GP models are substantially worse than the standard GP. You suggest this might be because the linex loss is "biased towards models with high variance." Does this not suggest a critical failure mode for your proposed models, making them potentially unreliable in certain real-world scenarios? Why should the community adopt a model that can perform so poorly under a plausible risk profile?
