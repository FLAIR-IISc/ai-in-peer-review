### Summary and Contributions

This paper makes a compelling case for shifting the focus of Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) from producing simple point estimates to modeling full predictive distributions that capture uncertainty. The authors argue that standard evaluation metrics like MAE and Pearson's r are insufficient for assessing probabilistic models and advocate for the adoption of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), which evaluates the entire distribution. The paper explores Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a suitable framework, investigating extensions like Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs that are better suited to the noisy and non-negative nature of QE data (post-editing time). The practical utility of this approach is demonstrated through a novel evaluation in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- and under-estimation of quality incur different costs, better reflecting real-world decision-making.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  **A strong methodological critique of current QE evaluation practices**, coupled with the introduction and effective demonstration of a superior, probabilistically sound metric (NLPD). This is the paper's most significant contribution, as it could help re-orient how the community evaluates and compares QE models.
2.  **The systematic application and evaluation of advanced GP models for QE**, particularly Warped GPs. The paper shows these models are not just theoretically more appropriate but also yield empirically better-calibrated predictive distributions on several QE datasets.
3.  **A novel and practical demonstration of how to leverage QE uncertainty in decision-making**, by framing the problem in terms of asymmetric risk and using Bayes Risk estimators. This connects the abstract concept of uncertainty to tangible application scenarios like post-editing and gisting.

### Strengths

1.  **High Importance and Timeliness:** The paper addresses a critical limitation in the practical application of QE. Real-world workflows require more than a single quality score; they need confidence measures to make robust decisions. By clearly articulating this need and providing a comprehensive solution and evaluation framework, the paper makes a timely and important contribution that could significantly increase the real-world impact of QE research.
2.  **Methodological Rigor and Clarity:** The work is methodologically excellent. The theoretical background of GPs, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs is explained with remarkable clarity. The experimental design is sound, employing cross-validation across multiple standard datasets. The analysis thoughtfully distinguishes between model performance on training data (NLL) and test data (NLPD), correctly identifying overfitting on the smaller dataset.
3.  **Powerful and Convincing Evaluation:** The dual evaluation strategy is a major strength. The intrinsic evaluation using NLPD clearly demonstrates that standard metrics like MAE fail to capture crucial differences in model quality. The extrinsic evaluation in asymmetric risk scenarios is particularly compelling, as it provides a concrete, application-grounded answer to the question "Why does better uncertainty matter?". This makes the paper's claims far more impactful than a purely theoretical discussion would be.
4.  **Excellent Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, the methods are described precisely, and the results are discussed with insightful interpretations. This high level of clarity makes the work accessible and its arguments more persuasive.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Comparison with Modern QE Architectures:** The experiments are confined to GP models with a small, 17-feature set. While justified by the paper's focus on comparing probabilistic models, this sidesteps the current trend in QE towards large, feature-rich neural models (e.g., Predictor-Estimator architectures using transformers like COMET). A key question left unanswered is how the quality of uncertainty estimates from these finely-tuned GPs compares to those from neural methods (e.g., using Monte Carlo Dropout or deep ensembles). Including even a simple neural baseline would have substantially strengthened the paper's claims about the superiority of GPs for this task.
2.  **Scalability Concerns Are Understated:** The paper notes that the small size of QE datasets makes the O(n^3) complexity of GPs manageable. However, this is a significant limitation for the future. As data collection becomes more automated and larger datasets become available, these models will not scale. A brief discussion of how scalable GP approximations (e.g., sparse variational methods, which are cited in other contexts) would perform and whether they preserve the high-quality uncertainty estimates would have made the proposed approach appear more robust and future-proof.
3.  **Inconclusive Findings Weaken the Narrative:** The empirical results for Matèrn kernels are reported as "less conclusive," and the analysis of the pessimistic linex loss scenario reveals that the baseline GP outperforms the more sophisticated Warped GP models. While the authors provide a plausible hypothesis for this surprising result (the loss's sensitivity to variance), it remains an unresolved issue that slightly detracts from the otherwise clean and strong narrative of the paper. It suggests that the interaction between probabilistic models and specific loss functions can be complex and non-intuitive.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The focus on GPs is well-motivated for the experimental setting. However, given the prominence of neural networks in modern NLP and QE, could you elaborate on how you see your proposed framework (i.e., evaluation with NLPD and under asymmetric loss) applying to neural QE models? Do you have intuitions on whether the explicit covariance modeling of GPs would still offer superior uncertainty estimates compared to techniques like MC Dropout or deep ensembles in a high-dimensional, large-data QE setting?
2.  Your finding that standard GPs outperform Warped GPs in the pessimistic linex loss scenario is very interesting. You hypothesize this is due to the loss function's bias towards models with higher variance. Does this suggest that the linex loss may not be the ideal choice for this specific "pessimistic" scenario in practice? Did you consider testing other asymmetric losses (e.g., asymmetric quadratic/expectile loss) that might be less sensitive to the variance and more dependent on the mean, and would you expect Warped GPs to perform better under such a loss?
3.  You convincingly show that Warped GPs are a better model for QE scores than standard GPs because they respect the positivity constraint. Another common approach for positive data is to use a log-Gaussian process (i.e., a standard GP on log-transformed data). Your `log(y)` baseline is one instance of this. The tanh warping function is more flexible, but also adds complexity and hyperparameters. In your experiments, were the gains of the tanh warping function over the simpler log-transform substantial enough to justify its use in all cases, especially on smaller datasets where it seemed to underperform?
