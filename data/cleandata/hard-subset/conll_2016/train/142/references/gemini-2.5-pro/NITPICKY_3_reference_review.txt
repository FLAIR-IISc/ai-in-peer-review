**Summary of the Paper and Contributions**

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on modeling predictive uncertainty. The authors argue that conventional point estimates are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios. They compare a standard GP with an exponentiated quadratic (EQ) kernel against models using Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs, which are designed to handle noisy data and non-Gaussian response variables, respectively. The models are evaluated not only on standard metrics like MAE and Pearson's r but also on Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), which directly assesses the quality of the predictive distributions. Finally, the paper demonstrates the utility of these probabilistic models in asymmetric risk scenarios by deriving Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric loss functions, thus avoiding costly model retraining.

The main contributions are:
1. An empirical study comparing several GP variants for QE, using the NLPD metric to highlight the benefits of Warped GPs for uncertainty modeling, which are not always captured by point-estimate metrics.
2. A practical demonstration of applying a single, trained probabilistic model to different asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., pessimistic post-editing, optimistic gisting) by calculating optimal estimators for different loss functions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper correctly identifies a significant gap in QE research: the over-reliance on point estimates. The motivation for needing well-calibrated uncertainty for downstream tasks is clear, practical, and effectively communicated through concrete examples (post-editing and gisting). This positions the work as highly relevant to the operationalization of QE systems.
2.  **Sound Choice of Methods:** The selection of Gaussian Processes as the core modeling framework is well-justified. The extensions explored—Matèrn kernels for noisy data and Warped GPs for positive-only, non-Gaussian quality scores—are theoretically sound and directly address potential weaknesses of a standard GP model in this application context. The use of NLPD as a primary evaluation metric is a major strength, as it properly evaluates the probabilistic nature of the predictions.
3.  **Novel Application to Asymmetric Risk:** The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios is a valuable contribution. The demonstration of how to derive Bayes risk estimators from a pre-trained model is elegant and practically significant, as it allows a single QE model to flexibly serve different downstream applications with varying cost-benefit profiles without retraining.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Precision:** Several key methodological details are either imprecise or insufficiently justified.
    *   In Section 2.3, the procedure for obtaining the median of the Warped GP posterior is unclear. The paper states the inverse warping function is not in closed form and that its gradient is used for a "numerical estimate," but fails to specify the actual numerical method (e.g., a root-finding algorithm).
    *   In Section 3.1, the choice to use the median for *all* point-prediction metrics is not justified. While optimal for MAE, the mean is the optimal estimator for squared-error-related metrics like Pearson’s r. This choice may affect the reported correlations.
    *   The hyperparameter optimization process is described as a "two-pass procedure," which appears to be a reasonable heuristic but lacks formal justification. Details about the optimizer used (e.g., L-BFGS, ADAM) are also omitted.

2.  **Insufficiently Supported Claims and Analysis:** The discussion of results contains several strong claims that are not adequately supported by empirical evidence.
    *   The central explanation for the poor performance of Warped GPs in the pessimistic linex scenario (Section 4.3) is that they "tend to have less variance." This is a testable hypothesis, yet the paper provides no data (e.g., a table of average predictive variances per model) to substantiate it. Without this evidence, the subsequent conclusion that the linex loss is "biased towards models with high variance" remains speculative.
    *   The conclusion that Matèrn kernels show "inconclusive" gains (Section 3.2) is a missed opportunity for deeper analysis. The authors suggest a dependency on the language pair but do not investigate this further, for instance, by analyzing the learned ARD lengthscales to see if data characteristics differ in ways that would favor one kernel over another.

3.  **Limited Experimental Scope:** The experimental setup, while clean, is limited in ways that temper the generality of the conclusions.
    *   The use of a small, 17-feature baseline set, while understandable for isolating model differences, raises questions about whether the findings would hold on state-of-the-art systems that use hundreds or thousands of features. The paper’s claim that scalability issues do not affect QE may not be true in such high-dimensional settings.
    *   The comparison is restricted to GP variants. To robustly claim the superiority of the proposed models, a comparison against other methods for uncertainty quantification—such as bootstrapping/bagging an SVR or using Monte Carlo dropout in a simple neural network—would have been highly informative.

4.  **Numerous Formatting and Citation Errors:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of presentation flaws, indicating a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   The reference list is plagued with errors. Several entries contain BibTeX artifacts (e.g., `Beck et al\.,? \Q2014\E`), repeated author names, and inconsistent formatting. The citation for Bojar et al. (2014), which is essential for the en-es dataset, appears to be missing entirely from the bibliography.
    *   In-text citations are also inconsistent (e.g., "Callisonburch" vs. "Callison.burch").
    *   Minor grammatical errors ("the remaining of this paper") and awkwardly placed footnotes detract from the paper's professionalism. Footnote 4, which mentions omitting results "for the sake of clarity," reduces transparency and reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please specify the exact numerical procedure used to compute the median of the Warped GP posterior from the gradient of the (non-invertible) warping function?
2.  Your hypothesis regarding the poor performance of Warped GPs under the pessimistic linex loss is that they produce lower-variance predictions. Could you provide empirical data from your experiments (e.g., the mean predictive variance for each model on each test set) to support this specific claim?
3.  The bibliography contains significant and numerous formatting errors, including what appear to be BibTeX compilation artifacts and at least one missing reference (Bojar et al., 2014). Do you plan to conduct a thorough audit and correction of the reference list prior to publication?
