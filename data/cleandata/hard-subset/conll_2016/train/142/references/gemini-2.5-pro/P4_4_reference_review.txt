### Summary

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) to provide not just point estimates of quality, but also measures of prediction uncertainty. The authors argue that standard evaluation metrics like MAE and Pearson's r are insufficient for comparing probabilistic models, as they ignore uncertainty. The paper advocates for Gaussian Processes (GPs), specifically exploring extensions like Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs, which are better suited for the typically noisy and strictly positive data (e.g., post-editing time) found in QE. The models are evaluated using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), a metric that accounts for the entire predictive distribution. Furthermore, the paper demonstrates a practical application of these uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-estimation of quality has different costs, by deriving Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric loss functions. Experiments on three language pairs show that Warped GPs provide better-calibrated uncertainty estimates (lower NLPD) and offer advantages in asymmetric decision-making scenarios compared to standard GPs.

### Contributions

1.  **Empirical demonstration of uncertainty-aware evaluation for QE:** The primary contribution is the application and rigorous evaluation of probabilistic modeling techniques in QE, coupled with a strong argument for shifting evaluation away from point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) towards distribution-aware metrics like NLPD. This provides a more principled framework for assessing and comparing QE models intended for real-world decision-making.
2.  **Systematic comparison of advanced Gaussian Process models for QE:** The paper presents a structured analysis of different GP model configurations for QE. It specifically investigates the impact of relaxing smoothness assumptions via Matèrn kernels and modeling non-Gaussian target variables (post-editing time) using Warped GPs. The results provide clear evidence for the suitability of Warped GPs for this task.
3.  **A practical framework for applying QE in asymmetric risk scenarios:** The work demonstrates how to leverage the predictive distributions from probabilistic models to optimize for asymmetric loss functions. This provides a concrete, practical methodology for tailoring QE model outputs to specific downstream applications (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic filtering) without needing to retrain the underlying model.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong motivation and problem formulation:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It clearly articulates the limitations of point estimates in practical QE workflows and makes a compelling case for the importance of quantifying prediction uncertainty. The examples provided (post-editing filtering, gisting) are concrete and effectively ground the research in real-world needs.
2.  **Methodologically sound evaluation:** The adoption of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as the primary evaluation metric is a major strength. The experiments clearly show that traditional metrics like MAE and Pearson's r fail to distinguish between models with different quality of uncertainty estimates (Table 1), validating the authors' central claim. The use of 10-fold cross-validation adds to the robustness of the empirical results.
3.  **Clear practical utility:** The section on asymmetric risk scenarios (Section 4) successfully bridges the gap between theoretical modeling and practical application. By showing how to derive optimal estimators for different loss functions from a single trained probabilistic model, the authors present a flexible and efficient solution for production environments where decision criteria may change. This significantly increases the potential impact of the work.
4.  **Clarity and organization:** The paper is very well-written, with clear explanations of technical concepts like Gaussian Processes, warping functions, and asymmetric losses. The structure is logical, guiding the reader from the motivation and models through intrinsic evaluation and finally to a practical application. This makes the paper accessible and its arguments easy to follow.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited novelty of core methods:** The techniques explored—Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, and asymmetric loss functions—are all well-established in the machine learning literature. The contribution lies in their application and framing within the QE domain. While this is a valuable contribution, the work does not introduce new methodology from a machine learning perspective.
2.  **Limited scope of model comparison:** The experiments exclusively compare variants within the GP family. The paper would be strengthened by including comparisons to other common methods for uncertainty quantification, such as deep ensembles or Monte Carlo dropout with neural network-based QE models, which represent the current state-of-the-art for point prediction. Without such a comparison, the claim that GPs are the best-suited framework for this task is not fully substantiated.
3.  **Inconclusive results in some experimental settings:** The authors acknowledge that the advantage of Matèrn kernels over the standard EQ kernel is not consistently demonstrated across datasets. More significantly, the experiments with the Linex loss in the pessimistic scenario show the proposed Warped GP models performing worse than the standard GP baseline. While a plausible hypothesis is offered, the analysis is deferred to future work, leaving a notable unresolved issue in the experimental results.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The results for the Linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are counter-intuitive. You hypothesize this is due to the loss function's sensitivity to variance and the standard GP assigning probability mass to nonsensical negative values. Could this hypothesis be tested by applying a post-hoc truncation to the standard GP's predictive distribution (e.g., renormalizing over positive values) and re-calculating the Bayes risk estimator? This could help isolate whether the issue is truly the non-sensical support or another property of the Warped GP's posterior.
2.  The experiments use a standard set of 17 features. State-of-the-art QE has largely moved to neural architectures (e.g., Predictor-Estimator models) that learn representations directly. How do you see the methods proposed here being integrated with such architectures? Do you believe GPs could still be effectively used as a final layer on top of learned neural features, or would other uncertainty quantification techniques like MC dropout or deep ensembles be more natural fits?
3.  The paper focuses on post-editing time, which is an unbounded, strictly positive variable. How do you expect your findings to generalize to other common QE labels, such as HTER (which is also positive but often has a different distribution with mass near 0 and 1) or DA scores (which are typically bounded, e.g., on a [0, 100] scale)? Would Warped GPs still be the most appropriate choice, or would other likelihoods/models (e.g., Beta regression for bounded scores) be more suitable?
