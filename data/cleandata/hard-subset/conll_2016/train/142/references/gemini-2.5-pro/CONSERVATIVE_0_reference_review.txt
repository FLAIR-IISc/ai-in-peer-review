### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The central thesis is that standard point-estimate predictions and their corresponding evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) are insufficient for real-world applications, and that models providing well-calibrated uncertainty estimates are more useful. The authors explore extensions to standard GPs, namely Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs, and advocate for evaluation metrics like Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) that account for the entire predictive distribution. The utility of these models is further demonstrated in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-estimation of quality incurs different costs.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  An empirical comparison of several Gaussian Process variants (standard GP, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) for the task of QE, evaluated on three datasets.
2.  The advocacy for and application of alternative evaluation methodologies for QE, specifically the use of NLPD to assess probabilistic predictions and the use of asymmetric loss functions to simulate practical application scenarios.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear Motivation:** The paper provides a compelling motivation for moving beyond point estimates in QE. The post-editing and gisting examples in the introduction and Section 4 effectively illustrate scenarios where prediction uncertainty is critical for decision-making. This frames the work in a practical context that is highly relevant to the field.

2.  **Methodologically Sound Exploration:** The paper explores established and well-understood models from the machine learning literature (Gaussian Processes and their variants). The choice of Matèrn kernels to relax smoothness assumptions and Warped GPs to handle non-Gaussian, strictly positive quality scores are sensible and well-justified modeling decisions for the QE task.

3.  **Highlights Limitations of Standard Metrics:** A key strength of the work is its critique of conventional QE evaluation. By showing that standard metrics like MAE and Pearson’s r fail to distinguish between models with different uncertainty profiles (Table 1), the paper makes a strong case for the adoption of probabilistic metrics like NLPD when the goal is to assess the quality of uncertainty estimates.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper's most significant weakness is its failure to compare against the dominant non-probabilistic methods in the field. The authors themselves state that Support Vector Regression (SVR) is the "most popular method for QE". By only comparing different GP variants to each other, the paper leaves a critical question unanswered: what is the performance trade-off for adopting a probabilistic approach? It is essential to demonstrate that these GP models are at least competitive with a well-tuned SVR on standard metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) before arguing for their superiority on other, less conventional metrics. Without this baseline, the practical utility of the proposed methods is difficult to assess.

2.  **Inconclusive Empirical Results:** The evidence presented in favor of the proposed model extensions is not compelling. The authors note that the gains from using a Matèrn kernel are "less conclusive" (Section 3.2). More problematically, in the pessimistic asymmetric risk scenario with linex loss, the proposed Warped GP models perform consistently *worse* than the standard GP baseline (Table 2). The paper speculates on the reason but does not resolve the issue. This contradictory result substantially undermines the central claim that these more complex models provide universally better or more useful uncertainty estimates for decision-making.

3.  **Limited and Potentially Dated Experimental Setup:** The experiments are conducted using a standard set of only 17 handcrafted features. The authors concede that top-performing systems use larger, more complex feature sets. While the goal is a comparative study of models, using a weak feature set risks producing findings that do not generalize to more realistic, state-of-the-art settings. The conclusions about the relative performance of different kernels and warping functions might change significantly in a higher-dimensional feature space. This makes the findings feel preliminary and of uncertain relevance to the current state of QE research.

4.  **Contribution is Primarily Applicational:** The methods presented—Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, and asymmetric loss functions—are all well-established in the machine learning literature. The paper's contribution is to apply them to the QE task and advocate for their use. While application papers are valuable, the work is presented as a novel exploration without providing the rigorous validation needed to convince the community to adopt these methods. The inconclusive results and lack of strong baselines prevent the paper from being a definitive demonstration of a superior approach.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide results for a competitive SVR baseline, trained on the same data folds and feature set? This would allow reviewers to properly situate the performance of your GP models within the broader context of established QE methods.

2.  The poor performance of Warped GPs under the pessimistic linex loss is a significant concern. Your explanation suggests it is because the model has lower variance. Could this not be interpreted as the warped model's uncertainty being poorly calibrated for this scenario? Why should we trust the uncertainty estimates from a model that leads to demonstrably worse decisions under a plausible risk scenario?

3.  How do you expect your findings to translate to modern neural QE architectures (e.g., based on pre-trained transformers), which have largely superseded models based on small sets of handcrafted features? Do you believe the same conclusions regarding kernels and warping functions would hold in that setting?
