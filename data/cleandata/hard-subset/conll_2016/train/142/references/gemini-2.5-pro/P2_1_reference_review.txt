### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of uncertainty estimation in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that standard QE models, which provide point estimates of quality, are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios where the model's confidence in its prediction is critical. The paper proposes using probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), and explores more sophisticated variants like Matern kernels and Warped GPs to better model the underlying data distribution. The core argument is that evaluation should also be uncertainty-aware, and thus the paper advocates for using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) over traditional metrics like MAE or Pearson's r. Finally, it demonstrates a practical application of these uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-prediction carry different costs.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A strong argument and demonstration for uncertainty-aware evaluation in QE.** The paper compellingly argues that metrics like MAE are blind to model confidence and introduces NLPD as a more appropriate metric for probabilistic models. The experiments in Section 3 clearly show that models can have similar MAE scores but vastly different NLPDs, effectively demonstrating the inadequacy of point-estimate metrics for this purpose.
2.  **Systematic application and evaluation of advanced GP models for QE.** The paper moves beyond standard GPs and explores Warped GPs to handle the non-Gaussian, strictly positive nature of quality scores (post-editing time), and Matern kernels to handle noisy data. It shows empirically that Warped GPs, in particular, provide significantly better-calibrated predictive distributions.
3.  **A novel application-driven evaluation framework using asymmetric loss.** The introduction of pessimistic and optimistic scenarios (Section 4) provides a concrete, practical motivation for needing good uncertainty estimates. By deriving Bayes risk estimators for these scenarios, the paper shows how a single probabilistic model can be flexibly adapted to different downstream user needs without retraining, and that the proposed Warped GP models generally perform better in these settings.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction provides a clear and intuitive example of a post-editing workflow where a QE model's confidence is as important as its prediction. This framing immediately establishes the relevance and potential impact of the work. The problem is not just "how to get a better MAE score," but "how to build more useful and reliable QE models for real-world applications."
2.  **Methodological Soundness:** The authors' choice of methods and evaluation metrics is principled and well-justified. Recognizing that post-editing time is a strictly positive value and that the underlying data may not conform to the smoothness assumptions of standard kernels is key. The subsequent proposal to use Warped GPs and Matern kernels is a direct and appropriate response to these data characteristics. Most importantly, the use of NLPD aligns the evaluation directly with the paper's central claim about the importance of the entire predictive distribution, not just its mean.
3.  **Clear and Insightful Experimental Analysis:** The experiments are designed to convincingly support the paper's narrative. Table 1 provides a stark contrast between NLPD and the point-estimate metrics, powerfully illustrating the main thesis. The discussion of results is insightful, correctly identifying potential overfitting on the smaller en-es dataset as a reason for discrepancies between NLL and NLPD. The asymmetric risk experiments in Section 4 are a valuable contribution, demonstrating the tangible benefits of well-calibrated uncertainty in a practical setting.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Models:** The paper's entire analysis is confined to Gaussian Processes. While GPs are indeed a strong choice for probabilistic regression, the broader field of QE has largely moved towards neural network-based architectures (e.g., predictor-estimator models). The paper feels disconnected from this current trend. The argument for uncertainty is model-agnostic, yet the solution presented is model-specific. A discussion of how these principles could apply to neural models—for instance, through techniques like Monte Carlo dropout, deep ensembles, or Bayesian neural networks—would have significantly strengthened the paper's relevance and impact.
2.  **Use of a Basic and Potentially Outdated Feature Set:** The authors use a standard set of 17 features from the QuEst framework. They acknowledge this is not state-of-the-art but justify it by focusing on model comparison. However, this choice raises questions about the generalizability of the findings. Modern QE systems leverage rich representations from large pre-trained language models. It is unclear if the benefits of Matern kernels or warping functions would be as pronounced when the input features are already highly informative and less noisy. The conclusions might be specific to QE with shallow, hand-crafted features.
3.  **Inconclusive and Under-analyzed Results in the Asymmetric Scenario:** A key part of the application-driven evaluation yields a puzzling result: in the pessimistic scenario with the linex loss, the standard GP baseline outperforms the more sophisticated warped models. The authors' hypothesis—that this is due to the warped models having lower (and more realistic) variance—is plausible but not deeply investigated. This feels like a significant loose end. If a standard, misspecified model (one that assigns probability to negative time) outperforms a better-specified one under a given loss function, it might suggest a flaw in the loss function itself or a more complex interaction that merits deeper analysis rather than being deferred to future work.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The core ideas of this paper—the importance of uncertainty and the use of metrics like NLPD—are highly relevant to modern neural QE models. Could you comment on how you see this work being extended to current state-of-the-art architectures? How would you expect methods like deep ensembles or MC dropout to compare with GPs in terms of producing well-calibrated distributions for the asymmetric risk scenarios you presented?
2.  The results for the pessimistic linex loss were counter-intuitive, with the standard GP performing best. Your hypothesis is that the estimator is not "pushed" enough due to the lower variance of the warped models. Could this also imply that the linex loss, with its dependency on variance in the Bayes risk estimator (ŷ = µy − wσ²y/2), might unfairly penalize well-calibrated models that correctly report low variance for their predictions?
3.  Do you believe the strong performance of Warped GPs is primarily due to their ability to prevent probability mass from being assigned to negative values, or from learning a more complex and better-fitting mapping from the latent space to the observation space? Have you considered simpler alternatives like a log-normal likelihood, and how would that compare?
