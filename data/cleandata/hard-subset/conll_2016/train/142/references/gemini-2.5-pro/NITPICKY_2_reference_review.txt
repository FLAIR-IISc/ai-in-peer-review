**Summary of the paper**
This paper addresses the limitation of point estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) by exploring probabilistic models that provide uncertainty information. The authors focus on Gaussian Processes (GPs) and propose using extensions—namely Matérn kernels and Warped GPs—to better model the noisy and non-Gaussian nature of QE data (post-editing time). The paper introduces the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric to the QE task as a more appropriate way to evaluate probabilistic models. Finally, it demonstrates a practical application of these predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-estimation of quality has different costs, by deriving Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric loss functions.

**Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the systematic evaluation of advanced probabilistic models (Warped GPs with Matérn kernels) for the QE task. The paper compellingly demonstrates through the use of the NLPD metric that these models offer superior uncertainty estimates compared to standard GPs, a nuance missed by traditional point-estimate metrics like MAE.
2.  A second contribution is the application of these probabilistic QE models to asymmetric risk scenarios. This frames the QE problem in a more realistic decision-making context and shows how to derive optimal estimators for different loss functions without retraining the model, which is a novel and practical perspective for the field.

**Strengths**
1.  **Clear Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It clearly articulates the shortcomings of point estimates in practical QE workflows (e.g., post-editing filtering) and presents uncertainty quantification as a principled solution. The asymmetric risk scenarios in Section 4 are concrete, plausible, and effectively highlight the benefits of having a full predictive distribution.
2.  **Sound Methodological Approach:** The choice of Gaussian Processes is well-justified, and the proposed extensions are sensible. Using Matérn kernels to relax smoothness assumptions for noisy data and Warped GPs to handle the strictly positive, non-Gaussian nature of the target variable (post-editing time) are appropriate and elegant modeling decisions.
3.  **Introduction of a More Informative Evaluation Metric:** The paper makes a strong case for adopting NLPD for evaluating probabilistic QE models. The results in Table 1 convincingly show that standard metrics like MAE and Pearson's r are insufficient, as they fail to distinguish between models with clearly different probabilistic characteristics. This is an important message for the QE community.

**Weaknesses**
1.  **Insufficient Experimental Detail and Rigor:** Several key details of the experimental setup are either missing or underdeveloped, which hinders reproducibility and confidence in the results.
    *   The paper uses a "standard set of 17 features" but provides no information about them. Are they normalized? What are their scales? This information is crucial for understanding the effect of the ARD kernel.
    *   The hyperparameter optimization procedure is described as a "two-pass procedure," but lacks critical details. What optimization algorithm was used (e.g., L-BFGS, Adam)? What were the initialization ranges or priors for the hyperparameters? Without these details, the results are not fully reproducible.
    *   The choice to use the median for all point predictions is questionable. While optimal for absolute error (MAE), the mean is optimal for squared error and more closely related to Pearson's r. This choice is not justified and may have affected the point-estimate metric comparisons. An analysis comparing predictions from the median vs. the mean would have made the evaluation more complete.
2.  **Incomplete Analysis of Asymmetric Loss Results:** The discussion in Section 4.3 regarding the poor performance of Warped GPs with the pessimistic linex loss is speculative and unconvincing. The authors hypothesize that the lower variance of Warped GPs is the cause, but provide no evidence. The conclusion that a standard GP might be "preferred" because its flawed probability mass on negative values leads to higher variance and coincidentally a better score on this specific, biased loss function is problematic. This suggests a potential flaw in the linex loss or its estimator in this context, rather than a genuine advantage of the standard GP. This section requires a much deeper, more rigorous analysis.
3.  **Numerous Formatting and Presentation Flaws:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of minor errors that collectively suggest a lack of careful preparation.
    *   The reference section is severely flawed, containing unprocessed LaTeX commands (`\Q2015\E`), stray question marks, duplicate author entries, and inconsistent formatting throughout (e.g., "Abe and Mamitsuka.,? 1998, Abe and Mamitsuka.,"). This is unacceptable for a camera-ready submission.
    *   There are grammatical errors and awkward phrasings (e.g., "The remaining of this paper", "logged on an sentence basis", "has recently proposed by").
    *   A footnote marker is missing in Section 2.2 ("exponentiated quadratic (EQ) kernel1").
    *   The bulleted list outlining the paper's structure in the introduction is unconventional and stylistically weak for a formal research paper.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you please justify the exclusive use of the median for point predictions across all evaluation metrics? Did you perform any experiments using the posterior mean, and if so, did this change the conclusions drawn from the MAE and Pearson's r results?
2.  Your hypothesis about the linex loss results in the pessimistic scenario is that the standard GP's higher variance is beneficial. Could you provide quantitative evidence for this, for example by reporting the average predictive variance for the standard GP vs. the Warped GP models on the test sets? Could this not also indicate that the linex loss is overly sensitive to variance and perhaps not a suitable loss function for this task?
3.  The bibliography is in a state of disrepair. Can you confirm that this will be meticulously corrected, and that the entire manuscript will be professionally proofread to fix the various typographical and grammatical errors before publication?
