### Summary and Contributions

This paper explores the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that point estimates are insufficient for real-world applications and that well-calibrated uncertainty estimates are crucial for decision-making. The paper introduces and evaluates two extensions to the standard GP framework for QE: Matérn kernels to relax smoothness assumptions and Warped GPs to better model non-Gaussian, strictly positive quality scores. The evaluation is conducted using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to assess the quality of the predictive distributions, and the utility of these models is demonstrated in asymmetric risk scenarios using specialized loss functions.

As I see them, the contributions of this work are:
1.  A focused empirical study on applying more sophisticated GP variants (Warped GPs, Matérn kernels) to the task of QE, providing a useful comparison of their ability to model predictive uncertainty.
2.  The advocacy for and application of NLPD as a more appropriate evaluation metric for probabilistic regression models in NLP, highlighting the shortcomings of traditional point-estimate metrics like MAE.
3.  A novel application of Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric loss functions in QE, demonstrating how a single probabilistic model can be adapted to different decision-making scenarios without retraining.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper is well-motivated, addressing the practical need for uncertainty quantification in QE systems used in production pipelines. The authors clearly articulate why point estimates are insufficient and how uncertainty can inform crucial decisions, such as whether to send a segment for post-editing. This framing is a significant strength and relevant to the broader NLP community.

2.  **Principled Introduction of Advanced Methods:** The paper does a good job of introducing concepts from the machine learning literature (Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD) and arguing for their suitability in the context of QE. This is a valuable contribution, as it encourages the QE community to look beyond standard models and evaluation metrics.

3.  **Novel Application Scenario:** The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios in Section 4 is a compelling and novel application. Connecting QE to established concepts from econometrics like the AL and linex loss functions opens up an interesting new direction for research and demonstrates a concrete benefit of having access to a full predictive distribution.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of Professionalism in Presentation:** The paper suffers from a striking lack of care in its preparation, which severely undermines its credibility. The entire references section appears to be an unformatted BibTeX dump, replete with errors (e.g., `Beck et al\.,? \Q2014\E`, `Nguyen and O.Connor.,?`), inconsistent author formatting (`Callison-burch` vs. `Callisonburch`), and extraneous characters. Grammatical errors are present in the main text (e.g., "The remaining of this paper..."). This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a conference submission and suggests a rushed and careless process.

2.  **Outdated Experimental Setup:** The experiments rely on a 17-feature set from the QuEst framework. While the authors state their goal is not to achieve state-of-the-art point predictions, this choice makes the findings difficult to generalize. Modern QE is dominated by approaches using large pre-trained language models. It is unclear whether the benefits of Warped GPs or specific kernels would hold in a high-dimensional feature space derived from such models, which limits the paper's relevance and impact on the current QE landscape.

3.  **Insufficiently Rigorous Evaluation and Analysis:** Despite using 10-fold cross-validation, the results in Tables 1 and 2 are presented as point averages without any measure of variance (e.g., standard deviation or standard error) or significance testing. This makes it impossible to determine if the reported differences, especially the small gains from Matérn kernels, are statistically significant or simply due to chance. The baseline is also weak; comparing only against a standard GP is not enough to prove the superiority of the proposed methods for uncertainty estimation. A comparison against a bootstrapped ensemble of a strong non-probabilistic model like SVR would have been more convincing.

4.  **Arbitrary Choices and Unconvincing Explanations:** Several methodological choices appear arbitrary. The parameters `w` for the asymmetric losses are presented without justification. More critically, for the en-de dataset, the `w` parameter for the linex loss was changed post-hoc because the original value led to extremely large losses. This undermines the robustness of the approach. Furthermore, the paper observes that the proposed Warped GP models perform poorly with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario and dismisses this significant negative result by stating, "We leave further investigation of this phenomenon for future work." This is an unsatisfying way to handle a clear failure case of the proposed method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The reference section is currently unreadable and filled with formatting errors. Can you confirm that, if accepted, the paper will be thoroughly proofread and all citations will be correctly and consistently formatted?

2.  Given that state-of-the-art QE has moved beyond hand-crafted feature sets, how do you expect your findings on the benefits of Warped GPs and Matérn kernels to generalize to systems based on high-dimensional representations from pre-trained transformers?

3.  Could you supplement Tables 1 and 2 with standard error or confidence intervals to allow for a proper assessment of the statistical significance of your results?

4.  Could you provide a more principled motivation for the specific `w` values chosen for the asymmetric loss experiments? How would a user of your system determine the appropriate `w` for their specific application, especially given the sensitivity you observed on the en-de dataset?
