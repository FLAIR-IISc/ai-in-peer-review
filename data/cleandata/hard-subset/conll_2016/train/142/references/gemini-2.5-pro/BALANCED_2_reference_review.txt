### Summary and Contributions

This paper explores the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The central argument is that standard point estimates of quality are insufficient for real-world applications and that modeling predictive uncertainty provides crucial additional information for decision-making. The authors investigate extensions to standard GPs, namely Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs, to better model the properties of QE data. They advocate for moving beyond traditional evaluation metrics like MAE and Pearson's r, proposing the use of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to assess the quality of the entire predictive distribution. Finally, the paper demonstrates the practical utility of these probabilistic models in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., when underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating it), showing how Bayes risk estimators can derive optimal predictions for different needs without model retraining.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **A strong, practical demonstration of the value of probabilistic modeling and uncertainty-aware evaluation for QE.** The paper compellingly shows how modeling the full predictive distribution can inform more nuanced decision-making (as in the asymmetric risk scenarios) and highlights how standard metrics fail to capture important differences between probabilistic models. This serves as an important methodological contribution to the field.
2.  **The introduction and empirical validation of Warped GPs for QE.** While not a novel machine learning method, the paper systematically shows that Warped GPs are a significantly better model class for QE score prediction than standard GPs, as they can learn appropriate transformations for strictly positive, non-Gaussian quality scores.
3.  **The application of asymmetric loss functions to QE via Bayes risk estimation.** This is a novel and valuable application for the QE task, providing a principled framework for tailoring a single QE model's output to varying downstream business requirements (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic use cases).

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction provides clear, intuitive examples from post-editing and gisting workflows that effectively illustrate the limitations of point estimates and the practical need for uncertainty quantification. This framing makes the work highly relevant and its contributions easy to appreciate.
2.  **Sound and Thorough Experimental Design:** The empirical evaluation is robust and well-structured. The use of three datasets of varying sizes allows for insightful analysis, such as identifying the potential for more complex models to overfit on smaller datasets (en-es). The experiments are logically divided into an intrinsic evaluation of the predictive distributions (Section 3) and an extrinsic, application-oriented evaluation (Section 4), which strengthens the paper's narrative. The systematic comparison of models (standard GP vs. Matèrn vs. Warped) clearly demonstrates the incremental benefits of the proposed modeling choices.
3.  **Clear Evidence for Superior Evaluation Metrics:** The paper makes a powerful case for adopting NLPD for evaluating probabilistic QE models. Table 1 provides clear evidence that standard metrics like MAE and Pearson’s r are insensitive to substantial improvements in uncertainty modeling. For example, while Warped GPs achieve dramatically better NLPD scores than standard GPs, the improvement in MAE is much less pronounced. This is a crucial finding that should encourage the QE community to adopt more appropriate metrics when assessing probabilistic systems.
4.  **Insightful Analysis and Discussion:** The discussion of the results is thoughtful and goes beyond a superficial summary of the numbers. The authors correctly hypothesize that differences in performance on the en-es dataset are due to its smaller size. The analysis in Section 4.3, which attempts to explain the poor performance of Warped GPs with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, is particularly insightful, demonstrating a deep understanding of the interplay between the models and the evaluation framework.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** The core methods presented—Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs, and asymmetric loss functions—are all well-established in the machine learning literature. The paper's contribution lies in the application and synthesis of these methods for QE. While this is a valuable empirical contribution, the paper could be strengthened by more explicitly positioning itself as an application/methodological study and discussing its place within the broader ML literature more clearly.
2.  **Constrained Model and Feature Scope:** The experiments are restricted to GP models using a standard set of 17 baseline features. The field of QE has largely progressed to neural architectures (e.g., predictor-estimator models) that utilize pre-trained language models and achieve state-of-the-art performance. It is therefore unclear how the findings from this GP-based study translate to modern neural QE systems. A discussion of how these concepts could be applied in a neural context (e.g., using Monte Carlo dropout, deep ensembles, or conformal prediction) would have significantly broadened the paper's impact and relevance.
3.  **Inconclusive Results for a Key Proposal:** The paper proposes Matèrn kernels as a way to better handle noisy QE data by relaxing the smoothness assumptions of the standard EQ kernel. However, the empirical results are, in the authors' own words, "less conclusive." Table 1 shows that the benefits of using a Matèrn kernel are marginal at best on two of the three datasets. This weakens one of the paper's core modeling claims.
4.  **The Asymmetric Risk Scenarios Feel Somewhat Contrived:** While the motivation for asymmetric risk is strong, the specific experimental setup feels a bit artificial. The weights for the loss functions (`w`) are chosen without external justification. A more compelling experiment might have involved simulating a downstream task (e.g., filtering sentences for post-editing) and showing how tuning `w` affects a real-world metric like post-editor productivity or overall translation cost, thereby demonstrating how the optimal `w` could be selected in practice.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your work convincingly demonstrates the benefits of probabilistic modeling within the GP framework. How do you envision applying the core ideas of this paper—uncertainty-aware evaluation with NLPD and adaptation to asymmetric risks—to the current state-of-the-art neural QE models? What techniques (e.g., deep ensembles, MC-dropout, Bayesian neural networks) do you believe are most promising for generating the necessary predictive distributions in that context?
2.  The empirical evidence for the utility of Matèrn kernels was weak. Do you have a hypothesis for why the expected benefits of relaxing the smoothness assumption did not consistently appear? Could it be that the 17 hand-crafted features are already relatively smooth, such that the stronger assumptions of the EQ kernel are not detrimental?
3.  In Section 4.3, you discuss the failure of the linex loss for Warped GPs in the pessimistic scenario. You suggest the loss function may be biased towards high-variance models. An alternative hypothesis could be that for the highly non-Gaussian posteriors produced by Warped GPs, the Bayes risk estimator for the linex loss (which relies only on the posterior mean and variance) is a poor approximation and thus yields suboptimal point estimates. Could you comment on this alternative explanation?
