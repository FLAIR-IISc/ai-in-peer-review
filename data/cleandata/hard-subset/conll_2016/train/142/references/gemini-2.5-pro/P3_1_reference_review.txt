### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the limitations of point estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) and advocates for probabilistic models that provide uncertainty information. The authors argue that uncertainty is crucial for practical decision-making in translation workflows, such as determining whether a segment should be post-edited. The paper focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs) and explores extensions like Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better model the noisy and non-negative nature of QE labels (e.g., post-editing time). A key part of the work is the proposal to evaluate these probabilistic models using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), a metric that, unlike MAE or Pearson's r, directly assesses the quality of the entire predictive distribution. Finally, the practical utility of these models is demonstrated in an asymmetric risk setting, where over- or under-estimation of quality has different costs.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:
1.  **A compelling argument and practical demonstration for shifting QE evaluation from point-estimates to probabilistic predictions.** The paper clearly articulates why uncertainty matters and successfully demonstrates its value in a realistic asymmetric risk scenario, a significant contribution to how the QE task should be framed.
2.  **The introduction and validation of NLPD as a more informative intrinsic evaluation metric for probabilistic QE.** By showing that standard metrics like MAE fail to capture significant improvements in uncertainty modeling, the authors provide the community with a better tool for model selection when confidence is important.
3.  **A rigorous empirical comparison of several GP variants for QE.** The study shows that Warped GPs are particularly well-suited for this task, offering substantially better-calibrated predictive distributions, which is a valuable finding for practitioners building probabilistic QE systems.

### Strengths

1.  **High practical relevance and timely motivation.** The paper tackles a fundamental limitation of most current QE systems. The move from "what is the quality score?" to "how confident are we in this quality score?" is a crucial step for integrating QE tools into real-world, risk-sensitive applications like post-editing workflows. The motivation is exceptionally clear and persuasive.
2.  **Methodological rigor and soundness.** The paper is well-grounded in the machine learning literature on probabilistic regression. The choice of GPs is well-justified for the problem setting, and the explored extensions (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) are sensible and clearly explained. The use of NLPD and Bayes risk estimators for evaluation represents the application of best practices from the ML community to an NLP task, strengthening the paper's foundations.
3.  **Strong and convincing experimental design.** The two-pronged evaluation strategy is highly effective. The intrinsic evaluation (Section 3) convincingly demonstrates the shortcomings of traditional metrics and highlights the superior probabilistic modeling of Warped GPs. The extrinsic evaluation in asymmetric risk scenarios (Section 4) provides a concrete, practical validation of why better probabilistic models matter, directly linking the intrinsic NLPD improvements to better performance on a downstream decision task.
4.  **Clarity and Readability.** The paper is very well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The authors do an excellent job of explaining complex concepts like Warped GPs and asymmetric loss functions in an accessible manner, making the work's contributions clear to a broad audience.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited scope of models and features.** The paper's empirical evaluation is confined exclusively to feature-based GP models. While this serves to make a focused point, the QE field has largely shifted towards end-to-end neural architectures (e.g., predictor-estimator models based on transformers). The claim that "other common models used in QE can only provide point estimates" is outdated, as methods like MC Dropout and deep ensembles are well-established for uncertainty estimation in neural networks. The lack of discussion on how these ideas would apply to modern SOTA models limits the paper's immediate impact and relevance to researchers working with neural QE.
2.  **Use of a dated feature set.** The experiments are based on a small set of 17 handcrafted features from the QuEst framework. While the authors' goal is not to achieve SOTA point-prediction performance, this choice makes it difficult to ascertain whether the findings would generalize to systems that use rich, high-dimensional representations from large pre-trained language models. The interaction between feature representation power and uncertainty modeling is a critical and unaddressed aspect.
3.  **Some experimental results are inconclusive.** The benefits of Matérn kernels over the standard EQ kernel were found to be marginal at best. More importantly, the results for the pessimistic scenario using the linex loss (Table 2) are counter-intuitive: the standard GP, argued to be a poorer probabilistic model, outperforms the superior Warped GP models. The authors' explanation is brief and speculative. This finding weakens the otherwise strong narrative that better probabilistic models (as measured by NLPD) universally lead to better downstream decisions. A deeper analysis of this phenomenon would have strengthened the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your work compellingly makes the case for probabilistic modeling using GPs. How do you envision these principles being applied to the current generation of neural QE models (e.g., COMET, TransQuest)? Could you briefly comment on the potential of techniques like Monte Carlo Dropout, deep ensembles, or Bayesian neural networks for capturing uncertainty in neural QE and how their uncertainty characteristics might differ from those of GPs?
2.  The experiments rely on a small, handcrafted feature set. What is your intuition about how using dense, high-dimensional features from pre-trained transformers would impact the results? For example, would the strong smoothness assumptions of the EQ kernel become more or less appropriate, and would you still expect Warped GPs to provide the same level of benefit over a standard GP?
3.  In the pessimistic scenario with linex loss, the standard GP outperforms the Warped GP models, despite the latter having much better NLPD scores. You suggest this is related to the variance of the predictive distribution. Could you elaborate on this? Does this imply that for certain high-risk, asymmetric applications, a model with less accurate calibration but higher variance might be paradoxically "safer"? What are the broader implications of this for model selection beyond just optimizing for NLPD?
