### Summary and Contributions

This paper argues for the importance of modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors contend that traditional point-estimate predictions and metrics like MAE are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios, such as post-editing workflows. The paper focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs) as an inherently probabilistic framework for QE. It systematically explores extensions to the standard GP model—namely, Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs—to better model the noisy and non-Gaussian nature of QE data. The core of the paper is an evaluation methodology that goes beyond point estimates, using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to assess the quality of the entire predictive distribution and demonstrating the utility of these distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios using specialized loss functions.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A strong argument and demonstration for evaluating QE models based on their predictive uncertainty.** The paper makes a compelling case for adopting metrics like NLPD that assess the quality of a model's confidence, rather than just its accuracy. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the QE evaluation paradigm.
2.  **A rigorous empirical comparison of different GP formulations for QE.** The paper systematically evaluates the impact of kernel choice (Matèrn vs. EQ) and likelihood modeling (Standard vs. Warped GPs), showing that modeling choices that improve the predictive distribution are not always captured by standard point-estimate metrics.
3.  **A practical application of probabilistic predictions in asymmetric risk scenarios.** By using Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric loss functions, the authors show how a single, well-calibrated probabilistic model can be flexibly adapted to different operational needs (e.g., optimistic vs. pessimistic decision-making) without retraining.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated and clearly written. The introduction uses a concrete post-editing scenario to immediately convince the reader of the need for uncertainty estimation. The theoretical background on GPs and their extensions is presented concisely and accessibly. This clarity makes the paper's arguments powerful and easy to follow.

2.  **Sound and Thorough Evaluation:** The experimental design is a major strength. The authors don't just propose a new model and show it gets a slightly better MAE. Instead, they introduce a more appropriate evaluation metric for their stated goal (NLPD) and show how their proposed models excel on it. The inclusion of the asymmetric risk evaluation in Section 4 is particularly strong, as it grounds the abstract concept of "better uncertainty" in a tangible application with practical consequences.

3.  **Significant and Actionable Findings:** The results provide clear takeaways for practitioners. The finding that MAE and Pearson's r fail to capture crucial differences in model quality (Table 1) is an important negative result. Conversely, the consistent and significant improvements in NLPD from using Warped GPs provide a strong, positive recommendation. This work effectively shifts the conversation from "what is the best quality score?" to "which model provides the most reliable information for decision-making?".

4.  **Broader Applicability:** While framed within QE, the paper's core message and methods are applicable to any text regression task where predictions inform decision-making (e.g., predicting review scores, emotion intensity, or financial metrics from text). The authors acknowledge this, and the paper serves as an excellent case study that could inspire similar work in other NLP subfields.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The techniques explored—Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs, and the NLPD metric—are all well-established in the machine learning literature. The primary contribution is their application to and advocacy within the QE domain. While this is a valuable contribution, the paper could be strengthened by more explicitly positioning itself as an application and analysis paper, rather than implying the development of new methods.

2.  **Comparison to a Limited Set of Baselines:** The experiments focus exclusively on variants of GPs. While SVR is mentioned as a popular non-probabilistic method, no quantitative comparison is provided. More importantly, the paper does not engage with contemporary neural network-based approaches to QE. Many modern QE systems use pre-trained transformers, and techniques exist to extract uncertainty from these models (e.g., Monte Carlo dropout, ensembling, or dedicated probabilistic layers). A comparison against such a baseline would have made the paper's conclusions more impactful and relevant to the current state-of-the-art in QE. The use of a small, 17-feature set, while justified for the sake of model comparison, feels somewhat dated.

3.  **Inconclusive Results in Pessimistic Scenario:** The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario (Table 2) are counter-intuitive, with the standard GP baseline outperforming the supposedly better Warped GP models. The authors offer a plausible hypothesis related to the variance of the predictive distributions, but the investigation feels incomplete. This surprising result undermines the otherwise clean narrative that Warped GPs are universally better for asymmetric risk. A more in-depth analysis of this phenomenon would have strengthened the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your work convincingly demonstrates the benefits of GP-based models over a standard GP baseline. However, the state-of-the-art in QE has largely moved towards large neural models (e.g., based on XLM-R). How do you see your findings translating to this new paradigm? Do you believe that methods for estimating uncertainty in neural networks could achieve similarly well-calibrated distributions, and would you expect a similar disconnect between point-estimate performance and uncertainty quality?

2.  In Section 4.3, you observe that for the pessimistic linex loss, the standard GP outperforms the Warped GP models, and you hypothesize this is because the linex loss is "biased towards models with high variance". Could you elaborate on this? Does this suggest that the linex loss is perhaps not a suitable evaluation metric for this task, or does it point to a fundamental weakness in the Warped GP's predictive distribution where it might become overconfident (i.e., have too little variance) in certain regions of the input space?
