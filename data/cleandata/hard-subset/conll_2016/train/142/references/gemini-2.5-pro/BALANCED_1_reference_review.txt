**Summary of the paper**

This paper advocates for moving beyond point estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) to probabilistic predictions that capture model uncertainty. The authors argue that uncertainty is critical for real-world decision-making applications, such as filtering sentences for post-editing. The work focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs), exploring extensions like Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs to better model the properties of QE data. A key part of the paper is the proposal to use Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as an evaluation metric that assesses the entire predictive distribution, rather than just a single point prediction. The authors demonstrate that NLPD reveals model improvements that are missed by standard metrics like MAE. Finally, they showcase the practical utility of these probabilistic models by applying them to asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., preferring to overestimate or underestimate quality), using Bayes risk estimators to tailor predictions to specific costs without needing to retrain the model.

**Main Contributions**

1.  **A compelling case for evaluating QE models probabilistically.** The paper's primary contribution is its well-executed argument that standard point-estimate metrics like MAE are insufficient for evaluating QE models intended for downstream decision-making. By systematically demonstrating that the NLPD metric captures significant improvements in model calibration and uncertainty representation that are otherwise invisible, the work provides a valuable methodological contribution to the field.
2.  **A practical framework for applying QE in asymmetric risk scenarios.** The paper successfully translates the abstract concept of predictive uncertainty into a practical tool for real-world applications. By framing different QE use-cases (e.g., gisting vs. post-editing for novices) as problems with asymmetric costs and using Bayes risk estimators to derive optimal predictions, the authors present an elegant and efficient way to adapt a single QE model to multiple, changing operational needs.
3.  **A thorough empirical analysis of advanced GP models for QE.** The paper provides a clear and insightful comparison of standard GPs against more sophisticated variants. The experiments convincingly show that Warped GPs, which learn a non-linear transformation of the target variable, are significantly better suited for modeling QE scores, leading to much better-calibrated predictive distributions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Framing.** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It clearly articulates the limitations of point estimates and provides concrete, intuitive examples (post-editing filtering, gisting) that ground the technical work in practical needs. This strong framing makes the contributions more accessible and impactful.
2.  **Sound and Novel Evaluation Methodology.** The use of NLPD is a major strength and is expertly justified. The experiments in Section 3 successfully prove the paper's thesis: that significant modeling improvements can be entirely missed by traditional metrics. The subsequent evaluation in Section 4, which directly assesses performance under asymmetric loss, is a novel and highly relevant way to demonstrate the practical value of the proposed models.
3.  **Insightful Analysis and Discussion.** The analysis of the results is clear and thoughtful. The authors provide plausible explanations for their findings, such as why Warped GPs outperform standard GPs on NLPD (they avoid assigning probability mass to impossible negative scores) and why certain models perform differently on smaller versus larger datasets. The discussion of the counter-intuitive results for the linex loss (Section 4.3) is particularly strong, demonstrating a deep engagement with the model behaviors.
4.  **Constructive and Generalizable Message.** The paper's core message—that uncertainty matters and should be properly modeled and evaluated—is broadly applicable beyond QE to many other NLP regression tasks. The work serves as a high-quality example of how to approach this problem systematically.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Model Architectures.** The paper's focus is exclusively on Gaussian Processes. While GPs are a reasonable choice for the small datasets used, the field of QE has largely shifted towards neural network-based architectures. The paper would be significantly stronger if it discussed how its core ideas could be extended to neural models, for instance via Bayesian NNs, Monte Carlo Dropout, or deep ensembles. The claim that "other common models used in QE can only provide point estimates" is an oversimplification that weakens the paper's positioning relative to the current state of the art.
2.  **Use of a Basic Feature Set.** The authors use a standard but simple set of 17 handcrafted features. State-of-the-art QE models now rely on rich representations from large pretrained language models. It is an open question whether the observed benefits of Warped GPs and the specific behaviors of different kernels would persist with these much more powerful and dense features. Experiments with a stronger feature set would have improved the generalizability of the findings.
3.  **Evaluation of Asymmetric Risk Remains Intrinsic.** While the framing of asymmetric risk scenarios is a strength, the evaluation in Section 4 is still based on calculating loss values rather than simulating the outcome of a downstream task. The paper would be more compelling if it included a simulation showing, for example, how using a risk-adjusted quality threshold improves a metric like "total post-editing time saved while maintaining a low rate of bad sentences passed to editors" compared to a baseline using only point estimates. This would provide a more concrete link between lower asymmetric loss and tangible application value.
4.  **Inconclusive Findings on Matèrn Kernels.** The paper proposes Matèrn kernels as a potentially better alternative to the standard EQ kernel for noisy data, but the results are reported as "less conclusive." While this is an honest reflection of the data, the paper does not delve deeper into why this might be. A brief analysis of whether the feature space is smoother than expected, or if the datasets are simply not large enough to show a significant difference, would have made this part of the investigation more complete.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  In Section 4.3, you hypothesize that the poor performance of Warped GPs with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario is due to their lower predictive variance. The Bayes risk estimator for the linex loss depends only on the mean and variance. Given that the posterior of a Warped GP is non-Gaussian, is it possible that this specific estimator is a poor approximation? Would an estimator derived via numerical optimization of the expected loss over the full posterior distribution yield different results?
2.  How do you envision applying the central ideas of this paper (probabilistic evaluation with NLPD, adaptation to asymmetric risk) to modern, large-scale neural QE models? What challenges do you foresee in obtaining well-calibrated uncertainty estimates from such models compared to the GP framework you used?
