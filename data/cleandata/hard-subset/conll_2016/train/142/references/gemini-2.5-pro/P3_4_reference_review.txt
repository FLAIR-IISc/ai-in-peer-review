### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the important but often overlooked issue of uncertainty estimation in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that traditional point estimates are insufficient for real-world applications where QE models inform decision-making, such as in post-editing workflows. The core of the work is to advocate for and explore probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), which provide a full predictive distribution over quality scores. The paper evaluates extensions to standard GPs, namely Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, which are theoretically better suited to the noisy and non-Gaussian nature of QE data.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:
1.  **A strong argument for better evaluation of QE models:** The paper effectively demonstrates the limitations of standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) and advocates for the adoption of a proper scoring rule, Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), which evaluates the entire predictive distribution, including uncertainty. This is a crucial contribution towards building more reliable and trustworthy QE systems.
2.  **Systematic application and evaluation of advanced GP models for QE:** The authors introduce and empirically validate the use of Warped GPs and Matérn kernels for QE. They show that Warped GPs, in particular, provide significantly better-calibrated predictions by correctly modeling the domain of the quality scores (e.g., positive post-editing times).
3.  **A practical demonstration of uncertainty in an application scenario:** The paper presents a compelling use case for predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios. By using Bayes risk estimators, it shows how a single probabilistic model can be adapted to different operational needs (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic decision-making) without any retraining, highlighting a key advantage of the probabilistic approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The critique of point estimates and the clear articulation of why uncertainty is critical for decision-making in practical MT workflows is compelling and timely. This work addresses a genuine gap between academic evaluation of QE and its practical deployment.
2.  **Sound and Principled Methodology:** The choice of methods is well-justified. GPs are a natural fit for this problem, and the specific extensions explored—Matérn kernels for relaxing smoothness assumptions and Warped GPs for handling non-Gaussian targets—are directly motivated by the known properties of QE data. The methodological explanation is clear and technically sound.
3.  **Rigorous and Insightful Evaluation:** The experimental design is strong. The use of NLPD as a primary metric is a major strength, and the results clearly show its ability to distinguish between models where traditional metrics cannot. The inclusion of an application-driven evaluation with asymmetric losses is a novel and significant contribution to the QE literature, moving beyond abstract metrics to tangible use cases.
4.  **Clarity of Exposition:** The paper is very well-written, accessible, and logically structured. Complex concepts like Warped GPs and Bayes risk are explained with admirable clarity, making the work easy to follow even for readers not deeply familiar with the GP literature. The discussion of the results is nuanced and provides valuable insights.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Model Comparison:** The paper focuses exclusively on Gaussian Processes. While the authors justify this by pointing to their strength in low-data regimes, the broader NLP and QE fields are increasingly dominated by neural network-based approaches. A discussion of how these uncertainty quantification principles could apply to neural models (e.g., via Monte Carlo dropout, deep ensembles, or Bayesian neural networks) is missing. The statement that "other common models used in QE can only provide point estimates" is an oversimplification in the current landscape and weakens the paper's positioning relative to contemporary research.
2.  **Dated Feature Set:** The experiments rely on a small, standard set of 17 hand-crafted features. While the goal is to compare model architectures rather than achieve state-of-the-art performance, modern QE systems leverage powerful pre-trained representations (e.g., from XLM-R). It is unclear if the findings—particularly the marginal benefit of Matérn kernels and the specific behavior under asymmetric loss—would hold with much richer, higher-dimensional feature sets that might exhibit different noise properties and non-linear relationships.
3.  **Inconclusive Experimental Findings:** Some of the experimental results are inconclusive, which slightly tempers the strength of the conclusions. The benefits of Matérn kernels over the standard EQ kernel were found to be marginal and dataset-dependent. More notably, the perplexing result for the pessimistic linex loss scenario, where the theoretically inferior standard GP outperforms the warped models, is not fully resolved. The authors' hypothesis is plausible but speculative, leaving an important part of the analysis as future work.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The pessimistic linex loss results (Section 4.3) are fascinating. You hypothesize that the standard GP's higher variance (partially from placing mass on nonsensical negative values) is beneficial under this specific loss. Could this also imply that the Warped GP models, while better calibrated overall according to NLPD, might be systematically underestimating the variance (i.e., being overconfident) in certain regions of the input space, a flaw that this particular exponential loss function is highly sensitive to?
2.  Your argument for focusing on GPs is based on their suitability for the "low-data regimes typical for this task." With the advent of large pre-trained models, there is a trend towards fine-tuning on these small QE datasets. How do you see your proposed models comparing to a fine-tuned transformer-based QE model for which uncertainty is estimated using a technique like Monte Carlo dropout or ensembling? Do you believe GPs would still hold an advantage in terms of calibration quality?
3.  In your main experiments, point predictions were based on the posterior median. Given that the Warped GP posterior is asymmetric, the mean and median will differ. Did you also evaluate using the posterior mean for the point-estimate metrics? While MAE is minimized by the median, other metrics like RMSE are minimized by the mean, and it would be interesting to see if the choice of optimal estimator affects the (admittedly secondary) point-estimate comparison.
