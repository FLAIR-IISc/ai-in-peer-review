### Summary of the paper

This paper investigates the importance of modeling prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that traditional point estimates (e.g., a single quality score) are insufficient for real-world decision-making workflows, such as determining whether a translation should be post-edited. The work proposes using Gaussian Processes (GPs), a probabilistic regression framework, to generate well-calibrated predictive distributions instead of single-point predictions. The paper explores extensions to the standard GP model, namely the use of Matérn kernels to handle noisy data and Warped GPs to model the non-Gaussian, strictly positive nature of quality scores like post-editing time.

The core of the paper is a two-part evaluation. First, an intrinsic evaluation introduces the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric to the QE task, arguing it is more suitable than standard metrics like Mean Absolute Error (MAE) for assessing probabilistic models because it evaluates the entire predictive distribution. Second, an extrinsic evaluation demonstrates the practical utility of these models in scenarios with asymmetric risk (where over- or under-estimation of quality have different costs), showing how Bayes risk estimators can be derived from the predictive distribution to optimize for different loss functions without retraining the model. Experiments on three language pairs show that Warped GPs generally provide better-calibrated predictions (lower NLPD) and perform better in asymmetric risk scenarios.

### Contributions

1.  **Introduction of a more appropriate evaluation framework for probabilistic QE models.** The paper makes a strong case for moving beyond point-estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson's r. Its primary contribution is the application and advocacy of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) for evaluating QE models. This shifts the focus of evaluation from prediction accuracy alone to the quality and calibration of the model's uncertainty estimates, which is crucial for the downstream applications that motivate the paper.
2.  **A systematic application and analysis of advanced Gaussian Process models for QE.** The paper successfully applies established but non-trivial GP extensions—specifically Warped GPs and Matérn kernels—to the QE task. It provides a clear justification for why these extensions are theoretically well-suited for QE data (e.g., strictly positive labels, potential for noise) and demonstrates their empirical benefits through a well-designed set of experiments.
3.  **A practical demonstration of leveraging predictive uncertainty for decision-making.** The work provides a concrete example of how to use the full predictive distribution in asymmetric risk scenarios. By deriving Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric loss functions, the paper shows how a single probabilistic model can be flexibly adapted to different operational needs (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic filtering of translations) without costly retraining.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear motivation and problem framing.** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It clearly articulates the practical limitations of point estimates in QE and builds a compelling narrative around the need for uncertainty. The use of the post-editing workflow as a running example makes the abstract concept of uncertainty tangible and its importance immediately obvious. The introduction of asymmetric risk scenarios in Section 4 is a strong, concrete application that reinforces this motivation.
2.  **Technically sound and well-explained methodology.** The paper's technical core is solid. The description of Gaussian Processes, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs is clear and concise. The choice of these particular methods is well-justified within the context of the problem (e.g., using Warped GPs to handle the positive-only domain of post-editing time). The derivations and explanations are easy to follow for a reader familiar with machine learning fundamentals.
3.  **Rigorous and insightful evaluation.** The evaluation is a key strength. The explicit comparison between NLPD and standard point-estimate metrics (Table 1) effectively demonstrates the paper's central thesis: that models can differ significantly in the quality of their uncertainty estimates even when their point-prediction accuracy is similar. This analysis provides strong evidence that NLPD is a more informative metric for this type of modeling.
4.  **Demonstrated practical utility.** The asymmetric risk experiments in Section 4 are a valuable contribution. They elevate the paper from a purely methodological exploration to one that offers a practical solution for real-world QE deployment. The ability to derive optimal estimators for different loss functions from a single trained model is a significant practical advantage, and the experiments successfully demonstrate the benefits of the proposed probabilistic models in these settings.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited methodological novelty.** The methods presented—Gaussian Processes, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs—are well-established techniques from the machine learning literature. The paper's contribution lies in their application to QE, not in the development of new methods. While this application-focused exploration is valuable for the NLP community, the work's novelty from a core machine learning perspective is limited.
2.  **Experimental setup is not state-of-the-art for QE.** The experiments rely on a small set of 17 hand-crafted features and datasets in the order of a few thousand instances. In recent years, the state-of-the-art in QE has shifted decisively towards large, pre-trained neural models (e.g., architectures based on BERT or XLM-R) that operate directly on text and have demonstrated superior performance on point-estimate tasks. It is unclear how the findings from this feature-based GP analysis would translate to these more complex and currently dominant neural architectures, which limits the immediate applicability of the results to modern QE systems.
3.  **Some experimental results are inconclusive or underexplored.** The paper itself notes that the benefits of Matérn kernels over the standard EQ kernel are "less conclusive". More significantly, the analysis of the pessimistic scenario with the linex loss function reveals that the proposed Warped GP models perform worse than the standard GP baseline. The authors offer a plausible hypothesis for this phenomenon (related to the model's posterior variance) but leave a deeper investigation for future work. This unresolved issue slightly weakens the paper's otherwise strong argument about the universal applicability of its proposed models in asymmetric scenarios.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The experiments are conducted on feature-based systems, whereas the current state-of-the-art in QE is dominated by neural architectures. How do you see the ideas presented in this paper—particularly the use of GPs and the NLPD evaluation metric—applying in the context of modern neural QE? Could GPs be used to model the uncertainty of features extracted from a neural model, or would you advocate for directly estimating uncertainty within the neural models themselves (e.g., via MC Dropout or deep ensembles)?
2.  The result for the pessimistic linex loss, where Warped GPs underperform the standard GP, is very interesting. Your hypothesis is that the estimator is not "pushed" enough due to the model's lower variance. Could this suggest a misalignment between the properties of the Warped GP posterior (which is well-calibrated in terms of NLPD) and the specific demands of the linex loss, which heavily penalizes errors exponentially? Does this imply a potential pitfall where a model that is "better" in a general probabilistic sense (lower NLPD) might be worse for a specific, highly-skewed loss function?
