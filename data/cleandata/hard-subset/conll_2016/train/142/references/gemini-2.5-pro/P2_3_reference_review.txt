### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper argues for the importance of modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors contend that standard point-estimate predictions and their corresponding evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) are insufficient for real-world applications where QE models inform decision-making, such as post-editing workflows. The paper proposes using Gaussian Processes (GPs), specifically more advanced variants like Warped GPs and GPs with Matèrn kernels, to produce well-calibrated predictive distributions. The core of the work is a demonstration that these models provide better uncertainty estimates, which is revealed by a probabilistic evaluation metric (Negative Log Predictive Density, NLPD) but is largely invisible to standard metrics. Finally, the authors show the practical utility of these uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-estimation of quality has different costs.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A strong, practical argument and demonstration for evaluating QE models probabilistically.** The paper effectively introduces and motivates the use of NLPD for QE, showing empirically that it captures crucial differences in model quality that metrics like MAE and Pearson's r miss. This is the most significant contribution, as it advocates for a more principled evaluation methodology for this task.
2.  **An application-driven showcase of uncertainty's value in asymmetric risk scenarios.** The framing of "pessimistic" and "optimistic" scenarios (e.g., deciding whether to post-edit vs. publish directly) is a compelling and realistic application. Demonstrating how to use Bayes risk estimators to cater to these scenarios without retraining the model is a valuable practical insight.
3.  A systematic comparison of different GP models for QE, successfully arguing that standard GP assumptions (Gaussian likelihood, infinitely smooth functions) are suboptimal for QE data and that models like Warped GPs are a better fit.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and motivated. The introduction immediately grounds the abstract concept of uncertainty in a concrete, relatable QE scenario (post-editing). The distinction between intrinsic evaluation (Section 3) and an application-based one (Section 4) provides a clear and logical structure to the paper's argument. The explanations of GPs, warping functions, and asymmetric losses are clear and concise.

2.  **Sound and Convincing Experimental Design:** The experiments are thoughtfully designed to support the central thesis.
    *   The choice to compare standard GPs with more sophisticated variants (Warped, Matèrn) directly tests the paper's modeling hypotheses.
    *   The results in Table 1 provide compelling evidence for the main claim: Warped GPs achieve substantially better NLPD scores, demonstrating superior uncertainty modeling, while the point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) show much less pronounced or inconsistent differences. This highlights the inadequacy of standard metrics for this purpose.
    *   The asymmetric risk experiments in Section 4 are a strong, practical validation. Instead of just claiming that uncertainty is useful, the authors provide a concrete mechanism (Bayes risk estimators) and show that models with better probabilistic calibrations (the Warped GPs) perform better under these application-specific loss functions.

3.  **Important Message for the NLP Community:** The paper's core message—that we should move beyond point estimates for tasks that inform downstream decisions—is timely and important. QE is a perfect example of such a task, but the lessons are broadly applicable to many other text regression and classification problems in NLP. This paper serves as both an excellent case study and a practical tutorial on how to model and evaluate uncertainty.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The primary weakness is that the techniques employed—Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, and asymmetric loss functions—are all well-established in the machine learning literature. The contribution lies entirely in their novel application, combination, and argumentation within the context of QE. While this is a valuable contribution, the paper does not propose any new methods itself.

2.  **Narrow Focus on Gaussian Processes:** The paper exclusively focuses on GPs, justifying this by their probabilistic nature and historical strength in QE. However, the state-of-the-art in QE has largely shifted to large pre-trained neural models (e.g., based on XLM-R). The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of how its arguments and principles apply to these modern architectures. For instance, it could have mentioned or briefly compared GP-based uncertainty to methods like Monte Carlo Dropout or deep ensembles, which are commonly used to estimate uncertainty in neural networks. This omission makes the work feel slightly disconnected from the current SOTA in the field.

3.  **Use of a Basic Feature Set:** The experiments rely on a small set of 17 hand-crafted features. The authors rightly state that their goal is not to achieve SOTA point-prediction performance but to compare models. However, this choice limits the generalizability of the findings. The behavior and relative performance of these GP models might differ significantly when used with high-dimensional, dense features extracted from modern transformer-based encoders. For example, the utility of ARD kernels is less clear with thousands of opaque features. This makes it harder to assess how these specific models would perform as part of a modern, competitive QE system.
