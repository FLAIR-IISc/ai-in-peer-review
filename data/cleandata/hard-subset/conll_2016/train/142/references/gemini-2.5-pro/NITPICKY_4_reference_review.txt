### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on quantifying prediction uncertainty. The authors argue that standard point-estimate metrics like MAE are insufficient for evaluating models intended for real-world decision-making.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  An empirical evaluation of several GP model variants for QE, including the use of Matérn kernels and Warped GPs. The paper demonstrates that these more sophisticated models provide better-calibrated predictive distributions, a difference that is captured by the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric but often missed by standard point-estimate metrics.
2.  A practical demonstration of how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios. The authors show that by using Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric loss functions (Asymmetric Linear and Linex), predictions can be tailored to specific application needs (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic) without retraining the underlying model.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and Important Problem:** The paper addresses a crucial, often-overlooked aspect of QE: the inadequacy of point estimates for practical applications. The motivation provided in the introduction, particularly the post-editing scenario, is clear and compelling. Arguing for the importance of uncertainty is a valuable contribution to the QE community.

2.  **Principled Probabilistic Evaluation:** The adoption of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a primary evaluation metric is a major strength. This moves the evaluation beyond simple error metrics and directly assesses the quality of the entire predictive distribution, which is the core focus of the paper. The comparison in Section 3.2, which shows that MAE and Pearson's r fail to distinguish between models with different uncertainty characteristics, effectively proves the authors' point.

3.  **Novel Application to Asymmetric Risk:** The exploration of asymmetric loss scenarios in Section 4 is a strong and practical contribution. It provides a concrete example of how uncertainty estimates can be operationalized. The use of Minimum Bayes Risk estimators to derive optimal predictions without retraining is an elegant and efficient approach that would be highly relevant for production systems where decision-making criteria might change.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Numerous Formatting and Presentation Errors:** The paper suffers from a lack of polish that detracts from its overall quality.
    *   The reference section is riddled with errors, likely from a corrupted BibTeX file. Entries contain stray characters like `\.,?` and `\Q \E` (e.g., Beck et al., 2014b; Nguyen and O.Connor., 2015). Author names are formatted inconsistently ("Callisonburch" vs. "Callison-burch", "Hernández.Lobato", "O.Connor"). Several entries are incomplete or have formatting issues (e.g., double comma in "WMT14,,"). This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a conference submission.
    *   There are minor grammatical errors, such as "The remaining of this paper" instead of "The remainder..." in the introduction.
    *   Figure 1 is mentioned in the text but is not included in the paper.

2.  **Limited Empirical Scope and Generalizability:** The experiments are conducted using a small, standard set of 17 features. While the authors justify this by stating their goal is not to achieve state-of-the-art point predictions, it raises questions about how these uncertainty models would perform in a more realistic setting with hundreds or thousands of sparse features, which is common in top-performing QE systems. The conclusions about the superiority of certain kernels or warping functions may not generalize to a higher-dimensional feature space. Furthermore, the `en-es` dataset is very small (858 instances), and the authors themselves note that this likely leads to overfitting, which weakens the conclusions drawn from it.

3.  **Inconclusive and Under-analyzed Results for Linex Loss:** The analysis in the pessimistic scenario for the linex loss is weak. The authors observe that warped GP models perform worse than the standard GP baseline and offer a speculative explanation that the models' lower variance prevents the estimator from being sufficiently conservative. This is not deeply investigated. More critically, Footnote 3 reveals that for the `en-de` dataset, the initial `w=-0.75` led to "loss values on the order of 10^7", indicating extreme numerical instability. Simply choosing a different `w` sidesteps this serious issue rather than analyzing its cause. This failure undermines the claim that these models are robustly beneficial in *all* asymmetric scenarios.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The poor performance and numerical instability of the warped GP models under the pessimistic linex loss is a significant negative result. Could you elaborate on why this might be happening? Does this point to a fundamental incompatibility between the posterior distributions of warped GPs (which are transformed and potentially skewed) and the linex loss estimator, which assumes a simple mean and variance? Was any other diagnostic performed besides observing the large loss values?

2.  Your experiments use a small, 17-feature set. State-of-the-art QE systems often use vastly larger and sparser feature sets. How do you expect the comparison between standard, Matérn, and Warped GPs to change in such a high-dimensional setting? In particular, do you believe the more complex warping function (3 tanh terms) would be more or less prone to overfitting relative to a simple log-transform?

3.  Given the sheer number of formatting errors in the reference list and other minor issues throughout the paper, can you assure the reviewers that a thoroughly proofread and corrected version will be provided if the paper is accepted?
