### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper argues for the importance of modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors posit that traditional point estimates (e.g., a single quality score) and their corresponding evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios, such as determining whether a translation should be post-edited.

The main contributions of this work, as I see them, are:

1.  **Advocacy and Demonstration of Probabilistic QE:** The primary contribution is a compelling case for shifting the focus of QE from simple point prediction to probabilistic modeling. The paper introduces and evaluates Gaussian Process (GP) models specifically chosen for their suitability to the task (Warped GPs to handle positive-only quality scores, and Matérn kernels to handle noisy data). It successfully demonstrates that these models provide better-calibrated predictive distributions.
2.  **Introduction of Principled Evaluation Metrics:** The paper highlights the inadequacy of standard QE metrics and introduces Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to the QE task. This metric evaluates the entire predictive distribution, not just a single point, and the experimental results clearly show that NLPD can distinguish between models that appear similar under MAE or Pearson's r.
3.  **A Practical Application Framework using Asymmetric Risk:** A significant contribution is the framing of QE as a decision problem under asymmetric risk. The paper provides a concrete and practical application for uncertainty estimates by showing how to derive minimum Bayes risk estimators for different loss functions (e.g., being overly optimistic vs. overly pessimistic) without retraining the model.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong and Clear Motivation:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction clearly explains *why* uncertainty is crucial in practical QE workflows. The post-editing and gisting examples in Section 4 are concrete, intuitive, and effectively illustrate the limitations of point estimates and symmetric loss functions. This strong connection to real-world applications makes the work highly relevant.
2.  **Sound and Well-Justified Methodological Choices:** The selection of GP-based models is well thought out. Standard GPs are a reasonable probabilistic baseline, but the extensions are particularly apt. The use of **Warped GPs** is a principled way to address the domain mismatch between the Gaussian likelihood (support over all real numbers) and the nature of QE scores (strictly positive values like post-editing time). Similarly, the exploration of **Matérn kernels** to relax the strong smoothness assumption of the common EQ/RBF kernel is a sensible choice for potentially noisy data.
3.  **Rigorous and Insightful Evaluation:** The experimental design is solid. The use of NLPD as the primary evaluation metric is a major strength, and the results in Table 1 compellingly demonstrate its value over traditional metrics. The discussion is nuanced and insightful, for example, by correctly identifying that the better performance of Warped GPs is due to correctly modeling the support of the data, and by hypothesizing that differences between warping functions (log vs. tanh) may be due to dataset size and overfitting.
4.  **Novel and Practical Application:** The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios in Section 4 is a standout feature. Showing how a single probabilistic model can be used to generate optimal predictions for various, changing loss functions via Bayes risk estimation is elegant and highly practical. This moves the work beyond a purely academic exercise and provides a clear blueprint for deploying more sophisticated QE systems in production environments. The honest discussion of the inconclusive results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario is also a strength, as it provides a thoughtful analysis of the interaction between the loss function and model properties (variance).

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Models:** The paper's focus is exclusively on Gaussian Processes. While this is justified by their inherently probabilistic nature, the state-of-the-art in QE has largely shifted to neural network-based architectures (e.g., predictor-estimator models using pre-trained transformers). The paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of how its core ideas—the need for uncertainty and appropriate evaluation—apply to these more modern architectures. Mentioning techniques like Monte Carlo dropout, deep ensembles, or Bayesian neural networks would connect the work more directly to current research trends. As it stands, the contributions might be perceived as being limited to an older class of models.
2.  **Use of a Basic Feature Set:** The experiments rely on a set of 17 handcrafted features from the QuEst framework. The authors acknowledge this is not state-of-the-art but state their goal is to compare models, not achieve top performance. However, this choice makes it difficult to know if the conclusions would hold in a modern QE system that uses rich, dense representations from models like BERT or XLM-R. The behavior of kernels and warping functions might be different when the input space is a high-dimensional embedding space rather than a low-dimensional space of handcrafted features.
3.  **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing:** The paper presents averaged results from 10-fold cross-validation but does not report whether the observed improvements are statistically significant. For instance, in Table 1 for the fr-en dataset, the NLPD scores for models with the tanh3 warping function are very close (-2.31, -2.30, -2.29). Are these differences statistically significant? Adding significance tests (e.g., a paired t-test) would make the claims about model superiority more robust.
4.  **Potentially Ad-Hoc Parameter Choice for Asymmetric Loss:** In Section 4.2, the authors note that they had to choose a different weight `w` for the linex loss on the en-de dataset to avoid extremely large loss values. While they provide a plausible explanation in the discussion, this adjustment feels somewhat post-hoc and could be seen as cherry-picking a parameter that makes the results look reasonable. A more thorough justification for the chosen range of `w` values, or an analysis of model performance across a wider range of these parameters, would strengthen this section.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your work makes a very strong case for the importance of uncertainty in QE using Gaussian Processes. How do you see these principles and evaluation methods (especially NLPD and asymmetric risk) translating to the current state-of-the-art neural QE models (e.g., COMET, or BERT-based predictor-estimator models)? Would you expect Warped GPs to still be a competitive approach if used on top of embeddings from these models, or would methods like deep ensembles or MC dropout be more appropriate?
2.  Could you comment on how the choice of a relatively simple, 17-dimensional feature set might influence your findings? Specifically, do you believe the benefits of Matérn kernels (over EQ) or learned tanh warping functions (over a simple log transform) would be more or less pronounced when using high-dimensional, dense features from pre-trained language models?
