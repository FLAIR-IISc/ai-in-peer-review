### Summary and Contributions

This paper argues for a shift in how Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) is modeled and evaluated. Instead of focusing on point estimates, the authors advocate for probabilistic models that provide uncertainty estimates. The core of the paper is built around three main ideas:

1.  **A new evaluation paradigm for QE:** The primary contribution is the introduction and advocacy of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate evaluation metric for QE. Unlike traditional metrics like MAE or Pearson's r, NLPD evaluates the entire predictive distribution, properly rewarding well-calibrated models that know when they are uncertain.
2.  **Application of advanced probabilistic models to QE:** The paper explores extensions to Gaussian Processes (GPs), specifically Warped GPs and Matèrn kernels, arguing that these are better suited to the statistical properties of QE data (e.g., strictly positive scores, noisy labels) than standard GP regression.
3.  **Demonstration of practical utility:** The work shows how predictive distributions can be used to make decisions in scenarios with asymmetric risk (e.g., when underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating it). It demonstrates how Bayes risk estimators can be derived for different loss functions without retraining the underlying model.

### Strengths

1.  **High Conceptual Novelty and Potential Impact:** This paper's greatest strength is its bold and compelling argument to re-evaluate how we approach QE. By moving the focus from incremental improvements in point-estimate accuracy to the quality of uncertainty estimation, the work addresses a deep, practical need. The push to adopt NLPD is a significant contribution that could beneficially reorient future research in the field toward building more reliable and useful models for real-world decision-making. This is exactly the kind of direction-setting work the community needs.
2.  **Excellent Practical Motivation:** The work is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction clearly lays out why point estimates are insufficient for deployment scenarios like post-editing filtering. The entire section on asymmetric risk is a brilliant demonstration of this principle, connecting the abstract concept of a predictive distribution to concrete, adaptable business logic ("pessimistic" vs. "optimistic" scenarios). This provides a clear and powerful answer to the "why should I care?" question.
3.  **Principled and Thoughtful Modeling:** The authors demonstrate a sophisticated understanding of their chosen method. Instead of simply applying a standard GP, they carefully consider the nature of the QE task and data. The introduction of Warped GPs to handle the non-Gaussian, positive-only distribution of quality scores is an elegant and effective solution. The experiments clearly show that this modeling choice leads to substantially better-calibrated distributions (lower NLPD), a difference entirely missed by traditional metrics.
4.  **Insightful and Honest Analysis:** The discussion is a highlight. The authors astutely identify that the discrepancy between NLL on training data and NLPD on test data for the smaller dataset is evidence of overfitting, underscoring the importance of proper out-of-sample probabilistic evaluation. Furthermore, they don't shy away from inconclusive or counterintuitive results, such as the mixed performance of Matèrn kernels or the puzzling behavior under the pessimistic linex loss. This honest reporting, complete with hypotheses and suggestions for future work, strengthens the paper's credibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Empirical Scope on Modern Architectures:** The paper's empirical validation rests on feature-based systems and datasets that, while appropriate for a proof-of-concept, do not reflect the current state of the art in QE, which is dominated by large-scale, pretrained neural models. The central claims about uncertainty would be immensely more powerful if they were demonstrated on a modern neural QE architecture. It remains an open question how these specific GP-based techniques and findings translate to the deep learning paradigm where uncertainty estimation is notoriously difficult.
2.  **Inconclusive Findings on Specific Model Choices:** While the benefit of Warped GPs is clearly demonstrated, the case for Matèrn kernels is weak, with the authors themselves noting the results are "less conclusive". Similarly, the investigation of asymmetric losses yields a confusing result for the pessimistic linex loss, where the better-calibrated models perform worse. While the authors' hypothesis is plausible, this unresolved issue slightly weakens the paper's prescriptive power, leaving the reader with a clear mandate to use Warped GPs but less guidance on other modeling details. This is a minor flaw, however, as the core message remains intact.
3.  **Lack of Discussion on Scalability:** The paper correctly states that GP scalability is not an issue for the small datasets used. However, this sidesteps a critical question for the future relevance of the proposed methods. As the field moves toward ever-larger datasets, the computational cost of standard GPs will become prohibitive. A brief discussion acknowledging this limitation and pointing towards potential solutions (e.g., sparse GPs, integration with neural models via methods like deep kernel learning or variational inference) would have strengthened the paper's forward-looking perspective.

### Questions to Authors

Your work makes a very convincing case for probabilistic modeling in the context of feature-based QE. How do you see these ideas translating to the now-dominant paradigm of neural QE (e.g., predictor-estimator models based on XLM-R)? Do you believe the need for well-calibrated uncertainty is even more acute for these large models, which are often criticized for their overconfidence, and could methods like deep GPs or Bayesian neural networks be a fruitful direction?
