### Summary of the Paper
This paper advocates for moving beyond point estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) to probabilistic predictions that quantify uncertainty. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs), a well-established probabilistic kernel method, for this purpose. They explore extensions to the standard GP model, namely the use of Matèrn kernels to relax smoothness assumptions and Warped GPs to better model non-Gaussian, strictly positive quality scores (e.g., post-editing time). The paper introduces the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more suitable evaluation metric for such probabilistic models, arguing that it captures uncertainty better than standard metrics like MAE or Pearson's r. Finally, the authors present a case study on how these predictive distributions can be applied in scenarios with asymmetric risk, where over- or under-estimation of quality carry different penalties.

### Contributions
1.  **Application and Analysis of Probabilistic Models for QE:** The primary contribution is the application of established, but not widely used in this subfield, GP variants (Warped GPs, Matèrn kernels) to the QE task. The paper provides an analysis of how these modeling choices affect the predictive distributions.
2.  **Advocacy for Uncertainty-Aware Evaluation:** The paper makes a case for using NLPD as an evaluation metric for QE, arguing that it better reflects the quality of a probabilistic model compared to traditional point-estimate metrics.
3.  **A Case Study on Asymmetric Risk:** The paper demonstrates how the predictive distributions from GP models can be used to derive optimal estimators under asymmetric loss functions, providing a proof-of-concept for applications where the cost of errors is not symmetric.

### Strengths
1.  **Clear Motivation:** The paper makes a compelling and well-reasoned argument for why uncertainty quantification is important for the practical application of QE in real-world workflows, such as deciding whether a segment should be post-edited or translated from scratch. This framing correctly identifies a limitation of the standard point-estimate paradigm.
2.  **Methodologically Sound Presentation:** The authors provide a clear and concise overview of the underlying theory of Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs. The introduction of NLPD and the discussion on asymmetric losses are also well-grounded in existing machine learning and econometrics literature.
3.  **Reproducible Experimental Setup:** The experiments are conducted on publicly available datasets using a standard feature set (QuEst-17) and a clear cross-validation protocol. This transparency facilitates reproducibility and allows for a clear understanding of the experimental conditions.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core methods presented—Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs, and the NLPD metric—are all well-established techniques from the machine learning literature, dating back over a decade (e.g., Snelson et al., 2004; Rasmussen and Williams, 2006). The contribution of this work lies entirely in their application to QE, which frames the paper as an application study rather than a work presenting novel techniques.
2.  **Lack of Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper's most significant weakness is its failure to compare against the dominant non-probabilistic baseline in QE, Support Vector Regression (SVR). SVR has been the workhorse of QE for years, consistently performing strongly in WMT shared tasks. While the authors mention SVR, they do not include it in their experiments. Furthermore, uncertainty estimates can be derived from SVR models using techniques like bootstrapping. A comparison against a bootstrapped SVR would have provided a much stronger baseline and helped to convincingly argue for the superiority of the GP-based approach. As it stands, the paper only compares different flavors of GPs against a basic GP, which is an insufficient comparison.
3.  **Unconvincing Empirical Gains on Standard Metrics:** While the authors argue that point-estimate metrics are insufficient, the results in Table 1 show that the proposed models offer only marginal, and often inconsistent, improvements in MAE and Pearson's r over the standard GP. The entire argument for the superiority of Warped GPs rests on the NLPD metric, which is not standard in the QE community. This creates a somewhat circular argument where a new evaluation metric is proposed primarily to demonstrate the value of the proposed models, without a corresponding significant improvement on metrics the community already uses and understands.
4.  **Limited Scale and Scope of Experiments:** The experiments are conducted on relatively small datasets with a basic 17-feature set. It is unclear whether the findings would generalize to more competitive settings that use hundreds or thousands of features, or to modern neural QE architectures where GPs would be computationally intractable. The asymmetric risk scenarios, while interesting, remain a hypothetical exercise and are not validated through a realistic simulation or user study that demonstrates tangible benefits in a post-editing workflow. The results for the linex loss are also admitted to be inconclusive, which weakens the claims about the general applicability of these models.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you justify the exclusion of Support Vector Regression (SVR) from your experimental comparisons? Given its status as a state-of-the-art method in non-neural QE, a comparison of both point estimates and uncertainty (e.g., via bootstrapping SVR) seems critical to properly contextualize the performance of your proposed GP models.
2.  The practical utility of your work is motivated by decision-making in workflows (e.g., post-editing). However, the evaluation under asymmetric risk is based on applying loss functions to the test data. Have you considered a more practical simulation? For example, one could simulate a post-editing budget by using the model's predictions (with an uncertainty threshold) to decide which sentences to post-edit, and then measure the true, cumulative post-editing time or final translation quality. This would provide more direct evidence of the real-world utility of your approach.
3.  Gaussian Processes scale poorly with the number of data points and features. How do you see the proposed methods being applied in the context of modern QE, which is moving towards large datasets and high-dimensional neural representations? Do you believe the conclusions drawn from this small-scale, feature-based setting would hold?
