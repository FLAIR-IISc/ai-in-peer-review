### Summary of the Paper
This paper argues for the importance of modeling predictive uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), moving beyond simple point estimates. The authors motivate this by highlighting downstream decision-making tasks, such as filtering sentences for post-editing, where confidence in a prediction is as important as the prediction itself. The paper focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a suitable probabilistic framework. It proposes and evaluates several extensions to the standard GP model for QE: using Matern kernels to relax smoothness assumptions and employing Warped GPs to better model the non-negative, non-Gaussian nature of quality scores (post-editing time). The core of the evaluation rests on the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric, which assesses the entire predictive distribution, unlike traditional metrics like MAE or Pearson's r. The authors show empirically that Warped GPs significantly improve uncertainty modeling, a benefit not fully captured by point-estimate metrics. Finally, they demonstrate the practical utility of these probabilistic models in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., where underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating), showing that Bayes risk estimators derived from their models outperform baselines without requiring model retraining.

### Contributions
1.  **Advocacy and Demonstration of Probabilistic Evaluation for QE:** The primary contribution is the compelling argument, supported by strong empirical evidence, that the QE field should move beyond point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) and adopt probabilistic metrics like NLPD. The paper clearly shows that standard metrics fail to distinguish between models with vastly different qualities of uncertainty estimation, which is crucial for real-world applications.
2.  **Application and Evaluation of Advanced GP Models for QE:** The paper successfully applies and analyzes more sophisticated GP models—specifically Warped GPs and GPs with Matern kernels—to the task of QE. It demonstrates that Warped GPs, which learn a non-linear mapping of the target variable, are substantially better suited for modeling post-editing time than standard GPs, leading to much better-calibrated predictive distributions.
3.  **A Novel Application-driven Evaluation Framework for QE:** The work introduces and formalizes the concept of asymmetric risk scenarios for QE evaluation. By using asymmetric loss functions (AL and Linex) and deriving the corresponding Bayes risk estimators, the authors provide a practical framework to test the utility of predictive distributions for downstream tasks, showing that better uncertainty modeling leads to better decision-making under varying risk profiles.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong and Important Motivation:** The paper's central premise—that point estimates are insufficient for many practical QE applications—is well-argued and highly relevant. The post-editing and gisting examples are concrete and effectively illustrate why uncertainty is a critical piece of information for decision-making. This work addresses a conceptual gap in how QE is often evaluated.
2.  **Sound and Rigorous Methodology:** The choice of GPs as the modeling framework is well-justified. The proposed extensions (Matern kernels, Warped GPs) are grounded in established machine learning literature and are theoretically appropriate for the data's characteristics (noise, positive-only scores). The experimental setup, using three datasets and 10-fold cross-validation, is robust.
3.  **Convincing and Insightful Evaluation:** The dual evaluation strategy is a major strength. The intrinsic evaluation using NLPD clearly demonstrates the superiority of Warped GPs in modeling the predictive distribution. The extrinsic evaluation in asymmetric risk scenarios provides a compelling, practical validation of *why* better uncertainty estimates matter. The finding that MAE and Pearson's r do not capture these improvements powerfully supports the paper's main thesis.
4.  **Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. Complex concepts like Gaussian Processes, warping functions, and asymmetric loss are explained clearly and concisely. The structure is logical, guiding the reader from motivation to methodology, results, and conclusions effectively.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Scope of Models:** The paper focuses exclusively on kernel methods (GPs). While this is justified by the goal of exploring probabilistic modeling, the broader QE and NLP fields have largely shifted to deep neural network (DNN) based approaches. The paper does not discuss how its findings might translate to these modern architectures. A discussion of uncertainty estimation techniques for DNNs (e.g., MC Dropout, deep ensembles, Bayesian neural networks) and how they might compare to GPs in this context would have significantly broadened the paper's impact and relevance.
2.  **Use of a Basic Feature Set:** The experiments rely on a standard but relatively small set of 17 handcrafted features. State-of-the-art QE systems now predominantly use features extracted from large pretrained language models (e.g., XLM-R). It is unclear if the same conclusions—particularly regarding the choice of kernels and the benefits of warping—would hold in a high-dimensional, dense feature space where the data's underlying geometry might be different. The strong non-linearities might be better captured by the deep models themselves, potentially altering the relative performance of the GP variants.
3.  **Inconclusive Results for Some Components:** The investigation into Matern kernels does not yield a conclusive benefit over the standard EQ kernel, weakening one of the paper's specific modeling suggestions. Similarly, the puzzling results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, while honestly reported and discussed, are left unresolved. While the authors suggest a plausible cause (low variance of warped models), this part of the analysis feels incomplete and slightly detracts from the otherwise strong empirical story.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Your work makes a very strong case for probabilistic modeling using GPs. How do you envision these principles being applied to the current state-of-the-art in QE, which is dominated by large transformer-based models? Do you believe methods like Monte Carlo dropout or deep ensembles could provide similarly well-calibrated uncertainty estimates, and would you expect them to face similar challenges (e.g., the need for a "warping"-like output transformation)?
2.  The experiments use a 17-feature set. Do you have any intuitions on how your findings, especially the comparison between EQ and Matern kernels, might change if you used a modern, high-dimensional feature set from a pretrained encoder like XLM-R? Would the smoothness assumptions made by different kernels become more or less critical?
3.  Regarding the pessimistic linex loss results (Table 2), your hypothesis that the estimator is not "pushed" enough due to the lower variance of warped models is interesting. Could this also suggest that the linex loss itself is perhaps too sensitive or unstable for this task, especially given its exponential nature? Did you consider evaluating with other asymmetric losses, like the asymmetric quadratic loss, which might be more stable?
