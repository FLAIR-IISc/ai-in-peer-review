### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) to move beyond simple point estimates and model predictive uncertainty. The authors argue that uncertainty is crucial for real-world applications like post-editing workflows. They explore extensions to standard GPs, namely Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, to better model the noisy and non-Gaussian nature of QE data. The paper proposes evaluating these models using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to assess the quality of the entire predictive distribution. Finally, it demonstrates the utility of these distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios using specialized loss functions, showing that a single probabilistic model can be adapted to different decision-making contexts without retraining.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  An empirical study arguing for the use of probabilistic evaluation metrics like NLPD for QE, demonstrating that common point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to distinguish between models with different qualities of uncertainty estimation.
2.  An application of asymmetric loss functions to QE, illustrating how predictive distributions from a single GP model can be used to derive optimal estimators for different risk profiles (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic scenarios).

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper is well-motivated, making a compelling case for the inadequacy of point estimates in practical QE pipelines. Highlighting the need for well-calibrated uncertainty to inform decision-making (e.g., whether to post-edit or discard a translation) is a valuable perspective for the field.

2.  **Sound Methodological Choices for Probabilistic Modeling:** The choice of Gaussian Processes is well-justified for this task, given their proven performance and inherent probabilistic nature. The exploration of more sophisticated variants like Matérn kernels (to handle noise) and Warped GPs (to handle non-Gaussian targets) is methodologically sound and appropriate for the problem domain.

3.  **Practical Application to Asymmetric Risk:** The investigation in Section 4, which connects predictive distributions to asymmetric loss functions, is a significant strength. This provides a concrete example of how uncertainty can be operationalized, moving the discussion from a purely academic exercise to one with clear practical implications for users of MT and QE technology.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited and Unconvincing Experimental Comparison:** The experiments exclusively compare different flavors of GP models. This narrow scope fails to convincingly establish the superiority of the proposed approach. The authors dismiss SVR as non-probabilistic but do not compare against simple and effective methods for generating uncertainty from it, such as bootstrapping, which they mention in passing. Furthermore, the evaluation omits comparisons to other modern probabilistic regression models, like Bayesian Neural Networks. The use of a weak 17-feature baseline, justified by a desire to focus on models rather than state-of-the-art performance, unfortunately, weakens the generalizability of the conclusions.

2.  **Lack of Rigor in the Asymmetric Risk Analysis:** The analysis in Section 4 feels superficial and raises more questions than it answers. The selection of weights `w` for the loss functions seems arbitrary. More concerning is the ad-hoc adjustment of `w` for the en-de dataset with the linex loss (Footnote 3), which was necessary to avoid numerically explosive values. This suggests a potential instability in the model or the loss function that is not properly investigated. The subsequent explanation for the poor performance of Warped GPs in the pessimistic linex scenario is speculative ("we believe...") and insufficient. A more thorough analysis is required before claiming these models are beneficial in these settings.

3.  **Numerous and Severe Presentation Flaws:** The paper suffers from a startling lack of polish, which detracts significantly from its credibility.
    *   **Technical Errors:** The formula for the Warped GP posterior distribution in Section 2.3 appears to be incorrect. The term in the exponent should almost certainly be squared and divided by 2, i.e., `- (f(y*) - µ*)² / (2σ*²)`. This is a fundamental error in the description of the model.
    *   **Formatting:** The reference list is exceptionally poorly formatted, containing numerous errors such as bibtex artifacts (`\Q2014\E`), stray question marks, and inconsistent author formatting (`Callison-burch` vs. `Bojar et al.`). This level of carelessness is unacceptable and makes it difficult to trust the scholarship.
    *   **Lack of Detail:** Critical experimental details are missing. For instance, in Section 3.1, the post-editing time is normalized by "the length of the machine translated sentence," but it is not specified whether this is character or word length—a detail that matters for reproducibility.
    *   **Grammar and Typos:** Minor but noticeable grammatical errors exist, such as "The remaining of this paper..." instead of "The remainder...".

These issues, taken together, suggest a rushed submission that has not undergone careful proofreading or review. While the core ideas are interesting, the execution and presentation are not up to the standards of a top-tier conference.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The formula for the Warped GP posterior density in Section 2.3 appears to have a typo in the exponential term. Could you please confirm the correct formula?

2.  The instability observed with the linex loss on the en-de dataset (`w=-0.75`) is a significant finding. Does this point to a failure of the Warped GP model to produce well-calibrated variance estimates in the tails of the distribution, or is it an inherent pathology of the linex loss for this type of data? A deeper investigation seems warranted.

3.  Could you provide a stronger justification for comparing only GP variants and omitting other standard methods for uncertainty quantification, such as deep ensembles or bootstrapping on strong non-probabilistic models like SVR? Without these baselines, the claimed benefits of the proposed GP models are not fully substantiated.
