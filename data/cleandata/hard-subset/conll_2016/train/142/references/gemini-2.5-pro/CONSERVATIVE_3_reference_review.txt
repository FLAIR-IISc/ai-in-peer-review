### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) to provide uncertainty estimates alongside quality predictions. The authors argue that such uncertainty information is valuable for downstream decision-making. The work focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs) and explores two extensions from the existing machine learning literature: Matérn kernels, to relax smoothness assumptions, and Warped GPs, to better model the distribution of strictly positive quality scores. The models are evaluated not only on standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) but also using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to assess the quality of the full predictive distribution. Finally, the paper demonstrates a use case for these predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-estimation of quality incurs different costs.

As I see them, the contributions of this work are:
1.  The application and analysis of Warped Gaussian Processes for the task of MT Quality Estimation, arguing for their suitability over standard GPs due to the nature of QE target variables.
2.  An argument for shifting QE evaluation towards probabilistic metrics like NLPD when uncertainty is a key consideration, demonstrating that standard metrics may fail to differentiate models with different uncertainty characteristics.
3.  A conceptual demonstration of how probabilistic QE models can be applied to asymmetric loss scenarios using Bayes risk estimators, which is a practical and relevant perspective for the field.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated problem.** The paper provides a clear and compelling motivation for moving beyond point estimates in QE. The post-editing workflow example effectively illustrates the limitations of standard QE models and highlights the practical need for reliable uncertainty estimates. This is a thoughtful and important direction for QE research.
2.  **Methodologically clear.** The paper does a good job of explaining the underlying theory of the GP models and the proposed extensions. The motivations for introducing Matérn kernels and Warped GPs are well-articulated from a theoretical standpoint. The introduction of asymmetric losses and Bayes risk estimators is also presented clearly.
3.  **Novel application scenario for QE.** The exploration of asymmetric risk is an interesting contribution. Framing QE evaluation in terms of downstream utility with differing costs for errors (the "pessimistic" and "optimistic" scenarios) is a more realistic setup than what is typically assumed by symmetric metrics like MAE or RMSE. This perspective is a valuable addition to the QE literature.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of a competitive baseline.** The paper identifies Support Vector Regression (SVR) as the "most popular method for QE" and the standard in WMT shared tasks. Despite this, all comparisons are made against a standard GP, not an SVR model. This is a significant methodological omission. The authors dismiss bootstrapping for SVR in a single sentence, but this is a standard and well-understood technique for deriving uncertainty estimates from non-probabilistic models. To convincingly argue for a shift in methodology, a rigorous comparison against the established state-of-the-art is required. Without it, the paper fails to demonstrate that GPs offer a tangible benefit over simpler, more established methods.
2.  **Inconclusive and under-analyzed results.** The experimental results do not consistently support the paper's claims. First, the advantage of Matérn kernels over the standard EQ kernel is "less conclusive," showing no benefit on two of the three datasets. More importantly, in the asymmetric risk evaluation—the paper's key application scenario—the proposed Warped GP models perform substantially *worse* than the standard GP baseline under the pessimistic Linex loss. The authors' explanation is speculative ("this might be a case where a standard GP is preferred") and the issue is deferred to "future work." This is a critical negative result that undermines the central thesis that these more complex models provide more useful uncertainty estimates for decision-making. If the model fails in a key scenario it was designed to address, its utility is questionable.
3.  **Limited experimental setup.** The experiments are conducted using a standard 17-feature set, which the authors acknowledge is not state-of-the-art. While the goal may be model comparison, this choice limits the generality of the findings. The behavior of kernel methods, particularly regarding hyperparameter optimization and the benefits of ARD, can change significantly in high-dimensional spaces. It is not clear that the conclusions drawn from this low-dimensional feature space would generalize to the larger, more complex feature sets used in competitive QE systems. This weakens the practical relevance of the results.
4.  **Novelty is limited to application.** The core techniques presented—Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, and asymmetric Bayes risk—are all well-established methods in the machine learning and statistics communities, as the authors acknowledge. The paper's contribution is therefore an application of these methods to a new domain. While such application papers are useful, the significance of the contribution is diminished by the inconclusive results and the lack of comparison to the established QE paradigm (SVR).

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given that SVR has been the dominant method in QE, why was it not included as a baseline? Could you provide a more detailed analysis or empirical evidence to support the claim that bootstrapping SVR is an inadequate approach for generating uncertainty estimates in this context?
2.  The failure of Warped GPs under the pessimistic Linex loss seems to be a major counterpoint to the paper's claims. Could this point to a more fundamental issue, such as the variance estimates from Warped GPs being poorly calibrated or systematically underestimated? Simply deferring this significant negative result to future work feels insufficient.
3.  How do you expect the relative performance of the different GP models (Standard, Matérn, Warped) to change when applied to modern, high-dimensional feature sets or end-to-end neural QE architectures? Is there a risk that the benefits observed here are an artifact of the simple, low-dimensional feature space used in the experiments?
