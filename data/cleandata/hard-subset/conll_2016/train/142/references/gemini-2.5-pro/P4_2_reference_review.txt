### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The central thesis is that conventional point-estimate predictions and their corresponding evaluation metrics (e.g., MAE) are insufficient for real-world applications of QE, which often involve decision-making under uncertainty. The authors advocate for models that produce predictive distributions and for evaluation metrics that assess the quality of these distributions. They explore extensions to standard GPs, such as Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, to better model the characteristics of QE data. The performance of these models is evaluated both intrinsically, using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to assess the quality of the predictive distributions, and extrinsically, in simulated asymmetric risk scenarios where over- or under-estimation of quality incurs different penalties.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **The introduction and application of a principled probabilistic evaluation framework for QE.** The paper makes a strong case for moving beyond point-estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson's r. By adopting the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric, it provides a method to evaluate the entire predictive distribution, rewarding well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. This is a significant conceptual contribution to the QE field.
2.  **A systematic empirical comparison of advanced Gaussian Process models for QE.** The authors apply and evaluate Warped GPs and GPs with Matérn kernels, demonstrating their advantages over standard GPs for modeling QE data, which often involves non-Gaussian, strictly positive scores. The analysis shows that these models yield better-calibrated predictive distributions, a finding not captured by traditional metrics.
3.  **A demonstration of the practical utility of predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios.** By using Bayes risk estimators with asymmetric loss functions (Asymmetric Linear and Linex), the paper provides a concrete example of how uncertainty information can be leveraged to tailor QE predictions for specific applications (e.g., pessimistic post-editing filtering vs. optimistic content gisting) without retraining the model.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong and well-motivated problem formulation.** The paper's primary strength is its compelling argument for the importance of uncertainty in QE. The introduction clearly outlines practical scenarios where point estimates are inadequate and predictive distributions are necessary for informed decision-making. This reframing of QE evaluation from pure accuracy to reliability and risk management is a valuable and timely contribution to the field.
2.  **Technically sound methodology and rigorous experimentation.** The choice of GP models and their extensions (Warped GPs, Matérn kernels) is well-justified and appropriate for the task. The experimental design is thorough, employing 10-fold cross-validation across three distinct datasets. The separation of the evaluation into an intrinsic part (Section 3) and an extrinsic, application-oriented part (Section 4) provides a comprehensive and convincing analysis. The results in Table 1 clearly demonstrate that NLPD can distinguish between models where point-estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson's r cannot, supporting the central thesis.
3.  **Clarity of presentation.** The paper is exceptionally well-written and organized. The theoretical background on Gaussian Processes and the proposed extensions is presented concisely and accessibly. The motivation, experimental setup, and results are explained clearly, making the paper easy to follow and its conclusions straightforward to understand. The discussion of the results is insightful, particularly in explaining why Warped GPs outperform standard GPs and linking observed performance differences to dataset characteristics like size.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited novelty of the machine learning models.** The core techniques explored—Gaussian Processes, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs—are well-established in the machine learning literature. The paper does not propose a new model but rather applies existing, sophisticated probabilistic models to the QE task. The novelty lies entirely in the application and, more significantly, in the evaluation framework (NLPD and asymmetric loss). While this is a valid and important contribution, the lack of a novel modeling component may be seen as a limitation.
2.  **Comparison is not against current state-of-the-art QE systems.** The authors acknowledge that they use a standard baseline feature set to isolate the effects of the probabilistic models, rather than aiming for state-of-the-art (SOTA) point-prediction performance. However, this makes it difficult to assess how these GP-based approaches would fare against modern neural QE architectures (e.g., Transformer-based models). A key question left unanswered is whether the benefits of well-calibrated uncertainty come at the cost of raw predictive power compared to SOTA models. Including a stronger, more contemporary baseline would have helped to contextualize the performance of the GP models more effectively.
3.  **Inconclusive results for the pessimistic asymmetric risk scenario.** The analysis in Section 4.3 reveals that the proposed Warped GP models perform worse than the standard GP baseline under the Linex loss in the pessimistic scenario. The authors offer a plausible hypothesis for this phenomenon but ultimately leave a deeper investigation to future work. This unresolved finding weakens the paper's claims about the universal benefits of the proposed models in asymmetric settings and suggests that the interaction between model properties (like predictive variance) and specific loss functions is more complex than presented.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The experiments focus exclusively on GP-based models. Given the current prevalence of neural network-based approaches in NLP and QE, could you elaborate on how the core ideas of this paper—evaluating with NLPD and leveraging uncertainty for asymmetric risk—could be applied to modern neural QE architectures? For instance, could methods like MC-Dropout or Deep Ensembles be used to generate the necessary predictive distributions, and would you expect them to face similar challenges (e.g., with the Linex loss) as the Warped GPs?
2.  In the discussion of the pessimistic Linex loss results (Section 4.3), you hypothesize that the lower variance of the Warped GP models leads to predictions that are not conservative enough. Does this suggest that the Warped GP, while providing a better fit to the bulk of the data distribution (as shown by NLPD), might be underestimating tail uncertainty? Could this be an artifact of the chosen `tanh` warping function, and have you considered alternative warping functions that might allow for heavier tails?
3.  Could you provide some insight into the computational cost of the proposed models, particularly the Warped GP with 3 `tanh` terms, compared to the standard GP and a more traditional baseline like SVR? Specifically, how does the more complex hyperparameter optimization for the warping function affect training time?
