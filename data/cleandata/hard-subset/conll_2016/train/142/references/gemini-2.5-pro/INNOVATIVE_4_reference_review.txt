### Summary and Contributions

This paper argues for a shift in how the NLP community approaches and evaluates Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). Instead of focusing solely on point estimates (like a single quality score), the authors advocate for modeling and evaluating the entire predictive distribution, which captures uncertainty.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A conceptual shift in QE evaluation:** The paper makes a compelling case for moving beyond standard metrics like MAE and Pearson's r, which are blind to model confidence. It introduces and validates the use of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more appropriate metric for evaluating probabilistic QE models, showing that it can distinguish between models that appear identical under traditional metrics.
2.  **Application of advanced probabilistic models to QE:** The authors demonstrate that standard Gaussian Process (GP) models have significant shortcomings for QE (e.g., assuming Gaussian noise for strictly positive scores). They show that applying more suitable existing techniques, particularly Warped GPs, leads to better-calibrated uncertainty estimates and superior models according to probabilistic metrics.
3.  **A novel framework for QE in practical scenarios:** The paper introduces the concept of asymmetric risk to QE, motivated by realistic use-cases (e.g., pessimistic post-editing vs. optimistic gisting). It shows how to use the predictive distributions from their models to derive optimal predictions under different asymmetric loss functions without costly retraining.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Boldness of Vision:** This is the paper's greatest strength. It challenges the standard paradigm of QE evaluation. Instead of delivering another incremental improvement on a leaderboard, it proposes a fundamentally different and more useful way to frame the problem. The core idea—that knowing *how confident* a model is in its prediction is as important as the prediction itself—is crucial for deploying QE systems in the real world. This is precisely the kind of thought-provoking, direction-setting work the community needs.

2.  **Strong Motivation and Practical Relevance:** The work is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction clearly explains *why* point estimates are insufficient for decision-making in translation workflows. The asymmetric risk scenarios in Section 4 are a brilliant and concrete demonstration of this, connecting abstract statistical concepts to tangible business needs. This makes the paper's contributions immediately understandable and highly relevant.

3.  **Convincing Empirical Evidence for the Core Thesis:** The experiments in Section 3 provide a clear and powerful argument for the paper's claims. The fact that Warped GPs show massive improvements in NLPD while showing only modest or inconsistent gains in MAE/Pearson's r is the smoking gun. It proves that the standard metrics are indeed hiding important differences in model quality and that NLPD is necessary to reveal them. This is a very strong result.

4.  **Elegant and Principled Methodology:** The choice of models and techniques is well-justified and elegant. Using Warped GPs to learn the data distribution instead of making flawed assumptions is a clever modeling choice. Furthermore, using Bayes risk estimators to handle asymmetric losses is far more flexible and efficient than retraining models for every possible risk scenario. This shows a deep understanding of the underlying machine learning principles.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Scope of Base Model/Features:** The experiments are conducted using a standard, non-neural baseline with only 17 hand-crafted features. While sufficient to demonstrate the paper's core concepts, it leaves open the question of how these methods apply to the current state-of-the-art in QE, which is dominated by large pretrained language models (e.g., predictor-estimator architectures). The paper would be stronger if it discussed or demonstrated how these uncertainty estimation techniques could be integrated with modern neural architectures. However, given the novelty of the core ideas, I view this as a minor limitation and an obvious avenue for exciting future work, rather than a fatal flaw.

2.  **Inconclusive Results on Some Modeling Choices:** The paper's investigation yields some mixed results, which slightly weaken parts of the narrative. For instance, the benefits of using Matern kernels over the standard EQ kernel were not consistently demonstrated. More strikingly, the linex loss experiments in the pessimistic scenario produced counter-intuitive results where the theoretically weaker standard GP performed best. While the authors commendably report and attempt to explain this, it suggests that the interaction between specific model properties (like variance) and specific loss functions is more complex than presented.

3.  **Novelty is in Application, not Invention:** To be clear, the paper does not invent Warped GPs, NLPD, or asymmetric loss functions. Its novelty lies entirely in identifying QE as a prime application for these existing ML concepts and being the first to combine them into a coherent framework for this task. This is not a strong weakness—this kind of cross-pollination is vital for scientific progress—but it is a point worth noting.

### Overall Recommendation

This is an excellent paper that I strongly recommend for acceptance. It is bold, thought-provoking, and addresses a critical, practical gap in the way we currently approach Quality Estimation. The authors' main contribution is not a new model that inches ahead on a leaderboard, but a new perspective that could change how the community evaluates and deploys these systems. The paper prioritizes a significant new concept over methodological perfection, and while there are minor weaknesses in the empirical scope, they are far outweighed by the strength, novelty, and importance of the core ideas. This work has the potential to inspire a new and fruitful direction of research in QE and other NLP regression tasks.
