### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the limitation of standard Quality Estimation (QE) models, which typically provide only point estimates of translation quality. The authors argue that for practical applications, such as deciding whether to post-edit a machine-translated segment, uncertainty estimates are crucial. The work proposes leveraging Gaussian Processes (GPs), a probabilistic regression framework, to model predictive uncertainty. The authors explore several modeling choices, including Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs, to better suit the characteristics of QE data. The performance of these models is evaluated not just with standard point-estimate metrics like MAE, but also with the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), which assesses the quality of the entire predictive distribution. Finally, the paper demonstrates how these probabilistic predictions can be used to make optimal decisions in scenarios with asymmetric risk (e.g., when underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating it).

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong, well-supported argument for evaluating and using predictive uncertainty in QE.** The paper compellingly shows that standard metrics like MAE fail to capture significant improvements in model calibration and uncertainty estimation. By introducing and justifying the use of NLPD, it advocates for a more principled evaluation paradigm for probabilistic QE models, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
2.  **A practical demonstration of applying probabilistic models to asymmetric risk scenarios in QE.** By framing realistic use-cases and using Bayes risk estimators with asymmetric loss functions, the paper provides a concrete methodology for leveraging uncertainty in downstream tasks. This shifts the focus from simple prediction to informed decision-making, which is highly relevant for real-world QE deployment.
3.  **A systematic empirical comparison of different GP modeling choices for QE.** The paper evaluates standard GPs, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs, providing clear insights into their suitability for this task. The finding that Warped GPs significantly outperform standard GPs by correctly modeling the positive, non-Gaussian nature of quality scores is a key practical takeaway.

### Strengths

1.  **Clear motivation and problem formulation.** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction makes a strong and intuitive case for why uncertainty quantification is critical for deploying QE systems in real-world workflows like post-editing. This framing addresses a significant and often-overlooked gap between academic QE research and practical industry needs.
2.  **Principled methodology and evaluation.** The choice of GPs is well-justified for the typically small datasets available for QE. Crucially, the introduction of NLPD as a primary evaluation metric is a major strength. It allows for a proper assessment of the model's predictive distribution, a facet completely ignored by traditional metrics. The experiments are methodologically sound, employing cross-validation across multiple datasets.
3.  **Novel and practical application of asymmetric risk analysis.** The exploration in Section 4 is a highlight of the paper. Demonstrating how to derive optimal point estimates for different user needs (via Bayes risk) from a single probabilistic model is an elegant and powerful idea. This provides a clear pathway for tailoring QE outputs to specific business or user requirements without costly model retraining.
4.  **Insightful discussion of results.** The authors provide thoughtful analysis of their findings. For instance, the discussion on why Warped GPs improve NLPD but not necessarily point-estimate metrics (Table 1) reinforces the paper's main thesis. Similarly, the hypothesis for the surprising underperformance of Warped GPs with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario (Section 4.3) shows a deep engagement with the model behaviors and is an interesting finding in its own right.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited scope of models and features.** The experiments are based on a standard but relatively small set of 17 handcrafted features. The current state of the art in QE is dominated by large, pre-trained neural models (e.g., COMET, TransQuest). While the authors' goal is not to achieve state-of-the-art point predictions, the paper would be significantly strengthened by a discussion of how these uncertainty quantification methods relate to or could be integrated with modern neural architectures. It is unclear if the specific benefits of Warped GPs would persist against neural models that can also be made probabilistic (e.g., through deep ensembles or MC-dropout).
2.  **Potential inconsistency in the description of asymmetric risk scenarios.** There appears to be a contradiction in the setup of Section 4. The target variable is post-editing time, where a higher value signifies worse quality. The "pessimistic" scenario is described as one where underestimating this time (predicting `ŷ < y`, i.e., assessing a bad translation as good) should be heavily penalized. However, the asymmetric linear (AL) loss function provided seems to apply the extra weight `w > 1` to overestimates (`ŷ > y`). This confusion makes the results in this section difficult to interpret correctly and weakens an otherwise strong part of the paper.
3.  **The benefits of Matèrn kernels are not clearly established.** The paper hypothesizes that Matèrn kernels, which relax the smoothness assumptions of the standard EQ kernel, would be better suited for noisy QE data. This is a sound hypothesis. However, the empirical results in Table 1 are inconclusive, showing minimal and inconsistent gains. While reporting this negative result is scientifically honest, it weakens the claim that the choice of Matèrn kernels is a key contribution for this task.
4.  **The datasets are relatively small.** While acknowledged by the authors as typical for QE, the use of small datasets (the largest being <3k instances) limits the generalizability of the conclusions. For example, performance differences between log-warping and tanh-warping are attributed to dataset size, but this is hard to confirm without experiments on larger-scale data.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the formulation of the asymmetric loss scenarios in Section 4? As I understand it, post-editing time/rate is the target variable, where higher values mean worse quality. A pessimistic scenario would heavily penalize underestimating this time (predicting `ŷ < y`), as this means a poor translation is wrongly assessed as good. However, your AL loss function seems to apply the weight `w > 1` to overestimates (`ŷ > y`). Could you clarify this apparent discrepancy?
2.  Your work focuses on GPs, which are well-suited for the small-data regime of traditional QE. How do you see these methods for uncertainty quantification applying to the current state-of-the-art in QE, which is dominated by large pre-trained neural models? Do you see a path for combining the strengths of, for example, Warped GPs with neural feature extractors, or would you advocate for alternative uncertainty methods (like deep ensembles or Bayesian NNs) in that context?
