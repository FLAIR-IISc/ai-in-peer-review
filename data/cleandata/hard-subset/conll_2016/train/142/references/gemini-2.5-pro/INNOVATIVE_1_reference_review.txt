### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper presents a compelling argument for shifting the focus of Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) from point predictions to probabilistic distributions that capture uncertainty. The authors explore Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a suitable framework, extending the standard model with Matern kernels and, more significantly, Warped GPs to better handle the characteristics of QE data. The core of the paper is a call to action for the QE community: to adopt more appropriate evaluation metrics like Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) that assess the quality of the entire predictive distribution, and to leverage this uncertainty information for practical applications like decision-making under asymmetric risk.

From my perspective, the main contributions are, in order of importance:
1.  **A Conceptual Reframing of QE Evaluation:** The paper’s most significant contribution is its forceful and well-supported argument against the status quo of evaluating QE with point-estimate metrics like MAE. By introducing and demonstrating the superiority of NLPD, it advocates for a more principled evaluation paradigm that properly assesses a model's confidence, a critical aspect for any real-world deployment. This is a bold and necessary intervention in the field.
2.  **A Practical Framework for Asymmetric Risk in QE:** The authors introduce a novel and highly practical application of uncertainty estimates for handling asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic post-editing triage). The use of Bayes risk estimators to derive optimal predictions for different loss functions without model retraining is an elegant solution to a real-world problem. This demonstrates the tangible benefits of the probabilistic approach beyond mere theoretical appeal.
3.  **Introduction of More Suitable Probabilistic Models for QE:** The paper effectively introduces Warped GPs to the QE task, showing that learning a non-linear mapping of the target variable leads to substantially better-calibrated models. This is a solid technical contribution that directly addresses a key mismatch between standard modeling assumptions (Gaussian likelihood) and the nature of QE labels (strictly positive scores).

### Strengths

1.  **High Conceptual Novelty and Potential for Impact:** This work is refreshing. Instead of an incremental improvement on existing benchmarks, it challenges the very foundation of how the community measures progress in QE. The push towards probabilistic evaluation and uncertainty-aware applications has the potential to steer the field in a more meaningful and practically relevant direction. This is exactly the type of forward-looking, thought-provoking research that our field needs.
2.  **Excellent Motivation and Clear Narrative:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction uses a simple, intuitive example of a post-editing workflow to immediately convince the reader of the limitations of point estimates. This clear framing makes the subsequent technical proposals feel necessary and justified. The paper tells a coherent and persuasive story from problem to solution to application.
3.  **Strong Empirical Evidence for the Central Thesis:** The experiments, while not on a massive scale, provide powerful evidence for the paper's core claims. The results in Table 1, where models show dramatic improvements in NLPD while MAE and Pearson's r remain largely stagnant, are a stark demonstration that current evaluation practices are failing to capture crucial aspects of model quality. This finding alone is a significant contribution.
4.  **Creative and Elegant Application of Probabilistic Modeling:** The synthesis of GPs, warping functions, NLPD, and asymmetric Bayes risk is masterful. While the individual components are known in the ML community, their combination and application to solve a concrete problem in NLP is novel and insightful. The asymmetric risk section, in particular, showcases a sophisticated and practical use case that is rarely explored in NLP literature.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are areas that could be strengthened. I view these primarily as limitations to be addressed in future work rather than fatal flaws.

1.  **Limited Empirical Scope and Connection to SOTA:** The experiments are based on a classic feature-based QE setup. While this is sufficient to demonstrate the core concepts, the paper's impact would be amplified by discussing or demonstrating how these ideas apply to modern, neural-based QE architectures (e.g., predictor-estimator models). It is an open question whether the findings would hold in that setting, which now represents the state of the art.
2.  **Inconclusive and Under-analyzed Results:** The benefit of using Matern kernels was not clearly established, with results being inconsistent across datasets. More importantly, the puzzling result where Warped GPs underperform in the pessimistic Linex loss scenario is not fully unpacked. The authors' hypothesis is plausible, but a deeper analysis (e.g., of model calibration) is needed to fully understand this phenomenon. This loose end slightly detracts from the otherwise tight narrative.
3.  **Novelty is Primarily in Application:** A critic might point out that the paper does not propose any new machine learning algorithms but rather applies an existing, if sophisticated, toolkit to a new domain. For me, this is a strength—it is a brilliant work of bridging ML theory and NLP practice—but it is true that the paper's novelty is in its perspective and application rather than in core methodology.

### Questions to Authors

The analysis of the pessimistic Linex loss scenario was fascinating. You hypothesize that the lower variance of the Warped GP models is the cause. Could this also imply that the standard GP, by assigning probability mass to non-sensical negative values, is producing artificially high-variance (and thus poorly calibrated) predictions that happen to "game" this specific estimator? Have you considered analyzing the calibration of the models (e.g., with reliability diagrams) to investigate this further?

Your work makes a powerful case for probabilistic thinking in feature-based QE. How do you envision these concepts being integrated into large-scale, end-to-end neural QE models? Would approaches like MC-Dropout, deep ensembles, or proper Bayesian neural networks be the natural successors to GPs in this framework, and do you foresee new challenges in applying concepts like asymmetric risk estimation to them?
