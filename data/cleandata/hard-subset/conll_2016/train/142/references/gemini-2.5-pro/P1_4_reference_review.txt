### **Summary**

This paper addresses the limitation of point estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) by advocating for probabilistic models that provide uncertainty estimates. The authors argue that uncertainty is crucial for real-world decision-making, such as in post-editing workflows. The paper focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs) and explores extensions like Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better model the noisy and non-Gaussian nature of QE data (specifically, post-editing time). A key part of their contribution is the promotion of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more suitable evaluation metric that assesses the entire predictive distribution, not just a single point estimate. Finally, they demonstrate the practical utility of these predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-estimation of quality has different costs, by deriving optimal Bayes risk estimators without model retraining.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **Methodological Shift in QE Evaluation:** The paper's primary contribution is its compelling argument for moving beyond point-estimate metrics (like MAE and Pearson's r) towards evaluating the full predictive distribution. The introduction and successful application of NLPD clearly demonstrates that standard metrics can be insensitive to significant improvements in uncertainty modeling, providing a valuable methodological insight for the QE community.
2.  **Application of Advanced Probabilistic Models to QE:** The work successfully applies and analyzes sophisticated GP variants—specifically Warped GPs and Matérn kernels—to the QE task. The authors provide a well-motivated case for why these models are theoretically better suited for QE data (e.g., handling non-negativity via warping) and empirically validate their superiority in terms of probabilistic calibration (lower NLPD).
3.  **A Practical Framework for Asymmetric Risk in QE:** The paper presents a clear and useful application of uncertainty modeling in asymmetric risk scenarios. By using Bayes risk estimators for different loss functions (asymmetric linear and linex), they show how a single probabilistic model can be flexibly adapted to different operational needs (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic filtering) without costly retraining.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The introduction clearly explains the practical limitations of point estimates in QE with a concrete post-editing scenario. This framing makes the work immediately relevant and its contributions easy to appreciate.
2.  **Principled Model Selection and Analysis:** The authors demonstrate a strong understanding of the chosen models. The justification for using Matérn kernels (to relax smoothness assumptions for noisy data) and Warped GPs (to handle the strictly positive, non-Gaussian nature of post-editing time) is clear and theoretically sound. This careful selection elevates the paper beyond a simple "let's try this model" approach.
3.  **Strong Empirical Evidence for NLPD:** The results in Table 1 provide powerful evidence for the paper's central thesis. The stark contrast between the significant NLPD improvements from Warped GPs and the much smaller, less consistent changes in MAE and Pearson's r is a convincing demonstration that standard metrics are inadequate for evaluating probabilistic models. This finding has important implications for future QE research.
4.  **Novel and Relevant Application Scenario:** The section on asymmetric risk is a major strength. It grounds the abstract concept of "better uncertainty" in a tangible application. The ability to derive optimal estimators for different loss functions on the fly is an elegant and powerful feature that showcases the practical benefits of the proposed approach over traditional regression models.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Compared Models:** The paper's focus is exclusively on Gaussian Processes. While GPs are a strong choice, the claim that they are the state-of-the-art is based on citations from 2013-2014. The paper dismisses bootstrapping SVR very quickly, and there is no comparison to other modern methods for uncertainty quantification, such as Bayesian Neural Networks, MC-Dropout, or even simple deep ensembles. This narrow focus makes it hard to gauge how these findings generalize across different modeling paradigms.
2.  **Outdated Experimental Setup:** The experiments rely on a small, 17-feature set from the QuEst framework. The field of QE has largely moved on to using features from large pre-trained language models (e.g., XLM-R based predictors), which have become the de-facto standard. While the authors state their goal is not to achieve SOTA point predictions, it raises a critical question of whether the observed benefits of Warped GPs and Matérn kernels would still hold when using modern, high-dimensional, and far more powerful neural features. The findings may not be representative of the current QE landscape.
3.  **Inconclusive and Under-analyzed Results:** Some of the paper's findings are inconclusive. The benefits of Matérn kernels over the standard EQ kernel are marginal at best. More importantly, the failure of the linex loss estimator in the pessimistic scenario for Warped GPs is a significant negative result that is not sufficiently explored. The authors speculate it is due to the model's lower variance but relegate a deeper investigation to future work. This is a crucial point, as it suggests that either the uncertainty estimates from the Warped GP are flawed in the tails or the Bayes risk estimator itself is brittle, which somewhat weakens the claims about its general applicability.
4.  **Small Dataset Sizes:** The datasets used are relatively small (858 to 2,828 instances). The authors themselves attribute some of the performance differences (e.g., log-warping being better than tanh-warping on the smallest dataset) to size. While typical for QE, this limitation, combined with the small feature set, makes the conclusions less robust and generalizable.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The experimental setup uses a classic feature-based approach. How do you expect your findings, particularly the significant advantage of Warped GPs, to translate to modern neural QE architectures? Can uncertainty estimation methods for neural networks (like MC-Dropout or ensembles) similarly benefit from a "warping" concept to handle non-Gaussian outputs, and would you still expect GPs to be competitive in that high-dimensional setting?
2.  The poor performance of the linex loss estimator in the pessimistic scenario for Warped GPs is a very interesting result. Your hypothesis is that the estimator is not "pushed" enough due to lower variance. Could it also be that the Bayes risk estimator for the linex loss, which depends on both mean and variance, is highly sensitive to the accuracy of the variance estimate, and perhaps the variance estimated by the Warped GP is less reliable in the distribution's tails, even if the overall NLPD is better? A more detailed discussion on this point would strengthen the paper.
3.  For clarity on the experiments: Table 1 results use the median of the posterior as the point prediction. In the asymmetric loss experiments in Section 4, are the reported predictions derived from the specific Bayes risk estimators (i.e., the appropriate quantile for AL loss and the µ - wσ²/2 formula for linex loss)?
