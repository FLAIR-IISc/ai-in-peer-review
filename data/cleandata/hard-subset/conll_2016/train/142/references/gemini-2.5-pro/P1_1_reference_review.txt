### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the limitations of point estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) and advocates for probabilistic models that provide uncertainty information. The authors focus on Gaussian Processes (GPs) and explore extensions—specifically Matern kernels and Warped GPs—to better model QE data, which is often noisy and has a non-Gaussian, strictly positive target variable (e.g., post-editing time). The core of the paper is a dual evaluation. First, it introduces the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more suitable intrinsic metric than standard error metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) for assessing the quality of predictive distributions. Second, it demonstrates the practical utility of these distributions in an extrinsic evaluation based on asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- and under-estimation of quality have different costs.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A strong argument and empirical demonstration for evaluating QE models beyond point-estimate metrics.** The paper convincingly shows that metrics like MAE fail to capture crucial differences in model confidence. By introducing and using NLPD, it provides a principled way to evaluate the quality of uncertainty estimates, which is a valuable contribution to QE evaluation methodology.
2.  **The application and analysis of Warped GPs for QE in asymmetric risk scenarios.** The paper shows that Warped GPs, which learn a non-linear transformation of the target variable, are a better fit for QE data than standard GPs. It frames a practical use case for uncertainty through asymmetric loss functions, demonstrating how a single probabilistic model can be adapted to different decision-making scenarios without retraining.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper does an outstanding job of motivating the need for uncertainty in QE. The post-editing and gisting examples are concrete, practical, and clearly illustrate why relying on point estimates is suboptimal for real-world decision-making. This grounds the work in a tangible application context, making its contributions more impactful.
2.  **Sound and Principled Methodology:** The choice of methods is well-justified and grounded in established machine learning literature. The argument for moving from the standard EQ kernel to Matern kernels (to handle noise) and for using Warped GPs (to handle positive-only, non-Gaussian targets) is clear and logical. The adoption of NLPD for evaluation and Bayes risk for adapting to asymmetric losses are both principled approaches that add rigor to the study.
3.  **Comprehensive and Insightful Evaluation:** The experimental design is strong. By evaluating on three datasets, the authors increase the generalizability of their findings. The combination of intrinsic (NLPD) and extrinsic (asymmetric loss) evaluation provides a holistic view of model performance. The discussion of the results is particularly good, for instance, noting how NLL can be a misleading proxy for generalization on small datasets (Table 1, en-es) and providing a thoughtful analysis of the counter-intuitive results for the linex loss.
4.  **High Clarity and Readability:** The paper is very well-written, well-structured, and easy to follow. Complex concepts like Warped GPs and Bayes risk estimators are explained clearly and concisely, making the work accessible to a broad NLP audience, not just those with a deep background in Bayesian machine learning.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Comparison to Alternative Uncertainty Estimation Methods:** The paper focuses exclusively on GP-based models, arguing that other common models like SVR only provide point estimates. However, this overlooks widely-used techniques for uncertainty quantification like bootstrapping/ensembling (which are briefly mentioned but quickly dismissed) or methods for neural networks like Monte Carlo dropout. A comparison against a strong non-GP baseline that can produce uncertainty estimates (e.g., a bagged SVR) would have made the case for the superiority of the proposed GP models much stronger. Without this, it is unclear if the benefits come from the GP framework itself or simply from the act of modeling uncertainty in general.
2.  **Use of an Outdated QE Architecture:** The experiments are based on a small, hand-crafted feature set (17 features from QuEst) and kernel methods. While this is sufficient for a controlled comparison between GP variants, the state-of-the-art in QE has largely shifted to end-to-end neural models based on large pre-trained transformers (e.g., XLM-R). The paper's conclusions, while valid for the models tested, may not directly transfer to these modern architectures. The work would have a greater impact if it discussed how its principles could be integrated into the current neural QE paradigm.
3.  **Some Experimental Results are Inconclusive:** The benefits of the proposed extensions are not uniformly positive. The Matern kernels show no consistent improvement over the standard EQ kernel. More significantly, in the pessimistic asymmetric risk scenario, the proposed Warped GP models perform substantially worse than the standard GP baseline under the linex loss. While the authors offer a plausible hypothesis for this failure, it weakens the overall claim that the proposed models are universally better and suggests that the interaction between model, estimator, and loss function is more complex than presented.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  The state-of-the-art in QE has shifted towards large pre-trained language models. How do you see the ideas presented here—specifically the use of Warped GPs and NLPD evaluation—being integrated with or adapted for modern neural QE architectures? For instance, could a GP layer be used on top of embeddings from a transformer, and could a warping function be learned as part of the end-to-end training?
2.  The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are very interesting. You hypothesize this is due to the lower variance of the warped models' predictive distributions. Could this also suggest that the variance from the standard GP, while higher, is poorly calibrated (e.g., it is artificially inflated by placing probability mass on impossible negative post-editing times)? Have you considered visualizing the predictive distributions for some specific test instances to gain more insight into this phenomenon?
3.  You briefly dismiss bootstrapping for SVR as not being "well-calibrated". While perhaps less elegant than a fully Bayesian approach, ensembles are a common and often effective way to estimate uncertainty. Could you elaborate on this point and perhaps speculate on how a well-tuned SVR ensemble might perform in your experimental settings, particularly in the asymmetric risk scenarios?
