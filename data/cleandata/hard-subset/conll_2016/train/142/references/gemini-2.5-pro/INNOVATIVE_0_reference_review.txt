**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**

This paper argues for a fundamental shift in how Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) is approached: moving from providing simple point estimates of quality to producing full probabilistic predictions that explicitly model uncertainty. The authors contend that standard metrics like MAE are insufficient for real-world applications where decisions carry asymmetric risks. The paper's primary contributions, as I see them, are:

1.  **A powerful conceptual argument for probabilistic QE:** The paper's core contribution is its compelling case for why the QE community must move beyond point estimates. It reframes QE not as a simple regression task, but as a risk-management tool, where understanding the model's confidence is as important as its prediction.
2.  **Introduction and validation of more suitable probabilistic models:** The paper introduces Warped Gaussian Processes (GPs) to the QE task, demonstrating that they are a much better fit for modeling non-Gaussian, strictly-positive quality scores (like post-editing time) than standard GPs.
3.  **A practical demonstration of uncertainty in asymmetric risk scenarios:** The paper showcases the real-world value of predictive distributions by evaluating models under asymmetric loss functions, simulating scenarios where under- or over-estimation of quality has different costs. This provides a concrete, compelling use case that is hard to address with traditional methods.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Bold and Visionary:** This is precisely the kind of paper our field needs. It challenges the established paradigm of chasing incremental gains on metrics like MAE and Pearson's r. The authors are not just proposing a new model; they are proposing a new way of thinking about the entire task of Quality Estimation. This focus on the "why" and "what for" of QE is refreshing and has the potential to set a new research direction for the community.
2.  **Compelling and Practical Motivation:** The asymmetric risk scenario in Section 4 is a "killer app" for this line of work. It elegantly demonstrates that a single, well-calibrated probabilistic model is far more flexible and useful in a production environment than a point-estimate model. The ability to adjust decision thresholds based on changing business needs (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic scenarios) by simply changing the Bayes risk estimator, without any model retraining, is a significant practical advantage. This moves the discussion from abstract statistical properties to concrete operational benefits.
3.  **Strong Methodological Argument:** The paper makes an excellent case for using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a primary evaluation metric. The results in Table 1 clearly show that MAE and Pearson's r fail to capture the significant improvements in model fit offered by Warped GPs. By highlighting this discrepancy, the paper provides strong evidence that the community's standard evaluation practices may be misleading and hindering progress on what truly matters: building well-calibrated, reliable models.
4.  **Introduction of Well-Suited Techniques:** While Warped GPs and Matèrn kernels are not new to the ML community, their application to QE is novel and extremely well-motivated. The authors correctly identify the mismatch between the Gaussian likelihood assumption of standard GPs and the nature of QE data (strictly positive, noisy) and provide an elegant solution. This is a great example of thoughtfully applying the right tool for the job.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Empirical Scale and Scope:** The experiments are performed on older, smaller datasets using a basic 17-feature set. This is a clear limitation, as it's uncertain how these findings translate to the current landscape of large, neural, pre-trained QE models (e.g., CometKiwi). While I appreciate that the paper's focus is on the concept rather than state-of-the-art performance, demonstrating these ideas in a more modern setting would have made the case even more compelling. However, for a paper introducing a new direction, I see this as an acceptable starting point.
2.  **Inconclusive Results on Secondary Aspects:** The benefits of the Matèrn kernel were marginal at best, and the analysis of the pessimistic scenario with the Linex loss function was confusing and the results were poor. The authors' explanation for this felt speculative. While this weakness slightly undermines one part of the experimental section, it does not detract from the paper's central, and much more important, message about the value of Warped GPs and probabilistic modeling in general. I view this as an interesting loose end for future work, not a fatal flaw.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The poor performance of the Warped GP models under the pessimistic Linex loss is an interesting finding. You hypothesize it's due to the model having lower variance. Could this also suggest that the parametric `tanh` warping function, while good at modeling the bulk of the distribution, is not flexible enough to capture the behavior in the extreme tails, which are precisely what an exponential loss function penalizes so heavily?
2.  Given the dominance of large neural networks, how do you envision these principles being carried forward? Do you believe that integrating techniques like deep ensembles or Bayesian neural networks is the most promising path to achieving well-calibrated uncertainty estimates in modern SOTA QE architectures? Or do you see a continued role for hybrid systems that might use GPs on top of neural features?
