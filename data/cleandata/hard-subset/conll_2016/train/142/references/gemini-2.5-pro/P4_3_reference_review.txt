### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that standard point-estimate predictions and their corresponding evaluation metrics (e.g., MAE) are insufficient for real-world applications where decision-making depends on the model's confidence. The paper explores extensions to the standard GP framework, namely the use of Matern kernels and Warped GPs, to better model the data characteristics of QE tasks. It advocates for the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more informative evaluation metric that accounts for predictive uncertainty. Finally, it demonstrates the practical utility of these probabilistic models in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-estimation of quality incurs different costs, by using Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric loss functions.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  A practical demonstration of how advanced probabilistic models (Warped Gaussian Processes) can be applied to the task of MT Quality Estimation to handle asymmetric risk scenarios. The paper shows that a single, well-calibrated probabilistic model can be adapted to different loss functions post-training, providing flexibility for real-world deployment where risk profiles may change.
2.  An empirical analysis highlighting the limitations of point-estimate evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) for probabilistic QE models. The work provides evidence that NLPD is a more suitable metric as it can distinguish between models with similar point-estimate performance but different quality of uncertainty estimation.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Motivation and Clarity:** The paper is well-written and provides a clear, compelling motivation for moving beyond point estimates in QE. The introduction effectively uses the post-editing scenario to illustrate why uncertainty quantification is crucial for practical applications. This framing makes the work accessible and highlights its relevance to the QE community. The technical concepts (GPs, Warping, NLPD, asymmetric loss) are introduced methodically and explained clearly.

2.  **Sound Experimental Design:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors use three datasets with different language pairs, apply 10-fold cross-validation for robustness, and systematically compare different model configurations (standard GP vs. Warped GP, EQ vs. Matern kernels). The inclusion of both an intrinsic evaluation of uncertainty (Section 3) and an extrinsic, application-oriented evaluation (Section 4) provides a comprehensive assessment of the proposed methods.

3.  **Practical Relevance and Application:** The investigation of asymmetric risk scenarios (Section 4) is a significant strength. It connects the abstract concept of predictive uncertainty to a concrete application with tangible consequences (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic gisting). Showing that Bayes risk estimators can be derived from a single trained model to cater to different asymmetric losses without retraining is a strong argument for the practicality and efficiency of the proposed probabilistic approach.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core methods explored in this paper—Gaussian Processes, Matern kernels, Warped GPs, and the NLPD metric—are well-established in the machine learning literature. The paper's contribution is primarily in the application and analysis of these existing techniques within the specific domain of MT Quality Estimation. The work does not propose novel models or algorithms but rather makes a case for adopting a particular set of existing tools. While this is a valid contribution, the novelty from a pure machine learning perspective is low.

2.  **Use of a Basic Feature Set:** The experiments are conducted using a standard set of 17 handcrafted features from the QuEst framework. While the authors justify this as a means to focus on the comparison of probabilistic models, it limits the generalizability of the findings. State-of-the-art QE systems have largely moved towards using rich, high-dimensional representations from pre-trained language models. It is unclear whether the observed benefits of Matern kernels and, particularly, the complex warping functions would hold or be as significant in a high-dimensional, dense feature space where the input-output relationships may differ.

3.  **Inconclusive Analysis of Pessimistic Linex Loss:** The results for the pessimistic scenario under the linex loss are counterintuitive, showing that the standard GP outperforms the more sophisticated Warped GP models. The paper's discussion of this phenomenon is brief and speculative, suggesting the loss may be "biased towards models with high variance." This is an interesting but underdeveloped finding. The analysis would be stronger if it provided a deeper investigation into *why* the standard GP's variance, which is partly assigned to non-sensical negative values, leads to better performance under this specific loss. This unresolved point weakens the paper's conclusions regarding the universal applicability of Warped GPs.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your experiments are based on a small set of handcrafted features. How do you expect your findings, particularly the advantages of Warped GPs over standard GPs and the utility of Matern kernels, to transfer to modern QE systems that use high-dimensional features from large pre-trained language models (e.g., embeddings from XLM-R)? Would the assumptions made by these GP models still be as effective in that setting?

2.  The results in the pessimistic linex loss scenario (Table 2) are surprising. You hypothesize that this is because the loss function favors models with higher variance. Could you elaborate on this? Is it possible that the warping function, by constraining the output space, is producing overconfident (low-variance) predictions in certain regions, which are then heavily penalized by the exponential nature of the linex loss? A more detailed analysis or visualization of the predictive distributions for a few key examples could help clarify this phenomenon.
