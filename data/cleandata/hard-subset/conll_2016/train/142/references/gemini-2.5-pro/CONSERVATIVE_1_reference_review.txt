### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Process (GP) models for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), with a focus on modeling predictive uncertainty. The authors argue that standard point-estimate metrics are insufficient for real-world applications and advocate for probabilistic models. They explore extensions to the standard GP framework, namely the use of Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, to better model the noisy and non-Gaussian nature of QE data (post-editing time). The models are evaluated both intrinsically, using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric, and extrinsically in a simulated application with asymmetric loss functions.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  An application study of established GP variants (Warped GPs, Matérn kernels) to the QE task, demonstrating that they can provide better-calibrated predictive distributions as measured by NLPD compared to a standard GP.
2.  A practical demonstration of how these predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios, allowing for the derivation of optimal point estimates under different loss functions without retraining the model.

### Strengths

1.  **Relevant Problem Formulation:** The paper correctly identifies a significant limitation of standard QE approaches. Focusing on point estimates (like MAE) ignores the confidence of a prediction, which is crucial for downstream decision-making tasks such as determining whether to post-edit or discard a translation. The motivation to explore predictive uncertainty is well-grounded and practically relevant.
2.  **Principled Evaluation of Uncertainty:** The authors make a strong case for using NLPD as an evaluation metric. This is a more appropriate measure for probabilistic regression models than standard point-estimate metrics like MAE or Pearson's r, as it evaluates the entire predictive distribution. The results in Table 1 effectively illustrate how models with similar MAE can have vastly different predictive likelihoods, justifying the need for such a metric in QE.
3.  **Exploration of Asymmetric Risk:** The application scenario in Section 4 is a valuable contribution. Real-world costs of over- or under-estimation are rarely symmetric. Showing how a single probabilistic model can be used to generate optimal predictions for different risk profiles (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic) by calculating Bayes risk estimators is a compelling use case for the proposed methods.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The primary weakness of this work is its lack of methodological novelty. Gaussian Processes, Matérn kernels, Warped GPs, the NLPD metric, and the use of asymmetric loss functions are all well-established techniques from the machine learning and statistics literature, some dating back decades. The contribution of this paper is confined to applying this existing toolkit to the QE domain. While such application papers can be valuable, the insights gained here feel somewhat incremental.
2.  **Insufficient Comparison to Established Baselines:** The paper's experiments are conducted entirely within the GP framework, comparing different GP variants against a standard GP. However, the authors themselves state that Support Vector Regression (SVR) is the most popular and successful method in WMT QE shared tasks. A crucial missing piece of this evaluation is a comparison against a strong SVR baseline. While SVR does not naturally produce probabilistic outputs, uncertainty estimates can be derived via methods like bootstrapping. The authors dismiss this approach with a single sentence, but an empirical comparison is necessary to convincingly argue that GPs are superior for this task, even when considering uncertainty. Without this comparison, the claims about the practical benefits of the proposed models remain insufficiently validated against the de facto standard in the field.
3.  **Limited Empirical Scope and Inconclusive Results:** The experiments are based on a standard, but small, set of 17 features and are run on datasets of modest size. It is unclear if the findings would generalize to state-of-the-art QE systems that employ much larger and more complex feature sets. Furthermore, some of the results are inconclusive. The authors report that the benefits of Matérn kernels are not clear-cut, and more critically, the proposed Warped GP models perform worse than the standard GP baseline under the pessimistic linex loss scenario. This latter finding undermines the central claim that these more complex models provide universally better uncertainty estimates for practical applications.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to exclude a comparison to SVR, the established baseline in the QE field? Given that uncertainty can be estimated from SVR models (e.g., via ensembles/bootstrapping), a direct comparison of the quality and utility of these uncertainty estimates versus those from your GP models seems essential to support the paper's core claims.
2.  The poor performance of Warped GPs in the pessimistic linex loss scenario is a significant finding that seems to contradict the overall message of the paper. Your explanation suggests this is a quirk of the loss function's interaction with the model's variance. Could this also suggest that the uncertainty estimates from Warped GPs are not as robust as claimed, or that the standard GP is preferable in certain high-risk scenarios? This seems to complicate the practical recommendations one might draw from this work.
3.  The paper's motivation is centered on practical utility. However, GPs have a computational complexity that is cubic in the number of training instances. While the datasets used here are small, this is a major limitation for scaling up. Could you comment on the practical feasibility of these methods in larger, industry-scale QE scenarios and whether approximations (which were not used here) would affect the quality of the uncertainty estimates you aim to model?
