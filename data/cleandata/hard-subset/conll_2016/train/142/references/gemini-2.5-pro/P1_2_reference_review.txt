### **Summary of the paper**

This paper advocates for moving beyond point estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) towards a probabilistic framework that explicitly models prediction uncertainty. The authors argue that uncertainty estimates are crucial for real-world decision-making, such as in post-editing workflows. The work focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs) and explores two key extensions to better model QE data: Matérn kernels to handle noisy data and Warped GPs to account for the non-Gaussian, strictly positive nature of quality labels like post-editing time. The authors introduce the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more suitable metric for evaluating probabilistic models, demonstrating that standard metrics like MAE fail to capture improvements in uncertainty modeling. A key part of the paper is a practical demonstration of how these probabilistic models can be applied in asymmetric risk scenarios (where under- or over-estimation have different costs), using Bayes risk estimators to provide optimal predictions without needing to retrain the model.

### **Main Contributions**

1.  **A strong argument and clear demonstration for shifting QE evaluation from point estimates to probabilistic predictions.** The paper successfully highlights the limitations of traditional QE evaluation (e.g., MAE) and introduces the NLPD metric to the QE community, providing a principled way to assess the quality of a model's predictive uncertainty. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could change how QE systems are evaluated.
2.  **A novel and practical application of probabilistic QE in asymmetric risk scenarios.** The framework for using a single trained probabilistic model to derive optimal decisions under various, potentially changing, asymmetric loss functions is a key practical contribution. This directly addresses real-world needs where the cost of prediction errors is not symmetric (e.g., gisting vs. high-quality post-editing).
3.  **An empirical comparison of advanced GP models for QE.** The paper provides a systematic evaluation showing that Warped GPs are significantly better suited for modeling positive-valued QE scores (post-editing time) than standard GPs. This offers concrete modeling advice to practitioners in QE and other text regression tasks.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Framing.** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It starts with a clear, practical example of why point estimates are insufficient in a post-editing workflow and consistently ties its technical contributions back to this real-world problem. This makes the work highly relevant and its impact easy to understand.
2.  **Methodologically Sound and Insightful Experiments.** The experimental design is thorough and robust. The use of multiple datasets, cross-validation, and a principled comparison of different model components (kernels, warping functions) is commendable. The analysis of the results is particularly insightful, for example, in identifying how NLL can be a misleading indicator of generalization on small datasets, and in the nuanced discussion of the anomalous results with the linex loss.
3.  **Clarity and High-Quality Presentation.** The paper is very well-written, clear, and easy to follow. Complex topics like Gaussian Processes, warping functions, and Bayes risk are explained with sufficient detail for the reader to understand their relevance without getting bogged down in theory. The structure logically builds the argument from the need for uncertainty to its evaluation and practical application.
4.  **Practical Relevance.** The work goes beyond a purely academic exercise of improving a metric. The section on asymmetric risk demonstrates a tangible benefit for deploying QE systems in production environments. The idea of decoupling the loss function from the model training by using Bayes risk estimators is elegant and highly practical, as it allows for on-the-fly adaptation to different business needs without costly retraining.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Comparison to State-of-the-Art QE Architectures.** The experiments are conducted using a standard but relatively small set of 17 features. While this is a reasonable choice for isolating the effects of the probabilistic modeling choices, it leaves open the question of how these benefits translate to more modern and powerful QE models that use large feature sets or neural architectures (e.g., predictor-estimator models). A discussion on how these uncertainty modeling techniques could be integrated with SOTA models would strengthen the paper's claim of relevance.
2.  **Inconclusive Findings on Matérn Kernels.** One of the proposed modeling improvements, the use of Matérn kernels, yielded "less conclusive" results. While this is reported honestly, it weakens this part of the contribution. The paper does not explore potential reasons for this (e.g., are the datasets not noisy enough for the difference to be apparent?), leaving the reader with unclear guidance on when to prefer Matérn over the standard EQ kernel.
3.  **Unresolved Anomaly in Asymmetric Risk Evaluation.** The finding that standard GPs outperform Warped GPs under the pessimistic linex loss is a significant and counter-intuitive result. The authors' hypothesis—that the loss function is biased towards high-variance models—is plausible but is left for future work. Given that this is a core part of the paper's application-focused contribution, this unresolved issue feels like a major loose end. It raises questions about the reliability of the linex loss or its Bayes risk estimator for this type of problem, which warrants a more thorough investigation.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  You convincingly show the benefits of Warped GPs for post-editing time. How do you see these methods applying to other QE labels, such as HTER, which is bounded in [0, 1]? Would a different likelihood (e.g., Beta regression) or a different warping function be more appropriate? How might this choice impact the asymmetric risk analysis?
2.  Your finding on the pessimistic linex loss scenario is intriguing. You hypothesize a bias in the loss function towards models with high (and potentially nonsensical) variance. The Bayes risk estimator for linex loss depends only on the mean and variance of the posterior, which may be insufficient for the skewed distributions from Warped GPs. In contrast, the AL loss estimator uses quantiles, which seems more robust. Could you comment on whether you believe the issue lies with the linex loss itself, or with its standard Bayes risk estimator, and if an alternative estimator or loss function might be more stable?
3.  With the increasing popularity of neural network-based QE, how do you see this work informing that subfield? Do you believe that methods like GPs will retain an advantage over neural approaches (e.g., using MC Dropout or deep ensembles for uncertainty) in terms of the quality of calibration or flexibility, particularly in the low-data regimes common to many QE tasks?
