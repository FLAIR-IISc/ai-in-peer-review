### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the limitations of point estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) and advocates for probabilistic models that provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors argue that such estimates are crucial for real-world decision-making, such as in post-editing workflows. The work focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs) and explores advanced variants, namely models with Matérn kernels (to relax smoothness assumptions) and Warped GPs (to better model the non-Gaussian, positive-only nature of QE scores like post-editing time). The models are evaluated not only on standard regression metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) but also on Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), a metric that properly assesses the quality of a predictive distribution. Finally, the paper demonstrates the practical utility of these probabilistic models in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-estimation of quality carry different penalties, by deriving optimal predictions using Bayes risk estimators without model retraining.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A compelling argument and practical demonstration for adopting uncertainty-aware evaluation in QE.** The paper's primary strength is its successful case for moving beyond point-estimate metrics like MAE. By introducing and justifying the use of NLPD, the authors show that significant differences in model quality (specifically in uncertainty modeling) are missed by standard evaluation protocols. This is an important message for the QE community.
2.  **A thorough application and analysis of advanced GP models for the QE task.** While Warped GPs and Matérn kernels are not new methods, their application to QE is well-motivated and empirically validated. The paper clearly demonstrates that Warped GPs are a much better fit for modeling post-editing time than standard GPs, providing a strong methodological takeaway for future work in this and similar text regression tasks.
3.  **An elegant and practical framework for handling asymmetric risk in QE.** The demonstration of how a single probabilistic model can be used to generate optimal predictions for various asymmetric loss functions (via Bayes risk estimators) is a significant practical contribution. This shows how to build flexible QE systems that can be adapted to changing business requirements without costly retraining.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Strong motivation and high practical relevance.** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It grounds the research in a clear, practical need—improving decision-making in translation workflows. The asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic post-editing vs. optimistic gisting) are concrete, intuitive, and directly reflect real-world applications of QE, making the work highly impactful.
2.  **Methodologically sound and insightful evaluation.** The experimental setup is robust, using multiple datasets and cross-validation. The core strength of the evaluation is the principled use of NLPD. The results in Table 1 compellingly show that while MAE and Pearson's r see little difference between models, NLPD reveals substantial improvements from using Warped GPs. This provides strong evidence for the paper's central thesis.
3.  **Clear and accessible presentation.** The paper is very well-written and structured. Complex topics like Warped GPs and Bayes risk are explained with clarity, making the work accessible to a broad audience. The discussion of the results is nuanced, correctly identifying, for example, that the benefits of Matérn kernels are less conclusive and that overfitting may be an issue on smaller datasets.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited technical novelty and engagement with modern QE architectures.** The core methods (GPs, warping, NLPD) are established in the machine learning literature. The contribution lies in their application to QE. However, the experimental setup is based on an outdated feature set (17 QuEst features). The field of QE has largely moved to systems based on large pre-trained language models (e.g., TransQuest, COMET). It is unclear whether the paper's conclusions—particularly the benefits of relaxing smoothness assumptions with Matérn kernels—would hold for high-dimensional, dense features from transformers. This limits the immediate applicability of the findings to the current state of the art.
2.  **Inconclusive and underexplored results for the pessimistic risk scenario.** The results for the Linex loss in the pessimistic scenario (Table 2) are a significant concern. The proposed Warped GP models perform substantially *worse* than the standard GP baseline. The authors' hypothesis—that this is due to the estimator's dependence on variance—is plausible but not sufficiently investigated. This finding contradicts the paper's main narrative that Warped GPs are superior and is too critical to be deferred to future work. A deeper analysis is required to understand this phenomenon, as it undermines the general applicability of the proposed solution.
3.  **Lack of comparison to alternative uncertainty estimation methods.** The paper focuses exclusively on GPs. While the motivation for GPs is clear, the claims would be much stronger if contextualized by a comparison to other popular methods for uncertainty estimation in NLP. For instance, deep ensembles or Monte Carlo dropout are widely used with neural networks to produce predictive distributions. Comparing the GP-based approach to these alternatives, even on a smaller scale, would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs involved and better situate the contribution within the broader landscape of uncertainty-aware modeling.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the surprising results for the pessimistic Linex loss, where standard GPs outperform Warped GPs: Your hypothesis about the estimator's dependence on variance is interesting. Could you provide a more detailed analysis? For instance, could you visualize the predictive distributions from both models for a few test instances? Is it possible that the standard GP's poor modeling (i.e., placing probability mass on impossible negative values) artificially inflates its variance in a way that coincidentally benefits this specific Bayes risk estimator?
2.  Your experiments are based on a small, traditional feature set. How do you foresee your findings translating to modern, neural QE models that use high-dimensional embeddings from transformers? Specifically, do you believe the relaxed smoothness of Matérn kernels would still be advantageous, or might the inherent smoothness of the EQ kernel be a better inductive bias for semantic embedding spaces?
3.  Could you elaborate on the decision to focus solely on GPs and not include comparisons to other widely used uncertainty estimation techniques like deep ensembles or MC dropout? Such a comparison would be valuable for readers to understand the relative advantages of your proposed approach against strong, more mainstream neural alternatives.
