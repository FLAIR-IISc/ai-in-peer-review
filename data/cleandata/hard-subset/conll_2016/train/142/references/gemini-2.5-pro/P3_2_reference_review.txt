### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the important but often-overlooked issue of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that conventional point estimates (e.g., a single quality score) are insufficient for real-world decision-making workflows, such as determining whether a translation should be post-edited or discarded. The core of the work is to advocate for and explore probabilistic models that provide not just a prediction, but a full predictive distribution. The paper focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs), investigating extensions like Matérn kernels (to relax smoothness assumptions) and Warped GPs (to model non-Gaussian, strictly positive quality scores). The contributions are demonstrated through two sets of experiments: an intrinsic evaluation using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to assess the quality of the predictive distributions, and an extrinsic evaluation showing the practical utility of these distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios using specialized loss functions.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  A clear, well-motivated, and practical application of advanced probabilistic regression models (Warped GPs) to the task of QE. The work compellingly demonstrates that modeling the predictive uncertainty provides significant benefits over standard point-estimate approaches, particularly in realistic application scenarios involving asymmetric costs.
2.  A strong argument, backed by empirical evidence, for shifting the evaluation paradigm in QE (and other NLP regression tasks) from purely point-estimate metrics like MAE to principled probabilistic metrics like NLPD, which better reflect a model's confidence and calibration.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem. The introduction effectively argues why point estimates are insufficient for QE and how uncertainty can enable more sophisticated and robust decision-making. The post-editing and gisting examples are concrete, convincing, and ground the research in real-world needs. This work represents a step towards more mature and deployable QE systems.
2.  **Sound and Principled Methodology:** The choice to focus on Gaussian Processes is well-justified, given their inherent probabilistic nature and strong performance in low-data regression. The exploration of specific extensions—Matérn kernels and especially Warped GPs—is methodologically sound and directly addresses known challenges with QE data (noise, non-Gaussian targets). The explanation of the models and the rationale behind them is exceptionally clear.
3.  **Comprehensive and Insightful Evaluation:** The evaluation is a key strength. Rather than simply claiming state-of-the-art on a single metric, the authors provide a multi-faceted analysis.
    *   The use of NLPD for intrinsic evaluation is principled and correctly highlights the shortcomings of MAE and Pearson's r in assessing probabilistic models. The results in Table 1 convincingly show that models can have similar point-estimate performance but vastly different probabilistic calibration.
    *   The extrinsic evaluation under asymmetric risk (Section 4) is a novel and significant contribution to the QE literature. It demonstrates a tangible benefit of the proposed models, showing how one can derive optimal decisions for different cost scenarios from a single trained model, which is highly valuable in practice.
4.  **Clarity and Quality of Writing:** The paper is very well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The authors successfully introduce complex concepts from the GP literature to an NLP audience, provide clear justifications for their choices, and offer thoughtful discussions of their results, including the inconclusive or surprising ones (e.g., the Linex loss results).

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Comparison to Alternative Models and Uncertainty Methods:** While the focus on GPs is well-motivated, the paper would be strengthened by a broader comparison. The most popular QE method, SVR, is dismissed for being non-probabilistic, but a simple baseline using bootstrapped SVR ensembles could have provided a valuable comparison point for uncertainty quality. More importantly, the paper does not engage with modern neural network-based QE. It would be highly relevant to discuss how the principled uncertainty from GPs compares to heuristic but scalable methods for neural networks like Monte Carlo dropout or deep ensembles, which are now the standard for SOTA QE.
2.  **Use of a Basic Feature Set:** The experiments rely on a 17-feature set from the older QuEst framework. While the authors state their goal is model comparison rather than achieving SOTA results, this choice limits the generalizability of the findings. The behavior of these models and the benefits of different kernels might change dramatically in the high-dimensional, dense feature spaces produced by modern pre-trained encoders (e.g., XLM-R). Verifying that these conclusions hold with more powerful, contemporary features would make the work more impactful.
3.  **Scalability is Under-addressed:** The paper dismisses scalability concerns by noting that QE datasets are typically small. While historically true, this is changing. Furthermore, the value of QE extends to scenarios with massive data (e.g., filtering web-crawled parallel corpora), where standard GPs are computationally infeasible. The paper cites work on scalable GPs (Hensman et al., 2013) but does not use or discuss them. A brief discussion on the path towards scaling these methods would have made the paper more forward-looking.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Your hypothesis regarding the poor performance of Warped GPs under the pessimistic Linex loss is very interesting—that their lower (and more realistic) variance prevents the Bayes risk estimator from making sufficiently conservative predictions. This suggests a potential fragility in the Linex loss itself, as it seems to favor poorly-calibrated models that assign high variance everywhere. Did you consider evaluating with other asymmetric losses, such as the asymmetric quadratic loss, which is less extreme than the exponential Linex loss and might provide a more stable evaluation?
2.  Could you comment on the computational overhead of the Warped GP compared to the standard GP, particularly during hyperparameter optimization? The warping function introduces several new hyperparameters (a, b, c for each tanh term), and understanding the impact on training time and optimization stability would be useful.
3.  Given the current dominance of large pre-trained models in NLP, how do you envision integrating these principled uncertainty estimation techniques with modern neural QE architectures? Do you see a path for hybrid models, or do you believe methods like deep ensembles and MC dropout are sufficient for practical uncertainty estimation in that context?
