### Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The central argument is that standard point-estimate predictions and their corresponding evaluation metrics (like MAE) are insufficient for real-world QE applications, which often involve decision-making under uncertainty. The authors explore extensions to the standard GP framework, namely the use of Matern kernels to handle noisy data and Warped GPs to model non-Gaussian, strictly positive quality scores. They advocate for evaluating these models using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), a metric that assesses the entire predictive probability distribution rather than just a single point estimate. The utility of these probabilistic models is demonstrated in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., when underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating it), showing how Bayes risk estimators can be derived for different loss functions without retraining the model.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A strong, well-supported argument for shifting the evaluation of QE models from point-estimate metrics to uncertainty-aware metrics.** The paper effectively demonstrates that standard metrics like MAE and Pearson's r fail to distinguish between models with substantially different predictive distributions, whereas NLPD successfully captures these differences. This highlights a methodological shortcoming in standard QE evaluation practices.
2.  **An application and empirical evaluation of more suitable probabilistic models for QE.** The authors show that Warped GPs, which learn a non-linear mapping of the target variable, are a better fit for the strictly positive nature of QE scores (post-editing time) and lead to significantly better-calibrated predictive distributions as measured by NLPD.
3.  **A practical demonstration of how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in decision-making.** The paper frames QE in the context of asymmetric risk and shows how to derive optimal estimators for different scenarios (optimistic vs. pessimistic) from a single probabilistic model, providing a concrete example of the benefits of modeling uncertainty.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and clearly framed problem.** The paper provides a compelling motivation for moving beyond point estimates in QE. The introduction clearly outlines a realistic post-editing scenario where prediction uncertainty is crucial for decision-making. This framing is practical and addresses a significant limitation of how QE is often approached, making the work highly relevant to the field.
2.  **Technically sound methodology and experimentation.** The paper is grounded in established machine learning literature on Gaussian Processes. The choice of Matern kernels and Warped GPs is well-justified for the properties of QE data (noisy, non-Gaussian targets). The experimental design is robust, using 10-fold cross-validation on three distinct datasets. The use of NLPD for intrinsic evaluation and Bayes risk for extrinsic evaluation is appropriate and provides a comprehensive assessment of the models.
3.  **Clear and convincing results demonstrating the inadequacy of standard metrics.** Table 1 presents a key result: while MAE and Pearson's r show minimal differences between models, the NLPD metric reveals substantial improvements from using Warped GPs. This provides strong empirical evidence for the paper's central claim that standard evaluation practices can be misleading and that the quality of uncertainty estimates is a critical, and often overlooked, aspect of model performance.
4.  **Novel application of asymmetric risk analysis to QE.** While asymmetric losses are not new, their application to QE is novel and impactful. Section 4 provides a concrete framework for using probabilistic QE models in real-world workflows where the cost of errors is not symmetric. This significantly enhances the practical utility of QE and opens a promising direction for future research.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited methodological novelty.** The core techniques presented in the paper—Gaussian Processes, Matern kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, and Bayes risk for asymmetric losses—are all well-established methods from the machine learning literature. The primary contribution is the application and synthesis of these methods in the context of QE. While this is a valuable contribution, the work does not propose a new model or algorithm, which may limit its impact from a purely methodological perspective.
2.  **Scope of experiments is limited to a small, traditional feature set.** The experiments are conducted using a standard set of 17 handcrafted features. State-of-the-art QE systems increasingly rely on high-dimensional features extracted from large pre-trained language models. It is unclear whether the findings, particularly the superiority of the proposed GP models and the efficiency of hyperparameter optimization, would generalize to these much larger and more complex feature spaces where GPs may face scalability and performance challenges.
3.  **Inconclusive results and insufficient analysis of failure cases.** The paper reports some results that are either inconclusive or counterintuitive without providing a full explanation. For instance, the benefits of Matern kernels are not consistently observed across all datasets. More importantly, the Warped GP models perform significantly worse than the standard GP baseline under the pessimistic linex loss (Table 2). The authors briefly speculate this is due to the model's lower variance but leave a deeper investigation to future work. This is a significant negative result that contradicts the overall narrative and warrants a more thorough analysis, as it suggests the proposed models may not be universally superior in all asymmetric risk scenarios.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The poor performance of the Warped GP models under the pessimistic linex loss is a surprising and important finding. Could you elaborate on your hypothesis that this is due to the model's lower variance? Does this point to a fundamental limitation of the Warped GP for this type of loss, or could it be an artifact of the specific estimator used? Have you considered whether the linex loss itself might be overly sensitive to variance, potentially favoring poorly calibrated models that assign high variance even to nonsensical negative values?
2.  Your experiments are based on a 17-dimensional feature set. How do you expect these findings to translate to modern QE systems that use high-dimensional neural features (e.g., embeddings from XLM-R)? Would the GP-based models explored here remain computationally feasible and effective, or would alternative methods for uncertainty quantification (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, ensembles) be more appropriate in that setting?
