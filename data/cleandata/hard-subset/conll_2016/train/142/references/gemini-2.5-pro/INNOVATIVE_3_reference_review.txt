**Summary of the paper and main contributions**

This paper advocates for a shift in how Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) is approached and evaluated. The authors argue that conventional point estimates are insufficient for practical applications and that models should instead produce well-calibrated predictive distributions that quantify uncertainty. The work focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs), exploring extensions like Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better model the properties of QE data. The paper makes three main contributions as I see them:

1.  **Championing a new paradigm for QE:** The primary contribution is the compelling conceptual argument to move QE from a simple regression task focused on point estimates towards a probabilistic prediction task where uncertainty is a first-class citizen. This reframing has significant implications for the utility and real-world applicability of QE systems.
2.  **Introducing a more appropriate evaluation framework:** The paper introduces and validates the use of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as an evaluation metric for QE. It provides strong empirical evidence that standard metrics like MAE and Pearson's r are blind to crucial differences in model uncertainty, making NLPD a much more suitable tool for evaluating and comparing probabilistic QE models.
3.  **Demonstrating practical utility in decision-making:** The authors provide a concrete and highly relevant application of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., optimistic gisting vs. pessimistic post-editing). This section powerfully demonstrates how a single probabilistic model can be flexibly adapted to different business needs by deriving Bayes risk estimators, making the abstract concept of uncertainty tangible and actionable.

**Strengths**

1.  **High Conceptual Novelty:** This work is a breath of fresh air. Instead of presenting an incremental improvement on existing benchmarks, it challenges the very foundation of how QE is commonly evaluated and used. By focusing on uncertainty, the paper opens up a new and exciting direction for the field, moving the conversation towards building more reliable and useful tools for real-world translation workflows. This kind of bold, perspective-shifting work is exactly what our community needs.
2.  **Principled and Convincing Evaluation:** The introduction of NLPD is a major strength. The authors don't just propose it; they clearly demonstrate its necessity. The results in Table 1 are striking, showing how NLPD can distinguish between models that appear identical under MAE or Pearson's r. This provides a clear path forward for more meaningful evaluation in future QE research.
3.  **Strong Connection to Real-World Applications:** The section on asymmetric risk is outstanding. It grounds the entire paper in a practical context and shows the tangible benefits of the proposed approach. The ability to cater to different risk profiles ("pessimistic" vs. "optimistic") without retraining the model is a powerful feature that would be highly valuable in any production environment. This makes the work not just an academic exercise but a genuinely useful contribution.
4.  **Excellent Exposition:** The paper is very well-written, with clear motivation and a concise explanation of the underlying GP framework. The arguments are easy to follow, and the connection between the theoretical models and their practical implications is maintained throughout.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Empirical Scope:** The experiments are based on a simple 17-feature set and relatively small datasets. While sufficient for demonstrating the core concepts, it leaves open the question of how these methods perform in the context of modern, large-scale neural QE models that use rich representations from pre-trained transformers. It is unclear if the specific benefits of, for example, Warped GPs would persist when a powerful neural model might implicitly learn similar data transformations.
2.  **Inconclusive Findings and Unexplored Phenomena:** The paper honestly reports some inconclusive results, such as the mixed benefits of Matérn kernels. More notably, the counter-intuitive result where Warped GPs perform poorly under the pessimistic linex loss is fascinating but not fully explained. The authors' hypothesis about variance is plausible but not investigated deeply. While I commend the transparency, this feels like a significant loose end that slightly undermines the conclusiveness of the asymmetric risk experiments.
3.  **Absence of Alternative Uncertainty Baselines:** The paper's focus is exclusively on Gaussian Processes. While GPs are an elegant choice, the argument for probabilistic modeling in general would have been stronger if compared against other methods for uncertainty quantification. For instance, a simple but effective baseline like deep ensembles or even bootstrapping a standard SVR model could have provided a valuable point of comparison to better isolate the benefits of the GP framework itself versus the general benefit of modeling uncertainty.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the negative result for Warped GPs with the pessimistic linex loss: You suggest this is due to the lower variance of the warped models. Could this also indicate that the linex loss, with its exponential penalty, is perhaps ill-suited for this task, as it might unfairly reward poorly-calibrated models that simply exhibit high variance (like the standard GP assigning mass to impossible negative time values)? Have you considered evaluating with other asymmetric losses, such as the asymmetric quadratic loss, which also incorporates variance but may be more stable?
2.  Your work makes a powerful case for probabilistic QE using a classic machine learning framework. How do you envision these ideas being integrated with the current state-of-the-art in QE, which is dominated by large neural network architectures? Should future work focus on developing hybrid models, or on applying different uncertainty quantification techniques (e.g., deep ensembles, Bayesian neural networks) to existing neural QE systems?
