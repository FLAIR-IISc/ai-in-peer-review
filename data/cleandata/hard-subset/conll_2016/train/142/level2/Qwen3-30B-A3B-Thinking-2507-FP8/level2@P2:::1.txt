

**Summary and Main Contributions**  
The paper investigates probabilistic approaches for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) that provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates, rather than relying solely on point predictions. The authors propose extending Gaussian Process (GP) models with Matèrn kernels and warped GPs to better capture uncertainty, and introduce NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) as a superior evaluation metric that considers the full predictive distribution. They demonstrate the practical utility of these models in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., post-editing workflows where overestimation is more costly than underestimation). The primary contributions are: (1) establishing NLPD as a critical metric for evaluating uncertainty in QE, (2) showing that GP extensions improve uncertainty calibration, and (3) illustrating how predictive uncertainty enables better decision-making in asymmetric risk contexts.

**Strengths**  
1. **Strong motivation and relevance**: The paper effectively argues why uncertainty estimation is crucial for real-world QE applications, such as post-editing workflows where point estimates alone can lead to suboptimal decisions. This addresses a meaningful gap in current QE research, which predominantly focuses on point estimate metrics.  
2. **Compelling evidence for NLPD**: The authors convincingly demonstrate that NLPD captures uncertainty differences missed by traditional metrics (MAE, Pearson's r). For example, on fr-en and en-de datasets, NLPD distinguishes between warped GP variants (log vs. tanh), while MAE fails to do so. This is a significant contribution, as it provides a clear methodological shift for evaluating probabilistic QE models.  
3. **Practical application in asymmetric risk**: The asymmetric risk scenario analysis (e.g., optimistic/pessimistic settings) is well-motivated and demonstrates tangible benefits of probabilistic models. The results show that warped GP models consistently outperform standard GPs in optimistic scenarios, proving that uncertainty information directly improves real-world decision-making.  
4. **Rigorous theoretical grounding**: The paper thoroughly explains GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) and their relevance to QE, avoiding superficial claims. The use of established metrics (NLPD) and loss functions (AL, linex) ensures methodological soundness.

**Weaknesses**  
1. **Limited comparison with non-probabilistic baselines**: The paper claims GP extensions improve uncertainty estimation but does not rigorously compare their *point estimate performance* (e.g., MAE) against state-of-the-art non-probabilistic QE models (e.g., SVR or neural networks). Without this, it's unclear if the uncertainty benefits come at the cost of degraded point predictions, which is critical for adoption.  
2. **Scalability concerns unaddressed**: While the authors note QE datasets are "small," they provide no analysis of how their GP-based approach scales to larger datasets (e.g., >10k examples), which are increasingly common in NLP. GPs have O(n³) training complexity, making them impractical for large-scale QE. This omission undermines the paper's real-world applicability.  
3. **Inconsistent results on small datasets**: The en-es results (smallest dataset) contradict trends observed on larger datasets (e.g., log warping outperforms tanh, while NLL is unreliable). The authors attribute this to overfitting but do not propose solutions (e.g., regularization, data augmentation), leaving the viability of their approach unclear for low-resource settings.  
4. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper does not isolate the impact of individual GP extensions (e.g., Matèrn vs. warped GPs). For instance, it's unclear whether the performance gains stem from warping, Matèrn kernels, or both. This weakens the causal claims about model improvements.

**Questions to Authors**  
1. How do the authors plan to address the scalability limitations of GPs for large-scale QE tasks (e.g., using sparse approximations or neural GP hybrids)?  
2. Can the authors provide a comparison of their point estimate performance (MAE) against the top non-probabilistic QE systems from recent WMT challenges?  
3. What specific strategies would the authors recommend for handling the inconsistent results observed on the small en-es dataset?
