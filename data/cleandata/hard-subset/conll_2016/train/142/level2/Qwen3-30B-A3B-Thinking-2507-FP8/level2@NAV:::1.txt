

This paper investigates probabilistic approaches for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) that provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates, addressing a critical gap in current point-estimate-focused QE models. The authors' primary contributions are: (1) demonstrating that Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) is a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models compared to traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r), as it captures uncertainty information; (2) showing that warped Gaussian Processes (GPs) with tanh warping functions consistently outperform standard GPs in NLPD and asymmetric risk scenarios; and (3) proving the practical utility of predictive distributions in real-world translation workflows with asymmetric risk (e.g., post-editing prioritization).

Strengths include a compelling real-world motivation for uncertainty in translation workflows, robust experimental validation across three language pairs (en-es, fr-en, en-de) using 10-fold cross-validation, and a clear demonstration that uncertainty modeling differences are invisible to standard metrics. The NLPD evaluation framework is well-justified through theoretical grounding (Quiñonero-Candela et al., 2006) and practical examples in asymmetric loss scenarios (e.g., pessimistic/optimistic post-editing strategies). The paper effectively bridges GP theory with applied QE, offering actionable insights for practitioners.

Key weaknesses are: (1) The small en-es dataset (858 samples) leads to inconsistent results (e.g., log warping outperforms tanh), raising overfitting concerns that are not thoroughly addressed; (2) Limited comparison to non-GP probabilistic models (e.g., Bayesian neural networks), despite claiming GPs are the natural choice; (3) The paper fails to resolve why Matérn kernels show marginal gains only for fr-en, suggesting insufficient exploration of kernel impact. Additionally, while asymmetric loss experiments are insightful, the pessimistic scenario results for linex loss with warped GPs are counterintuitive (worse than standard GPs) without sufficient analysis.

The authors should clarify the en-es dataset limitations, expand probabilistic model comparisons, and investigate the Matérn kernel inconsistency. The work is promising for QE practitioners but requires deeper analysis of dataset-specific effects and broader model benchmarking to strengthen its claims. The core contribution—NLPD as a metric and uncertainty's utility in asymmetric settings—remains highly valuable for the field.
