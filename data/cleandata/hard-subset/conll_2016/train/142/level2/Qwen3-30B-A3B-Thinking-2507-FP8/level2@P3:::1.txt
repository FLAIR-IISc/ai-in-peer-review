

This paper explores the integration of uncertainty estimation into Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point-estimate approaches are insufficient for real-world translation workflows where decisions require understanding prediction reliability. The main contributions are: (1) proposing the use of Gaussian Processes with Matèrn kernels and Warped GP extensions for probabilistic QE modeling; (2) advocating for NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r); and (3) demonstrating how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios, such as post-editing workflows with different costs for overestimation versus underestimation.

Strengths of this work include: First, the paper provides a compelling motivation for why uncertainty estimation matters in QE, particularly in practical translation workflows where the cost of different types of prediction errors varies significantly. The examples of pessimistic and optimistic scenarios are well-chosen and clearly illustrate the practical value of uncertainty information. Second, the paper makes a strong case for NLPD as a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic models, showing that standard metrics like MAE fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling that NLPD reveals. This is an important contribution to the evaluation methodology in QE. Third, the paper demonstrates a practical application of uncertainty information by showing how it can be used in asymmetric risk scenarios without retraining models, which is valuable for real-world deployment where loss functions may change over time.

The paper has several weaknesses that would need addressing for stronger impact. First, the evaluation of Matèrn kernels shows inconsistent performance across datasets (better for fr-en but not for en-es or en-de), yet the paper does not provide sufficient analysis to explain these differences. Without understanding why Matèrn kernels work better for some language pairs but not others, it's difficult to determine when these kernels should be preferred over the standard EQ kernel. Second, the paper's comparison of probabilistic models is limited to Gaussian Processes and does not include other probabilistic approaches that have been successfully applied in QE, such as Bayesian neural networks or ensemble methods. This limits the generalizability of the findings about the benefits of probabilistic modeling. Third, the results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are confusing and not adequately explained - warped GPs consistently perform worse than standard GPs despite having better uncertainty estimates, which contradicts the paper's main thesis. The paper should investigate this anomaly more thoroughly to determine whether it reflects a limitation of the models or a problem with the evaluation metric.

The paper would benefit from additional experiments comparing the proposed models with other probabilistic approaches, as well as a more detailed analysis of the inconsistent performance of Matèrn kernels across datasets. The authors should also provide a clearer explanation for the unexpected results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, as this undermines the paper's main argument about the benefits of uncertainty estimation.

Questions to authors:
1. The paper states that "the gains from using a Matèrn kernel over EQ are less conclusive" but doesn't provide statistical significance testing for these differences. How were statistical significance tests conducted to determine whether the observed performance differences between kernels are meaningful?
2. The paper shows that Warped GPs with tanh-based warping outperform log warping for fr-en and en-de but not en-es. Could the authors provide additional analysis of dataset characteristics (e.g., size, noise level, feature distributions) that might explain this language-pair dependent behavior?
3. The paper reports that warped GPs perform worse than standard GPs in the pessimistic scenario with linex loss, despite having better uncertainty estimates. Could the authors elaborate on why this might occur, particularly given that the linex loss is designed to be more sensitive to uncertainty information?
