

Summary of Contributions: The paper's primary contribution is establishing NLPD as a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models by demonstrating its ability to capture uncertainty differences that standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) miss. The second contribution is the practical demonstration of how calibrated uncertainty estimates enable better decision-making in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., post-editing workflows). The third contribution is the proposal of specific model extensions (Matérn kernels and warped GPs) for improved uncertainty calibration in QE.

Strengths:
1. The paper compellingly argues for the practical necessity of uncertainty estimation in QE, moving beyond theoretical interest to concrete workflow applications (e.g., post-editing filtering based on confidence thresholds). This real-world relevance significantly strengthens the paper's contribution to the field.
2. The evaluation methodology using NLPD is well-justified and effectively demonstrates the limitations of traditional metrics. The results showing NLPD captures meaningful differences between models (e.g., tanh vs. log warping) that MAE misses provide strong empirical support for adopting this metric.
3. The asymmetric risk experiments successfully bridge theory and practice. The clear demonstration that warped GPs outperform standard GPs in optimistic scenarios (with AL loss) and the thoughtful analysis of why linex loss behaves differently in pessimistic settings show the work's practical utility.

Weaknesses:
1. The experimental scope is limited to three language pairs with a fixed feature set. While the authors acknowledge this, the paper lacks sufficient analysis of why Matérn kernels show inconsistent benefits across datasets (e.g., fr-en vs. en-es). More extensive experiments with diverse language pairs and feature sets would better support the claim that Matérn kernels are generally advantageous.
2. The discussion of the linex loss's performance in pessimistic scenarios is underdeveloped. The paper notes warped GPs performed worse due to "less variance" but doesn't explore whether this reflects a fundamental limitation of warped models or an artifact of the specific loss function. A deeper analysis would strengthen this section.
3. The paper claims "NLPD should be preferred as an evaluation metric," but doesn't provide clear guidance on interpreting NLPD values across different datasets or model configurations. Adding a discussion of typical NLPD ranges for QE tasks would make this recommendation more actionable for practitioners.

Questions to Authors:
1. The paper uses 3 tanh terms for the warped GP. Could you share ablation results on the impact of varying the number of tanh terms (e.g., 1, 2, 3) on NLPD and asymmetric risk performance?
2. For the small en-es dataset, log warping outperformed tanh. Given the overfitting concerns noted, is there a correlation between dataset size and optimal warping function choice that could be generalized?
3. The paper states NLPD "penalizes the overconfident model more than the underconfident one." Could you clarify how the NLPD penalty differs quantitatively between these two scenarios, perhaps with a simple example?
