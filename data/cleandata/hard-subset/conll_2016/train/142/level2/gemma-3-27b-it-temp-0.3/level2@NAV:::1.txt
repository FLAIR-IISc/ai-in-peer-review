## Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic methods, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors argue that traditional point-estimate evaluation metrics are insufficient for real-world QE applications where decision-making relies on understanding prediction confidence. They explore model extensions within the GP framework and evaluate their performance using both intrinsic metrics (NLPD) and in an asymmetric risk scenario simulating post-editing workflows.

The primary contribution of this work is a compelling argument for moving beyond point-estimate evaluation in QE and adopting metrics like NLPD that consider the full predictive distribution. The authors demonstrate that differences in uncertainty modeling are not captured by standard metrics like MAE and Pearson’s r. A second contribution lies in the exploration of different GP configurations (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) and their impact on uncertainty calibration. Finally, the paper provides a practical demonstration of how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios, showing potential benefits for decision-making in post-editing.

**Strengths:**

* **Relevance and Timeliness:** The paper addresses a critical limitation in QE – the lack of consideration for prediction uncertainty – which is increasingly important for practical applications.
* **Sound Methodology:** The use of GPs is well-justified, and the exploration of kernel variations and warping functions is thorough. The experimental setup with multiple language pairs and cross-validation is robust.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the theoretical background and experimental details.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results convincingly demonstrate the inadequacy of point-estimate metrics and the benefits of using NLPD for evaluating probabilistic QE models. The asymmetric risk scenario provides a compelling use case.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Scope of Models:** The focus is almost exclusively on GPs. While justified, exploring other probabilistic models (e.g., Bayesian Neural Networks) could broaden the scope and provide a more comprehensive comparison.
* **Asymmetric Loss Results:** The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and require further investigation. The authors acknowledge this, but a more detailed discussion of potential reasons would be beneficial.
* **Feature Set:** While the authors justify the use of a standard feature set, it might be worth briefly discussing the potential impact of more sophisticated features on the results.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the different GP configurations (e.g., Matèrn kernels vs. warped GPs) and how this might affect their scalability to larger datasets?
2.  The paper highlights the limitations of point-estimate metrics. Do you foresee any challenges in adopting NLPD as a standard evaluation metric in the QE community, and how might these be addressed?
3.  Regarding the inconclusive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, could you explore whether different parameter settings or regularization techniques might improve performance?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper is a valuable contribution to the field of QE and provides a strong foundation for future research on uncertainty modeling and its applications. The authors are encouraged to address the weaknesses identified above in a revised version of the paper.
