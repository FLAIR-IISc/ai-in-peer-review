This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on well-calibrated uncertainty estimates and their application to asymmetric risk scenarios. The central argument is that relying solely on point estimates is insufficient for real-world MT workflows, and incorporating uncertainty can improve decision-making, particularly in post-editing scenarios.

The primary contribution of this work is a thorough empirical evaluation of different GP configurations (kernels, warping functions) for QE, using metrics that assess the quality of the predictive distributions (NLPD) rather than just point predictions. This detailed comparison, showcasing the limitations of standard evaluation metrics like MAE and Pearson’s r, is valuable. A second contribution lies in demonstrating the practical benefit of these uncertainty estimates by applying them to asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic), using asymmetric loss functions and Bayes risk estimation. Finally, the paper provides a clear and comprehensive overview of relevant GP techniques, making it a useful resource for researchers entering this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novel Evaluation Perspective:** The paper convincingly argues for moving beyond point estimate-based evaluation in QE, advocating for metrics like NLPD that truly assess the quality of the predictive distribution. This is a significant contribution, as it addresses a crucial shortcoming in current QE research and evaluation practices. The empirical evidence presented strongly supports this claim, demonstrating that differences in uncertainty modeling are not captured by traditional metrics.
2. **Comprehensive GP Exploration:** The authors systematically explore different GP configurations (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs with different warping functions) and provide a detailed explanation of the underlying theory. This thoroughness allows for a nuanced comparison of the models and contributes to a better understanding of their strengths and weaknesses in the context of QE.
3. **Practical Application & Asymmetric Risk:** The application of the probabilistic models to asymmetric risk scenarios is a strong point. This demonstrates the tangible benefits of uncertainty estimation in a realistic use case (post-editing or gisting) and provides a clear justification for the research. The use of asymmetric loss functions and Bayes risk estimation adds to the practical relevance of the work.
4. **Well-Written and Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the complex concepts of Gaussian Processes and probabilistic modeling. The structure is logical, and the explanations are easy to follow, even for readers not deeply familiar with the field.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide sufficient details about the datasets, features, and experimental settings, facilitating reproducibility of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Probabilistic Models:** The paper focuses exclusively on Gaussian Processes. While GPs are a strong baseline, exploring other probabilistic models (e.g., Bayesian Neural Networks, Deep Ensembles) could provide a more comprehensive comparison and potentially uncover more effective approaches for uncertainty estimation in QE. The justification for focusing on GPs (scalability and low-data regimes) feels somewhat dated given recent advancements in deep learning.
2. **Linex Loss Inconclusiveness:** The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and require further investigation, as acknowledged by the authors. The potential bias of the loss function towards models with higher variance deserves more attention. This weakens the overall argument for the benefits of probabilistic modeling in all asymmetric risk scenarios.
3. **Feature Set Limitations:** While the authors acknowledge the use of a standard feature set, the potential impact of more sophisticated features on the performance of the probabilistic models is not fully explored. More modern feature engineering techniques might reveal additional benefits.
4. **Dataset Dependency:** The observed differences in performance between the warping functions (tanh vs. log) seem to be somewhat dataset-dependent. A more systematic investigation of this dependency across a wider range of datasets would strengthen the conclusions.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis of the predictions. Understanding *why* the models make errors, and how the uncertainty estimates correlate with these errors, could provide valuable insights for future research.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the NLL is not a reliable metric for small datasets. Could you elaborate on this observation and provide a more formal justification?
2.  Regarding the inconclusive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, do you have any hypotheses as to why the model is not effectively leveraging the variance information? What steps could be taken to address this issue?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly for the tanh-based warped GPs? Did you observe any significant variability across different random restarts?
4.  Could you comment on the computational cost of training and evaluating the different GP models, especially in comparison to simpler point-estimate based QE models?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation could be improved for clarity. For example, defining σv before using it in the EQ kernel formula would be helpful.
*   Figure 1 showing the asymmetric loss functions is helpful.
*   The related work section could be expanded to include more recent advances in uncertainty estimation in deep learning.
