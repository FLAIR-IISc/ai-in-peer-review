This paper investigates the use of probabilistic methods, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors argue that relying solely on point estimates in real-world translation workflows is limiting, and that incorporating uncertainty can improve decision-making processes, such as determining whether a segment requires post-editing or can be accepted directly. They evaluate different GP models (varying kernel and likelihood choices) using both intrinsic evaluation (Negative Log Predictive Density - NLPD) and an asymmetric risk scenario.

The primary contribution of this work is the thorough empirical investigation of probabilistic QE models, particularly GPs, and a strong argument for evaluating these models using metrics that consider the full predictive distribution (NLPD). The authors demonstrate that differences in uncertainty modeling are not captured by traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r). Secondly, the exploration of asymmetric loss functions to model different real-world scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic) and the application of Bayes risk estimation is a valuable addition. Finally, the comprehensive comparison of different GP configurations (kernels, warped GPs) provides a useful resource for researchers in this area.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance and Motivation:** The paper addresses a crucial limitation of current QE systems - the lack of uncertainty estimates - and convincingly argues for its importance in practical applications. The post-editing scenario provided is a clear and relatable example.
2. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation using multiple datasets, different GP configurations, and both intrinsic and extrinsic metrics. The use of NLPD is particularly commendable and provides a more nuanced evaluation than traditional metrics.
3. **Clear Explanation of Technical Details:** The paper provides a well-structured and clear explanation of the underlying concepts, including GPs, Matèrn kernels, and warped GPs. The mathematical formulations are presented in a digestible manner.
4. **Practical Application Focus:** The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios and Bayes risk estimation demonstrates a practical understanding of how uncertainty estimates can be leveraged in real-world translation workflows. This moves beyond simply providing uncertainty estimates to suggesting how they can be used effectively.
5. **Comprehensive Related Work:** The related work section is extensive and covers relevant areas, including QE, GPs, and asymmetric loss functions, establishing the paper’s position within the broader research landscape.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Probabilistic Models:** The paper focuses exclusively on GPs, while acknowledging other probabilistic models exist. While GPs are a strong choice, a brief discussion of the limitations of using *only* GPs (e.g., computational cost for large datasets, potential limitations in capturing complex dependencies) and potential avenues for exploring other probabilistic approaches (e.g., Bayesian Neural Networks) would strengthen the work.
2. **Inconclusive Results for Linex Loss:** The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are described as inconclusive, suggesting potential issues with the loss function or its interaction with the model. While acknowledged, a more in-depth discussion of *why* these results are inconclusive is needed. Specifically, the authors propose the lower variance of the warped GPs might be the issue, but this could benefit from further analysis or exploration of alternative loss functions tailored for low-variance predictions.
3. **Feature Set Limitation:** The authors intentionally use a limited feature set to ensure comparability across language pairs. However, this may limit the overall performance of the models. While the focus is on uncertainty modeling, acknowledging the potential impact of feature selection on point estimates (and thus indirectly on uncertainty) would be beneficial.
4. **Dataset Size Disparities:** The differences in dataset sizes across language pairs (especially the en-es dataset being significantly smaller) could influence the robustness of the results. The authors mention this as a possible explanation for some observations, but a more formal analysis of the impact of dataset size on the evaluation metrics would be valuable.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the computational challenges of scaling GPs to larger datasets and whether exploring approximate inference techniques (e.g., sparse GPs) could address these challenges?
2. Regarding the inconclusive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario, have you considered alternative loss functions specifically designed for low-variance predictions?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of features? Did you experiment with larger feature sets for specific language pairs and how did that affect the observed differences in uncertainty modeling?
4.  Could you provide more insight into why the en-es dataset exhibits different behavior compared to fr-en and en-de, and how this might impact the generalizability of the findings?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. A minor suggestion would be to include a table summarizing the different GP configurations tested (kernel, likelihood, warping function).
*   The use of consistent notation and terminology throughout the paper is appreciated.
*   The inclusion of relevant citations and a comprehensive related work section demonstrates a thorough understanding of the field.
