- Paper investigates probabilistic QE using Gaussian Processes (GPs) to provide calibrated uncertainty estimates for MT quality estimation.
- Core claim: point estimates are insufficient for real-world QE; modelling uncertainty improves decision-making in human-in-the-loop scenarios.
- Main contribution: extensive empirical evaluation of GP configurations, highlighting warped GPs and the use of NLPD to assess predictive uncertainty.
- Secondary contribution: application of asymmetric loss functions (e.g., AL, linex) to frame QE as a risk-sensitive decision problem using probabilistic outputs.
- Additional contribution: systematic comparison of Matèrn kernel choices within the GP framework for QE.
- Strength — Relevance: strongly motivated problem and practical post-editing scenario illustrating the need for uncertainty.
- Strength — Empirical rigor: experiments across multiple language pairs/datasets with 10-fold cross-validation.
- Strength — Metrics: advocates for NLPD as a superior metric to evaluate full predictive distributions over point estimates.
- Strength — Practicality: demonstrates concrete use of probabilistic outputs via asymmetric loss and Bayes risk estimation.
- Strength — Presentation: paper is well-written with clear, concise mathematical derivations.
- Weakness — Model scope: limited to GPs; other probabilistic models are not explored or compared.
- Weakness — Linex results inconclusive: explanation for pessimistic scenario performance is speculative and needs deeper analysis.
- Weakness — Feature set: relatively limited features used, possibly constraining overall model performance.
- Weakness — Dataset size effects: lack of systematic study on how dataset size affects model performance and reliability of NLL.
- Weakness — Error analysis: paper lacks detailed error-case analysis to identify specific failure modes.
- Question: what data or model characteristics cause tanh-warped GPs to outperform standard GPs in the optimistic scenario?
- Question: were variance-control strategies (regularization, variance reduction) tried to address linex loss issues in the pessimistic scenario?
- Question: how sensitive are results to the number of tanh terms (I) in warped GPs and was this parameter systematically tuned?
- Question: were other asymmetric loss functions considered, and why were AL and linex chosen over others?
- Additional suggestion: include a dataset summary table (size, language pair, post-editing time distributions).
- Additional suggestion: report confidence intervals or statistical significance tests when comparing models.
- Additional suggestion: discuss computational cost of different GP configurations as a practical consideration.
