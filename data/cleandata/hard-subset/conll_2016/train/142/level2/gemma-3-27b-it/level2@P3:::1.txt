This paper investigates probabilistic methods for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs) and their ability to provide calibrated uncertainty estimates. The core argument is that point estimate metrics are insufficient for real-world QE applications, particularly in scenarios involving human-in-the-loop post-editing, and that incorporating uncertainty can improve decision-making. The work explores different GP kernel choices (Matèrn kernels) and warping functions, evaluates these models using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), and demonstrates their utility in asymmetric risk scenarios through the use of asymmetric loss functions.

The primary contribution lies in the thorough empirical evaluation of different GP configurations for QE, specifically highlighting the benefits of warped GPs and the importance of using metrics like NLPD that account for predictive uncertainty. This is a significant contribution, as the field has largely relied on point estimate metrics, potentially overlooking valuable information contained in the model’s predictive distribution. A second, related contribution is the application of asymmetric loss functions to QE, framing the problem in a more realistic risk-sensitive manner and demonstrating how the uncertainty estimates from probabilistic models can be leveraged to optimize decision-making. Finally, the systematic comparison of different kernel choices within the GP framework for this task also represents a valuable addition to the existing literature.

Strengths:

1. **Relevance and Motivation:** The paper addresses a crucial limitation of current QE systems – the lack of uncertainty information – and persuasively argues its importance in practical applications. The post-editing scenario is well-motivated and effectively illustrates the potential benefits of incorporating uncertainty.
2. **Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The work conducts extensive experiments across multiple language pairs and datasets, systematically comparing different GP configurations. The use of 10-fold cross-validation enhances the reliability of the results.
3. **Appropriate Metrics:** The paper rightly advocates for the use of NLPD as a more informative evaluation metric than traditional point estimate metrics, as it directly assesses the quality of the predictive distribution. The demonstration that point estimate metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling is compelling.
4. **Practical Application:** The exploration of asymmetric loss functions and Bayes risk estimation provides a concrete example of how the probabilistic outputs of the models can be used to improve decision-making in real-world scenarios.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The mathematical derivations are concise and easy to follow.

Weaknesses:

1. **Limited Scope of Models:** While the focus on GPs is justified, the paper doesn’t explore other probabilistic modeling approaches beyond GPs. Considering other methods, even briefly, could strengthen the argument for the value of probabilistic QE in general.
2. **Asymmetric Loss Sensitivity:** The results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive, and the explanation regarding the impact of variance on the estimator is speculative. More investigation into the behavior of different asymmetric losses and their sensitivity to model variance is warranted. A more detailed sensitivity analysis of the w parameter would be valuable.
3. **Feature Set Limitations:** While acknowledging the trade-offs, the decision to use a relatively limited feature set might limit the overall performance of the models. Exploring the impact of more comprehensive feature sets, even on a subset of the data, could provide further insights.
4. **Dataset Size Impact:** The paper recognizes that the results are dataset-dependent. However, a more systematic investigation into how dataset size affects the performance of the different models, particularly regarding the reliability of NLL as a metric, would be beneficial.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on aggregate performance metrics. A deeper analysis of specific error cases and the types of instances where the models struggle could provide valuable insights for future research.

Questions to Authors:

1. The paper mentions that the tanh-based warped GP models consistently outperform standard GPs in the optimistic scenario. Can the authors elaborate on the specific characteristics of the data or the model that contribute to this improvement?
2. The results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive. Were any alternative strategies explored to address the potential issues related to variance, such as regularization or variance reduction techniques?
3. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the number of tanh terms (I) in the warped GP model? Was any systematic optimization or selection process used for this parameter?
4. The discussion of asymmetric loss functions focuses primarily on AL and linex. Were other asymmetric loss functions considered, and if so, why were they not included in the evaluation?

Additional Comments:

*   It would be beneficial to include a table summarizing the key characteristics of the datasets used in the experiments, such as size, language pair, and post-editing time distributions.
*   The use of confidence intervals or statistical significance tests to compare the performance of different models would further strengthen the analysis.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the computational cost associated with the different GP configurations, as this is an important practical consideration.
