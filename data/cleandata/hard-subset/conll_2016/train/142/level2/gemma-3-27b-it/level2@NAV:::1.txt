## Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic methods, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors argue that traditional QE evaluation metrics ignore prediction uncertainty and propose evaluating models based on their full posterior predictive distributions, demonstrating the usefulness of this approach in an asymmetric risk scenario – a common situation in MT workflows.

The primary contribution of this work is the empirical demonstration that differences in uncertainty modeling *are not* captured by standard point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r). This is a valuable finding, highlighting a limitation in current QE evaluation practices. A second contribution is the exploration and comparison of different GP configurations (kernels, warping functions) and their impact on uncertainty calibration. Finally, the paper presents a practical application of these uncertainty estimates through the use of asymmetric loss functions, showing potential benefits in real-world scenarios like post-editing workflows.

The strengths of this paper lie in its rigorous experimental setup, covering multiple language pairs and leveraging established datasets. The comprehensive evaluation using NLPD, alongside point estimate metrics, provides a nuanced view of model performance. The clear motivation stemming from practical MT workflow considerations strengthens the relevance of the work. The thorough exploration of different GP configurations is well-executed.

However, the paper suffers from several weaknesses. The discussion of asymmetric loss functions, while theoretically sound, feels somewhat underdeveloped. The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive, and the explanation feels tentative. The benefits of using Matèrn kernels over the standard EQ kernel are not consistently demonstrated. The paper could also benefit from a more thorough discussion of computational costs associated with the GP models, especially given their potential scalability limitations. While the authors acknowledge these limitations, expanding upon them would be beneficial. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the computational challenges associated with using GPs, particularly for larger datasets?
2.  You mention that the linex loss results in the pessimistic scenario were inconclusive. Do you have any hypotheses as to why this occurred, and what further experimentation could clarify these findings?
3. While acknowledging the potential of using uncertainty estimates in active learning, could you provide more detail on how that would be implemented within the QE framework you’re proposing?
