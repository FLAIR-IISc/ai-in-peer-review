**Summary of the paper**

This paper argues that the standard evaluation of Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) models via point-estimate metrics (like MAE or Pearson's r) is insufficient, as it ignores valuable information about prediction uncertainty. The authors advocate for a probabilistic approach and propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) to model the full predictive distribution over quality scores. The paper's contributions are:

1.  **The introduction and empirical validation of an uncertainty-aware evaluation paradigm for QE.** The authors make a compelling case for adopting the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a primary metric. Their experiments demonstrate that NLPD can distinguish between models that achieve similar point-estimate performance but have different quality of uncertainty estimates, thereby penalizing overconfident incorrect predictions.
2.  **A novel application of probabilistic QE models to asymmetric risk scenarios.** The paper shows how the predictive distributions from a single trained model can be used to derive optimal estimators for different asymmetric loss functions (e.g., in "pessimistic" or "optimistic" post-editing scenarios) without requiring model retraining. This demonstrates a significant practical benefit of the probabilistic approach.
3.  **A systematic comparison of GP model variants for the QE task.** The paper evaluates standard GPs against more flexible variants, such as Warped GPs (which learn a non-linear mapping of the output space) and GPs with Matèrn kernels (which relax smoothness assumptions). The results show that Warped GPs, in particular, provide better-calibrated predictive distributions as measured by NLPD.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong motivation and clear, practical relevance.** The paper addresses a clear and important limitation of current QE practices. The argument that point estimates are insufficient for real-world decision-making (e.g., deciding whether to post-edit or discard a translation) is very convincing. The framing of the problem in terms of asymmetric risk is excellent and connects the research directly to plausible industry use cases.
2.  **Sound and insightful experimental analysis.** The experimental design effectively supports the paper's core claims. By comparing NLPD with MAE and Pearson's r across three datasets (Table 1), the authors clearly show that standard metrics fail to capture important differences in model uncertainty. The experiments in Section 4 successfully demonstrate how a single probabilistic model can be flexibly adapted to different downstream tasks (optimistic vs. pessimistic) by deriving minimum Bayes risk estimators, which is an elegant and powerful application.
3.  **Advocates for a better evaluation paradigm.** The paper's main push to move the QE field towards probabilistic evaluation is a significant strength. By highlighting the shortcomings of existing metrics and proposing a well-established alternative from the machine learning community (NLPD), the work provides a clear and constructive path forward for future QE research.
4.  **The paper is very well-written and structured.** The concepts are introduced logically, from the motivation for uncertainty, to the description of the models, to the intrinsic and extrinsic evaluations. The connection between the probabilistic models and their application in asymmetric scenarios is made explicit and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Methodological novelty is limited.** The core methods explored (Gaussian Processes, Warped GPs, Matèrn kernels, NLPD, asymmetric losses) are all well-established in the machine learning literature. The paper's primary contribution is the application and synthesis of these ideas in the context of QE. While this is a valuable contribution, the paper could be strengthened by more explicitly positioning itself as an application and evaluation-focused paper, rather than implying methodological innovation.
2.  **Lack of comparison to simpler uncertainty baselines.** The paper focuses exclusively on GPs as the probabilistic model of choice. However, other simpler methods can produce uncertainty estimates. For example, a simple ensemble of SVR models (using bagging, as briefly mentioned by the authors) or a small neural network with dropout-based uncertainty could have served as important baselines. Without these comparisons, it is difficult to ascertain whether the complexity of GPs is truly necessary to achieve the reported benefits.
3.  **Inconclusive and underexplored results for the pessimistic linex loss.** The results in Table 2 for the pessimistic scenario with linex loss are counter-intuitive, showing the proposed Warped GP models performing consistently worse than the standard GP baseline. The authors' explanation—that the warped models have lower variance and are therefore not conservative enough—is brief and speculative. This result is a significant wrinkle in the paper's narrative and weakens the claims about the general applicability of these models. The issue deserves a much deeper analysis, as it may point to a fundamental limitation of the models or the estimators in this setting.
4.  **Experiments are based on a simple, low-dimensional feature set.** The authors use a standard set of 17 features, justifying this for comparability and simplicity. However, state-of-the-art QE systems often employ hundreds or thousands of features. The conclusions drawn here about the relative performance of different GP models might not generalize to higher-dimensional settings, where issues like the "curse of dimensionality" and kernel choice become more critical. It is unclear if the observed benefits would hold in a more competitive, feature-rich setting.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Regarding the poor performance of Warped GPs in the pessimistic linex loss scenario: Your explanation suggests the issue is linked to lower variance. Have you considered that the variance estimates themselves might be poorly calibrated, particularly in the tails of the distribution? Could an analysis via calibration plots shed more light on why the model fails in this specific high-penalty region?
2.  You motivate the choice of GPs over SVR by noting that SVR lacks a direct probabilistic interpretation. However, an ensemble of SVRs could provide empirical uncertainty estimates and would be a strong, relevant baseline. Could you comment on why such a baseline was not included and how you would expect it to perform relative to the GP models on the NLPD metric and in the asymmetric risk tasks?
