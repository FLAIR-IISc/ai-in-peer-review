**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the important but often overlooked issue of predictive uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that traditional point estimates and their corresponding evaluation metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) are insufficient for real-world applications where QE models guide decision-making. The core of the paper is an investigation into probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), that can provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates alongside quality predictions. The paper makes the following contributions:

1.  **Advocacy and Demonstration of Uncertainty-Aware Evaluation:** The primary contribution is the successful argument for and application of evaluation metrics that consider the full predictive distribution. The authors introduce the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to the QE task and show empirically that it captures significant modeling improvements (e.g., from using Warped GPs) that are invisible to standard point-estimate metrics like MAE. This highlights a crucial deficiency in how QE systems are typically evaluated.
2.  **Application to Asymmetric Risk Scenarios:** The paper presents a novel and practical application of predictive uncertainty in QE by framing decision-making problems (e.g., filtering sentences for post-editing) as asymmetric risk scenarios. They show how a single probabilistic model can be used to derive optimal predictions for different cost functions (optimistic vs. pessimistic) via Bayes risk estimators, avoiding the need for model retraining.
3.  **Systematic Exploration of Probabilistic Models for QE:** The authors explore and compare several extensions to the standard GP regression model for QE. They demonstrate that Warped GPs, which learn a non-linear mapping of the target variable, are significantly better suited for modeling post-editing time than standard GPs, leading to much better-calibrated predictive distributions.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The critique of point-estimate-based evaluation in QE is sharp and convincing. Grounding the need for uncertainty in a concrete post-editing scenario makes the work highly relevant and highlights a clear path toward making QE systems more useful in practice.
2.  **Sound and Appropriate Methodology:** The choice of Gaussian Processes as the modeling framework is well-justified for this task, and the exploration of Matérn kernels and Warped GPs represents a methodologically sound investigation. Most importantly, the use of NLPD for evaluation is principled and directly addresses the paper's core thesis. The experimental design, using multiple datasets and cross-validation, is robust.
3.  **Novel and Practical Application:** The section on asymmetric risk scenarios is a key strength. It moves beyond simply showing that one model has "better uncertainty" and demonstrates *how* that uncertainty can be leveraged to make better, more nuanced decisions in a realistic setting. The ability to derive different estimators from a single model for different risk profiles is an elegant and practical solution.
4.  **Clarity and Insightful Analysis:** The paper is very clearly written and easy to follow, with helpful background provided where needed. The discussion of the results is insightful, particularly the observation that MAE and Pearson's r fail to reflect the large improvements in NLPD, which powerfully supports the authors' main argument. The analysis of potential overfitting on the smaller dataset and the discussion of the counter-intuitive results for the pessimistic Linex loss are thoughtful and transparent.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Model Comparisons:** The paper focuses exclusively on the Gaussian Process framework. While GPs are a strong choice, the claims about the importance of probabilistic modeling would have been strengthened by comparison to other methods for estimating uncertainty. For example, a simple but common baseline like bootstrapping an SVR ensemble is mentioned and dismissed in the introduction but not empirically compared. Including such a baseline would help contextualize the performance and benefits of the more sophisticated GP models.
2.  **Use of a Basic Feature Set:** The experiments are conducted using a standard set of 17 handcrafted features. While the authors state that their goal is model comparison rather than state-of-the-art performance, this choice limits the generalizability of the findings. Modern QE systems often rely on high-dimensional features from pre-trained language models. It is unclear if the observed benefits of Warped GPs and the specific behaviors of the asymmetric loss estimators would hold in such a high-dimensional setting.
3.  **Inconclusive Results for Matérn Kernels:** One of the proposed model improvements, the use of Matérn kernels to relax the smoothness assumption of the EQ kernel, did not yield conclusive benefits across the datasets. The authors acknowledge this, but it slightly weakens the contribution regarding the exploration of model extensions.
4.  **Ambiguous Findings for the Pessimistic Linex Loss:** The result that the standard GP outperforms the better-calibrated Warped GPs under the pessimistic Linex loss is puzzling. The authors' hypothesis—that the loss function is biased towards high-variance models—is plausible but also raises questions about the suitability of this specific loss/estimator combination for this task. This part of the analysis feels less resolved and might suggest that the proposed Bayes risk estimator is not robust for the highly skewed predictive distributions of the Warped GP models.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you comment on how you expect these findings to transfer to modern QE settings that use high-dimensional feature representations from models like BERT? Would the benefits of ARD kernels become more pronounced, and would the flexible `tanh` warping function still be as effective?
2.  The paper argues that bootstrapping approaches to uncertainty estimation are less principled than GPs. However, given their simplicity, would it be possible to provide a comparison against a bootstrapped SVR baseline, evaluated using NLPD (by fitting a Gaussian to the bootstrap predictions) and on the asymmetric risk task? This would provide a valuable point of comparison for the practicality of the GP-based methods.
3.  Regarding the results for the pessimistic Linex loss, you suggest the loss may be biased towards high-variance models. Is it also possible that the Bayes risk estimator, which relies on only the mean and variance, is a poor approximation for the non-Gaussian posterior of the Warped GP? Have you considered alternative estimators (e.g., based on numerical integration over the posterior) or alternative pessimistic loss functions that might be more robust?
