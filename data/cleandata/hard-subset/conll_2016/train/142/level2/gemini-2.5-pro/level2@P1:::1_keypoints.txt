- Introduces uncertainty-aware evaluation (NLPD) for QE and shows it reveals modeling gains (e.g., Warped GPs) missed by MAE/Pearson.
- Frames practical asymmetric risk decision tasks (e.g., filtering for post-editing) and shows a single probabilistic model can produce optimal estimators for different cost functions without retraining.
- Systematically explores GP extensions for QE and finds Warped GPs yield better-calibrated predictive distributions for post-editing time than standard GPs.
- Strongly motivated problem formulation linking uncertainty to real-world QE decisions, making the work relevant and useful.
- Methodologically sound: appropriate use of GPs, exploration of Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, and principled evaluation with NLPD and robust experimental design.
- Novel practical application demonstrating how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged for nuanced decision-making via Bayes risk estimators.
- Clear writing and insightful analysis, notably the observation that MAE/Pearson fail to reflect NLPD improvements and transparent discussion of overfitting and counter-intuitive results.
- Limited model comparisons: study focuses only on GPs and omits empirical baselines like bootstrapped SVR ensembles.
- Uses a basic 17-feature handcrafted set, limiting generalizability to modern high-dimensional, pre-trained feature representations.
- Matérn kernel benefits are inconclusive across datasets, weakening that aspect of model-extension claims.
- Puzzling result where standard GP outperforms better-calibrated Warped GPs under pessimistic Linex loss, raising concerns about loss bias or estimator robustness.
- Question: How will findings transfer to high-dimensional features (e.g., BERT); would ARD kernels help and is tanh warping still effective?
- Question: Can you compare to a bootstrapped SVR baseline evaluated with NLPD and the asymmetric risk task to contextualize GP practicality?
- Question: Could the Bayes risk estimator (mean/variance-based) be inadequate for non-Gaussian Warped GP posteriors, and have you considered numerical integration or alternative pessimistic loss estimators?
