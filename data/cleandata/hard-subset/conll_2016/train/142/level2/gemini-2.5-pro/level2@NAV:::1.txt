**Summary of the paper and main contributions:**
This paper addresses the limitation of point estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) by advocating for probabilistic models that quantify prediction uncertainty. The authors explore Gaussian Process (GP) models, including Warped GPs and Matérn kernels, to generate well-calibrated predictive distributions. The main contributions of this work are: (1) A clear empirical demonstration that standard point-estimate metrics like MAE are insufficient for evaluating probabilistic QE models, highlighting the need for distribution-aware metrics like Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD). (2) A novel application of uncertainty estimates to asymmetric risk scenarios, showing how Bayes risk estimators can adapt a single trained model for different real-world decision-making needs (e.g., optimistic vs. pessimistic post-editing) without retraining.

**Strengths:**
1.  **Strong Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper makes a compelling and well-articulated argument for the importance of uncertainty in QE, moving beyond simple accuracy to address practical decision-making in translation workflows. This focus on utility is a significant strength.
2.  **Rigorous Intrinsic Evaluation:** The experiments convincingly show that while different probabilistic models may achieve similar MAE scores, their underlying predictive distributions can vary significantly in quality. The use of NLPD to capture these differences is a crucial methodological point that could positively influence future evaluation practices in the field.
3.  **Practical Application in Asymmetric Scenarios:** The study of asymmetric losses is a highlight. By connecting predictive distributions to Bayes risk estimators, the authors provide a principled and flexible framework for using QE models in realistic scenarios where the cost of over- or under-estimation is unequal. This is a novel and valuable contribution to the QE literature.

**Weaknesses:**
1.  **Limited Scope of Models:** The paper focuses exclusively on Gaussian Processes. While GPs are a strong choice, the current state-of-the-art in QE often involves neural network architectures. A discussion or comparison with uncertainty estimation techniques for neural models (e.g., MC dropout, ensembles) would have contextualized the contributions more broadly within the current landscape of NLP.
2.  **Reliance on a Basic Feature Set:** The experiments use a standard but relatively simple set of 17 features. Although this is justified for isolating the comparison of probabilistic modeling techniques, it leaves unanswered how these methods would interact with the richer representations learned by modern, high-performance QE systems.
3.  **Inconclusive Findings for Pessimistic Linex Loss:** The paper reports that the proposed warped models perform poorly under the pessimistic linex loss, offering a brief hypothesis related to predictive variance. This finding feels underexplored and slightly weakens the otherwise strong claims about the models' general utility in asymmetric settings.

**Questions to Authors:**
1.  The linex loss in the pessimistic scenario heavily penalizes the proposed warped models. Could you elaborate on whether this indicates a fundamental limitation of the models, or perhaps an issue with the stability of the linex loss itself when applied to the non-Gaussian posteriors produced by Warped GPs?
2.  Given the prevalence of neural models, how do you see these GP-based uncertainty estimation methods complementing or competing with techniques like deep ensembles or Bayesian neural networks for the task of QE?
