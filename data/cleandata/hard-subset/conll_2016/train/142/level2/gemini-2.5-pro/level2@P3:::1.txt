### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the limitations of using point estimates in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) and advocates for probabilistic approaches that provide uncertainty measures. The work investigates Gaussian Processes (GPs), specifically with Matérn kernels and warping functions, as a framework for generating well-calibrated predictive distributions. The evaluation methodology is a key focus, with the paper promoting the use of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) over standard metrics like MAE, as NLPD assesses the entire predictive distribution. Finally, the practical utility of these distributions is demonstrated in asymmetric risk scenarios, where Bayes risk estimators are used to optimize for different application-specific loss functions without requiring model retraining.

### Main Contributions
1.  **A principled argument and demonstration for evaluating QE models based on their full predictive distributions.** The paper successfully shows that standard point-estimate metrics can be misleading, as models with similar MAE scores can exhibit significant differences in the quality of their uncertainty estimates, a difference effectively captured by the NLPD metric. This shifts the focus of QE evaluation towards a more complete and practically useful perspective.
2.  **A practical framework for adapting a single QE model to various real-world scenarios with asymmetric costs.** By applying Bayes risk estimators to the posterior distribution of a trained GP, the work demonstrates how to derive optimal predictions for different asymmetric loss functions (e.g., pessimistic or optimistic post-editing scenarios) efficiently, avoiding the significant computational cost of retraining models for each specific need.
3.  **A thorough empirical analysis of advanced GP models for the QE task.** The paper explores the effectiveness of Warped GPs and Matérn kernels, demonstrating that these extensions, particularly warping, lead to substantially better-calibrated models by correctly handling the non-Gaussian, strictly positive nature of the target variable (post-editing time).

### Strengths
1.  **Strong and Clear Motivation:** The paper is built on a compelling and well-articulated motivation. The argument that point estimates are insufficient for real-world decision-making in translation workflows is highly persuasive. The use of concrete examples, such as post-editing filtering and gisting, makes the problem tangible and highlights the practical importance of the proposed approach.
2.  **Sound and Effective Evaluation Methodology:** The adoption of NLPD as a primary evaluation metric is a major strength. The experiments in Section 3 provide clear evidence that traditional metrics fail to distinguish between models with different qualities of uncertainty estimation. This rigorous evaluation supports the central thesis of the paper and sets a high standard for future work in probabilistic QE.
3.  **Practical Relevance of Asymmetric Risk Analysis:** The investigation into asymmetric loss scenarios is a significant contribution. The proposed method of using Bayes risk estimators is elegant and practical, directly addressing how a deployed QE model could be flexibly adapted to changing business needs or user requirements. The honest and insightful discussion of the unexpected results for the linex loss adds to the credibility of the work.
4.  **High Clarity and Readability:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and structured. Complex concepts like Warped GPs and Bayes risk are explained with sufficient detail for the reader to follow the methodology, and the connection between the theoretical models and the experimental results is made very clear.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Exclusive Focus on Gaussian Processes:** The study is confined to GPs. While the choice is well-justified, the paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion of how this framework compares to other potential probabilistic modeling techniques (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, or even simpler ensemble-based uncertainty methods like bootstrapping). Such a discussion could clarify whether the advantages shown are unique to GPs or are characteristic of probabilistic modeling in general for this task. It would also be helpful to touch on the scalability of GPs, which are known to be challenging for very large datasets, even if current QE datasets are small.
2.  **Potential for Broader Discussion on Feature Impact:** The experiments rely on a standard, 17-feature set. This is a reasonable choice for ensuring a controlled comparison of the models. However, the field is increasingly moving towards representations derived from large pre-trained models. A short discussion on how the findings—particularly the relative benefits of different kernels and warping functions—might generalize to settings with high-dimensional, dense feature vectors would enhance the paper's forward-looking impact.
3.  **Unresolved Finding on Linex Loss:** The finding that Warped GPs underperform standard GPs in the pessimistic linex loss scenario is intriguing, but the analysis remains speculative. The explanation offered is plausible (i.e., the linex estimator's dependence on variance penalizes the tighter, more realistic variance of the warped models). This point could be slightly expanded to consider if this reveals a potential flaw in the linex loss itself for distributions with bounded support, as it may inadvertently reward models that are poorly calibrated but have high variance. While a full investigation is likely out of scope, framing this more explicitly as a cautionary note or a direction for future research on evaluation metrics would be valuable.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could the paper elaborate on the choice to focus solely on GPs? In particular, how does the ease of deriving Bayes risk estimators for GP posteriors compare with the likely need for Monte Carlo approximation for other models like Bayesian Neural Networks, and does this practical consideration favor the GP approach in production environments?
2.  Regarding the results for the pessimistic linex loss, the paper hypothesizes that the standard GP performs better due to its higher variance. Does this imply that the standard GP's "advantage" comes from being a worse model (i.e., assigning probability mass to impossible negative post-editing times, thereby artificially inflating its variance)? If so, this might suggest that the linex loss is not an ideal metric in this context. It would be insightful to hear further thoughts on this interaction between the model, the estimator, and the loss function.
