Summary and contributions
- The paper studies prediction uncertainty for sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE) using Gaussian Processes (GPs), including Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, and evaluates models with proper probabilistic metrics (NLPD) rather than solely point estimates.
- It demonstrates how posterior predictive distributions enable decision-making under asymmetric risks via Bayes-optimal estimators for asymmetric linear and linex losses.
- It provides empirical evidence across three QE datasets that (i) Warped GPs yield better-calibrated posteriors than standard GPs and (ii) standard point metrics (MAE, r) often fail to reflect differences in uncertainty quality.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and problem framing: The paper convincingly argues why point estimates are inadequate for QE in real workflows and why uncertainty and calibration matter, grounding the discussion in realistic post-editing and gisting scenarios.
- Sound probabilistic evaluation: Using NLPD (a proper scoring rule) and analyzing NLL vs NLPD to diagnose overfitting (notably on en–es) shows methodological rigor and appropriate emphasis on calibration, not just accuracy.
- Practical decision-theoretic angle: Mapping predictive posteriors to Bayes-optimal decisions for asymmetric losses is a valuable and underexplored perspective in QE, with concrete takeaways (e.g., optimistic settings benefit from warped models).
- Careful model choices and analysis: The use of Warped GPs for positive targets, ARD, and Matérn kernels is well justified; results isolate when tanh warp outperforms log warp and highlight dataset-size effects.
- Reproducible setup: Cross-validation protocol, features (QuEst 17), and optimization details are clearly stated, enabling replication.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Core methods (GPs, Warped GPs, Matérn, NLPD, asymmetric Bayes estimators) are established; the contribution is primarily an application study rather than a new model or learning algorithm.
- Narrow baselines: No comparison to modern neural QE models with uncertainty (e.g., Bayesian NNs, MC dropout, deep ensembles) or heteroscedastic GPs; thus, the empirical significance is constrained.
- Calibration diagnostics are missing: Beyond NLPD, the paper lacks reliability diagrams, PIT histograms, or CRPS, which would strengthen claims about “well-calibrated” uncertainty.
- Asymmetric loss analysis is incomplete: The puzzling linex pessimistic behavior is noted but not resolved; more diagnostics (variance decomposition, tail coverage, sensitivity to w, truncation at zero) are needed.
- Feature and scalability limitations: The 17 handcrafted features and small datasets limit generality; no discussion of computational cost or extensions to larger QE settings.

Questions to authors
- Can you report calibration plots (e.g., coverage of predictive intervals, PIT) and CRPS to substantiate calibration claims?
- How do your Warped GP models compare to neural QE with uncertainty (MC dropout, ensembles) on the same datasets?
- Did you try heteroscedastic or truncated (nonnegative) likelihoods to address the linex pessimistic failure modes?
- How sensitive are results to the number of tanh terms in the warp and to ARD priors/regularization?
