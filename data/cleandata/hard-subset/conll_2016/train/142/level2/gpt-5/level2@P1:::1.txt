Summary and contributions
This paper argues that uncertainty estimates are crucial for practical QE, proposes Gaussian Processes (GPs) and Warped GPs to produce calibrated predictive distributions, and evaluates models using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) in addition to standard point metrics. It further shows how predictive distributions enable decision-making under asymmetric risk via Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses.

Main contributions (as I see them):
1) Empirical demonstration that probabilistic QE models (especially Warped GPs) yield better-calibrated uncertainty (lower NLPD) than standard GPs, and that differences in uncertainty are not captured by MAE/Pearson’s r.
2) Application of Bayes risk decision rules to asymmetric loss scenarios in QE, showing benefits (for AL loss) of using predictive distributions without retraining.
3) A careful, task-grounded case for adopting proper scoring rules (NLPD) and kernel/likelihood choices (Matérn, output warping) when modeling uncertainty in QE.

Strengths
- Clear and timely motivation: The paper convincingly explains why point estimates are insufficient in QE workflows and how uncertainty can drive better decisions (post-editing, gisting). The asymmetric-risk framing is realistic and valuable.
- Solid probabilistic modeling choices: GPs are well-suited to small QE datasets and allow principled uncertainty. The exploration of Matérn kernels and output warping is appropriate for noisy, positive-support targets such as post-editing time.
- Proper scoring and evaluation: Introducing NLPD to QE is a substantive contribution; the experiments show that point metrics miss uncertainty differences. The training–test distinction between NLL and NLPD is highlighted appropriately.
- Practical decision layer: The Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses demonstrate how to use the posterior, not just assess it. The “no retraining” story is appealing for production scenarios where risk preferences change over time.
- Careful experimental protocol: Use of cross-validation across three language pairs, a standard feature set to control confounds, random restarts, and ARD refinement are reasonable and reproducible choices for 2016-scale QE.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The work primarily applies established GP tools (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) and standard proper scoring rules to QE. This is acceptable for an application-focused paper, but the novelty is more in evaluation perspective than in modeling.
- Calibration analysis is underdeveloped: While NLPD is informative, calibration diagnostics (e.g., probability integral transform histograms, coverage of predictive intervals, reliability diagrams, or CRPS) are not reported. These would directly validate the “well-calibrated” claim and may clarify why some asymmetric outcomes (linex) behave unexpectedly.
- Asymmetric linex estimator appears mismatched: The paper derives ŷ = μy − wσy^2/2 for linex, which is the Gaussian-optimal estimator for the canonical linex loss L(a)=exp(wa) − wa − 1. However, the loss defined here is L(ŷ,y)=exp[w(ŷ−y)] − (ŷ−y) − 1, where the linear term’s coefficient is 1, not w. For this definition, the Bayes estimator solves exp(w ŷ) E[exp(−w y)] = 1, yielding ŷ = −(1/w) log E[exp(−w y)], which equals μ − (w σ^2)/2 only if y is Gaussian and the linear term uses w. Moreover, for Warped GPs, p(y|x) is non-Gaussian, so the Gaussian closed form is not applicable. This likely contributes to the poor “pessimistic linex” results. A general solution could be computed numerically from the predictive y distribution.
- Missing uncertainty baselines: There is no comparison to non-GP uncertainty estimators such as bootstrapped SVR, quantile regression (e.g., gradient boosting quantiles), or Bayesian linear regression. Including at least one strong non-GP baseline would strengthen claims that GPs are preferable for uncertainty in QE.
- Statistical testing and robustness: The paper reports cross-validated averages but no dispersion estimates or significance tests across folds/instances. Some reported differences are small; confidence intervals or paired tests would help. Sensitivity to warping complexity (I), kernel choice, and priors/regularization is only briefly discussed.
- Inconclusive kernel findings and small feature set: The Matérn vs EQ results are mixed outside fr–en. While the goal is uncertainty, it would be helpful to check whether findings persist with richer feature sets or on additional QE targets (e.g., HTER), to rule out dataset-specific artifacts.

Questions to authors
- Linex loss: Please clarify the exact loss definition and the corresponding Bayes estimator. If you intend the canonical linex L(a)=exp(wa) − wa − 1, the estimator ŷ=μ − (wσ^2)/2 holds for Gaussian predictive y. For your stated loss with linear term coefficient 1, the optimal estimator differs. Did you compute ŷ = −(1/w) log E[exp(−w y)] from the predictive y distribution (which for Warped GP is non-Gaussian), or did you approximate it with μ − (wσ^2)/2?
- Quantile computation for AL loss under Warped GP: For AL, you state that the optimal estimator is the w/(w+1) quantile. How did you compute quantiles of the predictive y distribution in the warped models (closed-form inverse, numerical inversion, or Monte Carlo)? Please confirm the τ = w/(1+w) mapping (the text typesetting is ambiguous).
- Calibration diagnostics: Did you examine coverage (e.g., 50%/90% intervals) or PIT for the predictive distributions? If so, can you include results? This would strengthen the claim of “well-calibrated” uncertainty.
- Robust/heteroscedastic likelihoods: Did you try Student-t likelihoods or heteroscedastic GPs? These might better handle heavy-tailed and input-dependent noise often present in post-editing times and could interact meaningfully with asymmetric risk.
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and hyperparameter settings (e.g., warping terms, priors/constraints) to facilitate replication?

Additional comments
- Consider adding CRPS as a complementary proper scoring rule; it is less sensitive to extreme tails than NLPD and directly rewards calibrated uncertainty.
- Reporting standard errors/CI over folds and conducting paired tests would clarify which improvements are robust.
- The discussion about en–es overfitting via NLL vs NLPD is insightful; a simple learning-curve analysis could support this claim.
- There are a few minor equation typos/formatting issues (e.g., kernel equations, ambiguous “ww+1” quantile). Please proof-read equations for clarity.

Overall, this is a well-motivated paper that brings uncertainty-aware evaluation and decision-making to QE with convincing empirical evidence. Addressing the linex estimator mismatch and adding calibration diagnostics and stronger baselines would significantly strengthen the submission.
