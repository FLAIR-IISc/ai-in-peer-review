Summary and contributions
This paper investigates uncertainty-aware quality estimation (QE) for machine translation by framing QE within probabilistic Gaussian Process (GP) models and evaluating models using full predictive distributions rather than only point estimates. The main contributions are:
1) An empirical study of probabilistic QE with calibrated uncertainty, comparing standard GPs to Warped GPs and different kernels (EQ vs. Matérn), and evaluating with Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) alongside standard point metrics. The study shows that uncertainty-sensitive metrics reveal differences not captured by MAE/Pearson’s r, and that Warped GPs substantially improve NLL/NLPD.
2) A decision-theoretic treatment of asymmetric risk scenarios in QE, demonstrating how to compute minimum Bayes risk estimators from predictive posteriors for the asymmetric linear (AL) and linex losses, enabling adaptation to changing costs without retraining.
3) An evaluation across three QE datasets (en–es, fr–en, en–de) with a standardized feature set and 10-fold CV, yielding practical insights (e.g., warping benefits, kernel effects are data-dependent, linex pessimistic setting is challenging).

Strengths
- Clear and practical motivation: The paper convincingly argues that uncertainty is essential for decision-making in translation workflows (e.g., post-editing triage, gisting), where point estimates alone are brittle. The asymmetric risk framing maps well to real scenarios.
- Sound probabilistic methodology: The use of GPs, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs is appropriate for noisy, low-data QE regimes. The Bayesian treatment is correctly leveraged to obtain calibrated posteriors and Bayes estimators for changing losses without retraining.
- Proper scoring and evaluation: NLPD is an established proper scoring rule that accounts for both accuracy and calibration. The analysis highlights that improvements in uncertainty quality are not reflected in MAE/Pearson’s r, underscoring the need for distribution-aware evaluation in QE.
- Transparent experimental protocol: Three datasets, standardized QuEst features, 10-fold CV, two-pass kernel optimization (isotropic then ARD), and consistent evaluation across symmetric/asymmetric losses make the results credible and reproducible in spirit.
- Insightful analysis of findings: The discussion identifies overfitting risks on smaller data (en–es) when relying on NLL, interprets why warping improves NLL/NLPD (support on positive domain), and offers a plausible explanation for linex pessimistic underperformance (variance dependence).

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty: The core techniques (GPs with Matérn kernels, Warped GPs, NLPD, Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses) are established; the contribution is primarily applying and analyzing them in QE. This is valuable but incremental.
- Narrow baseline coverage for uncertainty: Comparisons focus on GP variants; there is no baseline with uncertainty from non-GP methods (e.g., bootstrapped/bagged SVR, quantile regression, quantile/random forest, Bayesian linear regression, or Bayesian neural approaches). This limits conclusions about the relative merit of GPs in QE uncertainty.
- Calibration diagnostics are underdeveloped: While NLPD is reported, there are no reliability diagrams, PIT histograms, prediction interval coverage/width, or CRPS. These would directly assess calibration vs. sharpness and would help interpret the linex behavior.
- Scope of features and generality: Experiments use 17 QuEst features. This choice controls confounds but may understate the applicability of conclusions to stronger QE setups with larger feature sets or representation learning. The paper notes this trade-off but it remains a limitation.
- Handling heteroscedasticity and heavy tails: QE labels (length-normalized post-editing rates) are plausibly heteroscedastic and heavy-tailed. Only homoscedastic Gaussian likelihoods (and warping) are considered. Alternative likelihoods (e.g., Student-t) or heteroscedastic GPs could better address the linex pessimistic scenario and improve calibration.

Questions to authors
- Calibration: Are reliability diagrams, PIT histograms, prediction interval coverage, or CRPS consistent with the NLPD improvements? Providing these would strengthen the calibration claims.
- Linex pessimistic setting: How sensitive are results to w? Could heavier-tailed likelihoods (Student-t) or explicit variance inflation mitigate the variance-driven bias observed?
- Heteroscedasticity: Was a heteroscedastic noise model considered? Do residuals suggest input-dependent noise after warping?
- Baselines: How do bootstrapped SVR quantiles or quantile regression compare on NLPD/CRPS and asymmetric losses? Even a lightweight baseline would contextualize GP benefits.
- Practicality: What are training/inference times per fold and dataset, and how many hyperparameters were optimized (including warping terms)? Any optimization instabilities observed?
- ARD insights: Do learned lengthscales provide interpretable feature relevance across language pairs?

Suggestions for improvement
Essential:
- Add calibration diagnostics (reliability/PIT, interval coverage, CRPS) to substantiate well-calibrated uncertainty claims and to interpret asymmetric-loss results. This is low-effort and high-impact.
- Include at least one non-GP uncertainty baseline (e.g., bagged SVR with predictive quantiles or quantile regression), to situate the gains of GPs in QE.

Optional (nice-to-have):
- Explore a heavy-tailed or heteroscedastic likelihood for the pessimistic linex setting; even a small-scale experiment on one dataset could clarify whether variance dependence explains the observed behavior.
- Provide sensitivity analyses over w for AL and linex, and report performance curves rather than single points.
- Report ARD-derived feature relevance and discuss cross-lingual patterns.
- Release code and scripts for reproducibility.

Overall, the work makes a timely and useful case for uncertainty-aware QE, demonstrates concrete benefits of Warped GPs and NLPD, and shows how posterior information enables asymmetric decision-making. Strengthening calibration analysis and baseline comparisons would substantially improve the paper’s impact and generality.
