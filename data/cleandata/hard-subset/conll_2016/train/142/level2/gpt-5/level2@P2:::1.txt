Summary and contributions
This paper addresses uncertainty estimation for sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE). It studies Gaussian Process (GP) models, including Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, and argues for evaluating models via predictive distributions rather than only point metrics. It further demonstrates how predictive uncertainty enables cost-sensitive decisions through asymmetric losses.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) A systematic empirical evaluation of probabilistic QE models (standard GP vs. Warped GP; EQ vs. Matérn kernels) with proper scoring (NLPD) and evidence that warping yields materially better-calibrated posteriors than standard GPs.
2) A demonstration of decision-theoretic use of the predictive posterior in asymmetric risk settings (post-editing vs. gisting), including Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear and linex losses.
3) An analysis showing that conventional point metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) fail to capture important differences in uncertainty quality.

Strengths
- Timely problem framing and practical significance: The paper makes a strong case that uncertainty is crucial for QE in real workflows (e.g., post-editing filtering) and goes beyond common point-metric evaluations. This is well-motivated and relevant to CoNLL.
- Appropriate probabilistic modeling choices: Warped GPs are a good fit for strictly positive targets (post-editing time/rate) and the Matérn family is a sensible alternative to EQ under noisy, less-smooth functions. The modeling section is clear and technically sound for the GP and warping components.
- Proper scoring and uncertainty-centric evaluation: Using NLPD (predictive density on held-out data) is a correct and informative choice; the paper convincingly shows that NLL can be misleading on small data (overfitting), while NLPD better reflects generalization of uncertainty. The finding that warping markedly improves NLPD is valuable.
- Decision-theoretic perspective: The asymmetric-risk experiments are a useful step toward actionable QE, showing how to adapt predictions without retraining when business costs change. The quantile-based estimator for AL is principled and well-justified.
- Careful experimental setup and discussion: Multi-language datasets, standard features (QuEst), 10-fold CV, and a two-stage hyperparameter procedure are reasonable; the authors also discuss when differences are inconclusive (e.g., Matérn vs. EQ on some datasets) and highlight potential overfitting in small data.

Weaknesses
- Incorrect generality of the linex Bayes estimator: The paper states ŷ = μ − (w σ²)/2 as the optimal estimator for linex using the predictive posterior’s mean μ and variance σ². This is only exact if the predictive posterior over y is Gaussian (or more generally, when log MGF is quadratic). Warped GP predictive posteriors are non-Gaussian and skewed; in that case, the Bayes estimator is ŷ = −(1/w) log E[exp(−w y)], not generally reducible to μ and σ². Using the normal-form estimator on Warped GP posteriors likely biases the asymmetric results, particularly in the pessimistic linex case. This is a substantive theoretical and empirical issue that can change conclusions.
- Lack of calibration diagnostics beyond NLPD: NLPD is a proper score but does not reveal miscalibration modes. The paper does not report coverage of predictive intervals, PIT histograms, calibration curves, or sharpness vs. calibration trade-offs. Given the central claim of “well-calibrated uncertainty,” calibration assessment is needed to substantiate it.
- Limited baselines for uncertainty: The study focuses solely on GP variants, while strong alternative uncertainty methods (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, MC dropout, deep ensembles, quantile regression, quantile/random forest methods, or bootstrapped SVR) are not empirically compared. Even if not claiming SOTA point accuracy, an uncertainty-focused comparison would contextualize the gains.
- Sparse analysis of warping complexity and overfitting: Warped GPs with up to 3 tanh terms can overfit in small data; while this is mentioned, there is no regularization/prior sensitivity analysis, nor comparison to simpler monotone transforms (Box–Cox, log-shift) with calibration checks. The observation that log warp can outperform tanh on small data suggests this deserves deeper analysis.
- Statistical rigor and reporting: The paper references tables but does not present variance/SE across folds or significance tests for key comparisons. Claims like “tanh with 3 terms performs better than log” would be strengthened with confidence intervals and consistent effect sizes across datasets.

Questions to authors
- How did you compute the linex Bayes estimator for Warped GP posteriors? Did you assume Gaussianity of y|x, or did you compute ŷ = −(1/w) log E[exp(−w y)] by numerical integration/sampling under the true (non-Gaussian) predictive? Please clarify and, if Gaussian approximation was used, quantify its impact.
- For the AL loss, how were predictive quantiles obtained for Warped GPs? Via numerical inversion of the CDF, Gauss-Hermite quadrature in latent space, or sampling?
- Did you evaluate calibration (e.g., coverage of central/upper quantiles, PIT, reliability diagrams)? If yes, please report; if not, could you include such diagnostics?
- Are there zeros in post-editing rates? If so, how did you handle log-based warping (log or log-shift) and was the same preprocessing applied across models?
- How sensitive are results to the number of tanh terms, priors/regularization on warp parameters, and lengthscale ARD? Any signs of overfitting beyond the NLL/NLPD discrepancy on en–es?

Additional comments
- Consider adding calibration plots and interval coverage metrics to substantiate “well-calibrated” claims.
- Include at least one alternative uncertainty baseline (e.g., MC dropout regression or deep ensemble) and a non-GP quantile method to contextualize findings.
- The asymmetric setting would benefit from a small end-to-end decision simulation (e.g., accept/reject MT for post-editing with downstream utility) to make cost implications concrete.
- Minor: clean up references (formatting artifacts), and clarify if code/data splits will be released for reproducibility.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and generally solid paper that foregrounds an important and underexplored dimension of QE. Addressing the linex estimator issue and adding calibration diagnostics would substantially strengthen the contribution.
