Summary and contributions
The paper argues that MT Quality Estimation (QE) should provide calibrated uncertainty, not just point predictions, and evaluates Gaussian Process (GP) variants accordingly. It introduces evaluation with Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), explores Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better model the noise and positivity of post-editing time/rate, and demonstrates how full predictive posteriors enable decision-making under asymmetric loss.

Main contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
1) Empirical case for evaluating QE models with proper probabilistic scoring (NLPD) rather than only point metrics, showing that calibration differences are not captured by MAE/Pearson r.
2) Application and assessment of Warped GPs (with learned monotone warpings) and Matérn kernels for QE, yielding better-calibrated predictive distributions on several datasets.
3) Framing QE use-cases with asymmetric risk and deriving Bayes-optimal decision rules (quantiles and linex estimators) from predictive posteriors, with empirical illustration of optimistic vs. pessimistic settings.

Strengths
- Problem relevance and clarity of motivation: The paper compellingly motivates why uncertainty matters in QE workflows (post-editing, gisting). It connects model outputs to realistic decisions where symmetric errors are inappropriate. This is an important perspective for the QE community.
- Sound probabilistic modeling choices: Using GPs for small-data QE is appropriate; Matérn kernels relax over-smoothness assumptions and Warped GPs address positivity/non-Gaussian targets. The modeling is technically sound and well-grounded in the GP literature. The two-pass ARD optimization and median-based point predictions are sensible.
- Proper-scoring evaluation with NLPD: The experiments show clear divergences between NLL/NLPD and MAE/Pearson r, supporting the thesis that point metrics can mask uncertainty quality. The finding that training NLL can mislead on small datasets (overfitting) while test NLPD is more reliable is useful guidance.
- Demonstration of posterior-informed decision rules: The Bayes-risk estimators enable loss-parameter changes without retraining, which is practical in production scenarios. The asymmetric-loss experiments illustrate that model choice can depend on risk preferences (e.g., linex’s sensitivity to variance), a valuable lesson for practitioners.
- Breadth across language pairs: Evaluations on three QE datasets with cross-validation add credibility that the observations are not single-dataset artifacts (e.g., different behavior on en-es vs. fr-en/en-de linked to data size).

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to prior work: GPs (including multi-task and ARD variants) have been applied to QE, and Warped GPs and NLPD are established in the GP literature. The contribution is largely empirical synthesis and application rather than methodological innovation. The novelty rests mainly in bringing calibration-centric evaluation and asymmetric-risk framing to QE.
- Baselines and comparisons are incomplete: The paper motivates against SVR by lack of probabilistic outputs, yet does not include strong deterministic baselines (e.g., SVR, DNNs) to contextualize point accuracy trade-offs, nor uncertainty-capable alternatives (bootstrap/bagging SVR, quantile regression, quantile regression forests, Bayesian/dropout NNs). Without these, it is hard to judge whether GPs are the most effective route to calibrated uncertainty in QE.
- Calibration diagnostics are shallow: NLPD is informative but insufficient alone. There is no assessment of calibration quality via coverage of predictive intervals, PIT histograms, reliability diagrams, or sharpness vs. calibration trade-offs. Claims about “well-calibrated” uncertainty would be stronger with such diagnostics.
- Asymmetric-risk evaluation is underdeveloped: The choice of w values is ad hoc and not tied to concrete utility/costs; there is no task-level decision analysis (e.g., accept/reject filtering rates, productivity impact) or curves over w. The explanation for linex under pessimism (variance effects) is speculative and not empirically validated (e.g., via controlled variance manipulation or ablations). Quantile-regression baselines (for AL) are absent.
- Reproducibility and detail: Important details are missing or vague: how the number of warp terms (I) was selected per dataset; initialization and constraints for warping parameters; optimization settings and convergence criteria; numerical methods for inverse warping/quantile computation; handling of potential overfitting (regularization, early stopping). Code availability is not stated. Small, noisy datasets and the restricted 17-feature set also limit generality.

Questions to authors (optional)
- Warping details: How did you choose the number of tanh terms (I) per dataset? Did you use validation-based model selection? How were warping parameters initialized and constrained to ensure monotonicity and avoid overfitting?
- Calibration assessment: Can you report empirical coverage of central predictive intervals (e.g., 50%, 90%), PIT/reliability plots, and sharpness to substantiate claims of improved calibration beyond NLPD?
- Baselines for uncertainty: Did you try quantile regression (e.g., pinball loss with linear/SVR kernels), bootstrap ensembles of SVR, quantile regression forests, or Bayesian/dropout NNs on the same features? How do their NLPD/coverage compare?
- Asymmetric risk: Can you relate w to concrete operational costs and show decision-oriented metrics (e.g., acceptance rate vs. cost, productivity proxies)? For AL, how do GP-based quantile estimates compare to a direct quantile regression baseline?
- Sensitivity and scalability: How sensitive are results to feature sets (e.g., larger QuEst++ sets) and to kernel choice/ARD priors? What are training/inference runtimes and memory footprints, and do they remain practical as data grows?

Overall, this is a well-motivated and technically solid empirical study that makes a useful case for uncertainty-aware evaluation and decision-making in QE, but its impact would be strengthened by broader baselines, deeper calibration analysis, and a more concrete asymmetric-risk evaluation.
