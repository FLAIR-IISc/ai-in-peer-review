- Paper advocates uncertainty-aware QE using Gaussian Process variants (Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) and evaluates predictive distributions rather than only point estimates.
- Contributions: (1) Empirical evidence that Warped GPs yield better-calibrated predictive distributions (NLPD) and that point metrics can hide uncertainty differences; (2) Decision-theoretic use of asymmetric losses to obtain Bayes estimators from predictive posteriors without retraining.

- Strength: Addresses an important practical gap by framing QE probabilistically and using proper scoring rules relevant to post-editing and gisting workflows.
- Strength: Sound probabilistic modeling choices—GPs for low-data QE, Matérn kernels for flexible smoothness, and Warped GPs to handle positive targets—with reasonable optimization for small datasets.
- Strength: Proper probabilistic evaluation (NLPD) highlights over/under-confidence and train/test NLL/NLPD divergence on small data.
- Strength: Clear decision-theoretic illustration mapping QE use-cases to asymmetric losses and showing nuanced Bayes estimator behavior across risk regimes.
- Strength: Authors acknowledge limitations and future work (overfitting risk, limited kernel evidence, need for deeper loss-specific study).

- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—GP variants and warping are standard; novelty is mainly in applying probabilistic evaluation and asymmetric decision-making to QE.
- Weakness: Calibration assessment is incomplete—no predictive interval coverage, PITs, reliability diagrams, or complementary scores like CRPS reported.
- Weakness: Linex estimator appears to assume Gaussian predictive posteriors; Warped GP posteriors are non-Gaussian, so the presented closed-form may be unjustified or approximate.
- Weakness: Monotonicity and overfitting safeguards for tanh-sum warping are under-specified—unclear how f′(y)>0 was enforced or regularized during optimization.
- Weakness: Baselines are narrow—only 17 QuEst features and GP variants tested; no comparison to other uncertainty-capable methods (e.g., Bayesian deep models, quantile regression, bagged SVR).
- Weakness: Modeling choices may be misspecified for QE targets—post-editing times can be positive, heavy-tailed, and heteroscedastic; alternatives (lognormal/gamma/Student-t/heteroscedastic GPs) are not explored.
- Weakness: Decision-centric evaluation is limited to loss-level analysis; lacking simulated accept/reject policies, expected utility/regret, or threshold curves that connect to real workflows.

- Question: For Warped GP non-Gaussian predictive distributions, how was the linex Bayes estimator ŷ = µy − (w σy²)/2 derived or approximated, and what is the impact of any approximation?
- Question: How were warping parameters constrained or reparameterized to guarantee monotonicity (f′(y)>0) across y, and did you observe any non-monotone warps or negative Jacobians?
- Question: Did you evaluate calibration diagnostics (predictive interval coverage, PIT histograms, CRPS, reliability diagrams), and can you report them to support the “well-calibrated” claim?
- Question: Did you try robust or heteroscedastic likelihoods (Student-t, lognormal, gamma, input-dependent noise) given heavy tails and heteroscedasticity in post-editing time?
- Question: How do Bayes estimators compare to models trained explicitly for asymmetric loss (e.g., quantile regression SVR/GP) and is the “no retraining” advantage worth any performance gap?
- Question: How sensitive are results to number of warping terms, priors/regularization, number of restarts, and kernel choice beyond Matérn vs EQ?
- Question: Can you provide decision-oriented metrics (accept/reject utilities, expected utility/regret, threshold curves) and discuss scalability (e.g., sparse variational GPs) for deployment?
