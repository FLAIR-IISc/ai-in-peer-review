- Paper thesis: QE should provide calibrated uncertainty (not just point estimates); evaluates GP variants and uses NLPD, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs to model post-editing time/rate and enable decision-making under asymmetric loss.

- Contribution 1: Empirical case for using proper probabilistic scoring (NLPD) instead of only point metrics (MAE/Pearson r).

- Contribution 2: Application and assessment of Warped GPs (learned monotone warpings) and Matérn kernels, showing better-calibrated predictive distributions on multiple QE datasets.

- Contribution 3: Framing QE with asymmetric risk and deriving Bayes-optimal decision rules (quantiles and linex estimators) from predictive posteriors, with empirical illustrations.

- Strength: Clear motivation — uncertainty matters in realistic QE workflows (post-editing, gisting) and symmetric error metrics are often inappropriate.

- Strength: Sound probabilistic modeling — GPs are appropriate for small-data QE; Matérn kernels and Warped GPs address smoothness and positivity/non-Gaussian targets.

- Strength: Methodological choices — two-pass ARD optimization and median-based point predictions are sensible and well-grounded.

- Strength: Proper-scoring evaluation — NLPD reveals calibration differences missed by MAE/Pearson r and shows training NLL can overfit on small datasets.

- Strength: Decision-rule demonstration — posterior-informed Bayes-risk estimators allow changing loss preferences without retraining and highlight model choice dependence on risk attitude.

- Strength: Evaluation breadth — experiments on three language-pair datasets with cross-validation increase credibility beyond single-dataset findings.

- Weakness: Limited novelty — methods (GPs, Warped GPs, NLPD) are established; contribution is mainly empirical synthesis and reframing for QE.

- Weakness: Incomplete baselines — lacks strong deterministic baselines (SVR, DNN) and uncertainty-capable alternatives (bootstrap SVR, quantile regression, quantile forests, Bayesian/dropout NNs).

- Weakness: Shallow calibration diagnostics — NLPD alone is insufficient; missing interval coverage, PIT histograms, reliability diagrams, and sharpness analysis.

- Weakness: Asymmetric-risk evaluation underdeveloped — choice of loss weight w is ad hoc, lacks task-level decision metrics, and linex variance explanations are speculative without ablation.

- Weakness: Reproducibility/details missing — unclear selection of warp term count, initialization/constraints, optimization settings, inverse warping/quantile computation, regularization, and code availability.

- Weakness: Limited generality — small noisy datasets and restricted 17-feature set may limit applicability to broader QE settings.

- Question: How was the number of tanh warp terms (I) chosen per dataset and how were warping parameters initialized/constrained to ensure monotonicity and avoid overfitting?

- Question: Can you report empirical coverage of predictive intervals (e.g., 50%, 90%), PIT/reliability plots, and sharpness to substantiate calibration claims beyond NLPD?

- Question: Did you compare to uncertainty baselines like quantile regression, bootstrap SVR, quantile regression forests, or Bayesian/dropout NNs, and how do their NLPD/coverage compare?

- Question: Can w be related to concrete operational costs and can you present decision-oriented metrics (accept/reject rates, productivity impact) or compare GP quantiles to direct quantile-regression baselines?

- Question: How sensitive are results to larger feature sets, kernel/ARD priors, and what are the training/inference runtimes and scalability as data grows?
