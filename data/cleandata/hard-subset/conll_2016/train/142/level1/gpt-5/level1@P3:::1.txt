Summary and contributions
This paper argues for uncertainty-aware Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) and evaluates Gaussian Process (GP) models that produce posterior predictive distributions rather than only point estimates. Concretely, the authors (a) investigate GP variants (Matérn kernels; Warped GPs to handle positive-valued targets), (b) advocate proper probabilistic evaluation via Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) alongside standard point metrics, and (c) show how predictive posteriors enable minimum Bayes risk decisions under asymmetric loss (AL and linex) that reflect realistic post-editing and gisting workflows. Experiments on three sentence-level QE datasets (post-editing time normalized by length; 17 QuEst features) indicate that Warped GPs materially improve NLL/NLPD, point metrics often mask uncertainty differences, and Bayes estimators offer utility in “optimistic” scenarios, with nuanced behavior in “pessimistic” linex settings.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1) Empirical demonstration that uncertainty-aware GP models (particularly Warped GPs) yield better-calibrated predictive distributions for QE, as captured by NLPD, and that point metrics can be misleading.
2) A decision-theoretic treatment of QE using asymmetric losses, showing how to obtain Bayes estimators from predictive posteriors without retraining and illustrating improved utility in certain risk regimes.

Strengths
- Importance and timeliness: The paper addresses a genuine gap in QE practice—decisions are made on the basis of uncertain predictions, yet models are judged by symmetric point metrics. Framing QE probabilistically and evaluating with proper scoring rules is impactful for practical workflows (filtering for post-editing, gisting).
- Sound probabilistic framing with sensible model choices: GPs are natural for low-data QE; Matérn kernels relax EQ’s smoothness assumptions; Warped GPs address positivity of post-editing rates without ad hoc log transforms. The two-pass ARD optimization is reasonable for small datasets.
- Proper probabilistic evaluation: Using NLPD to expose over/under-confidence is appropriate and widely used in the GP literature. The analysis showing divergence between NLL (train) and NLPD (test) on the smallest dataset is instructive.
- Decision-theoretic illustration: Mapping typical QE use-cases to asymmetric losses and obtaining Bayes estimators from posteriors is well motivated. The finding that warped models excel in optimistic AL regimes but can underperform standard GP under pessimistic linex regimes is nuanced and practically relevant.
- Clarity on limitations and future directions: The discussion acknowledges overfitting risks on small datasets, limited evidence for kernel superiority across language pairs, and the need for further study of loss-specific behaviors.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty relative to prior QE/Gaussian Process work: GPs (with ARD) for QE and even multi-task GPs have been explored. Warped GPs and Matérn kernels are standard in GP literature. The novelty lies primarily in bringing proper scoring/evaluation and asymmetric decision-making to QE, not in new models or algorithms.
- Calibration assessment is incomplete: While NLPD is a proper score, the paper does not evaluate calibration directly (e.g., predictive interval coverage, PIT histograms, reliability diagrams for regression, sharpness vs calibration trade-offs) or complementary proper scores such as CRPS. Given the paper’s thesis on “well-calibrated uncertainty,” this is a missed opportunity.
- Linex Bayes estimator appears to assume Gaussian predictive posteriors: The reported estimator ŷ = µy − (w σy²)/2 is exact for Gaussian predictive distributions. However, Warped GPs produce non-Gaussian posteriors over y. The correct Bayes estimator under linex loss is a* = −(1/w) log E[exp(−w y)], which reduces to the given expression only for Gaussian y. The manuscript does not state an approximation (e.g., Gaussian moment matching) or justify applying the Gaussian formula; this undermines conclusions about linex behavior for warped models.
- Monotonic warping and overfitting safeguards are under-specified: The tanh-sum warping requires monotonicity (f′(y) > 0) for a valid density transformation. It is unclear how parameter constraints were enforced during optimization to guarantee monotonicity across y, and what regularization or priors were used to prevent overfitting with up to three terms. Without constraints, the warping can become non-monotone, breaking the model’s probabilistic validity.
- Baselines and breadth: Experiments use only 17 QuEst features and compare only among GP variants. There are no comparisons to strong non-GP baselines that also yield uncertainty (e.g., SVR with bagging/quantiles; deep regression with Bayesian or MC-dropout uncertainty; explicit quantile regression for AL). It’s therefore hard to attribute improvements to probabilistic modeling vs model class/feature choices.
- Modeling assumptions may be misspecified for QE targets: Post-editing rates are positive and likely heavy-tailed and heteroscedastic. Warping addresses support but not input-dependent noise. Alternatives (lognormal/gamma likelihoods; Student-t likelihood for heavy tails; heteroscedastic GPs) are not explored, and could materially affect NLPD and linex outcomes.
- Decision-centric evaluation is narrow: The asymmetric-loss analysis remains at the loss level. A more practical evaluation would simulate accept/reject policies with explicit cost matrices, report expected utility/regret, and examine Pareto frontiers (precision/recall vs cost) under uncertainty, to better connect to real workflows.

Questions to authors
- Linex estimator: For Warped GP predictive distributions over y (non-Gaussian), how was ŷ = µy − (w σy²)/2 derived? Did you assume Gaussian posteriors, perform moment matching, or approximate a* = −(1/w) log E[exp(−w y)] via quadrature? Please clarify the approximation and its impact on results.
- Warping monotonicity: What parameter constraints or reparameterizations did you use to guarantee f′(y) > 0 for all y during optimization? Did you observe any non-monotone warps or negative Jacobians?
- Calibration diagnostics: Did you evaluate coverage of predictive intervals (e.g., 50%, 90%), PIT histograms, or CRPS? If not, could you report these to substantiate the “well-calibrated” claim?
- Robust likelihoods and heteroscedasticity: Did you try Student-t likelihoods or input-dependent noise models? Given the heavy tails and heteroscedasticity of post-editing time, these might change NLPD and linex behavior.
- Baselines tailored to asymmetric loss: How do your Bayes estimators compare to models trained explicitly for AL (quantile regression SVR/GP) at τ = w/(w+1)? Is the “no retraining” benefit worth any performance gap?
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to (i) the number of warping terms, (ii) priors/regularization, (iii) number of restarts, and (iv) kernel choice beyond Matérn 5/2 vs EQ (e.g., rational quadratic)?
- Practical deployment: Could you report decision-oriented metrics (accept/reject utilities) and threshold curves to quantify improvements in realistic pipelines? How would your approach scale beyond a few thousand instances (e.g., sparse variational GPs)?
