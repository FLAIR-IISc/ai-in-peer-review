Summary
The paper argues that Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) should move beyond point predictions to provide calibrated uncertainty, enabling better decisions in real workflows. It instantiates Gaussian Process (GP) models with Matern kernels and Warped GPs, evaluates them using predictive distributions via Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), and demonstrates how posterior information supports asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic/optimistic) via Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses (AL, linex). Experiments on three QE datasets show that warping substantially improves likelihood-based metrics and that NLPD exposes differences obscured by MAE/Pearson’s r.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that predictive-distribution evaluation (NLPD) is more informative than point metrics for QE, leading to better model selection and diagnosing overfitting.
2) Practical instantiation of posterior-based decision-making for asymmetric risk via Bayes estimators on GP posteriors, with evidence it can change optimal choices without retraining.
3) Application-focused analysis of GP design choices (Matern vs EQ kernels, warping vs log) for QE uncertainty, yielding actionable guidance.

Strengths
- Clear, workflow-grounded motivation: The asymmetric decision scenarios (post-editing vs gisting) are realistic and show why uncertainty matters beyond MAE/r.
- Principled probabilistic treatment: Using GPs (including Warped GPs) provides coherent posterior predictions; the paper correctly leverages NLPD as a strictly proper scoring rule to evaluate calibration and sharpness jointly.
- Solid empirical evidence: Across three language pairs and 10-fold CV, warping markedly improves NLL/NLPD; the en–es case shows NLL can mislead on small data, justifying NLPD on held-out data.
- Actionable decision layer: The Bayes estimators for AL/linex illustrate how to adjust to changing risk without retraining, a practically valuable insight for production QE systems.
- Honest analysis of failure modes: The observation that linex with pessimistic weights may favor higher-variance models is insightful and invites further methodological work.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty: Warped GPs and NLPD are well-established; the contribution is largely an application to QE rather than methodological innovation.
- Narrow baselines: No comparison to modern neural QE or uncertainty methods (e.g., Bayesian deep nets, ensembles), limiting claims of general superiority.
- Calibration analysis is thin: Beyond NLPD, the paper lacks diagnostics (PIT, reliability diagrams, coverage) to substantiate “well-calibrated” claims.
- Feature and data limitations: Reliance on 17 QuEst features and older, small datasets may understate challenges and obscure scalability concerns.
- Sparse exploration of GP variants: No heteroscedastic likelihoods or sparse GP approximations; kernel conclusions are tentative.

Questions to Authors
- Can you report calibration diagnostics (e.g., PIT histograms, interval coverage) alongside NLPD?
- How sensitive are results to the number/parameterization of warping terms, and do you use regularization to avoid overfitting?
- Why not compare to quantile regression baselines for AL, or deep ensemble/Bayesian neural QE for uncertainty?
- Would heteroscedastic or input-dependent noise models mitigate the linex pessimistic failure case?
