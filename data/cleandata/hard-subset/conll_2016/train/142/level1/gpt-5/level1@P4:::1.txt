Summary
The paper studies uncertainty-aware modeling for machine translation Quality Estimation (QE). It argues that standard point-estimate metrics (e.g., MAE, RMSE, Pearson’s r) miss crucial information for practical decision-making, and proposes evaluating probabilistic QE models by their full predictive distributions. The work focuses on Gaussian Processes (GPs), including Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better match the positivity and non-Gaussianity of QE targets. It evaluates models using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) alongside point metrics on three post-editing-time datasets, and demonstrates how posterior predictive distributions can be exploited to make decisions under asymmetric risk via Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear and linex losses.

Contributions (as assessed)
1) Empirical evaluation of probabilistic QE with calibrated uncertainty: The paper demonstrates that Warped GPs (with tanh-based and log warpings) and Matérn kernels can yield better-calibrated predictive distributions (lower NLPD) than standard GPs, and that these differences are not captured by MAE or Pearson’s r.

2) Application of asymmetric-risk decision-making in QE: The paper shows how to transform a single probabilistic model’s predictions into risk-sensitive decisions via Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses (AL and linex), avoiding retraining when business needs change; it reports that warped models generally help under AL in both optimistic and pessimistic settings.

3) Practical insights on evaluation: It highlights that training likelihood (NLL) can be misleading with small data (overfitting), advocating test-time NLPD as the preferred metric for uncertainty-aware QE.

Strengths
1) Clear, practically motivated problem framing: The need for uncertainty in QE decisions (e.g., post-editing filtering, gisting) is well articulated. The asymmetric-risk scenarios are realistic and demonstrate how uncertainty informs decision thresholds beyond symmetric losses.

2) Technically sound methodology grounded in established probabilistic modeling: The GP/Warpped GP formulations, kernel choices (EQ vs Matérn), and Bayesian decision-theory derivations for asymmetric losses are standard and correctly applied. The use of ARD and multi-start optimization is appropriate for small data.

3) Uncertainty-aware evaluation that goes beyond point metrics: The use of NLPD provides evidence that models with similar MAE/r can differ materially in calibration. The finding that Warped GPs substantially reduce NLPD (by avoiding mass on negative outcomes) is compelling and aligns with the positivity of post-editing rates.

4) Practical decision utility via Bayes estimators without retraining: The demonstration that one can switch between pessimistic/optimistic settings by changing only the estimator (quantiles for AL; closed-form for linex under Gaussian assumptions) is valuable for deployment where risk preferences change.

5) Multi-dataset evaluation with consistent protocol: The study covers three language pairs, uses 10-fold cross-validation, and keeps features constant across datasets to isolate modeling effects on uncertainty.

Weaknesses
1) Limited novelty: GPs (including Warped GPs) have a history in QE; NLPD is a standard proper scoring rule in GP evaluation. The main novelty lies in applying these tools systematically to QE uncertainty and in the asymmetric-loss demonstration; methodological advances are minimal.

2) Narrow baselines and missing comparisons: The work compares mostly against standard GPs. It omits other uncertainty-aware baselines such as quantile regression (e.g., QR-SVR/GBM), Bayesian linear models, bootstrapped ensembles, Student-t likelihood GPs, or recent Bayesian neural approaches. Without such baselines, it is hard to assess the relative merit of Warped GPs for QE.

3) Calibration analysis is incomplete: While NLPD is reported, there are no calibration diagnostics (e.g., PIT histograms, coverage of predictive intervals, calibration curves, CRPS). The observed issues with linex in the pessimistic setting are discussed qualitatively; quantitative calibration analyses would substantiate claims about over/under-confidence.

4) Statistical rigor and reporting: The paper does not present statistical significance tests or confidence intervals for differences in NLPD/MAE/r or in asymmetric-loss evaluations. Given the small datasets, reported improvements (e.g., between tanh warps and log) may be within variance.

5) Methodological clarity for asymmetric estimators under warping: The AL estimator requires posterior quantiles and the linex estimator’s closed form assumes Gaussian predictive distributions. Under Warped GPs, the y-space posterior is non-Gaussian. The paper mentions numerical approximations but does not detail how quantiles or linex expectations are computed in y-space, or the accuracy/cost of these approximations.

Questions to Authors (to clarify during rebuttal)
- How exactly are AL quantiles computed under Warped GPs: in y-space via numerical inversion of the warping (and if so, with what solver/tolerances), or via approximations in latent space? Please specify computational cost and convergence behavior.

- For the linex estimator under Warped GPs, do you approximate µy and σ2y in y-space numerically (e.g., Gauss-Hermite after change of variables)? If the closed form is derived for Gaussian y, how do you justify applying it to non-Gaussian y posteriors?

- Can you provide calibration diagnostics (e.g., coverage of nominal predictive intervals, PIT histograms) to complement NLPD and support claims about well-calibrated uncertainty?

- How do the proposed models compare against quantile regression baselines (e.g., SVR/GBM quantiles) and a Student-t likelihood GP? These seem particularly relevant for asymmetric losses and heavy-tailed noise in post-editing time.

- Are differences in NLPD/AL/linex statistically significant across folds? Please report variability (e.g., mean±std) and significance tests.

- Do you observe heteroscedasticity in residuals? If so, did you consider heteroscedastic GPs, which might better capture input-dependent noise and improve calibration?

Overall, the paper is clear and technically correct, offering a useful uncertainty-centric perspective on QE with practical decision implications, though its novelty and comparative evaluation are limited.
