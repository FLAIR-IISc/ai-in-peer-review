Summary
The paper argues that point-estimate evaluation in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) limits usefulness in real workflows and instead emphasizes uncertainty-aware modeling and evaluation. It revisits Gaussian Processes (GPs) for QE, advocates Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better match the target variable support and smoothness, and evaluates models using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) in addition to standard metrics. It further demonstrates how posterior predictive distributions enable asymmetric risk decision-making (e.g., pessimistic vs optimistic workflows) via Bayes estimators under asymmetric losses, without retraining the model. Experiments on three sentence-level post-editing time datasets with basic QuEst features show Warped GPs reduce NLL/NLPD and can outperform standard GPs under asymmetric losses in some settings.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Introducing uncertainty-aware evaluation for QE via NLPD and analysis of calibrated predictive distributions, showing that point metrics (MAE, r) can miss substantial differences in predictive uncertainty.
2) Adapting and empirically validating GP variants (Warped GPs with learned monotonic tanh warpings, Matérn kernels with ARD) to QE to improve predictive density fit and better handle positive-valued labels.
3) Demonstrating asymmetric risk decision-making for QE by using Bayes risk estimators derived from predictive posteriors (quantiles for asymmetric linear loss; closed-form estimator for linex loss), avoiding retraining under changing costs.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and practical relevance: The paper convincingly argues why uncertainty matters for QE in production (post-editing filtering, gisting) and why point metrics are insufficient. The asymmetric-loss discussion is well-grounded in realistic workflows and the Bayes estimator approach is a pragmatic, low-cost way to adapt to changing business constraints.
- Sound probabilistic modeling choices: Warped GPs are a principled way to restrict support to positive labels and to learn appropriate transformations; Matérn kernels relax the overly smooth EQ assumptions common in QE. The paper reports large NLL/NLPD gains from warping, which aligns with the support mismatch argument and indicates improved calibration of predictive densities.
- Uncertainty-centric evaluation: Using both NLL and out-of-sample NLPD (Quinonero-Candela et al., 2006) is appropriate; the finding that NLL can mislead on small data (overfitting) while NLPD is more reliable is valuable guidance for QE researchers. The observation that MAE/r largely fails to reflect uncertainty differences is important for future evaluation protocols.
- Decision layer decoupling via Bayes estimators: The use of posterior quantiles (AL) and the linex estimator to tailor predictions to asymmetric costs without retraining is a strong, generalizable idea. The optimistic scenario results consistently favor warped models, illustrating end-to-end benefits of better-calibrated posteriors beyond likelihood metrics.
- Clarity and reproducibility of setup: The datasets, features (17 QuEst), and cross-validation protocol are clearly described, and the GP training procedure (two-pass isotropic→ARD with random restarts) is sufficiently specified to allow reproduction in principle.

Weaknesses
- Limited baselines and scope: The comparisons exclude strong non-GP baselines capable of uncertainty estimation or asymmetry, such as quantile regression (e.g., linear/SVR quantile regression), Bayesian linear/kernelized models, heteroscedastic GPs, Student-t likelihood GPs, and bootstrap ensembles. This narrows claims about the superiority of the proposed models and limits external validity.
- Calibration analysis is too thin: NLPD is informative but does not diagnose calibration and sharpness separately. The paper lacks standard calibration diagnostics (e.g., probability integral transform histograms, coverage of posterior intervals, CRPS) that would substantiate “well-calibrated” claims and further explain the linex loss behavior in the pessimistic setting.
- Asymmetric risk evaluation is partial and sometimes inconclusive: The pessimistic linex results are counterintuitive and attributed to variance differences, but no deeper analysis (e.g., sensitivity to w, variance decomposition, coverage on the right tail) is provided. Choice of w values appears ad hoc; there’s no task-driven mapping from workflow costs to loss parameters, and no utility-based or downstream accept/reject evaluation.
- Small features and dated QE setup: Restricting to 17 QuEst features simplifies the study but may underrepresent modern QE systems. It is unclear whether conclusions hold with stronger representations (e.g., source–MT joint features, neural embeddings), and whether the benefits generalize to other QE target variables (e.g., HTER, DA scores) or to document-level QE.
- Statistical rigor and reporting: The paper refers to Tables 1–2 but does not indicate statistical significance testing across folds, nor are effect sizes and variability reported. The kernel comparison conclusions (Matérn vs EQ) are weak and dataset-dependent; more systematic model selection (including ν) and ablations would strengthen claims.
- Missing details that affect reproducibility and interpretation: No discussion of hyperparameter priors/constraints, regularization for the warping parameters (risk of overfitting with multiple tanh terms), noise modeling (homoscedastic vs heteroscedastic), optimization stability, and computational costs. These are important for practical deployment in QE pipelines.

Questions to authors
- Calibration: Did you assess calibration explicitly (e.g., posterior interval coverage, PIT, CRPS)? Can you provide evidence that warped GPs are better calibrated, not just higher likelihood?
- Loss parameterization: How should a user set w in AL/linex from operational costs? Can you map realistic QE workflows (e.g., post-edit cost vs rejection cost) to loss parameters and show sensitivity curves?
- Linex pessimistic behavior: Can you quantify predictive variance and tail coverage differences across models to validate your explanation? Would a heavier-tailed likelihood or heteroscedastic GP mitigate the issue?
- Baselines: Did you try quantile regression (linear/SVR) for AL scenarios and compare against your AL Bayes-risk solution? What about Student-t GPs or heteroscedastic GPs?
- Generality: Do results replicate for other QE labels (HTER, DA) and with richer features/neural encoders? Any experiments at word-level or document-level QE?

Additional comments
- Consider reporting CRPS alongside NLPD for a single-score assessment that rewards both calibration and sharpness and is robust to support mismatch.
- Please include runtime and memory costs; even small datasets can be latency-sensitive in online QE.
- Clarify whether point predictions used for MAE/r are medians (as stated); for r, the choice of median vs mean may matter under non-Gaussian posteriors.
