**SUMMARY AND CONTRIBUTIONS**

This paper addresses the problem of uncertainty quantification in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that conventional QE models, which provide only point estimates, are insufficient for real-world decision-making scenarios like post-editing workflows. The paper advocates for the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), to provide well-calibrated predictive distributions. The authors explore extensions to standard GPs, including Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, to better model the noisy and non-Gaussian nature of QE data. A key part of the work is the proposal to evaluate QE models not just on point-estimate metrics like Mean Absolute Error (MAE), but on the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), a proper scoring rule that assesses the entire predictive distribution. Finally, the practical utility of these uncertainty estimates is demonstrated in an asymmetric risk setting, showing how a single probabilistic model can be adapted to different decision-making scenarios without retraining.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A compelling argument and empirical validation for shifting QE evaluation from point estimates to probabilistic assessment.** The paper's primary strength is its clear demonstration that standard metrics like MAE are insensitive to significant improvements in model fit and uncertainty calibration. By introducing and justifying the use of NLPD, the authors provide a principled path forward for evaluating and comparing probabilistic QE models.
2.  **A practical framework for applying QE in asymmetric risk scenarios.** The application of Bayes risk estimators to the output of a probabilistic QE model is a novel and valuable contribution to the field. It elegantly solves the real-world problem of needing different decision thresholds (e.g., optimistic vs. pessimistic) without the costly process of retraining models for each scenario.
3.  **A thorough empirical analysis of different GP configurations for QE.** The paper provides a useful comparison of various kernels and likelihood assumptions for QE, offering concrete guidance for practitioners interested in using these models.

**STRENGTHS**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper tackles a highly relevant but often-neglected aspect of QE. The introduction clearly articulates why point estimates are insufficient for practical applications and how uncertainty can enable more robust decision-making. This reframing of QE as a probabilistic prediction problem is a timely and important contribution.
2.  **Sound and Rigorous Methodology:** The choice of methods is well-justified. GPs are a natural fit for this problem, and the exploration of Matérn kernels and Warped GPs shows a deep understanding of the modeling challenges. The evaluation is particularly strong; using NLPD provides an appropriate intrinsic measure, while the asymmetric risk evaluation provides a convincing extrinsic, task-based validation of the approach's utility.
3.  **Clear and Insightful Experimental Results:** The results in Table 1 provide powerful evidence that standard metrics like MAE and Pearson's r are misleading, as they fail to capture the substantial gains in distributional fit (shown by NLPD) achieved by the Warped GP models. This is a significant finding for the QE community. Furthermore, the analysis of the asymmetric risk scenarios is insightful, particularly the discussion of how different models behave under different loss functions.
4.  **High-Quality Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The concepts are introduced clearly, the motivation is sustained throughout, and the experimental sections are logically structured to build a coherent and persuasive argument.

**WEAKNESSES**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core techniques—GPs, Warped GPs, NLPD, and Bayes risk estimation—are all established methods from the machine learning and statistics literature. The paper's contribution lies in the thoughtful application and synthesis of these methods to the QE domain. While this is a valuable contribution, the authors should be more explicit that the work is primarily an application and evaluation study, not the proposal of a new technique.
2.  **Use of an Outdated Feature-Based QE Paradigm:** The experiments are conducted using a set of 17 hand-crafted features. While sufficient for comparing the probabilistic models, this approach is becoming less common in a field rapidly moving towards end-to-end neural QE models. The paper would be significantly stronger and more forward-looking if it included a discussion of how these uncertainty principles could be applied to modern neural architectures (e.g., via Monte Carlo Dropout, Bayesian Neural Networks, or Deep GPs). Without this, the findings might be perceived as being relevant only to a receding paradigm of QE.
3.  **Lack of Simpler Uncertainty Baselines:** The paper effectively compares different variants of GPs but lacks a comparison to simpler, non-GP methods for uncertainty estimation. For example, an ensemble of standard SVR models (via bagging) can also produce a predictive distribution whose quality could be assessed with NLPD. While the paper dismisses bootstrapping in Section 2 as unprincipled, an empirical comparison would have been more convincing and would have strengthened the case for using the more complex GP framework.
4.  **Superficial Analysis of a Key Negative Result:** The finding that Warped GPs perform poorly under the pessimistic linex loss is fascinating and counterintuitive. The authors briefly speculate that this is due to the model's lower (and likely more realistic) predictive variance. This point warrants a deeper investigation. It raises important questions about the suitability of the linex loss itself for evaluating models with different underlying assumptions. The current discussion feels rushed and misses an opportunity for a more profound analysis of the interplay between model calibration and loss function choice.

**QUESTIONS TO AUTHORS**

1.  Your experiments are based on a feature-rich regression setup. How do you see these methods and evaluation principles transferring to modern, end-to-end neural QE architectures? Do you believe techniques like Monte Carlo Dropout could offer a scalable and effective alternative to GPs for uncertainty estimation in that context?
2.  Regarding the surprising result for the pessimistic linex loss, is it possible that the standard GP, which poorly models the data (e.g., by placing mass on negative post-editing times), produces an artificially high variance that "games" this specific loss function? In other words, its poor calibration coincidentally pushes the Bayes risk estimator in the correct direction for this loss. Could this suggest that linex loss is not a robust choice for comparing models with fundamentally different likelihood assumptions?
3.  Could you elaborate on the decision to omit an empirical comparison with a bootstrapping or ensemble-based baseline for uncertainty (e.g., bagging SVRs)? A direct comparison on the NLPD metric would have been very informative for practitioners weighing the trade-offs between different approaches to uncertainty quantification.
