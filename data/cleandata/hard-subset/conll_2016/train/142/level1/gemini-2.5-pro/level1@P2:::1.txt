**Summary of the paper**

This paper argues for a shift in how Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) is approached, moving from standard regression models that provide point estimates to probabilistic models that yield a full predictive distribution. The authors contend that uncertainty information is crucial for real-world applications, such as post-editing workflows. They propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs), exploring variants like Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs that are better suited to the noisy and strictly positive nature of QE data (e.g., post-editing time). The core of the work is an evaluation that goes beyond standard metrics like MAE and Pearson's r, instead using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to assess the quality of the uncertainty estimates. Finally, the paper demonstrates a practical use-case for these probabilistic predictions in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-estimation of quality carry different costs, showing how optimal estimators can be derived from a single model without retraining.

**Contributions**

1.  **Framing QE as a probabilistic prediction problem:** The primary contribution is the compelling argument and empirical demonstration that QE systems should provide and be evaluated on their predictive uncertainty. The introduction of NLPD as a more informative evaluation metric for QE is a significant step forward, highlighting the shortcomings of traditional point-estimate metrics in distinguishing between models with different confidence levels.
2.  **Application and validation of advanced GP models for QE:** The paper successfully introduces and validates the use of Warped Gaussian Processes for QE. It shows that these models, which learn a non-linear transformation of the target variable, provide a much better probabilistic fit for quality scores like post-editing time compared to standard GPs, as evidenced by large improvements in NLPD.
3.  **A practical framework for risk-aware decision making:** The work presents a concrete and useful application of uncertainty in QE through the lens of asymmetric risk. By using Bayes risk estimators, the authors show how a single probabilistic model can be flexibly adapted to various downstream decision-making scenarios (e.g., optimistic vs. pessimistic) without the need for costly retraining, making QE more adaptable to real-world business needs.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong motivation and conceptual clarity:** The paper addresses a critical and practical limitation of most current QE research. The argument that point estimates are insufficient for real-world decision-making is well-founded and clearly articulated. By focusing on uncertainty, the work advocates for building more reliable and useful NLP systems. The paper is exceptionally well-written, making complex topics like Warped GPs and Bayes risk accessible.
2.  **Rigorous and insightful evaluation:** A major strength is the principled evaluation methodology. Using NLPD to directly measure the quality of the predictive distribution is the right approach for the authors' claims. The comparison in Table 1, which shows that large gains in NLPD are not reflected in MAE or Pearson's r, is a powerful piece of evidence supporting the paper's main thesis. This highlights an important gap in standard evaluation practices.
3.  **Novel and impactful application scenario:** The exploration of asymmetric risk is an excellent demonstration of the practical value of uncertainty modeling. It connects the abstract concept of a predictive distribution to a concrete task: deciding whether to accept a translation when the cost of a mistake is not symmetric. This is a realistic scenario in translation workflows and provides a strong justification for adopting the proposed methods.
4.  **Reproducibility and sound experimental design:** The experiments are conducted on publicly available datasets using a standard feature set, and the methodology (10-fold cross-validation, hyperparameter optimization) is sound. This provides a solid and reproducible foundation for the paper's conclusions.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited methodological novelty:** The core methods—Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs, and NLPD—are established techniques from the machine learning literature. The contribution lies in their novel application and framing within the QE context, rather than in the development of new algorithms. While this is a valid and valuable type of contribution, the work could be strengthened by a clearer positioning against existing NLP literature that may have used similar probabilistic techniques.
2.  **Use of a baseline feature set:** The experiments rely on a small, standard set of 17 features. While the authors justify this as a means to focus on the models themselves, it leaves an important question unanswered: do the benefits of sophisticated uncertainty modeling persist when combined with the large, powerful feature sets used in state-of-the-art QE systems? It is possible that with much stronger predictive signals, the differences between the probabilistic models might become less pronounced.
3.  **Inconclusive results and explanation for the Linex loss:** The analysis in the pessimistic scenario using the Linex loss (Section 4.3) is puzzling. The finding that the standard GP—a model the paper argues is misspecified—outperforms the better-calibrated Warped GPs is counterintuitive. The authors’ explanation, that the loss function is biased towards models with higher variance even if that variance is nonsensical (i.e., covering negative time), is not fully convincing and points to a potential weakness in the chosen loss function or estimator for this scenario. This specific result undermines the otherwise consistent narrative of the paper and warrants a more thorough investigation.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the counterintuitive results for the Linex loss in the pessimistic scenario? Does this suggest that the Linex Bayes risk estimator is inherently flawed for bounded, non-Gaussian distributions like those from a Warped GP, or is there a deeper interaction at play? Would an alternative asymmetric loss, like the asymmetric quadratic loss, be more appropriate?
2.  While your goal was to compare models, the use of a simple feature set is a limitation. Do you have any intuition on how these results would scale to a modern, high-dimensional feature setting? For instance, would you expect the relative NLPD gains of Warped GPs over standard GPs to increase or decrease as the predictive power of the features grows?
3.  You optimize point predictions based on the median of the posterior, which is optimal for MAE. Have you experimented with using the mean of the posterior, which is optimal for RMSE? It would be interesting to see if the probabilistically superior Warped GP models also yield better point predictions for MAE/RMSE when the optimal estimator for the corresponding loss function is used.
