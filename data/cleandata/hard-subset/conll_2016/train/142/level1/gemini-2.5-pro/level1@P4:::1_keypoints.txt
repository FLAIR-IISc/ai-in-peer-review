- Tackles QE and argues probabilistic models with full predictive distributions and uncertainty are more useful than point estimates.  
- Proposes Gaussian Process variants (Warped GPs, Matèrn kernels) as a probabilistic QE framework.  
- Evaluates models intrinsically with NLPD and practically in asymmetric-risk decision scenarios.  
- Main contribution: argues for evaluating QE using full predictive distributions and NLPD rather than point-estimate metrics.  
- Main contribution: systematically applies and compares advanced GP models (Warped GPs, Matèrn) for QE.  
- Main contribution: shows how posterior distributions enable Bayes-risk estimators for different asymmetric cost scenarios without retraining.  
- Strength: strong motivation and clear writing linking uncertainty quantification to real translation workflows.  
- Strength: methodologically sound choice of GPs for small datasets and rigorous experimental design.  
- Strength: experiments show NLPD reveals distributional gains (especially for Warped GPs) not captured by MAE.  
- Strength: practical relevance — demonstrates tangible decision-making benefits under asymmetric risk and potential field impact.  
- Weakness: limited novelty — core methods are established and the contribution is mainly their application to QE.  
- Weakness: inconclusive results — Matèrn benefits are unclear and Warped GPs perform worse under pessimistic linex loss without thorough analysis.  
- Weakness: limited scope — experiments use 17 handcrafted features and small datasets, limiting generalization to modern high-dimensional QE.  
- Question: could the Warped GP’s poor performance under pessimistic linex loss be due to linex favoring higher variance models; have other asymmetric losses been tried?  
- Question: why did Matèrn kernels not show clear advantage — is the feature representation sufficiently smooth or mismatched to Matèrn assumptions?  
- Question: how will these GP methods scale to high-dimensional features (n-grams, neural) and is GP hyperparameter optimization (with ARD) computationally feasible?
