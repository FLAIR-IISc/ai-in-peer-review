**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the limitation of standard Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) models, which typically provide only point estimates of quality. The authors argue that for real-world decision-making, such as in post-editing workflows, uncertainty estimates are crucial. The paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), and evaluates extensions like Matérn kernels and Warped GPs. Crucially, it advocates for evaluating these models using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), which considers the entire predictive distribution, rather than traditional metrics like MAE. The utility of this approach is then demonstrated in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-estimation of quality carries different penalties.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A practical framework for applying and evaluating uncertainty in asymmetric risk scenarios for QE.** This is the strongest contribution, as it connects the theoretical benefits of probabilistic modeling to a concrete, important application in translation workflows and provides a method (Bayes risk estimators) to adapt to different risk profiles without retraining.
2.  **A strong empirical case for using NLPD as an evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models.** The experiments clearly show that traditional point-estimate metrics fail to capture significant improvements in uncertainty modeling, making NLPD a more appropriate choice for this line of research.

**Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Framing:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated by framing QE as a decision-making tool. The introduction of asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic/optimistic) is a novel and highly relevant perspective for the QE field, moving the discussion beyond simple prediction accuracy to practical utility.
2.  **Rigorous and Appropriate Evaluation:** The use of NLPD is a key strength that directly supports the paper's core thesis. By showing that metrics like MAE and Pearson's r are insensitive to the quality of uncertainty estimates (Table 1), the authors make a compelling case for its adoption. The experimental setup across multiple datasets is methodologically sound.
3.  **Clear Practical Implications:** The demonstration in Section 4, showing how a single probabilistic model's posterior can be used to derive optimal estimators for various asymmetric loss functions without retraining, is a powerful argument for the flexibility and cost-effectiveness of this approach in real-world production environments.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core machine learning models—Gaussian Processes, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs—are well-established techniques. The paper's contribution is primarily in their thoughtful application and evaluation in the QE context, rather than the development of new algorithms.
2.  **Inconclusive Results for Some Extensions:** The reported benefits of using Matérn kernels are marginal and inconsistent across datasets. Furthermore, the Warped GP models performed worse than the baseline under the pessimistic linex loss scenario, a counter-intuitive result that the authors acknowledge but do not fully resolve, slightly weakening the paper's overall claims.
3.  **Use of a Simple Feature Set:** The experiments rely on a standard set of 17 hand-crafted features. While this choice is justified for controlled model comparison, it leaves open the question of whether the findings generalize to state-of-the-art QE systems that leverage powerful representations from large pre-trained language models.
