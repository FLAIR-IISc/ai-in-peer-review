### Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the task of Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that conventional QE models, which provide single point estimates of quality, are of limited use in real-world decision-making scenarios. They propose that probabilistic models, which provide a full predictive distribution including uncertainty estimates, are more valuable. The paper investigates several variants of Gaussian Process (GP) regression, including models with Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs, as a framework for probabilistic QE. The evaluation is conducted on two fronts: first, an intrinsic evaluation using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to assess the quality of the predictive distributions, and second, an application-focused evaluation in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- and under-estimation of quality have different costs.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A methodological argument and demonstration for evaluating QE models based on their full predictive distributions.** The paper makes a compelling case for moving beyond point-estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson's r, advocating for NLPD as a more informative metric when uncertainty is a key consideration. This challenges the standard evaluation paradigm in the field.
2.  **A systematic application and evaluation of advanced GP models for the QE task.** The work explores specific extensions to the standard GP framework—namely Warped GPs to handle non-Gaussian, positive-only target variables and Matèrn kernels to relax smoothness assumptions for noisy data—and demonstrates their advantages over a standard GP baseline using the proposed probabilistic evaluation metric.
3.  **A novel application demonstrating the practical utility of predictive uncertainty in QE.** The paper frames QE as a decision problem under asymmetric risk and shows how Bayes risk estimators can leverage the posterior distributions from probabilistic models to optimize for different cost scenarios (e.g., pessimistic vs. optimistic) without model retraining.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong Motivation and Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. It clearly articulates the limitations of point estimates in practical translation workflows and presents a convincing argument for the importance of uncertainty quantification. The writing is clear, and the concepts (GPs, NLPD, asymmetric loss) are explained concisely and effectively, making the work accessible.
2.  **Technically Sound and Rigorous Methodology:** The choice of Gaussian Processes as the probabilistic framework is well-suited for the task, especially given the typically small datasets in QE. The exploration of specific, well-justified model variants like Warped GPs and Matèrn kernels is methodologically sound. The experimental design is rigorous, employing multiple datasets, 10-fold cross-validation, and appropriate hyperparameter optimization procedures.
3.  **Insightful Experimental Analysis:** The empirical results strongly support the paper's central claims. The intrinsic evaluation (Table 1) effectively demonstrates that while point-estimate metrics like MAE show some improvement, they fail to capture the substantial gains in predictive distribution quality revealed by NLPD, particularly for the Warped GP models. This provides clear evidence for the paper's argument about evaluation metrics.
4.  **Practical Relevance and Potential Impact:** The work connects theoretical modeling to a realistic application through the asymmetric risk scenarios. This demonstration of how to derive optimal estimators for different loss functions from a single probabilistic model is a significant strength, showcasing a tangible benefit of the proposed approach for practitioners. By shifting the focus from prediction accuracy to calibrated uncertainty, this work has the potential to influence future research and development in QE, pushing the field towards creating more practical and reliable tools.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Novelty of Core Methods is Limited:** The primary technical components of the paper—Gaussian Processes, Warped GPs, Matèrn kernels, and the NLPD metric—are established methods from the machine learning literature. The contribution lies in their novel application and combination within the QE domain. While this is a valid and useful contribution, the paper does not propose new modeling techniques per se.
2.  **Inconclusive and Under-analyzed Results for Some Components:** The results presented do not uniformly support all of the proposed modeling choices.
    *   The benefits of using Matèrn kernels over the standard Exponentiated Quadratic (EQ) kernel are described as "less conclusive." Given the motivation that QE data is noisy, this finding is somewhat surprising and is not deeply analyzed, weakening this part of the technical contribution.
    *   More significantly, in the asymmetric risk evaluation (Table 2), the proposed Warped GP models perform substantially *worse* than the standard GP baseline in the pessimistic linex loss scenario. The authors offer a brief hypothesis but leave a full investigation to future work. This is a confusing result that contradicts the overall narrative that the proposed models are superior and suggests a potential flaw or limitation in the approach that is not fully addressed.
3.  **Limited Scope of Features and Datasets:** The experiments rely on a small, standard set of 17 handcrafted features. It is unclear how the findings would generalize to modern QE systems that use high-dimensional, sparse, or neural features. While the authors justify this as a means to focus on model comparison, it limits the immediate applicability of the conclusions to the state-of-the-art in QE. Furthermore, while three datasets are used, one (en-es) is noted as being considerably smaller, leading to several instances of overfitting and different behavior compared to the other two, which complicates the interpretation of the results.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The poor performance of the Warped GP models under the pessimistic linex loss is a critical finding that appears to contradict your main claims. Your hypothesis is that the estimator is sensitive to the lower variance of the (better-calibrated) warped models. Could it be that the linex loss with its corresponding Bayes risk estimator is an inappropriate choice for this scenario, as it seems to systematically favor models with higher, potentially unrealistic, variance? Have you considered evaluating with other asymmetric loss functions, such as the asymmetric quadratic loss, to see if this counter-intuitive result is specific to the linex formulation?
2.  Given that Matèrn kernels did not show a clear advantage, could you elaborate on why this might be the case? Is it possible that the 17-feature representation is already smooth enough that the kernel's smoothness assumption is not a critical factor, or that the noise characteristics of these specific datasets do not align with the properties of the Matèrn class?
3.  How do you foresee your proposed methods scaling to settings with much larger feature sets (e.g., thousands of features from n-grams or neural models)? Would the computational complexity of GP hyperparameter optimization (especially with ARD) become prohibitive, and would the benefits of these kernel-based methods persist in a high-dimensional regime?
