### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper argues that for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) to be useful in real-world applications, models must provide not just point estimates of quality, but also well-calibrated measures of uncertainty. The authors investigate Gaussian Processes (GPs), a probabilistic regression framework, and explore extensions like Matérn kernels and Warped GPs to better model the data distribution. A key argument is that standard QE evaluation metrics like Mean Absolute Error (MAE) are insufficient, and the community should adopt metrics like Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) which evaluate the entire predictive distribution. Finally, the paper demonstrates the practical value of this approach by showing how the predictive distributions from trained GP models can be used with Bayes risk estimators to make optimal decisions in scenarios with asymmetric costs (e.g., where underestimating post-editing effort is more costly than overestimating it).

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A strong, well-supported argument for a paradigm shift in QE evaluation.** The paper convincingly demonstrates that standard point-estimate metrics fail to distinguish between models with very different predictive uncertainty profiles. By showing that model improvements (like using Warped GPs) are clearly reflected in NLPD but not in MAE or Pearson's r, the authors make a compelling case for the QE community to adopt probabilistic evaluation metrics.
2.  **A practical and elegant framework for applying QE in realistic decision-making scenarios.** The framing of QE as a problem of decision-making under asymmetric risk is highly insightful. The proposed method—training a single probabilistic model and then applying different Bayes risk estimators post-hoc to adapt to various loss functions—is far more practical than retraining models for each specific scenario. This contribution bridges the gap between QE research and its application.
3.  **A solid empirical investigation of advanced GP models for the QE task.** While the models themselves are not new to the machine learning community, their application and systematic evaluation for QE is novel. The paper provides strong evidence that Warped GPs, which learn a non-linear mapping of the output space, are particularly well-suited for QE, as they correctly model the non-negative nature of common quality labels like post-editing time.

### **Strengths**

1.  **High Relevance and Potential Impact.** The paper addresses a critical and often-overlooked limitation of standard QE: the reliance on single point estimates. The motivation is clear and directly tied to making QE more useful in practical translation workflows (e.g., deciding whether to post-edit or translate from scratch). This focus on utility and decision-making makes the work highly relevant and its recommendations could significantly improve how QE systems are designed and deployed.
2.  **Methodologically Sound and Rigorous Evaluation.** The choice of models is well-justified, and the experimental design is robust, using multiple datasets and cross-validation. The core strength is the dual evaluation using both traditional metrics and the probabilistically-grounded NLPD. The results in Table 1 provide clear and powerful evidence for the paper's central thesis: that significant improvements in model calibration and uncertainty estimation are invisible to metrics like MAE.
3.  **Novel and Insightful Application to Asymmetric Risk.** Section 4 is a standout part of the paper. It moves beyond just *measuring* uncertainty to demonstrating how to *use* it effectively. This is a crucial step towards operationalizing QE. The analysis is excellent, particularly the nuanced discussion of the differing results between the Asymmetric Linear (AL) and linex losses. The authors' hypothesis about why the standard GP performs better on the pessimistic linex loss (due to its higher variance pushing the estimator to be more conservative) is insightful and demonstrates a deep understanding of the models and their interaction with the evaluation framework.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Scope of Models and Features.** The paper's empirical evaluation is focused exclusively on Gaussian Processes. While this is a strong and appropriate choice, the general message about uncertainty would be stronger if it acknowledged or briefly discussed how these ideas could apply to other popular models in NLP, such as neural networks (e.g., via MC dropout or Bayesian NNs). Furthermore, the experiments rely on a standard but small set of 17 baseline features. It is unclear if the specific findings (e.g., the superiority of Warped GPs, the mixed results of Matérn kernels) would hold for state-of-the-art QE systems that use much larger and more complex feature sets.
2.  **Inconclusive Results for a Key Proposed Method.** The paper motivates the use of Matérn kernels as a way to relax the potentially overly strong smoothness assumptions of the standard EQ kernel, suggesting they would be a better fit for noisy QE data. However, the empirical results are, in the authors' own words, "less conclusive" and show inconsistent, marginal gains. This weakens one of the paper's specific modeling contributions, suggesting that for this task and feature space, the choice of kernel is less critical than the choice of likelihood/warping function.
3.  **The Asymmetric Risk Scenarios Lack a Grounding in Real-World Costs.** While the asymmetric risk framework is a major strength, the implementation in the paper is a proof-of-concept. The weights for the loss functions (`w` = 3, 1/3, etc.) are chosen for illustrative purposes. For this method to be truly deployed, a project manager would need a principled way to set these weights based on concrete business costs (e.g., dollars per hour of translator time, opportunity cost of delaying publication). The paper could be strengthened by acknowledging this gap between the proposed framework and its operational deployment, perhaps suggesting how these cost parameters could be elicited or learned.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Your finding that the standard (and arguably mis-specified) GP outperforms the better-calibrated Warped GPs under the pessimistic linex loss is fascinating. You hypothesize this is due to the standard GP's higher variance. Does this suggest that the linex loss, with its strong dependence on variance, may be an inappropriate choice for this kind of task, as it appears to reward a model that is poorly calibrated (i.e., one that doesn't respect the physical constraint of non-negative time)?
2.  You make a compelling case for adopting NLPD as a primary evaluation metric for QE. What do you see as the main practical barriers to its adoption in shared tasks like WMT? For example, would it require all participating systems to be probabilistic and to output full predictive distributions, and would this place an undue burden on teams using popular non-probabilistic methods like SVR?
