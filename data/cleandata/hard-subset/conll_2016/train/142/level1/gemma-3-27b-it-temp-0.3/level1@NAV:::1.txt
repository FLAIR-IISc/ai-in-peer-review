## Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic methods, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors argue that traditional point-estimate evaluation metrics are insufficient for real-world QE applications where decision-making relies on both predicted quality *and* confidence. They empirically evaluate different GP configurations and demonstrate the utility of the resulting predictive distributions in an asymmetric risk scenario, simulating post-editing workflows.

The primary contribution of this work is the thorough empirical evaluation of different GP configurations (kernels, warping functions) for QE, *specifically* through the lens of predictive uncertainty as measured by Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD). While GPs have been previously applied to QE, this work highlights the importance of evaluating beyond point estimates and demonstrates that differences in uncertainty modeling are not captured by standard metrics like MAE or Pearson’s r. A secondary contribution lies in the demonstration of how these predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios, offering a more nuanced approach to decision-making in translation workflows.

**Strengths:**

1. **Focus on Uncertainty:** The paper correctly identifies a critical gap in QE research – the lack of attention to predictive uncertainty. This is a significant and timely contribution, as real-world applications demand more than just point estimates.
2. **Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation of different GP configurations across multiple language pairs, providing a solid empirical basis for their claims. The use of NLPD as a key evaluation metric is particularly commendable.
3. **Practical Application:** The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios provides a concrete example of how uncertainty estimates can be practically applied to improve decision-making in translation workflows.
4. **Well-Written and Comprehensive:** The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and provides a thorough overview of relevant background material.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methodology:** The core methodology (using GPs for QE) is not novel. The primary novelty lies in the *evaluation* framework, rather than a new modeling approach.
2. **Overly Detailed Technical Descriptions:** While thoroughness is appreciated, some sections (e.g., detailed descriptions of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) could be more concise, potentially sacrificing readability for a broader audience.
3. **Inconclusive Results for Linex Loss:** The results regarding the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive and require further investigation, as acknowledged by the authors. This weakens the argument for the general applicability of the proposed approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the different GP configurations, particularly the warped GPs with tanh functions? This would help assess the practical feasibility of these models for large-scale applications.
2.  You mention that the choice of features was kept consistent across language pairs to facilitate comparison. However, could different feature sets potentially amplify the benefits of uncertainty modeling?
3.  The paper focuses on post-editing scenarios. Are there other translation workflows where the benefits of uncertainty-aware QE would be similarly pronounced?
