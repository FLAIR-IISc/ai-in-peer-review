

This paper investigates the importance of prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture uncertainty information critical for real-world decision-making. The authors propose using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a more comprehensive evaluation metric that assesses the full predictive distribution, and demonstrate how probabilistic models (specifically Gaussian Processes with Matérn kernels and Warped GPs) can provide better uncertainty estimates.

Primary contributions: (1) The introduction of NLPD as an evaluation metric that captures uncertainty information, which traditional point estimate metrics fail to do; (2) The demonstration that Warped GPs with parametric warping functions (tanh-based) significantly outperform standard GPs in uncertainty estimation; (3) The practical application of predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., post-editing workflows with different risk preferences), showing that models with better uncertainty estimates improve decision-making.

Strengths: The paper effectively identifies a critical gap in the QE field regarding uncertainty evaluation, which has significant practical implications for translation workflows. The NLPD metric is well-motivated and convincingly shown to distinguish between models with similar point estimate performance but different uncertainty characteristics. The asymmetric risk scenario demonstration is particularly compelling, showing how uncertainty information directly translates to better decision-making in real-world translation scenarios.

Weaknesses: The empirical evaluation is limited to three language pairs with relatively small datasets (particularly en-es with only 858 examples), reducing confidence in the generalizability of results. The paper lacks comparison against modern neural network approaches that have become dominant in QE, making it unclear how their GP-based methods compare to state-of-the-art. The asymmetric risk experiments are somewhat superficial, with limited analysis of practical implications for translation workflows (e.g., quantifying time/cost savings for human translators).

Questions to Authors: How do the authors plan to address the limited comparison against state-of-the-art neural network approaches in QE, given their dominance in the field? Can the authors provide more concrete evidence of practical benefits in real translation workflows, such as estimated productivity gains for translators using their uncertainty-aware approach?
