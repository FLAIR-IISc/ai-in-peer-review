

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Contributions
This paper investigates the use of probabilistic methods for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing that traditional point estimate approaches fail to provide uncertainty information crucial for real-world decision making. The authors propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) with extensions including Matèrn kernels and warped GPs to model uncertainty, and introduce NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) as a more appropriate evaluation metric than traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r). The paper demonstrates how these uncertainty estimates can be practically useful in asymmetric risk scenarios common in translation workflows.

Primary contributions:
1. Demonstrating that NLPD captures differences in uncertainty modeling that traditional metrics fail to detect, providing a more meaningful evaluation framework for probabilistic QE models.
2. Proposing and empirically validating model extensions (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) that improve uncertainty estimation for QE tasks.
3. Showing practical utility of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios through the application of asymmetric loss functions.

## Strengths
1. The paper makes a compelling case for why uncertainty estimation matters in QE applications, with concrete examples of how uncertainty information can improve decision-making in translation workflows (e.g., post-editing scenarios where underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating). This addresses a genuine gap in the field that has practical implications.

2. The theoretical foundation for using GPs in QE is well-developed, with clear explanations of how different kernel choices and warping functions affect uncertainty estimation. The paper provides a strong justification for why NLPD is superior to traditional metrics for evaluating probabilistic models, explaining how it penalizes overconfident models more than underconfident ones.

3. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, using multiple language pairs (en-es, fr-en, en-de) with proper 10-fold cross-validation. The results consistently show that NLPD can detect differences in uncertainty modeling that point estimate metrics cannot, with the en-es dataset providing evidence of overfitting when using NLL as a metric.

4. The paper successfully demonstrates the practical utility of uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios, showing that models with better uncertainty estimation can lead to improved performance in specific application contexts. This moves beyond theoretical evaluation to show real-world relevance.

5. The paper provides a clear and well-structured presentation of related work, situating their contributions within the broader context of probabilistic modeling, asymmetric loss functions, and quality estimation.

## Weaknesses
1. The paper does not adequately compare the point estimate performance of the proposed probabilistic models against state-of-the-art non-probabilistic QE systems. While the authors state they're not focused on improving point estimate performance, it's important to establish that their probabilistic models don't sacrifice point estimate accuracy for better uncertainty estimation.

2. The claim that Matèrn kernels consistently outperform EQ kernels is not strongly supported. Results show mixed performance across different datasets, and the authors acknowledge this may be due to dataset size rather than inherent superiority of Matèrn kernels. The paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of when each kernel type is appropriate.

3. The paper does not provide a thorough analysis of computational complexity for the proposed models. While kernel methods are typically scalable for QE due to small dataset sizes, the authors don't quantify the computational overhead of training and using their probabilistic extensions, which is important for real-world applicability.

4. The results for the pessimistic scenario with linex loss are described as "inconclusive" and show warped GPs performing worse than standard GPs. The authors don't provide a clear explanation for this counterintuitive result, which weakens their argument about the benefits of warped GPs.

5. The paper lacks a detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach, particularly regarding assumptions about the form of warping functions and the specific asymmetric loss functions tested. A more balanced assessment would strengthen the contribution.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you provide more details on the computational overhead of training and using your probabilistic models compared to standard QE systems, especially in terms of training time and inference speed?

2. The paper shows that warped GPs with parametric tanh warping outperform log warping for larger datasets. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, particularly regarding the relationship between dataset size and the ability to learn complex warping functions?

3. In the pessimistic scenario with linex loss, the warped GPs performed worse than standard GPs. Could you provide more insight into why this might be happening, especially given that warped GPs provide better uncertainty estimates in other scenarios?
