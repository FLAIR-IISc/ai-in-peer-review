

# Review for "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), with a focus on providing well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors argue that traditional QE models, which provide only point estimates, are insufficient for real-world translation workflows where uncertainty information is critical for decision making. They propose using Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a probabilistic framework for QE and introduce two key model extensions: Matèrn kernels to relax smoothness assumptions and Warped GPs to handle strictly positive quality scores.

The main contributions of this work, in order of strength, are:
1. Proposing NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) as a more appropriate evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models that captures uncertainty information, rather than relying on traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r).
2. Demonstrating how predictive uncertainty information can be practically utilized in asymmetric risk scenarios common in translation workflows (pessimistic and optimistic settings).
3. Introducing and evaluating model extensions (Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs) that better handle the specific challenges of QE data.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a Critical Gap in QE Practice**: The paper correctly identifies a significant limitation in current QE research - the exclusive focus on point estimate metrics without considering prediction uncertainty. In real-world translation workflows, knowing the confidence in quality predictions is crucial for decision making (e.g., whether to post-edit or translate from scratch). The authors effectively demonstrate how uncertainty information can directly impact practical decisions, making this a highly relevant contribution to the field.

2. **Introducing a Meaningful Evaluation Metric (NLPD)**: The authors convincingly show that standard point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, while NLPD does. Their experiments demonstrate that models with better uncertainty calibration can have similar point estimates but significantly different NLPD scores, making NLPD a more appropriate metric for evaluating probabilistic QE models. This is a substantial contribution to the field's methodology.

3. **Practical Demonstration of Uncertainty in Asymmetric Scenarios**: The paper provides a clear and practical demonstration of how uncertainty information can be used in real-world translation workflows through asymmetric risk scenarios. By showing how Warped GPs can outperform standard models in optimistic scenarios (where recall is important), the authors bridge the gap between theoretical probabilistic modeling and practical application. This connection to real-world decision making significantly strengthens the paper's contribution.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Comparison with Alternative Probabilistic Models**: While the authors correctly note that SVR and similar models cannot provide uncertainty estimates, they don't compare their GP-based approach with other probabilistic models that could also provide uncertainty (e.g., Bayesian neural networks, Bayesian linear regression). This limitation weakens their claim that GPs are uniquely suited for this task, as they don't demonstrate that their specific GP extensions are superior to other probabilistic approaches.

2. **Insufficient Analysis of Dataset Size Effects**: The authors note that the en-es dataset is smaller and might cause overfitting, but they don't provide a systematic analysis of how dataset size affects model performance and the reliability of NLL as an evaluation metric. This is a significant limitation since QE datasets are typically small, and the paper's conclusions about NLPD's superiority depend on reliable evaluation metrics across different dataset sizes.

3. **Inconsistent Results for Linex Loss in Pessimistic Scenario**: The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconsistent, with Warped GPs performing worse than standard GPs despite their better uncertainty calibration. The authors acknowledge this but don't provide a thorough explanation. This inconsistency undermines their claim that Warped GPs are superior in all asymmetric risk scenarios and suggests the approach might not generalize as expected.

## Questions to Authors

1. The authors note that the en-es dataset is smaller and might cause overfitting. Could you provide additional experiments or analysis showing how the relationship between dataset size and the reliability of NLL as an evaluation metric varies across different dataset sizes?

2. The paper focuses exclusively on GP-based approaches. How do you think your findings would generalize to other probabilistic models (e.g., Bayesian neural networks) that could also provide uncertainty estimates? Have you considered comparing your approach with such models?

3. The inconsistent results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are notable. Could you provide more insight into why Warped GPs perform worse than standard GPs in this specific scenario, particularly given that Warped GPs have better uncertainty calibration? Is this a limitation of the Warped GP approach or a characteristic of the linex loss itself?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of QE by addressing the critical need for uncertainty estimation in real-world translation workflows. The paper's main strengths are its practical focus, the introduction of NLPD as a more appropriate evaluation metric, and the demonstration of how uncertainty information can be used in decision making. With some additional analysis and comparisons, this work could be even more impactful.
