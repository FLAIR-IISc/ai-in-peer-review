

# Peer Review for "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Main Contributions
This paper investigates probabilistic modeling for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on providing well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors argue that traditional QE evaluation using point estimate metrics (MAE, RMSE, Pearson's r) fails to capture the uncertainty information that would be valuable for real-world translation workflows. The paper proposes evaluating models using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), which assesses the full predictive distribution rather than just point estimates. It also explores how uncertainty information can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., post-editing where underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating).

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. Introduction of NLPD as a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models that captures uncertainty information, demonstrating that traditional point estimate metrics fail to distinguish between models with different uncertainty profiles.
2. Empirical demonstration that Warped GPs with parametric tanh-based warping functions outperform standard GPs and log-transformed models in terms of uncertainty calibration across multiple language pairs.
3. Practical application of predictive uncertainty in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic settings), showing how well-calibrated uncertainty estimates can improve performance in real-world translation workflows.

## Strengths
1. **Significant methodological contribution to evaluation practice**: The paper identifies a critical gap in QE evaluation practices and proposes NLPD as a meaningful alternative to traditional point estimate metrics. The authors convincingly demonstrate that models with similar point estimate performance can have substantially different uncertainty profiles that are completely invisible to MAE or Pearson's r. This is a substantive contribution that could change how probabilistic QE models are evaluated in the field.

2. **Clear practical demonstration of uncertainty benefits**: The paper goes beyond theoretical discussion to show concrete applications where uncertainty estimates improve decision-making. The asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic) are well-motivated by real translation workflows, and the results demonstrate that models providing better uncertainty estimates can indeed produce superior outcomes in these scenarios. This makes the work relevant to practitioners.

3. **Rigorous experimental evaluation**: The authors use multiple datasets (en-es, fr-en, en-de), multiple evaluation metrics (NLL, NLPD, MAE, Pearson's r), and thorough model comparisons (Matérn vs. EQ kernels, log vs. tanh warping). The 10-fold cross-validation approach and careful analysis of dataset size effects (e.g., the en-es dataset's smaller size affecting results) demonstrate methodological rigor.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty of core methodology**: While the application to QE is novel, the core techniques (GPs, Matérn kernels, Warped GPs) are well-established in machine learning literature. The paper does not introduce fundamentally new modeling techniques but rather applies existing methods to a new domain. The authors acknowledge this in the related work, but this limits the paper's theoretical contribution.

2. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art point-estimate QE models**: The paper focuses exclusively on probabilistic models (GPs) and doesn't compare them with the best current point-estimate QE models (e.g., SVR, neural networks) that dominate the field. Without this comparison, it's unclear whether the probabilistic models provide competitive point-estimate performance while offering uncertainty estimates, which is crucial for assessing their practical value.

3. **Inconsistent results in asymmetric risk scenarios**: The results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are described as "inconclusive" and the paper doesn't adequately explain why Warped GPs perform worse than standard GPs in this case. This undermines the paper's claim about the general benefits of their approach in asymmetric scenarios and requires further investigation.

4. **Lack of discussion on practical scalability**: The paper acknowledges that GPs have scalability issues but doesn't address how these would be mitigated for larger QE datasets or real-world production systems. Given that the datasets used in the paper are relatively small (858-2828 examples), the practical applicability of the approach to larger-scale translation workflows remains unclear.

## Questions to Authors
1. The paper demonstrates that Warped GPs with tanh-based warping outperform standard GPs in most scenarios, but the results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconsistent. Could the authors provide more insight into why this happens, and under what conditions would standard GPs be preferable to Warped GPs in asymmetric scenarios?

2. The paper states that the goal is to compare probabilistic QE models through the predictive uncertainty perspective rather than improving point prediction performance. However, without showing that probabilistic models can compete with state-of-the-art point-estimate models in terms of point prediction quality, how can the authors justify the practical value of their approach?

3. The authors note that GPs have scalability issues, but the datasets used in the paper are relatively small. How would the proposed approach scale to larger QE datasets (e.g., millions of examples), and what practical solutions might be needed to address this limitation for real-world deployment?
