

# Peer Review

## Summary and Contributions

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) that provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates, moving beyond traditional point estimate metrics. The authors focus on Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a framework for probabilistic modeling, proposing two key extensions: Matèrn kernels (to handle non-smooth data) and warped GPs (to better model strictly positive quality scores). The main contributions, in order of strength, are:

1. Demonstrating that traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling, while the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric properly evaluates probabilistic models.
2. Providing evidence that well-calibrated uncertainty estimates can be practically useful in asymmetric risk scenarios common in translation workflows (e.g., post-editing and gisting).
3. Showing that the same probabilistic model can be adapted to different loss functions without retraining, which is valuable for real-world applications where loss functions might change.

## Strengths

1. **Strong practical motivation for uncertainty estimation in QE**: The paper effectively argues why uncertainty matters in real translation workflows (e.g., post-editing scenarios where translators need to decide whether to accept MT output based on quality and confidence). This goes beyond typical academic concerns to address actual industry needs, making the work highly relevant.

2. **Compelling evidence that traditional metrics are insufficient**: The results in Table 1 provide clear evidence that models with similar point estimate performance can have dramatically different uncertainty properties, which NLPD captures but standard metrics do not. This is a significant contribution to the field, as it challenges the prevailing evaluation paradigm in QE.

3. **Practical application to asymmetric risk scenarios**: The paper demonstrates a clear, valuable use case for uncertainty information—using predictive distributions to adapt to different loss functions in production settings without retraining. The examples of pessimistic (post-editing) and optimistic (gisting) scenarios are well-motivated and directly applicable to real translation workflows.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison with state-of-the-art QE models**: The paper uses a standard set of 17 features and compares to traditional kernel methods, but fails to compare with recent neural network-based approaches that have become dominant in QE. This limits the paper's contribution to the current state-of-the-art, as the authors claim their work is relevant for "any text regression task" where uncertainty matters, but don't demonstrate how their probabilistic models compare to modern non-probabilistic baselines.

2. **Insufficient analysis of dataset size effects**: The authors attribute the inconsistent performance of different warping functions (tanh vs. log) to the small size of the en-es dataset. However, they don't provide a systematic analysis of how model performance varies with dataset size, making it difficult to generalize their findings. For instance, they claim "for the en-es dataset, the log function tends to perform better" but don't quantify how much the dataset size matters.

3. **Superficial analysis of asymmetric risk results**: The paper reports that "the results for linex are consistently worse than the standard GP baseline" in the pessimistic scenario but doesn't adequately explain why. The authors suggest this might be due to "warped models tend to have less variance," but this requires deeper analysis to determine whether this is a limitation of the warped GP approach or a fundamental issue with the linex loss in this context.

4. **Lack of discussion on computational complexity**: While the paper acknowledges that GP scalability is not a major issue for QE (since datasets are small), it doesn't discuss how the proposed extensions (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) impact training time or memory requirements compared to standard GPs. This is important for practical adoption.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would the proposed probabilistic models perform on larger QE datasets (e.g., those with 10k+ samples) compared to the small datasets used here? Could the authors provide insights into the scalability of their approach?

2. The paper shows that warped GPs with tanh functions outperform log warping in most cases, but the en-es dataset is an exception. Could the authors provide more detailed analysis of the relationship between dataset size and the optimal choice of warping function?

3. The authors claim that "NLPD should be preferred as an evaluation metric when proper prediction uncertainty estimates are required." How do they reconcile this with the fact that the WMT QE shared tasks (the primary venue for QE evaluation) still use MAE and Pearson's r? What would be the barriers to adopting NLPD in the broader QE community?
