- Traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) miss differences in uncertainty modeling; Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) properly evaluates probabilistic models.
- Well-calibrated uncertainty estimates are practically useful in asymmetric risk scenarios in translation workflows (e.g., post-editing and gisting).
- A single probabilistic model can be adapted to different loss functions at decision time without retraining.
- Strong practical motivation: the paper convincingly argues uncertainty matters for real translation workflows and industry use.
- Compelling empirical evidence that models with similar point estimates can have very different uncertainty properties, which NLPD reveals.
- Practical demonstration of using predictive distributions to adapt to pessimistic (post-editing) and optimistic (gisting) loss scenarios.
- Limited comparison with state-of-the-art QE models; no evaluation against recent neural network–based baselines.
- Insufficient analysis of how dataset size affects model behavior and the choice/performance of warping functions.
- Superficial examination of asymmetric risk results (e.g., why linex performs worse); lacks deeper explanation for warped GP behavior.
- No discussion of computational complexity or how Matèrn kernels and warped GPs affect training time and memory compared to standard GPs.
- Question: How would the proposed models perform and scale on much larger QE datasets (e.g., 10k+ samples)?
- Question: Can the authors provide a detailed analysis of the relationship between dataset size and the optimal warping function (tanh vs. log)?
- Question: How should the community reconcile recommending NLPD when standard QE benchmarks (WMT) still use MAE and Pearson’s r, and what are barriers to adopting NLPD?
