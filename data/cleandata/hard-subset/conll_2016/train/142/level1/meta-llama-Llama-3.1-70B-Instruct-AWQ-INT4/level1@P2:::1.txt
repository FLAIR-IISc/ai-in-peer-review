**Summary of the Paper**

The paper "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation" proposes the use of probabilistic models for Quality Estimation (QE) in machine translation, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), to provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors argue that traditional point estimate metrics do not capture the uncertainty of predictions, which is crucial in real-world translation environments. They evaluate their proposed models using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric, which takes into account the full posterior predictive distributions. The paper also explores the application of predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios, where the cost of overestimation and underestimation are different.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Proposal of probabilistic models for QE**: The authors propose the use of GPs for QE, which can provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates.
2. **Evaluation using NLPD**: The authors evaluate their proposed models using NLPD, which is a more informative metric than traditional point estimate metrics.
3. **Application to asymmetric risk scenarios**: The authors demonstrate the application of predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios, where the cost of overestimation and underestimation are different.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to QE**: The authors propose a novel approach to QE by using probabilistic models, which can provide more informative predictions.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors evaluate their proposed models using a comprehensive set of metrics, including NLPD, MAE, and Pearson's r.
3. **Practical application**: The authors demonstrate the practical application of their approach in asymmetric risk scenarios, which is a common scenario in real-world translation environments.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited experimental setup**: The authors only experiment with three language pairs, which may not be representative of all possible language pairs.
2. **Small dataset size**: The authors note that the en-es dataset is small, which may lead to overfitting and affect the generalizability of the results.
3. **Limited exploration of hyperparameters**: The authors do not extensively explore the hyperparameters of their models, which may affect the performance of their approach.

**Questions to Authors**

1. **How do the authors plan to address the issue of overfitting in small datasets?**
2. **Can the authors provide more insights into the choice of hyperparameters for their models?**
3. **How do the authors plan to extend their approach to other language pairs and datasets?**

**Additional Comments**

The paper is well-written and provides a comprehensive evaluation of the proposed approach. However, the authors could benefit from exploring more language pairs and datasets to increase the generalizability of their results. Additionally, the authors could provide more insights into the choice of hyperparameters and how they plan to address the issue of overfitting in small datasets.
