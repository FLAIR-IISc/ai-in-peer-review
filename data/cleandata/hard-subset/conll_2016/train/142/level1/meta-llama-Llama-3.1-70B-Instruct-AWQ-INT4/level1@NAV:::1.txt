**Summary of the Paper**

This paper explores the use of probabilistic methods for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (MTQE) to provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors investigate Gaussian Processes (GPs) and their extensions, such as Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs, to model uncertainty in MTQE. They evaluate these models using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric, which takes into account the full posterior predictive distribution. The authors also demonstrate the usefulness of these models in asymmetric risk scenarios, where the cost of overestimation and underestimation are different.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Probabilistic MTQE models**: The authors propose the use of GPs and their extensions for MTQE, which provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. This is a significant contribution, as traditional MTQE models only provide point estimates.
2. **NLPD evaluation metric**: The authors use the NLPD metric to evaluate the performance of their models, which is a more comprehensive metric than traditional point estimate-based metrics.
3. **Asymmetric risk scenarios**: The authors demonstrate the usefulness of their models in asymmetric risk scenarios, where the cost of overestimation and underestimation are different. This is a significant contribution, as it shows the potential of probabilistic MTQE models in real-world applications.

**Strengths**

1. **Comprehensive evaluation**: The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of their models using multiple metrics, including NLPD, Mean Absolute Error (MAE), and Pearson's r correlation coefficient.
2. **Real-world applications**: The authors demonstrate the usefulness of their models in real-world applications, such as post-editing scenarios and gisting scenarios.
3. **Theoretical foundations**: The authors provide a solid theoretical foundation for their work, including a detailed explanation of GPs and their extensions.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited experimental setup**: The authors only experiment with three language pairs (English-Spanish, French-English, and English-German) and a limited number of datasets.
2. **Overfitting**: The authors mention that their models may be prone to overfitting, especially in smaller datasets.
3. **Lack of comparison with state-of-the-art models**: The authors do not compare their models with state-of-the-art MTQE models, which makes it difficult to assess the significance of their contributions.

**Questions to Authors**

1. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting in your models, especially in smaller datasets?
2. Can you provide a more comprehensive comparison with state-of-the-art MTQE models to assess the significance of your contributions?
3. How do you plan to extend your work to other language pairs and datasets?
