**Summary**

This paper explores the use of probabilistic methods for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) that can provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors investigate Gaussian Processes (GPs) and their extensions, such as Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs, to model uncertainty in QE. They evaluate these models using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric, which takes into account the full posterior predictive distribution. The authors also demonstrate the usefulness of these models in asymmetric risk scenarios, where the cost of overestimation and underestimation are different.

**Main Contributions**

1. **Investigation of probabilistic methods for QE**: The authors explore the use of GPs and their extensions for QE, which is a novel approach in this field.
2. **Evaluation of models using NLPD**: The authors use NLPD as a metric to evaluate the performance of their models, which is a more comprehensive metric than traditional point estimate-based metrics.
3. **Application of probabilistic models to asymmetric risk scenarios**: The authors demonstrate the usefulness of their models in scenarios where the cost of overestimation and underestimation are different.

**Strengths**

1. **Novel approach to QE**: The authors' use of probabilistic methods for QE is a novel approach that can provide more informative results than traditional point estimate-based methods.
2. **Comprehensive evaluation metric**: The authors' use of NLPD as a metric provides a more comprehensive evaluation of their models than traditional metrics.
3. **Useful application to asymmetric risk scenarios**: The authors' demonstration of the usefulness of their models in asymmetric risk scenarios is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses**

1. **Limited experimentation with different kernels and weighting schemes**: The authors only experiment with a limited set of kernels and weighting schemes, which may not be representative of all possible scenarios.
2. **Inconclusive results for linex loss in pessimistic scenario**: The authors' results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario are inconclusive, which may indicate a need for further experimentation.
3. **Limited discussion of computational complexity**: The authors do not provide a detailed discussion of the computational complexity of their models, which may be a concern for large-scale applications.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the computational complexity of your models and how they can be scaled up for large-scale applications?
2. How do you plan to address the inconclusive results for the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario?
3. Can you provide more experimentation with different kernels and weighting schemes to demonstrate the robustness of your approach?
