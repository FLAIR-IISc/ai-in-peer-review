This paper explores probabilistic methods for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on Gaussian Processes (GPs) and their ability to provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The authors argue that traditional point-estimate metrics are insufficient for real-world translation workflows where uncertainty is crucial for decision-making. They investigate different GP extensions, evaluate their calibration using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), and demonstrate the benefits of using posterior distributions in an asymmetric risk scenario.

The primary contribution of this work lies in its comprehensive empirical evaluation of different GP configurations for QE, specifically focusing on the often-overlooked aspect of uncertainty calibration. The authors systematically explore Matèrn kernels and warped GPs, providing a detailed comparison of their performance using NLPD, a metric that directly assesses the quality of probabilistic predictions. While the idea of using probabilistic models for QE isn’t entirely novel (citing Cohn & Specia, 2013), the thorough investigation into the effects of kernel choice and likelihood warping within the GP framework, coupled with a focus on NLPD, constitutes a significant contribution. A second contribution is the demonstration of how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios relevant to MT workflows. The exploration of the AL and linex loss functions and their associated Bayes risk estimators provides a practical application of the probabilistic QE models. Finally, the exploration of asymmetric loss functions and their impact on the choice of model (particularly highlighting potential issues with linex in specific scenarios) offers valuable insights for practitioners.

**Strengths:**

1. **Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation of different GP models, covering multiple datasets and evaluation metrics. The use of 10-fold cross-validation provides a robust assessment of model performance. The consistent use of a standard feature set facilitates comparison across language pairs.
2. **Focus on Uncertainty Calibration:** The emphasis on uncertainty calibration, as measured by NLPD, is a significant strength. The authors rightly point out the limitations of traditional point-estimate metrics in capturing the quality of probabilistic predictions. This is a valuable contribution to the QE field, which has historically focused heavily on point estimates.
3. **Practical Application to Asymmetric Risk:** Demonstrating the utility of predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios provides a compelling motivation for adopting probabilistic QE models. The exploration of different loss functions (AL and linex) and their corresponding Bayes risk estimators showcases a practical application of the research.
4. **Detailed Model Exploration:** The detailed explanation of different GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) and their theoretical underpinnings enhances the paper’s technical soundness. The authors clearly articulate the rationale behind their modeling choices.
5. **Well-Structured and Clearly Written:** The paper is well-organized, with a clear structure and concise writing style. This makes the technical content accessible to a broad audience.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the evaluation is thorough, the underlying methodology (using GPs for QE) isn’t entirely novel. The paper primarily builds upon existing work rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach.  The extensions themselves (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) are established techniques from the GP literature.
2. **Inconclusive Results with Linex Loss:** The observation that the linex loss performs poorly in the pessimistic scenario and the lack of a clear explanation for this behavior weakens the practical implications of the asymmetric risk analysis. The authors acknowledge this but don’t offer a definitive solution. The issue of variance impacting the linex loss requires further exploration.
3. **Feature Set Limitation:** While using a standard feature set simplifies comparison, it may limit the potential performance of the models. The authors acknowledge that state-of-the-art QE systems often employ larger feature sets, but the rationale for not exploring these is not fully convincing. It is possible the observed differences in performance are partially attributable to the limited feature representation.
4. **Dataset Size Limitations:** The relatively small size of the en-es dataset is identified as a potential source of overfitting and unreliable NLL scores.  Further investigation into the effect of dataset size on the performance of different models would be beneficial.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Newer Approaches:** The paper focuses heavily on GPs. A brief discussion of how GP-based approaches compare to more recent advancements in QE, such as neural network-based models with uncertainty estimation techniques (e.g., Bayesian Neural Networks, Monte Carlo Dropout), would strengthen the context and impact of the work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why the linex loss performed poorly in the pessimistic scenario, and what potential remedies could be explored to address this issue?
2.  Given the limitations of the chosen feature set, did you consider experimenting with larger feature sets, potentially using feature selection techniques, to assess whether this impacts the relative performance of the different GP configurations?
3.  How do the results compare when using different normalization methods for the post-editing time (beyond the length normalization used in this study)?
4.  Can you discuss the computational cost associated with training and predicting with the different GP models, particularly the warped GPs with multiple tanh terms, and how this might influence their scalability to larger datasets?



**Additional Comments:**

* The citation style is generally consistent.
* The use of tables and figures effectively presents the experimental results.
* The discussion of related work is adequate, but could be expanded to include more recent advancements in QE.
* Consider including a discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential avenues for future research in the conclusion.
