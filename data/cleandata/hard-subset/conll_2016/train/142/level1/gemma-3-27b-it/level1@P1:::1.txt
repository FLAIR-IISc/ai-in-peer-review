This paper investigates probabilistic methods, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) with a focus on well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. The core argument is that relying solely on point estimates in QE limits its practical utility in real-world translation workflows, and incorporating uncertainty can improve decision-making processes like determining whether a translation segment requires post-editing or can be accepted as-is. The authors explore different GP kernel extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) and advocate for evaluating QE models using metrics that consider the full posterior predictive distributions (like NLPD) rather than traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, RMSE, Pearson’s r). They further demonstrate the potential benefits of these uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios.

**Contributions:**

1. **Advocacy for probabilistic QE and full posterior evaluation:** The paper’s primary contribution is a strong argument for shifting the focus in QE from point estimates to probabilistic predictions and evaluating models using metrics like NLPD that assess the quality of the entire predictive distribution. This is a valid and important point, as it directly addresses the practical needs of QE in real-world applications where risk assessment and informed decision-making are crucial.
2. **Exploration of GP extensions for QE:** The authors systematically investigate and adapt several GP extensions (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) to the QE task. While these extensions are established within the GP literature, their application and comparative evaluation within the context of QE provide a valuable contribution. The finding that warped GPs, particularly those employing a tanh-based warping function, often outperform standard GPs is insightful.
3. **Demonstration of asymmetric risk application:** Showing how the predictive distributions can be utilized within asymmetric risk scenarios—specifically a post-editing task with differing penalties for false positives and false negatives—provides a concrete example of the practical benefits of probabilistic QE. This practical application helps bridge the gap between theoretical uncertainty estimation and real-world usage.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance and Motivation:** The paper addresses a significant limitation of current QE approaches: the lack of uncertainty estimation. The motivation is clear and well-articulated, highlighting the practical challenges of relying solely on point estimates in translation workflows. The post-editing scenario is a particularly compelling example.
2. **Thorough Exploration of GPs:** The paper demonstrates a good understanding of GPs and their extensions. The descriptions of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs are clear and concise, providing a solid foundation for the experimental work.
3. **Appropriate Evaluation Metrics:** The authors rightly criticize the reliance on point-estimate metrics and advocate for the use of NLPD, which is a more suitable metric for evaluating probabilistic models. Their results convincingly show that differences in uncertainty modeling are not captured by traditional metrics.
4. **Clear Presentation and Organization:** The paper is well-structured and easy to follow. The organization, starting with motivation, then delving into technical details, followed by experimental results, is logical and effective.
5. **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on multiple datasets (en-es, fr-en, en-de) provide strong evidence to support the authors' claims. The consistent performance gains observed with Warped GPs are particularly noteworthy.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Methods:** The core methods (GPs, Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) are not novel contributions *in themselves*. The paper’s strength lies in applying these established techniques to the specific problem of QE and demonstrating their benefits within that context. This isn’t necessarily a flaw, but it’s important to acknowledge.
2. **Sensitivity to Dataset Size:** The results indicate that the performance differences observed between models are affected by the size of the datasets. The inconsistent behaviour of NLPD and the varying performance of warping functions across datasets suggest that more data may be needed to reliably assess the benefits of these methods.
3. **Linex Loss Results:** The inconsistent and sometimes counterintuitive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario raise concerns. The authors suggest the high variance of standard GPs might be contributing, but further investigation is needed to understand this phenomenon. A clearer explanation of why the linex loss performs poorly in this setting would be beneficial.
4. **Feature Set Limitation:** While acknowledging the limitations of using a fixed feature set for cross-dataset comparison, the authors do not explore more sophisticated feature engineering or the potential benefits of leveraging larger feature sets that are language-dependent.
5. **Lack of Comparison to Modern QE Systems:** The paper primarily compares different GP variants against each other. A comparison against state-of-the-art QE systems that are not based on GPs (e.g., neural network-based models) would provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed methods' competitiveness.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why the linex loss performed poorly in the pessimistic scenario? What specific characteristics of the predictive distributions from standard GPs versus warped GPs could explain this behavior?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters for the Warped GPs (e.g., the number of tanh terms)? Did you perform a comprehensive hyperparameter search, and if so, what were the optimal values found for each dataset?
3.  Given the observed sensitivity to dataset size, do you have any recommendations for the minimum amount of data required to reliably assess the benefits of probabilistic QE and NLPD?
4.  Would the benefits of warped GPs translate to neural network based QE models? Have you considered applying similar warping techniques to the output of neural QE systems?

**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the optimal hyperparameters used for each model and dataset.
*   Consider providing a visual illustration of the predictive distributions generated by different models to help readers understand the differences in uncertainty estimation.
*   The references are comprehensive and well-formatted.
