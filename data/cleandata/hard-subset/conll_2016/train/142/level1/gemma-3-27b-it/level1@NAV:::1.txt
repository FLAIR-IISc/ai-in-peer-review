## Review of “Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation”

This paper investigates the importance of modeling and evaluating prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that traditional point-estimate metrics are insufficient for real-world applications where QE informs decision-making, and explore Gaussian Processes (GPs) as a method for generating well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. They demonstrate the value of these estimates through intrinsic evaluation using Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) and extrinsic evaluation in asymmetric risk scenarios.

The primary contribution of this work lies in highlighting the *insufficiency of standard point-estimate evaluation metrics* (MAE, Pearson’s r) for assessing the quality of probabilistic QE models. The authors convincingly demonstrate that these metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, and advocate for the use of NLPD. A second key contribution is the *exploration and comparison of different GP configurations* (kernels, warping functions) specifically within the QE task, showing that warped GPs, particularly those employing a tanh-based warping function, generally outperform standard GPs. Finally, the work provides a *practical demonstration of how calibrated uncertainty can be leveraged* within asymmetric risk scenarios—a relevant and often overlooked aspect of real-world QE applications.

Strengths of this submission include a clear motivation and a well-defined problem. The experiments are thorough, covering multiple datasets and configurations, and the results are presented clearly. The discussion of asymmetric risk is particularly insightful, offering concrete examples of how uncertainty information can be utilized to improve decision-making. The use of NLPD is a strong methodological choice.

However, the paper has weaknesses. The novelty of applying GPs to QE is limited, as previous work has already established their effectiveness. The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios, while insightful, feels somewhat limited in scope; a more detailed analysis of different weighting schemes and their impact would be beneficial. The discussion of why the linex loss performs poorly in the pessimistic scenario is also a bit underdeveloped.

Questions to Authors:

1. The paper mentions that the datasets are relatively small. How might the findings change with significantly larger datasets, and would this alter the preference for specific GP configurations (e.g., Matèrn kernels)?
2. Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with the different GP configurations (especially the warped GPs with tanh functions), and how this might impact their practical applicability?
3. While you demonstrate the benefits of uncertainty in asymmetric risk scenarios, could you discuss the potential downsides or limitations of relying heavily on uncertainty estimates in real-world MT workflows?



Additional Comments:
- It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the computational time for training different GP models.
- The references are extensive and well-formatted.
