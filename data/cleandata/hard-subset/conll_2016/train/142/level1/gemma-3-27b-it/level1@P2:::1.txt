This paper explores the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), with a focus on capturing and evaluating prediction uncertainty. The core argument is that point estimate metrics are insufficient for real-world QE applications where decision-making requires an understanding of confidence levels. The authors investigate model extensions and evaluate them using metrics that consider the full posterior predictive distributions, and demonstrate the utility of this information in an asymmetric risk scenario.

The primary contribution of this work is the thorough empirical investigation of probabilistic QE using GPs, demonstrating that differences in uncertainty modeling *are* detectable using metrics like NLPD, while being obscured by traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r). This is a significant finding, as it justifies the shift towards evaluating QE models based on their ability to provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. A second, related contribution is the application of asymmetric loss functions to the QE task, showcasing how predictive distributions can be utilized for improved decision-making in practical scenarios like post-editing and gisting. The paper also provides a well-structured and comprehensive review of relevant GP techniques (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) within the context of QE, which serves as a valuable resource for researchers in the field.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty in Evaluation:** The central strength lies in advocating for and demonstrating the utility of NLPD as an evaluation metric for QE. While NLPD isn’t new in the GP literature, its application to QE and the clear demonstration that it *reveals* differences between models that are hidden by traditional metrics is a strong contribution. The authors convincingly argue that relying solely on point estimates misses crucial information about model confidence.
2. **Practical Relevance through Asymmetric Risk:** The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios is a valuable addition. The framing of post-editing and gisting tasks as asymmetric decision problems, and the application of appropriate loss functions, highlights the potential for real-world impact of the proposed approach. This connection to practical application significantly strengthens the paper.
3. **Comprehensive GP Review:** The paper provides a clear and accessible overview of relevant GP techniques (Matèrn kernels, warped GPs) adapted for QE. This section is well-motivated and logically presented, making it easy for readers to understand the technical details and the reasoning behind the choices made. This creates a strong foundation for the experimental results.
4. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The use of multiple datasets (en-es, fr-en, en-de) and a robust cross-validation protocol increases the reliability and generalizability of the findings. The consistent use of a feature set from the QuEst framework ensures a fair comparison across models and datasets.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Models:** The focus is almost entirely on Gaussian Processes. While GPs are a strong baseline and well-suited for modeling uncertainty, the paper doesn’t explore other probabilistic models that might also be relevant (e.g., Bayesian Neural Networks, ensembles of models). This limits the broader impact of the findings. A brief discussion of why other models were not considered would be helpful.
2. **Overly Detailed Technical Descriptions:** Certain sections, particularly those detailing the mathematical formulation of GPs and the various kernels, are quite dense and may be difficult for readers not already familiar with the field to follow. While thoroughness is appreciated, streamlining these sections and focusing more on the *intuition* behind the methods could improve accessibility.
3. **Inconclusive Linex Results:** The results with the linex loss function in the pessimistic scenario are described as inconclusive, and the explanation (related to warped GPs having lower variance) is somewhat speculative. This weakens the overall impact of the asymmetric risk analysis. Further investigation into the behavior of the linex loss with different GP configurations would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper primarily focuses on aggregate metric scores. A more detailed error analysis, investigating specific examples where the probabilistic models outperform or underperform compared to traditional methods, could provide valuable insights and further justify the proposed approach.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Why were other probabilistic modeling techniques (e.g., Bayesian Neural Networks, ensembles) not considered in this study?
2.  Could you elaborate on the potential reasons for the inconclusive results with the linex loss function in the pessimistic scenario? Were any attempts made to address this issue during experimentation?
3.  Were any specific types of errors observed more frequently with certain models or loss functions? Providing examples of such cases would strengthen the analysis.
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of features? Would using a larger, more comprehensive feature set significantly alter the conclusions drawn in the paper?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The notation is generally clear, but consistently defining all symbols would be helpful.
*   Figure 1, showing the asymmetric loss functions, would benefit from clearer labeling and a scale indicating the magnitude of the loss.
*   Consider adding a brief discussion on the computational cost of the different GP models, as this is a practical consideration for real-world applications.
