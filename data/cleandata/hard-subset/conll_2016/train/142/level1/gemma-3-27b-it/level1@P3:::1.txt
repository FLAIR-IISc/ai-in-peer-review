This paper investigates probabilistic methods, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on well-calibrated uncertainty estimates and their application to asymmetric risk scenarios. The authors rightly point out the limitations of traditional QE evaluation, which relies on point estimates and ignores predictive uncertainty. The primary contribution lies in demonstrating the inadequacy of standard point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson’s r) for evaluating the quality of uncertainty estimates and advocating for the use of the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD). A secondary contribution is the exploration of different GP configurations (kernels, warping functions) and their impact on uncertainty modeling. Finally, the paper showcases a practical application of these uncertainty estimates within asymmetric risk scenarios, exemplified by post-editing and gisting tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addressing a Critical Gap:** The paper tackles a significant and often overlooked aspect of QE: the importance of predictive uncertainty. Relying solely on point estimates can be misleading and hinder effective integration of QE models into real-world translation workflows, as the authors convincingly argue. This focus is a strong positive.
2. **Sound Methodological Approach:**  The choice of Gaussian Processes is well-justified given their ability to provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates, particularly in low-data regimes typical of QE. The exploration of different kernel functions (Matèrn) and warping functions demonstrates a thorough understanding of GP methodology. The experimental setup, using standard QE datasets and features, allows for fair comparison and replicability.
3. **Compelling Empirical Evidence:** The results convincingly show that differences in uncertainty modeling are *not* captured by common point-estimate metrics. The NLPD metric emerges as a superior indicator of model performance when considering uncertainty, supporting the paper’s central claim. The results from the asymmetric risk scenarios highlight the potential benefits of incorporating uncertainty into decision-making.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The motivation is strong, the methodology is explained in detail, and the results are presented in a concise and understandable manner. The inclusion of relevant related work adds to the paper’s credibility.
5. **Practical Relevance:** The asymmetric risk scenarios directly address real-world applications of QE, demonstrating the practical value of incorporating uncertainty estimates. The discussion of how Bayes risk estimators can be used with the predictive distributions is insightful and avoids the need for constant retraining when loss functions change.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Scope of Probabilistic Models:** The paper solely focuses on Gaussian Processes. While GPs are a strong choice, it would be beneficial to acknowledge and briefly discuss other probabilistic modeling approaches (e.g., Bayesian Neural Networks, Monte Carlo Dropout) and their potential applicability to QE. Justifying the exclusive focus on GPs beyond the ease of evaluation is necessary.
2. **Sensitivity to Dataset Size:** The authors acknowledge that NLPD may be unreliable for smaller datasets, as observed in the en-es dataset. This raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings to real-world scenarios where data scarcity is often a challenge. More investigation into robust uncertainty evaluation methods for small datasets is needed.
3. **Linex Loss Interpretation:** The inconsistent performance and, particularly, the counterintuitive results with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario require further explanation. The authors’ suspicion that high-variance models are being favored is plausible, but a more in-depth analysis is needed. The sensitivity analysis of the weight ‘w’ in the linex loss is also limited, and more extensive testing with different values might reveal better insights.
4. **Feature Set Limitations:** While acknowledging the resource-intensive nature of larger feature sets, the authors’ choice of a relatively small feature set may limit the performance of the models and the detectability of differences in uncertainty estimation. Exploring the impact of different feature sets, even on a limited scale, could strengthen the conclusions.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis, examining specific cases where the uncertainty estimates are well-calibrated versus those where they are not, could provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed models and the underlying factors influencing uncertainty estimation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the reasons for exclusively focusing on Gaussian Processes, and briefly discuss other probabilistic modeling approaches that could be relevant to QE?
2. How robust are the findings regarding NLPD’s superiority to point-estimate metrics when dealing with datasets of varying sizes? Do you have any recommendations for evaluating uncertainty in low-resource scenarios?
3. Can you provide a more detailed explanation for the counterintuitive results observed with the linex loss in the pessimistic scenario? What factors might be contributing to this behavior, and are there any ways to mitigate it?
4. Could you comment on the potential impact of using a larger feature set on the performance of the models and the ability to discriminate between different uncertainty estimation methods?
5.  Are there plans to release the code and data used in the experiments to facilitate reproducibility and further research?




**Additional Comments:**

*  The notation throughout the paper is generally clear, but could benefit from more consistent use of mathematical symbols.
*  The discussion of related work is comprehensive but could be more focused on the most relevant papers in the field of probabilistic QE.
* Consider adding a visualization of the predictive distributions for a few representative examples to illustrate the differences between the models and their uncertainty estimates.
