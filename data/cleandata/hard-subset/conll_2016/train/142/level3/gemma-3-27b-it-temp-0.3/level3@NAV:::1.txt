## Review of “Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation”

This paper investigates the application of Gaussian Processes (GPs) to model prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), specifically focusing on post-editing time as the target variable. The authors rightly highlight the limitations of relying solely on point estimates in real-world QE applications and advocate for incorporating uncertainty information into decision-making processes. The paper is well-structured, clearly articulating the problem and motivation, and provides a comprehensive introduction to GPs, including kernel choices and warping techniques. The research questions are interesting and logically presented.

The primary contribution of this work lies in its demonstration of the benefits of probabilistic modeling – specifically GPs – for QE, going beyond standard point-estimate evaluation. The authors convincingly argue for the importance of considering full posterior predictive distributions and showcase how this information can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios. A second contribution is the thorough exploration of GP extensions, such as Matèrn kernels and warped GPs, within the QE context, providing a practical guide for researchers. Finally, the paper’s emphasis on evaluating models using metrics like NLL and NLPD, which account for predictive uncertainty, is a valuable contribution to the field.

Several strengths support acceptance. The explanation of asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications is particularly strong, providing a compelling justification for the need for uncertainty estimates. The detailed explanation of GP methodology is accessible, even for readers less familiar with the technique. Section 4, detailing the results and their intuitive interpretation, is exceptionally well-written. 

However, the paper has weaknesses. The justification for choosing post-editing rates over the more common HTER prediction is not fully convincing. Furthermore, Section 3.2 contains a confusing statement regarding the trends observed for different language pairs with warped GPs; clarification is needed. The results tables would benefit from including a baseline point-estimate model for contextualization. While NLL and NLPD are reported, their values are difficult to interpret without a qualitative sense of what constitutes a significant reduction. 

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind using post-editing rates instead of HTER as the target variable for QE?
2.  Please clarify the discrepancy in observed trends for NLL/NLPD across different language pairs in Section 3.2. Specifically, what explains the differing behavior between fr-en & en-de versus en-es?
3.  Would you consider adding a baseline point-estimate model (e.g., SVR) to the results tables to provide a clearer comparison of the proposed methods’ performance?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please change “over and underestimates” to “over- and underestimates” throughout the paper.
*   The line colors in Figure 1 caption are incorrect (blue and green, not blue and red).
*   Please cite the specific GP toolkit used for modeling (e.g., GPy, scikit-learn).
