**Summary of the Paper**

This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), with a focus on modeling prediction uncertainty. The authors argue that traditional point estimate metrics used in QE are insufficient, as they do not capture the uncertainty associated with predictions. They propose the use of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a metric to evaluate the performance of probabilistic QE models. The paper also investigates the application of GPs to asymmetric risk scenarios, where the cost of over- and underestimates differs.

**Main Contributions**

1. The paper proposes the use of NLPD as a metric to evaluate the performance of probabilistic QE models, which can capture the uncertainty associated with predictions.
2. The authors investigate the application of GPs to asymmetric risk scenarios, where the cost of over- and underestimates differs, and show how well-calibrated predictive distributions can be used to derive Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses.
3. The paper provides a thorough introduction to GPs and their application to QE, including a clear explanation of kernel choices and warping functions.

**Strengths**

1. The paper is well-structured and clearly written, making it easy to follow for both experts and non-experts in the field.
2. The authors provide a strong motivation for the use of probabilistic models in QE, highlighting the limitations of traditional point estimate metrics.
3. The paper provides a thorough introduction to GPs and their application to QE, including a clear explanation of kernel choices and warping functions.
4. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of GPs in modeling prediction uncertainty and their application to asymmetric risk scenarios.

**Weaknesses**

1. The paper does not advance the state-of-the-art in QE, as the proposed approach is based on existing GP methodology.
2. The authors do not provide a clear justification for choosing post-editing rates over the more common HTER prediction in QE research.
3. The paper could benefit from the inclusion of an existing point-estimate model baseline in result tables to contextualize the overall quality of the proposed approach.
4. The NLL and NLPD values reported in the paper are hard to interpret, and the authors could provide a notion of what constitutes a drastic reduction, e.g., qualitative examples.

**Questions to Authors**

1. Can you provide a clear justification for choosing post-editing rates over the more common HTER prediction in QE research?
2. How do you plan to address the limitation of not advancing the state-of-the-art in QE, and what future work do you propose to overcome this limitation?
3. Can you provide more details on the GP toolkit used for modeling, if applicable?
4. How do you plan to extend the proposed approach to other natural language processing applications, as mentioned in the conclusion?
