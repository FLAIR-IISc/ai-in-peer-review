This paper explores the application of Gaussian Processes (GPs) to machine translation quality estimation (QE), with a focus on modeling prediction uncertainty. The authors argue that traditional point estimate metrics used in QE are insufficient for real-world applications, where uncertainty information can be crucial for decision-making. The paper's primary contributions are:

1. **Application of GPs to QE**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of GPs in modeling post-editing time/rates for QE, which is a novel application of this methodology.
2. **Evaluation of uncertainty estimates**: The paper introduces the use of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a metric to evaluate the uncertainty estimates of probabilistic QE models.
3. **Asymmetric risk scenarios**: The authors explore the application of asymmetric loss functions to QE, which can capture the different consequences of over- and underestimates in real-world scenarios.

The paper's strengths include:

1. **Clear introduction and motivation**: The authors provide a well-structured introduction that highlights the problems of QE point estimates in real applications.
2. **Strong explanation of asymmetric risk scenarios**: The paper provides a clear and intuitive explanation of asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications for different estimators.
3. **GP methodology and kernel choices**: The authors explain GP methodology, kernel choices, and warping in a clear and easy-to-follow manner.

However, the paper also has some weaknesses:

1. **Lack of major modeling novelty**: The paper does not advance the state-of-the-art in QE or offer major modeling novelty.
2. **Interpretability of NLL and NLPD values**: The paper could benefit from providing a notion of what constitutes a drastic reduction in NLL and NLPD values, such as qualitative examples.
3. **Missing point-estimate model baseline**: The paper could benefit from including an existing point-estimate model baseline in the result tables to contextualize the overall quality of the GP models.

Questions to authors:

1. Can you justify choosing post-editing rates over the more common HTER prediction in QE research?
2. Can you provide more insight into the trend observed in Section 3.2's first paragraph, where NLL and NLPD decrease with warped GPs across datasets, but not for the en-es dataset?
3. Can you provide more information on the GP toolkit used for modeling, if applicable?

Minor edits and suggestions:

1. Change "over and underestimates" to "over- and underestimates."
2. Fix the Figure 1 caption, which lists wrong line colors (blue and green, not blue and red).
3. Consider citing the GP toolkit used for modeling, if applicable.
