This paper explores the application of Gaussian processes (GPs) to machine translation quality estimation (QE), with a focus on modeling uncertainty and providing well-calibrated predictive distributions. The authors argue that traditional point estimate metrics are insufficient for QE in real-world applications, where uncertainty information can be crucial for decision-making.

The paper is well-structured and provides a clear introduction to the problems of QE point estimates. The explanation of asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications for different estimators is strong. The authors also provide a substantial introduction to GPs, which may be lengthy for experts but is useful for general readers.

The GP methodology, kernel choices, and warping are explained clearly and are easy to follow. The research questions are interesting and well-phrased. However, the authors could justify choosing post-editing rates over the more common HTER prediction in QE research.

One point of confusion is in Section 3.2's first paragraph, where it is unclear what trend is seen for fr-en and en-de that isn't present for en-es (NLL/NLPD decrease with warped GPs across datasets). Additionally, including an existing point-estimate model baseline in result tables would help contextualize the overall quality of the proposed models.

The NLL and NLPD values are hard to interpret, and the authors could provide a notion of what constitutes a drastic reduction, e.g., qualitative examples. However, Section 4 is well-written and explains the results intuitively.

Overall, the paper usefully highlights the problems with point estimates and the importance of uncertainty, although it doesn't advance the state-of-the-art or offer major modeling novelty. Minor edits include changing "over and underestimates" to "over- and underestimates." A minor fix is needed for Figure 1's caption, which lists the wrong line colors (blue and green, not blue and red). It would also be helpful to cite the GP toolkit used for modeling, if applicable.

**Main Contributions:**

1. The application of Gaussian processes to machine translation quality estimation, with a focus on modeling uncertainty and providing well-calibrated predictive distributions.
2. The use of Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a metric for evaluating probabilistic models in QE, which can capture information on the whole predictive distribution.
3. The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios and the use of Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses, which can be beneficial in real-world QE applications.

**Strengths:**

1. The paper provides a clear introduction to the problems of QE point estimates and the importance of uncertainty information in real-world applications.
2. The explanation of asymmetric risk scenarios and their implications for different estimators is strong.
3. The use of NLPD as a metric for evaluating probabilistic models in QE is a significant contribution.
4. The exploration of asymmetric risk scenarios and the use of Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses is a valuable contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. The paper doesn't advance the state-of-the-art or offer major modeling novelty.
2. The choice of post-editing rates over HTER prediction in QE research could be justified more clearly.
3. The NLL and NLPD values are hard to interpret, and the authors could provide more qualitative examples to illustrate their significance.
4. The paper could benefit from including an existing point-estimate model baseline in result tables to contextualize the overall quality of the proposed models.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide more justification for choosing post-editing rates over HTER prediction in QE research?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of NLL and NLPD values being hard to interpret, and what qualitative examples can you provide to illustrate their significance?
3. Can you include an existing point-estimate model baseline in result tables to contextualize the overall quality of the proposed models?
4. How do you plan to extend this work to other natural language processing applications, and what potential challenges do you foresee?
