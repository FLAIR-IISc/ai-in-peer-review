Summary and contributions
This paper studies uncertainty-aware machine translation quality estimation (QE) using Gaussian Processes (GPs). It argues that point-estimate metrics (MAE, RMSE, r) are ill-suited for decision-making workflows and proposes evaluating models via the full posterior predictive distribution. The work applies Warped GPs and Matérn kernels, evaluates with Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD), and demonstrates how to use predictive distributions under asymmetric risks via Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses.

Contributions (as I see them), in order of strength:
- A shift in evaluation practice for QE: adopting distribution-aware metrics (NLPD) and showing that they reveal differences in uncertainty modelling invisible to point metrics.
- A decision-theoretic treatment of QE under asymmetric risk (pessimistic/optimistic scenarios), with practical Bayes estimators (quantiles for asymmetric linear loss; closed-form estimator for linex) demonstrating how posterior uncertainty can inform deployment-time trade-offs without retraining.
- An empirical analysis showing that Warped GPs (especially tanh-based warpings) improve calibration (NLL/NLPD) over standard GPs for post-editing time rates, with mixed but informative effects for kernel choice (Matérn vs EQ).

Strengths
- Clear problem motivation and practical framing: The introduction convincingly articulates why uncertainty matters in real QE workflows (e.g., post-editing vs gisting) and why point metrics are insufficient. The asymmetric risk scenarios in Section 4 are concrete and relevant.
- Solid probabilistic methodology and exposition: The paper provides an accessible, accurate introduction to GPs, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs, including decision rules under asymmetric losses. This makes the work reproducible and useful for the QE community.
- Empirical evidence that uncertainty-sensitive evaluation matters: Across datasets, Warped GPs substantially reduce NLL/NLPD relative to standard GPs, while MAE/r often fail to distinguish models, supporting the central claim about evaluation.
- Decision-theoretic results with intuitive explanations: The observed behavior under AL and linex losses, and the discussion on variance effects (e.g., linex pessimistic favoring higher-variance standard GPs), is insightful and practically instructive for deployment choices.
- Careful experimental design: Multiple language pairs, consistent feature set (QuEst 17), and 10-fold CV provide a balanced, controlled setup to isolate the impact of probabilistic modelling choices.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modelling: Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, and NLPD are established in the GP literature; the main novelty is in applying and advocating them for QE rather than introducing a new model or setting a new SOTA on point metrics.
- Missing contextual baselines for point quality: The tables lack a standard strong point-estimate baseline (e.g., SVR/RF) commonly used in WMT QE. Including such baselines would help readers gauge whether better uncertainty comes at a cost in point accuracy.
- Interpretability of NLL/NLPD improvements: The paper states “drastic” reductions but gives no anchor for what constitutes a large/small change. Without qualitative examples (e.g., calibration plots or predictive interval coverage), it is hard to assess practical significance.
- Ambiguity in Section 3.2 narrative: The first paragraph suggests trends for fr-en/en-de that are not observed for en-es, yet warping improves both NLL and NLPD on all datasets. The claim that NLL is unreliable for small datasets requires clearer evidence (e.g., train vs test NLL gaps).
- Metric scope could be broadened: Given the focus on distributional evaluation, additional proper scoring rules (e.g., CRPS) and calibration diagnostics (PIT histograms, coverage of prediction intervals) would strengthen the case and help interpret NLL/NLPD.

Questions to authors
- Why choose post-editing time rates as the target over HTER (more common in QE)? Please justify in terms of availability, noise properties, practical relevance, and how the choice affects modelling/uncertainty.
- Can you quantify the magnitude of NLL/NLPD improvements (e.g., percentage reductions) and provide calibration diagnostics (PIT, interval coverage) to substantiate “drastic” improvements?
- In Section 3.2, please clarify the discrepancy for en-es: what trend do NLL vs NLPD follow, and what specific evidence indicates overfitting? Could you show train/test NLL and NLPD per fold?
- For asymmetric-risk experiments, how sensitive are conclusions to the choice of w? A small sensitivity sweep (beyond the values used) or plots would help practitioners pick w.
- Which GP toolkit/library was used (e.g., GPML, GPy), and are the implementations and settings (warping parameterization, optimizers, restarts) available for reproduction?

Additional comments and suggestions
- Add a standard point-estimate baseline (e.g., SVR with RBF, tuned by CV) to the tables for MAE/r to contextualize absolute performance.
- Provide qualitative or visual calibration evidence: e.g., 80/90% predictive interval coverage, reliability diagrams, or PIT histograms. Consider reporting CRPS alongside NLPD.
- Consider moving some of the GP background to an appendix to streamline the main text for expert readers.
- Minor edits:
  - Replace “over and underestimates” with “over- and underestimates.”
  - Figure 1 caption: line colors appear to be blue and green (not blue and red).
  - Cite the GP toolkit used, if any, and any ancillary libraries used for numerical quadrature.
- Reproducibility details: Include random seeds, optimizer details, convergence criteria, and the exact warping settings (I terms per dataset) to mitigate overfitting concerns on small datasets.

Overall evaluation
A well-written, timely paper that foregrounds uncertainty in QE and demonstrates practical decision-theoretic benefits. While modelling novelty is limited, the evaluation perspective and asymmetric-risk treatment are valuable to the community. Strengthening calibration evidence and adding contextual baselines would further enhance impact.
