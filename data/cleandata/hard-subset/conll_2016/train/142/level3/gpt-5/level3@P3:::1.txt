Summary
This paper studies prediction uncertainty for sentence-level MT Quality Estimation (QE) by modeling post-editing rates with Gaussian Processes (GPs) and evaluating models by their full predictive distributions. It contrasts standard GPs with Matérn kernels and Warped GPs, and uses Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) to assess calibration on test data. The paper further shows how well-calibrated posteriors enable decision-making under asymmetric risks (pessimistic vs optimistic scenarios), deriving Bayes estimators for asymmetric linear and linex losses and demonstrating their impact on post-editing and gisting use cases.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical demonstration that probabilistic QE models (GPs, including Warped GPs) yield better-calibrated predictive distributions than standard point-estimate baselines when evaluated by NLPD, and that calibration matters for downstream asymmetric decision-making.
2) Clear, practical formulation and analysis of asymmetric risk scenarios in QE, including Bayes estimators for AL and linex losses and evidence that uncertainty-aware models can outperform when losses are asymmetric.
3) Careful exposition of GP modeling choices for QE (Matérn kernels, ARD, and warping), with transparent training and evaluation protocols.

Strengths
- Clear motivation and problem framing: The introduction convincingly explains why point estimates are insufficient in real workflows (post-editing, gisting) and why uncertainty is needed. The asymmetric risk argument is especially compelling for practitioners who must choose decision thresholds.
- Strong methodological exposition: The GP overview, kernel discussion (Matérn vs EQ), and Warped GP section are accurate and easy to follow. The warping discussion (median vs mean under different losses) is handled correctly and will help non-experts.
- Proper probabilistic evaluation: Using NLPD to evaluate test-time predictive densities is appropriate and overdue in QE. The paper highlights how MAE and Pearson’s r can miss uncertainty differences that NLPD captures.
- Asymmetric loss analysis is insightful: Section 4 is very well written and translates probabilistic modeling into actionable estimators under realistic operational constraints (ability to change the loss without retraining). The explanation for why linex behaves differently from AL in pessimistic settings is thoughtful and honest about limitations.
- Solid experimental design for the intended goal: Multiple language pairs, 10-fold CV, and a transparent feature set (QuEst 17) keep the focus on uncertainty rather than feature engineering.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and lack of SOTA advancement: The modeling choices (GPs, Matérn, warping) are established; the contribution is primarily in advocating evaluation and scenario design. While valuable, it does not introduce new methods nor advance point-estimate SOTA.
- Missing point-estimate baselines in tables: To contextualize overall quality, results tables should include a strong point-estimate baseline (e.g., SVR with RBF and/or linear L2 regression). This would show whether improved uncertainty comes at the cost of point accuracy or not.
- Interpretability of NLL/NLPD improvements: Reported NLL/NLPD reductions are hard to interpret. The paper would benefit from grounding improvements in intuitive terms (e.g., average likelihood ratio, nats/bits per example, test-set perplexity) and/or qualitative examples where uncertainty would flip a decision under asymmetric loss.
- Clarity issue in Section 3.2 trend discussion: The claim that NLL and NLPD follow similar trends for fr-en and en-de but not en-es is unclear without concrete numbers/plots juxtaposing train NLL and test NLPD. As written, it’s hard to see what trend is present in fr-en/en-de and absent in en-es (especially since warped GPs reduce both across datasets).
- Outcome variable choice needs justification: Post-editing rates (time per token) are reasonable and positive-valued (motivating warping), but HTER is more common in QE. The paper should justify the choice (e.g., stronger operational link to cost/productivity, known issues with HTER noise/annotator variability, data availability), and ideally show whether conclusions transfer to HTER.
- Limited calibration diagnostics: Beyond NLPD, the paper could include PIT histograms, predictive interval coverage (e.g., 50%/90% coverage), or CRPS to strengthen claims of “well-calibrated” uncertainty.

Questions to authors
- Why post-editing rate vs HTER? Please justify in terms of (a) availability/quality of labels, (b) operational relevance (cost and productivity), and (c) how conclusions might transfer to HTER-based QE.
- Section 3.2: Could you quantify and visualize the “similar trends” vs “not observed” claim? For each dataset, show train NLL vs test NLPD (with error bars across folds), and clarify how en-es deviates. Was overfitting diagnosed via gap magnitude, or via variance across folds?
- Can you add a point-estimate baseline in tables (e.g., SVR-RBF)? If you already ran it, how do its MAE/r compare to your GP variants? Does your best Warped GP also match/beat point-estimate baselines on MAE?
- Can you improve interpretability of NLPD changes? For instance, report average predictive likelihood ratios between models, bits-per-sentence, or a few qualitative cases where improved uncertainty changes the asymmetric-loss decision.
- Which software/toolkit was used (e.g., GPy, GPflow, scikit-learn)? Any custom implementation details affecting reproducibility (optimizers, random seeds, restarts, quadrature settings)?
- For the linex pessimistic case, did you examine posterior variance calibration directly (e.g., coverage vs nominal)? Could recalibration (e.g., variance inflation or temperature scaling) mitigate the bias toward high-variance models?

Additional comments
- Editorial: change “over and underestimates” to “over- and underestimates.”
- Figure 1 caption: fix line colors (blue and green, not blue and red).
- Please cite the GP toolkit used, if applicable, and consider releasing code/configs.
- Consider adding calibration diagnostics (coverage, PIT) and predictive interval plots.
- Minor suggestion: report sensitivity to the number of tanh terms in the warping and show learned warping functions for intuition.
- The paper is well structured and accessible; the GP tutorial length is fine for a CONLL audience, though you may consider moving some material to an appendix for space.
