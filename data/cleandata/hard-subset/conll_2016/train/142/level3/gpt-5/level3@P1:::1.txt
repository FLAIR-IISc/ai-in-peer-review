Summary
The paper investigates uncertainty-aware machine translation quality estimation (QE) using Gaussian Processes (GPs). It argues that point-estimate metrics (MAE, RMSE, Pearson’s r) are insufficient for downstream decision-making, and evaluates models using full predictive distributions via negative log predictive density (NLPD). The authors compare standard GPs with Warped GPs and different kernels (EQ vs. Matérn), and illustrate how posterior information supports asymmetric risk scenarios through Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses (asymmetric linear and linex). Experiments on three post-editing time datasets (en–es, fr–en, en–de) with 17 QuEst features and 10-fold CV show that Warped GPs yield substantially better NLL/NLPD, while point metrics barely differentiate models. In asymmetric scenarios, Warped GPs generally help under AL loss; under linex pessimistic settings, standard GPs sometimes fare better due to variance-driven behavior.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) A careful empirical study demonstrating that uncertainty-aware evaluation (NLPD) distinguishes QE models that look similar under point metrics, making a strong case for probabilistic evaluation in QE.
2) Application and assessment of Warped GPs and Matérn kernels for QE, showing that learned warping is beneficial for predictive uncertainty and sometimes for point accuracy.
3) A practical framework for asymmetric decision-making in QE using minimum Bayes risk estimators derived from predictive posteriors, with clear examples (post-editing vs gisting).

Strengths
- Compelling motivation and clear problem framing: The paper convincingly argues why point estimates are inadequate for production QE and why calibrated uncertainty is valuable for human-in-the-loop workflows and automation thresholds.
- Sound probabilistic evaluation: Using NLPD (and contrasting NLL vs NLPD) provides actionable insights. The observation that NLL can mislead on small data (en–es) while NLPD remains informative is particularly useful for practitioners.
- Asymmetric risk analysis is well executed: The translation of realistic workflow preferences into asymmetric losses and Bayes estimators is clear, interpretable, and practical. The explanation of why linex pessimistic settings penalize low-variance models is insightful.
- Methodological clarity and accessibility: The exposition of GPs, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs is crisp and self-contained, helping readers who are less familiar with probabilistic modeling to follow the choices and implications.
- Broad, controlled experiments: Three language pairs, standard features, and consistent 10-fold CV help isolate the effect of probabilistic modeling choices from feature engineering, and the trends are discussed thoughtfully.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling: Warped GPs and Matérn kernels are established; the contribution is primarily empirical and conceptual. Positioning the paper as a study/guideline piece would help set expectations.
- Lack of contextual point-estimate baselines: Including a strong non-probabilistic baseline (e.g., SVR as used in WMT QE tasks, and/or a modern neural baseline) would help readers gauge the absolute quality of predictions and the trade-offs when adopting probabilistic models.
- Interpretability of NLL/NLPD magnitudes: The paper asserts “drastic decreases,” but the practical meaning of the numbers is hard to parse without calibration diagnostics or qualitative illustrations (e.g., coverage of prediction intervals, PIT histograms, reliability curves).
- Clarity issue in Section 3.2: The first paragraph suggests a difference in NLL vs NLPD trends for fr–en and en–de versus en–es, but from the verbal description it’s unclear what exactly diverges across datasets beyond the small-data overfitting claim.
- Target choice not justified: Post-editing rates are used rather than HTER, which is the more common label in QE research. The implications for generality and comparability with prior work are not discussed.

Questions to Authors
- Could you justify the choice of post-editing rates over HTER in terms of availability, stability, correlation with utility, and comparability to prior QE work?
- In Section 3.2, can you precisely state the trend that holds for fr–en/en–de but not en–es? Do you have statistical tests showing NLL vs NLPD divergences and their relation to sample size?
- How should practitioners interpret NLL/NLPD values? Could you provide calibration diagnostics (e.g., PIT, reliability diagrams, interval coverage) or qualitative examples showing practical differences between models with similar MAE but different NLPD?
- Which GP toolkit and settings were used (e.g., library, optimizer, numerical quadrature details, jitter/regularization)? Any code release?
- Could you add or comment on results versus a strong SVR/neural baseline for point predictions to contextualize performance trade-offs?
- For linex pessimistic settings, have you explored variance calibration (e.g., tempering, variance inflation) or alternative w to confirm whether the effect is due to variance magnitude rather than model misspecification?
