Paper studies uncertainty-aware QE with Gaussian Processes, arguing point metrics are insufficient and evaluating full posterior predictive distributions using Warped GPs, Matérn kernels, NLPD, and Bayes estimators for asymmetric losses.

Contribution: advocate distribution-aware evaluation (NLPD) to reveal uncertainty differences unseen by MAE/RMSE/correlation.

Contribution: present a decision-theoretic treatment for asymmetric risk with practical Bayes estimators (quantiles for AL, closed-form for linex) to inform deployment without retraining.

Contribution: empirical evidence that Warped GPs (especially tanh warpings) improve calibration (NLL/NLPD) for post-editing time rates; kernel choice (Matérn vs EQ) has mixed effects.

Strength: clear, practical motivation showing why uncertainty matters in QE workflows and why point metrics are insufficient.

Strength: solid, accessible probabilistic methodology and exposition of GPs, Matérn kernels, Warped GPs, and asymmetric decision rules.

Strength: empirical results demonstrate that uncertainty-sensitive evaluation (NLPD) can distinguish models when point metrics cannot.

Strength: insightful decision-theoretic analysis showing how variance and loss shape deployment choices (e.g., linex favoring higher-variance models under pessimism).

Strength: careful experimental design (multiple language pairs, consistent QuEst17 features, 10-fold CV).

Weakness: limited modelling novelty—techniques (Warped GPs, Matérn, NLPD) are established; novelty is applying them to QE rather than new models.

Weakness: missing standard point-estimate baselines (e.g., SVR/RF) to contextualize MAE/r performance.

Weakness: NLL/NLPD improvements lack anchors or qualitative calibration evidence, making “drastic” claims hard to assess.

Weakness: ambiguous narrative in Section 3.2 about dataset-specific trends (fr-en/en-de vs en-es) and unsupported claim that NLL is unreliable for small datasets.

Weakness: metric scope is narrow; should include other proper scoring rules and calibration diagnostics (CRPS, PIT, interval coverage).

Question: why choose post-editing time rates as the target instead of the more common HTER—justify on availability, noise, relevance, and impact on uncertainty?

Question: can you quantify NLL/NLPD improvements (e.g., percentage reductions) and provide calibration diagnostics (PIT, interval coverage)?

Question: clarify the en-es discrepancy in Section 3.2—what trends do NLL vs NLPD follow, and show train/test NLL/NLPD per fold to evidence overfitting?

Question: how sensitive are asymmetric-risk conclusions to the choice of w—please include a sensitivity sweep or plots for practitioners?

Question: which GP toolkit/library and implementation details were used, and will code/parameters (warping, optimizers, restarts) be released for reproducibility?

Suggestion: add a standard point-estimate baseline (e.g., tuned SVR/RF) to tables to contextualize absolute performance.

Suggestion: provide visual calibration evidence (predictive interval coverage, reliability diagrams, PIT histograms) and consider reporting CRPS alongside NLPD.

Suggestion: move background GP material to an appendix to streamline the main text for expert readers.

Minor edits: fix “over and underestimates” to “over- and underestimates,” correct Figure 1 caption colors, and cite the GP toolkit and quadrature libraries used.

Reproducibility: include random seeds, optimizer settings, convergence criteria, exact warping parameterization, and number of restarts to mitigate overfitting concerns on small datasets.

Overall: well-written, timely paper that usefully foregrounds uncertainty and asymmetric-risk decision rules in QE; limited modelling novelty but valuable evaluation perspective—needs stronger calibration evidence and contextual baselines.
