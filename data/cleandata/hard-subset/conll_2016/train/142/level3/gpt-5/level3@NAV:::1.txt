Summary and contributions
- Primary: The paper demonstrates that uncertainty-aware QE with Gaussian Processes (including warped GPs) yields better-calibrated predictive distributions and more appropriate decision rules in asymmetric risk settings, evaluated via NLPD and Bayes-risk-optimal estimators. This is a useful reframing of QE evaluation beyond point estimates.
- Secondary: It provides a clear, educational exposition of GP choices (Matérn kernels, ARD, warping) tailored to QE, enabling practitioners to reproduce and extend the approach.
- Tertiary: It illustrates how predictive posteriors translate into actionable policies for post-editing and gisting via asymmetric linear and linex losses.

Strengths
- Strong motivation and problem framing: The paper convincingly argues why point estimates are insufficient in real QE workflows and why calibrated uncertainty matters for risk-sensitive decisions.
- Clear methodology: Kernel choices, warping, and training protocol are carefully explained; Section 4’s Bayes estimators connect theory to use cases with commendable clarity.
- Empirical insight: Warped GPs consistently improve NLL/NLPD and often MAE, highlighting that differences in uncertainty quality are not captured by point metrics.
- Practical relevance: The asymmetric loss analysis (especially AL) is compelling and directly actionable for translation pipeline design.
- Writing and structure: Well organized, accessible introduction to GPs for non-specialists, with intuitive discussion of results.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty/SOTA: The modeling choices (GPs, warping, Matérn) are established; the contribution is primarily evaluative and applicative rather than methodological or SOTA-improving.
- Missing baselines: Results lack comparison to a strong point-estimate model (e.g., SVR, recent neural regressors) to contextualize overall accuracy relative to common QE practice.
- Interpretability of NLL/NLPD: The paper does not convey what constitutes a “drastic” reduction; qualitative examples, interval coverage, or calibration plots (e.g., PIT, coverage vs nominal) would aid interpretation.
- Confusion in Section 3.2: The narrative claims a trend for fr-en and en-de not present for en-es, yet both NLL and NLPD decrease with warping in all datasets; please clarify the precise trend (e.g., NLL-NLPD alignment vs overfitting evidence).
- Scope of targets: Please justify choosing post-editing rates over HTER, which is more standard in QE, and discuss implications for applicability.

Questions to authors
- Why prioritize post-editing rates over HTER? How would conclusions change for HTER or other targets?
- Can you add a point-estimate baseline in tables (e.g., SVR with QuEst features) and optionally a recent neural baseline?
- Could you provide calibration diagnostics (interval coverage, reliability, CRPS) and a qualitative example to interpret NLL/NLPD magnitudes?
- Which GP toolkit and settings were used (e.g., GPy/GPflow/Scikit-learn)? Will code be released?
