

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on providing well-calibrated uncertainty estimates rather than just point predictions. The main contributions are: (1) demonstrating that standard point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling across models; (2) establishing NLPD (Negative Log Predictive Density) as a superior evaluation metric for probabilistic QE models that accounts for uncertainty; and (3) showing how predictive distributions can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios common in translation workflows.

Strengths: The paper makes a compelling case for why uncertainty estimation matters in practical QE applications, particularly in post-editing scenarios where decision-making requires knowing both quality predictions and confidence. The asymmetric risk framework is exceptionally well-motivated and clearly explained, with thoughtful examples of pessimistic (high confidence required) and optimistic (higher recall preferred) scenarios. The paper's thorough explanation of GP methodology, including Matern kernels and warping functions, is valuable for readers unfamiliar with probabilistic modeling in NLP. The evaluation using NLPD instead of traditional metrics is well-justified and reveals important insights about model behavior that point estimates miss.

Weaknesses: The paper's main limitation is that it doesn't advance the state-of-the-art in point estimate performance, which is the primary focus of most QE research. The choice of post-editing rates as the target variable instead of the more common HTER metric isn't adequately justified, making it difficult to compare with existing work. The results in Section 3.2 are confusingly presented, particularly regarding the en-es dataset where NLL/NLPD trends differ from other datasets. The paper lacks a comparison to strong point-estimate baselines (like SVR) to contextualize the performance of the proposed probabilistic models. The NLPD values are reported without sufficient interpretative context, making it hard to gauge the practical significance of the observed improvements.

Questions to Authors: Why was post-editing rate chosen over HTER as the target variable, given that most QE research uses HTER? Could the authors provide qualitative examples showing how the uncertainty estimates change the decision-making in asymmetric scenarios? What is the practical significance of the observed NLPD improvements (e.g., how much would the uncertainty estimates actually improve decision-making in a real workflow)? How do the authors explain the inconsistent performance of Matern kernels across different language pairs?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to raising awareness about the importance of uncertainty in QE, but its impact would be strengthened by better contextualization with existing point-estimate approaches and clearer justification for methodological choices. While the GP methodology is well-explained, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the proposed probabilistic models achieve better point estimates than existing methods, which is the primary metric for most QE applications. The asymmetric risk scenario demonstration is strong, but the paper would benefit from showing how these methods could be implemented in a production translation pipeline.

The writing is clear and well-structured, with particularly strong sections on the asymmetric risk framework. The paper successfully demonstrates that uncertainty information is valuable for decision-making, even if it doesn't significantly improve point estimate accuracy. However, the lack of baseline comparisons to current point-estimate models weakens the argument for adopting the proposed approach in practice. The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a comparison to strong point-estimate baselines like SVR or neural network models, which would help readers understand whether the uncertainty benefits come at a significant cost to point estimate quality.

The paper's focus on uncertainty is timely and important, but the lack of connection to the broader QE community's primary metrics (point estimate accuracy) makes it difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed approach. The authors should clarify how their probabilistic models compare to current state-of-the-art point-estimate approaches, as this is the main metric used in the QE literature and by practitioners. Without this context, it's hard to evaluate whether the benefits of uncertainty estimation outweigh the potential costs in point estimate accuracy.
