

# Review for CONLL_2016

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the critical gap in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE) where models traditionally provide only point estimates without uncertainty information, despite the practical importance of uncertainty in real-world translation workflows. The authors make three key contributions: (1) They argue for and demonstrate the importance of using probabilistic models (specifically Gaussian Processes) that provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates rather than point estimates alone; (2) They establish Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) as a superior evaluation metric that captures the full predictive distribution, revealing performance differences missed by traditional point estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r); and (3) They demonstrate how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., post-editing workflows where underestimating quality has higher costs), showing that probabilistic models outperform point estimate approaches in these settings.

## Strengths

1. **Strong motivation for uncertainty modeling in QE**: The paper compellingly demonstrates why uncertainty matters in practical translation workflows through well-structured examples (post-editing and gisting scenarios). The authors clearly explain how point estimates alone are insufficient for decision-making, as they fail to capture the risk associated with quality predictions. This is a significant contribution to the field, as most QE work focuses solely on point estimate accuracy.

2. **Demonstrated value of NLPD as an evaluation metric**: The authors provide clear evidence that NLPD reveals meaningful differences in model performance that traditional metrics miss. For example, they show that different warping functions (log vs. tanh) produce similar point estimate results but significantly different uncertainty profiles, which NLPD captures while MAE and Pearson's r do not. This is a crucial methodological contribution that will impact how future QE work is evaluated.

3. **Compelling application to asymmetric risk scenarios**: The paper's exploration of asymmetric loss functions (AL and linex) and their application to real-world translation scenarios is exceptionally well-executed. The authors clearly explain how probabilistic models can adapt to different risk preferences (pessimistic vs. optimistic) without retraining, and they provide concrete evidence that their models outperform point estimate approaches in these settings. This bridges theoretical probabilistic modeling with practical workflow considerations.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of justification for target variable choice**: The paper uses post-editing rates (time per word) as the target variable without adequately justifying why this is preferable to the more standard HTER (Human Translation Edit Rate) metric used in most QE research. This is a significant methodological choice that requires explanation, as it impacts the interpretability and applicability of the results.

2. **Absence of point-estimate baseline for context**: The results tables lack a comparison to a strong point-estimate baseline (e.g., SVR), making it difficult to gauge the absolute performance of the proposed probabilistic models. Without this baseline, it's unclear whether the improvements in uncertainty modeling come at the cost of reduced point estimate accuracy.

3. **Insufficient interpretation of NLPD values**: The paper presents NLPD values without context for interpretation. It's challenging to understand what constitutes a "good" or "bad" NLPD score in this domain, or how much improvement is significant. Providing qualitative examples of uncertainty profiles or comparisons to baseline models would substantially strengthen this contribution.

4. **Limited state-of-the-art contribution**: While the paper makes important methodological contributions to how QE should be evaluated and applied, it does not advance the state-of-the-art in QE performance. The focus on uncertainty modeling rather than improving point estimate accuracy means the paper is more about methodology than results, which may limit its impact for some readers.

## Questions to Authors

1. Why was post-editing time (normalized by sentence length) chosen as the target variable instead of HTER, which is the more common metric in QE research? How do your findings generalize to other QE evaluation metrics?

2. Could you include a strong point-estimate baseline (e.g., SVR) in your results tables to provide context for the performance of your probabilistic models? This would help readers understand whether the uncertainty improvements come at the cost of reduced point estimate accuracy.

3. Could you provide additional context for interpreting NLPD values? What constitutes a meaningful improvement in NLPD for QE tasks, and how do your results compare to previous work using this metric?
