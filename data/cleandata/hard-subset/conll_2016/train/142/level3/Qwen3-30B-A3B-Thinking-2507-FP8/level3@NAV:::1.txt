

This paper investigates the application of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for quality estimation in machine translation, focusing on providing well-calibrated uncertainty estimates rather than just point predictions. The main contributions are: (1) highlighting the limitations of point estimates in real-world translation workflows and demonstrating the value of uncertainty-aware models; (2) systematically evaluating GP-based methods with appropriate probabilistic metrics (NLL, NLPD) that capture uncertainty information; and (3) showing how predictive distributions can improve decision-making in asymmetric risk scenarios common in translation post-editing.

Strengths include a compelling motivation for uncertainty estimation in QE, clear explanations of asymmetric risk scenarios with practical examples, and well-structured technical exposition of GP methodology that balances accessibility with rigor. The paper's section on asymmetric risk (Section 4) is particularly strong, providing intuitive explanations of how uncertainty information can improve real-world translation workflows.

Key weaknesses include insufficient justification for using post-editing rates instead of the more common HTER metric in QE research, which weakens the paper's applicability to standard QE evaluation practices. The explanation in Section 3.2 regarding inconsistent trends across language pairs (fr-en, en-de vs. en-es) is confusing and requires clarification. Additionally, the paper lacks a comparison to existing point-estimate baselines, making it difficult to contextualize the overall quality of the proposed models. The NLPD values are reported without interpretation guidelines, making it challenging to assess the practical significance of the improvements.

The paper is well-written but doesn't advance the state-of-the-art in modeling or achieve significant performance gains. The minor errors noted (e.g., "over and underestimates" instead of "over- and underestimates") and Figure 1 caption discrepancy should be corrected.

Questions: Please clarify why post-editing rates were chosen over HTER, and provide qualitative examples to contextualize the NLPD improvements. Also, explain the inconsistent trend in Section 3.2 regarding the en-es dataset.
