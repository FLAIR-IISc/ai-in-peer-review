

# Review of "Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), emphasizing the importance of uncertainty estimation in real-world translation workflows. The authors argue that traditional QE models, which provide only point estimates, are insufficient for decision-making scenarios where uncertainty matters. The primary contributions are: (1) a clear demonstration of how uncertainty estimation can improve decision-making in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., post-editing vs. gisting), (2) a thorough evaluation of GP modeling choices (kernels, warping) for QE using uncertainty-aware metrics (NLL, NLPD), and (3) a compelling argument for why uncertainty-aware metrics should replace traditional point-estimate metrics in QE evaluation.

## Strengths
1. **Strong motivation for uncertainty estimation**: The paper's introduction effectively highlights the limitations of point estimates in real-world translation workflows, particularly in asymmetric risk scenarios where overestimating quality (allowing poor MT to proceed to post-editing) can have serious consequences. This practical motivation makes the work highly relevant to the QE community.

2. **Clear explanation of GP methodology**: The paper provides a well-structured introduction to Gaussian Processes, making the methodology accessible to general readers without sacrificing technical depth. The explanations of Matèrn kernels and warped GPs are particularly thorough and well-motivated for the QE task.

3. **Compelling demonstration of asymmetric risk applications**: Section 4 provides an excellent, intuitive explanation of how predictive uncertainty can be leveraged in different translation scenarios (pessimistic vs. optimistic settings). The use of Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric losses is well-explained and demonstrates practical utility.

4. **Strong emphasis on appropriate evaluation metrics**: The paper makes a convincing case that NLPD should be preferred over traditional point-estimate metrics (MAE, Pearson's r) when evaluating models with uncertainty estimates. The experiments show that point-estimate metrics fail to capture meaningful differences in uncertainty modeling.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of justification for post-editing rates vs. HTER**: The paper uses post-editing rates as the target variable but doesn't adequately justify this choice over the more common HTER (Human Translation Error Rate) prediction in QE research. This omission weakens the paper's applicability to broader QE research.

2. **Confusing trend in Section 3.2**: The paper states that "for fr-en & en-de, NLL/NLPD decrease with warped GPs across datasets" but then claims this trend "isn't present for en-es" in the same paragraph. This contradicts the data presented in Table 1, where all three datasets show similar trends with warped GPs. The explanation for the en-es dataset's behavior (smaller size leading to overfitting) is not convincing given the results.

3. **Missing point-estimate baseline**: The paper doesn't include a standard point-estimate model (like SVR or the WMT baselines) in the result tables, making it difficult to contextualize the overall quality of the GP models. This is particularly important since the paper claims to evaluate "how different modeling aspects should be taken into account" for QE.

4. **Poor interpretation of NLL/NLPD values**: The paper presents NLL/NLPD values without providing any sense of what constitutes a "good" or "drastic" reduction. Without qualitative examples or comparison to baseline values, these metrics remain difficult to interpret.

5. **Lack of novel modeling contributions**: While the paper makes a strong case for uncertainty estimation in QE, it doesn't offer major modeling innovations. The GP methodology used is largely standard from the literature, and the improvements are incremental rather than groundbreaking.

## Questions to Authors
1. Could you clarify why post-editing rates were chosen over HTER as the target variable, given that HTER is the standard metric in most QE research?

2. In Section 3.2, the paper claims that the trend of decreasing NLL/NLPD with warped GPs "isn't present for en-es" when the table shows similar trends across all datasets. Could you clarify this apparent contradiction?

3. Could you include a standard point-estimate model (e.g., SVR) in the result tables to provide context for the GP model performance?

## Minor Corrections
- Change "over and underestimates" to "over- and underestimates" in the abstract.
- Fix Figure 1 caption, which incorrectly lists line colors as "blue and red" instead of "blue and green".
- Consider citing the GP toolkit used for modeling (e.g., GPflow, GPy) for reproducibility.

## Overall Assessment
This is a well-written, well-structured paper that makes a valuable contribution to raising awareness about the importance of uncertainty estimation in QE. While it doesn't advance the state-of-the-art in modeling, it successfully demonstrates how existing probabilistic methods can be applied to address real-world limitations of current QE approaches. The paper's strongest contribution is its clear articulation of how uncertainty can improve decision-making in translation workflows, which should resonate with practitioners. With minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, this paper would make a strong contribution to the field.
