### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The central argument is that traditional point estimates (e.g., a single quality score) are insufficient for real-world applications where decision-making depends on the model's confidence. The authors explore several GP variants, including Warped GPs and models with Matèrn kernels, to better model the distribution of quality scores. They advocate for evaluation metrics like Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) that account for the entire predictive distribution, not just a single point. Finally, the paper demonstrates the practical utility of these uncertainty-aware predictions in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., when underestimating quality is more costly than overestimating it), showing how optimal estimators can be derived without retraining the model.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  A clear and well-executed empirical study on the application of different GP modeling choices (warping functions, kernels) to QE. The paper effectively demonstrates that standard point-estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson's r fail to capture important differences in uncertainty modeling, whereas probabilistic metrics like NLPD can distinguish between models with similar point-estimate performance.
2.  A compelling demonstration of how predictive distributions from a single probabilistic model can be used to make optimal decisions under various asymmetric loss functions. This highlights a significant practical advantage of the probabilistic approach for real-world QE deployment, where the costs of prediction errors are often unequal.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Framing:** The paper's strongest aspect is its clear motivation. It successfully argues that for QE to be truly useful in production environments (e.g., post-editing workflows), predictions must be accompanied by reliable uncertainty estimates. The framing of the problem moves beyond simply improving state-of-the-art point predictions and instead focuses on making QE models more practical and informative for downstream decision-making.

2.  **Clear Methodological Exposition:** The paper provides a thorough and accessible introduction to Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs. While potentially lengthy for experts, this clarity makes the work understandable to a broader NLP audience and ensures the experimental choices are well-grounded and easy to follow.

3.  **Strong and Intuitive Application Scenario:** The investigation into asymmetric risk scenarios in Section 4 is particularly well-conceived and executed. The distinction between "pessimistic" and "optimistic" settings is intuitive and maps directly to real-world use cases. The method of deriving Bayes risk estimators without model retraining is elegant and powerfully illustrates the flexibility and practical benefit of adopting a fully probabilistic framework.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Modeling Novelty:** The primary weakness is that the paper does not introduce novel machine learning methods. The techniques explored—Warped GPs, Matèrn kernels, and NLPD evaluation—are well-established within the machine learning literature. The contribution lies in their thoughtful application and analysis within the QE domain, rather than in advancing the state of the art of probabilistic modeling itself.

2.  **Lack of Contextualizing Baselines:** The experiments compare different GP variants to one another but lack a comparison to a strong, non-probabilistic baseline, such as the Support Vector Regression (SVR) model mentioned in the introduction as a popular method for QE. Including an SVR baseline in the point-estimate tables (Table 1) would help contextualize the performance of the GP models. Without it, it is difficult to assess whether the GP models' point-estimate accuracy is competitive with standard approaches, which is an important consideration for practitioners.

3.  **Interpretation of Uncertainty Metrics:** While the use of NLL and NLPD is well-justified, the paper asserts that Warped GPs lead to a "drastic" decrease in these metrics without providing much intuition for what constitutes a significant improvement. The absolute values of NLL/NLPD are not easily interpretable. The argument would be much stronger with some qualitative analysis, such as showing a few concrete examples where a model with a better NLPD score correctly assigns high uncertainty to an incorrect prediction, while a model with a poorer score is overconfident.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  In Section 3.2, you state that for the fr-en and en-de datasets, "NLL and NLPD follow similar trends," but this is "not observed in the en-es dataset," which you attribute to overfitting. However, looking at Table 1, the general trend—that Warped GPs significantly reduce both NLL and NLPD compared to the standard GP—appears to hold across all three datasets. Could you please clarify which specific trend you are referring to that distinguishes the en-es dataset from the others?

2.  Your experiments focus on predicting post-editing rates (normalized time), which is a valid and useful quality metric. However, a significant portion of the QE literature is focused on predicting HTER. Could you briefly comment on your choice to use post-editing time and discuss whether you believe your core findings about the importance of uncertainty modeling and asymmetric risk would generalize to the task of HTER prediction?
