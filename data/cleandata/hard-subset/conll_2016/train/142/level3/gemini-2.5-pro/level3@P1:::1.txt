This paper explores the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The central argument is that standard QE models, which provide only point estimates of quality, are insufficient for real-world applications where decision-making requires an understanding of predictive uncertainty. The authors investigate different GP configurations, including Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs, to better model the distribution of quality scores. They advocate for evaluation using the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) metric, which considers the full predictive distribution, rather than just point-estimate metrics like MAE. Furthermore, the paper demonstrates the utility of these probabilistic predictions in asymmetric risk scenarios, where over- or under-estimation of quality carry different costs, by deriving Bayes risk estimators for different loss functions.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  A compelling reframing of the QE task from one of pure predictive accuracy to one of probabilistic modeling and risk management. The paper provides a clear and practical demonstration of how predictive uncertainty can be used in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., pessimistic post-editing filtering vs. optimistic content gisting), which are highly relevant to real-world MT workflows.
2.  An empirical comparison of different GP models for QE, evaluated using a probabilistically sound metric (NLPD). This analysis effectively shows the limitations of standard point-estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson's r, which fail to capture important differences in how well models represent uncertainty.

### Strengths

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Framing:** The paper is exceptionally well-structured and motivated. The introduction clearly articulates the shortcomings of point estimates in practical QE applications and makes a strong case for the importance of uncertainty. This framing elevates the paper from a simple application of GPs to a thoughtful discussion on how QE models should be built and evaluated for real-world use.
2.  **Thorough and Insightful Analysis of Asymmetric Risk:** Section 4 is a standout part of the paper. The explanation of pessimistic and optimistic scenarios is intuitive and grounded in realistic use cases. The authors go beyond simply identifying the problem by applying principled solutions from decision theory, using Bayes risk estimators for asymmetric loss functions (AL and linex). This demonstrates a practical and flexible way to leverage a single probabilistic model for different, changing decision-making needs without costly retraining.
3.  **Clarity of Methodological Exposition:** The paper provides a clear and self-contained introduction to Gaussian Processes, Matèrn kernels, and Warped GPs. While potentially lengthy for experts in this specific modeling technique, this detailed explanation makes the work highly accessible to the broader NLP and MT community, who may be less familiar with these methods. The motivation for each modeling choice (e.g., Matèrn kernels for noisy data, Warping for non-Gaussian quality scores) is well-justified.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty and SOTA Comparison:** While the perspective is valuable, the paper's core methodology applies existing machine learning techniques (Warped GPs, NLPD evaluation, asymmetric risk estimators) to the QE task. The work does not propose novel modeling architectures or significantly advance the state-of-the-art in terms of predictive performance on standard QE benchmarks. The contribution lies more in the problem formulation and evaluation perspective rather than in a new technical method.
2.  **Lack of Contextualizing Baselines and Results:** The experiments compare different configurations of GP models against each other but lack a strong, non-probabilistic baseline in the result tables. The paper mentions that Support Vector Regression (SVR) is a popular and competitive method for QE, yet no SVR results are included. Adding a standard SVR baseline evaluated with MAE and Pearson's r would help contextualize the point-estimate performance of the GP models. Furthermore, the absolute values of NLL and NLPD are difficult for a reader to interpret. It is unclear what constitutes a "drastic" improvement. Providing qualitative examples of sentences where the best models assign much higher likelihood (lower NLPD) would greatly improve the intuition behind these results.
3.  **Unclear Interpretation of NLL/NLPD Trends:** The first paragraph of Section 3.2 contains a confusing statement. It claims that for the fr-en and en-de datasets, "NLL and NLPD follow similar trends," but "this is not observed in the en-es dataset." However, looking at Table 1, the trend of Warped GPs (e.g., M52+log) significantly improving over the standard GP baseline holds for both NLL and NLPD across all three datasets. The subtle difference in ranking between different warping functions for en-es does not seem to warrant the strong claim that the overall trend is "not observed." This point needs clarification.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The experiments predict post-editing rates (normalized post-editing time). While this is a valid quality metric, much of the recent QE literature has focused on predicting HTER (Human-targeted Translation Edit Rate). Could you justify the choice of post-editing rates over HTER and comment on whether you expect your conclusions regarding uncertainty and asymmetric risk to hold for HTER prediction as well?
2.  Could you please elaborate on the statement in Section 3.2 that "NLL and NLPD follow similar trends" for fr-en and en-de, but not for en-es? The primary trend—that Warped GPs drastically outperform standard GPs—seems consistent across all datasets according to Table 1. What specific divergence in trends for the en-es dataset are you highlighting?

### Additional Comments

- Minor typo in Section 4: "over and underestimates" should be hyphenated as "over- and underestimates."
- The caption for Figure 1 appears to have an error. It refers to a red line for the pessimistic scenario, but the line in the figure is green.
- For reproducibility, it would be helpful to mention which software package or library was used to implement the Gaussian Process models.
