### Summary of the Paper
This paper investigates the use of probabilistic models, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE). The authors argue that traditional QE models, which provide only point estimates of quality (e.g., post-editing time), are of limited use in real-world translation workflows where decision-making involves risk. The paper advocates for models that also provide uncertainty estimates. The authors explore different GP configurations, including various kernels (EQ, Matérn) and warped GPs to better model non-Gaussian, strictly positive quality scores. They evaluate these models not only with standard metrics like MAE but also with probabilistic metrics like Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) that account for the entire predictive distribution. Finally, they demonstrate the utility of these uncertainty-aware predictions in asymmetric risk scenarios (e.g., being pessimistic or optimistic about MT quality), showing how Bayes risk estimators can be derived for different loss functions without retraining the model.

### Contributions
1.  **Highlighting the importance of uncertainty for QE:** The primary contribution is a well-argued case for shifting the focus of QE from point prediction accuracy to probabilistic modeling that captures uncertainty. The paper effectively demonstrates the limitations of point estimates for practical decision-making and introduces metrics (NLPD) and application scenarios (asymmetric risk) that are better aligned with real-world needs.
2.  **Application and analysis of probabilistic models in asymmetric risk scenarios:** The paper provides a strong practical demonstration of how predictive distributions from a single GP model can be used to derive optimal estimators for various asymmetric loss functions (e.g., pessimistic or optimistic scenarios in post-editing). This shows a flexible and efficient way to adapt a QE system to changing business requirements without costly retraining.
3.  **An empirical comparison of GP configurations for QE:** The work provides a useful empirical study of different GP modeling choices (Matérn kernels, warped likelihoods) for the task of predicting post-editing rates. It shows that warped GPs significantly improve probabilistic model fit (as measured by NLPD) compared to standard GPs, a finding not always captured by point-estimate metrics like MAE or Pearson's r.

### Strengths
1.  **Excellent Motivation and Framing:** The paper is exceptionally well-structured and motivated. The introduction clearly outlines the practical shortcomings of point estimates in QE and makes a compelling case for the necessity of uncertainty quantification. This framing is a significant strength, as it addresses a crucial gap between academic QE research and its real-world application.
2.  **Clear Methodological Explanation:** The paper provides a substantial and very clear introduction to Gaussian Processes, Matérn kernels, and Warped GPs. While potentially lengthy for GP experts, this detailed explanation makes the work highly accessible to the broader NLP/MT community, which is the primary audience. The methodological choices are well-justified and easy to follow.
3.  **Insightful Analysis of Asymmetric Risk:** Section 4 is a highlight of the paper. It provides an intuitive explanation of asymmetric risk scenarios and demonstrates elegantly how probabilistic outputs can be leveraged to make optimal decisions under different loss functions. The analysis of the results is insightful, particularly the discussion on why certain models perform differently under AL and linex losses.
4.  **Well-Posed Research Questions:** The paper is guided by clear and interesting research questions concerning the evaluation of probabilistic models and their utility in practical scenarios. This focus helps maintain the clarity and impact of the work.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Lack of a Competitive Point-Estimate Baseline:** The experiments compare different GP variants against a standard GP but omit a comparison to a strong, non-probabilistic baseline like Support Vector Regression (SVR), which the authors themselves note is a popular method for QE. Including an SVR baseline in the result tables (for MAE and Pearson's r) would provide crucial context and help readers gauge how the GP models' point prediction accuracy compares to established methods.
2.  **Interpretability of Probabilistic Metrics:** The core of the intrinsic evaluation relies on NLL and NLPD. While these are standard metrics in the ML community, their absolute values are not easily interpretable for a general audience. The paper would be strengthened by providing some intuition for what constitutes a significant improvement. For instance, qualitative examples showing predictions (mean and variance) for a few sentences where a warped GP drastically reduces NLPD compared to a standard GP would make the benefits much more tangible.
3.  **Limited Novelty in Modeling:** The paper's contribution lies in the application and thoughtful evaluation of existing methods rather than in proposing novel modeling techniques. The GP models, kernels, and warping functions are all established in the machine learning literature. While this does not diminish the paper's value to the QE community, its novelty from a pure ML perspective is limited.

### Questions to Authors
1.  The work predicts post-editing *rates* (time normalized by MT sentence length). Much of the QE literature, particularly in the WMT shared tasks, has focused on predicting HTER (Human-targeted Translation Edit Rate). Could you justify the choice of post-editing rates over HTER and comment on whether you expect the findings regarding uncertainty and asymmetric risk to hold for HTER prediction as well?
2.  In the first paragraph of Section 3.2, you state: "For the fr-en and en-de datasets, NLL and NLPD follow similar trends... However, this is not observed in the en-es dataset." Looking at Table 1, it appears that for all three datasets, moving from a standard GP to a Warped GP (either log or tanh) results in a substantial decrease in both NLL and NLPD. Could you please clarify what specific trend you are referring to that distinguishes en-es from the other two datasets?

### Additional Comments (Minor Typos/Suggestions)
-   In the first paragraph of Section 4, "over and underestimates" could be hyphenated for clarity: "over- and underestimates."
-   In the caption for Figure 1, the text refers to blue and red lines, but the plot appears to use blue and green lines.
-   It would be helpful to cite the specific software toolkit or library used for the GP modeling (e.g., GPy, GPflow), as this aids reproducibility.
