**Summary of the paper and main contributions**
This paper investigates the application of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), arguing for a shift from traditional point estimates to probabilistic predictions that model uncertainty. The authors evaluate several GP configurations, including standard, Matérn, and Warped GPs, using metrics like Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD) that assess the full predictive distribution. They further demonstrate the utility of these uncertainty estimates in asymmetric risk scenarios relevant to real-world translation workflows.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A clear and compelling demonstration of the limitations of point-estimate evaluation in QE. The paper effectively highlights the practical importance of modeling and evaluating predictive uncertainty, particularly for decision-making in asymmetric risk scenarios where over- and underestimates carry different costs.
2.  A thorough empirical analysis comparing different GP models, which shows that extensions like Warped GPs provide better-calibrated uncertainty for predicting post-editing rates. This is an important finding not fully captured by standard metrics like MAE.

**Strengths**
1.  The paper’s primary strength is its excellent motivation for considering uncertainty in QE. The discussion of asymmetric risk scenarios is particularly strong, providing a concrete and practical grounding for why probabilistic predictions are more useful than point estimates in real-world applications like managing post-editing workflows.
2.  The methodological exposition is exceptionally clear and accessible. The background on Gaussian Processes, kernel choices, and warping is detailed enough to make the work understandable to a general NLP audience, broadening its potential impact.
3.  The experimental design effectively supports the paper's thesis. The use of NLPD provides convincing empirical evidence that standard metrics fail to capture significant improvements in model calibration, justifying the call for new evaluation practices in QE.

**Weaknesses**
1.  The experimental results lack crucial context due to the absence of a strong, non-probabilistic baseline. While the focus is on comparing different GP models, including a standard point-estimate model like Support Vector Regression (SVR) would have helped contextualize the overall performance and clarify any trade-offs between point-estimate accuracy and uncertainty quantification.
2.  The core evaluation metrics for uncertainty, NLL and NLPD, are difficult for the reader to interpret. The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing a notion of what constitutes a meaningful improvement in these scores, for instance, by linking NLPD reductions to qualitative examples of better-calibrated predictions.
3.  The work’s modeling contribution is limited. The paper applies existing, well-established GP techniques to the QE domain. While the application and analysis are valuable, the work does not propose novel methodology, positioning its contribution as one of problem framing and analysis rather than a technical advance.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you justify the choice to predict post-editing rates rather than the more common QE target of HTER? A brief explanation would help situate your results within the broader QE literature.
2.  In Section 3.2, you mention a difference in the NLL/NLPD trend for the en-es dataset compared to fr-en and en-de. However, the results in Table 1 seem to show a consistent reduction in both metrics with Warped GPs across all datasets. Could you please elaborate on the differing trend you observed?
