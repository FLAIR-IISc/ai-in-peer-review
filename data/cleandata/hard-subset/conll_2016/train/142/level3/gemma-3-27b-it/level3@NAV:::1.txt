## Review: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

This paper investigates the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for modeling prediction uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), moving beyond traditional point estimate evaluations. The authors correctly identify the limitations of point estimates in real-world scenarios, particularly when risk is asymmetric, and propose leveraging the full posterior predictive distributions offered by GPs. The paper is well-structured and clearly motivated, with a strong emphasis on the practical implications of uncertainty-aware QE. The introduction effectively frames the problem and justifies the focus on GPs, and the explanation of GP methodology, kernel choices, and warping functions is thorough and easy to follow, even for those less familiar with the technique. The research questions are well-phrased and address a significant gap in QE research.

The primary contribution of this work lies in highlighting the importance of considering uncertainty in QE, and demonstrating how this can be achieved using GPs within an asymmetric risk framework. While GPs have been previously used in QE, this work provides a detailed exploration of their utility for capturing uncertainty and provides a compelling argument for moving beyond purely point-estimate-based evaluations. A second, related contribution is the empirical analysis demonstrating that standard point estimate metrics fail to capture the nuances in uncertainty modeling achieved through different GP configurations (kernels and warping functions). Finally, the application of asymmetric loss functions, though established in other fields, is a valuable extension to QE, showing how uncertainty can be directly leveraged for improved decision-making in practical scenarios.

However, the paper does not significantly advance the state-of-the-art in QE in terms of raw prediction accuracy. The method itself lacks substantial novelty, building on established GP techniques. A key weakness is the justification for focusing on post-editing rates as the target variable. Why were these preferred over the more commonly used and widely comparable HTER predictions in the QE literature? This choice impacts the generalizability of the findings. Additionally, Section 3.2 contains a confusing statement regarding the NLPD/NLL trends for fr-en and en-de which differ from en-es; clarification is needed here. The results tables would also benefit from the inclusion of a baseline point-estimate model (e.g., SVR) to provide context for the observed improvements. The NLL and NLPD values are difficult to interpret without a sense of scale; providing qualitative examples of what constitutes a “drastic” reduction would be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Please justify the choice of post-editing rates over HTER as the target variable for QE.
2.  Could you clarify the differing trends observed in Section 3.2 for fr-en/en-de versus en-es regarding NLPD/NLL with warped GPs? What might explain this discrepancy?
3.  Could you provide qualitative examples to help interpret the magnitude of the NLL/NLPD improvements observed in your experiments?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please cite the specific GP toolkit used for modeling.
*   Correct the figure caption for Figure 1 – the line colors are blue and green, not blue and red.
*   Change “over and underestimates” to “over- and underestimates” throughout the paper.
