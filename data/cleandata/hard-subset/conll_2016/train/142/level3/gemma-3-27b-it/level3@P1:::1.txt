This paper explores the use of Gaussian Processes (GPs) for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on the importance of modeling prediction uncertainty, a crucial aspect often overlooked in traditional QE evaluation. The authors motivate their work by highlighting the limitations of point estimate metrics in real-world translation workflows, particularly in scenarios involving human post-editing. They propose leveraging well-calibrated probabilistic predictions from GPs, specifically examining kernel choices and warping functions, and demonstrate their utility in asymmetric risk scenarios. 

The primary contribution of this work is a strong argument for moving beyond point-estimate evaluation in QE and towards assessing the quality of probabilistic predictions via metrics like Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD). The authors convincingly demonstrate that differences in uncertainty modeling are not captured by standard metrics like MAE and Pearson’s r.  A second notable contribution lies in the exploration and comparison of different GP extensions – specifically Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs – for improving QE performance and calibration. Finally, the paper’s thorough analysis of asymmetric risk scenarios and demonstration of how to derive Bayes risk estimators from probabilistic predictions provides a practical application of the proposed methodology and highlights its potential benefits.

**Strengths:**

1. **Compelling Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper effectively articulates the limitations of conventional QE evaluation methods that rely solely on point estimates. By focusing on the practical implications for real-world translation workflows – particularly those involving human-in-the-loop post-editing – the authors provide a strong justification for their research. This is a significant contribution as it shifts the focus towards a more user-centric and decision-aware approach to QE.

2. **Thorough Exploration of Gaussian Processes:** The authors provide a comprehensive and accessible overview of GPs, including relevant kernel choices (Matèrn kernels) and extensions (Warped GPs). This detailed exposition is particularly valuable for readers unfamiliar with the GP framework and enables a solid understanding of the methodological foundation of the work. The clear explanations of concepts like Automatic Relevance Determination (ARD) and the rationale behind choosing different warping functions demonstrate a deep understanding of the underlying theory.

3. **Rigorous Evaluation and Insightful Analysis:** The experimental setup, utilizing datasets from multiple language pairs and a standard feature set, is well-defined.  The authors go beyond simply reporting results, providing a nuanced discussion of the findings.  The observation that standard point-estimate metrics fail to capture differences in uncertainty modeling, as quantified by NLPD, is particularly impactful. The analysis of asymmetric risk scenarios and the derivation of Bayes risk estimators further strengthens the practical relevance of the work.

4. **Clear Presentation and Well-Structured Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and logically organized. The sections flow seamlessly, and the explanations are clear and concise. The inclusion of a detailed discussion of related work demonstrates a strong awareness of the existing literature. The illustrative examples and clear explanations of complex concepts enhance the overall readability and accessibility of the paper.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Core Methodology:** While the application of GPs to QE is not entirely novel, the core methodological contributions – exploring different kernels and warping functions within the GP framework – are incremental rather than groundbreaking. The paper doesn't propose a fundamentally new GP-based QE model, but rather a systematic investigation of existing techniques.

2. **Interpretability of Evaluation Metrics:**  The reported NLL and NLPD values are difficult to interpret without a clear benchmark or point of reference. Providing a qualitative sense of what constitutes a “drastic reduction” in these metrics, for example by comparing them to baseline models or illustrating the impact on specific predictions, would enhance the understanding of the results.

3. **Choice of Response Variable Justification:** The paper justifies the use of post-editing *rates* (normalized post-editing time) rather than the more commonly used Human-targeted Translation Edit Rate (HTER) in QE research. However, the rationale for this choice could be more thoroughly explained. A discussion of the potential trade-offs between these two response variables would strengthen the paper. 

4. **Potential Bias in Asymmetric Risk Analysis:**  The analysis in Section 4 regarding asymmetric risk scenarios reveals that the linex loss, in some cases, performs *worse* than standard GPs. The authors speculate that this may be due to the warped models having lower variance. Further investigation is needed to confirm this hypothesis and potentially refine the analysis to avoid biases related to variance.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you elaborate on the rationale for choosing post-editing rates over HTER as the response variable, and discuss the potential impact of this choice on the results and generalizability of the findings?
2. Could you provide a more intuitive interpretation of the NLL and NLPD values? For example, could you illustrate the practical implications of a specific reduction in these metrics, perhaps by showing the corresponding improvement in post-editing time or quality?
3. You mention the potential for the lower variance of warped GPs to negatively impact performance with the linex loss. Could you explore this further by, for example, investigating the relationship between model variance and performance in the asymmetric risk scenarios?

**Additional Comments:**

- In Section 3.2, the first paragraph is somewhat confusing. The discussion of the trend observed for fr-en & en-de not being present for en-es requires clarification.
- Please include a baseline model employing a standard QE approach (e.g., SVR) in the result tables to provide context for the performance of the proposed GP-based models.
- Consider correcting the figure caption for Figure 1, as the line colors are incorrectly described.
- If applicable, please cite the specific GP toolkit or library used for the implementation of the models.
- Change “over and underestimates” to “over- and underestimates” throughout the paper.



Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of Machine Translation Quality Estimation. While the methodological novelty may be incremental, the paper’s strong motivation, thorough analysis, and clear presentation make it a worthwhile read and a solid contribution to the workshop proceedings.
