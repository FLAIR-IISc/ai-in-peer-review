This paper explores the application of Gaussian Processes (GPs) to regress post-editing time/rates for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), with a focus on modeling and utilizing prediction uncertainty. The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates the limitations of relying solely on point estimates in real-world QE applications, particularly in scenarios with asymmetric risk. The introduction effectively motivates the need for probabilistic QE models.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Emphasis on Uncertainty Modeling in QE:** The primary contribution lies in highlighting the importance of going beyond point estimates in QE and advocating for the use of probabilistic models that provide well-calibrated uncertainty estimates. This is a crucial point for practical application of QE systems.
2. **Thorough Exploration of GPs for QE:** The paper provides a comprehensive overview of GPs, including kernel choices (Matèrn kernels) and warping techniques, tailored for the QE task. The explanations are clear and accessible, making the methodology easy to follow, even for readers less familiar with GPs.
3. **Demonstration of Asymmetric Risk Application:** The paper effectively demonstrates how the posterior distributions generated by GPs can be utilized in asymmetric risk scenarios (pessimistic and optimistic) using asymmetric loss functions, showcasing a practical application of the uncertainty estimates.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Motivation and Problem Statement:** The paper clearly articulates the problem with relying on point estimates in QE, especially within the context of real-world translation workflows. The discussion of asymmetric risk scenarios is particularly strong and convincingly illustrates the need for uncertainty quantification.
2. **Comprehensive GP Explanation:** The paper provides a thorough introduction to GPs, suitable for a broad audience. While potentially lengthy for GP experts, it is a valuable resource for those unfamiliar with the methodology. The explanations of kernel choices and warping are exceptionally clear.
3. **Well-Written Results Section:** Section 4 is particularly well-written and presents the results intuitively. The discussion connects the modeling choices to the observed performance in the asymmetric risk scenarios effectively. The use of examples to illustrate the benefits of uncertainty modeling is helpful.
4. **Sound Experimental Design:** The use of multiple datasets with varying characteristics strengthens the findings. The 10-fold cross-validation protocol provides robust performance estimates.
5. **Thoughtful Discussion of Limitations:** The authors acknowledge the limitations of relying on point-estimate metrics like MAE and Pearson’s r when evaluating uncertainty modeling approaches.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Justification for Post-Editing Rates:** The paper would benefit from a stronger justification for choosing post-editing rates as the target variable instead of the more commonly used HTER prediction in QE research. While post-editing time is relevant, a clear explanation of *why* it’s a more appropriate target for *this* investigation is missing.
2. **Unclear Trend in Section 3.2:** The observation in Section 3.2 regarding the difference in NLL/NLPD trends between fr-en & en-de versus en-es is unclear. The explanation attributing this to overfitting in the smaller en-es dataset feels somewhat speculative and requires further elaboration. A more detailed analysis of this discrepancy would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Baseline Comparison:** The results tables would be significantly more informative with the inclusion of a baseline point-estimate model (e.g., SVR) to contextualize the overall quality of the GP models. This would provide a clear benchmark for assessing the improvements achieved by incorporating uncertainty modeling.
4. **Difficulty Interpreting NLL and NLPD:** The NLL and NLPD values are difficult to interpret in isolation. Providing a qualitative sense of what constitutes a drastic reduction in these metrics – e.g., by relating them to improvements in post-editing time or human evaluation scores – would enhance the understanding of the results.
5. **Linex Loss Results:** The inconclusive results from the linex loss in pessimistic scenarios suggest a potential issue with the loss function or its interaction with the predictive distributions. This requires further investigation.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed justification for choosing post-editing rates over HTER prediction as the target variable?
2. Can you elaborate on the discrepancy in NLL/NLPD trends observed in Section 3.2 between the different language pairs? What specific characteristics of the en-es dataset might explain this difference?
3. What specific modifications or analyses would be needed to investigate the interaction between the linex loss and the predictive distributions in the pessimistic scenario further?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please change “over and underestimates” to “over- and underestimates” throughout the paper.
*   The caption for Figure 1 incorrectly states the line colors as blue and green; they are actually blue and red.
*   Consider citing the specific GP toolkit (e.g., GPy) used for modeling to enhance reproducibility.



Overall, this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field of QE by highlighting the importance of uncertainty modeling and demonstrating the applicability of GPs in this context. While it doesn't push the state-of-the-art in terms of raw prediction accuracy, it offers a thoughtful and well-executed exploration of a crucial aspect often overlooked in QE research. Addressing the identified weaknesses would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
