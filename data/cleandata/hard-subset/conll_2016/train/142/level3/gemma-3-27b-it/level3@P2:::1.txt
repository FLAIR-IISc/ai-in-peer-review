## Review for CONLL 2016: Exploring Prediction Uncertainty in Machine Translation Quality Estimation

This paper investigates probabilistic methods, specifically Gaussian Processes (GPs), for Machine Translation Quality Estimation (QE), focusing on the often-overlooked aspect of prediction uncertainty. The authors argue that relying solely on point estimates is insufficient for real-world translation workflows and demonstrate how incorporating uncertainty estimates can improve decision-making, particularly in asymmetric risk scenarios.

The primary contribution of this work lies in its compelling demonstration of the importance of considering prediction uncertainty in QE. While GPs have been previously applied to QE, this paper meticulously explores extensions to standard GP models (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) and, crucially, advocates for evaluating these models using metrics that account for the full predictive distribution, specifically the Negative Log Predictive Density (NLPD). A second contribution is the insightful analysis of asymmetric risk scenarios relevant to MT workflows—post-editing and gisting—and the development of Bayes risk estimators tailored to these scenarios. Finally, the paper provides a comprehensive and accessible introduction to GPs, making the work valuable to readers potentially unfamiliar with this methodology.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Motivation & Relevance:** The paper effectively argues for the limitations of traditional point-estimate based QE evaluation in practical applications. The examples of post-editing and gisting are particularly illustrative and convincingly highlight the need for uncertainty quantification. This framing is a significant strength.
2. **Thorough Exploration of GPs:** The authors present a well-structured and detailed overview of GP methodology, including kernel choices and warping functions. This thoroughness allows readers to understand the nuances of the proposed models and facilitates reproducibility. The clear explanations of Matèrn kernels and Warped GPs are commendable.
3. **Appropriate Evaluation Metrics:** The emphasis on NLPD as a more informative evaluation metric than traditional MAE or Pearson’s r is well-justified. The results clearly demonstrate that point-estimate metrics fail to capture the differences in uncertainty modeling achieved by different GP configurations.
4. **Insightful Asymmetric Risk Analysis:** Section 4 presents a compelling argument for utilizing predictive distributions in asymmetric risk scenarios. The use of asymmetric loss functions (AL and Linex) and the derivation of Bayes risk estimators are well-explained and demonstrate a sophisticated understanding of the problem.
5. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The logical flow of ideas and the clarity of explanations enhance the overall impact of the work.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Modeling:** While the application of GPs to QE is not novel, the specific extensions explored (Matèrn kernels, Warped GPs) are established techniques within the GP literature. The paper doesn't introduce substantial algorithmic innovations. The primary novelty resides in the application of these techniques *specifically* to QE and the emphasis on uncertainty-aware evaluation.
2. **Interpretation of Results:** The discussion of NLL and NLPD in Section 3.2 is somewhat unclear. The authors state a trend is observed for fr-en and en-de that isn’t present for en-es, but the explanation relating this to dataset size and overfitting is speculative and could be strengthened. Providing more concrete examples of the implications of differing NLL/NLPD values would be beneficial.
3. **Lack of Baseline Comparison:** The results presented lack a crucial point of comparison: a strong, non-probabilistic QE baseline (e.g., a well-tuned SVR model). This makes it difficult to assess the absolute performance of the proposed GP models and contextualize the observed gains. While the focus is on uncertainty, demonstrating competitive performance on standard point-estimate metrics is still valuable.
4. **Linex Loss Performance:** The results for the Linex loss in the pessimistic scenario (Section 4.3) are concerningly poor and require further investigation. The authors speculate that the models may lack sufficient variance, but this needs more detailed justification.
5. **Practical Considerations:** The computational cost of training and evaluating GP models, particularly with complex kernels and warping functions, is not discussed. This is an important practical consideration for real-world deployment.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you justify the choice of post-editing *rates* as the response variable instead of the more commonly used HTER (Human-targeted Translation Edit Rate) in QE research? How does this choice impact the interpretation of your results?
2. In Section 3.2, could you elaborate on the specific trend observed for fr-en and en-de that is not present for en-es regarding NLL/NLPD? What further analysis could be conducted to understand this difference?
3. The paper mentions the use of a GP toolkit. Could you specify which toolkit was used to facilitate reproducibility?

**Additional Comments:**

- Change “over and underestimates” to “over- and underestimates” throughout the paper.
- Correct the colors in the caption of Figure 1 to reflect the actual line colors (blue and green, not blue and red).
- While not essential, a brief discussion of the computational complexity of the proposed methods would be valuable.



Overall, this is a well-written and thoughtful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of QE by highlighting the importance of prediction uncertainty. While the modeling innovations are incremental, the insightful analysis of asymmetric risk scenarios and the advocacy for uncertainty-aware evaluation metrics are significant. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
