### Summary of the paper and main contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a low-resource setting, where only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available. The authors propose reformulating the standard data selection task as a binary classification problem: identifying in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus. They employ a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for this purpose. To mitigate data sparsity, they use a "semi-supervised" approach where word embeddings pre-trained on the large general-domain corpus are used as input features to the CNN, in addition to one-hot representations. The trained classifier then scores all sentences in the general-domain corpus, and the top-scoring sentences are selected to train an adapted SMT system.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical validation that a classification-based approach to data selection using CNNs can outperform established language model (LM) perplexity-based methods. This is particularly demonstrated in the challenging and practical scenario where the in-domain seed data is extremely limited (as few as 100 sentences).
2.  A solid set of experiments on four language pairs with large-scale SMT systems, showing consistent and significant BLEU score improvements over strong and relevant baselines, including n-gram and RNNLM-based data selection.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a well-motivated and practical problem.** The scenario of adapting a large, general-purpose MT system to a niche domain using only a handful of examples is a significant real-world challenge. The paper's focus on "tiny" amounts of data is timely and relevant, especially for emerging domains like social media, which are used in the experiments.
2.  **Strong and convincing empirical results.** The proposed semi-supervised CNN (`sscnn`) method consistently outperforms all baselines across four diverse language pairs. The improvements over the `alldata` baseline are substantial (up to +3.1 BLEU), and more importantly, the method also shows statistically significant gains over the established `rnnlm` selection method. This demonstrates the practical effectiveness of the proposed approach.
3.  **Thorough investigation of the low-data regime.** The experiment presented in Figure 1, which varies the amount of in-domain data used to train the selection model, is a core strength of the paper. It provides clear evidence for the authors' central claim: that the CNN-based approach is more robust to data scarcity than traditional LM-based methods. The performance gap widens as the seed data size decreases, which strongly supports the use of this method in the targeted scenario.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited methodological novelty.** The technical contribution of the paper is modest. The use of CNNs for text classification is a standard technique, and the "semi-supervised" architecture is directly adopted from prior work (Johnson and Zhang, 2015b). The paper's contribution is primarily the application of this existing classifier to the data selection task. The term "semi-supervised" itself is an overstatement; the method is a standard supervised classifier that leverages pre-trained embeddings as features, a ubiquitous practice in NLP. This is not a semi-supervised learning algorithm in the traditional sense (e.g., self-training, co-training).
2.  **Insufficient analysis and lack of insight.** The paper does an excellent job of showing *that* the method works, but fails to provide a deep understanding of *why* it works better than the established LM-based baselines. The discussion in Section 5.4 is speculative and generic (e.g., "convolution captures important domain information"). The submission would be much stronger if it included a qualitative analysis. For example, presenting sentences that are correctly selected by the CNN but missed by the LM baselines would provide valuable insight into the different inductive biases of these models. Without such analysis, the CNN remains a black box.
3.  **Lack of methodological detail and justification.** The description of the CNN architecture and training procedure is too brief. Key details regarding hyperparameter tuning (e.g., the held-out set, the search space), optimization, and regularization are omitted. The authors state that parameters like region size and number of neurons were tuned, but provide no justification for the final choices or analysis of their sensitivity. This lack of transparency undermines the methodological soundness and reproducibility of the work.
4.  **Oversimplified experimental choices.** The strategy for selecting negative training examples—random sampling from the entire general corpus—is naive. It is well-known in classification tasks that the choice of negative samples can have a significant impact on performance. The authors should have explored or at least discussed more sophisticated strategies, such as hard negative mining, which could potentially train a more robust classifier. The brief dismissal of an alternative approach ("results from these two methods are very similar") without providing any data or details is unconvincing.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you clarify your use of the term "semi-supervised"? Your method appears to be a supervised classifier using pre-trained features. Did you experiment with any true semi-supervised learning algorithms where the unlabeled portion of the general-domain corpus is used during the CNN training phase itself (e.g., via pseudo-labeling)?
2.  The core of your argument rests on the CNN being a better domain discriminator than an LM. Can you provide a concrete, qualitative analysis to support this? For instance, could you show a few examples of sentences where your `sscnn` model and the `rnnlm` baseline strongly disagree on the "in-domain" score, and explain why the CNN's decision leads to better MT performance?
3.  Regarding the negative samples for training the classifier, why did you settle on random sampling? Given the potential for "easy negatives" to dominate the random sample, did you investigate the impact of sampling strategies on the final classification and MT performance? For example, what if you sample negative instances from data that an n-gram model already considers somewhat similar to the in-domain data?
