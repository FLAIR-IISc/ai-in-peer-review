**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very small amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to classify sentences from a large general-domain corpus as being either in-domain or out-of-domain. The core contribution is a semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) model that incorporates pre-trained word embeddings (from unlabeled general-domain data) alongside one-hot vector representations of labeled data. The trained classifier is then used to select a subset of the general-domain data for training an adapted SMT system. The primary contributions I identify are:

1.  An application of a semi-supervised CNN architecture for the task of data selection in SMT, demonstrating that this approach can outperform strong language model-based baselines.
2.  An empirical study showing the proposed method is particularly effective in extremely low-resource scenarios (as few as 100 in-domain seed sentences), and that it yields significant BLEU improvements across four language pairs in social media domains.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Relevance and Timeliness:** The paper tackles a significant and practical challenge in machine translation: adaptation with "tiny" amounts of in-domain data. This scenario is increasingly common with the rise of new, niche domains (e.g., social media), making the work highly relevant.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The proposed `sscnn` method consistently and significantly outperforms all baselines, including strong RNNLM-based data selection methods, across four separate tasks. The reported BLEU gains (e.g., +3.1 for zh-en, +1.4 for ar-en) are substantial. The analysis in Figure 1, which shows the method's robustness as the in-domain seed set shrinks, is particularly compelling.
3.  **Thorough Baseline Comparison:** The authors compare their method against a comprehensive set of established and competitive baselines, including `ngram`, `rnnlm`, and a combination (`comblm`), which are directly relevant prior art. This provides a solid context for evaluating the performance of the proposed CNN-based methods.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising results, the paper is marred by a substantial number of flaws ranging from critical formatting errors to significant methodological omissions.

1.  **Severe Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The paper exhibits a lack of care in its preparation that is unacceptable for a scientific publication.
    *   **Corrupted References:** The entire reference list appears to be corrupted by BibTeX compilation artifacts (e.g., "Axelrod et al\.,? \Q2011\E", "III and Jagarlamudi.,? 2011"), rendering it unprofessional and difficult to parse.
    *   **Typos and Grammatical Errors:** Numerous typographical errors ("unlabled corpus", "GloV e"), awkward phrasing ("as same as in"), and inconsistent punctuation are present throughout the text.
    *   **Improper Formatting:** Footnotes are embedded directly in the main text (e.g., "LDC1", "WMT3"), and a footnote marker appears to be misplaced in the text ("Witten-Bell 7 smoothing"). These issues severely detract from the paper's readability and credibility.

2.  **Lack of Methodological Clarity and Reproducibility:** Several key details about the model and experimental setup are either missing or vaguely described, making the work difficult to reproduce.
    *   **Ambiguous CNN Architecture:** The description of the CNN architecture is insufficient. The paper states "we generate both bow-regions and seq-regions and input them to the CNN," but does not explain *how* these are combined. The precise mechanism for combining one-hot vectors (`wl(x)`) and pre-trained embeddings (`ul(x)`) in Equation (2) is also unclear—are the outputs of the matrix multiplications added? Are the dimensions compatible?
    *   **Unspecified Hyperparameters:** The authors state that meta-parameters were "tuned on held-out data," but do not specify what this held-out data was, given that the entire `dev` set was used as positive training examples.
    *   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** Key design choices are justified with vague statements like "Experiments show that..." without providing any supporting data (e.g., for selecting based on the sum of source and target scores). The use of Witten-Bell smoothing for the n-gram LM baseline over the more standard Kneser-Ney is weakly justified in a footnote.

3.  **Confounding Variables in Experimental Design:** The main results presented in Table 2 are difficult to interpret due to a key confounding variable. The number of selected sentences is optimized independently for each method on each task. This makes it impossible to disentangle the quality of the selection method itself from the effect of the selected data size. The `sscnn` model might be better simply because its scoring function pairs better with a particular data subset size, not because its ranking of sentences is inherently superior. A controlled experiment fixing the number of selected sentences across all methods (as was done for Figure 1) is required for a fair comparison in the main results table.

4.  **Imprecise Terminology:** The use of the term "semi-supervised" is questionable. The method does not use unlabeled data during the classifier's training phase (e.g., via regularization or pseudo-labeling). It uses embeddings pre-trained on unlabeled data as fixed features. This is more accurately described as feature-based transfer learning or simply pre-training, a standard technique for text CNNs.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a precise and unambiguous description of your CNN architecture? Specifically, how are "bow-regions" and "seq-regions" combined as input, and what are the dimensions of the weight matrices W and V in Equation (2) that ensure the inputs can be combined?
2.  In your main results (Table 2), the size of the selected data subset is a free parameter. Could you present results from a more controlled experiment where the number of selected sentences is held constant across all competing methods for a given task? This would enable a more direct assessment of the quality of the data selection algorithms themselves.
3.  The reference list is corrupted with formatting artifacts. Can you confirm that this is a manuscript generation error that will be rectified?
