### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very small amount of in-domain data (a few hundred sentences) is available. The authors propose a data selection method that frames the task as a binary classification problem: identifying in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus. To overcome the data scarcity issue for training the classifier, they employ a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The model is trained on the tiny in-domain set as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negative examples, but critically, it also leverages word embeddings pre-trained on the entire large (unlabeled) general-domain corpus. The trained classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in the general-domain pool, and the top-scoring pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A Novel Application of CNNs for MT Data Selection:** The paper introduces a new approach for domain adaptation by reformulating data selection as a text classification task and applying a CNN, which is a departure from conventional language model perplexity-based methods.
2.  **An Effective Semi-supervised Strategy for Low-Resource Scenarios:** The key contribution is the use of pre-trained word embeddings within the CNN to effectively train a strong classifier from a tiny labeled set (as few as 100 sentences). This semi-supervised approach proves highly effective at leveraging large unlabeled corpora to compensate for the lack of labeled in-domain data.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation across four language pairs and three social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). The proposed method significantly outperforms strong baselines, including n-gram and RNNLM-based selection, with particularly impressive gains in the very low-resource setting.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-Motivated and Timely Problem:** The paper tackles a practical and important problem. As MT is applied to new and niche domains (like social media), the assumption of having tens of thousands of in-domain sentences for adaptation often does not hold. The focus on adaptation with "tiny" data is a valuable contribution to the field.
2.  **Convincing Experimental Results:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and compelling. The proposed `sscnn` method consistently outperforms all baselines across four different tasks, with BLEU gains of up to +3.1. The significance tests and comparisons against strong, established baselines (Duh et al., 2013) lend credibility to the results. The experiment depicted in Figure 1 is particularly powerful, as it clearly demonstrates that the advantage of the `sscnn` method widens as the amount of in-domain data shrinks, directly supporting the paper's central claim.
3.  **Sound and Elegant Methodology:** The idea of using a semi-supervised classifier is a very natural and effective solution to the problem. By pre-training word embeddings on all available data, the model gains access to rich semantic and syntactic information that would be impossible to learn from a few hundred labeled examples alone. The consistent improvement of `sscnn` over the `ohcnn` baseline validates this core design choice. The discussion in Section 5.4 provides a clear and plausible intuition for why this architectural choice is so effective.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Insufficient Architectural Details:** The description of the CNN model in Section 3 is high-level and lacks important specifics. The paper mentions using both "bow-regions" and "seq-regions," but it is unclear how these are combined as input. Equation (2) shows the combination of one-hot and embedding features for a single segment, but the overall network architecture (e.g., number of convolutional layers, filter sizes, specific pooling strategy, nature of the final classification layer) is not specified, relying instead on a reference to an external toolkit. This makes the work harder to reproduce and fully understand.
2.  **Comparison Framework could be Stronger:** The proposed method frames data selection as a supervised classification problem (with positive and negative examples), while the LM-based baselines are essentially unsupervised ranking methods based on perplexity scores. This is a comparison between different paradigms. It would have been more compelling to see if the supervised framing itself, rather than the CNN architecture, is the main source of the gain. For example, could one use the perplexity scores from the in-domain and general-domain LMs as features in a simple logistic regression classifier trained on the same positive/negative data? This would help isolate the contribution of the CNN model itself.
3.  **Lack of Deeper Qualitative Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the method works, but provides limited insight into *why* it works better than LM-based approaches. A qualitative analysis would have been highly beneficial. For instance, showing a few examples of sentences that are correctly selected by `sscnn` but missed by `rnnlm` (and vice-versa) could provide concrete evidence for the intuitions presented in Section 5.4. Does the CNN better capture domain-specific jargon, stylistic tone, or sentence structure? Without such analysis, the reasons for the model's success remain somewhat speculative.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Your method relies on randomly sampling negative examples from the general-domain corpus. Given that the zh-en task contained a 120K in-domain subset within the general data, this sampling strategy could introduce label noise (i.e., sampling an in-domain sentence as a negative example). Did you investigate the impact of this potential noise, or explore alternative negative sampling strategies like hard negative mining?
2.  The final selection score is the sum of the source and target sentence scores. This leverages bilingual information, whereas the LM baselines are typically monolingual. Did you run an ablation study to measure the performance when using only the source-side score or only the target-side score? This would clarify how much of the performance gain is attributable to the bilingual signal versus the superiority of the CNN classifier.
3.  The work is situated entirely within the SMT paradigm. Have you considered how this data selection method would perform in the context of adapting large, pre-trained Neural Machine Translation (NMT) models? NMT adaptation is often done via fine-tuning, and it would be interesting to know if fine-tuning on a small corpus selected by your method is more effective than fine-tuning on the original tiny dev set or a set selected by LM-based methods.
