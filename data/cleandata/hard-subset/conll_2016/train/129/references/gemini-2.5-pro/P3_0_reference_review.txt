### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a setting where only a very small amount of in-domain data is available (a few hundred to a few thousand sentences). The authors propose to frame data selection as a binary classification problem (in-domain vs. out-of-domain) and use a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to solve it. To overcome the scarcity of labeled in-domain data for training the classifier, they employ a semi-supervised approach where word embeddings pre-trained on large, unlabeled general-domain corpora are used as input features to the CNN. The trained classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in the large general-domain pool, and the top-scoring pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system.

From my perspective, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and highly effective method for data selection in low-resource SMT adaptation.** The paper successfully reframes data selection from a perplexity-based scoring task to a discriminative classification task. The use of a semi-supervised CNN is a well-motivated and elegant solution to the core challenge of data scarcity, proving more robust than established language model-based techniques in this setting.
2.  **Strong and comprehensive empirical validation.** The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method with significant BLEU score improvements over strong baselines across four different language pairs and domains. The analysis showing the method's stability and superior performance even when the in-domain seed data is reduced to just 100 sentences is particularly compelling and highlights the practical value of the proposed approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Significance and Novelty:** The paper tackles a practical and important problem. Real-world MT applications often face new domains with little to no parallel data. The proposed method provides a viable path for adaptation in such scenarios. While CNNs for text classification and data selection for MT were known, their combination in this semi-supervised framework for the "tiny data" scenario is novel and represents a significant conceptual advance over perplexity-based methods like Moore and Lewis (2010) or Duh et al. (2013).

2.  **Robustness in Low-Data Regimes:** The most impressive aspect of the paper is the experiment detailed in Figure 1. It clearly demonstrates that while LM-based methods degrade sharply as the amount of seed in-domain data decreases, the proposed `sscnn` method remains remarkably stable and effective. Achieving a 2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 seed sentences is a very strong result and underscores the method's primary advantage: leveraging distributional information from large unlabeled corpora (via embeddings) to compensate for the lack of labeled data.

3.  **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The evaluation is well-designed. The authors compare against strong and widely used baselines, including both n-gram and RNNLM-based selection. The experiments cover four diverse tasks, including translation from and to different languages (Chinese, Arabic, Spanish, English) and challenging social media domains (tweets, Facebook posts), which supports the generality of the findings. The use of significance testing adds statistical rigor to the claims.

4.  **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to follow. The motivation for using CNNs to capture local domain-specific signals and for using pre-trained embeddings to handle data sparsity is explained logically and intuitively.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited to SMT:** The work is evaluated exclusively within a phrase-based SMT framework. While this was a standard paradigm, the field was already transitioning towards Neural Machine Translation (NMT) at the time of this work. A key question is how this method would compare to standard NMT adaptation techniques, such as fine-tuning a pre-trained NMT model on the tiny in-domain data. Fine-tuning is a very strong baseline in NMT and might achieve similar or better results with less engineering effort than setting up a separate data selection pipeline. The paper would be strengthened by a discussion of its applicability and potential advantages in an NMT context.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis of the Classifier:** The paper demonstrates that the CNN classifier works well, but it provides little insight into *what* it learns. A qualitative analysis would be beneficial. For example, which n-grams or features does the model find most indicative of the in-domain style? Why is the sum of source and target scores the most effective combination strategy? A deeper analysis of the model's behavior would move the contribution from a successful engineering result to a more scientifically insightful one.

3.  **Ambiguity in Hyperparameter Tuning:** A crucial hyperparameter is the number of sentences selected to train the final SMT system (e.g., 300K for zh->en, 1.3M for en->es). The authors state this is "determined by the performance on held-out data." This raises a critical question: what is this held-out data? If it is an additional in-domain set, it partly undermines the "tiny amount of in-domain data" premise. If the dev set itself is used for both training the classifier and tuning this parameter, it risks overfitting. More clarity on this part of the experimental methodology is needed, as the practicality of the approach depends on how this parameter can be set without requiring substantial extra in-domain resources.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the procedure for selecting the optimal number of sentences for training the final MT system? Was a separate in-domain validation set required for this? If so, how large was it relative to the data used for training the classifier? How sensitive is the final BLEU score to this parameter choice?
2.  Your classifier is trained on positive examples from the in-domain set and negative examples randomly sampled from the general-domain corpus. Have you considered alternative negative sampling strategies, such as hard negative mining (i.e., using sentences that are difficult for the classifier to distinguish from in-domain data)? This could potentially train a more robust classifier.
3.  Given the current dominance of NMT, how do you envision this data selection method being used today? Would it be a pre-processing step before fine-tuning an NMT model, or do you see it being integrated differently?
