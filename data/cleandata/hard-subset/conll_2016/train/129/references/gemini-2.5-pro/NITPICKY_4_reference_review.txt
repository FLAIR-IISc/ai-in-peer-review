### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available. The authors propose framing data selection as a binary classification task and using a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to identify in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus. The "semi-supervised" nature comes from incorporating pre-trained word embeddings (learned on large unlabeled corpora) as features in the CNN, alongside one-hot representations of words from the small labeled (in-domain/out-of-domain) training set. The top-scoring sentences are then used to train an adapted SMT system.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A novel application of semi-supervised CNNs for the task of data selection for SMT domain adaptation, which is shown to be particularly effective in low-resource in-domain settings.
2.  A strong empirical demonstration across four language pairs that the proposed method (`sscnn`) significantly outperforms strong baselines, including n-gram and RNNLM-based data selection, as well as a purely supervised CNN variant (`ohcnn`).

### **Strengths**

1.  **Problem Relevance and Motivation:** The paper tackles a well-defined and highly practical problem. The need for adaptation with "tiny" amounts of data is increasingly common, especially for new and emerging domains like social media, which are aptly chosen as the test beds for this work. The motivation is clear and compelling.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are impressive. The proposed `sscnn` method consistently delivers substantial BLEU score improvements over the `alldata` baseline (up to +3.1 BLEU) and outperforms all other data selection methods across four diverse language pairs. The analysis in Figure 1, demonstrating robustness even with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, strongly supports the paper's central claim.
3.  **Sound Methodological Approach:** The idea to treat data selection as a classification problem is logical. The use of a semi-supervised framework, leveraging dense word embeddings from large unlabeled corpora to enrich the signal from a small labeled set, is an elegant solution to the data sparsity problem. The inclusion of the `ohcnn` baseline provides a valuable ablation study, helping to isolate the contribution of the pre-trained embeddings.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Insufficient Methodological Detail for Reproducibility:** The description of the core model is critically vague, hindering reproducibility.
    *   In Section 3.2, Equation (2) introduces `ul(x)` as the embedding learned from unlabeled data. However, the paper never specifies how this "segment embedding" is derived from the word embeddings within the segment (e.g., concatenation, component-wise average, etc.). This is a fundamental detail of the model.
    *   In Section 5.2, the authors state "we generate both bow-regions and seq-regions and input them to the CNN". It is entirely unclear how these two different representations are combined and fed into the network. Are they concatenated? Used as separate channels? This ambiguity makes it impossible to replicate the architecture.
2.  **Unsupported Claims and Ambiguous Experimental Protocol:** Several experimental choices are justified with statements like "Experiments show..." without any supporting data being presented.
    *   Section 4 states that using the sum of source and target scores "achieves the best performance," but no results are shown to substantiate this over using source-only or target-only scores.
    *   Section 4 also claims that two different negative sampling strategies yield "very similar" results, again without evidence.
    *   The number of selected sentences for MT training (Table 2) is a crucial hyperparameter, which the authors state is "determined by the performance on held-out data." It is not specified what this held-out set is. If it is the development set used to train the classifier, this is a flawed protocol, as it involves tuning on the training data. This needs clarification.
3.  **Incomplete Statistical Analysis:** The significance tests in Table 2 compare `sscnn` against the `alldata` baseline and the best LM-based method. However, a statistical comparison between `sscnn` and `ohcnn` is conspicuously absent. Given that the key contribution is the "semi-supervised" component, proving that its addition provides a *statistically significant* improvement over the purely supervised `ohcnn` is essential, rather than just stating an average improvement of "about 0.5 BLEU point."
4.  **Appalling State of the Bibliography:** The references section is in extreme disarray and appears not to have been proofread at all. It is replete with what look like BibTeX compilation errors (e.g., `Axelrod et al\.,? \Q2011\E, Axelrod et al\.,`), incomplete entries (Pennington et al., 2014 is truncated to "Glove: Global vectors for"), and gross inconsistencies in formatting. This level of carelessness severely undermines the credibility and professionalism of the work.
5.  **Minor Inconsistencies and Errors:** The paper contains numerous minor flaws. For example, "unlabled corpus" in Section 1, "Kneser-Ey" instead of "Kneser-Ney" in Footnote 7. While individually small, the sheer number of such issues suggests a lack of careful preparation. The main experiments (Table 2) use dev sets of up to 6,016 sentences, which stretches the definition of "tiny," while the truly "tiny" data experiment (Figure 1) is confined to a single language pair.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the precise operation used to create the segment embedding `ul(x)` from the constituent word embeddings in Equation (2)?
2.  How, specifically, were the `bow-regions` and `seq-regions` combined as input to the CNN model?
3.  Please specify the exact "held-out data" used to tune the number of selected sentences reported in Table 2. If this was the development set, please justify this choice in light of potential overfitting.
4.  Could you provide the comparative BLEU scores that led to the decision to use the sum of source and target classifier scores for sentence selection?
