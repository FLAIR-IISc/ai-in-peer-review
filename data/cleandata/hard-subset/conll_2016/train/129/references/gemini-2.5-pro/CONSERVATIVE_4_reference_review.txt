### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a setting where only a very small amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) as a classifier to select in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus. They introduce a "semi-supervised" CNN model, which leverages pre-trained word embeddings (from word2vec) in addition to one-hot vector inputs, to improve classification accuracy with limited labeled data. The selected sentences are then used to train a domain-adapted SMT system. The method is evaluated on four language pairs and compared against strong language model-based data selection baselines, demonstrating significant improvements in BLEU score, especially when the in-domain seed data is limited to a few hundred sentences.

### Contributions
1.  The paper provides an empirical study showing that a standard CNN-based classification approach can outperform well-established language model perplexity-based methods (n-gram and RNNLM) for the task of data selection in SMT domain adaptation.
2.  It demonstrates the particular effectiveness of this approach in extreme low-resource adaptation scenarios (e.g., with only 100 in-domain sentences), where traditional LM-based methods show diminishing returns.

### Strengths
1.  **Well-Motivated Problem:** The work tackles a practical and challenging scenario in MT: adaptation with a "tiny" amount of in-domain data. This is highly relevant for emerging domains like social media, where collecting large parallel corpora is often infeasible. The experiments that vary the amount of in-domain data from 6,000 down to 100 sentences (Figure 1) effectively highlight the robustness of the proposed method in such settings.
2.  **Strong Baselines and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors compare their method against strong and widely-used data selection techniques, including the n-gram approach of Axelrod et al. (2011) and the RNNLM approach of Duh et al. (2013). The evaluation is conducted across four different language pairs and domains, which lends credibility to the findings.
3.  **Consistent and Significant Improvements:** The proposed semi-supervised CNN (`sscnn`) method consistently outperforms all baselines across all four tasks. The reported BLEU score improvements are substantial (from +0.7 to +3.1 over the `alldata` baseline) and often statistically significant over the best LM-based method. The performance in the very low-data regime is particularly compelling, suggesting the method is more data-efficient than its counterparts.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** The core methodology relies on applying a standard CNN architecture for text classification, a technique that was already well-established at the time of this work (e.g., Kim, 2014; Johnson & Zhang, 2015a). The "semi-supervised" aspect is simply the use of pre-trained word embeddings, which is a common practice in neural network models for NLP and not a novel semi-supervised learning algorithm. The contribution is therefore an application of an existing method to a new task, rather than a novel technique.
2.  **Ambiguity in Experimental Setup:** The paper lacks clarity on crucial experimental details. It states that CNN meta-parameters are "tuned on held-out data," but does not specify the origin of this data. Given the "tiny" amount of in-domain data available, it is critical to know if this held-out set was partitioned from the already small dev set, which could make tuning unreliable. Similarly, the number of sentences to select is determined on a "separate validation set," but its relation to other data splits is not defined. This ambiguity makes it difficult to fully assess the soundness of the experimental protocol.
3.  **Potentially Unfair Comparison with Baselines:** The proposed CNN model selects sentence pairs based on the sum of scores from both the source and target sentences. However, the paper does not specify how the LM-based baselines were implemented. These methods are traditionally applied to only one side of the parallel corpus (typically the target side, to select data for the translation and language models). If the baselines only used monolingual information from one side, while the proposed model used information from both, the comparison is not entirely fair, as the CNN would have access to a richer signal.
4.  **Overstated Generalizability of Main Result:** The most impressive result (+3.1 BLEU on zh-en) is achieved on a task where the authors acknowledge a 120K sentence in-domain subset exists within the general-domain training data. While the CNN's ability to identify this subset is a success, this scenario may represent a best-case "needle-in-a-haystack" problem. The gains on the other three tasks, which presumably lack such a large, clean in-domain component, are much more modest (0.7-1.4 BLEU). This raises questions about how well the method performs in more typical, heterogeneous domain-mixing scenarios.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the procedure for hyperparameter tuning and model selection? Specifically, what data was used as the held-out set for tuning the CNN meta-parameters, and was it distinct from the data used to determine the optimal number of sentences to select for the final MT system?
2.  For the LM-based baselines, was data selection performed using the source language, the target language, or a combination of both? To ensure a fair comparison, the baselines should ideally have access to the same information (i.e., both source and target sentences) as your proposed CNN method.
3.  The term "semi-supervised" is used to describe the use of pre-trained word embeddings. While technically correct, this is now a standard initialization technique. Do you believe this framing accurately reflects the contribution, or would a description focused on leveraging pre-trained representations be more precise?
