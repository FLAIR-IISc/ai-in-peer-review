**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a very small amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose using a convolutional neural network (CNN) to perform data selection, classifying sentences from a large general-domain corpus as either in-domain or out-of-domain. To overcome the data scarcity for training this classifier, they employ a semi-supervised CNN that incorporates pre-trained word embeddings from a large unlabeled corpus. The top-scoring sentences are then used to train a phrase-based SMT system.

As I see them, the contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical study demonstrating that a semi-supervised CNN can be effectively applied to the task of data selection for SMT adaptation, particularly in extremely low-resource in-domain settings.
2.  A comprehensive experimental comparison across four language pairs, showing that the proposed method consistently outperforms strong language model-based data selection baselines, with significant improvements when the amount of labeled in-domain data is reduced to as few as 100 sentences.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a practical and important problem.** The scenario of adapting an MT system to a new domain with only a handful of examples (e.g., a small development set) is highly realistic and challenging. The paper's motivation is well-founded and tackles a significant gap in the literature.
2.  **Strong and consistent empirical results.** The proposed semi-supervised CNN (`sscnn`) method achieves the best performance on all four tasks, with substantial BLEU score improvements over the baseline trained on all data (up to +3.1 BLEU) and notable gains over strong LM-based selection methods.
3.  **Compelling low-data analysis.** The experiment detailed in Figure 1, which varies the amount of in-domain data used to train the selection model, is a key strength. It convincingly shows that while all methods suffer with less data, the CNN-based approaches, and `sscnn` in particular, are significantly more robust and graceful in their performance degradation than the LM-based methods. The ability to achieve a 2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 in-domain sentences is impressive.
4.  **Reproducibility.** The authors provide sufficient detail on the main components of their SMT system and cite the publicly available toolkits used for the CNN, word embeddings, and LM baselines, which facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**

Despite the promising results, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor-to-moderate flaws in its methodology, presentation, and analysis that, in aggregate, undermine its quality.

1.  **Limited methodological novelty.** The core ideas—using CNNs for text classification and augmenting them with pre-trained word embeddings in a semi-supervised manner—are not new. The authors themselves frame their approach as being "similar to (Johnson and Zhang, 2015b)". The contribution is therefore an application of existing techniques to a new task, rather than a novel method. While application papers are valuable, the execution here lacks the necessary rigor to compensate for the limited novelty.
2.  **Critical methodological details are ambiguous or missing.**
    *   The precise architecture and input representation for the semi-supervised CNN are unclear. Equation (2) presents `wl(x)` (one-hot vector) and `ul(x)` (embedding) for a "segment `l`". It is not explained how the segment embedding `ul(x)` is derived from the word embeddings within that segment (e.g., averaging, concatenation, max-pooling). Furthermore, the paper mentions both "bow-regions" and "seq-regions," but it is ambiguous how these concepts map to the `wl(x)` and `ul(x)` vectors in the equation. This lack of clarity on a core component of the proposed method is a major weakness.
    *   The process for determining the number of selected sentences is opaque. The authors state this crucial hyperparameter was "empirically determine[d]... on a separate validation set," but provide no further details. Given the wide variance in the optimal number of sentences across tasks and methods (from 300K to 1.6M), this tuning process has a profound impact on the final results. The lack of detail makes it impossible to assess the fairness of the comparison or to fully reproduce the results.
    *   The paper claims that using the sum of source and target scores for selection "achieves the best performance," but no results are presented to substantiate this claim.
3.  **Numerous formatting and presentation errors.** The paper exhibits a lack of care in its preparation, which detracts from its professional quality.
    *   The **References section is egregiously formatted**. Multiple entries are incomplete (e.g., Pennington et al., 2014), contain strange formatting artifacts (e.g., `Axelrod et al\.,? \Q2011\E`), and are inconsistent. This level of sloppiness is unacceptable for a conference submission.
    *   There are typographical errors (e.g., "unlabled" in Section 1).
    *   Footnote placement is awkward (e.g., footnote 6 in Section 5.2).
    *   The definition of the `+/++` significance markers in Table 2 is ambiguous, referring to the "best LM-based method" without specifying how this "best" baseline is determined for each task.
4.  **Superficial analysis and discussion.** The discussion in Section 5.4 offers only a generic, high-level explanation of why CNNs work for text classification. It fails to provide specific insights into why they outperform strong LM-based methods *for this specific task*. The hypothesis about handling unseen but semantically similar words (e.g., "Lenovo" vs. "Dell") is plausible but remains unsubstantiated. A qualitative analysis with concrete examples of sentences correctly selected by the CNN but missed by the LMs would have provided much-needed depth and evidence for the claims.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a precise definition of the input vectors `wl(x)` and `ul(x)` in Equation (2)? Specifically, how is a single "segment embedding" `ul(x)` constructed from the multiple word embeddings within that segment?
2.  Could you elaborate on the procedure used to tune the number of sentences selected for each data selection method? What data was used for this validation, and what was the range of values considered?
3.  The discussion hypothesizes that pre-trained embeddings allow the `sscnn` model to generalize to unseen domain-specific words. Can you provide any qualitative examples from your experiments (e.g., sentences with words that are OOV to the small in-domain training set) that support this claim and demonstrate the advantage over the `ohcnn` and LM-based models?
