**Summary of the paper and contributions**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available. The authors propose a novel approach that reframes data selection as a binary classification task. They train a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings from large unlabeled corpora to compensate for the lack of labeled in-domain data. This trained classifier is then used to score and select the most relevant sentence pairs from a large general-domain corpus to train an adapted SMT system.

As I see them, the main contributions are:

1.  **A New Paradigm for Data Selection:** The paper's most significant contribution is the introduction of a discriminative classification framework for data selection in MT. This marks a bold and creative departure from the established paradigm of using language model perplexity. By treating domain relevance as a classification problem, the authors open up a new and promising research direction for domain adaptation.
2.  **Demonstrating Effective Adaptation from "Tiny" Data:** The work successfully shows that adaptation is possible and highly effective even with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences). This is a critical result, as it makes domain adaptation practical for many real-world scenarios (e.g., social media, personal communication) where collecting large in-domain corpora is prohibitively expensive or impossible.

**Strengths**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Advance:** The core idea is genuinely novel and refreshing. Moving away from generative LM-based scoring to a discriminative CNN-based classifier is a significant conceptual leap. While prior work focused on refining perplexity-based metrics, this paper fundamentally changes the approach to the problem. This kind of bold, high-risk/high-reward research is exactly what the field needs to move forward. The successful execution and impressive results validate this new direction.
2.  **Tackles a Critical and Difficult Problem:** The paper focuses on the "tiny data" adaptation scenario, a practical yet under-explored problem. The ability to achieve significant gains from just a few hundred sentences is a powerful result. This makes the technique applicable to a vast new range of adaptation tasks, such as fine-grained topic adaptation or on-the-fly adaptation to user-generated content, which were previously out of reach for methods requiring thousands of in-domain sentences.
3.  **Strong and Compelling Empirical Evidence:** The experimental results are thorough and convincing. The proposed semi-supervised CNN (`sscnn`) method achieves substantial and statistically significant BLEU score improvements over strong baselines (including RNNLM-based selection) across four different language pairs and diverse domains. The gains of up to 3.1 BLEU are impressive in their own right. Crucially, Figure 1 provides a clear and powerful demonstration of the method's superiority in the intended low-data regime, where the performance of LM-based methods degrades sharply while the CNN approach remains robust.
4.  **Clear Intuition and Plausible Justification:** The discussion in Section 5.4 provides a compelling intuition for why the method is so effective. The authors correctly identify the two key advantages: the CNN's ability to learn and focus on local, domain-indicative features (phrases/n-grams), and the semantic generalization provided by pre-trained word embeddings. This analysis grounds the empirical success in sound reasoning.

**Weaknesses**

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few areas that could be improved. I view these as minor points that do not detract from the significance of the core contribution.

1.  **Limited Architectural Innovation:** The novelty of the work lies in the *application* of CNNs to this new task, not in the CNN architecture itself. The model is a fairly standard implementation for text classification, drawing on established designs. While this is perfectly acceptable, the paper would be stronger if it briefly discussed why this specific architecture was chosen over other potential classifiers (e.g., logistic regression on n-grams, LSTMs) for this task.
2.  **Selection Heuristic is Under-justified:** The decision to select sentence pairs based on the sum of their source and target scores is presented as an empirical choice. While effective, this is a simple heuristic. A more principled discussion on how to combine evidence from two classifiers would be welcome. For instance, what happens in cases of disagreement (e.g., high source score, low target score)? A brief analysis of alternative combination strategies might provide further insight.
3.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the method works, but offers little insight into *how* it works at a granular level. The contribution could be made even more impactful by including some qualitative analysis. For example, examining the features learned by the CNN or showing example sentences that are correctly selected by the CNN but missed by LM methods (and vice-versa) would provide a deeper understanding of the model's behavior and its advantages.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The negative training data for the classifier is generated by random sampling. Have you considered more advanced sampling techniques, such as hard negative mining, where you would select out-of-domain sentences that the classifier finds most confusable with the in-domain data? Do you believe this could lead to a more robust classifier and further improvements?
2.  Your experiments are conducted in a phrase-based SMT framework. How do you see this data selection methodology translating to the now-dominant Neural Machine Translation (NMT) paradigm? It seems ideally suited for selecting data to fine-tune a large pre-trained NMT model. Do you anticipate any unique challenges or benefits in applying this technique in an NMT context?
