### Summary and Contributions

This paper tackles the important and practical problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a "tiny" amount of in-domain data is available (a few hundred to a few thousand sentences). The authors propose to frame data selection as a binary classification task (in-domain vs. out-of-domain) and use a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to perform this classification. The model is trained using the small in-domain dev set as positive examples and a random sample from the general-domain data as negative examples. The "semi-supervised" aspect comes from leveraging pre-trained word embeddings (from word2vec) learned on the large general-domain corpus, which helps the classifier generalize from the tiny labeled set. The trained classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in the general-domain corpus to select a subset for training an adapted SMT system.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel application of a semi-supervised CNN architecture for the task of SMT data selection.** While the CNN model itself is adapted from prior work in text classification, its application to select training data for MT adaptation, particularly in an extremely low-resource setting, is a significant and effective contribution.

2.  **A strong and comprehensive empirical evaluation demonstrating the method's superiority over state-of-the-art baselines.** The experiments, conducted on four language pairs with large-scale data and challenging social media domains, show substantial BLEU score improvements (up to +3.1). Crucially, the paper demonstrates that the proposed method's advantage over strong LM-based selection methods grows as the amount of available in-domain seed data shrinks to as little as 100 sentences.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Well-Motivated and Practical Problem:** The scenario of adapting a large, general-domain MT system to a new domain with very limited data is highly realistic and challenging. This is especially relevant for emerging domains like social media (tweets, posts) or highly specialized topics where collecting large parallel corpora is prohibitively expensive. The paper's focus on this "tiny data" regime is a major strength.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Experimental Results:** The paper presents a robust set of experiments. The proposed `sscnn` method consistently outperforms both the `alldata` baseline and the state-of-the-art LM-based data selection methods (`ngram`, `rnnlm`) across four different language pairs and domains. The reported BLEU gains are not marginal but substantial and statistically significant.

3.  **Excellent Low-Data Analysis:** The experiment depicted in Figure 1 is the most compelling piece of evidence in the paper. It directly tests the core claim by varying the size of the in-domain seed data from 6,000 down to 100 sentences. The results clearly show that while all methods suffer with less data, the CNN-based methods, and `sscnn` in particular, are far more resilient. The widening performance gap between `sscnn` and the LM-based methods at the low-data end powerfully validates the authors' approach.

4.  **Solid Choice of Baselines and Experimental Setup:** The authors compare their method against the most relevant and competitive data selection techniques from Axelrod et al. (2011) and Duh et al. (2013). This provides a strong point of comparison and makes the reported improvements more meaningful. The overall SMT and experimental setup is standard and well-described.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core semi-supervised CNN architecture, which combines one-hot features with pre-trained region embeddings (Equation 2), is directly adapted from prior work in text classification, specifically Johnson and Zhang (2015b), which the authors cite. While the application to data selection is novel, the paper could be stronger if it more clearly acknowledged that the network architecture itself is not a contribution.

2.  **Lack of Discussion on Computational Cost:** The proposed method requires scoring every sentence pair in the large general-domain corpora (up to 15.2M pairs) using a trained CNN. This is a computationally intensive process, likely orders of magnitude slower than scoring with a pre-computed n-gram or even an RNN language model. This significant practical drawback is not discussed at all. For a system to be practical, the cost of adaptation is a critical factor. A discussion of inference time and a comparison with the baselines is a major omission.

3.  **Superficial Analysis of Why CNNs Outperform RNNLMs:** The discussion in Section 5.4 provides a plausible but high-level intuition for why the CNN works well (capturing local features, generalization via embeddings). However, the `rnnlm` baseline also uses word embeddings and captures sequential context. The paper does not offer a deep analysis into *why* the CNN's architectural biases (local convolutions + max-pooling) are superior to the RNN's for this specific task. Does it better identify key topic words regardless of their position? Is it less sensitive to fluency and more focused on content? A more detailed qualitative or quantitative analysis comparing the sentences selected by `sscnn` vs. `rnnlm` would have provided valuable insight and strengthened the paper's conclusions.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please comment on the computational cost (e.g., in CPU hours) of using the trained CNN to score the entire general-domain corpus, and how this compares to the cost of scoring with the `ngram` and `rnnlm` baselines? Given the scale of the training data (up to 15M sentences), this seems like a crucial practical consideration.

2.  The number of selected sentences (`#sent` in Table 2) was determined empirically on a validation set. Was this hyperparameter tuned separately for each of the five data selection methods? Assuming it was, have you analyzed whether the optimal corpus size differs systematically between LM-based and CNN-based methods?

3.  Your discussion suggests that the CNN excels at identifying key domain-specific words. Could you provide a more concrete analysis or examples to contrast what the `sscnn` classifier learns versus what the `rnnlm` perplexity captures? For instance, do you find that the CNN-selected data is topically more coherent but perhaps less fluent than the LM-selected data? This would help clarify the specific advantages of the convolutional architecture for this task.
