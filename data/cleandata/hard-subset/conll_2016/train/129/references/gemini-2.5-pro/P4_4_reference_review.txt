### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a setting where only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available (e.g., a few hundred to a few thousand sentences). The authors propose framing domain adaptation as a data selection task, which is in turn modeled as a binary text classification problem. The core of their method is a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) that learns to distinguish in-domain sentences from out-of-domain sentences. To overcome the scarcity of labeled in-domain data, the CNN is enhanced with pre-trained word embeddings learned from a large, unlabeled general-domain corpus. This semi-supervised classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in the large general-domain training corpus, and the top-scoring pairs are selected to train a domain-adapted SMT system. The method is evaluated on four large-scale translation tasks across different social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating significant improvements over both a general-domain baseline and strong language model-based data selection methods.

### Main Contributions
1.  **An effective application of semi-supervised learning for SMT data selection in a low-resource setting.** The primary contribution is the successful adaptation and application of a semi-supervised CNN to the task of selecting relevant training data for SMT. By leveraging pre-trained word embeddings, the method effectively mitigates the problem of having only a tiny amount of labeled in-domain data, a scenario where traditional data selection methods often struggle.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical validation demonstrating the robustness of the proposed method.** The paper presents a strong empirical case across four different language pairs and domains. The experiments systematically show that the proposed CNN-based approach consistently and significantly outperforms strong, widely-used language model-based baselines, especially when the amount of seed in-domain data is extremely limited (e.g., 100 sentences).

### Strengths
1.  **Well-motivated and addresses a practical problem.** The paper tackles the challenging and highly relevant problem of adapting MT systems to new domains where collecting large amounts of in-domain data is impractical or prohibitively expensive. The specific focus on "tiny" amounts of data (a few hundred sentences) makes the work particularly valuable for rapid adaptation to emerging domains like social media.
2.  **Strong and convincing experimental results.** The proposed `sscnn` method consistently outperforms all baselines across four diverse, large-scale SMT tasks. The BLEU score improvements are substantial (up to 3.1 points) and shown to be statistically significant. The experiment depicted in Figure 1, which analyzes performance as the size of the in-domain seed data decreases, is particularly compelling. It clearly demonstrates that the proposed method is more robust and degrades more gracefully than LM-based methods in very low-data scenarios.
3.  **The methodology is technically sound and clearly described.** The choice of a semi-supervised CNN is well-justified, and the authors provide a clear rationale for its potential advantages, such as the ability of word embeddings to capture semantic similarities beyond surface n-grams and the capacity of convolution/pooling layers to identify key domain-specific phrases. The experimental setup is thorough, including comparisons against multiple strong and relevant baselines from prior work.
4.  **Good analysis and support for claims.** The discussion in Section 5.4 provides a plausible explanation for the method's strong performance, linking it to the architectural properties of CNNs and the generalization power of pre-trained embeddings. Furthermore, the stability analysis, where the experiment with 100 seed sentences was repeated with different random samples, adds credibility to the results by showing a low variance in performance.

### Weaknesses
1.  **The novelty of the neural network model itself is limited.** The semi-supervised CNN architecture is directly adopted from previous work in text classification (Johnson and Zhang, 2015b). While the application to SMT data selection is novel and effective, the work does not introduce new techniques in neural network modeling. The contribution is one of application rather than architectural innovation.
2.  **Lack of comparison to alternative classification models.** The paper compares the CNN-based classifiers only to LM-based data selection methods. A more complete evaluation would have included comparisons to other standard, powerful text classifiers, such as a Support Vector Machine (SVM) with n-gram features. Comparing the `ohcnn` model to an SVM would have helped to more clearly isolate the specific benefits of the convolutional architecture over a strong linear classifier for this task.
3.  **Insufficient detail on hyperparameter selection.** The paper states that the number of selected sentences for SMT training was empirically determined based on performance on a validation set. However, it is unclear if this validation set was distinct from the dev set used to train the CNN classifier. This ambiguity raises potential concerns about tuning on the test condition. Furthermore, details on the tuning of the CNN's own hyperparameters (e.g., number of neurons, region size) are sparse, which could hinder reproducibility.
4.  **The mechanism for combining source and target scores is underexplored.** The authors state that using the sum of source and target sentence scores performed best, but they provide no ablation or analysis to support this. It would be valuable to see the results of using only the source score or only the target score to better understand their relative contributions and whether their importance varies by task or domain.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the procedure used to determine the optimal number of selected sentences for each task? Specifically, was a separate, held-out in-domain validation set used for this purpose, distinct from the `dev` set that provided the positive training examples for the CNN classifier?
2.  The paper uses the CNN to produce a binary classification, and then selects the top N sentences based on their scores. Did you consider framing the task as a ranking problem from the outset, where the model is trained to directly rank general-domain sentences by their relevance to the in-domain data?
3.  Could you provide an ablation study on the impact of using source-side scores, target-side scores, versus the sum of both? This would help clarify the relative contribution of each side to the data selection process.
