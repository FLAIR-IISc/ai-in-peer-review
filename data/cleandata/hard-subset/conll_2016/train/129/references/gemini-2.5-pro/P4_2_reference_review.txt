### Summary of the Paper
This paper proposes a method for domain adaptation in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available. The authors frame the problem of selecting relevant training data from a large general-domain corpus as a binary classification task (in-domain vs. out-of-domain). To overcome the data scarcity of the in-domain class, they propose using a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The model is trained using the tiny in-domain dataset as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negative examples. The semi-supervised aspect is the integration of pre-trained word embeddings, learned from the large general-domain corpus, as an additional input to the CNN. Once trained, this classifier scores all sentence pairs in the general-domain corpus. The highest-scoring pairs are selected to train a domain-adapted SMT system. The method is evaluated on four language pairs with social media domains, demonstrating significant BLEU score improvements over a baseline trained on all data and over established language model-based data selection methods.

### Main Contributions
1.  **An effective application of semi-supervised CNNs for data selection in SMT domain adaptation.** The paper successfully formulates data selection as a text classification problem and shows that a semi-supervised CNN, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings, is a superior method for this task compared to strong language model-based baselines.
2.  **A robust solution for domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data.** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that the proposed method is effective even when the seed in-domain data is as small as 100 sentences. It demonstrates greater stability and performance in this challenging low-resource scenario than prior data selection techniques.

### Strengths
1.  **Strong and Consistent Empirical Results:** The proposed method (`sscnn`) achieves statistically significant improvements over all baselines, including strong LM-based data selection techniques, across four different language pairs and domains (Table 2). The gains are substantial, reaching up to +3.1 BLEU over the `alldata` baseline and consistently outperforming the best LM-based method. This provides compelling evidence for the method's effectiveness.
2.  **Addresses a Practical and Important Problem:** The work focuses on domain adaptation with a "tiny amount" of in-domain data, a scenario that is highly relevant for adapting MT systems to new, specialized, or rapidly evolving domains (e.g., social media) where collecting large parallel corpora is infeasible. The experiments demonstrating strong performance with as few as 100 seed sentences (Figure 1) are particularly impactful, highlighting the method's utility in realistic low-resource conditions where previous methods are less effective.
3.  **Technically Sound Experimental Design:** The evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors compare against appropriate and widely-used baselines from the data selection literature (`ngram`, `rnnlm`). The experiments cover multiple language directions and domains, demonstrating the generalizability of the approach. The inclusion of significance tests and an analysis of performance as a function of in-domain data size (Figure 1) strengthens the validity of the conclusions.
4.  **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written, with a clear motivation, a logical explanation of the methodology, and a straightforward presentation of the experimental setup and results. The progression from the simpler `ohcnn` to the proposed `sscnn` helps the reader understand the components of the final model.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Model:** The CNN architecture and the semi-supervised learning strategy (combining one-hot inputs with pre-trained embeddings) are directly adopted from prior work in text classification (Johnson and Zhang, 2015a; 2015b). While the application to SMT data selection is novel and effective, the core model itself does not introduce new techniques. The paper could be strengthened by a deeper justification for choosing this specific architecture over other neural classifiers.
2.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the method works, but provides limited insight into *why*. For example, an ablation study comparing the full `sscnn` to a simpler classifier (e.g., Logistic Regression) that also uses pre-trained embeddings (by averaging word vectors) would help to isolate the specific contribution of the convolutional architecture versus the embeddings. Additionally, the claim that using the sum of source and target scores is optimal is presented without supporting data comparing it to source-only or target-only selection.
3.  **Exclusively Focused on SMT:** The work is evaluated entirely within the context of Statistical Machine Translation. At the time of this work, the field was heavily transitioning to Neural Machine Translation (NMT). The data selection method is model-agnostic and could be highly relevant for pre-training or fine-tuning NMT models, but this potential application is not explored or discussed. This limits the paper's long-term impact and relevance to the current state of MT research.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of a CNN for this classification task over other sequence models like LSTMs, which are also highly effective for text classification? Is there a specific property of the convolutional and pooling layers that you believe is particularly advantageous for identifying domain relevance?
2.  To better isolate the contribution of the CNN architecture itself, did you consider a baseline that uses a simpler classifier (e.g., SVM) on top of features derived from the same pre-trained word embeddings (such as sentence-level vector averages)? This would help clarify how much of the performance gain comes from the convolutional structure versus simply using the embeddings.
3.  The paper states that selecting sentence pairs based on the sum of source and target scores performed best. Could you provide the results for selection based on only the source-side score or only the target-side score? This information would be valuable for understanding the interplay between source and target domain relevance in this task.
