**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a tiny amount of in-domain parallel data is available (as little as a few hundred sentences). The authors propose using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to classify sentences from a large general-domain corpus as either in-domain or out-of-domain. To overcome the data scarcity for training this classifier, they employ a semi-supervised approach where word embeddings are pre-trained on the large general-domain corpus and used as input to the CNN, augmenting the one-hot representations from the small labeled set. The trained classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in the general-domain corpus, and the top-scoring pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and effective application of semi-supervised CNNs for the task of data selection for SMT domain adaptation. This approach is specifically tailored to the challenging and practical low-resource setting where in-domain seed data is extremely limited.
2.  Strong and comprehensive empirical results across four language pairs and diverse domains (sms, tweets, social media). The proposed method (sscnn) significantly outperforms strong baselines, including a system trained on all available data and state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods. The experiments convincingly demonstrate the method's robustness even when in-domain data is reduced to just 100 sentences.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Relevant and Difficult Problem:** The problem of adapting MT systems with very limited in-domain data is highly practical. As the authors note, for new social media domains or systems covering hundreds of fine-grained topics, collecting large in-domain corpora is often prohibitively expensive. This work provides a compelling solution for this important scenario.
2.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Method:** The application of CNNs for this task is well-justified. The authors provide a clear rationale for why the architecture—capturing local n-gram like features through convolutions and identifying the most salient ones through pooling—is suitable for domain classification. The key insight to use a semi-supervised approach by incorporating pre-trained word embeddings is particularly clever, as it allows the model to leverage vast amounts of unlabeled data to overcome the supervision bottleneck, which is the core of the problem being addressed.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and provide strong support for the paper's claims. The authors evaluate on four different tasks with large-scale training data and compare against multiple strong baselines, including the widely-used LM-based data selection methods. The most impactful result, shown in Figure 1, is the analysis of performance as the amount of in-domain seed data decreases. This experiment clearly demonstrates that the proposed `sscnn` method is not only superior but also significantly more robust in the very low-resource settings it targets. The stability check with 100 sentences is also a nice touch.
4.  **Significant and Consistent Performance Gains:** The proposed `sscnn` method achieves substantial and statistically significant BLEU score improvements across all four tasks (from +0.7 to +3.1 BLEU over the `alldata` baseline). Crucially, it also significantly outperforms the best LM-based methods in three out of four tasks. These are strong results that clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Architectural and Hyperparameter Details:** The paper is somewhat brief on the specifics of the CNN model and the experimental setup, which could hinder reproducibility. For instance, the architecture after the convolution and max-pooling layers is not described (e.g., are there fully-connected layers? What is the output activation and loss function?). Furthermore, the process for determining the optimal number of selected sentences for MT training is described as being done "empirically... on a separate validation set," which is vague. If the same development set used to train the CNN classifier was also used to tune this hyperparameter, it could lead to overfitting on that set. Clarification is needed here.
2.  **No Qualitative Analysis of Selected Data:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a qualitative analysis of *what* kind of sentences the CNN-based method selects. An analysis of sentences highly ranked by `sscnn` but lowly ranked by the `rnnlm` baseline (or vice-versa) would provide valuable insight into the differences between the methods and offer a deeper explanation for the performance gains. For the zh2en task, where a known in-domain subset exists, it would have been very informative to report what fraction of that subset was successfully recovered by the selection methods.
3.  **Evaluation Limited to Phrase-Based SMT:** The entire study is conducted within the phrase-based SMT (PBSMT) paradigm. While appropriate for the context of the cited baselines, the field of MT has largely shifted to Neural Machine Translation (NMT). NMT systems have different sensitivities to data size and domain mismatch. While outside the direct scope, the paper would be stronger if it acknowledged this and briefly discussed the potential applicability of the method to NMT data selection, which would significantly broaden its potential impact.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the process for tuning the number of sentences selected for MT training? Specifically, was a validation set used that was separate from the development set used as positive examples for training the CNN classifier?
2.  Could you provide more details on the full CNN architecture? What layers follow the max-pooling operation, and what loss function (e.g., cross-entropy) was minimized during training?
3.  For the zh2en task, you mention a 120K sentence in-domain subset exists within the training data. Did your `sscnn` method show a higher precision/recall in identifying sentences from this specific subset compared to the LM-based baselines? Such an analysis could provide direct evidence of the classifier's superior accuracy.
