### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a challenging but practical scenario: when only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available (as few as 100 sentence pairs). The authors propose using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) as a classifier to select domain-relevant sentence pairs from a large general-domain corpus. To overcome the data scarcity for training this classifier, they employ a semi-supervised approach where the CNN is fed not only with one-hot representations from the small labeled set but also with features derived from pre-trained word embeddings learned on large unlabeled corpora. The selected data is then used to train a specialized SMT system.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **An effective application of semi-supervised CNNs for data selection in low-resource SMT adaptation.** The key contribution is the demonstration that pre-trained word embeddings can successfully compensate for a lack of labeled in-domain data, enabling the training of a robust domain classifier. This method proves highly effective, especially when the amount of seed data is exceptionally small.
2.  **A strong empirical validation of the proposed method's superiority over established baselines.** The paper presents a thorough experimental evaluation on four language pairs with large-scale data. The proposed `sscnn` method consistently and significantly outperforms strong language model-based data selection techniques (n-gram and RNN-LM), with the performance gap widening as the amount of available in-domain data decreases.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and practically relevant problem.** The paper tackles the important problem of "tiny data" adaptation. In many real-world applications, especially for emerging domains like social media, it is prohibitively expensive or impossible to collect thousands of in-domain sentence pairs. The ability to adapt a system with only a few hundred, or even just one hundred, sentences is a significant practical advantage. The paper's motivation is clear and compelling.

2.  **Sound and elegant methodology.** The proposed semi-supervised CNN (`sscnn`) is a well-designed solution to the problem. The core idea of combining supervised signals from the small labeled set (via one-hot vectors) with unsupervised signals from a large general corpus (via pre-trained embeddings) is a standard semi-supervised learning pattern, but its application to SMT data selection is novel and effective. The architecture allows the model to leverage semantic similarities learned from the embeddings to classify sentences containing words unseen in the small in-domain set, which is a key weakness of purely supervised models in this setting.

3.  **Thorough and convincing experimental setup.** The experiments are conducted on four diverse language pairs and large training corpora (9-15M sentence pairs), demonstrating the general applicability of the method. The choice of baselines is excellent; the authors compare against not only a generic system trained on all data but also against state-of-the-art data selection methods based on n-gram LMs and RNN-LMs, even using the publicly available code from prior work for a fair comparison.

4.  **Excellent analysis of performance under data scarcity.** The experiment presented in Figure 1 is the most compelling part of the paper. By reducing the number of in-domain sentences used to train the classifier from 6,000 down to 100, the authors clearly show that their `sscnn` method is not only better than the baselines but also more robust to data scarcity. The fact that the performance gap *widens* as the data size shrinks provides powerful evidence for the paper's central claim. The stability check with multiple random samples of 100 sentences further strengthens this finding.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited novelty of the CNN architecture itself.** The paper's novelty lies in the *application* of the semi-supervised CNN to the SMT data selection task, not in the neural architecture. The described `sscnn` model appears to be a direct implementation of the model from Johnson and Zhang (2015b), which the authors appropriately cite. The paper would be slightly stronger if it more explicitly framed its contribution as the novel application of this existing architecture to a new problem and perhaps briefly discussed why this specific architecture is well-suited for domain classification over other alternatives.

2.  **Lack of clarity in some model details.** The description of the "one-hot CNN" in Section 3.1 is slightly ambiguous. It mentions that the input `wl(x)` can be either a concatenation of one-hot vectors (`seq-CNN`) or a bag-of-words vector (`bow-CNN`). In the experimental setup (Section 5.2), it simply states "we generate both bow-regions and seq-regions and input them to the CNN". It is unclear if both were used simultaneously, or if one was chosen over the other. This lack of precision hinders full reproducibility.

3.  **Evaluation is limited to Phrase-Based SMT.** At the time of this work, Neural Machine Translation (NMT) was rapidly becoming the dominant paradigm. The paper focuses exclusively on adapting a phrase-based SMT system. While the findings are valuable, the paper's impact would be broader if it included a discussion or preliminary experiments on how this data selection technique could be applied to NMT adaptation (e.g., for fine-tuning a pre-trained NMT model). This makes the work feel slightly dated from a contemporary perspective.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the input representation for the CNNs: For the `ohcnn` model and the `wl(x)` component of the `sscnn` model, did you use a bag-of-words representation of the text region, a concatenation of one-hot vectors, or a combination of both? Clarifying this detail would improve the paper's reproducibility.

2.  The number of selected sentences reported in Table 2 varies substantially across tasks (from 300K to 1.6M). The paper states this was determined on a validation set. Could you elaborate on this process? Was it a simple grid search? Does the optimal size of the selected data subset provide any insight into the nature of the general-domain corpus or the selection method's precision?

3.  Have you considered the applicability of this method to NMT? Data selection is also a key strategy for NMT adaptation. Do you believe the CNN-based selection would be as effective, or perhaps even more so, when the selected data is used to fine-tune a pre-trained NMT model rather than training a phrase-based SMT system from scratch?
