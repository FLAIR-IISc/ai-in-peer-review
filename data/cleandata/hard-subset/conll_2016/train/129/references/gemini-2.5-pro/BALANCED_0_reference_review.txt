### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a challenging but practical scenario: when only a tiny amount of in-domain parallel data is available (as few as 100 sentence pairs). The authors propose using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) as a classifier to select domain-relevant sentence pairs from a large general-domain corpus. To overcome the scarcity of labeled in-domain data for training the classifier, they employ a semi-supervised approach where the CNN is initialized with word embeddings pre-trained on large unlabeled corpora. The selected data is then used to train a phrase-based SMT system.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A novel and effective method for data selection in low-resource domain adaptation.** The paper successfully reframes the data selection problem from a language model perplexity task to a text classification task and demonstrates that a semi-supervised CNN is a superior tool for this purpose, especially when in-domain supervision is extremely limited.
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of the method's robustness.** The authors conduct extensive experiments across four language pairs and diverse social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). The results not only show significant BLEU score improvements over strong baselines but also compellingly illustrate that the performance advantage of the CNN-based method increases as the amount of available in-domain data decreases.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Well-Motivated and Practical Problem:** The paper tackles the scenario of domain adaptation with "tiny" amounts of in-domain data. This is a highly relevant problem, as collecting large, labeled in-domain corpora for every new domain (e.g., emerging social media topics, specialized technical fields) is often prohibitively expensive. The work provides a practical solution for improving MT performance in these common, resource-scarce situations.

2.  **Novel Application of CNNs for Data Selection:** While previous work relied on language model perplexity, this paper creatively applies CNNs to the data selection task. The motivation is sound: CNNs are well-suited to identifying local, position-invariant n-gram features indicative of a domain, while the semi-supervised use of pre-trained word embeddings provides crucial semantic generalization that is essential when the labeled training set is small. This approach is arguably a better fit for identifying topical relevance than sentence-level fluency measures like perplexity.

3.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed. The method is tested on four different language pairs and three distinct, challenging social media domains. The authors compare against strong and appropriate baselines, including both n-gram and RNNLM-based data selection methods. The experiment depicted in Figure 1 is particularly powerful, as it systematically reduces the amount of in-domain data and clearly shows the proposed `sscnn` method's superior robustness and performance in the targeted low-resource regime. The stability analysis, repeating the 100-sentence experiment three times, is a welcome addition that strengthens confidence in the results.

4.  **Significant and Consistent Performance Gains:** The proposed `sscnn` method achieves substantial and statistically significant BLEU improvements across all tasks, ranging from +0.7 to +3.1 BLEU over the `alldata` baseline and consistently outperforming the best LM-based methods. The fact that the method works well even when there is no known in-domain subset within the general training data (as in three of the four tasks) demonstrates its ability to find genuinely similar sentences, not just rediscover a pre-existing cluster.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Evaluation is Limited to Statistical Machine Translation (SMT):** The entire experimental framework is built around phrase-based SMT. While this work was likely conducted when SMT was still prominent, the field of machine translation has now largely transitioned to Neural Machine Translation (NMT). NMT models have different data appetites and sensitivities compared to SMT. A key question of the paper's current relevance is whether these data selection gains would transfer to an NMT paradigm (e.g., for fine-tuning a pre-trained NMT model). Without such experiments, the impact of the work on the current state of the art is unclear.

2.  **Lack of Deeper Qualitative Analysis:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the method works but provides limited insight into *why* it works better than LM-based approaches. The discussion in Section 5.4 is plausible but speculative. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a qualitative analysis. For example, showing examples of sentences that are highly ranked by the CNN but lowly ranked by the RNNLM (and vice versa) could provide concrete evidence for the hypothesis that CNNs are better at capturing key domain-specific terms. This would move the explanation from a hypothesis to an evidence-backed conclusion.

3.  **Insufficient Details on the Selection Criterion and Hyperparameters:** The paper states that using the sum of source and target classification scores worked best, but this important design choice is mentioned only in passing. It would be useful to know how this strategy compares to using only the source or target score. Does the optimal strategy depend on the language pair or domain? Furthermore, while the CNN architecture is described, there is no discussion of the sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., number of filters, region size) or the computational cost of training the CNN and scoring the entire general corpus compared to the LM baselines.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Given the field's shift to NMT, have you considered how this data selection method could be applied in an NMT context? For instance, could it be used to create a curated dataset for fine-tuning a large pre-trained NMT model, and do you hypothesize that the performance gains would be comparable to those seen in SMT?

2.  Could you provide a few qualitative examples of sentences from the general-domain corpus that were selected by your `sscnn` method but *not* by the `rnnlm` baseline (or were ranked much lower)? This would help to qualitatively understand the different types of sentences each method prioritizes and substantiate the claims made in your discussion section.
