**Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available (e.g., a few hundred to a few thousand sentences). The authors propose to frame this as a data selection task, using a classifier to identify in-domain-like sentences from a large general-domain training corpus. To handle the scarcity of labeled in-domain data, they employ a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (sscnn). The model is trained to distinguish the small set of in-domain sentences (positive examples) from randomly sampled general-domain sentences (negative examples), crucially augmenting the standard one-hot word representations with pre-trained word embeddings learned on the entire general-domain corpus. The trained classifier then scores all sentence pairs in the general-domain data, and the top-scoring subset is used to train a final, adapted SMT system.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  A novel and highly effective method for SMT domain adaptation that uses a semi-supervised classifier for data selection. This approach is particularly well-suited for the challenging and practical low-resource setting, where traditional perplexity-based methods are known to struggle due to data sparsity.
2.  A strong and comprehensive empirical validation across four language pairs. The experiments demonstrate that the proposed method significantly outperforms strong baselines, including RNNLM-based data selection, and that its advantage is most pronounced when the amount of in-domain seed data is extremely small.

**Strengths**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Application:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem in machine translation. While data selection for adaptation is not new, the proposed approach of using a discriminative classifier is a departure from standard LM perplexity-based methods. The motivation for using a semi-supervised CNN is particularly compelling: it directly leverages information from the large unlabeled corpus (via word embeddings) to build a robust classifier from a tiny labeled set, which is a clever way to address the core challenge of the problem.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Results:** The experiments are thorough, covering four language pairs with large-scale training data and diverse social media test domains. The proposed `sscnn` method achieves consistent and statistically significant BLEU score improvements over four baselines, including competitive LM-based selection methods. The gains are substantial (e.g., +3.1 BLEU on zh-en, +1.4 on ar-en and en-zh). The comparison to the `ohcnn` baseline effectively isolates and demonstrates the critical contribution of the semi-supervised component (the pre-trained embeddings).

3.  **Excellent Analysis in the Low-Resource Setting:** The experiment presented in Figure 1 is a key strength of the paper. It directly evaluates the central claim of the work—effectiveness with a "tiny amount of in-domain data"—by varying the size of the seed set from 6,000 down to 100 sentences. The results compellingly show that while all methods degrade with less data, the CNN-based methods, and `sscnn` in particular, are far more robust. The performance gap widens dramatically in the very low-resource regime, highlighting the practical value of the proposed technique. The stability analysis, showing low variance across different random samples of 100 sentences, further strengthens this conclusion.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Qualitative Analysis and Insight:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of *why* the CNN-based selection is superior. The discussion in Section 5.4 is plausible but remains speculative. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a qualitative error analysis. For example, providing examples of sentences that were correctly selected by `sscnn` but missed by the `rnnlm` baseline (or vice-versa) could offer concrete evidence for the claim that the CNN better captures local domain indicators. This would provide valuable insight into the mechanisms behind the performance gains.

2.  **Lack of Discussion on Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The size of the selected data subset is a crucial hyperparameter that significantly impacts both system performance and training cost. The paper states this was tuned on a validation set, but provides no analysis of how sensitive the final BLEU score is to this choice. A plot showing BLEU score as a function of the selected data size (for a given classifier) would be very informative for practitioners wanting to apply this method. Without this, it is difficult to gauge the cost-performance trade-off or understand how to set this parameter without an expensive search.

3.  **Methodological Novelty is in Application, Not the Model:** The paper is transparent that the semi-supervised CNN architecture is adapted from prior work in text classification (Johnson and Zhang, 2015b). Therefore, the primary novelty lies in the application of this existing model to the task of data selection for SMT adaptation. While this is a valid and impactful contribution, it is important to recognize that the paper does not propose a new neural network architecture or training algorithm.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  You mention that selecting sentence pairs based on the sum of source and target scores performed best. Could you provide more intuition on this? Did you analyze cases where, for instance, a high score on the target side compensated for a low score on the source side? It seems plausible that source-side matching would be more critical, so it would be interesting to understand the contribution of the target-side score.

2.  In the low-resource experiment (Figure 1), the amount of data selected for MT training was fixed at 300K sentences, even as the classifier training data was reduced to 100 sentences. Did you investigate whether the optimal size of the selected data subset changes with the size of the in-domain seed data? One might hypothesize that a classifier trained on fewer examples is "noisier," and that perhaps selecting a smaller, higher-confidence subset of data might be more effective in that scenario.
