### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a setting where only a very small amount of in-domain data (a few hundred sentences) is available. The authors propose a data selection approach that frames the problem as a binary classification task: identifying in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus. The core of their method is a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) classifier. This classifier is trained on the tiny in-domain set (positive examples) and randomly sampled general-domain sentences (negative examples), but crucially leverages word embeddings pre-trained on a large unlabeled corpus to overcome data sparsity. The trained classifier then scores all sentences in the general-domain corpus, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system.

The main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel application of semi-supervised CNNs for SMT data selection.** Instead of relying on language model perplexity, which can be unreliable when trained on very small seed sets, this work introduces a discriminative classification approach that leverages the feature extraction power of CNNs and the generalization capabilities of pre-trained word embeddings.
2.  **Strong empirical evidence demonstrating the method's effectiveness in extremely low-resource scenarios.** The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation on four language pairs with large-scale training data. The proposed `sscnn` method consistently and significantly outperforms strong data selection baselines (n-gram and RNN LMs), especially when the in-domain seed data is reduced to as few as 100 sentences, a scenario where LM-based methods falter.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackles an important and practical problem.** Domain adaptation is crucial for real-world MT systems, and the scenario of having only a "tiny" amount of in-domain data (e.g., a small development set, a handful of user-provided examples) is very realistic, particularly for emerging domains like social media. The paper's focus on this challenging setting is highly relevant.

2.  **Novel and well-motivated methodology.** The shift from generative LM-based scoring to a discriminative classification framework is a clever way to approach the problem. The use of a semi-supervised CNN is particularly well-motivated: the convolutional layers can identify domain-indicative n-grams, while the pre-trained word embeddings provide the necessary semantic generalization to overcome the extreme sparsity of the labeled training data. This allows the model to recognize domain-relevant words (e.g., 'Lenovo') even if they don't appear in the seed set, by leveraging their proximity to words that do (e.g., 'Dell') in the embedding space.

3.  **Convincing and thorough experimental results.** The authors compare their method against strong and appropriate baselines (Axelrod et al., 2011; Duh et al., 2013) across four different language pairs and domains. The consistent improvements shown in Table 2 are impressive. More importantly, the analysis in Figure 1 directly supports the central claim of the paper: the proposed method shows remarkable robustness and graceful degradation as the amount of in-domain seed data shrinks, significantly outperforming LM-based methods in the sub-1000 sentence regime. The stability check with 100-sentence samples further strengthens this finding.

4.  **The paper is clearly written and well-structured.** The motivation is clear, the methodology is explained well, and the experiments are easy to follow. The connection to prior work in both SMT adaptation and CNNs for text classification is well-established.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of qualitative analysis.** The paper convincingly shows *that* the method works, but provides limited insight into *why* it works better than the baselines beyond high-level intuition. A qualitative analysis of the selected data would be highly beneficial. For example, are the CNN-selected sentences more topically coherent? Do they contain more domain-specific entities that were unseen in the seed data but identified via word embeddings? Presenting a few examples of sentences ranked highly by `sscnn` but lowly by `rnnlm` (and vice-versa) would make the contribution more concrete.

2.  **Details on hyperparameter tuning are sparse.** The paper states that the number of selected sentences for the MT system was "empirically determined... on a separate validation set." This is a critical hyperparameter. It's unclear how this validation set was constructed (was it a split of the original dev set?), and how sensitive the final BLEU score is to this choice. Given that the in-domain dev set is already used to train the classifier, there's a potential risk of overfitting to this single set if it's also used for tuning the selection size. More clarity on this process is needed.

3.  **Computational cost is not addressed.** The proposed method involves training a CNN and then running inference over the entire multi-million sentence training corpus (for both source and target sides). This is almost certainly more computationally intensive than scoring the corpus with a pre-trained n-gram or even an RNN language model. A discussion of the computational trade-offs would be valuable for practitioners considering this method. How long does it take to train the classifier and score the full corpus compared to the LM baselines?

### Questions to Authors

1.  Regarding the selection of the number of sentences for MT training (the 'N' in "top N"): How was this parameter tuned? Was a portion of the in-domain dev set held out for this purpose? Could you provide some insight into the sensitivity of the final MT performance to this parameter? For example, how much does the BLEU score for the `sscnn` method on the zh2en task change if you select 200K or 400K sentences instead of 300K?

2.  Could you provide a brief qualitative comparison of the data selected by `sscnn` versus `rnnlm`? For instance, does your method favor sentences with specific keywords that are semantically related to the dev set, whereas the LM-based method favors sentences with more general stylistic or fluency matches? A few illustrative examples would be very helpful.

3.  Could you comment on the computational overhead of your approach compared to the `rnnlm` baseline? Specifically, what is the approximate time required for (a) training the `sscnn` classifier and (b) scoring the 12.2M sentence zh-en corpus, and how does this compare to the time taken by the RNNLM baseline?
