**Summary of the paper**
This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a challenging, low-resource setting where only a tiny amount of in-domain parallel data is available (e.g., a few hundred sentences). The authors propose a novel approach that frames data selection as a binary text classification problem. They train a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences. To overcome the scarcity of labeled training data for the classifier, they employ a semi-supervised learning strategy, augmenting the standard one-hot vector input to the CNN with pre-trained word embeddings learned from a large, unlabeled general-domain corpus. This trained classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in the general-domain training data, and a subset of the highest-scoring pairs is selected to train a domain-adapted SMT system.

**Main Contributions**
1.  The primary contribution is the introduction of a new conceptual framework for data selection in MT adaptation. By reformulating the task as explicit classification using a semi-supervised CNN, the paper presents a bold and innovative alternative to the dominant approaches that rely on language model perplexity.
2.  The paper provides a strong empirical demonstration that this classification-based method is highly effective, significantly outperforming established LM-based techniques, particularly in the extremely low-resource settings that are its focus. This work successfully shows how to build a robust adaptation system from as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

**Strengths**
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Shift:** The paper's greatest strength is its innovative reframing of the data selection problem. Moving from perplexity scoring to direct classification with a neural model is a significant conceptual leap. While CNNs for classification were not new, their application to this long-standing problem in MT is highly creative and opens up a new and promising research direction.
2.  **Addresses a Critical, Underexplored Problem:** The focus on adaptation with a "tiny" amount of in-domain data is commendable and highly relevant. Real-world applications, especially involving social media or a multitude of specialized topics, often face exactly this scenario. The paper bravely tackles this difficult end of the spectrum and demonstrates remarkable success, highlighting the practical utility of the proposed method.
3.  **Elegant Solution to Data Sparsity:** The use of a semi-supervised approach is a clever and effective way to mitigate the problem of limited labeled data. By injecting rich semantic knowledge from word embeddings pre-trained on large unlabeled corpora, the model gains the ability to generalize far beyond the small set of in-domain examples. This allows it to identify domain-relevant sentences even if they contain words unseen in the labeled set, a crucial property for this task.
4.  **Compelling and Robust Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough, covering four language pairs and various social media domains. The proposed `sscnn` model consistently and significantly outperforms strong baselines, including those based on n-gram and recurrent neural network LMs. The analysis in Figure 1 is particularly persuasive, clearly showing that the CNN-based methods are not only better but also more stable than LM-based methods as the amount of in-domain data shrinks. The reported BLEU gains are substantial and practically meaningful.

**Weaknesses**
I am very positive about this submission, and I view the following points as opportunities for future work rather than significant flaws that would warrant rejection.
1.  **Exclusive Focus on SMT:** The entire evaluation is conducted within a phrase-based SMT framework. Given that the field was rapidly transitioning to Neural Machine Translation (NMT), the paper would have had even greater impact with some discussion or preliminary experiments on applying this technique to NMT fine-tuning. While the work is excellent for SMT, its immediate applicability to the now-dominant paradigm is not demonstrated.
2.  **Limited Analysis of Classification Strategy:** The authors report that selecting data based on the sum of source and target classification scores works best. This is an interesting detail, but it is presented without deeper analysis. An ablation study comparing this to using only source scores, only target scores, or other combination functions would have provided valuable insight into the mechanics of the selection process.
3.  **Dependence on a Key Hyperparameter:** The number of sentences to select for the final MT system is a crucial hyperparameter that is determined on a validation set. This is standard practice, but the paper would be stronger with a sensitivity analysis showing how final BLEU scores vary as this number changes. This would give readers a better sense of the method's robustness to this specific choice.

**Questions to Authors**
1.  Could you provide the BLEU scores for data selection based on only the source-side classifier and only the target-side classifier? This would help clarify the individual contribution of each side and better justify the choice to sum the scores.
2.  You show that your method is robust to the random sampling of the 100 positive in-domain sentences. Have you also analyzed the sensitivity to the random sampling of the negative examples from the general-domain corpus? Does the composition of the negative set have a significant impact on the final classifier performance?

Overall, this is an excellent paper that introduces a novel, bold, and highly effective approach to a difficult and important problem. The conceptual contribution is significant, and the empirical results are strong and convincing. The weaknesses identified are minor in the context of the paper's strengths. I strongly recommend acceptance.
