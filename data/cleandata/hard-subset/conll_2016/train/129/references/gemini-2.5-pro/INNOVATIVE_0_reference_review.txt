### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper tackles the challenging problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a very small amount of in-domain data is available (as few as 100 sentences). The authors propose a novel approach that frames data selection as a text classification task. Their key idea is to train a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences. The classifier is trained on the tiny in-domain set as positive examples and a random sample of general-domain data as negative examples. The "semi-supervised" aspect comes from augmenting the standard one-hot input to the CNN with pre-trained word embeddings learned from the entire large, unlabeled general-domain corpus. This allows the model to leverage rich lexical-semantic information to overcome the scarcity of labeled data. The trained classifier is then used to score and select the most relevant sentences from the general-domain corpus to train a domain-adapted SMT system.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A New Paradigm for Data Selection in Low-Resource Settings:** The paper introduces a bold and effective method for data selection by reframing it as a classification task solved with a semi-supervised CNN. This is a significant departure from established perplexity-based methods and proves to be particularly powerful in the "tiny data" regime, an important and under-explored problem space.
2.  **Compelling Empirical Validation of Robustness:** The paper provides strong evidence that the proposed method is not just effective, but dramatically more robust to a scarcity of in-domain data than strong LM-based baselines. The demonstration that significant BLEU gains can be achieved with as few as 100 seed sentences is a powerful and convincing result.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Impact:** The central idea of using a semi-supervised CNN for data selection is highly innovative. At a time when most adaptation research focused on refining perplexity-based methods or instance weighting, this work proposes a fundamentally different, discriminative approach. By showing how to effectively leverage large unlabeled corpora via word embeddings to bootstrap a powerful classifier from almost no labeled data, the paper opens a new and exciting direction for domain adaptation research, particularly for practical, low-resource scenarios. This is precisely the kind of bold, concept-driven work the field needs.

2.  **Addresses a Critical and Practical Problem:** The paper tackles the problem of adaptation with a *tiny* amount of data. This is a much harder and more realistic scenario than that addressed by much of the prior work, which often assumes the availability of thousands of in-domain sentences. The ability to adapt a massive SMT system to a new domain like "tweets" or "sms" with just a few hundred examples is a significant practical breakthrough. The chosen experimental domains (social media) perfectly highlight the relevance of this problem.

3.  **Extremely Strong and Clear Empirical Results:** The experiments are well-designed and the results are striking. The proposed `sscnn` method not only outperforms all baselines across four different language pairs but does so by a significant margin. The analysis presented in Figure 1 is the paper's crowning achievement, clearly illustrating the method's superior performance and stability as the seed data size shrinks. A 2.1 BLEU improvement from just 100 sentences is a fantastic result that speaks for itself and powerfully validates the authors' core claims. The fact that this performance is stable across different random samples further strengthens the finding.

4.  **Methodological Elegance:** The proposed solution is elegant in its simplicity. It does not invent a complex new neural architecture but instead cleverly combines existing, well-understood components (a standard CNN, pre-trained word embeddings) to solve a difficult problem. This demonstrates that a deep understanding of the problem can lead to a powerful solution without unnecessary complexity.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, there are a few areas that could be strengthened. I consider these to be minor points that do not detract from the core contribution, but are worth noting.

1.  **Limited Qualitative Analysis:** The paper provides a strong quantitative case but offers limited qualitative insight into *why* the CNN-based selection is superior. The discussion in Section 5.4 is plausible but speculative. It would have been more convincing to include examples of sentences that were correctly selected by the `sscnn` but missed by the LM-based methods. An analysis of what the convolutional filters might be learning could also have provided valuable insight into the model's decision-making process.

2.  **Unexplored Sensitivity to Embeddings:** The success of the semi-supervised model is clearly tied to the information captured in the pre-trained word embeddings. The paper does not investigate how sensitive the method is to the size, quality, or training data of these embeddings. For instance, would the method still work well if the general-domain corpus used to train the embeddings had a significant vocabulary mismatch with the target domain? Understanding these dependencies would better define the applicability of the method.

3.  **Heuristic for Score Combination:** The method for combining the source and target classification scores is a simple sum. While this heuristic is reasonable and effective, it feels somewhat arbitrary. A brief exploration or discussion of alternative combination strategies (e.g., product, a weighted sum, using only the source score) would have made the methodological description more complete, even if the final results were unchanged.
