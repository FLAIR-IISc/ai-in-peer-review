### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a very small amount of in-domain data is available (a few hundred sentences). The authors propose framing data selection as a binary classification problem: identifying in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus. To overcome the lack of labeled data, they employ a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). The model is trained on the tiny in-domain set (positive examples) and randomly sampled general-domain sentences (negative examples), but critically, it is augmented with word embeddings pre-trained on the entire large, unlabeled general-domain corpus. This allows the model to leverage rich semantic information to generalize effectively from the small seed set. The selected data is then used to train an adapted SMT system.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and effective application of semi-supervised CNNs for SMT data selection.** While CNNs are standard for text classification, their use as a data selection mechanism for domain adaptation, particularly in a low-resource setting, is a significant and well-executed idea.
2.  **Strong empirical evidence demonstrating the method's superiority in extremely low-resource scenarios.** The paper presents a thorough evaluation on four diverse language pairs, showing consistent and significant BLEU score improvements over strong language model-based baselines. The analysis showing the method's robustness as the in-domain seed data is reduced to just 100 sentences is particularly compelling and directly supports the paper's primary claim.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Well-Motivated and Practical Problem:** The paper tackles the important and challenging problem of domain adaptation with a "tiny" amount of seed data. This is a highly realistic scenario for many practical MT applications, such as adapting to new social media trends or specific customer domains, where collecting thousands of in-domain sentence pairs is often prohibitively expensive or time-consuming.
2.  **Elegant and Effective Solution:** The proposed method is well-designed. Framing data selection as a classification task is intuitive, and the use of pre-trained word embeddings in a semi-supervised framework is a smart and effective way to mitigate the data sparsity of the labeled set. The results strongly support this design, as the semi-supervised model (sscnn) consistently outperforms the purely supervised one-hot model (ohcnn), demonstrating the value of the unlabeled data.
3.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are a key strength of this paper. The authors evaluate their method on four different language pairs and domains, including challenging social media text (tweets, Facebook posts). They compare against multiple strong and widely-used baselines (ngram and RNNLM-based selection). The most convincing piece of evidence is Figure 1, which clearly illustrates that as the amount of in-domain data shrinks, the performance gap between the proposed CNN-based methods and the LM-based methods widens, highlighting the robustness of their approach in the target low-resource setting. The inclusion of significance tests adds rigor to their claims.
4.  **Clarity and Potential for Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, clearly structured, and easy to understand. The authors clearly explain their methodology and experimental setup. By pointing to the public toolkits and datasets used, they provide a good foundation for others to reproduce or build upon their work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty in the CNN Architecture:** The paper applies an existing CNN architecture and toolkit for text classification to a new task. While the application is novel, the paper provides little insight into the architectural choices themselves. Key hyperparameters (e.g., region size, number of neurons) are stated without justification, and there is no exploration of how different architectural choices might impact performance on this specific task. The CNN is treated largely as a black box, which slightly diminishes the technical depth of the contribution.
2.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper presents strong quantitative results but misses an opportunity to provide qualitative analysis to explain *why* the CNN-based selection is more effective than LM-based selection. The discussion in Section 5.4 offers plausible hypotheses (e.g., generalization via embeddings), but these would be far more convincing if supported by examples. For instance, showing a few sentences that were correctly identified as in-domain by `sscnn` but missed by `rnnlm` could provide valuable insight into the models' respective strengths and weaknesses.
3.  **Dated SMT Framework:** The experiments are conducted exclusively on phrase-based SMT systems. While appropriate for the time the baseline methods were developed, the field of machine translation has since been dominated by Neural Machine Translation (NMT). NMT models have different properties concerning data efficiency and domain sensitivity. It is therefore unclear whether the method and the impressive BLEU gains would transfer to modern NMT systems. This limits the immediate relevance of the findings to the current state of the art. A discussion on the potential applicability to NMT would have strengthened the paper.
4.  **Insufficient Justification for Scoring Method:** The authors state that sentence pairs were selected based on the "sum of the source and target scores," claiming this performed best. However, no data is provided to support this claim. Comparing this combination strategy to alternatives (e.g., using only the source score, only the target score, the minimum, or the product of the scores) is important, as this is a key, non-trivial step in the proposed pipeline.

### Questions to Authors

1.  You report that using the sum of source and target scores for selection performed best. Could you provide the results (or at least a summary) for alternative combination strategies (e.g., source-only, target-only, product of scores) to substantiate this claim?
2.  The paper focuses on phrase-based SMT. Have you considered how this data selection approach might apply to domain adaptation for Neural Machine Translation? NMT is often adapted via fine-tuning; do you believe selecting data for fine-tuning with your method would yield similar benefits?
3.  Could you provide some insight into the choice of CNN hyperparameters? Specifically, how sensitive was the classification performance to choices like the region size (5) and the number of neurons (500)? Was any tuning performed on a held-out set?
