### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a challenging but practical scenario: when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (e.g., a few hundred sentences). The authors propose framing data selection as a binary classification task—identifying in-domain vs. out-of-domain sentences. Their core proposal is to use a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for this classification. The model leverages word embeddings pre-trained on a large general-domain corpus to overcome the data sparsity of the small labeled (in-domain) set. The trained classifier is then used to score and select the most relevant sentence pairs from a massive general-domain corpus to build an adapted SMT system.

In my view, the main contributions are:

1.  **A Novel and Effective Application for a Practical Problem:** The primary contribution is the successful application of a semi-supervised CNN to the task of data selection for SMT adaptation, specifically demonstrating its effectiveness in a very low-resource setting. This provides a strong solution to the important and practical problem of adapting MT systems to new domains where collecting large amounts of parallel data is prohibitively expensive or time-consuming.

2.  **Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a strong empirical evaluation across four different language pairs and diverse social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). The method is shown to significantly outperform strong, established language model-based baselines, especially when the amount of in-domain seed data is extremely limited.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Well-Defined and Important Gap:** The paper convincingly argues that while domain adaptation is well-studied, the specific scenario of having only a "tiny amount" of in-domain data has been largely overlooked. This is a highly realistic scenario for many modern applications (e.g., adapting to emerging topics on social media). The work provides a clear and effective solution to this problem.

2.  **Strong and Compelling Experimental Results:** The experiments are thorough and the results are impressive. The proposed `sscnn` method consistently outperforms all baselines across four diverse tasks. The reported BLEU gains are not only statistically significant but also practically meaningful (e.g., +3.1 BLEU on zh-en, +1.4 on ar-en and en-zh). The most powerful piece of evidence is Figure 1, which clearly demonstrates that the CNN-based methods, and `sscnn` in particular, are far more robust than LM-based methods as the in-domain sample size shrinks to just 100 sentences. This provides direct and compelling support for the paper's central claims.

3.  **Well-Motivated and Clearly Explained Methodology:** The rationale for using a CNN is sound. The authors correctly point out that CNNs are well-suited to capturing local, position-invariant features (like domain-specific n-grams or keywords), and that pre-trained embeddings provide the necessary generalization power when labeled data is scarce. The description of the model and the overall adaptation pipeline is clear and easy to follow.

4.  **Good Choice of Baselines and Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors compare their method against strong and relevant baselines from prior work (Axelrod et al., 2011; Duh et al., 2013), including both n-gram and RNN-based language models. This allows for a fair assessment of their method's contribution. The use of significance testing adds rigor to their claims. The stability check (repeating the 100-sentence experiment three times) is also a nice touch that strengthens confidence in the results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core CNN architecture (combining one-hot features with pre-trained region embeddings) is not novel in itself and is directly adopted from prior work in text classification (Johnson and Zhang, 2015b), which the authors appropriately cite. The novelty lies entirely in the application of this existing model to the SMT data selection problem. While the application is successful and the results are significant, the paper does not propose a new neural architecture or learning technique.

2.  **Lack of Discussion on Computational Cost:** The proposed method requires scoring every sentence in the general-domain corpus (up to 15.2M pairs) with the trained CNN classifier. This could be a computationally expensive step compared to scoring with an n-gram LM. The paper does not provide any analysis of the computational overhead (e.g., inference time) of their method compared to the LM-based baselines. This information would be important for practitioners considering deploying this technique at scale.

3.  **Evaluation Limited to Phrase-Based SMT:** The experiments are conducted exclusively on phrase-based SMT (PBSMT) systems. While this was a standard paradigm, the field was rapidly moving towards Neural Machine Translation (NMT) at the time of this work. The paper would have a much broader impact if it demonstrated that this data selection technique is also effective for adapting NMT models (e.g., by selecting data for fine-tuning). While the principle should carry over, empirical validation is necessary.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide some details on the computational cost of the data selection step? Specifically, how does the time required to score the full general-domain corpus with the `sscnn` model compare to the `rnnlm` baseline?

2.  The negative examples for the CNN classifier are sampled randomly from the general-domain corpus. Did you consider or experiment with any form of hard negative mining? For instance, selecting sentences that are lexically similar to the in-domain data but are known to be out-of-domain might force the classifier to learn more subtle domain distinctions.

3.  Given the dominance of NMT, have you performed any preliminary experiments applying your data selection method to fine-tune a pre-trained NMT model? It would be very valuable to know if the significant gains you observed on PBSMT transfer to the neural paradigm.
