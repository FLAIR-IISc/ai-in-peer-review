### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available (as few as 100 sentence pairs). The authors propose using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to perform data selection from a large general-domain corpus. The core idea is to frame data selection as a binary classification task: a CNN is trained to distinguish in-domain sentences from out-of-domain sentences. To overcome data scarcity, the authors employ a semi-supervised approach where word embeddings pre-trained on large unlabeled corpora are used as additional input to the CNN, augmenting the supervised signal from the small labeled (in-domain) set. The trained classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in the general-domain corpus, and the top-scoring pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system.

The main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A Novel Application of CNNs for SMT Data Selection:** The paper successfully reframes the data selection problem for SMT adaptation as a text classification task and demonstrates that a semi-supervised CNN is a highly effective tool for this purpose. While CNNs for text classification were known, their application to select bilingual training data for SMT is a novel contribution.
2.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration of Effectiveness in a Low-Resource Scenario:** The paper provides compelling evidence that this method is particularly well-suited for adaptation with a tiny amount of in-domain data. The experiments, conducted across four language pairs and diverse social media domains, show that the proposed `sscnn` method significantly outperforms strong baselines, including established n-gram and RNNLM-based data selection techniques. The analysis showing robust performance with as few as 100 in-domain sentences is a key result that highlights the practical value of the approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Practical and Important Problem:** The paper tackles the challenging yet highly realistic problem of adapting MT systems to new domains where collecting large amounts of in-domain data is prohibitively expensive or time-consuming. The focus on social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) further enhances the relevance of the work, as these are dynamic domains where adaptation is crucial.

2.  **Thorough and Convincing Experimental Results:** The evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors test their method on four different language pairs with large-scale training data. The chosen baselines are strong and represent the state-of-the-art in data selection at the time (e.g., Axelrod et al., 2011; Duh et al., 2013). The consistent and significant BLEU score improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) across all tasks strongly support the paper's claims.

3.  **Excellent Analysis of Low-Data Robustness:** The experiment detailed in Figure 1, which shows the performance of different methods as the amount of in-domain training data for the classifier is reduced, is a major strength. It clearly demonstrates that the CNN-based methods, and particularly the semi-supervised `sscnn` variant, are far more robust to data scarcity than the LM-based methods. This analysis directly validates the paper's central hypothesis.

4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is clearly written and easy to follow. The methodology is explained well, and the experimental setup is detailed. The authors helpfully point to the publicly available toolkits they used for both their own method and the baselines, which is good practice and aids in reproducibility.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core CNN architecture for text classification is not novel in itself but is rather an application of existing models (e.g., Kim, 2014; Johnson & Zhang, 2015b) to a new task. The main novelty lies in the problem framing and the strong empirical results, rather than in the development of a new neural architecture. This is a minor weakness, as the contribution is clearly positioned as an application, but it is worth noting.

2.  **Lack of Intrinsic Evaluation of the Classifier:** The paper only evaluates the final SMT performance (extrinsic evaluation), which is the most important metric. However, it provides no insight into the performance of the CNN classifier itself. Reporting classification metrics like accuracy, precision, recall, or F1-score on a held-out set would have provided a clearer picture of how well the model is actually learning to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences. This could help explain *why* the `sscnn` method is superior to others at the selection stage.

3.  **Insufficient Qualitative Analysis:** The discussion section provides a plausible hypothesis for why semi-supervised CNNs perform well (capturing local features and generalizing via embeddings). However, the paper would be much stronger if this was supported by qualitative analysis. For example, showing a few example sentences that were correctly selected by `sscnn` but missed by the LM-based methods or the `ohcnn` baseline would provide concrete evidence for the benefits of the approach.

4.  **Focus on SMT:** The work is evaluated exclusively within a phrase-based SMT framework. While this was standard at the time, the field has largely shifted to Neural Machine Translation (NMT). The paper would have broader impact if it included a brief discussion on how this data selection methodology could be adapted for fine-tuning pre-trained NMT models or selecting data for continued NMT training.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you report the classification performance (e.g., accuracy or F1-score) of your `ohcnn` and `sscnn` models on a held-out set of labeled in-domain/out-of-domain data? This would help clarify how much of the final BLEU improvement comes from having a more accurate sentence-level domain classifier.

2.  In Section 4, you state that you classify source and target sentences separately and found that selecting based on the sum of their scores works best. Could you provide more detail on this? Specifically, what were the results when selecting based on only the source-side score or only the target-side score? Is the contribution from both sides roughly equal, or is one more informative for domain classification?
