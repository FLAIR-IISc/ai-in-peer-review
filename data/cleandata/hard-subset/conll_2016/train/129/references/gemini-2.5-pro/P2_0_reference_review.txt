**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a low-resource scenario, where only a tiny amount of in-domain parallel data (as few as 100 sentence pairs) is available. The authors frame the data selection task as a binary classification problem (in-domain vs. out-of-domain) and propose using a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to perform this classification. The CNN is trained on the small set of in-domain data (positive examples) and a random sample of general-domain data (negative examples). The "semi-supervised" aspect comes from initializing the CNN with pre-trained word embeddings learned on a large, unlabeled corpus. The trained classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in the large general-domain pool, and the top-scoring pairs are selected to train a smaller, adapted SMT system.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A novel and effective method for SMT data selection in an extreme low-resource setting.** The paper successfully reframes data selection from a language model perplexity-based task to a text classification task. The use of a semi-supervised CNN is well-motivated, as pre-trained embeddings are crucial for generalization when labeled data is scarce.
2.  **Strong empirical evidence demonstrating the superiority of the proposed approach over established baselines.** The experiments are comprehensive, covering four language pairs and diverse social media domains. The results convincingly show that the semi-supervised CNN method significantly outperforms strong language model-based selection techniques, especially when the amount of seed in-domain data is reduced to just a few hundred sentences.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a significant and practical problem.** The scenario of adapting a large, general MT system to a new, specific domain with very little available data is highly realistic. As the authors note, this is common for social media domains or highly specialized topics where data collection is expensive. This makes the work highly relevant and potentially impactful.
2.  **The experimental results are strong and compelling.** The proposed `sscnn` method consistently outperforms all baselines, including a system trained on all data and three strong LM-based selection methods. The BLEU gains are substantial (e.g., +3.1 for zh-en, +1.4 for en-zh). The ablation study comparing `sscnn` with `ohcnn` clearly demonstrates the value of the pre-trained embeddings. Furthermore, the analysis in Figure 1, which shows the method's robustness as the amount of in-domain data shrinks to just 100 sentences, is a very powerful demonstration of its effectiveness in the target scenario.
3.  **The paper is well-written, clear, and easy to follow.** The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, the related work is appropriately situated, the proposed method is described with sufficient detail, and the experimental setup is sound and reproducible. The authors make their claims and back them up with solid evidence.
4.  **Clever application of existing techniques to a new problem.** While the CNN architecture itself is not novel, its application to the data selection problem for MT adaptation is. Framing the problem as classification allows the model to leverage rich, distributed representations (word embeddings), which can capture semantic similarities that n-gram or even RNN language models might miss, as discussed in Section 5.4. This is a key insight of the paper.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Exclusively focused on Statistical Machine Translation (SMT).** The paper is framed entirely within the context of phrase-based SMT. At the time of this review, Neural Machine Translation (NMT) has largely superseded SMT as the state-of-the-art. The authors do not discuss or evaluate how their data selection method would apply to adapting NMT models. Adaptation for NMT often involves fine-tuning on in-domain data, and it is not obvious that the optimal data selection strategy for training a phrase-based SMT system from scratch would be the same for fine-tuning a pre-trained NMT model. This limits the paper's immediate relevance and impact on the current MT landscape.
2.  **The analysis of why the CNN works better is somewhat superficial.** The discussion in Section 5.4 provides plausible hypotheses (capturing local features via convolutions, generalization via embeddings) but lacks concrete, qualitative analysis. It would have been more convincing to include examples of sentences that were correctly selected by the CNN but missed by the LM-based methods (and vice-versa). This would provide deeper insight into the qualitative differences between the approaches beyond just the final BLEU scores.
3.  **The number of selected sentences is a critical hyperparameter that requires a validation set.** The method relies on empirically determining the optimal number of sentences to select (e.g., 300K for zh-en, 1300K for en-es) by tuning on a validation set. In a true low-resource scenario, a sufficiently large and representative in-domain validation set may not be available. The paper does not explore the sensitivity of the results to this parameter or suggest a more principled way of setting this threshold.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Your work convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of CNN-based data selection for SMT. Could you elaborate on how you envision this method being applied in the context of Neural Machine Translation (NMT)? Would you use the selected data for fine-tuning a pre-trained NMT model, or for training an NMT model from scratch? Do you anticipate similar performance gains in an NMT paradigm?
2.  The number of sentences to select for the final MT system training appears to be a crucial hyperparameter tuned on held-out data. How sensitive is the final BLEU score to this number? For example, in the zh-en task where you selected 300K sentences, what would the performance be if you selected 200K or 500K sentences instead? This would help clarify how critical this tuning process is.
3.  Could you provide a few examples of source or target sentences that receive a high in-domain score from your `sscnn` model but a low score from the `rnnlm` baseline? This would provide valuable intuition about what kind of domain-relevant information the CNN is capturing that the language model is missing.
