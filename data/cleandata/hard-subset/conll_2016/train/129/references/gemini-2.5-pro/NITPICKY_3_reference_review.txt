**Summary of the paper and contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) in a setting where only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available (as few as 100 sentence pairs). The authors propose using a convolutional neural network (CNN) to classify sentences from a large general-domain corpus as either in-domain or out-of-domain. These classifications are then used to select a subset of the general-domain data for training an adapted SMT system. The main contribution is an empirical demonstration that a "semi-supervised" CNN, which incorporates pre-trained word embeddings from the general-domain corpus, significantly outperforms strong language model-based data selection baselines, especially when the amount of in-domain seed data is extremely limited. A secondary contribution is the analysis showing the robustness of this method across four different language pairs and domains.

**Strengths**

1.  **Addresses a Relevant and Challenging Problem:** The scenario of domain adaptation with a "tiny" amount of seed data is highly practical. As new domains emerge, particularly in social media, it is often prohibitively expensive to collect large in-domain corpora. The paper's focus on making use of as few as 100 sentences is well-motivated and valuable to the community.

2.  **Strong and Consistent Empirical Results:** The proposed method (`sscnn`) achieves the best performance across all four tasks, with substantial BLEU score improvements over a baseline trained on all available data (e.g., +3.1 BLEU for zh-en). More importantly, it consistently outperforms strong, widely-used LM-based selection methods (`ngram`, `rnnlm`), with significance testing to support the claims.

3.  **Effective Low-Data Performance:** The most compelling argument is presented in Figure 1. This experiment clearly demonstrates that as the amount of in-domain seed data shrinks, the proposed `sscnn` method's performance degrades gracefully and maintains a significant advantage over other methods. This robustness in the extreme low-data regime is the key strength of the paper.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Severe Lack of Methodological Clarity and Reproducibility:** The description of the core CNN models in Section 3 is critically underspecified. The explanation of the "one-hot CNN" is confusing; representing a sentence or window with a concatenation of one-hot vectors is computationally infeasible, and the description seems to conflate one-hot vectors with bag-of-words representations. Furthermore, the construction of the input vectors `wl(x)` (one-hot) and `ul(x)` (embedding) in Equation 2 is not defined. The paper mentions using both "bow-regions" and "seq-regions" but does not explain how they are generated or combined in the model architecture. Simply citing an external toolkit is insufficient for a scientific paper; the model architecture must be described in enough detail to be reimplemented. This lack of precision is a major flaw.

2.  **Questionable Baseline Behavior and Analysis:** The behavior of the LM-based baselines in Figure 1 is highly suspect and unexplained. The performance of `ngram` and `comblm` methods decreases when the in-domain sample size increases from 400 to 800. This is counter-intuitive and suggests a potential flaw in the experimental setup or tuning process for the baselines. Without a plausible explanation, this casts doubt on the fairness of the comparison. Additionally, the authors' claim that LM-based methods achieve only a "small improvement" with 100 sentences is a mischaracterization; the graph shows improvements of 0.6-1.0 BLEU, which is not insignificant.

3.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:** The paper is marred by numerous errors that betray a lack of care in its preparation. The bibliography is severely corrupted, with multiple entries showing raw BibTeX errors (e.g., `Axelrod et al\.,? \Q2011\E, Axelrod et al\.,`). There are also grammatical errors ("The later" instead of "The latter", "unlabled"), inconsistent use of diacritics ("Lü" vs. the correct form in other citations), and awkward phrasing ("as same as in"). While individually minor, the sheer number of such issues undermines the overall credibility and professionalism of the work.

4.  **Overstated Novelty of "Semi-Supervised" Framework:** The paper frames the use of pre-trained word embeddings as its semi-supervised contribution. However, using embeddings pre-trained on large, unlabeled corpora as input features for a supervised model is a standard and ubiquitous technique in modern NLP. While effective, labeling this as a novel semi-supervised framework overstates the methodological contribution. The contribution is the application of this standard technique to the data selection task, not the technique itself.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you provide a precise architectural and mathematical description of your CNN models? Specifically, how are the input vectors for "bow-regions" and "seq-regions" constructed from a text segment, and how are they combined as input to the network? What are the dimensions of `wl(x)` and `ul(x)` in Equation 2?

2.  Can you explain the erratic behavior of the LM-based baselines in Figure 1, where performance sometimes degrades as more in-domain data is added (e.g., between 400 and 800 sentences)? Was a fixed selection threshold used, or was the number of selected sentences re-tuned for each data point?

3.  The number of selected sentences in Table 2 is determined on a "separate validation set." Was this validation set disjoint from the `dev` set used to provide the positive examples for training the CNN classifier? Using the same data for both would constitute training on the validation set.

4.  For the significance tests in Table 2, how was the single "best LM-based method" chosen for comparison, given that the best-performing baseline differs across tasks?
