### Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available (as few as 100 sentence pairs). The authors propose to reframe the standard data selection problem as a binary text classification task. They employ a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to classify sentences from a large general-domain corpus as either in-domain or out-of-domain. Their primary proposal is a semi-supervised CNN model (`sscnn`) which augments a standard one-hot CNN (`ohcnn`) with pre-trained word embeddings learned from the large general-domain corpus. The trained classifier is then used to score all sentences in the general-domain pool, and the top-scoring sentences are selected to train an adapted SMT system. Experiments on four language pairs show that this method outperforms strong language model-based data selection baselines.

### Contributions
1.  The paper presents an empirical study showing that a semi-supervised CNN can be effectively used for data selection in SMT adaptation, outperforming established language model-based approaches.
2.  The work provides evidence that this classification-based approach is particularly robust in extremely low-resource settings (i.e., with only a few hundred in-domain seed sentences), a scenario where traditional perplexity-based methods are shown to be less effective.

### Strengths
1.  **Well-motivated Problem:** The paper tackles a practical and challenging problem. Domain adaptation with only a "tiny" amount of in-domain data is a realistic scenario, particularly for emerging domains like social media, and existing methods that rely on building reliable in-domain language models often struggle in this setting.
2.  **Strong Baselines:** The authors compare their method against appropriate and strong baselines from the literature, including both n-gram and RNN language model-based data selection (`ngram`, `rnnlm`). This provides a solid foundation for evaluating the performance of the proposed approach.
3.  **Comprehensive Experimental Results:** The proposed `sscnn` method demonstrates consistent and statistically significant improvements over all baselines across four different language pairs and domains. The BLEU score gains are non-trivial, suggesting a real practical benefit.
4.  **Effective Low-Resource Performance:** The experiment detailed in Figure 1, which varies the amount of in-domain seed data, is a key strength. It convincingly demonstrates that the proposed CNN-based methods, especially `sscnn`, are more stable and effective than LM-based methods when the seed data is sparse. This directly supports the paper's central claim.

### Weaknesses
1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core components of the proposed method are not new. Framing data selection as a classification problem is a straightforward idea. The CNN architecture itself is a standard one for text classification (Kim, 2014; Johnson and Zhang, 2015b), and the use of pre-trained word embeddings as features (the "semi-supervised" aspect) has been a standard technique in NLP for several years. The contribution appears to be more of a successful application of existing tools to a new task, rather than a novel methodology.
2.  **Insufficient Justification for Complexity:** The proposed approach introduces significant complexity compared to the established LM-based methods. It requires training word embeddings and then training and tuning a separate, complex neural network classifier. The baselines, particularly the n-gram LM, are far simpler to implement and faster to run. While the results are better, the paper does not include a comparison to a simpler classification baseline, such as a logistic regression or SVM classifier with n-gram features. Such a baseline would be crucial for determining whether the performance gains stem from the sophisticated convolutional architecture or simply from framing the problem as a discriminative classification task. Without this comparison, the necessity of using a CNN is not fully established.
3.  **Lack of In-depth Analysis:** The discussion (Section 5.4) on *why* the CNN performs better is speculative and lacks empirical support. The authors suggest that convolution and pooling help identify key domain-specific phrases, and that word embeddings help generalize to unseen words (e.g., classifying a sentence with "Lenovo" based on seeing "Dell" in training). While these are plausible hypotheses, the paper provides no qualitative analysis to back them up. An analysis of the features learned by the CNN or case studies of sentences where the CNN and LM-based methods disagree would have made the claims much more convincing.
4.  **Details Regarding Hyperparameter Tuning:** Key details about the experimental setup seem to be missing. The number of selected sentences for the final SMT system is a critical hyperparameter that was "empirically determine[d]...on a separate validation set." The sensitivity of the system's performance to this parameter is not discussed. Furthermore, while some CNN meta-parameters are listed, crucial training details like learning rate, optimizer, number of epochs, and regularization are omitted, which could pose challenges for reproducibility.

### Questions to Authors
1.  Could you provide a comparison against a simpler, well-established classification model like logistic regression with bag-of-n-grams features? This would help to better isolate the contribution of the convolutional architecture over and above simply using a discriminative classifier for this task.
2.  The discussion section offers an intuitive explanation for the success of the word embeddings (generalizing from "Dell" to "Lenovo"). Could you provide any concrete examples from your experiments where your `sscnn` model correctly classifies a sentence containing an important but unseen in-domain word, while the baseline methods fail? This would provide stronger evidence for your hypothesis.
3.  The number of sentences selected for MT training appears to vary significantly across tasks (from 300K to 1600K). How was this number optimized? How sensitive is the final BLEU score to this selection threshold? Does your method produce a smoother performance curve with respect to this threshold compared to the LM baselines?
