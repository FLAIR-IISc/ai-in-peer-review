### Summary

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available (e.g., a few hundred to a few thousand sentence pairs). The authors propose framing the adaptation problem as a data selection task, using a classifier to identify in-domain-like sentences from a large general-domain corpus. The core of their method is a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for this classification task. The CNN is trained using the small in-domain set as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negative examples. The "semi-supervised" aspect is realized by incorporating pre-trained word embeddings (learned via word2vec on the large general-domain corpus) as input features to the CNN, alongside one-hot representations. This leverages unlabeled data to improve classifier generalization. The trained classifier is then used to score all sentence pairs in the general-domain corpus, and the top-scoring pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system. The authors conduct experiments on four language pairs with social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating that their method significantly outperforms both a baseline trained on all data and established language-model-based data selection methods.

### Contributions

1.  **A novel application of semi-supervised CNNs for SMT data selection in a low-resource adaptation setting.** While CNNs for text classification and data selection for MT are established techniques, this work successfully combines them to address the specific and challenging problem of adaptation with a tiny amount of in-domain data. The paper demonstrates that this approach is particularly robust when the available in-domain data is extremely limited (as few as 100 sentences).
2.  **A strong empirical demonstration of the proposed method's effectiveness across multiple language pairs and domains.** The paper provides a thorough evaluation against strong, standard baselines on four different translation tasks. The results consistently show significant BLEU score improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU), validating the approach's utility and generalizability for adapting SMT to specialized domains like social media.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a significant and practical problem.** The scenario of adapting a translation system to a new domain with very limited labeled data is a common and important challenge. The rise of new domains, such as various forms of social media, makes this problem highly relevant. The paper offers a well-motivated and effective solution to this problem.
2.  **Technically sound and thorough experimental validation.** The authors compare their proposed method (sscnn) against four relevant baselines, including a system trained on all data and three established language-model-based data selection techniques (ngram, rnnlm, comblm). The experiments span four different language pairs and domains, which demonstrates the robustness of the approach. The inclusion of significance tests adds confidence to the reported results.
3.  **The results in the extremely low-data regime are particularly compelling.** The experiment detailed in Figure 1, which shows performance as the amount of in-domain seed data is reduced, is a key strength. It clearly illustrates that while all methods degrade with less data, the proposed semi-supervised CNN method is significantly more resilient, maintaining a substantial BLEU gain over the baseline even with only 100 in-domain sentences. This highlights the primary advantage of leveraging pre-trained embeddings in this setting.
4.  **The paper is well-written and easy to follow.** The motivation is clearly articulated, the methodology is presented in a logical progression from a simpler model (one-hot CNN) to the full proposed model (semi-supervised CNN), and the experimental results are clearly presented and analyzed. The discussion section provides an intuitive explanation for the method's success.

### Weaknesses

1.  **The work is exclusively focused on Statistical Machine Translation (SMT).** While the experiments are sound within the SMT paradigm, the field of machine translation has largely transitioned to Neural Machine Translation (NMT). The paper does not discuss or evaluate how this data selection method would perform with NMT systems, which are now the state-of-the-art. This omission limits the paper's immediate impact and relevance to the current landscape of MT research and practice.
2.  **The technical description of the CNN model lacks some detail.** The paper describes the model at a high level and refers to an external toolkit. However, the exact formulation of the input representation in Equation 2 is ambiguous. It is not specified how the one-hot vector `wl(x)` and the segment embedding `ul(x)` are constructed from the words in a given segment (e.g., via concatenation, averaging, or bag-of-words). This lack of detail hinders the full reproducibility of the core classification model.
3.  **Potential sensitivity to hyperparameters that require a validation set.** The paper states that the number of sentences selected for the final MT system is empirically determined on a separate validation set for each task. In a true low-resource scenario, which is the core motivation of the paper, a sufficiently large validation set for tuning such a critical parameter may not be available. A discussion on the sensitivity of the results to this parameter, or an exploration of methods to set it without a large validation set, would strengthen the practical claims of the paper.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the precise input representation used in the semi-supervised CNN (Equation 2)? Specifically, for a given text segment, how are the one-hot component `wl(x)` and the pre-trained embedding component `ul(x)` constructed? For instance, is `wl(x)` a bag-of-words vector over the segment, or a concatenation of the one-hot vectors of the words within it?
2.  The number of sentences to select for the final MT system (e.g., 300K for zh2en, 1.3M for en2es) appears to be a critical hyperparameter tuned on a validation set. How sensitive is the final MT performance to this value? A performance curve showing BLEU score versus the number of selected sentences would be very informative for understanding the robustness of this method.
3.  Given that NMT is now the dominant paradigm, have you considered applying your data selection method to an NMT system? Do you foresee any specific challenges or necessary modifications for integrating this approach into an NMT training pipeline (e.g., for fine-tuning a pre-trained NMT model)?
