### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) in a low-resource setting, where only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available. The authors propose framing the data selection task as a binary classification problem: identifying in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus. They employ a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for this classification task. The CNN is trained on the small set of in-domain sentences (as positive examples) and randomly sampled general-domain sentences (as negative examples). The "semi-supervised" aspect comes from initializing the CNN with pre-trained word embeddings learned on a large unlabeled corpus. The trained classifier is then used to score all sentences in the general-domain pool, and the top-scoring sentences are selected to train an adapted SMT system.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  An empirical demonstration that a CNN-based classifier can be effectively used for data selection for SMT domain adaptation, outperforming strong, established language model perplexity-based baselines.
2.  A compelling showcase of this method's robustness in extremely low-resource scenarios, showing significant BLEU gains even when the in-domain seed data is as small as 100 sentence pairs.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong and Consistent Empirical Results:** The proposed method (sscnn) achieves statistically significant improvements over all baselines across four different language pairs and domains. The gains are substantial, particularly the +3.1 BLEU improvement on the zh-en SMS task. This consistent outperformance against strong baselines like RNNLM-based selection (Duh et al., 2013) is a significant strength.

2.  **Addresses a Practical and Challenging Problem:** The paper tackles the highly relevant problem of adaptation with a "tiny" amount of in-domain data. This is a common and difficult scenario in practice, especially for new or niche domains like social media, where collecting large in-domain corpora is often infeasible. The experiments directly validate the method's utility in this setting.

3.  **Thorough Comparison to State-of-the-Art Baselines:** The authors compare their approach against well-established and competitive methods for data selection, including both n-gram and RNN language model-based techniques. This provides a solid and convincing context for evaluating the performance of the proposed CNN-based method.

4.  **Effective Analysis of Data Sparsity:** Figure 1 provides a clear and persuasive analysis of the method's performance as the quantity of in-domain seed data is reduced. This experiment effectively highlights the primary advantage of the proposed approach: its stability and effectiveness when traditional LM-based methods begin to falter due to data scarcity.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The core technical contribution is the application of a standard semi-supervised CNN architecture (Kim, 2014; Johnson and Zhang, 2015b) to the problem of data selection. Framing data selection as a classification task is not new, and using pre-trained word embeddings to bolster performance on small labeled datasets is a standard technique in semi-supervised learning. The work is therefore more of a successful application of an existing method to a new task rather than a fundamental advance in methodology.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Explanation:** The paper demonstrates *that* the method works, but provides very little insight into *why* it works better than the LM-based baselines. The discussion in Section 5.4 is speculative and lacks empirical support. A proper analysis would involve examining the sentences selected by the CNN versus the baselines. Are they lexically richer? Do they contain more domain-specific n-grams that perplexity-based methods miss? Without such analysis, the CNN remains a "black box," and the paper misses an opportunity to provide deeper insights into the nature of domain similarity.

3.  **Lack of Comparison to Simpler Classification Models:** The paper makes a direct leap from LM perplexity methods to a relatively complex CNN model. It is plausible that a much simpler, well-understood classifier, such as a linear SVM or logistic regression with bag-of-n-gram features, could perform competitively. Without this baseline, it is difficult to ascertain whether the performance gains stem from the sophisticated feature learning of the convolutional architecture or simply from framing the problem as a discriminative classification task. This represents a significant gap in the experimental evaluation.

4.  **Ambiguity in Experimental Protocol:** Details regarding hyperparameter tuning are sparse. The paper states that meta-parameters were "tuned on heldout data," but the dev set is also used as the positive training data for the classifier. This raises concerns about potential overfitting. It is unclear how the optimal number of selected sentences for each task was determined. Was this tuned on the test set, or a separate validation set carved out from the dev set? This lack of clarity undermines the reproducibility and soundness of the experimental setup.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Can you provide a more concrete, qualitative analysis of the data selected by your `sscnn` method compared to the `rnnlm` baseline? For example, could you present examples of sentences that are highly ranked by the CNN but lowly ranked by the RNNLM (and vice versa) and explain why the CNN's choice is superior for adapting the MT system?

2.  The `ohcnn` baseline performs quite well, often matching the LM-based methods. This suggests the convolutional architecture itself provides a strong advantage. To disentangle the effects of the architecture from the pre-trained embeddings, could you provide results for a simple classifier (e.g., logistic regression) that uses averaged pre-trained word embeddings as input features? This would help clarify the relative contributions of the classification model versus the semi-supervised word representations.

3.  Could you please clarify the procedure for hyperparameter tuning and model selection? Specifically, how was the number of sentences to select for the final MT system (e.g., 300K for zh2en) determined without potentially overfitting to the test set? Was a portion of the dev set held out for this validation?
