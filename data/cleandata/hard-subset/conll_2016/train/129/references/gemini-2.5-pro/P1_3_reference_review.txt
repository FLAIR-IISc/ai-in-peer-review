### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available (as few as 100 sentence pairs). The authors propose using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) as a classifier to score sentences from a large general-domain corpus for their relevance to the target domain. The top-scoring sentences are then selected to train an adapted SMT system. The key idea is to frame data selection as a binary classification problem and leverage a semi-supervised CNN, which incorporates pre-trained word embeddings from large unlabeled corpora, to overcome the data scarcity of the in-domain examples.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **Strong Empirical Demonstration:** The paper provides compelling evidence that a semi-supervised CNN is a highly effective tool for MT data selection, especially in the "tiny data" setting. The experiments across four language pairs and challenging social media domains show consistent and significant BLEU score improvements over strong, established language model-based baselines.
2.  **Valuable Low-Resource Adaptation Method:** The work successfully demonstrates a practical and effective methodology for a realistic and difficult scenario. The analysis showing that the proposed method's advantage *increases* as the amount of in-domain seed data decreases (Figure 1) is a particularly strong result and the core finding of the paper.

### Strengths

1.  **Addresses a Well-Motivated and Practical Problem:** The scenario of needing to adapt an MT system to a new domain with only a handful of examples (e.g., a small dev set) is extremely common and practical. Providing a robust solution for this "tiny data" problem is a significant contribution. The choice of social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook) further highlights the relevance, as these are domains where large parallel corpora are often unavailable.

2.  **Strong and Rigorous Experimental Setup:** The authors compare their method not just to a weak "train on all data" baseline, but also to state-of-the-art data selection methods based on n-gram and RNN language models (Duh et al., 2013). This makes the reported gains (e.g., +0.3 to +1.0 BLEU over the best LM baseline) much more convincing. The evaluation on four different language directions also demonstrates the generalizability of the approach.

3.  **Excellent Analysis of the Low-Data Regime:** Figure 1 is the most compelling part of the paper. It directly investigates the central claim by varying the amount of in-domain seed data from 6,000 down to 100 sentences. The results clearly show that while all methods suffer with less data, the CNN-based methods, particularly the semi-supervised one, are far more robust and degrade much more gracefully. The widening performance gap between `sscnn` and the LM-based methods as data becomes scarcer is powerful evidence.

4.  **Clarity and Potential for Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and the proposed method is described clearly. The authors point to the publicly available toolkits used for both their CNN models and the baseline methods, which greatly aids in the reproducibility of their work.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The primary weakness is that the proposed technique is a direct application of existing methods. The semi-supervised CNN architecture for text classification, including the use of pre-trained word embeddings combined with one-hot representations, follows directly from prior work (e.g., Kim, 2014; Johnson and Zhang, 2015b). The contribution is therefore not in developing a new neural architecture, but in successfully applying an existing one to the task of data selection for MT adaptation. The paper would be strengthened by more explicitly positioning itself as an application paper that demonstrates the effectiveness of this technique in a new context.

2.  **Lack of Deeper Analysis/Interpretation:** While the results are strong, the discussion (Section 5.4) offers only a high-level, intuitive explanation for why the CNN works well (i.e., it captures local features and word embeddings handle synonyms). The paper would be more insightful if it included some qualitative analysis. For example, what kinds of sentences are selected by the `sscnn` but missed by the `rnnlm` baseline? Can the authors visualize or extract the n-grams that the CNN learns are most indicative of a particular domain? This would move the explanation from speculation to evidence-based analysis.

3.  **Insufficient Detail on the Classification Task Setup:** A few key details are missing. The authors state they use the dev set as positive examples and an "equal number of randomly selected general-domain sentences as negative examples." Given the massive and diverse general-domain corpus, randomly sampling negative examples could create a trivially easy classification task (e.g., distinguishing short SMS messages from long UN proceedings). The robustness of the classifier against harder, near-domain negative examples is not explored. Furthermore, the paper mentions combining source and target scores by summing them, but doesn't justify this choice over other operators (e.g., min, product) or discuss whether the scores (e.g., logits, probabilities) are properly calibrated for this.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the combination of source and target scores? Are the scores raw logits from the network or probabilities from a softmax layer? Have you experimented with other combination functions besides summation (e.g., taking the minimum, maximum, or product of the scores), and if so, how did they perform?

2.  The discussion provides a good high-level intuition for the model's success. To make this more concrete, could you provide a small qualitative analysis? For example, showing one or two sentences that were correctly identified as in-domain by `sscnn` but missed by the `rnnlm` baseline, and speculating on which features (e.g., specific n-grams, or semantic similarity via embeddings) allowed the CNN to succeed?

3.  Regarding the training of the classifier, your negative examples are sampled randomly from the entire general-domain corpus. Have you considered the effect of a "hard negative mining" strategy, where negative samples are deliberately chosen to be more similar to the positive (in-domain) samples? Do you believe the classifier's strong performance is partly due to the relative ease of distinguishing very different text styles?
