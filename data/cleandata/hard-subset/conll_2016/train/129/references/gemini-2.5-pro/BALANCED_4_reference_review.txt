### **Review**

**Summary and Main Contributions**

This paper addresses the challenge of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a very small amount of in-domain data is available (a few hundred to a few thousand sentences). The authors propose to reframe the standard data selection problem as a text classification task. They use a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to classify sentences from a large general-domain corpus as either in-domain or out-of-domain. To overcome the data scarcity of the in-domain seed set, the core of their proposal is a semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) which leverages pre-trained word embeddings learned from the large general-domain corpus. The trained classifier is then used to score and select the most relevant sentence pairs from the general-domain data to train a domain-adapted SMT system.

As I see them, the main contributions of this work are:

1.  **A novel and highly effective data selection method for SMT adaptation in a critically low-resource setting.** The application of a semi-supervised CNN to classify and select training data is a creative departure from conventional perplexity-based methods. It is particularly well-suited for the "tiny data" regime, which is a practical and important problem.
2.  **Strong and comprehensive empirical evidence supporting the proposed method's superiority.** The paper presents a thorough evaluation across four language pairs and diverse social media domains, showing significant BLEU score improvements over strong baselines, including RNNLM-based selection. The experiments demonstrating the method's robustness with as few as 100 in-domain sentences are particularly compelling and directly validate the paper's central claim.

**Strengths**

1.  **Problem Motivation and Significance:** The paper tackles a well-defined, practical, and challenging problem. The need for adaptation methods that work with minimal in-domain data is highly relevant, especially for new or niche domains like social media, or for building systems that can adapt to hundreds of fine-grained topics. The proposed solution directly addresses this need.
2.  **Excellent Experimental Results and Rigorous Evaluation:** The empirical results are the standout strength of this paper. The proposed `sscnn` method consistently and significantly outperforms all baselines, including the SMT system trained on all data and three competitive LM-based data selection methods. The BLEU gains are substantial (e.g., +3.1 on zh-en, +1.4 on ar-en). The analysis in Figure 1, which tracks performance as the in-domain seed data size is reduced, is crucial. It clearly shows that while all methods degrade with less data, the CNN-based methods, and `sscnn` in particular, are far more robust and maintain a significant advantage in the targeted low-data regime. The stability check (repeating the 100-sentence experiment) is a welcome addition that strengthens the reliability of the claims.
3.  **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. The description of the method and the experimental setup is detailed enough for replication. The authors' use of and reference to publicly available toolkits for their method and the baselines is commendable and greatly aids reproducibility.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis of Selected Data:** The paper convincingly shows that the selected data leads to better BLEU scores, but it offers little insight into *why* the `sscnn` selection is better. A qualitative or quantitative analysis of the selected data could significantly strengthen the paper. For example, how does the selected data differ from that chosen by the LM-based methods in terms of vocabulary overlap with the in-domain set, sentence length, or syntactic structure? Such analysis would help elucidate the mechanisms behind the CNN's success and move beyond a purely results-driven conclusion.
2.  **Sensitivity to the Number of Selected Sentences:** The number of sentence pairs selected for SMT training is a critical hyperparameter that is "empirically determine[d]... on a separate validation set." The paper does not provide details on this tuning process or the sensitivity of the results to this parameter. For instance, in Table 2, the number of selected sentences for `sscnn` ranges from 300K to 1300K. Does this hyperparameter need to be carefully tuned for each new task? Is the performance curve relatively flat around the optimal point? A more detailed discussion of this aspect would improve the practical applicability of the method.
3.  **Limited Discussion on the SMT Context:** All experiments are performed on a phrase-based SMT system. While appropriate for the time, the field has largely shifted to Neural Machine Translation (NMT). NMT models have different properties regarding domain sensitivity and generalization. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion on how these findings might translate to NMT. Would this selection method be equally effective, more effective, or less effective for fine-tuning a pre-trained NMT model? While experiments are not required, acknowledging this context would broaden the paper's impact.
4.  **Details on Classifier Training:** The setup for training the CNN classifier could be slightly more detailed. The negative examples are simply "randomly select[ed]" sentences. Given the vastness of the general-domain data, this random sample might not be representative or provide challenging negative examples. It would be interesting to know if the authors explored any form of hard negative mining or other sampling strategies and what their effect was.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the process used to determine the optimal number of sentences to select for the final MT system? How sensitive are the final BLEU scores to this number? For example, for the zh-en task, what would the performance be if 400K or 200K sentences were selected instead of 300K?
2.  In Section 4, you note that combining source and target classification scores worked best. Could you provide the results for using only the source-side scores and only the target-side scores for selection? This would help clarify the relative importance of adapting to the source vs. target domain characteristics.
3.  Could you provide any simple comparative statistics on the data selected by `sscnn` versus `rnnlm` for one of the tasks? For example, what is the vocabulary overlap with the in-domain dev set, or the out-of-vocabulary (OOV) rate on the test set for the two selected subsets? This could provide concrete evidence for the qualitative claims made in the discussion section.
