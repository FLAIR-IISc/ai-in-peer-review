### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a very small amount of in-domain data is available (a few hundred sentences). The authors propose a novel data selection method that frames the problem as a binary classification task (in-domain vs. out-of-domain) and leverages a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to perform this classification. Pre-trained word embeddings from large unlabeled corpora are used to augment the supervised signal from the tiny labeled in-domain set, allowing the model to generalize effectively. The selected data is then used to train a domain-adapted SMT system.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A New Paradigm for Data Selection:** The paper's primary contribution is conceptual: it shifts the data selection paradigm from perplexity-based scoring (using n-gram or neural language models) to discriminative classification with a CNN. This is a bold and creative approach that uses a model designed for content classification to directly learn features indicative of a specific domain, representing a significant departure from prior art.
2.  **An Effective Solution for Extreme Low-Resource Adaptation:** The work provides a compelling solution to the challenging and practical problem of adaptation with truly tiny datasets. By demonstrating that their semi-supervised method remains robust and effective with as few as 100 in-domain sentences, they address a critical limitation of previous methods that required thousands of sentences to be effective.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and a Bold New Direction:** The core idea of using a CNN for data selection is highly novel and refreshing. While previous methods relied on generative language models to score sentences based on fluency and fit, this paper proposes to directly *learn* what makes a sentence "in-domain". This discriminative approach, which learns to focus on key domain-specific phrases via convolutions and pooling, is a fundamentally different and powerful way to tackle the problem. It opens up an entirely new research avenue for domain adaptation that is not tied to the limitations of perplexity-based metrics.
2.  **Addresses a Critical and Underexplored Problem:** The paper tackles the "tiny data" adaptation scenario, a problem of immense practical importance, especially for emerging domains like social media where collecting large in-domain corpora is often infeasible. The experiments convincingly show that while state-of-the-art LM-based methods falter when the in-domain seed set is reduced, the proposed `sscnn` method maintains a strong advantage (Figure 1). This is a significant result that pushes the boundary of what is possible in low-resource adaptation.
3.  **Strong and Compelling Empirical Evidence:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation across four language pairs and several challenging social media domains. The consistent and significant BLEU improvements (up to +3.1 over a strong baseline) are impressive. The fact that the method outperforms not only a system trained on all data but also state-of-the-art selection methods (`rnnlm`) demonstrates its effectiveness. The stability of the results, even when sampling only 100 sentences, further solidifies the robustness of the approach.
4.  **Intelligent Use of Semi-Supervised Learning:** The integration of pre-trained word embeddings is not just an add-on; it is central to solving the data scarcity problem. This allows the model to leverage semantic knowledge gleaned from massive unlabeled corpora, enabling it to recognize domain-relevant words and concepts that are absent from the tiny labeled training set. This clever use of semi-supervision is what makes the model so effective in the target low-resource setting.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this work, there are a few areas that could be strengthened. I see these as opportunities for future work rather than fatal flaws.

1.  **Lack of Qualitative Analysis:** The paper provides strong quantitative results but lacks a qualitative analysis of *what* the CNN is learning. What kinds of features (n-grams) do the filters capture? What are some examples of sentences that are correctly selected by the `sscnn` but missed by the LM-based baselines? Such an analysis would provide valuable insight into *why* the method works so well and build stronger intuition about the differences between the discriminative and perplexity-based approaches. The discussion in Section 5.4 is plausible but remains speculative without this evidence.
2.  **Limited Architectural Exploration:** The paper employs a fairly standard CNN architecture and uses an existing toolkit. While this is perfectly acceptable, the work would have been stronger with some exploration of the model design space. For instance, the method for combining the one-hot and embedding features (simple addition) is not justified against other choices like concatenation. A brief ablation study or discussion of architectural choices would have enhanced the contribution.
3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity is Underexplored:** A key hyperparameter is the number of sentences selected to train the final MT system. This is determined on a held-out set, but the paper does not discuss how performance varies with this number. Is there a sharp peak, or is performance relatively stable across a wide range of selected data sizes? Providing this information would be very useful for practitioners looking to apply this method.

### Questions to Authors

1.  The classifier scores for source and target sentences were combined via summation. Could you elaborate on the results for using only the source-side classifier or only the target-side classifier? This could shed light on which side benefits more from this type of adaptation.
2.  Your hypothesis that word embeddings help the model generalize to unseen but semantically similar words (e.g., "Lenovo" vs. "Dell") is very compelling. Have you performed any analysis to verify this? For example, by analyzing the classification of sentences containing in-domain words that were OOV with respect to the small labeled training set?
3.  You use a dev set as the source of in-domain data for training the classifier. Have you considered a scenario where the in-domain data might be monolingual (e.g., only target-side)? How would your method adapt to such a setting?
