### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available. The proposed method frames data selection as a binary classification task: identifying in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus. The authors employ a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for this classification. The model is trained using the tiny in-domain dataset as positive examples and an equal number of randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negative examples. The "semi-supervised" aspect comes from augmenting the one-hot word representations with pre-trained word embeddings learned from the large general-domain corpus. The trained classifier then scores all sentences in the general-domain corpus, and the top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train a domain-adapted SMT system. Experiments on four language pairs with social media domains show that this method significantly outperforms strong baselines, particularly when the amount of in-domain data is extremely limited (e.g., 100 sentences).

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A novel and effective application of semi-supervised CNNs for SMT data selection in a low-resource adaptation setting.** While the CNN architecture itself is not new, its application to frame data selection as a classification task for SMT adaptation is novel and shown to be highly effective, especially compared to established perplexity-based methods.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical demonstration of the method's robustness with extremely small amounts of in-domain data.** The paper provides strong evidence across four language pairs that the proposed method yields significant improvements over baselines even with as few as 100 labeled in-domain sentences, a challenging and practical scenario where prior methods are less effective.

### Strengths

1.  **Strong and Consistent Empirical Results:** The primary strength of the paper lies in its experimental results. The proposed semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) method consistently and significantly outperforms all baselines across four different language pairs and three distinct social media domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts). The reported BLEU score improvements over the system trained on all data are substantial (e.g., +3.1 for zh-en, +1.4 for ar-en and en-zh). More importantly, the method also shows statistically significant improvements over strong, state-of-the-art data selection baselines based on n-gram and recurrent neural network language models.
2.  **Addresses a Practical and Challenging Problem:** The paper tackles the highly relevant problem of domain adaptation when in-domain data is extremely scarce. This scenario is common for new or niche domains (e.g., social media, specific technical fields) where collecting large parallel corpora is prohibitively expensive or time-consuming. The experiment detailed in Figure 1, which shows the performance of different methods as the size of the in-domain seed data is reduced from 6,000 to 100 sentences, is particularly compelling. It clearly demonstrates that the proposed CNN-based methods, especially sscnn, are more robust to data scarcity and degrade more gracefully than the LM-based baselines.
3.  **Clear Methodology and Sound Experimental Design:** The paper presents its methodology clearly, building upon established work in text classification with CNNs. The experimental setup is sound, employing a standard phrase-based SMT pipeline and comparing against appropriate and widely-used data selection methods (Moore and Lewis, 2010; Duh et al., 2013). The evaluation is conducted on large-scale training corpora (9-15M sentence pairs), demonstrating the method's applicability in realistic settings. The use of bootstrap resampling for significance testing further strengthens the validity of the reported results.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty of the Core Model:** The semi-supervised CNN architecture used for classification is directly adopted from prior work in text classification (Johnson and Zhang, 2015b), which the authors acknowledge. The contribution of the paper is therefore not in the development of a new neural architecture but in its application to a new task. The method essentially reframes data selection as a standard classification problem and applies a suitable existing classifier. While the application is novel and the results are strong, the work's contribution to core methodology is limited.
2.  **Lack of Intrinsic Evaluation of the Classifier:** The paper evaluates the proposed data selection method entirely extrinsically, through its impact on the final SMT system's BLEU score. There is no intrinsic evaluation of the domain classifier itself. Providing metrics such as classification accuracy, precision, and recall on a held-out set would help to directly assess how well the CNN performs the task of identifying in-domain sentences compared to the LM-based approaches (by thresholding their scores). Without this analysis, it is difficult to determine whether the BLEU improvements are due to superior classification or other confounding factors, and it provides less insight into *why* the method works well.
3.  **Analysis of Model Behavior is Superficial:** The discussion in Section 5.4 posits two reasons for the CNN's success: its ability to capture local domain-specific n-grams and its generalization power via word embeddings. These claims are plausible but are not substantiated with any analysis. The paper could be strengthened by including qualitative examples of sentences that were correctly selected by the CNN but missed by the LM-based methods, or by analyzing the learned filters to see if they correspond to intuitive domain indicators. Similarly, the claim about word embeddings helping with unseen words (e.g., "Lenovo" vs. "Dell") could be supported by an analysis of the vocabulary overlap between the selected data and the seed in-domain data.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you provide an intrinsic evaluation of the classifiers? Specifically, what are the classification accuracy, precision, recall, and F1-scores of the `sscnn` and `ohcnn` models on a held-out portion of the labeled data? How do these compare to the classification performance of the LM-based methods if their scores are used to classify sentences?
2.  The number of sentences selected to train the final SMT system (e.g., 300K for zh-en, 1300K for en-es) is a key hyperparameter tuned on a validation set. How sensitive is the final BLEU score to this parameter? A sensitivity analysis would help to understand the robustness of the method and the effort required to tune it for a new task.
3.  In the experiments shown in Figure 1, the number of selected MT training sentences is fixed at 300K while the CNN training data size is varied. For the LM-based methods, is the in-domain LM also trained on this diminishing set of sentences? If so, the degradation in performance could be due to poor LM estimation on tiny data, rather than the selection criterion itself. Could you clarify the experimental setup for the baselines in this particular experiment?
