### **Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a challenging but practical scenario: when only a very small amount of in-domain data (as little as 100 sentences) is available. The authors propose using a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to perform data selection. The method involves training a binary classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences. The semi-supervised aspect comes from leveraging pre-trained word embeddings (from word2vec) on a large general-domain corpus, which helps the classifier generalize even with tiny amounts of labeled (in-domain) data. This trained classifier is then used to score and select the most relevant sentence pairs from a large general-domain pool to train a specialized SMT system.

In my view, the main contributions of this work are:
1.  **A highly effective method for data selection in a low-resource adaptation setting.** The key contribution is not just the application of a CNN to data selection, but the demonstration that a semi-supervised approach using pre-trained embeddings is particularly robust and effective when in-domain labeled data is extremely scarce. This successfully tackles a well-defined and important gap in existing adaptation literature.
2.  **A strong and convincing empirical evaluation.** The authors validate their method on four different language pairs and diverse social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). They compare against strong and relevant baselines, including n-gram and RNN-LM based data selection, and show statistically significant improvements. The ablation study on the amount of in-domain data (Figure 1) is particularly compelling evidence for the paper's central claim.

### **Strengths**

1.  **Well-motivated and impactful problem.** The paper tackles a realistic and difficult problem. Standard data selection methods for MT adaptation often assume the availability of thousands of in-domain sentences. The scenario addressed here—adapting with only a few hundred sentences—is highly relevant for new or niche domains, such as social media topics, where collecting large in-domain corpora is prohibitively expensive or time-consuming. A successful solution has significant practical value.
2.  **Excellent experimental results and rigorous evaluation.** The proposed `sscnn` method consistently outperforms all baselines across four different language pairs and domains. The reported BLEU score improvements are not just statistically significant but also practically meaningful (e.g., +3.1 BLEU on zh->en, +1.4 on ar->en and en->zh). The comparison against strong LM-based methods (Axelrod et al., 2011; Duh et al., 2013) demonstrates the superiority of the proposed approach.
3.  **Compelling analysis of the low-data regime.** Figure 1 is the strongest piece of evidence in the paper. It directly tests the central hypothesis by varying the amount of in-domain data used to train the selection model. The results clearly show that while all methods suffer as data decreases, the proposed `sscnn` method degrades far more gracefully. Its ability to achieve a +2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 in-domain sentences is a powerful result that highlights the benefit of leveraging unsupervised word embeddings to compensate for the lack of labeled data. The check for stability by re-sampling the 100 sentences is also a good piece of experimental diligence.
4.  **Clarity and Reproducibility.** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly, and the authors specify the public toolkits used for both their method and the baselines (conText, word2vec, RNNLM Toolkit). This level of detail greatly aids in the potential for reproducibility.

### **Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited novelty in the CNN architecture.** The CNN model itself is a standard architecture for text classification, explicitly building on prior work by Johnson and Zhang (2015a, 2015b). The novelty lies entirely in its application to the specific task of MT data selection in a low-resource context. While this application is highly effective and constitutes a valid contribution, the paper could be strengthened by acknowledging this more directly and perhaps comparing against other strong classification models (e.g., logistic regression with n-gram features) to better isolate the benefits of the CNN architecture over simpler alternatives.
2.  **Lack of detail on a critical hyperparameter.** The number of sentences selected from the general-domain corpus is a crucial hyperparameter that directly impacts the final MT system's performance and size. The authors state that this number was "empirically determine[d]... on a separate validation set," but provide no further details. The optimal numbers vary widely across tasks (from 300K to 1600K). An analysis of the sensitivity of the final BLEU score to this parameter would be very valuable. For instance, is there a sharp performance peak at 300K for the zh->en task, or is performance relatively stable over a wide range? This information is important for practitioners seeking to apply this method.
3.  **Absence of qualitative analysis.** The discussion section provides plausible hypotheses for why the `sscnn` method works well (capturing local domain indicators, generalizing via embeddings). However, the paper lacks any qualitative analysis to support these claims. It would have been insightful to see examples of sentences that were correctly selected by the `sscnn` model but missed by the `rnnlm` baseline. Such an analysis would provide a more intuitive understanding of what the model is learning and why it is more effective than LM-based perplexity measures.

### **Questions to Authors**

1.  Could you please elaborate on the process for determining the optimal number of sentences to select for training the final MT system? How sensitive is the final BLEU score to this number? A plot showing BLEU score vs. number of selected sentences for one of the tasks would be very helpful.
2.  The core of your method is a binary classifier for sentence relevance. Did you experiment with any other classification models besides the CNN (e.g., a simple logistic regression or SVM with bag-of-words/n-gram features)? Such a comparison would help clarify whether the performance gains stem from the general classification-based approach or specifically from the convolutional architecture's ability to learn feature representations.
