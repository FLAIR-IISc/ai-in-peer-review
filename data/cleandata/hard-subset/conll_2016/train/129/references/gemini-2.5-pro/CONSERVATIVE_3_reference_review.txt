**Summary and Contributions**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) in a setting where only a very small amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose to reframe the standard data selection task as a binary classification problem: identifying in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus. To perform this classification, they employ a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) trained on the small set of in-domain sentences as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negative examples. The key aspect of their method is its semi-supervised nature, where pre-trained word embeddings (from word2vec) are used to initialize the network, allowing it to generalize effectively from a tiny labeled set.

The primary contributions of this work are:
1.  The application of a standard text classification CNN architecture to the task of data selection for SMT domain adaptation.
2.  An empirical demonstration that this classification-based approach outperforms strong, established language model (LM) perplexity-based methods (n-gram and RNNLM) across four different language pairs.
3.  Strong evidence that the proposed method is particularly robust and effective in the very low-resource regime, achieving significant BLEU gains even when the classifier is trained on as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

**Strengths**

1.  **Strong Baselines and Rigorous Comparison:** The authors compare their method against the established state-of-the-art in data selection for SMT adaptation, namely the perplexity-based methods of Axelrod et al. (2011) and Duh et al. (2013). The inclusion of an RNNLM baseline is particularly important, as it represents a strong neural-based competitor. The consistent outperformance of the proposed `sscnn` method across all four tasks lends significant credibility to the claims.

2.  **Well-Motivated Problem Setting:** The paper tackles a practical and challenging scenario: adaptation with a "tiny amount" of in-domain data. Many prior works assume the availability of thousands of in-domain sentences, which is often not feasible. The experiments specifically investigating the impact of reducing the labeled set size (Figure 1) are a major strength, providing compelling evidence for the method's utility in its target scenario.

3.  **Solid Empirical Results:** The evaluation is conducted on large-scale SMT systems for four diverse tasks, including challenging social media domains. The reported BLEU improvements are consistent and, in several cases, statistically significant over strong baselines. The finding that the method works well even when there is no known in-domain subset within the general training data (unlike the zh-en task) demonstrates its general applicability.

**Weaknesses**

1.  **Limited Methodological Novelty:** The proposed method is a straightforward application of existing techniques. The CNN architecture for text classification and the use of pre-trained word embeddings are standard practice, directly following prior work (e.g., Kim, 2014; Johnson & Zhang, 2015). The contribution lies entirely in applying this established classification pipeline to the data selection problem, rather than in advancing the underlying neural network methodology. While the results are good, the work feels more like an engineering contribution than a fundamental research advance.

2.  **Insufficient Analysis and Intuition:** The paper falls short in providing a deep analysis of *why* a supervised CNN classifier should be fundamentally better than a finely-tuned language model for this task. The discussion in Section 5.4 offers high-level intuition but lacks concrete evidence. For instance, RNNLMs also utilize word embeddings and model word sequences. A qualitative analysis comparing the sentences selected by the CNN versus the LM-based methods would have been highly valuable. Without this, it is difficult to understand the source of the improvement beyond simply observing the final BLEU scores.

3.  **Potential Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The success of data selection methods often hinges on the crucial hyperparameter of how much data to select. The paper states that this number was determined empirically on a held-out set but provides no details on this process. It is unclear how this validation set relates to the dev set used for training the classifier, or how sensitive the final results are to this choice. A more thorough discussion of this tuning process would strengthen the paper's claims of robustness. The stability of the method is tested with three random samples for the 100-sentence experiment, which is a good first step, but a more comprehensive analysis would be preferable.

**Questions to Authors**

1.  The amount of data selected for the final MT system (e.g., 300K for zh-en, 1300K for en-es) varies significantly across tasks. Could you elaborate on the procedure used to tune this hyperparameter? Was a separate, in-domain validation set used for this tuning? How did its size compare to the "tiny" dev set used for training the CNN, and how might this affect the practicality of the approach?

2.  The core comparison is between a supervised classifier and an unsupervised perplexity measure. Could you provide a more detailed analysis or a few examples of sentences that were correctly selected by your `sscnn` model but missed by the `rnnlm` baseline (and vice versa)? This would help to build a more concrete intuition for why the supervised approach is superior.

3.  You select data based on the sum of scores for the source and target sentences, which are classified independently. Did you experiment with any models that classify the sentence pair jointly? It seems plausible that a joint model could capture cross-lingual domain indicators that are missed by two separate monolingual classifiers.
