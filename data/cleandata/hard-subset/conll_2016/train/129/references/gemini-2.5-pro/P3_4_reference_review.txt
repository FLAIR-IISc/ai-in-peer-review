### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a scenario where only a very small amount of in-domain parallel data is available (as little as a few hundred sentences). The authors propose framing data selection as a classification task and employ a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to identify in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus. The small in-domain set serves as positive examples for training the classifier, while the semi-supervised aspect comes from using pre-trained word embeddings learned on large-scale unlabeled data. The selected data is then used to train an adapted SMT system.

In my view, the main contributions are:
1.  **A highly effective method for MT domain adaptation in a very low-resource setting.** The paper compellingly demonstrates that a semi-supervised CNN-based classifier can significantly outperform strong language model-based baselines when the in-domain seed data is extremely limited (e.g., 100-800 sentences). This addresses a practical and challenging real-world scenario.
2.  **Strong empirical validation across multiple language pairs and domains.** The authors conduct experiments on four different language directions, including challenging social media domains like tweets and Facebook posts. The consistent and significant BLEU score improvements over competitive baselines strongly support the paper's claims. The ablation study on the size of the seed data (Figure 1) is particularly convincing and highlights the robustness of the proposed approach.

### Strengths

1.  **Tackles a relevant and difficult problem.** Traditional data selection methods for MT adaptation often assume the availability of several thousand in-domain sentences to train a reliable in-domain language model. This paper focuses on the more challenging and practical "tiny data" scenario, which is common when adapting to new, emerging, or niche domains. The proposed solution is well-motivated for this setting.
2.  **The methodology is sound and the results are very strong.** The application of semi-supervised CNNs for this task is well-justified. The convolution/pooling layers are suited to identifying key domain-specific phrases, while the pre-trained embeddings provide the necessary generalization to overcome the sparsity of the small labeled seed set. The experimental results are impressive, showing consistent gains of up to 3.1 BLEU points and outperforming strong baselines (ngram, RNNLM) by a significant margin, especially in the low-data regime.
3.  **The experimental setup is thorough and convincing.** The authors compare their method against multiple well-established baselines from the literature (Axelrod et al., 2011; Duh et al., 2013). The use of four diverse language pairs and realistic social media test domains enhances the generality of the findings. The analysis in Figure 1, showing the performance curve as the amount of in-domain data shrinks, is a key strength, as it directly proves the method's superiority in the exact scenario it claims to address.
4.  **The paper is well-written and easy to follow.** The motivation is clearly laid out, the proposed method is described logically, and the experimental results are presented clearly. The connection to prior work in both SMT adaptation and text classification with CNNs is well-established.

### Weaknesses

1.  **Lack of comparison with simpler classification models.** The core idea is to replace an LM-based scoring function with a discriminative classifier. While the CNN is a reasonable choice, the paper does not compare it against simpler but powerful classification baselines, such as Logistic Regression or a linear SVM using bag-of-n-gram features. For the supervised `ohcnn` variant, it is not clear that the complexity of a CNN is necessary. A simple linear classifier could potentially achieve similar performance. Even for the semi-supervised case, a linear model could be augmented with features derived from averaging the pre-trained word embeddings. Without these baselines, it is difficult to isolate the specific contribution of the convolutional architecture versus simply using a discriminative classifier.
2.  **Limited novelty in the CNN architecture itself.** The paper applies an existing semi-supervised CNN architecture (drawing from Kim, 2014 and Johnson & Zhang, 2015b) to a new problem. While the application is novel and impactful, the methodological contribution in terms of neural network architecture is limited. This is more of an application paper, albeit a very successful one.
3.  **The analysis of why the model works could be deeper.** The discussion in Section 5.4 provides plausible hypotheses for the model's success (capturing local features, generalization via embeddings). However, this could be substantiated with more concrete analysis. For instance, the authors could have provided qualitative examples of sentences that `sscnn` correctly selects while LM-based methods fail, or vice-versa. Visualizing what the learned filters are responsive to could also offer more insight into whether the model is truly capturing domain-specific lexical items as hypothesized.
4.  **Potential sensitivity to hyperparameters.** The number of sentences to select from the general-domain corpus is a critical hyperparameter that appears to be tuned on a held-out set. The paper reports the final optimal number for each task but does not discuss how sensitive the final BLEU score is to this choice. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the paper by showing the robustness of the method with respect to this hyperparameter.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you comment on why you chose a CNN architecture over simpler discriminative classifiers like logistic regression with n-gram features? Given the success of such models in many text classification tasks, it would be valuable to understand if you performed any preliminary experiments and how they compared, especially against your `ohcnn` baseline.
2.  Your hypothesis that word embeddings help generalize (e.g., identifying "Lenovo" as in-domain based on its proximity to "Dell") is very compelling. Can you provide any qualitative examples from your experiments that support this? For instance, are there sentences selected by `sscnn` containing in-domain words that were absent from the tiny seed set, which the LM-based methods failed to select?
3.  The amount of data selected for training the final SMT system varies significantly across tasks (from 300K to 1.6M). Could you provide more detail on how this hyperparameter was tuned? A plot showing BLEU score as a function of the number of selected sentences for one of the tasks would be very insightful.
