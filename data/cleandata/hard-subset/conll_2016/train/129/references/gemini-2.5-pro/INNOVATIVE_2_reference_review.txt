### Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in a low-resource setting, where only a tiny amount of in-domain data (as little as a few hundred sentences) is available. The authors propose a novel data selection method that reframes the task from a language model perplexity calculation to a binary classification problem: identifying in-domain sentences from a large general-domain corpus. The core of their contribution is the use of a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for this classification. This model leverages pre-trained word embeddings from the large general-domain corpus to compensate for the scarcity of labeled in-domain data. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on four large-scale SMT tasks, showing significant BLEU improvements over strong language model-based baselines, particularly when the amount of in-domain data is extremely limited.

From my perspective, the main contributions are:

1.  **A New Paradigm for Data Selection:** The primary contribution is the conceptual shift of framing data selection as a semi-supervised classification task. This is a bold and creative departure from the established paradigm of using language model cross-entropy, opening up a new direction for research in MT domain adaptation.
2.  **An Effective Solution for a Critical Low-Resource Scenario:** The paper provides a compelling solution to the practical and difficult problem of adaptation with truly tiny amounts of in-domain data. The demonstration that the method remains robust with as few as 100 labeled sentences is a significant finding and a major strength of the work.
3.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The proposed semi-supervised CNN model is shown to be highly effective, delivering substantial and consistent BLEU score improvements (up to +3.1) over competitive baselines across multiple language pairs and challenging social media domains.

### Strengths

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Innovation:** The standout strength of this paper is its novelty. Instead of making an incremental improvement to existing language model-based data selection techniques, the authors propose an entirely different approach. Applying CNNs, which excel at finding important local features, to the problem of "domainness" is a clever and non-obvious idea. This kind of innovative thinking is exactly what drives the field forward, and I find this aspect of the paper very exciting.

2.  **Addresses a Practical and Under-Explored Problem:** The focus on adaptation with *tiny* data is a significant merit. Much previous work assumes the availability of thousands of in-domain sentences, which is often not feasible for new, emerging, or highly specific domains (e.g., social media, personal communication). The experiments that explicitly vary the in-domain data size down to 100 sentences are excellent, providing convincing evidence that the proposed method is not just better, but uniquely suited to this challenging regime where other methods begin to fail.

3.  **Intelligent Use of Semi-Supervised Learning:** The integration of pre-trained word embeddings is key to the method's success and is well-motivated. It allows the classifier to generalize from the few examples it sees to a much broader vocabulary and semantic space, capturing relationships between words (e.g., 'Dell' and 'Lenovo') that would be impossible to learn from the small labeled set alone. The comparison between the one-hot CNN (`ohcnn`) and the semi-supervised CNN (`sscnn`) clearly validates the importance of leveraging the large unlabeled corpus.

4.  **Impressive and Convincing Results:** The empirical results are strong and unambiguous. The proposed `sscnn` method consistently and significantly outperforms not only the `alldata` baseline but also three strong LM-based baselines, including one using RNNLMs. The gains are substantial across all four language pairs. The result on the zh-en task, where the method selects data that leads to a better system than one trained on a known 120K-sentence in-domain corpus, is particularly striking and speaks volumes about the classifier's ability to find the most relevant data.

### Weaknesses

While I am very enthusiastic about this paper, I will note a few areas that could be strengthened. I consider these to be minor points that do not detract from the overall contribution.

1.  **Limited Architectural Details and Justification:** The paper is somewhat brief on the specifics of the CNN architecture and the rationale behind certain hyperparameter choices (e.g., region size, number of neurons). While the authors reference the toolkit used, a more self-contained description and perhaps a brief exploration of alternative choices would have improved the methodological rigor. However, given the novelty of the core idea, I view this as a forgivable omission. The strong results provide sufficient proof of concept.

2.  **Analysis Could Be Deeper:** The discussion in section 5.4 provides good intuition for why the CNN model works well, but it remains at a high level. The paper would have been even stronger with a more concrete analysis. For instance, an examination of specific sentences where the CNN and LM-based methods disagree, or a visualization of what features the network learns, could have provided more direct evidence for the authors' hypotheses.

3.  **Evaluation Limited to SMT:** The experiments are conducted exclusively within a phrase-based SMT framework. While appropriate for the time, the field has since moved towards Neural Machine Translation (NMT). The paper does not explore whether this data selection approach would be similarly effective for adapting NMT systems. While I believe the core idea is general enough to be applicable, demonstrating this would have broadened the work's impact. This is more of a limitation in scope than a fundamental flaw.

### Recommendation

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. It introduces a highly novel and creative approach to a long-standing problem in machine translation. By reframing data selection as a classification task solved with a semi-supervised CNN, the authors open up a promising new research avenue. The paper's focus on the challenging and practical scenario of adaptation with tiny amounts of in-domain data is commendable, and the empirical results are impressive and convincing. While there are minor weaknesses in methodological detail and analysis, they are far outweighed by the significance and novelty of the core contribution. This is an exciting paper that presents a bold idea that works remarkably well.
