### Summary of the Paper and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) in scenarios where the amount of in-domain data is extremely limited (as few as 100 sentences). The authors propose using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) as a classifier to select domain-relevant sentence pairs from a large general-domain corpus. The primary contribution is a semi-supervised variant of this classifier (`sscnn`) that leverages pre-trained word embeddings (from word2vec on general-domain data) in addition to one-hot input representations. This approach is shown to be highly effective for data selection.

As I see them, the main contributions are:
1.  A strong empirical demonstration that a semi-supervised CNN-based classifier significantly outperforms established n-gram and RNN language model-based data selection methods for SMT adaptation, especially in low-resource in-domain settings.
2.  A compelling analysis of the proposed method's robustness, showing that it can yield substantial BLEU improvements even when the in-domain seed data for training the classifier is reduced to just a few hundred, or even 100, sentence pairs.

### Strengths

1.  **Well-motivated and Relevant Problem:** The paper tackles a practical and challenging problem: adapting MT systems to new domains where collecting large amounts of parallel data is infeasible. The focus on "tiny" amounts of data is particularly relevant for adapting to niche topics or emerging social media domains.
2.  **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The authors conduct experiments on four different language pairs and domains, using large-scale general-domain corpora (9-15M sentence pairs). The comparison against strong and widely-used baselines, including both n-gram and RNNLM-based data selection, provides a solid foundation for evaluating the proposed method's efficacy.
3.  **Strong and Consistent Results:** The proposed `sscnn` method consistently achieves the best performance across all four tasks, with statistically significant improvements over both the `alldata` baseline and the best LM-based methods. The BLEU gains (e.g., +3.1 for zh-en, +1.4 for en-zh) are substantial.
4.  **Effective Low-Data Analysis:** Figure 1 provides a very clear and powerful illustration of the method's primary advantage. It demonstrates that while all methods suffer as the amount of in-domain seed data decreases, the CNN-based methods, and `sscnn` in particular, are far more robust and degrade much more gracefully than the LM-based baselines. This is a key finding of the paper.

### Weaknesses

Despite the strong results, the paper is marred by a significant number of minor flaws, technical ambiguities, and presentation issues that collectively undermine its quality and reproducibility.

1.  **Vague Methodological Description:** The description of the core CNN model is critically underspecified.
    *   In Section 3.2, Equation 2 presents the core of the semi-supervised model: `σ(W · wl(x) + V · ul(x) + b)`. It is unclear how `wl(x)` (the one-hot part) and `ul(x)` (the embedding part) are constructed and combined for a given text segment. Section 5.2 states, "we generate both bow-regions and seq-regions and input them to the CNN," but fails to explain *how* they are input. Are they concatenated? Processed in separate channels? The text in Section 3.1 mentions `wl(x)` can be a concatenation of one-hot vectors or a bag-of-words vector. Which was used? A concatenated one-hot representation for a 5-word region would be impractically large. This ambiguity makes the work difficult to replicate.
    *   In Section 4, the authors state sentence pairs are selected based on "the sum of source and target scores." This is a crude heuristic. Are the scores raw logits from the network, or probabilities from a softmax layer? Combining scores from two independently trained models (source and target) without any form of normalization or principled weighting is questionable and lacks justification.
2.  **Insufficient Experimental Transparency:**
    *   The number of sentences selected for the final MT system (e.g., 300K, 800K, 1600K in Table 2) is a crucial hyperparameter. The paper only states this was determined "on a separate validation set." However, the dev set was already used to provide positive examples for training the CNN classifier. Was a third, distinct validation set used for this tuning? This process is not described, making the results less transparent.
    *   The choice of SMT as the evaluation framework is somewhat dated. While valid, the field was rapidly moving to Neural Machine Translation (NMT) when this work was likely conducted. The absence of any discussion or experiment on NMT adaptation is a notable omission. It is not self-evident that these gains would transfer directly to NMT fine-tuning or data augmentation strategies.
3.  **Superficial Analysis:** The discussion in Section 5.4 is purely speculative ("We think there are two main reasons..."). The example of "Lenovo" vs. "Dell" is anecdotal. The paper would be much stronger with a more rigorous analysis, such as visualizing word embeddings, examining the features learned by the CNN filters, or providing qualitative examples of sentences that were correctly selected by `sscnn` but missed by the baselines.
4.  **Numerous Formatting and Presentation Errors:** The paper suffers from a lack of careful proofreading, which detracts from its professional quality.
    *   The reference section is riddled with severe formatting errors (e.g., "Axelrod et al\.,? \Q2011\E", "Pennington et al\.,? \Q2014\E"). Several entries are incomplete, such as Pennington et al. (2014). This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier conference.
    *   Minor typos and grammatical errors are present throughout the text (e.g., "unlabled corpus," "compensate the limit," "language model methods get").
    *   Footnote 7 makes an empirical claim ("Witten-Bell smoothing had performed better") that should have been properly situated and justified within the main experimental section.

### Questions to Authors

1.  Could you please provide a precise description of the CNN input layer? Specifically, for a given text segment, how are the one-hot representations (`wl(x)`) and the pre-trained embeddings (`ul(x)`) formulated and combined before being passed to the convolution operation in Equation (2)?
2.  Could you clarify the procedure for combining the source and target classification scores? Were the raw network outputs summed, or were they first passed through a softmax and/or normalized?
3.  Please elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process for determining the optimal number of sentences to select for MT training. Was a dedicated validation set, separate from the dev set used to train the CNN, utilized for this purpose?
4.  Could you comment on why SMT was chosen for evaluation over NMT and whether you have any insights into how this data selection method would perform in an NMT adaptation context?
