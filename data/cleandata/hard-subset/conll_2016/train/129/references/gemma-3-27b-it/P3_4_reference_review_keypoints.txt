- Summary: Semi-supervised CNNs classify sentence pairs to select general-domain data for SMT adaptation with tiny in-domain sets, outperforming LM-based selection across 4 language pairs and social-media domains.  
- Contribution: Empirically shows semi-supervised CNNs can select useful in-domain-like data even with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.  
- Contribution: Strong experimental evaluation across multiple language pairs and domains, comparing to strong baselines (including RNNLM).  
- Contribution: Practical method leveraging pre-trained word embeddings and CNNs to capture domain-specific features for data selection.  
- Strength: Addresses an important, under-addressed low-resource domain adaptation problem.  
- Strength: Consistent and statistically significant BLEU improvements, notably large gain on zh→en SMS, robust with very limited in-domain data.  
- Strength: Thorough baseline comparisons using well-established LM-based methods.  
- Strength: Sound methodology combining semi-supervision and pre-trained embeddings.  
- Strength: Clear, well-organized presentation and experimental description.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty in CNN architecture—uses standard convolutional and pooling layers without architectural innovation.  
- Weakness: Missing ablation studies (e.g., impact of pre-trained vs. trained-from-scratch embeddings, CNN size, filter/window settings).  
- Weakness: Insufficient explanation for why summing source and target scores is the best selection criterion.  
- Weakness: Over-reliance on BLEU; lacks other automatic metrics or human evaluation.  
- Weakness: Limited error analysis on which error types are corrected or remain.  
- Question: Why does summing source and target CNN scores yield best performance—any theoretical justification or deeper analysis?  
- Question: What are the computational costs (training/inference) of the CNN classifier versus LM-based selection?  
- Question: Was the 120K in-domain subset for zh→en SMS known a priori or discovered, and how does that affect interpretation?  
- Question: How were the limited in-domain sentences selected—randomly or deliberately for representativeness?  
- Additional comment: Some citations are incomplete (e.g., "Axelrod et al.,?").  
- Additional comment: Paper would benefit from a brief discussion of limitations and future research directions.  
- Overall assessment: Solid, practical contribution with strong empirical support; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
