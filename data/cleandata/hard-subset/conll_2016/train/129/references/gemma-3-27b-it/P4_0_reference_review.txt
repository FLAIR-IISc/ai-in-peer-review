This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, and then select high-scoring sentence pairs from the general-domain training data for SMT training.

The primary contribution of this work is the successful application of a semi-supervised CNN for data selection in a low-resource domain adaptation scenario. While CNNs have been used for text classification, applying them specifically to select training data for SMT, *particularly* with such limited in-domain data (down to 100 sentences), is a novel and valuable contribution. The experimental results demonstrating improvements over strong baselines, including RNNLM-based data selection, are compelling.  A secondary contribution lies in the comprehensive experimental evaluation across four language pairs and different test domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts), which increases the generalizability of the findings. The clear demonstration of performance gains even when in-domain data is extremely scarce is noteworthy.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents convincing experimental results on four different language pairs and diverse test domains, consistently showing improvements over strong baselines.  The gains of over 3 BLEU on the Chinese-to-English SMS task and the sustained improvements with as little as 100 in-domain sentences are significant. The statistical significance testing further supports the validity of the findings.
2. **Novelty in Low-Resource Setting:** The application of CNNs for data selection in the extremely low-resource setting (less than a few hundred in-domain sentences) is a genuine contribution. Prior work on data selection typically requires significantly larger in-domain datasets for effective language model training. This work effectively addresses a gap in the literature.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:**  The comparison against several strong baselines, including both n-gram and RNNLM-based data selection methods, provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed approach.  The ablation study varying the size of the in-domain data used for CNN training further strengthens the analysis.
4. **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – domain adaptation with limited resources – is highly relevant to real-world machine translation applications, particularly with the rise of social media and other rapidly evolving domains where labeled data is scarce.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Discussion of Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates strong performance, it lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *why* the CNN is effective and identifying the types of errors it makes would provide valuable insights. For example, a discussion of the features the CNN learns to distinguish in-domain from out-of-domain data would be beneficial.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning the CNN meta-parameters on held-out data, but provides limited details on the search space and the specific hyperparameter settings used. Reporting these details would increase reproducibility and provide insights into the robustness of the approach.
3. **Dependence on Pre-trained Word Embeddings:** The method relies heavily on pre-trained word embeddings. While this is a common practice, the impact of the quality and source of these embeddings on the overall performance is not fully explored. Could the performance be further improved by fine-tuning the embeddings during CNN training?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the features learned by the CNN? What types of n-grams or word combinations are most strongly associated with in-domain data according to the CNN?
2. What was the specific search space and tuning procedure used for the CNN hyperparameters (window size, stride, number of kernels, etc.)?
3. Have you experimented with fine-tuning the pre-trained word embeddings during CNN training? If so, what was the impact on performance?
4.  The paper mentions in-domain data "subset" within the Chinese-to-English SMS task. Could you elaborate on how you identified this subset, and why it influenced the results so significantly?

**Additional Comments:**

* The introduction could be more concise.
* Consider adding a visualization of the CNN architecture.
* The reference list is extensive.
