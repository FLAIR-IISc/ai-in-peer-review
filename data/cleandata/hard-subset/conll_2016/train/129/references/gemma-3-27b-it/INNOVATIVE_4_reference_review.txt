This paper tackles the challenging problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The core idea – leveraging semi-supervised convolutional neural networks to classify and select training data – is genuinely novel and potentially impactful. While the experimental setup isn't groundbreaking, the consistent performance improvements across multiple language pairs, *especially* with minimal in-domain data (down to 100 sentences), are compelling. The authors clearly articulate the problem and position their work within the existing literature.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Application of CNNs for Data Selection:** The use of CNNs, particularly in a semi-supervised setting, for identifying relevant training data in low-resource domain adaptation is a fresh approach.  Previous work largely focused on LM-based similarity, and this offers a promising alternative.
2. **Demonstrated Effectiveness with Extremely Limited Resources:** The results showing substantial gains (up to 3.1 BLEU) even when training the CNN classifier with only 100 in-domain sentences are the strongest aspect of this paper. This addresses a critical gap in existing adaptation techniques.
3. **Strong Empirical Validation:**  Experiments across four language pairs and diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) provide reasonable confidence in the generalizability of the approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea is not a minor tweak. It’s a fundamentally different way to approach domain adaptation, and if it holds up, it could significantly lower the barrier to deploying MT systems in new domains.  I value the authors’ willingness to explore a less-trodden path.
2. **Robustness to Data Scarcity:**  The 100-sentence result is extremely impressive.  Many existing methods struggle to provide meaningful adaptation with such limited in-domain data. This is a key strength and directly addresses a practical need.
3. **Clear Presentation & Solid Experimental Design:**  The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The experimental setup, while using standard SMT components, is well-defined and the comparisons to strong baselines are appropriate. The ablation study reducing the in-domain data size is particularly insightful.
4. **Effective Use of Pre-trained Word Embeddings:** The incorporation of word embeddings trained on large general-domain corpora is a sensible approach, mitigating the risk of overfitting with limited in-domain data. It shows a good understanding of semi-supervised learning principles.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Behavior:** The paper lacks detailed analysis of *why* the CNN is effective. What features are being learned? Are specific words or n-grams driving the classification?  Visualizing the learned filters or performing an error analysis would provide valuable insights.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t extensively explore the sensitivity of the CNN to different hyperparameters (kernel sizes, filter numbers, etc.). A more thorough hyperparameter search and reporting would strengthen the findings.
3. **Reliance on Standard SMT System:**  While a fair comparison, the use of a phrase-based SMT system feels somewhat dated given the recent advancements in neural machine translation.  Testing the approach with a modern NMT system could reveal further benefits or limitations.
4. **Lack of Statistical Significance Reporting in All Results:** While statistical significance is reported for some results, it's not consistently applied throughout the tables. This makes it harder to assess the robustness of the improvements.


**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide some examples of the sentences that your CNN correctly identified as in-domain, and sentences it misclassified? This would help understand the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.
2. Did you experiment with different architectures for the CNN (e.g., deeper networks, different pooling strategies)? If so, what were the results?
3.  Could you comment on the computational cost of training the CNN classifier compared to the LM-based data selection methods?
4. How do you believe this approach would generalize to truly low-resource language pairs where large general-domain corpora for word embedding training are unavailable?



**Additional Comments:**

The references are comprehensive. Minor edits for clarity (e.g., consistency in abbreviation usage) would be beneficial.



**Overall:**

Despite some minor weaknesses, I believe this paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of domain adaptation for machine translation. The demonstrated effectiveness with extremely limited in-domain data is particularly impressive. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. The boldness of the approach and the potential impact outweigh the shortcomings.
