This paper tackles the challenging problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The core idea – leveraging semi-supervised convolutional neural networks to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain – is a genuinely novel and promising approach. While the methodology isn't groundbreaking in terms of CNN architecture itself, its application to *data selection* for low-resource domain adaptation in SMT is where the significant contribution lies. The empirical results, demonstrating substantial BLEU improvements even with as little as 100 in-domain sentences, are compelling.

**Main Contributions:**

1.  **Novel Application of CNNs for Data Selection:** The paper presents a creative application of CNNs, traditionally used for classification tasks, to the specific problem of identifying relevant training data for SMT in extremely low-resource scenarios. This is a non-trivial adaptation and offers a fresh perspective on domain adaptation.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors demonstrate consistent and substantial BLEU improvements across four language pairs and diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). The results are particularly impressive given the scarcity of in-domain data.
3.  **Demonstration of Effectiveness with Minimal Data:** The experimental results highlighting performance with only 100 labeled in-domain sentences is a key result showing the robustness of the method.

**Strengths:**

1.  **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The approach is significantly different from existing data selection techniques (LM-based methods) and shows clear advantages, particularly in low-resource settings. This has the potential to be a useful tool for rapidly adapting MT systems to new domains.
2.  **Compelling Experimental Results:** The consistently positive results across multiple language pairs and domains provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed method. The 3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task is particularly noteworthy.
3.  **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of domain adaptation with limited in-domain data and provides a strong justification for exploring new approaches. The growth of social media and the need for fast adaptation are compelling motivations.
4.  **Thorough Comparison to Baselines:** The authors compare their method to multiple strong baselines, including state-of-the-art LM-based data selection techniques, providing a comprehensive evaluation.
5.  **Well-written and Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written, and the methodology is described in a clear and understandable manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1.  **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture:** While the application is novel, the paper doesn’t offer much analysis of *why* the CNN works well. Exploring different CNN architectures, filter sizes, or pooling strategies could yield further improvements and insights. A more detailed ablation study would be beneficial.
2.  **Pre-trained Embeddings Reliance:** The reliance on pre-trained word embeddings is a potential limitation. While understandable given the limited in-domain data, the performance is inherently tied to the quality and relevance of these embeddings. Investigating methods for fine-tuning these embeddings or learning task-specific embeddings could be explored.
3.  **Lack of Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis of the data selection process would be useful. Understanding *which* sentences are correctly/incorrectly classified as in-domain could provide insights into the limitations of the method and potential areas for improvement.
4.  **Scalability Concerns (Implied):** While the results are promising, the paper doesn't explicitly address the computational cost of training and applying the CNN, particularly for very large datasets.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study of the CNN architecture (e.g., filter sizes, number of layers, pooling strategies) to demonstrate the sensitivity of the results to these parameters?
2.  Have you experimented with fine-tuning the pre-trained word embeddings on the general-domain data or incorporating a task-specific embedding layer? If so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide examples of sentences that were incorrectly classified as in-domain or out-of-domain and discuss potential reasons for these misclassifications?
4.  What is the computational cost of training the CNN classifier, and how does it scale with the size of the general-domain training data?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are comprehensive.
*   Minor: Some sentences could be rephrased for clarity (e.g., "The semi-supervised CNN achieved the best performance for all the tasks: its improvements...").



Overall, despite some minor weaknesses, this is a strong and promising paper that presents a novel and effective approach to domain adaptation for SMT. The novelty of the idea, coupled with the compelling experimental results, outweighs the methodological limitations. I recommend **acceptance**.
