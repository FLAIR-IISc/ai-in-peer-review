## Review for "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, enabling effective data selection from a larger general-domain corpus.  They demonstrate through experiments across four language pairs and various test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) that their method outperforms standard language model-based data selection techniques and even systems trained on the entirety of the general-domain data. A key finding is the effectiveness of the method even with as little as 100 in-domain sentences for training the CNN classifier.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of CNN Effectiveness for Low-Resource Domain Adaptation:** The strongest contribution is the robust empirical validation that CNNs *can* effectively address the low-resource domain adaptation problem in SMT. While CNNs have been applied to text classification, successfully applying them to guide data selection for SMT with extremely limited in-domain resources is a significant practical finding.
2. **Highlighting the Benefit of Semi-Supervision:**  The paper clearly demonstrates the advantage of leveraging large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data via pre-trained word embeddings within the CNN framework. This is crucial given the scarcity of in-domain labeled data, and the results indicate a clear gain from this approach.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their method across four language pairs and diverse test domains, making the findings more generalizable and convincing than evaluations on a single setup.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Results:** The consistently positive results across multiple language pairs and domains are compelling.  The BLEU improvements of 3.1 on Chinese-to-English SMS and the ability to achieve gains with only 100 in-domain sentences are particularly noteworthy. This is a practically significant advance.
2. **Well-Motivated Problem:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of domain adaptation with limited in-domain data, and frames it within the context of emerging social media domains where such data is often scarce. The motivation is very relevant to current MT research trends.
3. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized. The method is explained clearly, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail to allow for replication.  The related work section provides a good overview of the existing landscape.
4. **Thorough Comparison to Baselines:** The inclusion of multiple strong baselines (including state-of-the-art LM-based data selection methods) provides a convincing benchmark for the proposed approach.
5. **Robustness Analysis:**  The experiment varying the size of the in-domain training data for the CNN (Figure 1) effectively demonstrates the robustness of the approach to extremely limited resources and adds significant insight.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Method Components:** While the *combination* of CNNs and semi-supervised learning for this specific SMT domain adaptation task is valuable, the individual components (CNNs, word embeddings, data selection) are not novel in themselves. The paper doesn't introduce a fundamentally new CNN architecture or word embedding technique. A more thorough discussion of the novelty of the combination would be beneficial.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses heavily on quantitative results (BLEU scores).  A more in-depth qualitative error analysis – examining *why* the selected data improves translation quality – would provide deeper insight into the method's strengths and weaknesses. Are certain types of errors consistently reduced?  What characteristics of the selected sentences are most beneficial?
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning meta-parameters, but doesn't provide substantial detail about the tuning process.  A discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., the impact of different window sizes, embedding dimensions, or pooling methods) would be helpful.
4. **Implicit Domain Assumption:** Although the paper states that it doesn't consider domain information *about* the data, the *selection* of test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook) inherently implies a domain-specific focus. A more general discussion of the method's applicability to truly unknown domains would strengthen the claims.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the hyperparameter tuning process? What search strategies were employed, and how sensitive were the results to different parameter settings?
2.  Can you share some examples of sentences selected by your method that are particularly illustrative of its effectiveness? What characteristics do these sentences share?  Conversely, are there examples where the method selects sentences that *don’t* improve translation, and what might explain those failures?
3. The paper mentions a 120K in-domain subset in the zh2en task. Was this known *a priori*, or discovered during experimentation? If known, was it used for hyperparameter tuning, potentially biasing the results?
4.  Have you experimented with different CNN architectures (e.g., deeper networks, different types of pooling layers) beyond the one described in the paper?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., some citations include "et al." while others don't).
*   Consider adding a brief discussion of the computational cost of the proposed method compared to the baselines.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper with a clear contribution. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method for a challenging problem. While the individual components are not novel, the combination and empirical validation are significant.  Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
