- Summary: Proposes a semi-supervised CNN using pre-trained word embeddings to select in-domain training sentence pairs for SMT with very small in-domain data; outperforms baselines on four language pairs.  
- Contribution 1: Demonstrates CNNs with pre-trained embeddings can effectively select relevant data for low-resource MT adaptation (down to 100 sentences).  
- Contribution 2: Provides strong empirical validation across multiple language pairs and real-world domains, showing consistent improvements over established baselines.  
- Contribution 3: Shows robustness of the method when trained with extremely limited in-domain data.  
- Strength 1: Tackles a highly relevant, practical problem for real-world MT where in-domain data is scarce.  
- Strength 2: Compares against strong, well-established baselines (ngram and RNNLM-based selection).  
- Strength 3: Presents a clear methodology and thorough experiments, including an insightful ablation on in-domain data size.  
- Strength 4: Effectively leverages pre-trained word embeddings to boost performance in low-resource settings.  
- Strength 5: Uses significance testing (bootstrap resampling) to validate results.  
- Weakness 1: Limited novelty in the CNN architecture itself; main novelty is adaptation of existing techniques to this problem.  
- Weakness 2: Lacks detailed error analysis explaining what types of errors are reduced and why the method works.  
- Weakness 3: Relies on general-domain pre-trained embeddings, raising concerns about domain bias and representation of domain-specific nuances.  
- Weakness 4: Insufficient detail on hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity, affecting reproducibility.  
- Question 1: Please provide detailed examples and analysis of sentence pairs correctly versus incorrectly selected by the method.  
- Question 2: How sensitive are results to the choice of pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe vs. word2vec)?  
- Question 3: Elaborate the CNN hyperparameter tuning process (window size, stride, number of filters) and performance variability.  
- Question 4: Would incorporating training-data domain labels or topic information into the model improve performance?  
- Additional comment 1: Related work section is comprehensive and well-organized.  
- Additional comment 2: Paper is generally well-written and easy to understand.  
- Additional comment 3: Releasing code and scripts would aid reproducibility and further research.
