This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the scarcity of in-domain data. The core contribution is demonstrating that a CNN, trained with a small amount of labeled in-domain data and leveraging general domain word embeddings, can effectively select a subset of the large general-domain training data that yields superior translation performance compared to systems trained on all the general-domain data or with traditional language-model based data selection methods. A second, supporting contribution is the demonstration of the effectiveness of this approach across four language pairs and on test sets representing social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). Finally, the experiments showing consistently strong performance even with as little as 100 labeled in-domain sentences is a notable result.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a practical and important problem:** Domain adaptation with limited in-domain data is a significant challenge in SMT, particularly for emerging domains or low-resource languages. The problem setting is clearly motivated and relevant to real-world applications.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The experimental results across four language pairs demonstrate consistent and substantial improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art LM-based data selection methods. The improvements are particularly impressive given the small amount of in-domain data used for training (and even the classifier). The BLEU score gains (up to 3.1) are substantial.
3. **Effective use of semi-supervised learning:** The authors effectively combine supervised learning (with limited in-domain data) and unsupervised learning (pre-trained word embeddings) to build a robust classification model. This is a sensible approach for handling the data scarcity issue and leverages readily available resources. The analysis in the discussion section provides justification for *why* this approach is effective.
4. **Thorough experimentation:** The paper includes experiments varying the size of the in-domain training data, demonstrating the robustness of the method even with extremely limited supervision. The ablation study through comparison to a one-hot CNN provides insight into the value of the pre-trained embeddings. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of the CNN architecture:** The CNN architecture itself is fairly standard and lacks significant novelty. The contribution lies more in the *application* of CNNs to the data selection problem within the SMT context, rather than a novel CNN design.
2. **Lack of detailed analysis of error cases:** While the overall results are strong, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the system. Understanding *where* the system struggles would provide valuable insights for future improvements. An example of this might be identifying types of domain-specific terminology the system fails to capture, and why.
3. **Dependency on pre-trained word embeddings:** The method heavily relies on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. While this is a common practice, the paper doesn't discuss the potential impact of different embedding models or the sensitivity of the results to the choice of embeddings.
4. **Limited discussion of computational cost:** The paper doesn't provide any information about the computational cost of training the CNN classifier or the data selection process. This information would be useful for assessing the practicality of the approach.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions the use of “online dynamic adaptation” as a direction for future work. Could you elaborate on how you envision this working, and what challenges you anticipate?
2.  Have you explored different CNN architectures or hyperparameter settings? If so, what were the results? Were there particular architectural choices that significantly impacted performance?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding models (e.g., GloVe, fastText)?
4.  Could you provide an estimate of the computational resources (time and memory) required to train the CNN classifier and perform data selection for a dataset of a given size (e.g., 10 million sentence pairs)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is adequate but could be more focused on recent advances in data selection methods.
*   The notation in Equation 2 is slightly unclear. It would be helpful to explicitly state the dimensions of the matrices and vectors involved.
*   The paper would benefit from a more visually appealing presentation of the experimental results, such as error bar plots.
