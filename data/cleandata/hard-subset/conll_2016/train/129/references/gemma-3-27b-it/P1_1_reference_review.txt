## Peer Review: Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, and then use this classifier to select a subset of general-domain training data for SMT training. The key idea is to leverage pre-trained word embeddings (word2vec) to compensate for the scarcity of in-domain data.  Experiments on four language pairs demonstrate that their method outperforms baseline data selection techniques, particularly in low-resource scenarios.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of CNNs for Data Selection in Low-Resource MT Adaptation:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that CNNs, when combined with pre-trained word embeddings, can effectively select relevant training data for SMT adaptation even with extremely limited in-domain resources (down to 100 sentence pairs).  This is a practically relevant finding given the prevalence of low-resource domains.
2. **Strong Experimental Results:** The consistent BLEU score improvements across four language pairs and diverse test domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts) provide solid evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The comparison against strong baselines (including RNNLM-based data selection) strengthens these findings.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The authors provide a clear explanation of *why* their method works, highlighting the benefits of convolutional features for capturing domain-specific information and the importance of pre-trained word embeddings for handling out-of-vocabulary words.



**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance and Importance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant to the field of MT, particularly with the growing need to adapt systems to specialized domains where labelled data is scarce.  This addresses a genuine bottleneck in MT development.
2. **Solid Experimental Design:** The experiments are well-designed, with clear baselines, a reasonable number of language pairs and domains, and appropriate statistical significance testing. The ablation study (reducing the size of the in-domain data) is particularly valuable.
3. **Clear and Well-Written:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly, and the results are presented in a concise and informative manner.
4. **Novelty of Combination:** While CNNs and data selection are not novel in themselves, the *combination* of these techniques in a semi-supervised framework specifically targeted at the low-resource domain adaptation problem *is* a valuable contribution.
5. **Practical Impact:** The demonstrated improvements, particularly the 3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task with only 3% of the training data, suggests a significant potential for practical impact.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of *why* the method fails in certain cases.  Investigating misclassified sentence pairs could reveal weaknesses in the CNN model or the data selection process. What types of sentences are consistently misclassified?
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters, but does not provide much detail about this process.  A discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., the impact of different window sizes, embedding dimensions, or pooling methods) would strengthen the work.  Were the hyperparameters consistent across all language pairs?
3. **CNN Architecture Details:**  The CNN architecture is described relatively briefly.  More details on the number of layers, filter sizes, and activation functions would be helpful for reproducibility.  A diagram illustrating the network architecture would also be beneficial.
4. **Reliance on Word2Vec:** While leveraging pre-trained embeddings is a strength, the paper does not explore alternative embedding methods or investigate the impact of the quality of the pre-trained embeddings.  Could fine-tuning the embeddings during training improve performance?




**Questions to Authors:**

1. **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a qualitative analysis of the types of sentence pairs that are frequently misclassified by the CNN? What characteristics distinguish these sentences from those that are correctly classified?
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Can you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process?  Specifically, what search strategy was used, and how sensitive were the results to different hyperparameter settings?  Were the hyperparameters language-pair specific?
3. **Embedding Alternatives:** Have you experimented with different word embedding methods (e.g., GloVe, fastText) beyond word2vec? If so, how did the performance compare?
4. **CNN Architecture Justification:**  Why were the specific CNN architecture choices (number of layers, filter sizes, pooling method) made?  Were these choices based on prior work, or were they determined empirically?
5. **Generalizability to NMT:** While focused on SMT, do you anticipate that this approach could be effectively adapted to Neural Machine Translation (NMT) systems? If so, how?




**Additional Comments:**

*  The paper frequently refers to previous work (Duh et al., 2013). While appropriate, it might be helpful to briefly summarize the key differences between their approach and the one presented here in the introduction.
* The code is not available. Making the code publicly available would significantly enhance the reproducibility of the results.



Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and effective approach.  The weaknesses are relatively minor and could be addressed through additional analysis and detail. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
