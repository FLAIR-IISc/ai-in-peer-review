## Peer Review: Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as either in-domain or out-of-domain.  Pre-trained word embeddings are used to augment the limited in-domain data, and the trained CNN is then used to select a subset of general-domain data for training the SMT system.  The paper reports significant BLEU score improvements on four language pairs and several test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) over strong baselines, including LM-based data selection.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of CNN Effectiveness for Low-Resource Domain Adaptation:**  The paper convincingly demonstrates, through solid experimentation, that CNNs can be effectively leveraged for data selection in a very low-resource domain adaptation scenario – a setting often overlooked in prior work.  This is the core strength of the paper.
2. **Practical System & Results:** The authors provide a practical system and strong empirical results showing improvement across multiple languages and domains. The gains observed, particularly the 3.1 BLEU improvement on Chinese-to-English SMS with only 3% of the training data, are noteworthy.
3. **Insightful Analysis:** The discussion section provides valuable insights into *why* the CNN approach works, highlighting the importance of convolution for capturing domain-specific information and the benefits of leveraging pre-trained word embeddings.



**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance & Importance:** The problem addressed – low-resource domain adaptation – is highly relevant and practical. The increasing prevalence of new domains with limited parallel data necessitates research in this area.
2. **Strong Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-designed, using four language pairs and multiple test domains.  The comparison against strong and well-established baselines (LM-based data selection) provides a robust evaluation of the proposed method.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized. The explanation of the semi-supervised CNN framework is understandable and the experimental setup is detailed enough for reproducibility.
4. **Significant Improvements:** The reported BLEU score improvements are substantial, particularly considering the limited amount of in-domain data used. This demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
5. **Thorough Ablation Study:** The experiment exploring the effect of the amount of in-domain data used for CNN training provides valuable insight into the robustness of the approach and confirms its advantages in truly low-resource settings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the CNN Architecture:** The CNN architecture itself is fairly standard. The primary novelty lies in its application to the specific problem of low-resource domain adaptation for SMT data selection, rather than a fundamentally new CNN design.  The paper doesn't fully acknowledge this.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates *that* the method works, it doesn't delve into *why* specific sentences are selected or misclassified.  An error analysis, identifying common patterns in the selected/rejected data, would provide valuable insights and help understand the limitations of the approach.
3. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Tuning:**  While the paper states that hyperparameters were tuned, it lacks details on the tuning process itself. Providing more information about the hyperparameter search space and selection criteria would enhance reproducibility and trust in the results.  For example, what size was the held-out data set used for hyperparameter tuning?
4. **Reliance on Pre-trained Word Embeddings:** While using pre-trained embeddings is sensible given the limited in-domain data, the paper doesn’t explore the sensitivity of the results to the quality or source of those embeddings. Would the performance change significantly with different pre-trained embeddings?




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Error Analysis:** Could you provide a qualitative error analysis? Specifically, can you provide examples of sentence pairs that were incorrectly classified by the CNN (i.e., in-domain classified as out-of-domain, or vice versa) and discuss potential reasons for these misclassifications?
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:**  Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process? What range of values was explored for key parameters (e.g., window size, stride size, number of filters, embedding dimension)? How was the best configuration selected?
3. **Sensitivity to Word Embeddings:**  Have you experimented with different pre-trained word embeddings?  How sensitive are the results to the choice of embeddings?
4. **Computational Cost:**  What is the computational cost of training the CNN and performing the data selection process, compared to the LM-based methods? This is important for understanding the practicality of the approach for very large datasets.
5.  **Generalizability to Neural MT:** While the paper focuses on SMT, could you speculate on the potential benefits of applying this semi-supervised CNN approach to domain adaptation for Neural Machine Translation (NMT) systems?



**Additional Comments:**

* The paper would benefit from a more concise introduction.
* The related work section is fairly comprehensive.
* The references are formatted correctly.
* The tables and figures are clear and easy to understand.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation for machine translation.  The proposed semi-supervised CNN approach is effective, particularly in low-resource scenarios, and the experimental results are compelling.  While the CNN architecture itself isn’t groundbreaking, the application and the robust experimental evaluation justify acceptance. Addressing the weaknesses identified above, particularly the error analysis and discussion of hyperparameter tuning, would further strengthen the paper.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
