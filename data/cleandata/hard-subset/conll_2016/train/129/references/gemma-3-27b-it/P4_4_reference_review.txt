This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, and then use this classifier to select training data for the SMT system. The key contribution lies in successfully adapting SMT systems with extremely limited in-domain data (down to 100 sentence pairs), leveraging pre-trained word embeddings and the power of CNNs for domain classification. A second contribution is the empirical demonstration of the effectiveness of this approach across four language pairs and various test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts), showing consistent improvements over strong baselines. Finally, the detailed experimental analysis and ablation studies provide valuable insights into the impact of different components, such as the semi-supervised learning framework and CNN architecture.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses an Important Problem:** The problem of domain adaptation with limited in-domain data is a significant practical challenge in machine translation, particularly with the rise of new and niche domains. The authors clearly articulate this problem and propose a novel approach.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The proposed method consistently outperforms strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model-based data selection techniques, especially in the low-resource in-domain data setting. The reported BLEU score improvements (up to 3.1) are substantial.
3. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation across four language directions and multiple test domains. The ablation studies and parameter tuning further demonstrate the robustness and effectiveness of the proposed approach. The fact that the method continues to improve performance even when using only 100 in-domain sentences is impressive.
4. **Effective Use of Semi-Supervised Learning:** The integration of pre-trained word embeddings (word2vec) into the CNN framework is a smart choice, allowing the model to leverage large amounts of unlabeled data to improve domain classification accuracy, particularly when in-domain labeled data is scarce.
5. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The methodology is explained clearly, and the experimental setup is described in detail, facilitating reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of CNN Application:** While the application of CNNs to domain classification is reasonable, it's not entirely novel. CNNs have been widely used in text classification tasks, and the core CNN architecture used in this work is relatively standard. The novelty lies more in the specific application to SMT data selection and the semi-supervised framework in a low-resource domain adaptation scenario.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions hyperparameter tuning but doesn't provide extensive details on the specific values used or the sensitivity of the results to these parameters. A more in-depth discussion of hyperparameter selection would be valuable.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, the paper lacks a qualitative error analysis. Examining specific examples where the proposed method succeeds or fails could provide insights into the limitations of the approach and suggest directions for future improvement. Why does the method fail in some cases?
4. **Limited Comparison to Other Adaptation Techniques:** The comparison to other SMT adaptation techniques (e.g., self-training, instance weighting) is somewhat limited. Including a broader range of baselines could provide a more comprehensive assessment of the proposed method's effectiveness.
5. **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of training the CNN classifier and selecting the data compared to other data selection methods. Assessing the efficiency of the proposed approach is important for practical applications.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and the criteria used to select the optimal values?
2.  Can you provide specific examples of sentence pairs that your method correctly classifies as in-domain but other methods fail to identify, and vice versa?
3.  How does the computational cost of your method compare to the baseline LM-based data selection methods, both in terms of training time and data selection time?
4.  Have you considered exploring different CNN architectures (e.g., deeper networks, different pooling strategies) to further improve performance?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The citation style is inconsistent (e.g., some citations include year, others don't).
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used in the experiments.
*   The phrase “tiny amount of in-domain data” is subjective. Providing a more precise definition of what constitutes “tiny” would be helpful.
