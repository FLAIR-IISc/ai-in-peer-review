This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The core idea is to use a CNN to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the limited in-domain data. Selected in-domain sentences are then used to train an SMT system.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the exploration of CNNs for *data selection* specifically in the low-resource domain adaptation scenario for SMT, and demonstrating performance improvements over strong baselines. While CNNs have been used extensively in NLP, their application to intelligently select training data for SMT adaptation, particularly with minimal in-domain resources, represents a potentially valuable direction. A secondary contribution is the comprehensive experimental evaluation across four language pairs and multiple test domains, showcasing the robustness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper consistently demonstrates improvements over competitive baseline methods (LM-based data selection and a system trained on all data) across various language pairs and domains. The gains, particularly on the Chinese-to-English SMS task (3.1 BLEU), are significant and suggest the effectiveness of the approach.
2. **Addresses a Practical Problem:** The scenario of very limited in-domain data is a common challenge in real-world MT applications, especially for emerging domains or low-resource languages.  The proposed method directly tackles this problem.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The experiments are reasonably well-designed, employing multiple language pairs, diverse test domains, and a comparison with established data selection techniques. The inclusion of ablation studies (e.g., varying the amount of in-domain data) strengthens the analysis.
4. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the proposed methodology is described in a straightforward manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Methodology:** The use of CNNs for text classification and the concept of semi-supervised learning are not novel contributions in themselves. The main novelty lies in the *application* to the specific problem of SMT data selection, but the CNN architecture and training procedure are fairly standard. The authors do not convincingly demonstrate why CNNs are uniquely suited for this task compared to other classification methods.
2. **Insufficient Error Analysis:** While the results are positive, the paper lacks a detailed error analysis to understand *why* the method succeeds or fails. Identifying specific types of sentences or domain characteristics where the method performs well or poorly would provide valuable insights. Are there specific linguistic features the CNN effectively captures?
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning meta-parameters, but provides limited detail about the tuning process. The performance may be sensitive to specific hyperparameter settings, and a more thorough sensitivity analysis would be beneficial.  What happens if the window size or the number of neurons is significantly altered?
4. **Minor Formatting/Presentation Issues:** There are several instances of inconsistent formatting (e.g., use of "\Q" and "\E" around citations) and minor grammatical errors throughout the paper. While not critical, these detract from the overall polish.  Table 1 is also oddly formatted.
5. **Limited Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of training and deploying the CNN-based data selection method compared to the baseline approaches. This is an important consideration for practical applications.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of why you believe CNNs are particularly well-suited for this task compared to other classification algorithms (e.g., Support Vector Machines, Random Forests)?
2.  Can you provide examples of sentences that the CNN classifier correctly identifies as in-domain and out-domain, and sentences where it makes errors? This would help to understand the strengths and weaknesses of the method.
3.  What was the procedure for tuning the hyperparameters of the CNN?  Could you provide a sensitivity analysis showing how performance varies with different hyperparameter settings?
4. What is the computational overhead (training and inference time) of using the semi-supervised CNN-based data selection method compared to the baseline data selection methods?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The citations are somewhat inconsistent. Please ensure a uniform style.
*   Review Table 1's formatting for clarity.
*   Consider adding a section discussing limitations and future work, explicitly addressing the computational cost.



Overall, while the paper presents a valuable application of existing techniques to a practical problem and achieves promising results, the lack of substantial methodological novelty and the limited error analysis lead me to lean towards a *weak reject* recommendation. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its potential impact.
