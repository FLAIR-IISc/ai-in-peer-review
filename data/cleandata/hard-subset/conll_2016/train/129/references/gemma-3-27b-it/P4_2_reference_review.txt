This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, and then use this classifier to select relevant data for SMT training.

The primary contribution of this work is the demonstration of effective data selection for low-resource domain adaptation using a semi-supervised CNN. The authors effectively combine pre-trained word embeddings with a CNN classifier, showing significant improvements in translation quality, particularly in scenarios with extremely limited in-domain data (down to 100 sentence pairs). A secondary contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation across four language pairs and several test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts), providing strong evidence for the robustness of the proposed approach. The paper also provides a well-structured overview of existing domain adaptation techniques and related work on CNNs for text classification.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Results:** The experimental results are compelling. The reported BLEU score improvements, especially in the low-resource settings, are substantial and demonstrate the practical value of the proposed method. The results consistently outperform baseline systems, including those using established language model-based data selection techniques.
2. **Novelty in Low-Resource Setting:** While CNNs have been used in text classification, applying them specifically for data selection in the extremely low-resource domain adaptation scenario for SMT is novel. The use of a semi-supervised approach to leverage large unlabeled data further enhances the method's practicality.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation is comprehensive, covering four language pairs and various social media domains. This provides confidence in the generalizability of the approach and mitigates concerns about domain-specific performance. The ablation study, particularly showing performance with only 100 labeled examples, is a significant strength.
4. **Clear Explanation and Structure:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method and experimental setup. The organization is logical, and the technical details are presented in a way that is easy to understand. The inclusion of related work provides context and highlights the contribution of this research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture:** The paper lacks detailed analysis of the impact of different CNN hyperparameters (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters, pooling strategies) on performance. While the authors mention tuning these parameters, the specific choices and their rationale are not fully discussed. Understanding which aspects of the CNN architecture are most critical for this task would be valuable.
2. **Dependence on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The success of the method relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. While the authors acknowledge this, they don't explore the impact of different pre-trained embeddings (e.g., different training corpora or embedding dimensions). It would be beneficial to investigate how the choice of embeddings affects the overall performance.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed error analysis of the data selection process. Identifying common types of errors (e.g., misclassifying domain-specific terms) could provide insights for improving the classifier and the overall adaptation strategy.
4. **Baseline Selection:** The chosen baselines, while standard, could be further strengthened. Including more recent and sophisticated data selection techniques would provide a more rigorous comparison.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for the CNN, including the specific values explored and the criteria used for selection?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embeddings, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Can you provide examples of sentences that were incorrectly classified by the CNN, and discuss the reasons for these errors?
4.  Have you considered using more advanced CNN architectures, such as those incorporating attention mechanisms or residual connections? Would these architectures potentially improve the performance of the data selection process?
