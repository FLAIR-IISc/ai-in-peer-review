## Review for "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of adapting Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) systems to new domains when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) approach to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, using pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the scarcity of in-domain data.  The selected in-domain sentence pairs are then used to train the SMT system. Experiments on four language pairs and several test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms baseline data selection techniques, particularly when the amount of in-domain data is very small.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of CNNs for Low-Resource Domain Adaptation in SMT:** The most significant contribution is the strong empirical evidence demonstrating that semi-supervised CNNs can effectively select relevant training data for SMT even with extremely limited in-domain resources (down to 100 sentences).  This is a practical and important result for scenarios where obtaining labelled in-domain data is expensive or impossible.
2. **Effective Combination of Pre-trained Embeddings and CNNs:** The use of pre-trained word embeddings within the CNN framework is a crucial component of the success. It leverages large amounts of unlabelled data to provide useful semantic representations when in-domain training data is scarce.
3. **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:**  The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple language pairs and diverse test domains. This strengthens the generalizability of the findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) are substantial, especially considering the limited in-domain data available.  The fact that the method outperforms strong baselines, including RNNLM-based data selection, is compelling.
2. **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – low-resource domain adaptation – is highly relevant in the real world, where new domains emerge frequently and labelled data is scarce. The demonstrated ability to adapt with only 100 sentences is a significant achievement.
3. **Clear Methodology:** The proposed approach is clearly explained and well-motivated. The semi-supervised learning framework is logically presented and easy to understand. The use of readily available tools like word2vec and existing SMT toolkits promotes reproducibility.
4. **Detailed Analysis:**  The ablation study reducing the in-domain training data to 100 sentences is particularly valuable.  It clearly demonstrates the robustness of the CNN-based approach compared to LM-based methods in low-resource settings.
5. **Rigorous Evaluation:** The use of bootstrap resampling for statistical significance testing adds confidence to the reported results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the CNN Architecture:** The CNN architecture itself is relatively standard and lacks significant novel contributions.  The primary innovation lies in *applying* CNNs effectively to the domain adaptation problem in SMT, rather than introducing a new CNN architecture. While effective, this limits the novelty of the work.
2. **Lack of Detailed Error Analysis:**  The paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis of *why* the CNN selects certain sentences and *what types* of errors are still present after adaptation. Understanding the types of sentences misclassified by the CNN could lead to further improvements.
3. **Dependence on General-Domain Embeddings:** The success hinges on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings.  The paper does not discuss potential issues with embedding bias or how well the embeddings generalize to the specific test domains.  A sensitivity analysis exploring the impact of different pre-trained embeddings would be beneficial.
4. **Focus on SMT:** While the problem is framed within SMT, the core idea of using CNNs for domain classification could potentially be applicable to Neural Machine Translation (NMT) as well.  The paper does not explore this possibility, which limits its broader impact.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide examples of sentence pairs correctly classified as in-domain by your system that the baseline methods missed, and vice-versa? This would help understand the specific strengths of your approach.
2.  Did you experiment with different pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., GloVe) and how did they affect the results?
3.  Have you considered applying this approach to Neural Machine Translation (NMT) systems? If so, what were the results?
4.  Can you elaborate on the computational cost of training and applying the CNN classifier, particularly for very large datasets?  




**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.
*   The references are comprehensive and up-to-date.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the hyperparameters used for the CNN model for each language pair.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a practical and effective solution to a challenging problem in machine translation. While the CNN architecture itself is not novel, the application to low-resource domain adaptation and the demonstrated empirical results are significant contributions.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
