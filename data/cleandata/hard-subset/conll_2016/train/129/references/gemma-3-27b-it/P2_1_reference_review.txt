## Review for "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of adapting Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) systems when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) approach to classify sentences as in-domain or out-of-domain, then use this classifier to select training data for the SMT system. They demonstrate through experiments on four language pairs and various test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) that their method outperforms baseline data selection techniques, especially in low-resource scenarios.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of CNNs for Data Selection in Low-Resource MT Adaptation:** The paper demonstrates the practical effectiveness of using CNNs, combined with pre-trained word embeddings, for identifying and selecting relevant training data when limited in-domain resources exist. This is a valuable contribution, as existing data selection techniques often rely on larger in-domain corpora.
2. **Empirical Validation on Diverse Domains:** The evaluation across four language pairs and multiple social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) provides strong evidence for the robustness and generalizability of the proposed method.  This broad experimental setup is a strength.
3. **Demonstration of Performance with Extremely Limited In-Domain Data:**  The results showing continued improvements even with as little as 100 in-domain sentences are particularly compelling and address a key gap in existing adaptation techniques.



**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Results:** The presented results consistently show significant improvements over strong baselines, particularly in the low-resource setting. The BLEU score improvements (up to 3.1) are substantial and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
2. **Well-Motivated Problem:** The problem of adapting MT systems with scarce in-domain data is a highly relevant and practical challenge in the field. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches and the need for methods that can perform well with minimal supervision.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The paper evaluates the proposed method on four different language pairs and a variety of test domains, representing a diverse range of real-world scenarios.  The inclusion of social media data is particularly valuable, as this domain often suffers from a lack of parallel resources.
4. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined.  The inclusion of tables summarizing the data and results enhances clarity.
5. **Insightful Discussion:** The authors provide a thoughtful discussion of the reasons for the method's success, highlighting the importance of both convolutional feature extraction and the use of pre-trained word embeddings.



**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the combination of CNNs and semi-supervised learning for data selection is effective, the individual components (CNNs, word embeddings, data selection) are not novel in themselves.  The paper's novelty lies in the specific application and the demonstrated effectiveness within the low-resource MT adaptation context. This isn't a fatal flaw, but it needs to be acknowledged.
2. **Lack of Ablation Study:**  While the paper compares the “ohcnn” and “sscnn” models, a more detailed ablation study exploring the contribution of different components (e.g., word embeddings, convolution layer parameters) would strengthen the analysis.  How much of the gain comes from the semi-supervised aspect versus the CNN architecture itself?
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters on held-out data, but lacks specific details about the tuning process. Providing more information about the hyperparameter search space and the optimization strategy would increase reproducibility.
4. **Dependency on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The reliance on pre-trained word embeddings is a strength in the low-resource scenario, but also a potential limitation. The performance is tied to the quality and coverage of the pre-trained embeddings. A brief discussion of this dependency and potential mitigations would be beneficial.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space and optimization strategy used?
2.  What is the impact of the size of the pre-trained word embedding vocabulary on the performance of your system?
3.  Have you explored other methods for combining the source and target sentence scores for data selection (e.g., weighted averaging)? If so, what were the results?
4.  Could you elaborate on the computational cost associated with training the CNN classifier compared to the baseline data selection methods?



**Additional Comments:**

*   It would be interesting to see a comparison against more recent data selection techniques based on neural networks (beyond the RNNLM baseline).
*  The citation style is a bit inconsistent.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a practical and effective approach to a challenging problem in machine translation.  While the individual components are not entirely novel, the combination and experimental validation are compelling.  The paper addresses a clear need in the field and provides valuable insights into adapting MT systems with limited in-domain data.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
