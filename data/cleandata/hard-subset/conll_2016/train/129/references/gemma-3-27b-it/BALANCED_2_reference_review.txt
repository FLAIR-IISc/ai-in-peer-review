This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, and then use this classifier to select a subset of the general-domain training data for SMT training. The core idea of leveraging pre-trained word embeddings in a semi-supervised CNN framework for data selection is a notable contribution, particularly given the scarcity of in-domain resources. A second, significant contribution lies in the empirical demonstration across four language pairs and multiple test domains—social media posts (SMS, tweets, Facebook)—that their method outperforms strong baselines, particularly when the in-domain data is extremely limited (down to 100 sentence pairs). Finally, the thorough experimental evaluation and ablation studies, including comparisons with LM-based methods, provide a compelling case for the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance and Importance:** The problem addressed is highly relevant and practically important. The increasing availability of data from diverse domains, coupled with the cost of manually annotating in-domain data, makes automatic domain adaptation techniques crucial for building effective MT systems. The focus on low-resource scenarios is particularly commendable.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are convincing, showing consistent improvements over strong baselines across multiple language pairs and domains. The gains observed, especially with extremely limited in-domain data, are substantial (e.g., >3 BLEU improvement on zh-en SMS). The inclusion of statistical significance testing strengthens the validity of these findings.
3. **Novelty and Technical Soundness:** While CNNs have been used for text classification, the application to *data selection* for SMT domain adaptation, coupled with the semi-supervised learning framework utilizing pre-trained word embeddings, is a novel and technically sound approach. The reasoning behind using CNNs to capture domain-specific features and leveraging word embeddings to handle out-of-vocabulary words is well-motivated.
4. **Thorough Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive evaluation, including ablation studies (one-hot CNN vs. semi-supervised CNN), comparisons with multiple baselines (LM-based methods, all data), and analysis of the impact of varying the amount of in-domain data.  This demonstrates a careful and rigorous approach to evaluation.
5. **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly presents the proposed method and experimental results. The related work section provides a good overview of existing approaches to domain adaptation.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the results are positive, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of *why* the proposed method works and *where* it fails. Investigating the types of sentences that are correctly/incorrectly selected could provide valuable insights for future improvements.  For example, are there specific linguistic phenomena or domain characteristics that pose challenges to the classifier?
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning the CNN meta-parameters on held-out data, but doesn’t provide detailed information on the tuning process or the sensitivity of the results to these parameters.  It would be helpful to understand how robust the method is to different hyperparameter settings.
3. **CNN Architecture Detail:** The description of the CNN architecture is somewhat limited. Specifying the number of layers, filter sizes, and pooling strategies in more detail would enhance reproducibility and understanding. While the paper references the context toolkit, a brief outline within the paper itself is beneficial.
4. **Generalizability to Other SMT Systems:** The experiments are conducted with a phrase-based SMT system. It would be worthwhile to discuss the potential applicability of the proposed data selection method to other SMT paradigms, such as neural machine translation (NMT). Would the same approach be effective in selecting data for training an NMT model?



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of sentences that are correctly and incorrectly selected by the CNN classifier? Are there any patterns or characteristics that differentiate them?
2.  What was the process for tuning the CNN hyperparameters (e.g., window size, stride, number of filters)?  How sensitive are the results to different parameter settings?
3. Could you elaborate on the CNN architecture used in your experiments (number of layers, filter sizes, pooling strategies)?
4. Do you anticipate that the proposed data selection method would be effective for training neural machine translation (NMT) models?  Have you considered applying it to NMT, or do you foresee any challenges?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The reference list is extensive and well-organized.
*   The use of tables and figures is effective in presenting the results.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of the proposed method compared to the baseline methods.



Overall, this is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a novel and effective approach. The thorough experimental evaluation and positive results make a compelling case for the proposed method. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance.
