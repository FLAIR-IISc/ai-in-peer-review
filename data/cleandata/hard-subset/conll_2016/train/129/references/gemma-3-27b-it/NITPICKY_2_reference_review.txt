This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT), specifically addressing the scenario where only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors leverage pre-trained word embeddings and CNNs to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, then select high-scoring sentences to train an SMT system.

The primary contribution, as I see it, is a demonstration of the effectiveness of CNNs, combined with pre-trained embeddings, for data selection in a low-resource domain adaptation setting. While CNNs have been used for text classification, applying them specifically to *select* training data for SMT, and showing gains with extremely limited in-domain data, is a worthwhile empirical contribution. A secondary contribution is the thorough experimentation across four language pairs and multiple test domains, demonstrating a degree of robustness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Empirically Strong Results:** The reported BLEU score improvements, particularly the 3.1 BLEU gain on the Chinese-to-English sms task with only 3% of the training data, are substantial and demonstrate the potential of the proposed method.  The consistent improvements across multiple domains lend credibility to the approach.
2. **Addressing a Practical Problem:** The paper tackles a very relevant and often encountered problem in machine translation – adapting to new domains with limited labeled data. This scenario is common in real-world applications, making the research highly practical.
3. **Clear Methodology:** The paper clearly outlines the methodology, including the CNN architecture, training process, and data selection strategy.  The use of existing tools (word2vec, context) is well-documented, promoting reproducibility.
4. **Comprehensive Experimentation:** Testing across four language pairs and multiple test domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts) is commendable. This provides a more generalizable evaluation of the method's effectiveness.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in Core Methodology:** The individual components – CNNs, word embeddings, and data selection – are not novel in themselves. The combination, while effective, doesn’t represent a breakthrough in any single technique. The paper relies heavily on established methods.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** While the paper compares against several baselines, a more detailed ablation study examining the contribution of individual components (e.g., the impact of pre-trained embeddings vs. training embeddings from scratch, different CNN architectures, different pooling methods) is missing. This limits understanding of *why* the method works.
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *what types* of errors the adapted system still makes, and *why*, would provide valuable insights and guide future work. What kind of sentences are still misclassified? How do these misclassifications impact translation quality?
4. **Inconsistent Reporting & Lack of Statistical Significance Detail:** While statistical significance is mentioned, the details are inconsistently reported within Table 2 (using asterisks to denote p < 0.05 and 0.01, but not consistently applied). Furthermore, the significance testing methodology isn’t fully detailed. It’s unclear if corrections for multiple comparisons were applied.
5. **Writing Quality/Formatting:** The paper suffers from numerous minor stylistic and grammatical issues (e.g., awkward phrasing, inconsistent use of abbreviations).  Also, references are not always consistently formatted and the numbering appears to be incorrect in the reference section.  (e.g., Axelrod et al. appears twice). These issues detract from the paper’s overall clarity and professionalism.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study, specifically examining the impact of pre-trained word embeddings versus training them from scratch on the performance of the CNN classifier and the final SMT system?
2.  Please provide a more detailed description of the statistical significance testing procedure used, including the specific test applied and whether any corrections for multiple comparisons were made.
3.  Can you present a qualitative error analysis, outlining the common types of misclassifications made by the CNN and how these errors translate into errors in the final translation output?
4.  Please address the inconsistent formatting of references.

**Additional Comments:**

*   The claim about the 120K in-domain subset in the zh2en sms task is interesting. Was this known *a priori*, or was it discovered as a result of the experiments? If known, why wasn't this used as a baseline?
*   The connection between the size of the in-domain data and the performance of the classifier could be further explored. A plot showing the relationship between in-domain data size and classifier accuracy (or F1-score) might be informative.
*   Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost of the proposed method compared to the baseline methods.



Overall, the paper presents a solid empirical study with potentially significant implications for low-resource domain adaptation. However, the lack of methodological novelty and the numerous minor flaws prevent a strong recommendation for acceptance in its current form. A major revision addressing the weaknesses, particularly the ablation study, error analysis, and writing quality, is required. I lean towards **rejection** due to the cumulative effect of numerous, albeit minor, flaws.
