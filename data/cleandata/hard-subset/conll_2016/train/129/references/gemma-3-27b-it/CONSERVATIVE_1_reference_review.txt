This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in- or out-of-domain, and then use this classification to select a subset of the general-domain training data for SMT training. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that this CNN-based data selection can outperform standard language model-based methods, particularly in low-resource in-domain scenarios. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation of this approach across four language pairs and several test domains, including social media text.

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Problem & Clear Motivation:** The paper tackles a very relevant and practical problem in SMT – domain adaptation with scarce resources. The motivation for addressing this challenge, particularly with the rise of new domains from social media, is well-articulated.
2. **Empirically Sound:** The experiments are reasonably comprehensive, using four language pairs and different test domains (including the challenging social media domains). The inclusion of multiple baselines, including established LM-based data selection methods, strengthens the evaluation. The statistical significance testing is also a plus.
3. **Demonstrated Improvement:** The reported BLEU score improvements, particularly the 3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task with a small subset of data, are significant and suggest that the proposed method is effective. The results with only 100 in-domain sentences are particularly encouraging.
4. **Well-Established Components:** The paper builds on established techniques (CNNs, word embeddings, SMT) rather than proposing entirely novel architectures. This pragmatic approach increases confidence in the validity of the results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Method:** While the application of CNNs to data selection is a reasonable idea, the proposed method lacks substantial novelty. CNNs are already widely used for text classification, and applying them to this specific task doesn’t represent a breakthrough. The semi-supervised aspect, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings, is also a common practice.
2. **Insufficient Ablation Study:** The paper presents results for two CNN variants ("ohcnn" and "sscnn") but lacks a detailed ablation study to understand the contribution of each component. For example, how much of the improvement comes from using word embeddings vs. the CNN architecture itself? The impact of different CNN parameters (kernel size, number of filters) isn't explored, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the approach.
3. **Reliance on Positive/Negative Sample Creation:** The method relies on creating a training set for the CNN by simply labeling the development set as positive examples and randomly selecting sentences from the general domain as negative examples. This approach is overly simplistic and could introduce bias. A more sophisticated approach to negative sampling, perhaps based on similarity metrics, would be more robust. The potential impact of the random selection process is not discussed.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper doesn’t provide any qualitative error analysis. Understanding *why* the proposed method works (or fails) in specific cases would be valuable. Are there particular types of sentences or domains where the method struggles?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study to isolate the contribution of different components of the proposed system (e.g., word embeddings, CNN architecture, different pooling strategies)?
2.  What considerations were given to the potential bias introduced by the simplistic approach to creating negative samples? Have you explored alternative negative sampling strategies?
3.  Can you provide examples of sentences correctly classified by your system that the LM-based methods misclassified, and vice versa? This would help understand the strengths and weaknesses of your approach.
4.  Were any attempts made to adapt the CNN model itself to the specific test domain, beyond simply selecting the training data? For example, could fine-tuning the word embeddings on a small amount of in-domain data improve performance?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference list is extensive and appropriately formatted. The writing is generally clear and concise. The paper would benefit from a more critical discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and directions for future work. While the results are promising, the method feels more like a pragmatic application of existing techniques rather than a significant advancement in the field.
