This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, and then select high-scoring sentences to train the SMT system. The core contribution is the application of semi-supervised CNNs, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings, for data selection in a low-resource domain adaptation scenario. A secondary contribution lies in demonstrating consistent improvements over strong baselines across multiple language pairs and domains, particularly when the in-domain data is extremely scarce. Finally, the experimental setup and evaluation on several datasets provides a reasonable empirical assessment of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Relevant and Important Problem:** Low-resource domain adaptation is a significant bottleneck in practical MT deployment. The scenario presented—very limited in-domain data—is common in emerging domains and is well-motivated.
2. **Empirically Sound Results:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments across four language directions and multiple test domains. The consistent BLEU improvements over competitive baselines, including strong LM-based methods, provide strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The ablation study examining the impact of in-domain data size (down to 100 sentences) is particularly compelling.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is well-defined. The inclusion of related work is comprehensive.
4. **Demonstrated Robustness:** The results indicate that the semi-supervised CNN approach is relatively robust to the limited amount of in-domain data available. The stable performance, even when training the CNN with only 100 labeled sentences, demonstrates the method's practicality.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** While the application to SMT domain adaptation is a reasonable direction, the core techniques—CNNs for text classification and semi-supervised learning with pre-trained word embeddings—are well-established. The novelty is incremental rather than transformative. The authors do not offer a particularly new or innovative CNN architecture or training procedure.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of *Why* It Works:** The paper provides empirical evidence *that* the method works, but lacks a deeper analysis of *why*. For example, a more detailed examination of the features learned by the CNN could shed light on the types of domain-specific signals it is capturing. A qualitative analysis of selected vs. non-selected sentences could also be insightful.
3. **Over-reliance on BLEU:** While BLEU is a standard metric, it is known to have limitations. Considering additional evaluation metrics, such as human evaluation, could provide a more comprehensive assessment of the translation quality. Furthermore, error analysis would strengthen the conclusions.
4. **Baseline Selection and Parameter Tuning:** The comparison to baseline data selection methods is good, but a more thorough exploration of hyperparameter settings for both the CNN and the baseline methods could further strengthen the results. The limited discussion of hyperparameter tuning leaves some question regarding the robustness of the findings.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the features learned by the CNN? For example, what types of n-grams or word combinations are most strongly associated with in-domain sentences?
2. Could you provide examples of sentence pairs that were correctly classified by your method but misclassified by the LM-based baselines, and vice versa? What insights do these examples provide?
3. Have you explored alternative CNN architectures or training procedures? If so, what were the results?
4. The paper mentions "short messages," "tweets," and "Facebook posts" as test domains. Are there significant differences in the performance of your method across these different domains? If so, what might explain these differences?
