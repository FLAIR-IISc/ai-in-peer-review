## Peer Review: Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of domain adaptation in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) approach to classify training sentence pairs as either in-domain or out-of-domain, allowing for the selection of a more relevant subset of the general-domain training data. The key idea is to leverage pre-trained word embeddings (word2vec) to compensate for the scarcity of in-domain data during CNN training.  Experiments across four language pairs demonstrate that this method outperforms baseline data selection techniques, particularly in low-resource scenarios.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of CNNs for Data Selection in Low-Resource MT:**  The paper successfully demonstrates that CNNs, combined with pre-trained embeddings, can effectively classify sentence pairs for data selection in SMT, even with extremely limited in-domain data (down to 100 sentences). This is a practical contribution for scenarios where acquiring domain-specific data is expensive or impossible.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors provide comprehensive experimental results on four diverse language pairs and real-world domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), showcasing consistent improvements over strong baselines. This rigorous evaluation strengthens the credibility of the proposed approach.
3. **Demonstration of Robustness with Minimal Data:** The study highlights the robustness of the semi-supervised CNN approach, showing that performance is maintained even when training with a very small in-domain dataset. This is a crucial finding for truly low-resource adaptation.



**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance and Practicality:** The problem tackled – domain adaptation with limited in-domain data – is highly relevant to real-world MT applications.  The proposed solution offers a viable pathway for improving translation quality in scenarios where large, labeled in-domain datasets are unavailable.
2. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The authors compare their approach against well-established data selection methods (ngram and RNNLM-based techniques), providing a solid benchmark for evaluating its effectiveness.  The consistent outperformance of their method demonstrates its advantage.
3. **Clear Methodology and Thorough Experiments:** The paper is well-structured and clearly explains the proposed approach. The experiments are well-designed, with multiple language pairs and domains, and the results are presented comprehensively. The ablation study focusing on the amount of in-domain data is particularly insightful.
4. **Effective Use of Pre-trained Embeddings:** The judicious use of pre-trained word embeddings significantly enhances the performance of the CNN, especially in low-resource settings. This demonstrates a good understanding of the challenges associated with limited data and a thoughtful approach to addressing them.
5. **Significance Testing:** The inclusion of significance testing (bootstrap resampling) strengthens the validity of the results and increases confidence in the observed improvements.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in CNN Application:** While the application of CNNs to data selection is effective, the core CNN architecture itself isn't particularly novel. The primary contribution lies in the successful adaptation of existing CNN techniques to the specific problem of low-resource MT adaptation.  This is not necessarily a fatal flaw, but it weakens the overall "innovation" aspect of the work.
2. **Lack of Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper focuses on overall BLEU score improvements. A more in-depth analysis of the types of errors reduced by the proposed method would provide valuable insights. Understanding *why* the method works, beyond just *that* it works, would be beneficial.  For example, analyzing examples where the data selection correctly identified relevant sentences versus those it missed could reveal areas for further improvement.
3. **Dependence on General-Domain Word Embeddings:** The reliance on pre-trained word embeddings introduces a potential bias from the general domain. While effective, it's unclear how well these embeddings capture the nuances of the specific test domains. Investigating the impact of different embedding sources or fine-tuning them on a small amount of in-domain data could be worthwhile.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning meta-parameters but doesn't provide details about the tuning process or the sensitivity of the results to different parameter settings.  Addressing this would increase the reproducibility and robustness of the findings.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors reduced by your data selection method?  Specifically, could you show examples of sentence pairs that were correctly selected versus those that were incorrectly selected, and explain the reasons behind these outcomes?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embedding models (e.g., GloVe) and observe any significant differences in performance?
3.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process for the CNN? How were the window size, stride, number of filters, and other parameters selected, and how much did performance vary with different settings?
4.  You mention that you don't consider domain information about the training data.  Would incorporating such information (e.g., topic labels) into the CNN model potentially lead to further improvements?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The related work section is comprehensive and well-organized.
*   The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand.
*   The code and scripts used for the experiments would be valuable for reproducibility and further research.  Consider making them publicly available.
