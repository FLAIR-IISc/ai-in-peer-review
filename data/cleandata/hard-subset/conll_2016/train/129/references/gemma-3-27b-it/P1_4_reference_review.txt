## Peer Review: Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only very limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised approach utilizing Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain. The CNN is trained using a small amount of in-domain data paired with randomly selected out-of-domain data, and leverages pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the limited in-domain training signal. The trained CNN is then used to select relevant sentence pairs from the general domain corpus for training the SMT system. The authors demonstrate improvements over baseline data selection techniques, particularly in low-resource scenarios.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Demonstration of CNN effectiveness for low-resource domain adaptation in SMT:** The paper provides empirical evidence that CNNs can effectively identify in-domain data even with extremely limited labeled examples (down to 100 sentence pairs), offering a viable alternative to language model-based methods requiring larger in-domain corpora.
2. **Effective combination of semi-supervised learning with data selection:**  The intelligent use of pre-trained word embeddings within the CNN framework significantly boosts performance when in-domain data is scarce, capitalizing on information learned from large unlabeled corpora.
3. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation:** The authors evaluate their approach across four language pairs and multiple test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), providing a robust assessment of its generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a significant practical problem:** Domain adaptation with limited in-domain data is a common bottleneck in real-world MT deployments. This work directly addresses that challenge.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The reported BLEU score improvements, especially the 3.1 BLEU gain on the Chinese-to-English SMS task and the continued improvements with only 100 in-domain sentences, are substantial and convincing.
3. **Well-defined experimental setup:** The methodology is clearly described, and the comparison to established baselines (LM-based data selection) is fair and informative.  The bootstrap resampling for statistical significance is commendable.
4. **Clear and logical presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with a logical flow from problem statement to method description to experimental results. The motivation is clearly articulated.
5. **Insightful discussion of results:** The authors provide a reasonable explanation for why the semi-supervised CNN approach outperforms the baselines, highlighting the benefits of leveraging pre-trained word embeddings and the CNN's ability to capture key domain indicators.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of the CNN architecture itself:** The CNN architecture used is fairly standard. The primary contribution lies in *applying* CNNs to this specific domain adaptation problem and combining them with pre-trained embeddings, rather than proposing a novel CNN design. While effective, the architectural novelty is minimal.
2. **Lack of detailed error analysis:** The paper would benefit from a deeper investigation of *what* types of errors the system still makes, and *why*.  For example, are there specific types of in-domain sentences the CNN consistently misclassifies? This analysis would offer valuable insights for future improvements.
3. **Hyperparameter tuning details are sparse:**  While the authors mention tuning hyperparameters, they do not provide a detailed description of the tuning process.  More information on the search space and selection criteria would strengthen the reproducibility of the results.
4. **Reliance on publicly available data:** All experiments are conducted on publicly available datasets. While this is practical, it would be beneficial to demonstrate the method's performance on a more challenging, proprietary dataset with a unique domain.
5. **The role of source vs target language classification is unclear**: The authors mention classifying both source and target sentences, but the reasoning behind combining these scores (sum) isn’t thoroughly explained. Further clarification on the impact of each score would be useful.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, what search space was used for the CNN parameters (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters, learning rate) and how were the best parameters selected?
2.  Can you elaborate on the types of errors your system still makes? Providing examples of misclassified sentences and analyzing the underlying reasons would be very helpful.
3.  Why did you choose to sum the in-domain scores from the source and target sentences?  Were other combination methods (e.g., averaging, taking the maximum) explored, and if so, what were the results?
4. What is the computational cost of the CNN based selection compared to the LM based approach?
5.  You mention the potential for online dynamic adaptation. Could you briefly describe how this would be implemented and the challenges involved?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The referencing is generally good, but some citations could be more specific (e.g., when referencing "Axelrod et al.", specify the year).
* The paper could benefit from visualizing the learned word embeddings to show how the model clusters domain-specific terms.



Overall, this is a solid paper that addresses a relevant problem with a promising approach. The strong experimental results and clear presentation make it a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation for SMT. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above would further strengthen the work. I recommend acceptance.
