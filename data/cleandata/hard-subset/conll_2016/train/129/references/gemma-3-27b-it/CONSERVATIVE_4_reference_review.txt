This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, and then use this classifier to select training data. The primary contribution is the demonstration that a CNN, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings, can effectively perform data selection even with as few as 100 labeled in-domain sentence pairs, outperforming systems trained on all available general-domain data. A secondary contribution lies in the thorough experimental evaluation across four language pairs and diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). Finally, the systematic exploration of the impact of varying amounts of in-domain training data for the CNN classifier is valuable.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a significant practical problem:** The scenario of limited in-domain data is common in real-world MT applications, particularly with emerging domains like social media.  Existing domain adaptation techniques often rely on substantial in-domain resources. This work directly targets this limitation.
2. **Empirically strong results:** The reported BLEU score improvements, particularly the 3.1 BLEU gain on the Chinese-to-English SMS task and the consistent gains across other domains, are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The consistent outperformance of the “alldata” baseline is compelling.
3. **Rigorous experimental setup:**  The authors evaluate their method against strong and well-established baselines (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) using standard SMT tools and evaluation metrics. The inclusion of bootstrap resampling for significance testing strengthens the validity of the results.
4. **Demonstrated robustness to limited labeled data:** The ablation study showing performance with as few as 100 labeled sentences is highly valuable.  It highlights the effectiveness of the semi-supervised approach and the pre-trained word embeddings.
5. **Clear and well-structured presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically organized, making it relatively easy to follow the proposed approach and understand the experimental results.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of novelty in the core methodology:** While the *application* to low-resource domain adaptation is novel, the underlying techniques (CNNs for text classification and semi-supervised learning with pre-trained word embeddings) are well-established. The paper doesn’t introduce any fundamentally new CNN architectures or training strategies. The authors rely heavily on existing components.
2. **Limited analysis of *why* the method works:** The discussion section touches on the benefits of convolution and word embeddings, but a more detailed analysis of which features the CNN learns to identify as indicative of in-domain data would be beneficial.  What specific n-grams or word patterns are being prioritized?  A visualization of the learned convolutional filters would provide valuable insight.
3. **Dependence on pre-trained word embeddings:** While the use of pre-trained embeddings is crucial for performance, it introduces a dependency on the quality and domain relevance of those embeddings. The paper doesn't explore the impact of using different pre-trained embeddings (e.g., embeddings specifically trained on social media data) or fine-tuning the embeddings during CNN training. This is a potential limitation.
4. **Potential for bias in negative sample selection:**  The random selection of negative samples might introduce bias, particularly if the general-domain data contains some sentences that are inadvertently similar to the in-domain test data. A more sophisticated negative sampling strategy (e.g., hard negative mining) could potentially improve performance.
5. **Limited comparison to more recent data selection techniques:** The baselines, while strong, are somewhat dated. There's no comparison to more recent data selection methods that leverage deep learning beyond simple language models.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide examples of the convolutional filters learned by the CNN, and discuss which features they appear to be identifying as indicative of in-domain data?
2.  Have you experimented with fine-tuning the pre-trained word embeddings during CNN training, and if so, what was the impact on performance?
3.  Did you consider any alternative negative sampling strategies beyond random selection? If so, what were the results?
4.  Could you elaborate on the characteristics of the in-domain data that contribute to the largest performance gains? Are certain domains more amenable to this approach than others?
5.  How does the performance of your system degrade when the in-domain test data is significantly different from the general-domain data used to train the word embeddings?
