## Review for "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) approach to classify training sentence pairs as either in-domain or out-of-domain, allowing for the selection of a more relevant training subset. They demonstrate improvements over baseline data selection methods, particularly in low-resource scenarios, across four language pairs.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of CNNs to Data Selection for SMT:** The paper successfully adapts CNNs – a technique primarily used in image and text classification – to the specific problem of identifying in-domain data for SMT, demonstrating a practical and effective method.
2. **Strong Performance in Low-Resource Scenarios:**  The presented results demonstrate a clear benefit of the proposed method when in-domain data is scarce (e.g., 100 sentences), outperforming baseline methods and achieving significant BLEU score improvements.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The authors conduct experiments across four language pairs and diverse test domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts), providing robust evidence of the method's generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Relevance:** While CNNs are not new, applying them to *data selection* for SMT, especially with a semi-supervised approach designed for low-resource scenarios, is a valuable contribution. The problem addressed is practically relevant given the difficulties of acquiring large in-domain datasets.
2. **Strong Experimental Results:** The consistent improvements over strong baselines (including RNNLM-based data selection) are compelling. The BLEU score gains, particularly in the low-data regimes, are significant and highlight the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  The inclusion of statistical significance testing strengthens the validity of the findings.
3. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The evaluation across diverse language pairs and test domains lends credibility to the generalization ability of the method.  The controlled comparison against multiple baselines establishes a clear advantage for the proposed approach.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method, experimental setup, and results.  The inclusion of related work provides useful context. 

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of *Why* it Works:** While the paper demonstrates *that* the method works, it lacks in-depth analysis of *why*.  The discussion touches on the benefits of CNNs in capturing local features and leveraging pre-trained word embeddings, but a more detailed examination of the learned CNN features and their correlation with domain adaptation success would be valuable. For example, visualizing the filters learned by the CNN could provide insights.
2. **Dependence on Pre-trained Word Embeddings:** The semi-supervised nature of the approach relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. While this is acknowledged, the paper doesn't explore the sensitivity of the results to different pre-training datasets or embedding sizes.  Could the method perform similarly well without pre-trained embeddings, given enough general-domain data for training the embeddings from scratch?
3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning meta-parameters, but lacks details on the search process and the sensitivity of performance to different hyperparameter settings (e.g., window size, stride, number of neurons).  A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter optimization would strengthen the reproducibility of the results.
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** A targeted error analysis, focusing on examples where the proposed method fails, could provide valuable insights for future improvement. Identifying patterns in the errors could suggest areas for refinement.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide further insights into the specific features learned by the CNN that contribute to effective domain identification?  Perhaps visualizations of learned filters or an analysis of the activation patterns?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., different training corpora, embedding size)?  Have you experimented with training the word embeddings from scratch on the general domain data?
3.  Could you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? What range of values were explored for key parameters like window size, stride, and the number of neurons, and how did the performance vary with these settings?
4. Could you provide a few example cases where your method fails, and explain why those failures occur?



**Additional Comments:**

* The reference formatting appears slightly inconsistent.
* It would be helpful to briefly mention the computational cost of training and applying the CNN-based data selection method compared to the baseline methods.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid paper that presents a novel and effective approach to domain adaptation for SMT, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The experimental results are convincing, and the presentation is clear. While there are areas for further analysis and discussion, the contributions are significant and warrant acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
