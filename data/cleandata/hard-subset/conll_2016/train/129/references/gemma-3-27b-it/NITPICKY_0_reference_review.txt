## Review

This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT), specifically addressing the scenario of extremely limited in-domain data. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish in-domain from out-of-domain sentence pairs, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the scarcity of labeled in-domain examples. The trained classifier then selects sentences from the general domain corpus for SMT training.

The primary contribution, as I perceive it, is the empirical demonstration that a relatively simple semi-supervised CNN can effectively perform data selection for SMT adaptation even with a minimal amount of in-domain data (down to 100 sentences). While CNNs have been used in text classification, their application to *data selection* for SMT, particularly in this low-resource setting, is a worthwhile exploration. A secondary, albeit less impactful, contribution is the comparative evaluation against existing data selection techniques, showcasing the advantages of the proposed method in the scenarios tested. 

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments demonstrate consistent improvements in BLEU scores across four language pairs and diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) compared to strong baselines. The particularly notable result is the significant BLEU improvement achieved even with only 100 in-domain sentences, highlighting the method’s effectiveness in truly low-resource scenarios.
2. **Well-Defined Problem & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data, a realistic challenge in many real-world MT applications. The motivation for addressing this problem is convincingly presented.
3. **Clear Methodology:** The proposed approach is well-explained, with a clear description of the CNN architecture, training procedure, and data selection process. The use of pre-trained word embeddings is justified and relevant.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  The experimental setup includes multiple language pairs, different test domains, and comparisons to a variety of established data selection methods, providing a reasonably thorough evaluation of the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in CNN Architecture:** The CNN architecture employed is quite standard. While its application to this specific task is valuable, the technical novelty surrounding the CNN itself is minimal. The paper does not explore more sophisticated CNN architectures or configurations.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper presents overall BLEU scores but lacks a detailed analysis of *where* the system fails. Investigating error cases (e.g., specific types of sentences the method misclassifies) could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach and potential avenues for improvement.
3. **Limited Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but lacks a detailed discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters (e.g., window size, stride, number of convolutional filters). This limits the reproducibility and generalizability of the findings.  For instance, the choice of ReLU as the activation function is stated, but not justified.
4. **Inconsistent Reporting and Formatting:** There are minor inconsistencies in formatting throughout the paper, particularly in the table captions (e.g., inconsistent use of “M” and “K”). Furthermore, the reference list format isn't consistent and lacks completeness (e.g., "Axelrod et al.,?" and missing publication details). These details, while small, detract from the overall professionalism.
5. **Vague Description of Data Selection Criterion:** While the paper states that sentence pairs are selected based on the sum of source and target scores, it doesn't explicitly define the threshold or selection mechanism used to choose the top N sentences. A more precise description would be helpful.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the CNN classifier? What characteristics distinguish the sentences that are misclassified as in-domain vs. out-of-domain?
2.  How sensitive are the results to different hyperparameters of the CNN (e.g., window size, number of filters, learning rate)? Please provide a brief sensitivity analysis.
3.  What criteria were used to determine the number of sentences (N) selected for training the SMT system? Was this determined through a separate validation set, and can you describe the process?
4.  Can you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing ReLU as the activation function for the CNN? Were other activation functions considered, and if so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   Please carefully review the reference list for completeness and consistency in formatting.
*   A more detailed discussion of related work on active learning for data selection would be beneficial.
*   The term "domain" is used loosely. A more precise definition of what constitutes a "domain" in the context of this work would be helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

Despite some shortcomings, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation for SMT. The empirical results are compelling, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach in a challenging low-resource setting. However, the lack of technical novelty in the CNN architecture and the absence of detailed analysis weaken the overall impact of the work.  **I recommend rejection, but encourage the authors to address the identified weaknesses and resubmit a revised version.** The empirical findings are significant enough to warrant further investigation, but the paper currently falls short of the standards for acceptance at a competitive conference.
