This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT), specifically addressing the challenge of limited in-domain data. The core idea is to train a CNN to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the scarcity of labeled in-domain data. The method then selects high-scoring sentence pairs from the general domain corpus for SMT training.

The primary contribution is the demonstration of effective domain adaptation using CNNs with very limited in-domain data (down to 100 sentence pairs). This is a significant contribution, as many real-world MT scenarios involve low-resource domains. The use of semi-supervised learning with pre-trained word embeddings to address this scarcity is a sensible and well-motivated approach. A secondary contribution is the empirical validation of this approach across four language pairs and several test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), providing a robust assessment of its generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a critical problem:** The problem of domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data is a significant challenge in MT, and this paper directly tackles it. Existing methods often require thousands of in-domain sentences, which are frequently unavailable.
2. **Strong empirical results:** The experimental results consistently show improvements over strong baselines, including state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.
3. **Robust evaluation:** The evaluation across four language pairs and diverse test domains (social media data) strengthens the confidence in the generalizability of the approach. The inclusion of a bootstrap resampling test for statistical significance is commendable.
4. **Clear and logical approach:** The methodology is clearly explained and logically sound. The use of CNNs for domain classification, combined with pre-trained word embeddings, is a well-motivated and effective strategy for handling limited in-domain data.
5. **Detailed ablation study:** The experimentation demonstrating the effectiveness of reducing the in-domain training set to 100 sentences adds valuable insight and shows the robustness of the approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited novelty of individual components:** While the combination of CNNs and semi-supervised learning for data selection is effective, the individual components are not novel. CNNs for text classification and semi-supervised learning with pre-trained word embeddings are well-established techniques. The primary novelty lies in the specific application to this MT domain adaptation problem, which is significant but should be acknowledged.
2. **Hyperparameter tuning details:** The paper mentions meta-parameters were tuned on held-out data but lacks specific details on the tuning process. A more detailed description of the hyperparameter optimization would strengthen the reproducibility of the results. Specifically, how were the window size and stride selected?
3. **CNN architecture specifics:** The CNN architecture used (one layer, max pooling) is relatively simple. It would be valuable to briefly discuss why a more complex architecture was not explored. While simplicity is a virtue, a justification for the chosen architecture would be helpful.
4. **Data selection rationale:** The paper describes *that* data selection works, but less on *why* the selected sentences are better. Analyzing the characteristics of the selected sentences (e.g., n-gram overlap with the test set, presence of domain-specific terms) could provide further insights.
5. **Comparison to more recent baselines:** While comparisons to established methods like Duh et al. (2013) are included, comparing to more recent neural machine translation (NMT) adaptation techniques would provide a more comprehensive benchmark.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, specifically for the CNN architecture and training parameters?
2. What analyses did you perform to understand *why* the selected sentences are more relevant to the test domain? (e.g., do they contain specific keywords, have lower perplexity on an in-domain language model?)
3. Have you experimented with more complex CNN architectures (e.g., multiple convolutional layers, different pooling strategies)? If so, what were the results?
4. How do the results compare to recent NMT-based domain adaptation techniques?
