## Review for "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised approach leveraging Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, thereby enabling targeted data selection for SMT training. The method utilizes pre-trained word embeddings (word2vec) to augment the limited in-domain signal. Experiments across four language pairs and multiple test domains demonstrate significant BLEU score improvements compared to baseline data selection methods, particularly when in-domain data is scarce.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of CNNs to Data Selection for Low-Resource Domain Adaptation:** The paper demonstrates a practical and effective application of CNNs for a crucial problem in SMT – adapting to new domains with minimal resources.  While CNNs have been used for text classification, their targeted use for *data selection* in an SMT pipeline, particularly with limited in-domain data, is a valuable contribution.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The results consistently show improvements across all language pairs and domains, showcasing the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach. The particularly strong performance when using very small in-domain datasets (down to 100 sentences) is notable.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The paper provides a well-defined experimental setup, including comparisons to strong baseline methods (LM-based data selection) and clear reporting of results, making it easy to assess the effectiveness of the proposed method.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Problem:** Domain adaptation in MT with limited resources is a significant bottleneck for real-world deployment. This work directly tackles that issue.
2. **Novel Combination of Techniques:** The combination of semi-supervised learning with CNNs and pre-trained word embeddings provides a powerful framework for leveraging both labeled and unlabeled data for domain adaptation.  The use of word embeddings is especially clever given the data scarcity.
3. **Robustness and Generalizability:**  The consistent performance gains across diverse language pairs (zh-en, ar-en, en-es, en-zh) and different domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) demonstrate the effectiveness and generalizability of the proposed method.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized, making it easy to understand the proposed approach and the experimental results.  The ablation study (reducing the size of in-domain data) is particularly insightful.
5. **Strong Baseline Comparisons:** Comparing against established LM-based data selection methods provides a solid benchmark and clearly highlights the advantages of the CNN-based approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture:** The paper describes the CNN architecture used (one-layer, max pooling) but lacks a detailed justification for these choices.  It would be beneficial to explore the impact of different CNN architectures (e.g., multiple layers, different pooling methods) on performance.  A more thorough hyperparameter search would strengthen the results.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, a qualitative error analysis would provide valuable insights into the types of errors the system still makes and potential areas for improvement. Understanding *why* the model makes mistakes could guide future research.
3. **Dependence on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The method heavily relies on pre-trained word embeddings.  While this is understandable given the limited in-domain data, it raises a question about potential bias introduced by the pre-trained embeddings. Investigating the sensitivity of the method to different pre-trained embeddings would be valuable.
4. **Comparison to More Recent Adaptation Techniques:** The related work section primarily focuses on methods predating the surge in neural MT.  A comparison to more recent neural MT adaptation techniques (e.g., fine-tuning pre-trained translation models) would position this work more effectively within the current research landscape.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. **CNN Architecture Justification:** Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind the choice of a one-layer CNN with max pooling? Did you experiment with other architectures (e.g., deeper networks, average pooling) and if so, what were the results?
2. **Error Analysis:**  Can you provide some examples of sentences where the system performs poorly and analyze the reasons for the errors?  Are there specific types of sentences or domains where the method struggles?
3. **Sensitivity to Pre-trained Embeddings:** How sensitive is the performance of your method to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Did you explore using different embedding models or training embeddings on different corpora?
4. **Neural MT Baselines:** Have you considered comparing your method against more recent neural MT adaptation techniques like fine-tuning pre-trained models? If not, what were the reasons for excluding them?

**Additional Comments:**

* The use of bootstrap resampling for significance testing is appropriate and strengthens the validity of the results.
* The code and scripts used in the experiments would be valuable for reproducibility and further research.  Consider making them publicly available.
* The discussion of why the semi-supervised CNN performs well is insightful and adds to the overall understanding of the method.



Overall, this is a strong and valuable contribution to the field of machine translation. The proposed approach effectively addresses a challenging problem and demonstrates promising results. While some areas could benefit from further investigation, the paper is well-written, well-motivated, and provides a solid foundation for future research. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
