This paper tackles the challenging problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation when only a *very* limited amount of in-domain data is available – a scenario becoming increasingly common with the proliferation of niche online content. The core idea – leveraging semi-supervised convolutional neural networks to classify training data as in- or out-of-domain – is genuinely novel and represents a compelling departure from traditional data selection techniques reliant on larger in-domain corpora or complex language modeling. While the experimental setup isn’t groundbreaking, the consistent performance gains, *especially* with extremely small in-domain datasets (down to 100 sentences), are noteworthy.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Adaptation Approach:** The central contribution is the application of semi-supervised CNNs for domain classification in the context of SMT adaptation. This is a conceptually fresh approach that moves beyond reliance on extensive in-domain resources.
2. **Demonstrated Effectiveness with Limited Data:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that this method can significantly improve translation quality even when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available, outperforming established baselines in these low-resource scenarios.
3. **Practicality & Scalability:** The use of pre-trained word embeddings and CNNs lends itself to practical implementation and scaling to new domains, given the relative ease of training CNNs compared to more complex adaptation methods.

**Strengths:**

1. **Bold Idea & Clear Motivation:** The problem addressed is both important and increasingly relevant. The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing approaches when in-domain data is scarce, providing strong motivation for the proposed method. The idea of framing domain adaptation as a classification problem is clever.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported BLEU improvements, particularly on the Chinese-to-English SMS task and with reduced in-domain data sizes, are substantial and provide strong evidence of the method’s effectiveness. The statistical significance testing adds further credibility.
3. **Effective Use of Semi-Supervision:** Leveraging pre-trained word embeddings from large general-domain corpora is crucial for success, particularly with limited in-domain data. The semi-supervised approach effectively capitalizes on readily available unlabeled data.
4. **Thorough Baseline Comparison:** Comparing against multiple established data selection methods (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) provides a solid benchmark for evaluating the proposed approach.
5. **Detailed Experimental Setup:** The authors provide sufficient details about the data, experimental setup, and hyperparameter tuning, enabling reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture:** The paper largely treats the CNN as a "black box."  More analysis of *why* the CNN is successful in identifying in-domain data would be beneficial. Investigating which features/patterns the CNN learns to discriminate between domains would strengthen the work.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  While hyperparameters were tuned, the paper doesn't explicitly discuss the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings. A more thorough sensitivity analysis would build confidence in the robustness of the findings.
3. **Generalizability Beyond Test Domains:** The test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) are relatively informal and text-specific. It's unclear how well this approach would generalize to more complex domains (e.g., medical texts, legal documents) with drastically different linguistic characteristics.
4. **Lack of Ablation Study:** An ablation study to evaluate the contribution of different components (e.g., pre-trained embeddings vs. one-hot vectors) would provide a more nuanced understanding of the method’s strengths and weaknesses.
5. **Missing Error Analysis:** The paper lacks an error analysis of the SMT systems trained with data selected by the CNN. Identifying the types of errors that remain would offer valuable insights for future improvement.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more insight into the features learned by the CNN? Are certain words or n-grams consistently identified as indicative of in-domain data? Visualizing the learned filters could be insightful.
2.  How sensitive are the results to the size of the negative sample used during CNN training? Did you experiment with different ratios of positive to negative samples?
3.  Do you observe any domain-specific patterns in the CNN’s performance? Does it perform better on certain types of domains (e.g., those with distinctive vocabulary) compared to others?
4.  Have you explored other CNN architectures or pooling strategies? Could more sophisticated CNN designs further improve performance?



**Additional Comments:** The paper is well-written and clearly presented. While some aspects of the methodology could be more rigorously analyzed, the novelty of the approach and the demonstrated performance gains with extremely limited data are compelling. I would lean towards acceptance.
