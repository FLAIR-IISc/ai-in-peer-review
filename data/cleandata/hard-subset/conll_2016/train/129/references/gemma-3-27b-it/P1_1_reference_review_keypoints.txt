- Summary: Proposes a semi-supervised CNN using pre-trained word embeddings to select general-domain SMT training sentences for adaptation when only tiny in-domain data exist, showing improvements across four language pairs and informal domains.

- Contribution 1: Empirically shows CNNs with pre-trained embeddings can effectively select relevant training data for MT adaptation even with as few as 100 in-domain sentence pairs.

- Contribution 2: Presents strong experimental results with consistent BLEU improvements over solid baselines (including RNNLM-based selection) across multiple language pairs and domains.

- Contribution 3: Provides analysis explaining why convolutional features and pre-trained embeddings help capture domain-specific signals and handle OOVs.

- Strength 1: Addresses a highly relevant and practical problem for low-resource domain MT adaptation.

- Strength 2: Uses a solid experimental design with clear baselines, multiple language pairs, domains, and statistical significance testing; includes valuable ablation on in-domain data size.

- Strength 3: Paper is clear and well-written, with methodology and results presented concisely.

- Strength 4: Novelty lies in combining CNNs and semi-supervised data selection targeted at low-resource adaptation.

- Strength 5: Shows practical impact, e.g., a 3.1 BLEU gain on Chinese→English SMS using only 3% of training data.

- Weakness 1: Lacks in-depth error analysis to explain failure modes and types of misclassified sentence pairs.

- Weakness 2: Insufficient detail on hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity (window sizes, embedding dims, pooling), and whether settings vary by language pair.

- Weakness 3: CNN architecture is described briefly; more specifics (layers, filter sizes, activations) and a diagram are needed for reproducibility.

- Weakness 4: Relies on word2vec without exploring alternative embeddings or the effect of embedding quality and fine-tuning.

- Question 1: Request for qualitative error analysis of commonly misclassified sentence pairs and distinguishing characteristics.

- Question 2: Ask for details on hyperparameter search strategy, sensitivity, and per-language adjustments.

- Question 3: Inquire whether other embeddings (GloVe, fastText) were tried and how they compare.

- Question 4: Ask for justification of specific CNN architecture choices and whether they are empirically or literature-driven.

- Question 5: Would this approach generalize to NMT, and if so, how might it be adapted?

- Additional comment 1: Suggest briefly summarizing key differences from prior work (e.g., Duh et al., 2013) in the introduction.

- Additional comment 2: Recommend releasing code to improve reproducibility.

- Overall recommendation: Strong paper with minor revision suggested; recommend acceptance after addressing analysis, architecture, and reproducibility details.
