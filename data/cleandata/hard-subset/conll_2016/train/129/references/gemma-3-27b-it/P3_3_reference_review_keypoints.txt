- Summary: Proposes a semi-supervised CNN to classify sentence pairs as in/out-of-domain for SMT data selection, showing strong gains with very limited in-domain data.

- Contribution 1: Demonstrates CNNs with pre-trained embeddings effectively identify in-domain data in low-resource domain adaptation.

- Contribution 2: Provides strong empirical validation across four language directions and multiple informal domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook).

- Contribution 3: Offers an ablation study quantifying performance as in-domain training size decreases, showing robustness with tiny labeled sets.

- Strength 1: Tackles a practical, important problem in MT—domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data.

- Strength 2: Solid experimental setup using established SMT frameworks, BLEU, and competitive baselines for fair comparison.

- Strength 3: Clear, well-structured presentation and sound positioning within related work.

- Strength 4: Substantial BLEU improvements (up to 3.1), including gains with only 100 labeled in-domain sentences.

- Strength 5: Insightful discussion explaining why CNNs plus embeddings capture domain-specific features and future directions.

- Weakness 1: CNN architecture is standard (one-layer + max-pooling); more advanced architectures are not explored.

- Weakness 2: Insufficient detail on hyperparameter tuning (search space, methodology, evaluation criteria).

- Weakness 3: Heavy dependence on pre-trained embeddings without sensitivity analysis or exploration of alternatives/fine-tuning.

- Weakness 4: Missing qualitative error analysis to show failure cases and guide improvements.

- Question 1: Please detail the hyperparameter tuning process, search space, optimization strategy, and evaluation metric.

- Question 2: How sensitive are results to the choice of pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe, FastText)? Any experiments comparing them?

- Question 3: Did you try fine-tuning embeddings during CNN training, and if not, why?

- Question 4: Can you provide example sentences that were classified correctly but still translated poorly, and vice versa?

- Question 5: For the 100-sentence scenario, how were sentences selected, and how much did selection variability affect performance?

- Additional Comment 1: Add a table summarizing key hyperparameter settings for reproducibility.

- Additional Comment 2: Compare computational cost of the proposed method versus baselines.

- Overall Assessment: Strong, practical contribution with compelling results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing noted weaknesses.
