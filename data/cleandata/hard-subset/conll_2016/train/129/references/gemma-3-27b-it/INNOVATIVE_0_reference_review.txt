This paper tackles the challenging problem of domain adaptation for SMT when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The core idea – leveraging semi-supervised CNNs for data selection – is genuinely novel and potentially impactful. While the methodology isn't groundbreaking in its individual components (CNNs, word embeddings, data selection), the *combination* and application to this specific, difficult problem is what stands out. The experimental results, particularly the significant BLEU improvements with extremely limited in-domain data (down to 100 sentences), are compelling.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Novel Application of CNNs to Data Selection for Low-Resource Domain Adaptation:** The core contribution is framing domain adaptation as a classification problem solvable with CNNs, and specifically employing a semi-supervised approach to mitigate the lack of in-domain training data. This is a clever and promising approach.
2. **Demonstration of Effectiveness with Minimal In-Domain Data:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that their method can achieve substantial improvements even when only a handful (e.g., 100) of in-domain sentences are available. This addresses a critical gap in existing domain adaptation techniques.
3. **Empirical Validation across Multiple Language Pairs and Domains:** Experiments across four language directions and diverse test domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts) lend credibility to the generalizability of the proposed approach.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Potential Impact:** The core idea is fresh and directly addresses a significant practical problem in MT – adapting to new domains with limited resources. This isn’t an incremental improvement; it’s a different way of thinking about domain adaptation.
2. **Strong Experimental Results:** The BLEU scores reported are impressive, particularly the gains achieved with minimal in-domain data. The 3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task with only 3% of the training data is noteworthy.
3. **Robustness Demonstrated Through Multiple Experiments:** The experiments aren’t limited to a single scenario. Testing across different language pairs and domains strengthens the argument that the method is generally effective.
4. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is relatively easy to follow, with a clear introduction, well-defined problem statement, and logical flow of ideas.
5. **Comparison to Strong Baselines:** Comparing against established data selection methods (ngram, RNNLM) provides a solid benchmark for evaluating the proposed approach.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture Choices:** While the paper describes the CNN architecture, it lacks a thorough analysis of *why* specific choices (e.g., window size, stride, number of neurons) were made, and how they impact performance. A more detailed ablation study would be beneficial.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper focuses on BLEU scores but doesn’t provide much insight into *what types* of errors the system still makes after adaptation. Error analysis could reveal areas for further improvement.
3. **Word Embedding Dependence:** The performance relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. While this is common, the paper doesn’t explore the sensitivity of the results to different embedding models or training data.
4. **Supervised CNN Training Detail**: The details of the CNN training itself are a bit sparse. Things like learning rate, regularization, and optimization algorithm are not detailed.
5. **Reproducibility**: While the toolkit and baselines are referenced, a complete recipe for reproducibility isn't explicitly provided.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed ablation study of the CNN architecture choices, specifically investigating the impact of window size, stride, and number of neurons on performance?
2.  Have you conducted any error analysis to understand the types of errors the adapted system still makes?
3.  How sensitive are the results to different pre-trained word embedding models or training data? Have you experimented with embeddings trained on domain-specific data (even if small)?
4. Could you elaborate on the CNN training process? What learning rate, regularization techniques, and optimization algorithm were used?
5. Can you provide more detail on the specific validation set used for hyperparameter tuning, and how you determined the optimal number of sentences to select?



**Additional Comments:**

The paper's strengths – novelty, strong results, and clear presentation – outweigh its weaknesses. I believe this work represents a significant step forward in low-resource domain adaptation for SMT and is worthy of acceptance, even with the minor methodological limitations. The boldness of the approach and the promising results are particularly encouraging.
