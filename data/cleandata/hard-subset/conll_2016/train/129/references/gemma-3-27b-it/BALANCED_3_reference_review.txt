This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, and then use this classifier to select a subset of the general-domain training data for SMT training. The paper is well-motivated, tackling a practical and important issue in MT.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Novel Application of Semi-Supervised CNNs for Data Selection:** The primary contribution is the application of semi-supervised CNNs for domain adaptation in SMT, specifically targeting the low-resource in-domain data scenario. While CNNs have been used for text classification, their application to intelligently *select* data for SMT training, particularly with limited in-domain data, is novel. The use of pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the lack of in-domain data is a smart approach.
2. **Empirical Validation on Multiple Language Pairs & Domains:** The authors evaluate their approach on four different language directions (zh-en, ar-en, en-es, en-zh) and three diverse test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts). This broad experimental setup provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the proposed method.
3. **Demonstration of Effectiveness with Extremely Limited In-Domain Data:** The results clearly demonstrate significant improvements even when only 100 in-domain sentence pairs are used to train the CNN classifier. This is particularly compelling given the common difficulty of obtaining substantial amounts of in-domain data.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Experimental Results:** The reported BLEU score improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU on zh-en sms) are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The consistent improvements across multiple language pairs and domains further strengthen the validity of the findings.
2. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the motivation, approach, and experimental setup. The related work section provides a good overview of existing domain adaptation techniques.
3. **Thorough Comparison to Baselines:** The authors compare their approach against strong baselines, including LM-based data selection methods. This allows for a fair assessment of the benefits of the proposed CNN-based approach.
4. **Detailed Ablation Study:** The experiments investigating the impact of the amount of in-domain data used to train the CNN classifier (Figure 1) provides valuable insights into the behavior of the proposed method. 
5. **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed and the proposed solution have high practical relevance in real-world MT applications where in-domain data is often scarce.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of Error Cases:** While the paper presents compelling overall results, a more in-depth analysis of the types of errors made by the adapted SMT systems would be valuable. Understanding *why* the method works well (or fails) in certain cases could provide insights for further improvement. Are there specific types of sentences or linguistic phenomena where the method struggles?
2. **CNN Architecture Details:** The CNN architecture used (number of layers, filter sizes, pooling strategy) is relatively basic and could potentially be further optimized. A more detailed justification for the chosen architecture would be helpful. Exploring different CNN architectures or hyperparameter tuning strategies could lead to even better performance.
3. **Reliance on Pre-trained Word Embeddings:** While using pre-trained word embeddings is a reasonable strategy given the limited in-domain data, it introduces a dependency on the quality and domain coverage of those embeddings. A discussion of how the choice of pre-trained embeddings might affect the results would be beneficial. Could fine-tuning the word embeddings on a larger general-domain corpus improve performance?
4. **Lack of Statistical Significance Reporting for All Results:** While some results are marked as statistically significant, not all comparisons have corresponding significance tests reported. Providing p-values for all reported results would enhance the rigor of the evaluation.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying specific types of errors made by the adapted SMT systems?
2.  What impact did different choices of pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., different training corpora, embedding dimensionality) have on the results?
3.  Did you experiment with different CNN architectures or hyperparameter tuning strategies? If so, could you share the results?
4.  Could you provide p-values for all the results reported in Table 2?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The phrase "tiny amount of in-domain data" is used frequently. Quantifying this as a specific number of sentence pairs (e.g., less than 500) would make the definition more precise.
*   The reference to "gigaword" 5-gram language model in section 5.2 seems slightly out of place; it doesn't contribute directly to the presented method.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a novel and effective approach to domain adaptation for SMT in the low-resource setting. The experimental results are compelling, and the paper is well-written and clearly presented. Despite a few minor weaknesses, the contribution is significant, and I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions addressing the questions and suggestions outlined above.
