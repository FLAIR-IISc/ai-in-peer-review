## Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

**Summary:** This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only very limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) approach to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, then use this classifier to select a subset of general-domain data for training the SMT system. They demonstrate improvements over baseline data selection methods (LM-based) across four language pairs and multiple test domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of CNN Effectiveness for Low-Resource Domain Adaptation:** The paper convincingly demonstrates that CNNs, particularly in a semi-supervised setting, can effectively select relevant training data for SMT adaptation even with extremely limited in-domain resources (down to 100 sentences). This is a practical and valuable finding given the scarcity of in-domain data in many real-world scenarios.
2. **Strong Experimental Evaluation:** The experiments are comprehensive, covering four language pairs and diverse test domains. The comparison to several strong baseline data selection methods (including RNNLM-based approaches) lends credibility to the claims.
3. **Practical Approach & Insightful Analysis:** The methodology is reasonably straightforward to implement and the discussion around the benefits of leveraging pre-trained word embeddings and the ability to capture domain-specific features through convolutional layers provides useful insight.



**Strengths:**

1. **Relevance to a Significant Problem:** Domain adaptation is crucial for real-world MT deployments. The scenario of extremely limited in-domain data is particularly common and under-addressed, making this work highly relevant.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent BLEU score improvements across all experimental settings, particularly the substantial gain on the Chinese-to-English SMS task and the robustness even with only 100 in-domain sentences, are compelling. The statistical significance testing further strengthens these results.
3. **Well-Established Baseline Comparison:** Comparing against established LM-based data selection methods provides a clear benchmark and demonstrates the advantages of the proposed CNN approach. The thoroughness of the baselines is commendable.
4. **Sound Methodology:**  The use of a semi-supervised CNN with pre-trained word embeddings is a sensible approach, leveraging the strengths of both supervised learning with limited data and the general semantic knowledge captured in word embeddings.
5. **Clear Presentation and Organization:** The paper is well-written, logically structured, and easy to follow. The experimental setup and results are clearly presented.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in the CNN Architecture:** While the application to data selection is valuable, the CNN architecture itself (standard convolutional and pooling layers) is not novel. The paper doesn't introduce any architectural innovations. The core contribution lies in *how* CNNs are used for this specific task rather than *what* CNN is used.
2. **Lack of Ablation Studies:**  The paper would be strengthened by ablation studies investigating the contribution of different components.  For example, what is the performance gain achieved by using pre-trained word embeddings compared to training them from scratch (even with limited in-domain data)? How sensitive is the performance to the size of the CNN (number of layers, filters)?  What impact does the window size in the convolutional layers have?
3. **Data Selection Criteria Explanation:** The paper states the selection is based on the *sum* of source and target scores. While the experimental results justify this choice, a more detailed explanation of *why* this combination works better than using individual scores would be beneficial. Is it simply balancing coverage or are there more nuanced reasons?
4. **Over-Reliance on BLEU:** While BLEU is a standard metric, it has limitations. Including human evaluation or other metrics (e.g., TER, chrF) could provide a more comprehensive assessment of translation quality.
5. **Limited Error Analysis:**  The paper doesn't offer any detailed error analysis. Understanding *what types* of errors the selected data helps to correct (and what types it doesn’t) would provide valuable insight into the strengths and limitations of the approach.




**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why summing the source and target CNN scores yields the best performance for data selection? Is there a theoretical justification or a more nuanced analysis beyond the empirical observation?
2.  What is the computational cost of training and applying the CNN classifier compared to the LM-based data selection methods? (e.g., training time, inference time). Is there a significant trade-off between the improvement in BLEU and the computational cost?
3. You mention the presence of a 120K in-domain subset within the general domain data for the zh2en SMS task. Was this known *a priori* or discovered during the experiments?  If known, how does this influence the interpretation of the results?
4.  Regarding the limited in-domain data, how were those sentences chosen?  Was it random sampling, or a more deliberate selection based on perceived representativeness of the target domain?




**Additional Comments:**

* The referencing is generally good, but some citations (e.g., "Axelrod et al.,?") appear incomplete.
* The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of the limitations of the approach and potential directions for future research.




**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and valuable contribution to the field of machine translation domain adaptation. While the CNN architecture itself is not novel, the application to low-resource data selection is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and demonstrates significant practical benefits. The weaknesses are relatively minor and could be addressed with further experimentation and analysis.  I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions.
