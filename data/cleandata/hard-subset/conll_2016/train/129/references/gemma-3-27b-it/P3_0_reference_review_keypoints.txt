- Problem: Domain adaptation for SMT with very limited in-domain data, using semi-supervised methods.  
- Approach: Use CNNs to classify sentence pairs as in-domain vs out-of-domain for targeted data selection, augmented with pre-trained word2vec embeddings.  
- Results: Significant BLEU improvements across four language pairs and multiple domains, especially with tiny in-domain sets (down to 100 sentences).  
- Contribution 1: Practical application of CNNs for data selection in low-resource domain adaptation.  
- Contribution 2: Strong empirical performance and robustness across languages and domains.  
- Contribution 3: Comprehensive experimental setup with comparisons to strong LM-based baselines.  
- Strength 1: Addresses a critical real-world problem in MT deployment.  
- Strength 2: Novel combination of semi-supervised learning, CNNs, and pre-trained embeddings.  
- Strength 3: Demonstrates robustness/generalizability across diverse language pairs and domains.  
- Strength 4: Clear, well-structured presentation and informative ablation study.  
- Strength 5: Strong baseline comparisons that highlight advantages of the method.  
- Weakness 1: Limited analysis and justification of the chosen CNN architecture and hyperparameters.  
- Weakness 2: No qualitative error analysis to explain remaining model errors.  
- Weakness 3: Heavy reliance on pre-trained embeddings raises concerns about bias and sensitivity.  
- Weakness 4: Lacks comparisons to more recent neural MT adaptation techniques (e.g., fine-tuning).  
- Question 1: Why choose a one-layer CNN with max pooling, and were other architectures tested?  
- Question 2: Can the authors provide examples and analysis of sentences where the system fails?  
- Question 3: How sensitive are results to different pre-trained embedding models or corpora?  
- Question 4: Have the authors compared to neural MT adaptation methods like fine-tuning pretrained translation models?  
- Additional: Bootstrap resampling for significance testing is appropriate and recommended to release code for reproducibility.  
- Overall: Strong contribution; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses.
