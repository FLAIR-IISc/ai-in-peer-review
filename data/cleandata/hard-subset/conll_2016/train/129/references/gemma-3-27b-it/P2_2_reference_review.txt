## Review for "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised approach using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to classify sentences as in-domain or out-of-domain, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the lack of in-domain training data. They then use this classifier to select a subset of the general-domain training data for SMT training. Experiments on four language pairs and three test domains demonstrate improvements over strong baselines, particularly when the in-domain data is extremely scarce.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Practical Approach to Low-Resource Domain Adaptation:**  The paper presents a relatively simple and effective method for domain adaptation that performs well in a challenging setting - extremely limited in-domain data.  This is a significant practical contribution, as obtaining large amounts of in-domain data is often a major bottleneck.
2. **Demonstration of CNN Effectiveness for Data Selection:** The work demonstrates that CNNs, combined with pre-trained word embeddings, can effectively differentiate between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences, enabling data selection for SMT.
3. **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The experiments cover four language pairs and three diverse test domains, offering solid evidence for the proposed approach's robustness and generalizability.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results consistently show improvements over strong baselines, especially in low-resource scenarios. The gains of over 3 BLEU on Chinese-to-English SMS is compelling. The ablation study showing improvement even with just 100 in-domain sentences is particularly strong.
2. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of domain adaptation with limited in-domain data and provides a strong motivation for exploring new approaches. The rise of social media data with new domains lacking labeled resources further strengthens the relevance.
3. **Well-Established Methodology:** The use of CNNs and pre-trained word embeddings are well-grounded in existing literature. The integration of these techniques into a semi-supervised framework for data selection is logical and well-explained.
4. **Thorough Experiment Setup:**  The experiments are well-designed, covering multiple language pairs and diverse test domains, providing strong support for the claimed benefits. The inclusion of multiple baselines including strong LM-based data selection techniques is good practice.
5. **Detailed Analysis and Discussion:** The paper offers a reasonable explanation for why the proposed approach works, focusing on the ability of CNNs to capture domain-specific features and the benefits of pre-trained word embeddings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of Individual Components:** While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, neither CNNs nor pre-trained word embeddings are novel in themselves. The core novelty lies in applying them specifically to this data selection problem for low-resource SMT, which is less groundbreaking than a completely new architectural component.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters, but doesn’t provide enough detail regarding the search space and the criteria used.  The optimal settings for the CNN (window size, stride, number of neurons) could be crucial and may not generalize across all domains.
3. **Black Box Nature of CNN:** While the method works well, understanding *why* the CNN selects certain sentences over others remains a challenge. Analyzing the learned convolutional filters or attention mechanisms could provide valuable insights into the model's behavior and could potentially lead to further improvements. This would make the approach less of a “black box”.
4. **Focus Solely on Data Selection:** The paper primarily focuses on the data selection stage.  It would be beneficial to investigate whether further adaptation of the SMT model itself (e.g., fine-tuning the parameters on the selected data) could yield additional improvements.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details regarding the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, what search space was used for the CNN parameters (window size, stride, number of neurons) and what metric was used to evaluate different configurations?
2.  Have you explored any techniques to interpret the decisions made by the CNN classifier? For example, could you visualize the learned convolutional filters or identify which words/phrases are most influential in the classification process?
3.   Did you experiment with any further adaptation of the SMT system *after* data selection, such as fine-tuning the translation model on the selected in-domain data? If so, what were the results?
4.  The paper highlights the effectiveness with limited in-domain data.  How does the performance of your method compare to the baselines when *more* in-domain data is available (e.g., 30K or 100K sentences)?  Does the advantage of your approach diminish with increasing amounts of in-domain data?



**Additional Comments:**

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive. The writing is generally clear and concise. The presentation of the experimental results is well-organized and easy to understand.  The reference to the code and scripts for the baseline systems is appreciated.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a solid and well-executed paper that addresses an important problem in SMT. While the individual components are not groundbreaking, the combination of techniques and the strong experimental validation make a compelling case for the proposed approach. I recommend acceptance, with minor revisions to address the weaknesses and answer the questions raised above.
