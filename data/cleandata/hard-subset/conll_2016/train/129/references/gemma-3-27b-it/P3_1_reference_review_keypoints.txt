- Paper tackles SMT domain adaptation with very limited in-domain data using a semi-supervised CNN to select in-domain-like sentence pairs from general data.  
- Key idea: leverage pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for scarce labeled in-domain examples.  
- Empirical finding: CNNs with pre-trained embeddings effectively identify relevant in-domain data down to 100 labeled sentences.  
- Strong baseline comparisons show the method outperforms n-gram and RNNLM-based data selection techniques.  
- Practical focus: solution is immediately applicable to low-resource and emerging-domain SMT scenarios.  
- Significant BLEU improvements reported across four language pairs, notably >3 BLEU on Chinese→English SMS.  
- Experiments are thorough, covering diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook) and large general-domain corpora.  
- Ablation study on in-domain data size demonstrates robustness even with very small labeled sets.  
- Presentation is clear and method description is straightforward.  
- Uses established techniques appropriately (word2vec embeddings, phrase-based SMT) as a solid foundation.  
- Weakness: CNN architecture is fairly standard and lacks exploration of more advanced architectures or attention.  
- Weakness: limited hyperparameter/architecture variation and tuning details reported.  
- Weakness: no detailed error analysis explaining which sentences are selected or misclassified.  
- Weakness: heavy reliance on pre-trained embeddings without sensitivity analysis to embedding choice or corpus.  
- Weakness: computational cost and scaling of CNN training/inference on very large datasets are not discussed.  
- Questions: please detail hyperparameter tuning ranges (filter sizes, number of filters, learning rates).  
- Questions: provide error analysis and examples of misclassified sentences with explanations.  
- Questions: how sensitive are results to embedding choice (e.g., GloVe, fastText) or embeddings trained on in-domain/general corpora?  
- Questions: report computational cost (training/inference time) and how it scales with general-domain data size.  
- Questions: why use max-pooling specifically, and were other pooling strategies (average, k-max) tried?  
- Additional suggestions: add discussion of broader transfer learning/domain adaptation literature and clarify the meaning of "domain" (topic/genre/style).  
- Overall assessment: recommend acceptance — solid, practical contribution with strong results, though could be strengthened by error analysis and computational discussion.
