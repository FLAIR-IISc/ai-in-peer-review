This paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The core idea is to train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentence pairs, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the limited in-domain training data. The trained CNN is then used to select relevant sentence pairs from the general-domain training data for SMT training.

The primary contribution of this work lies in the empirical demonstration that a relatively simple semi-supervised CNN approach can effectively perform data selection for SMT adaptation, even with extremely limited in-domain resources (down to 100 sentence pairs). While the individual components (CNNs, word embeddings, data selection) are not novel, the combination and successful application to this specific low-resource domain adaptation scenario is a worthwhile contribution. A secondary, though significant, contribution is the thorough experimental evaluation across four language pairs and diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), providing compelling evidence for the method’s effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent and statistically significant improvements over strong baselines (including LM-based data selection methods) across all four language pairs. The gains, particularly in the low-resource scenarios (e.g., 3.1 BLEU improvement on Chinese-to-English SMS with only 3% of the training data), are substantial and practically relevant.
2. **Comprehensive Experimental Setup:** The evaluation framework is well-designed, comparing against multiple established baselines and varying the amount of in-domain data used for training the CNN classifier. The use of bootstrap resampling for statistical significance testing is commendable.
3. **Practical Relevance:** The problem addressed – domain adaptation with limited in-domain data – is a highly practical challenge in machine translation, especially in the context of emerging domains and social media translation. The proposed solution offers a potential pathway to building MT systems in such settings.
4. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental setup is described in sufficient detail to allow for reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Detailed Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares the proposed “sscnn” method against variations (one-hot CNN, LM-based selection), it lacks detailed ablation studies to dissect the contribution of individual components. For example, the impact of different pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., GloVe vs. word2vec) or different CNN architectures is not investigated. A more thorough ablation study would provide insights into the key factors driving the performance gains.
2. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis to understand *why* the proposed method succeeds or fails in specific cases. Examining misclassified sentence pairs could reveal valuable information about the limitations of the CNN classifier and potential areas for improvement. Knowing what types of sentences the model struggles with would be helpful.
3. **Minor Formatting and Presentation Issues:** There are numerous minor inconsistencies in formatting and presentation. For example, citations are not always consistently formatted, and the table captions are sometimes incomplete. While not critical, these inconsistencies detract from the overall polish of the paper. Specifically, the references section needs a very thorough clean-up - inconsistent capitalization, missing information (e.g. journal/conference details), and outright errors (e.g. \Q2011\E) are present.
4. **Superficial Discussion of Related Work:** The related work section, while covering relevant areas, feels somewhat superficial. More in-depth discussion of recent advances in domain adaptation and neural network-based data selection would strengthen the paper. The discussion of the limitations of prior work is also somewhat brief.
5. **Missing Hyperparameter Details:** The paper mentions tuning meta-parameters but lacks a detailed description of the specific values used for the CNN architecture (e.g., number of filters, filter sizes) and training process (e.g., learning rate, batch size). Reporting these details is crucial for reproducibility.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors made by the CNN classifier? Specifically, what characteristics of the misclassified sentence pairs contribute to the errors?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the proposed method to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., word2vec vs. GloVe)?
3. Could you provide a table listing the specific hyperparameter values used for training the CNN models (e.g., learning rate, batch size, number of filters, filter sizes)?
4. The paper states that there is "no domain knowledge about the training data." Was any initial data filtering or cleaning performed on the general domain training data (e.g. removal of HTML tags, normalization of text)?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The sentence "What’s more, if one wishes to build a large scale topic-specific MT system with hundreds of topics, it is prohibitively expensive to collect tens of thousands of in-domain sentences for each topic." feels slightly conversational and could be rephrased for a more formal tone.
*   The reference to “conText4” is not fully informative. Please provide a complete citation.
*   Please carefully review and correct all formatting inconsistencies in the references section. The quality of the reference list significantly impacts the credibility of the work.



Despite the weaknesses outlined above, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation for machine translation. The strong empirical results and practical relevance of the proposed method outweigh the minor flaws. However, addressing the weaknesses, particularly the lack of detailed ablation studies and error analysis, would significantly strengthen the paper. **I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.**
