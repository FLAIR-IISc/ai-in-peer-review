This paper tackles the challenging problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the lack of in-domain training data. The selected in-domain data is then used to train an SMT system.

The primary contribution of this work is the application of a semi-supervised CNN for data selection in a low-resource domain adaptation setting. While CNNs have been used for text classification, their application to *select* training data for SMT, particularly with minimal in-domain supervision, is a novel idea. The experimental results demonstrating BLEU improvements – particularly the substantial gains on the Chinese-to-English SMS task even with only 100 labeled examples – are compelling. 

A secondary contribution is the empirical demonstration that pre-trained word embeddings are critical in this low-resource setting. The “semi-supervised” aspect, utilizing general-domain embeddings, clearly outperforms the purely supervised “one-hot CNN” approach. This highlights the importance of transfer learning in these scenarios.

Finally, the thorough experimental evaluation across four language pairs and diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) strengthens the validity of the findings. The comparisons against strong baselines, including LM-based data selection, provide a good benchmark.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty & Impact:** The core idea of using CNNs for data selection in a low-resource MT adaptation scenario is genuinely novel. Existing approaches heavily rely on larger amounts of in-domain data for language model building, which this work circumvents. The potentially significant BLEU improvements, especially with minimal data, offer a real impact for practical applications.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported gains, especially the 3.1 BLEU improvement on Chinese-to-English SMS with a tiny in-domain dataset, are impressive. The consistent improvements across diverse domains indicate the robustness of the approach.
3. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of domain adaptation with limited resources, which is increasingly relevant in the age of social media and emerging domains. The motivation is well-established and relatable.
4. **Comprehensive Evaluation:**  Testing on four different language directions and different test domains (social media) demonstrates the generalizability of the proposed method. Comparison with several strong baselines further validates the results.
5. **Practicality:** The method appears relatively straightforward to implement and doesn’t require complex feature engineering, making it practically appealing.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the optimal CNN architecture. While the authors mention tuning hyperparameters, the rationale behind the choices (window size, stride, number of neurons) isn't fully explained. Further ablation studies could reveal which aspects of the CNN are most critical.
2. **Data Selection Strategy Details:** The paper states selecting based on the "sum of source and target scores", but doesn’t provide a strong justification for this choice, nor does it explore other combination strategies. The impact of different aggregation methods could be explored.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper doesn’t delve into *why* the CNN is successfully identifying in-domain data. Error analysis – examining misclassified examples – could offer valuable insights and guide future improvements. For example, is the CNN picking up on specific keywords or stylistic features?
4. **Dependence on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The method relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. While necessary given the limited in-domain data, this dependence isn’t fully discussed. A sensitivity analysis on different embedding models could be beneficial.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing the sum of source and target scores for data selection, and have you experimented with alternative aggregation strategies (e.g., maximum, average)?
2. Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the CNN architecture choices, including the justification for the chosen window size, stride, and number of neurons? Were any attempts made to optimize these parameters using a more systematic approach (e.g., grid search)?
3. Can you present some examples of sentences correctly and incorrectly classified by the CNN, and discuss what features seem to be driving the classification decisions? (Error Analysis).
4. Have you investigated the sensitivity of the method to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., comparing word2vec with GloVe)?



**Additional Comments:**

The related work section is comprehensive. The writing is generally clear and concise. The paper feels slightly rushed in its presentation – a bit more detail in the architecture and experimentation sections would be welcome. Overall, despite these minor weaknesses, this paper presents a significant and novel contribution to the field of domain adaptation for machine translation. I recommend acceptance.
