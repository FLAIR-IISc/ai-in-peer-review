## Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

**Summary:** This paper tackles the challenging problem of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, and then use the learned classifier to select a subset of general-domain data for SMT training.  The key idea is leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the limited in-domain data. Experiments on four language pairs demonstrate that the proposed method consistently outperforms strong baselines, including LM-based data selection techniques, and achieves significant BLEU score improvements, particularly in low-resource scenarios.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of CNN Effectiveness for Low-Resource Domain Adaptation:** The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that CNNs, particularly when incorporating pre-trained embeddings, are effective for identifying relevant in-domain data even with extremely limited labelled in-domain examples (down to 100 sentences). This is a valuable finding for practical SMT deployment in emerging domains.
2. **Strong Baseline Comparison:** The comprehensive comparison against several strong and established data selection methods (ngram, RNNLM, combined LM) establishes the proposed method's superiority and provides a solid benchmark for future research.
3. **Practical Focus:** The paper addresses a genuinely practical problem in SMT - the lack of in-domain data - and offers a solution that is potentially readily applicable to a wide range of translation tasks.

**Strengths:**

1. **Significant Performance Gains:** The reported BLEU improvements, particularly the >3 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task, are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The consistent improvements across multiple language pairs strengthens the generalizability of the findings.
2. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-designed, utilizing a diverse set of test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) and a large amount of general-domain data. The ablation study exploring the impact of in-domain data size is particularly insightful.
3. **Clear and Concise Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for the work is clearly articulated, and the method is described in a straightforward manner.
4. **Appropriate Use of Established Techniques:** The integration of pre-trained word embeddings (word2vec) and the use of a standard phrase-based SMT system provide a solid foundation for the proposed method.
5. **Robustness to Small Data:** The results showing stable performance even with only 100 labeled in-domain sentences are particularly compelling and highlight the method's practical value.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the CNN Architecture:** While the *application* to data selection for SMT is novel, the CNN architecture itself is fairly standard. The paper doesn't explore more sophisticated CNN architectures or attention mechanisms which could potentially further improve performance. The choice of hyperparameters is briefly mentioned, but a more thorough exploration of architecture variations is lacking.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks detailed error analysis. Understanding *why* the CNN selects certain sentences and fails to select others would provide valuable insights into the method's strengths and weaknesses. Identifying common error patterns could inform future improvements.
3. **Dependence on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The method relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. While word2vec is a good choice, the performance might be sensitive to the specific corpus used for pre-training. The paper doesn't explicitly address this dependency or explore alternative embedding methods.
4. **Scaling Concerns:** The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of training and applying the CNN classifier, particularly for very large datasets. While the data selection step reduces the amount of data fed into the SMT system, the CNN training itself could be computationally expensive.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed discussion of the hyperparameter tuning process for the CNN, including the range of values explored for different parameters (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters, learning rate)?
2.  Did you perform any error analysis to understand the types of sentences the CNN correctly classifies and the types it misclassifies? Could you share some examples of misclassified sentences and discuss the potential reasons for the errors?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embedding models (e.g., GloVe, fastText) or train embeddings specifically on your general-domain data?
4.  What is the computational cost (training and inference time) of the CNN classifier, and how does it scale with the size of the general-domain data?
5.  Could you elaborate on the choice of using max-pooling specifically? Have you experimented with average pooling or other pooling strategies, and if so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper's contribution is solid and the experimental results are convincing.
*   Consider including a discussion of related work on transfer learning and domain adaptation in the broader NLP field.
*   The use of the term "domain" is a bit vague. Clarifying the specific characteristics of the test domains (e.g., topic, genre, style) would be helpful.



**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. While the CNN architecture itself isn’t groundbreaking, the application to low-resource domain adaptation in SMT is novel and the experimental results are strong.  The paper addresses a practical problem with a well-motivated and effective solution. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing more detailed error analysis and discussing computational cost, would further strengthen the paper.
