This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose leveraging semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, and then selecting high-scoring sentences to train the SMT system. The core idea of utilizing CNNs for data selection with limited in-domain data is a significant contribution. Additionally, the experimental evaluation across four language pairs and several test domains demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed approach, particularly in low-resource scenarios, and provides a strong empirical validation. A further contribution is the thorough experimental analysis showing that the semi-supervised CNN consistently outperforms both baseline LM-based approaches and the supervised CNN, especially when very little in-domain data is available for training the classifier.

**Strengths:**

1. **Novelty and Relevance:** The problem addressed – domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data – is highly relevant and practically important. Existing techniques often require substantial in-domain resources. The proposed approach offers a promising solution for scenarios where such resources are scarce.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results across four language pairs and diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) are compelling. The consistent improvements over strong baselines, particularly with only 100 in-domain sentences, demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The 3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task is particularly notable.
3. **Thorough Experimental Design:** The paper includes a detailed experimental setup, clear descriptions of the baselines, and a comprehensive ablation study exploring the impact of the amount of in-domain data used for training the CNN classifier. The bootstrap resampling test for significance is appropriate.
4. **Effective Use of Semi-Supervision:** The incorporation of pre-trained word embeddings (word2vec) is well-justified and demonstrably improves performance. This highlights the benefit of leveraging information from large unlabeled corpora to compensate for the limited in-domain data.
5. **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is clearly explained, and the experimental results are presented in a organized manner.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture:** While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of CNNs, it lacks a detailed exploration of the CNN architecture itself.  A more in-depth analysis of the impact of different hyperparameters (e.g., kernel sizes, number of filters, pooling methods) would be beneficial.  Why were the chosen values optimal?
2. **Lack of Qualitative Error Analysis:** The paper focuses primarily on quantitative results (BLEU scores).  A qualitative error analysis, examining specific examples where the proposed method succeeds or fails, would provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach. What types of errors does the method make, and why?
3. **Dependence on Pre-trained Word Embeddings:**  The success of the method relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. The paper does not address the potential impact of using different pre-trained embeddings or training embeddings specific to the general domain. How sensitive is the performance to the choice of pre-trained embeddings?
4. **Scalability Concerns:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of training the CNN classifier, especially for very large datasets. Could this be a bottleneck for practical applications?
5. **Comparison to more recent DA techniques:** The related work section would benefit from referencing more recent domain adaptation techniques in NMT beyond those mentioned. Since the work is based on SMT, it may not be directly comparable, but acknowledging advances in NMT-based domain adaptation would broaden the context.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of different CNN hyperparameters (kernel size, number of filters, pooling methods) on the performance of the data selection process?
2. Can you provide examples of sentences that were correctly classified as in-domain and led to improved translation quality, as well as examples of misclassified sentences that negatively impacted the results?
3. How sensitive is the performance of the method to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embeddings (e.g., GloVe, fastText) or train custom embeddings on the general domain data?
4. What is the computational cost of training the CNN classifier, and how does it scale with the size of the general domain data?
5. Have you considered applying this semi-supervised CNN-based data selection approach to Neural Machine Translation (NMT) systems? If so, what were the results?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper references a CNN toolkit ("conText") but does not provide a specific version or link to the source code.
*   It would be helpful to include a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used for each experiment.
*   Consider including a discussion of the limitations of the proposed approach and potential directions for future research.
