## Review for "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain. This classification is then used for data selection to train a more effective SMT system. They demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines across multiple language pairs and domains, particularly when the in-domain data is extremely scarce.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of CNNs for Data Selection in Low-Resource Domain Adaptation:** The paper successfully demonstrates the utility of CNNs, coupled with pre-trained word embeddings, for identifying relevant in-domain data, even with very limited labeled examples. This is a practical and valuable contribution given the frequent lack of substantial in-domain resources.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The thorough experimentation across four language directions and multiple test domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts) provides compelling evidence of the proposed method's effectiveness. The consistent improvements over competitive baselines bolster the robustness of the findings.
3. **Detailed Analysis of Data Requirements:** The ablation study (Figure 1) quantifying the performance gains as the size of the in-domain training set for the CNN decreases is a significant contribution, showcasing the method's resilience to extremely low-resource scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. **Addresses a Critical Problem:** The problem of domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data is highly relevant and practical in the field of machine translation. The authors clearly articulate this challenge and its implications.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-designed, using established SMT frameworks and evaluation metrics (BLEU). The inclusion of strong baselines (including LM-based methods) allows for a fair comparison.  The use of multiple language pairs and diverse domains strengthens the generalizability of the findings.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is clearly written and well-organized.  The methodology is explained in a logical and understandable manner. The inclusion of relevant related work demonstrates a solid understanding of the field.
4. **Significant Improvements:** The reported BLEU score improvements (up to 3.1) are substantial and demonstrate the practical benefit of the proposed approach, especially on the Chinese-to-English SMS task. The ability to achieve improvements even with only 100 labeled in-domain sentences is particularly impressive.
5. **Insightful Discussion:** The discussion section provides a reasonable explanation for why the method works (capturing domain-specific features with CNNs and leveraging pre-trained word embeddings) and points toward promising directions for future research.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in CNN Architecture:** While the *application* of CNNs to this specific problem is novel and effective, the CNN architecture itself (one-layer CNN with max pooling) is relatively standard.  The paper doesn’t explore more sophisticated CNN architectures or variations (e.g., multiple convolutional layers, different pooling strategies) that might further improve performance.
2. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions meta-parameter tuning, but lacks detail regarding the specific search space and methodology used. Providing more specifics about the hyperparameter tuning process would increase reproducibility and transparency. What criteria were used to evaluate the hyperparameters?
3. **Dependency on Word Embeddings:** The method heavily relies on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. While the authors acknowledge this, they don’t explore the sensitivity of the performance to different pre-trained embeddings or investigate the potential for fine-tuning the embeddings during the CNN training process. A discussion of potential biases inherent in the pre-trained embeddings would also be valuable.
4. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper would benefit from a qualitative error analysis. Examining examples where the system fails could provide insights into the limitations of the approach and guide future improvements. What types of sentences are consistently misclassified?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space, optimization strategy, and evaluation metric used?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of your method to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embedding models (e.g., GloVe, FastText) and how did they affect the results?
3.  Did you consider fine-tuning the word embeddings during the CNN training process? If not, could you explain why?
4.  Could you provide some examples of sentences that were correctly classified by your method but still resulted in translation errors, and vice versa? This would help understand the limitations of the approach.
5. Regarding the 100 sentence in-domain training set scenario, could you provide more details on how you selected these sentences, and whether the performance varied significantly depending on the specific sentences chosen?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The paper would be improved by including a table summarizing the key hyperparameter settings used for each experiment.
*   It would be helpful to compare the computational cost of the proposed approach with the baseline methods.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a practical and effective solution to a challenging problem. The experimental results are compelling, and the analysis is insightful. While the CNN architecture is relatively standard, the novel application and thorough evaluation make this a valuable contribution to the field of machine translation. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above.
