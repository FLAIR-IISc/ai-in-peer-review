This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the scarcity of labeled in-domain data. The core idea is to use the CNN to select a subset of the general domain training data most relevant to the target domain, ultimately improving translation quality.

The primary contribution of this work lies in demonstrating that a relatively simple semi-supervised CNN approach can achieve surprisingly effective data selection for SMT adaptation, even with extremely limited in-domain data (down to 100 sentence pairs). While CNNs have been applied to text classification, applying them specifically for *data selection* in a low-resource SMT domain adaptation scenario, and achieving substantial BLEU improvements, is a notable result. The experimental validation across four language pairs and diverse test domains provides some robustness to the findings.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The reported BLEU score improvements are significant, particularly on the Chinese-to-English SMS task (over 3.1 BLEU).  The consistent improvements across multiple languages and domains suggest the approach isn’t simply overfitting to a specific dataset. The results demonstrate a clear benefit over strong baselines, including those employing recurrent neural network language models for data selection.
2. **Addressing a Practical Problem:** The scenario of limited in-domain data is highly relevant in many real-world machine translation applications, particularly emerging domains and social media translation. The paper tackles a genuinely important practical challenge.
3. **Clear and Logical Presentation:** The paper is well-structured and the methodology is explained in a relatively straightforward manner.  The motivation is clearly stated, and the connection between CNNs, semi-supervised learning, and data selection is logical.
4. **Thorough Experimental Setup:** The comparison against multiple well-established data selection baselines (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) strengthens the validity of the proposed approach. The ablation study reducing the in-domain data size further validates the robustness of the method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of the CNN Architecture:** The CNN architecture itself is fairly standard. The primary novelty resides in *applying* this well-established technique to the specific problem of SMT data selection in a low-resource setting. The paper doesn't present any significant architectural innovations to the CNN.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of Error Cases:** The paper presents quantitative results but lacks a detailed qualitative analysis of *why* the CNN is successful in selecting relevant data. Examining examples of correctly and incorrectly selected sentence pairs would provide valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of the method.  What types of features is the CNN actually learning to discriminate between in-domain and out-of-domain data?
3. **Dependency on Pre-trained Word Embeddings:** The success of the approach heavily relies on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. While using pre-trained embeddings is a sensible strategy given the limited in-domain data, the paper doesn’t adequately discuss the potential impact of embedding choice on performance.  A sensitivity analysis of different embedding models would be beneficial.
4. **Lack of Comparison to More Recent Data Selection Techniques:** While the baselines are strong, the field of data selection has progressed since the work cited (particularly Duh et al., 2013). Comparisons to more recent methods, potentially incorporating attention mechanisms or other modern NLP techniques, would better position the proposed approach within the current landscape.



**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide examples of sentence pairs that were correctly and incorrectly selected by the CNN, illustrating the features that influenced the classification decision?
2. How sensitive is the performance of the system to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding models, and what were the results?
3.  Have you considered incorporating attention mechanisms within the CNN architecture to potentially improve its ability to identify relevant features for data selection?
4. Could you discuss the computational cost of training the CNN and performing data selection, especially when dealing with very large general domain corpora?



**Additional Comments:**

The reference list is extensive. The phrase "semi-supervised CNN" is used frequently, but a more precise definition within the context of this paper would be helpful (e.g., clarifying the role of pre-trained embeddings). Consider providing a discussion of the limitations of the approach.
