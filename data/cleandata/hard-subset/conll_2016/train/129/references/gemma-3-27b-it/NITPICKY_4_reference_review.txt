The paper proposes a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only limited in-domain data is available. The core idea is to use a CNN to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the scarcity of labeled in-domain data. The selected in-domain data is then used to train an SMT system. 

The primary contribution, as I see it, is the empirical demonstration that a relatively simple CNN-based data selection method can outperform strong baselines, including those based on recurrent neural network language models, in a low-resource domain adaptation scenario. A secondary contribution lies in the systematic evaluation across four language pairs and diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), providing a reasonably broad assessment of the approach’s effectiveness.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results are the strongest aspect of this paper. The consistently reported BLEU improvements over strong baselines, particularly on the Chinese-to-English SMS task and, crucially, even with as little as 100 labeled in-domain sentences, are compelling. This demonstrates practical utility in a challenging setting.
2. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates a relevant and important problem: adapting SMT systems with extremely limited in-domain data. The motivation, stemming from the rise of social media and the impracticality of collecting large in-domain datasets for numerous specialized topics, is well-explained.
3. **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The evaluation across four language pairs and varied test domains strengthens the claims of generalizability. Testing across different data settings increases confidence in the robustness of the proposed approach.
4. **Detailed Experimental Setup:** The paper provides sufficient detail regarding the experimental setup, including the datasets used, the baseline systems implemented, and the CNN architecture employed. This facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Lack of Novelty in the Method:** While the results are strong, the core methodological contribution – employing CNNs for domain classification and leveraging pre-trained word embeddings – is not particularly novel. CNNs for text classification and semi-supervised learning with pre-trained embeddings were already well-established at the time of submission. The innovation lies in the *application* to this specific domain adaptation problem, but the method itself doesn't present a breakthrough.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of CNN Architecture:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the impact of different CNN architectural choices (e.g., filter sizes, number of filters, pooling strategies). The parameters are stated to be tuned, but the tuning process itself is not described, and a more systematic exploration of the architecture's influence on performance would be beneficial. Why were specific choices made, and what impact did those choices have on the overall results?
3. **Limited Error Analysis:** While the paper demonstrates *that* the method works, it doesn’t provide much insight into *why* it works. An error analysis, identifying the types of sentences that are correctly/incorrectly classified and the corresponding translation quality improvements/degradations, would be valuable. This would help understand the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.
4. **Inconsistent Terminology/Formatting:** There are minor inconsistencies in terminology and formatting throughout the paper. For example, the use of “in-domain” vs. “test domain” is sometimes ambiguous. Additionally, some references are incomplete (e.g. "Axelrod et al.,? 2011"). These issues, while minor, detract from the paper’s overall polish.
5. **Data Leakage Potential:** The description of negative sample selection mentions randomly selecting from the "whole general-domain data." If the general-domain data includes data similar to the test domains, this could introduce a form of data leakage, potentially inflating performance. The paper doesn't explicitly address this concern.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the CNN hyperparameter tuning process? What search space was explored, and what criteria were used to select the final configuration?
2. Can you elaborate on the potential for data leakage in the negative sample selection process? Were any steps taken to mitigate this issue?
3. What types of errors does the CNN classifier make most frequently? Could you provide examples of sentences that were misclassified and the impact on the final translation quality?
4. Can you provide complete reference lists?

**Additional Comments:**

* Please address the minor formatting and terminology inconsistencies throughout the paper.
* While the results are promising, the lack of significant methodological novelty is a concern.



Overall, this paper presents a solid empirical study demonstrating the effectiveness of a relatively straightforward approach to a challenging problem. However, the lack of methodological innovation and the absence of in-depth analysis prevent me from recommending unconditional acceptance. While the strong results are noteworthy, the paper falls short of demonstrating a truly significant advancement in the field. I lean towards **rejection** at this time, but would consider a revision if the authors address the weaknesses outlined above, particularly providing more detailed analysis of the CNN architecture, error analysis, and a discussion of potential data leakage.
