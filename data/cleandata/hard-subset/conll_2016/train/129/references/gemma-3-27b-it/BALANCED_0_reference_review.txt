This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a very limited amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, then use this classifier to select training data for the SMT system. The core idea of leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for limited in-domain data is promising and well-motivated.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Data Selection with Limited In-domain Data:** The primary contribution is demonstrating that a semi-supervised CNN can effectively select a small subset of general-domain data that improves SMT performance even with extremely limited (down to 100 sentences) in-domain data. This is a significant advancement as many real-world scenarios suffer from this data scarcity.
2. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents comprehensive experimental results across four language pairs and multiple test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), consistently showing improvements over strong baselines, including LM-based data selection methods. The improvements are particularly notable when in-domain data is scarce.
3. **Demonstration of CNN Effectiveness for Domain Classification:** The work provides a practical demonstration of how CNNs, combined with pre-trained word embeddings, can be effectively used for domain classification in the context of SMT, which is a potentially valuable component in more complex adaptation pipelines.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of domain adaptation with limited in-domain data and motivates the proposed solution effectively. The introduction accurately highlights the challenges and the practical relevance of the research.
2. **Solid Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-designed, using multiple language pairs and diverse test domains. The comparison with strong baselines (LM-based data selection) provides a solid foundation for evaluating the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The inclusion of a bootstrap resampling test adds rigor to the evaluation.
3. **Thorough Analysis:** The ablation study reducing the size of the in-domain data to train the CNN classifier is particularly insightful, clearly demonstrating the robustness of the semi-supervised approach even with extremely limited labeled data. The discussion section offers a plausible explanation for why the method works well.
4. **Reproducibility:** The paper references publicly available datasets and tools (word2vec, CNN toolkit), increasing the potential for reproducibility.
5. **Strong Performance Gains:** The reported BLEU improvements, particularly in the low-resource scenarios, are substantial and demonstrate the practical value of the proposed method.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of CNN Application:** While the application of CNNs to domain classification is relevant, the CNN architecture and training procedure described are relatively standard and lack significant novelty. The core innovation lies in the *combination* of CNNs with pre-trained embeddings *specifically* for the task of SMT data selection. The paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of how this work builds on existing CNN applications in NLP.
2. **Lack of Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Understanding *which* types of errors the proposed method reduces (and which it doesn't) would provide valuable insights and guide future research. For example, is the method more effective at adapting to specific linguistic features of the new domain?
3. **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** The paper mentions meta-parameters being tuned, but lacks specifics about the tuning process (e.g., search space, optimization strategy). More details would enhance reproducibility and provide insights into the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices.
4. **Dependence on Pre-trained Embeddings:** While leveraging pre-trained embeddings is a key strength, the paper doesn't explore the impact of *different* pre-trained embeddings (e.g., embeddings trained on domain-specific data, if available). Could further improvements be achieved by fine-tuning the embeddings during CNN training?

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process for the CNN (search space, optimization strategy)?
2. Did you perform any error analysis to identify the types of errors that are most effectively reduced by your method? If so, could you share your findings?
3. Have you experimented with different pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., embeddings trained on domain-specific corpora)? If so, what were the results?
4. The paper mentions “selecting the negative samples from the whole general domain for simplicity”. Does this mean other negative sampling strategies were explored? Could the performance be improved using more sophisticated negative sampling?

**Additional Comments:**

* The reference list is extensive and well-formatted.
* Consider adding a table summarizing the hyperparameter settings used in the experiments.
* The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand, but some sentences could be more concise.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that addresses an important problem with a well-motivated and effective solution. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate the practical value of the proposed approach. While the CNN architecture itself is not particularly novel, the combination with pre-trained embeddings for SMT data selection is a significant contribution. I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions to address the weaknesses outlined above. Addressing the questions will further strengthen the paper and provide more insights into the proposed method.
