## Peer Review: Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

**Summary:**

This paper addresses the challenge of domain adaptation in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) approach to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to overcome the data scarcity issue.  The selected in-domain data is then used to train an SMT system. The authors demonstrate through experiments on four language pairs that their method outperforms baseline data selection techniques, particularly when the amount of in-domain data is extremely small.

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Effective Application of CNNs for Data Selection in Low-Resource Domain Adaptation:** The paper successfully demonstrates the utility of CNNs, in a semi-supervised setting, for selecting relevant in-domain training data for SMT, a task traditionally dominated by language model-based methods. This is particularly valuable in scenarios with extremely limited in-domain resources.
2. **Empirical Validation on Diverse Domains:** The evaluation across four language pairs and diverse test domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts) provides a convincing demonstration of the method's robustness and generalizability.
3. **Demonstration of Scalability to Very Small In-Domain Datasets:** The experiments convincingly show that the proposed method remains effective even with as few as 100 labeled in-domain sentences, a significant advantage in truly low-resource adaptation scenarios.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents compelling experimental results showing consistent improvements over strong baselines, particularly in low-resource scenarios. The BLEU gains are significant and the statistical significance testing adds confidence to the findings.
2. **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The authors clearly articulate the problem of domain adaptation with limited in-domain data, a common and critical challenge in real-world MT applications. The motivation for using CNNs and semi-supervised learning is well-justified.
3. **Well-Structured & Readable Presentation:** The paper is well-written, logically organized, and easy to follow. The technical details are explained clearly, and the experimental setup is described thoroughly.
4. **Thorough Experimental Design:** The comparison against multiple strong baselines (including state-of-the-art LM-based methods) and the ablation study regarding the size of in-domain data demonstrate a careful and rigorous evaluation of the proposed approach.
5. **Insightful Discussion:** The discussion section provides a good explanation of *why* the method works, linking the results to the strengths of CNNs and pre-trained word embeddings.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in CNN Architecture:** The CNN architecture itself isn't particularly novel. The core idea lies in *applying* CNNs to this specific data selection problem. While effective, the architectural choices are standard and could benefit from more exploration of different CNN configurations or attention mechanisms.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning meta-parameters, but doesn't provide extensive details about the search process or sensitivity analysis. Reporting the impact of different hyperparameters on performance would strengthen the findings.
3. **Lack of Error Analysis:** While the results are positive, a deeper error analysis could reveal specific types of errors the system still makes and provide insights for further improvements.  For example, analyzing examples where the data selection fails could be insightful.
4. **Focus on Sentence-Level Selection:** The approach focuses on selecting sentence pairs. Exploring phrase-level or word-level selection strategies could potentially further improve performance, especially in cases where only parts of a sentence are domain-specific.



**Questions to Authors:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, what ranges were explored for key parameters like window size, stride, and the number of neurons in the convolutional layers? Did you observe significant sensitivity to any particular parameter?
2. **Error Analysis:** Have you conducted any error analysis to understand the types of errors the selected data still leads to?  Are there specific characteristics of sentences that are misclassified by the CNN?
3. **Architectural Exploration:**  Did you experiment with different CNN architectures or attention mechanisms? If so, what were the results? If not, why did you choose the specific architecture presented in the paper?
4. **Computational Cost:** What is the computational cost of training the CNN model and performing data selection compared to the LM-based methods? Does this approach introduce significant overhead?

**Additional Comments:**

*   The references are generally well-cited.
*   The use of publicly available data is commendable.
*   Consider mentioning the computational resources used for the experiments.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation for SMT. The proposed method is effective, well-evaluated, and addresses a critical challenge in real-world MT applications.  While the CNN architecture itself isn't groundbreaking, the successful application to data selection in a low-resource setting, combined with thorough experimentation, makes this a worthy contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses outlined above.
