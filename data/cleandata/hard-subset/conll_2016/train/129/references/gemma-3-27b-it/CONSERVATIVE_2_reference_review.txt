This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, using this classification to select a subset of the general-domain training data for SMT training. The main contribution lies in demonstrating that CNNs, particularly when leveraging pre-trained word embeddings, can effectively identify relevant in-domain data even with very small labeled in-domain sets (down to 100 sentences).

**Strengths:**

1. **Practical Problem & Demonstrated Improvement:** The paper tackles a genuinely practical problem – low-resource domain adaptation – which is increasingly common with the rise of social media and specialized translation tasks. The experimental results, particularly the 3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task, and sustained gains with only 100 labeled sentences, are compelling and suggest a tangible benefit.
2. **Sound Baseline Comparison:** The comparison against established data selection methods (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) is crucial. Demonstrating superiority over these baselines strengthens the claim of the proposed method’s effectiveness. The authors appropriately acknowledge and build upon previous work in data selection.
3. **Thorough Experimentation:** The experiments are conducted across four language directions and multiple test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), providing a reasonable degree of robustness to the findings. The ablation study reducing the size of the in-domain training data is well-motivated and informative.
4. **Clear Methodology:** The paper provides a relatively clear explanation of the CNN architecture and training process. While details are omitted for brevity (as is typical), the core concepts are understandable. The use of pre-trained word embeddings and the semi-supervised learning framework are well-justified.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty of CNN Application:** The core idea of applying CNNs for text classification is not novel. While the adaptation to the domain adaptation *problem* is a contribution, the CNN architecture itself is standard and lacks significant innovation. The paper doesn’t sufficiently motivate *why* CNNs are particularly well-suited for this specific task beyond a general statement about capturing local features.
2. **Insufficient Analysis of CNN Decision Making:** The paper demonstrates *that* the CNN works, but provides little insight into *why*.  What specific features are the CNNs learning to distinguish in-domain vs. out-of-domain sentences? A qualitative analysis of the learned filters or attention mechanisms (if any) would be highly valuable and build confidence in the method’s reliability. Simply stating it highlights "domain-specific words" is insufficient.
3. **Dependence on Pre-trained Embeddings:** The strong performance relies heavily on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings. While this is acknowledged, the paper doesn't explore the sensitivity of the results to different embedding methods or the potential impact of domain-specific vocabulary not well-represented in the pre-trained embeddings. Is performance maintained if embeddings are trained on a different, albeit large, general domain corpus?
4. **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Where does the method fail? Are there specific types of sentences or domains where performance is poor? Understanding the limitations would be crucial for future work.

**Questions to Authors:**

1. Can you provide examples of the filters learned by the CNN that are indicative of in-domain vs. out-of-domain data?
2. How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding models (e.g., fastText, GloVe) or embeddings trained on different corpora?
3. Can you provide a more detailed error analysis, identifying common failure cases and potential reasons for the errors?
4. The paper states “even when the in-domain data is very limited.” Is there a lower bound on the amount of in-domain data required for the method to be effective? What happens with truly *zero-shot* domain adaptation?
