## Review for "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

**Summary:**

This paper tackles the challenging problem of adapting statistical machine translation (SMT) systems when only limited in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentences as in-domain or out-of-domain, leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to overcome data scarcity. They then use this classifier to select a subset of the general-domain training data for SMT training, demonstrating improvements over baseline data selection methods across multiple language pairs and domains (SMS, Tweets, Facebook posts).

**Main Contributions (as I see them):**

1. **Empirical Demonstration of CNNs for Data Selection in Low-Resource Domain Adaptation:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence that CNNs, particularly when combined with pre-trained word embeddings, can effectively select relevant training data for SMT adaptation even with extremely limited in-domain supervision (down to 100 sentences). This is the most significant contribution.
2. **Practical Approach & Strong Experimental Results:** The authors present a complete and practical approach, incorporating readily available tools like word2vec and existing SMT pipelines.  The experimental results across four language pairs and several test domains are compelling and demonstrate consistent improvements over strong baselines.
3. **Addressing a Real-World Problem:** The problem of adapting MT systems to new domains with scarce resources is a highly relevant and practical challenge.  The proposed method directly addresses this need.

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough, utilizing multiple language pairs, diverse test domains (social media), and robust statistical significance testing. The consistent improvements across these experiments significantly bolster the claims made. The ablation studies (reducing in-domain data to 100 sentences) are particularly convincing.
2. **Effective Use of Semi-Supervised Learning:**  The integration of pre-trained word embeddings is crucial and well-justified. The authors clearly explain how these embeddings compensate for the limited in-domain data, leveraging broader semantic knowledge.  This is a smart application of semi-supervised learning.
3. **Clear and Well-Structured Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The introduction clearly motivates the problem, and the method is explained in a clear and concise manner. The related work section provides adequate context.
4. **Reproducibility:** The use of standard tools and publicly available datasets enhances the reproducibility of the work. The detailed experimental setup information is appreciated.
5. **Practicality and Impact:** The method is likely to be directly applicable to real-world MT systems facing the challenges of rapidly adapting to new and emerging domains where labelled data is scarce.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Novelty in Method Components:**  While the *combination* of techniques is valuable, individual components (CNNs for text classification, word embeddings, data selection) are not novel. The paper’s novelty lies in applying these established components to the specific problem of low-resource domain adaptation for SMT, and demonstrating its effectiveness. This needs to be more explicitly acknowledged and framed in the paper.
2. **CNN Architecture Details:** The paper lacks detailed explanation of the CNN architecture choices (number of layers, filter sizes, etc.). While the authors mention some meta-parameters were tuned, a more thorough discussion of these choices and their impact on performance would be beneficial.  A diagram of the CNN architecture would be helpful.
3. **Analysis of Error Cases:** While the results are positive, the paper lacks analysis of *why* the method succeeds or fails in specific cases.  Investigating misclassified sentences could provide valuable insights for future improvements.  For example, are there specific linguistic phenomena that are difficult for the classifier to handle?
4. **Limited Comparison to Recent Neural MT Approaches:** The comparisons are primarily against phrase-based SMT baselines.  It would be valuable to briefly discuss how this approach might compare to more recent neural machine translation (NMT) techniques for domain adaptation, even if a direct comparison isn't performed.

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed description of the CNN architecture (number of layers, filter sizes, activation functions, pooling strategies)?  Why were these specific choices made?
2.  Can you provide examples of sentences that were correctly/incorrectly classified by the CNN? What patterns do you observe in the errors?
3.  How would you anticipate this approach to perform in comparison to more recent NMT based adaptation techniques (e.g., fine-tuning a pre-trained NMT model on a small amount of in-domain data)?
4.  The paper mentions the in-domain data having a 120K subset within the general domain data for the zh2en task. How does this affect the generalizability of the results to cases where there is no such overlap?




**Additional Comments:**

*   The discussion of related work is adequate, but could be slightly more focused on recent advances in domain adaptation for both SMT and NMT.
*   Consider adding a table summarizing the key hyperparameters used in the experiments.




**Overall Assessment:**

I recommend **Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of low-resource domain adaptation for SMT.  The strong empirical results and practical approach make this work highly relevant and impactful. Addressing the weaknesses outlined above (particularly providing more detail on the CNN architecture and analyzing error cases) would further strengthen the paper.
