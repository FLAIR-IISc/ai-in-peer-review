- Problem & approach: Proposes a semi-supervised CNN to classify sentence pairs as in-domain vs out-of-domain for SMT data selection with very limited in-domain data.
- Key result: Shows BLEU improvements over baseline selection methods across four language pairs, especially in low-resource settings (e.g., 100 sentences).
- Contribution 1: Effectively applies CNNs to SMT data selection, adapting classification techniques to this task.
- Contribution 2: Demonstrates strong performance gains in extremely low-data regimes.
- Contribution 3: Provides comprehensive experiments across multiple languages and social-media-style domains.
- Strength: Novel and relevant application of semi-supervised CNNs to a practical domain-adaptation problem.
- Strength: Consistent, statistically significant BLEU improvements over strong baselines, including RNNLM-based selection.
- Strength: Thorough, diverse experimental validation supports generalizability.
- Strength: Clear, well-structured presentation and useful related-work context.
- Weakness: Lacks in-depth analysis of why the CNN approach works; no exploration of learned features or filter visualizations.
- Weakness: Heavy dependence on pre-trained word embeddings without sensitivity analysis to embedding source or size.
- Weakness: Insufficient detail on hyperparameter search and sensitivity (e.g., window size, stride, neuron counts).
- Weakness: Limited targeted error analysis—no examples or patterns of failure discussed.
- Question 1: What specific features or filters learned by the CNN drive domain identification (visualizations or activation analysis)?
- Question 2: How sensitive are results to choice of pre-trained embeddings or to training embeddings from scratch?
- Question 3: What hyperparameter ranges were explored and how did performance vary across them?
- Question 4: Can the authors provide example failures and explanations for those errors?
- Additional comment: Reference formatting inconsistent.
- Additional comment: Report computational cost of training/applying the CNN vs baseline methods.
- Overall assessment: Solid, practically relevant work with convincing results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses.
