This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, and then use this classifier to select training data for the SMT system. The core idea is sound, and the experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines, particularly in low-resource scenarios.

**Main Contributions:**

1. **Effective Semi-Supervised CNN for Data Selection:** The most significant contribution is the successful application of a semi-supervised CNN to the task of selecting relevant training data for SMT with limited in-domain resources. This approach leverages pre-trained word embeddings to compensate for the scarcity of labeled in-domain data, leading to substantial performance gains.
2. **Strong Experimental Validation:** The authors perform comprehensive experiments on four language pairs with different test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). The consistent improvements over competitive baselines provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of their proposed method. The ablation study highlighting the benefits of the semi-supervised approach is particularly valuable.
3. **Demonstration of Scalability to Extremely Low-Resource Settings:** The results show that the proposed method is remarkably robust even when trained with only 100 in-domain sentence pairs, which is crucial for real-world applications where obtaining large amounts of in-domain data is often impractical.

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the problem of domain adaptation in low-resource settings and provides a compelling motivation for exploring new approaches. The limitations of existing methods, especially their reliance on large in-domain datasets, are well-explained.
2. **Solid Methodology:** The proposed method is well-described and appears logically sound. The use of CNNs for sentence classification is a natural fit for capturing domain-specific features, and the semi-supervised approach effectively leverages unlabeled data.
3. **Comprehensive Experiments:** The experiments are well-designed and include several strong baselines, including LM-based data selection methods. The use of multiple language pairs and test domains enhances the generalizability of the findings.
4. **Detailed Analysis:** The ablation study and the discussion of the results provide valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of the proposed method. The analysis of the importance of pre-trained word embeddings and the impact of different CNN configurations is particularly helpful.
5. **Reproducibility:** The authors provide sufficient details about the experimental setup and resources used, which facilitates reproducibility.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Limited Analysis of CNN Architecture:** While the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the CNN-based approach, the paper lacks a detailed analysis of the optimal CNN architecture (e.g., number of layers, filter sizes). Exploring different configurations and providing a rationale for the chosen settings would strengthen the paper.
2. **Black Box Nature of Data Selection:** It is not entirely clear *why* the CNN selects specific sentences as in-domain. A qualitative analysis of the selected sentences, perhaps with examples of key features identified by the CNN, would provide further insights into the decision-making process.
3. **Dependency on Pre-trained Word Embeddings:** The method relies heavily on pre-trained word embeddings. While this is a reasonable approach given the limited in-domain data, it raises the question of how well the method would perform with languages or domains where high-quality pre-trained embeddings are not readily available. This is not discussed.
4. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn't explicitly address the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters. Mentioning the range of values explored and the impact on performance would enhance the robustness of the findings.
5. **Lack of Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis could provide valuable insights into the types of errors made by the system and suggest potential avenues for improvement. For example, are there specific types of sentences or domains where the method struggles?

**Questions to Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the CNN architecture? Specifically, what experiments did you conduct to determine the optimal number of layers, filter sizes, and pooling strategies?
2.  Can you provide examples of sentences that were selected as in-domain by the CNN and explain what features or patterns led to their selection?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding models or considered training embeddings from scratch using the general-domain data?
4.  What is the computational cost of training the CNN classifier and selecting the training data compared to the baseline methods?
5.  What kinds of errors does the system still make, and what are the most common sources of these errors?



**Additional Comments:**

*   The references section is comprehensive.
*   The writing is generally clear and concise.
*   Consider discussing potential applications beyond SMT, such as cross-lingual information retrieval or domain adaptation for other NLP tasks.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation for machine translation. The proposed semi-supervised CNN approach is effective, well-motivated, and supported by comprehensive experiments. While there are some minor weaknesses, they do not detract significantly from the overall quality of the work. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions and suggestions outlined above.
