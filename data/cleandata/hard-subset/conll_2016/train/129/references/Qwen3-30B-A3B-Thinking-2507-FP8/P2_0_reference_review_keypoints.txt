- Summary: Proposes a semi-supervised CNN for domain classification to select in-domain SMT training data when only tiny in-domain corpora (hundreds of sentences) exist.  
- Contribution 1: Identifies the practical gap in domain adaptation when only very small amounts of in-domain data are available.  
- Contribution 2: Shows semi-supervised CNN yields up to +3.1 BLEU and consistent gains even with ~100 in-domain sentences.  
- Contribution 3: Provides extensive evaluation across four language pairs and diverse social-media domains with ablation studies.  
- Strength 1: Addresses a meaningful, practical problem relevant to emerging domains like social media.  
- Strength 2: Strong empirical gains over baselines, including significant improvement with very limited in-domain data.  
- Strength 3: Well-designed, comprehensive evaluation with statistical testing and multiple baselines.  
- Strength 4: Clear technical description and reproducible implementation details (embeddings, CNN params).  
- Weakness 1: Limited comparison to recent neural domain-adaptation or NMT methods.  
- Weakness 2: Insufficient discussion of limitations (extremely small data, scaling, compute costs).  
- Weakness 3: Lacks detailed analysis of what features the CNN learns (important words/n-grams).  
- Weakness 4: Potential overfitting risk with tiny in-domain samples not fully analyzed.  
- Weakness 5: Limited discussion of broader applicability to other NLP tasks with limited data.  
- Question 1: How does the method compare to recent NMT domain-adaptation approaches and can it be applied to NMT?  
- Question 2: What specific words or n-grams does the CNN rely on for domain discrimination?  
- Question 3: What are the computational costs versus LM-based baselines (training time/resources)?  
- Question 4: Would modern pre-trained models (BERT, XLM-R) improve performance with very limited data?  
- Question 5: How does performance degrade with extremely limited in-domain data (e.g., <50 sentences)?  
- Overall recommendation: Strong accept with minor revisions to address limitations, overfitting concerns, and broader implications.
