

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only very limited in-domain data is available (as few as 100 sentence pairs). The authors propose using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, then using this classifier to select the most relevant training data. The main contributions I see are:

1. Demonstrating that CNN-based data selection can outperform traditional language model (LM)-based approaches for domain adaptation, particularly when in-domain data is extremely limited (100 sentences).
2. Providing evidence that their semi-supervised CNN approach is more stable and effective than LM-based methods when the amount of in-domain data is small.
3. Showing practical applicability through experiments across four language pairs with social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts).

## Strengths

1. **Real-world relevance**: The problem addressed is genuinely important in practical translation systems. Many emerging domains (social media, niche topics) have limited in-domain data, making this a valuable contribution to practical MT system development.

2. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The authors conduct experiments across four language pairs (Chinese-English, Arabic-English, English-Spanish, English-Chinese) with multiple domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), providing strong evidence for their approach's effectiveness.

3. **Consistent performance improvements**: The results show consistent improvements over baselines, particularly with very limited in-domain data (2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 in-domain sentences for training the classifier). This demonstrates practical value for real-world scenarios where in-domain data is scarce.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of novelty**: The paper doesn't present a truly novel approach. CNNs for text classification have been widely used since 2014 (Kalchbrenner et al., 2014; Kim, 2014), and semi-supervised CNNs with word embeddings are well-established (Johnson & Zhang, 2015b). The authors don't convincingly demonstrate why this specific application to MT domain adaptation is fundamentally different or better than existing text classification methods.

2. **Inadequate baseline comparison**: The authors compare against LM-based methods (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) but fail to include more relevant baselines. For example, they don't compare against simpler text classification methods like TF-IDF or cosine similarity that would provide a stronger baseline for their approach. They also don't compare against contemporary text classification techniques that were available at the time (2015-2016).

3. **Misrepresentation of "tiny amounts"**: The paper claims to work with "tiny amounts" of in-domain data, but the dev set used as in-domain data contains 6,000 sentences (for Chinese-English), which is substantial. The 100 sentences refers only to the amount used to train the classifier, not the final MT system. This misrepresentation undermines the paper's main claim.

4. **Lack of evidence for CNN superiority**: The paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence that CNNs are fundamentally better than simpler approaches for this specific task. The explanation in Section 5.4 about why CNNs work better is superficial and doesn't demonstrate why CNNs would be particularly suited to this problem over other classification techniques.

5. **Overstated impact**: The paper claims "significant improvement" (3.1 BLEU points), but this is achieved by using only 300K of the 12.2M training sentences. This is a 2.5% reduction in training data, not a fundamentally new approach that solves the problem with minimal data.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to use "tiny amounts" of in-domain data (100 sentences), but uses 6,000 sentences for the dev set (which they call "in-domain" data for training the classifier). What is the minimum amount of in-domain data needed to achieve comparable results? How does this compare to the 100 sentences mentioned in the paper?

2. Why didn't the authors compare against simpler text classification approaches (e.g., TF-IDF, cosine similarity) that would provide a stronger baseline for their CNN approach?

3. The paper states that "we do not always have access to large or even medium amounts of in-domain data," yet they use 6,000 sentences as their dev set. How does this dev set size compare to the typical amount of in-domain data available for the scenarios they claim to address?

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses a relevant problem and demonstrates consistent improvements over baselines, the lack of novelty and inadequate comparison against simpler approaches prevent me from recommending acceptance. The authors present a standard application of an established technique (CNN text classification) to a new domain (MT data selection) without providing compelling evidence that their approach is fundamentally better than simpler alternatives. The misrepresentation of "tiny amounts" of data and the failure to compare against more relevant baselines undermine the paper's main claims. The work is incremental rather than innovative, and the results could be achieved with more straightforward approaches.

I recommend rejection as the paper fails to meet the high standards of novelty and methodological rigor expected at CoNLL. The authors should either provide stronger evidence of the superiority of their approach or demonstrate a more novel contribution to the field.
