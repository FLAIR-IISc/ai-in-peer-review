

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary of the Paper
The paper addresses the challenge of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (e.g., hundreds of sentences) is available. The authors propose using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for domain classification to select relevant training data from general-domain corpora. They use a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly selected general-domain sentences as negative samples to train the CNN. Pre-trained word embeddings from large general-domain data (using word2vec) are combined with one-hot vectors as input to the CNN. The trained model is then used to select sentences from the general-domain data for SMT training. The approach is evaluated on four language directions with test domains including SMS, tweets, and Facebook posts, showing significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1 points) even when using only 100 in-domain sentences for training.

## Main Contributions
1. **Novel application of semi-supervised CNNs for domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data**: The paper identifies and addresses a significant gap in the literature (domain adaptation with only hundreds of in-domain sentences), which previous work typically assumes at least thousands of sentences. This is a meaningful contribution to the field of machine translation adaptation.

2. **Effective use of pre-trained word embeddings to overcome data scarcity**: The authors demonstrate how pre-trained word embeddings from large general-domain data can be effectively combined with minimal labeled in-domain data to build a robust domain classifier, which is particularly valuable when in-domain data is scarce.

3. **Strong empirical validation showing substantial improvements with minimal in-domain data**: The paper provides compelling evidence that their approach can achieve significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1 points) even when using only 100 in-domain sentences for training the domain classifier, with stable results across multiple runs (standard deviation of 0.12).

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant research gap**: The paper effectively identifies a real-world problem (domain adaptation with only tiny amounts of in-domain data) that is not addressed by prior work, which typically assumes thousands of in-domain sentences. This is particularly relevant for social media translation applications where in-domain data is often scarce.

2. **Technical soundness of the proposed method**: The semi-supervised CNN approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the architecture, the use of pre-trained word embeddings, and the data selection process. The experimental setup is appropriate and well-described.

3. **Strong empirical results demonstrating effectiveness**: The paper provides compelling results showing that the proposed method (sscnn) outperforms baseline methods by significant margins, especially when in-domain data is limited. The results show that even with only 100 in-domain sentences, the method achieves a 2.1 BLEU improvement over the baseline, and the stability of results across multiple runs (standard deviation of 0.12) is particularly convincing.

4. **Comprehensive evaluation across multiple language pairs and domains**: The evaluation on four different language directions (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) with three different test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the approach.

5. **Insightful discussion of why the approach works**: The authors provide a clear explanation for why their method works well, particularly highlighting the role of convolution in capturing domain-specific words and the benefit of using pre-trained word embeddings to handle out-of-vocabulary words.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty compared to existing CNN approaches**: While the application to MT domain adaptation is novel, the CNN architecture itself (semi-supervised CNN with pre-trained word embeddings) is largely based on prior work (Johnson and Zhang, 2015b). The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate its approach from these prior works in terms of technical novelty.

2. **Lack of comparison with more recent deep learning approaches**: The paper compares with LM-based methods and a one-hot CNN, but doesn't compare with more recent deep learning approaches for text classification that might outperform the CNN-based methods (e.g., transformer-based models).

3. **Ambiguity in the role of the dev set**: The paper states they use the dev set as in-domain data, but it's unclear whether this dev set is domain-specific (e.g., SMS messages for the SMS task). If the dev set is not from the same domain as the test data, the results are less meaningful.

4. **Lack of analysis of failure cases**: The paper doesn't provide any analysis of cases where the method fails or where it's particularly challenging, which limits the understanding of the approach's boundaries.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to show the contribution of each component of the proposed method (e.g., the effect of using pre-trained word embeddings vs. training embeddings from scratch).

## Questions to Authors

1. Is the dev set used as in-domain data domain-specific (e.g., SMS messages for the SMS task), or is it a general development set? If it's not domain-specific, how does this affect the validity of the results?

2. Why did the authors choose to use the dev set as in-domain data rather than using a separate in-domain dataset? How would the method be applied in a scenario where there is no separate dev set (which would be the case if you only have a tiny amount of in-domain data)?

3. How would the proposed method compare with more recent deep learning approaches for text classification (e.g., transformer-based models) that have emerged since the Johnson and Zhang (2015b) work?

4. In Figure 1, the LM-based methods show significant performance drops when the in-domain sample size is reduced to 100 sentences. How does this compare to the performance of the authors' method when using a random selection of 100 sentences (rather than the CNN-based selection)?

5. On the zh2en SMS task, the authors claim that their method outperforms the "in-domain data only" baseline. How was this baseline created? Was it a subset of the general-domain data that was known to be in-domain, or was it created from the dev set?
