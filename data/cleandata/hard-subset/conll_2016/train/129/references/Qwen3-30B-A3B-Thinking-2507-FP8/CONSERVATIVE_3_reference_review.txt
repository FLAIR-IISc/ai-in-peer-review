

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Contributions

This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only very limited in-domain data is available (as few as 100 sentences). The method uses a small amount of in-domain data as positive examples and randomly selected general-domain data as negative examples to train a CNN classifier, which then selects relevant sentences from the general-domain data for training the SMT system.

From my perspective, the paper's primary contribution is not the CNN approach itself (which is well-established in text classification), but rather the application of this approach to a specific SMT adaptation scenario with very limited in-domain data. The most significant contribution is the demonstration that this method can achieve meaningful improvements even with extremely limited in-domain data (100 sentences), though the paper's claims about the novelty and significance of this contribution require careful evaluation.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a genuine practical problem**: The paper correctly identifies a real-world challenge in machine translation - domain adaptation when in-domain data is extremely scarce (e.g., social media domains where only hundreds of sentence pairs might be available). This is a meaningful problem with practical implications for real-world MT deployment.

2. **Rigorous experimental validation**: The paper conducts experiments across four different language pairs and domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts), providing substantial empirical evidence that their method works across different settings. The comparison with multiple established baselines (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) adds credibility to their findings.

3. **Demonstrates stability with minimal in-domain data**: The paper's most compelling evidence is the consistent performance even when using only 100 in-domain sentences (2.1 BLEU improvement over baseline), with stable results across multiple random samples of the development set (std dev 0.12). This provides strong evidence that the approach is not dependent on lucky sampling.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient novelty validation**: The paper claims novelty in applying CNNs to this specific SMT adaptation scenario, but CNNs for text classification are well-established (cited papers from 2014-2015). The paper fails to demonstrate how their application is substantially different from previous text classification work, and it doesn't adequately compare against other established text classification approaches that might be more appropriate for this task. The "semi-supervised" aspect is also not novel - the paper merely uses word2vec embeddings trained on general-domain data, which is a standard practice.

2. **Insufficient comparison with state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares against LM-based data selection methods (ngram, rnnlm, comblm), but fails to include recent neural approaches to domain adaptation or even basic neural network-based methods. Crucially, the paper does not compare against modern neural machine translation (NMT) systems, which have largely replaced SMT in the field. The paper's reliance on SMT rather than NMT makes it less relevant to current research.

3. **Inadequate explanation of why the method works**: The paper's discussion of why CNNs work better than LM-based approaches is superficial ("convolution captures important domain information" and "word embeddings help with unseen words"). There's no ablation study showing which components are actually contributing to the improvement, nor is there analysis of the specific types of sentences that the CNN selects versus the LM-based methods. The claim that "the word embedding of 'Lenovo' is close to that of 'Dell'" is hand-wavy and not supported by evidence.

4. **Methodological concern with using development set as in-domain data**: The paper uses the development set as the in-domain data for training the classifier. This is problematic because the development set is typically not considered in-domain data for the test set, potentially leading to overfitting. The paper doesn't address this methodological flaw or provide evidence that the results would hold with truly out-of-domain development data.

5. **Modest performance improvements**: The claimed improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU points) are modest in the context of the field. While the paper states "over 3.1 BLEU improvement," this is a relatively small gain in an area where even 1 BLEU point is considered significant. The paper doesn't provide sufficient context about whether these improvements are practically meaningful or statistically significant at a rigorous level (the paper mentions bootstrap resampling but doesn't provide p-values for all comparisons).

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that they use the development set as in-domain data. How would your results change if you used a separate, truly out-of-domain development set for training the classifier? Could you provide results using a separate in-domain set not related to the development set?

2. Why did you choose to use the development set as in-domain data rather than a separate in-domain set? Given that the development set is typically used for hyperparameter tuning, this seems like a methodological flaw that could lead to overoptimistic results.

3. The paper doesn't compare against more recent neural approaches to domain adaptation or even basic neural text classification methods. Could you provide a comparison with a simple neural text classifier (e.g., a feedforward network) to demonstrate that the CNN architecture is actually necessary for this task?

4. The paper claims "semi-supervised" CNN, but the semi-supervised aspect is limited to using pre-trained word embeddings from general-domain data. Is this truly a semi-supervised approach, or is it just using a standard pre-trained embedding technique? Could you clarify the semi-supervised component?

## Overall Assessment

The paper addresses a practical problem in machine translation with limited in-domain data, and provides some empirical evidence that their approach works. However, the methodological approach lacks sufficient novelty, the experimental validation is not as rigorous as it could be (particularly regarding the use of development set as in-domain data), and the performance improvements are modest. The paper fails to adequately demonstrate why their CNN approach is significantly better than existing methods or how it differs from standard text classification techniques applied to this problem. Given the field's shift toward neural machine translation (NMT), the focus on SMT makes this work less relevant to current research directions. While the results are promising for a specific scenario, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to warrant endorsement of this approach as a general solution. I recommend rejection due to insufficient novelty and lack of rigorous validation against more recent approaches.
