- Addresses domain adaptation for SMT with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as ~100 sentence pairs).  
- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN for domain classification that leverages large amounts of unlabeled data and word2vec embeddings.  
- Demonstrates empirical gains across four language pairs (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) and social-media test domains, with up to +3.1 BLEU over LM-based baselines.  
- High practical significance: helps adapt MT to emerging domains (e.g., social media) where collecting large in-domain corpora is infeasible.  
- Strong empirical results and analysis, including ablations on in-domain data size and stability checks for the 100-sentence setting.  
- Novel technical approach: embeddings from general-domain data help handle OOVs and domain-specific terminology versus LM-based selection.  
- Comprehensive evaluation design: multiple language pairs/domains, careful data selection, validation for sentence selection, and repeated experiments for stability.  
- Clear presentation and reproducible methodology with well-explained CNN architecture and experimental setup.  
- Major relevance concern: work targets SMT while the field has largely moved to NMT, with no discussion of applicability to NMT.  
- Lacks qualitative analysis of what the CNN learns (no example correct/incorrect classifications or feature attribution).  
- Potential bias from randomly sampled negative examples—general-domain negatives may overlap with in-domain content and skew results.  
- No discussion of computational cost or overhead of training CNNs and embeddings compared to LM-based methods.  
- Limited discussion of failure cases or conditions where the method may not perform well.  
- Question: How would the semi-supervised CNN and selection strategy translate to NMT or need modification for NMT adaptation?  
- Question: Please provide qualitative examples of correct/incorrect classifications and analysis of influential words/phrases.  
- Question: Explain why the CNN-based approach outperformed the in-domain-data-only baseline (e.g., +3.1 BLEU vs. 25.7 BLEU).  
- Question: Elaborate on how word embeddings specifically help handle OOVs and new domain-specific terms for domain adaptation.  
- Additional comment: Paper is well-written and valuable, but should address relevance to modern NMT and discuss adaptation/compatibility with NMT techniques.
