

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenge of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (e.g., a few hundred sentence pairs) is available. The authors propose using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for domain classification to select the most relevant training data from a large general-domain corpus. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel approach to domain adaptation that works effectively with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences), addressing a significant gap in the literature where previous methods typically required thousands of in-domain sentences.

2. A data selection method that outperforms traditional language model-based approaches, particularly when in-domain data is scarce (e.g., 2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 in-domain sentences).

3. An effective application of semi-supervised learning with pre-trained word embeddings to overcome the limited labeled data challenge in domain adaptation.

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement addressing a significant gap**: The paper effectively identifies and justifies the importance of domain adaptation with tiny in-domain data, which is a critical challenge for practical applications (e.g., social media translation). The authors clearly explain why previous methods are insufficient for this scenario, as they typically require thousands of in-domain sentences.

2. **Strong experimental validation**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments across four language pairs (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) with multiple test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). The results consistently show that the semi-supervised CNN approach outperforms baselines, with statistically significant improvements (e.g., 3.1 BLEU improvement on zh2en SMS task with around 3% of training data). The method remains effective even with as little as 100 in-domain sentences (2.1 BLEU improvement).

3. **Technical soundness and appropriate baselines**: The authors use well-established techniques (CNNs, word embeddings) and compare against relevant baselines from the literature (n-gram, RNNLM, combined LM). The experimental setup is clearly described with sufficient detail about hyperparameters, training procedures, and evaluation metrics.

4. **Practical relevance**: The method is highly relevant for real-world applications where collecting large amounts of in-domain data is impractical, such as for new domains emerging from social media. The paper demonstrates that their method can achieve significant improvements with only a small fraction of the training data.

5. **Demonstrated stability**: The authors show the stability of their approach by repeating experiments with different random samples of the in-domain data (100 sentences), obtaining consistent results with low standard deviation (0.12 BLEU), indicating the method is robust even with very limited in-domain data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the CNN approach itself**: While the application of CNNs to this specific problem is novel, the CNN architecture and semi-supervised learning technique are not particularly novel. The paper builds on existing work (Johnson and Zhang, 2015) rather than introducing a new CNN architecture or learning technique. The main contribution is the application rather than the method itself.

2. **Lack of comparison with more recent approaches**: The paper compares against older language model-based methods (n-gram, RNNLM) but doesn't compare against more recent neural approaches to domain adaptation. For instance, there are more recent methods that use neural networks for domain adaptation in neural machine translation (NMT) that would be relevant to compare against.

3. **Limited analysis of why CNN works better**: While the paper provides some intuition about why CNNs work well (capturing local features, word embeddings capturing semantic similarities), it lacks deeper analysis of the specific features or patterns that the CNN learns that make it superior to language models. More detailed analysis (e.g., visualization of learned features, error analysis) would strengthen the paper.

4. **Potential overfitting concerns**: With such a small amount of in-domain data (100 sentences), there's a risk of overfitting the CNN classifier. The paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis to show this isn't happening, such as training vs. validation performance or cross-validation results.

5. **Lack of discussion on computational cost**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of training and using the CNN classifier compared to the language model-based approaches. This is important for practical adoption, as SMT systems often need to be deployed in resource-constrained environments.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the computational cost of training and using your CNN-based data selection method compare to the traditional language model-based methods (n-gram, RNNLM)? Is the trade-off in performance worth the additional computational cost?

2. Could you provide more details on how the performance scales as the amount of in-domain data increases beyond 100 sentences? For example, how much improvement is gained when moving from 100 to 500 in-domain sentences?

3. The paper states that word embeddings are trained on general-domain data, but it's not clear if this is the same as the general-domain training data used for the SMT system. Could you clarify this?

4. How would your method compare to more recent neural approaches to domain adaptation in machine translation (e.g., for neural machine translation systems), which have become more prevalent in recent years?
