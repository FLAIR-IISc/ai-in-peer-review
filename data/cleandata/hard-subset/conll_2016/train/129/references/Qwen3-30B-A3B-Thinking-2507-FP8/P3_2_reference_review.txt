

# Peer Review: "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a significant challenge in statistical machine translation (SMT) - domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (e.g., just a few hundred sentence pairs). The authors propose using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for data selection to adapt SMT systems to target domains with minimal in-domain resources. Their approach uses limited in-domain data as positive samples and randomly selected general-domain data as negative samples to train a CNN classifier. Crucially, they incorporate word embeddings learned from large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data to compensate for the limited labeled in-domain data. The trained CNN is then used to select the most relevant sentences from the general-domain corpus for training the SMT system. The authors demonstrate their method on four language pairs with test domains including SMS, tweets, and Facebook posts, showing significant improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) even with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

## Main Contributions

1. **Novel semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data**: The authors present a practical solution to a significant problem in machine translation where traditional data selection methods require thousands of in-domain sentences.

2. **Demonstration of effectiveness with extremely limited in-domain data**: The paper provides compelling empirical evidence that their method can achieve substantial improvements (2.1 BLEU with just 100 in-domain sentences) where traditional approaches fail.

3. **Practical integration of semi-supervised learning**: The effective use of word embeddings trained on large unlabeled data to compensate for limited labeled in-domain data is a well-executed and practical approach.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a highly relevant and practical problem**: The paper tackles a very real challenge in machine translation - adapting to new domains (like social media) where in-domain data is scarce but translation needs are high. The authors clearly articulate why this problem matters, especially for emerging domains with limited resources.

2. **Strong empirical results with clear demonstration of the core innovation**: The experiments are well-designed to show the key contribution - the effectiveness of their method with minimal in-domain data. The results with 100 in-domain sentences (2.1 BLEU improvement) are particularly impressive and demonstrate the practical utility of the approach.

3. **Robustness and stability analysis**: The authors conducted an important experiment where they repeated the 100-in-domain-sentence selection three times, showing a small standard deviation (0.12 BLEU), which demonstrates the stability of their approach even with very limited in-domain data.

4. **Clear methodology and reproducibility**: The paper provides sufficient technical detail about the CNN architecture, hyperparameters, and data preprocessing. The use of the publicly available conText toolkit and standard datasets makes the work reproducible.

5. **Effective comparison with relevant baselines**: The comparison with LM-based data selection methods (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) is appropriate and shows that the proposed approach outperforms these baselines, especially with limited in-domain data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with modern NMT adaptation approaches**: The paper focuses exclusively on SMT adaptation, but the field has largely moved to neural machine translation (NMT). The authors should address how their approach would work with NMT systems, which are now dominant in the field. Without this comparison, the relevance of the work to current machine translation practice is limited.

2. **Insufficient analysis of why the semi-supervised CNN outperforms other methods**: While the paper provides some intuitive explanations (e.g., convolution capturing domain-specific words, word embeddings grouping similar words), it lacks a deeper analysis of the specific mechanisms that make their approach work better. Ablation studies or detailed error analysis would strengthen the contribution.

3. **Potential data leakage concern**: The paper mentions that for the zh2en sms task, there is a 120K in-domain subset within the general-domain data. This raises concerns about data leakage - if this subset was used in training the general-domain data, then the "in-domain" data might not actually be truly in-domain. The paper should clarify whether this subset was used in training or if it's a separate test set.

4. **Limited discussion of computational cost**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of training and applying the CNN model for data selection. For large-scale translation systems with 9-15M sentence pairs, the additional computational overhead of the CNN-based selection might be prohibitive compared to simpler LM-based approaches.

5. **Lack of qualitative analysis**: The paper doesn't provide any examples of the sentences selected by their method versus baseline methods. Qualitative examples would help readers understand the specific advantages of the CNN approach, such as how it handles domain-specific vocabulary that might be missing from the small in-domain training set.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your approach scale to neural machine translation (NMT) systems, which are now the dominant approach in machine translation? Would you need to adapt the data selection process for NMT?

2. The paper mentions that for the zh2en sms task, there is a 120K in-domain subset within the general-domain data. Can you clarify whether this subset was used in the training data, and if so, how this affects the interpretation of the results? If it was used in training, would the "in-domain" improvement be artificially inflated?

3. The paper states that "sscnn achieved the best performance for all the tasks" but doesn't provide detailed analysis of why it works better than the one-hot CNN (ohcnn) or LM-based methods. Could you provide more insights into the specific aspects of the model that contribute most to the performance gain?

4. How does the computational cost of your method compare to the baseline LM-based data selection methods? Given the large datasets used (9-15M sentence pairs), is the additional cost of the CNN-based selection feasible for production systems?

5. The paper uses the dev set as in-domain data. In real-world scenarios, the dev set is typically not representative of the test domain. How would your method perform if the dev set is not from the test domain? Would you need to collect some small in-domain data specifically for the test domain?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed study addressing a practical and important problem in machine translation. The authors successfully demonstrate that their semi-supervised CNN approach can significantly outperform traditional data selection methods when only minimal in-domain data is available. The empirical results are compelling, and the approach is well-motivated and technically sound.

The main limitation is the focus on SMT rather than NMT, which is now the dominant paradigm in machine translation. The authors should address how their approach would work with NMT systems in their revision. Despite this limitation, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation, particularly for scenarios with very limited in-domain data, which is increasingly relevant for emerging translation domains like social media.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the questions above, particularly regarding the relevance to NMT systems and clarification of the data leakage concern for the zh2en task.
