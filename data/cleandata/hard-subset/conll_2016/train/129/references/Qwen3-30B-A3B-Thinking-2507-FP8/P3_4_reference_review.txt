

# Peer Review: Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a critical challenge in machine translation: domain adaptation when only extremely limited in-domain data is available (as few as 100 sentence pairs). The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach for domain classification that leverages large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data through pretrained word embeddings. The main contributions are: (1) a novel semi-supervised CNN framework for domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data; (2) empirical validation showing significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1 points) over LM-based baselines with just 100 in-domain sentences; and (3) a theoretical justification for why CNNs and word embeddings are effective for this task.

## Strengths

1. **Practical Significance**: The paper tackles a genuinely important problem in real-world MT systems. The authors correctly identify that existing data selection methods require thousands of in-domain sentences (30K-100K), which is impractical for emerging social media domains where only hundreds of sentences might be available. Their demonstration that 100 in-domain sentences suffice for meaningful adaptation directly addresses a major pain point for practitioners.

2. **Compelling Experimental Results**: The results are strong and well-documented. The semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) achieves 2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 in-domain sentences (vs. negligible gains from LM-based methods), and maintains consistent performance across four language pairs. The stability analysis (standard deviation of 0.12 BLEU across three random samples) demonstrates robustness that's crucial for real-world deployment.

3. **Theoretical Justification**: The authors provide a clear explanation for why CNNs work well for domain classification: convolution captures domain-specific features in word windows, and max-pooling focuses on the most relevant features. Crucially, they explain how pretrained word embeddings from general-domain data help handle out-of-vocabulary words (e.g., "Lenovo" is grouped near "Dell" in embedding space), which is a significant advantage over LM-based approaches.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of NMT Comparison**: The paper evaluates only on phrase-based SMT systems, despite the field's clear shift toward neural machine translation (NMT). The authors should compare their method to recent NMT adaptation techniques (e.g., transfer learning, meta-learning) to establish relevance to current research and practice. Without this, the practical impact of their approach is limited to legacy systems.

2. **Overstated "Tiny Amount" Claim**: The paper claims to work with "tiny amounts" of data, but 100 sentences is still relatively large for many real-world scenarios (e.g., niche domains with only 10-20 sentences). The authors should clarify the practical limits of their approach and provide results with even smaller datasets to strengthen their claim.

3. **Computational Cost Analysis**: The paper fails to address the computational overhead of training and deploying the CNN classifier. For production systems, the additional time and resources required for CNN-based data selection could be prohibitive, especially for real-time applications. A runtime analysis would be necessary to assess practical viability.

4. **Limited Language Coverage**: The experiments cover only four language pairs (all high-resource: zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh), which may not generalize to low-resource language pairs. The authors should discuss how their approach would perform with less data-rich language pairs, which is a common scenario in domain adaptation.

5. **Overfitting Risk**: With only 100 labeled in-domain sentences, the CNN classifier is highly susceptible to overfitting. The paper doesn't discuss regularization techniques or cross-validation strategies to mitigate this risk, which is a significant methodological weakness.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your method perform with even smaller amounts of in-domain data (e.g., 10-20 sentences), which might be more representative of some social media domains?

2. Have you considered adapting your approach to neural machine translation systems, where domain adaptation techniques differ significantly from phrase-based SMT? How would your CNN-based data selection integrate with modern NMT pipelines?

3. Could you provide runtime analysis comparing the computational costs of your CNN-based approach versus traditional LM-based data selection methods, including both training and inference times?

## Overall Assessment

This is a well-executed study that addresses a practical problem in MT with a novel solution. The experimental results are strong and the theoretical justification is sound. However, the lack of NMT comparison and insufficient discussion of computational costs limit the paper's broader impact. The authors should address these limitations to strengthen their contribution to the field. With minor revisions to address these concerns, this paper would make a valuable contribution to the CONLL conference, particularly for practitioners dealing with domain adaptation challenges in real-world MT systems.
