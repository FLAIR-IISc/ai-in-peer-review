

# Review for CONLL: "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary of the Paper
This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation in Statistical Machine Translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (e.g., hundreds of sentence pairs rather than the typical thousands or more required by existing methods). The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach for domain classification, which leverages large amounts of general-domain data to learn word embeddings and then uses these embeddings to train a classifier that can distinguish in-domain from out-of-domain sentences. The trained classifier is then used to select the most relevant training data for SMT adaptation.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. The paper identifies and effectively addresses a significant gap in domain adaptation research where previous work assumes reasonable amounts of in-domain data (thousands of sentences), but real-world scenarios often have only tiny amounts (hundreds or less), particularly for emerging domains like social media.
2. The authors demonstrate that their semi-supervised CNN approach can achieve substantial improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) over baseline SMT systems even when using only 3% of the training data and with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.
3. The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation across four different language directions (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) with diverse test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts), including ablation studies on the amount of in-domain data needed.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a meaningful and practical gap in the literature**: The paper clearly identifies a significant problem in domain adaptation for SMT: the assumption that reasonable amounts of in-domain data are available, which is not true for many emerging domains like social media. The authors effectively articulate this gap in the introduction, noting that "we do not always have access to large or even medium amounts of in-domain data" and that "it is prohibitively expensive to collect tens of thousands of in-domain sentences for each topic." This is a genuinely important problem with practical implications for real-world translation systems.

2. **Strong empirical results with significant improvements**: The paper demonstrates compelling results, showing that their semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms all baselines by up to 3.1 BLEU points, which is a substantial improvement in the field. The ablation study showing consistent performance even with only 100 in-domain sentences (2.1 BLEU improvement over baseline) is particularly impressive and directly addresses the core problem the paper aims to solve. This is not just incremental improvement but a meaningful advance for the specific scenario they target.

3. **Well-designed and comprehensive evaluation**: The evaluation is thorough, covering four different language pairs with diverse test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts). The authors compare against four relevant baselines (including the state-of-the-art LM-based methods from Duh et al., 2013), provide statistical significance testing, and conduct an ablation study on the amount of in-domain data required. The results consistently show that their semi-supervised approach outperforms others, especially when in-domain data is limited.

4. **Clear technical contribution with sufficient implementation details**: The paper provides a well-structured description of the semi-supervised CNN approach, including the one-hot CNN and semi-supervised CNN variants. The authors provide enough implementation details (word2vec embeddings, CNN parameters, etc.) to be reproducible. The explanation of why semi-supervised CNNs work well (convolution capturing domain-specific words and word embeddings grouping similar words) is clear and well-supported by examples.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited comparison to more recent methods**: The paper focuses on SMT adaptation and compares against LM-based methods from 2011-2013, but doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches for domain adaptation. Given the rapid advancement in neural machine translation (NMT), it would have strengthened the paper to include comparisons with state-of-the-art NMT-based domain adaptation methods, especially since the authors are using neural networks for domain classification.

2. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: While the paper demonstrates strong results, it doesn't adequately discuss the limitations of their approach. For example, how does the method perform with extremely limited data (e.g., fewer than 50 sentences)? How does it scale to more diverse domains or languages? What are the computational costs compared to baseline methods? These limitations would help readers understand the boundaries of where this approach is most applicable.

3. **Lack of detailed analysis on why the method works**: The paper provides some explanation of why their approach works (convolution capturing domain-specific words, word embeddings grouping similar words), but a more detailed analysis of the specific features the CNN is learning would have been valuable. For example, which words or n-grams are most important for the classification, and how do these differ between domains?

4. **Potential overfitting concerns with tiny in-domain data**: With only 100 in-domain sentences, there's a significant risk of overfitting the domain classifier. While the authors show consistent results across three random samples (with low standard deviation), they don't provide evidence that the classifier isn't overfitting to this small sample. A more detailed analysis of the generalization ability of the classifier would have strengthened this claim.

5. **Limited discussion of broader implications**: The paper focuses on SMT adaptation, but doesn't discuss how this approach might generalize to other NLP tasks with limited in-domain data (e.g., sentiment analysis, named entity recognition). A brief discussion of broader applicability would have increased the paper's impact.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your semi-supervised CNN approach compare to recent neural machine translation (NMT) methods for domain adaptation with limited data? Have you considered applying your domain selection approach to NMT systems?

2. Can you provide more details on the specific features or patterns that the CNN is learning to distinguish in-domain from out-of-domain sentences? For example, what are the most important words or n-grams identified by the model?

3. How do the computational costs of your method compare to the baseline LM-based methods, especially in terms of training time and resource requirements? Given that the paper targets scenarios with limited in-domain data, are there any efficiency concerns?

4. Have you considered using more advanced pre-trained language models (e.g., BERT, XLM-R) for the domain classification task, which might further improve performance with very limited data?

5. What are the specific limitations of your approach when the in-domain data is extremely limited (e.g., fewer than 50 sentences)? How does the performance degrade as the in-domain data size decreases further?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that effectively addresses a meaningful problem in domain adaptation for machine translation with limited in-domain data. The authors make a clear contribution to the field by demonstrating that semi-supervised CNNs can significantly outperform existing methods when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The empirical results are compelling, the evaluation is thorough, and the approach has clear practical value.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the weaknesses noted above, particularly regarding the discussion of limitations, potential overfitting concerns, and broader implications of the work. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of domain adaptation in machine translation and is well-suited for CONLL.
