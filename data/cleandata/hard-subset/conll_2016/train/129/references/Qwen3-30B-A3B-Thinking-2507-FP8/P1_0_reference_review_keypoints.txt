- Summary: Proposes semi‑supervised CNNs using a tiny labeled in‑domain seed and word embeddings to select relevant general‑domain sentences for SMT domain adaptation, yielding up to 3.1 BLEU gains across four language pairs and social‑media domains.

Main contributions:
- Novel approach: Introduces a semi‑supervised CNN method for domain adaptation when only a very small in‑domain set (e.g., 100 sentences) is available.
- Effectiveness with tiny data: Demonstrates substantial BLEU improvements (e.g., +2.1 BLEU using 100 sentences) showing practical viability.
- Robustness: Validates the method across multiple language pairs and domains (short messages, tweets, Facebook posts).

Strengths:
- Addresses a clear gap: Targets an underexplored but important problem—domain adaptation with extremely limited in‑domain data.
- Strong empirical gains: Achieves meaningful BLEU improvements using a small fraction (~3%) of training data.
- Theoretical justification: Explains why CNNs and pretrained embeddings capture domain signals within general text.
- Comprehensive evaluation: Includes multiple languages, domains, ablations (one‑hot vs semi‑supervised CNN), and data‑size analyses.
- Practical significance: Offers a deployable solution for real‑world cases where collecting in‑domain data is costly or infeasible.

Weaknesses:
- Limited baselines: Does not compare against recent deep learning classifiers or transformer/BERT‑based domain adaptation methods.
- Selection size unclear: No principled method is provided for choosing the optimal number of sentences to select per domain.
- Insufficient ablations: Lacks component‑level analyses to isolate the contributions of embeddings, CNN architecture, and semi‑supervised training.
- SMT focus: Works in an SMT framework with no discussion of adapting the approach to modern NMT systems.
- Potential data leakage: Uses the development set as in‑domain training data, raising concerns about evaluation validity.

Questions to authors:
- How would you adapt and integrate this data selection approach into NMT pipelines, and what challenges do you anticipate?
- For the zh→en task, how was the 120K “in‑domain” subset identified—manually labeled or otherwise—and does that contradict the tiny in‑domain premise?
- Why use the development set as in‑domain training data, and how do you rule out data leakage or evaluation bias?
- How was the 120K subset verified as truly in‑domain relative to the claim of limited available in‑domain data?
- How do you ensure pretrained word embeddings capture domain‑specific signals and not just general language patterns, and what evidence supports their suitability for domain adaptation?
