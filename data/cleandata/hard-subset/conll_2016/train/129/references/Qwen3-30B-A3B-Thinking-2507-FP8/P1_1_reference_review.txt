

# Peer Review: "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (e.g., just a few hundred sentence pairs). The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach for domain classification that leverages large amounts of unlabeled data to compensate for limited in-domain data. The main contributions of the paper, ranked by strength, are:

1. A practical solution to the real-world problem of domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences), which has not been adequately addressed in prior work. This is particularly relevant for emerging domains like social media translation where domain-specific data is scarce.

2. A novel semi-supervised CNN framework for domain classification that effectively uses word embeddings trained on large general-domain corpora (via word2vec) to improve classification performance when in-domain data is scarce.

3. Comprehensive empirical evaluation across four different language pairs (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) with multiple test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) over state-of-the-art LM-based approaches, especially when in-domain data is limited.

## Strengths

1. **High Practical Significance**: The paper tackles a genuinely important problem in real-world machine translation systems - adapting to new domains with very limited in-domain data. The ability to achieve significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) with only 100 in-domain sentences is highly valuable for practical applications, particularly for social media translation where domain-specific data is scarce. The authors convincingly argue that collecting tens of thousands of in-domain sentences for hundreds of topics is prohibitively expensive, making this work highly relevant to industry applications.

2. **Strong Empirical Results with Thorough Analysis**: The experimental results are impressive and well-supported. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple language pairs and test domains, with the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforming all baselines, including LM-based methods that previously set the state-of-the-art. The ablation study on varying the amount of in-domain data (from 6,000 down to 100 sentences) provides particularly compelling evidence of the method's robustness, showing that the approach can still achieve 2.1 BLEU improvement with just 100 in-domain sentences.

3. **Novel Technical Approach with Clear Justification**: The semi-supervised CNN approach is well-motivated and logically structured. The authors clearly explain how word embeddings learned from general-domain data help the model handle out-of-vocabulary words and domain-specific terminology, which is a key advantage over traditional LM-based methods. The paper demonstrates a deep understanding of both the problem and the technical solution.

4. **Comprehensive Evaluation Strategy**: The evaluation covers multiple language pairs and domains, with careful attention to the data selection process and the impact of different parameters. The use of a separate validation set to determine the optimal number of selected sentences, and the repetition of the 100 in-domain sentence experiment three times to demonstrate stability, shows thorough experimental design.

5. **Clear Presentation and Methodology**: The paper is well-written with clear explanations of the CNN architecture, semi-supervised learning approach, and experimental setup. The methodology is sufficiently detailed to be reproducible, and the results are presented in a clear and organized manner.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited Relevance to Current MT Paradigm**: The paper focuses exclusively on statistical machine translation (SMT) systems, while the field has largely shifted to neural machine translation (NMT) as the dominant paradigm. The authors do not discuss how their approach would translate to NMT systems, which is a significant omission given that NMT is now standard practice. Without this discussion, the practical relevance of the work may be limited to legacy SMT systems.

2. **Lack of Qualitative Analysis**: The paper provides strong quantitative results but lacks any qualitative analysis of what the CNN is actually learning. The authors don't provide examples of correctly and incorrectly classified sentences, or analyze which features (words or phrases) the CNN uses to make its domain classification decisions. This qualitative insight would greatly strengthen the paper's contribution.

3. **Potential Bias in Negative Sample Selection**: The paper randomly samples negative examples from the general-domain data pool. However, this approach could potentially introduce bias if the general-domain data contains sentences that are similar to the in-domain data but were not selected as positive examples. The authors should discuss this potential limitation and how it might affect the results.

4. **No Discussion of Computational Cost**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of the proposed approach compared to baseline methods. Training the CNN and generating embeddings adds significant computational overhead compared to traditional LM-based data selection. For practical deployment, especially in resource-constrained environments, this cost is important to consider.

5. **Limited Discussion of Failure Cases**: While the results are strong overall, the paper doesn't discuss any failure cases or limitations of the approach. It would be valuable to know under what conditions the method might not work well, or what types of domains are particularly challenging for the CNN-based approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper focuses exclusively on SMT systems, but the field has largely moved to NMT. How would your semi-supervised CNN approach translate to neural machine translation systems? Would the same data selection strategy work for NMT, or would additional modifications be needed?

2. Could you provide some qualitative examples of sentences that the CNN classified correctly and incorrectly, along with an analysis of which features (words or phrases) contributed most to the classification decision? This would help understand what the model is learning.

3. The paper states that the semi-supervised CNN approach achieved better results than training on the in-domain data alone for the zh2en task (3.1 BLEU improvement vs. 25.7 BLEU for the in-domain data only baseline). This is surprising since the CNN-based approach should be learning from the same in-domain data. Could you elaborate on why the CNN-based approach outperformed the in-domain data-only baseline?

4. The paper mentions that the CNN can handle out-of-vocabulary words through word embeddings. Can you elaborate on how this specifically helps with domain adaptation, especially for new domain-specific terms that might not appear in the in-domain training data?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and presents a valuable solution to a practical problem in machine translation. The authors have clearly demonstrated the effectiveness of their approach through comprehensive experiments. However, the lack of discussion about the relevance to modern NMT systems is a significant limitation that should be addressed in the revision. The paper would be strengthened by including a brief discussion of how this approach might be adapted to NMT systems or how it could complement existing NMT domain adaptation techniques.
