- Problem & contribution: Proposes a semi-supervised CNN using pretrained embeddings for domain adaptation with very limited in-domain data (~100 sentences), showing BLEU gains and providing theoretical justification.

- Contribution detail: Claims up to 3.1 BLEU improvement over LM-based baselines and explains why CNNs + embeddings capture domain features and handle OOVs.

- Strength — practical significance: Addresses a real-world MT need where only hundreds (not tens of thousands) of in-domain sentences are available.

- Strength — experimental results: Strong, stable BLEU improvements (e.g., 2.1 BLEU with 100 sentences) across four language pairs with low variance.

- Strength — theoretical justification: Clear explanation of convolution + max-pooling capturing domain features and embeddings grouping related terms (e.g., brand names).

- Weakness — no NMT comparison: Evaluation is limited to phrase-based SMT despite the field’s shift to NMT; relevance to modern pipelines is unclear.

- Weakness — “tiny amount” claim overstated: 100 sentences may not be tiny for some domains; performance with 10–20 sentences is unspecified.

- Weakness — missing computational cost analysis: No runtime or resource comparison versus LM-based selection, which matters for production deployment.

- Weakness — limited language coverage: Experiments only on four high-resource pairs; generalization to low-resource languages is not shown.

- Weakness — overfitting risk: Training a CNN with ~100 labeled sentences risks overfitting; regularization and validation strategies are not discussed.

- Question 1: How does the method perform with even smaller in-domain datasets (e.g., 10–20 sentences)?

- Question 2: Can the approach be adapted to NMT, and how would it integrate with modern NMT adaptation techniques?

- Question 3: Can the authors provide runtime/training/inference cost comparisons with LM-based selection methods?

- Overall assessment: Well-executed and practically relevant with strong empirical and theoretical support, but limited impact without NMT comparisons and computational cost analysis; minor revisions recommended.
