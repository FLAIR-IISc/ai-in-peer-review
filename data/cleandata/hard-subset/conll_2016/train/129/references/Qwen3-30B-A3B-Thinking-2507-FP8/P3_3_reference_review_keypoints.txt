- Problem & contribution: Tackles domain adaptation for SMT with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) using a semi-supervised CNN that leverages word embeddings from large general-domain corpora to select in-domain training data.

- Empirical validation: Demonstrates consistent improvements across four language pairs and three social media domains, with gains up to 3.1 BLEU and a notable 2.1 BLEU improvement using only 100 in-domain sentences.

- Practical relevance: Focuses on a realistic and underexplored scenario (emerging social media domains) where labeled in-domain data is scarce.

- Robustness: Results are stable across runs (std. dev. 0.12), supporting the reliability of the approach.

- Novelty: Proposes a well-motivated semi-supervised framework that uses abundant unlabeled data via embeddings, potentially applicable beyond translation.

- Weakness — baselines: Does not compare the CNN to other classification methods (e.g., SVMs, RNNs, feature-based classifiers), leaving unclear whether CNN architecture is essential.

- Weakness — ablations: Lacks ablation studies to disentangle the effects of the CNN architecture versus the semi-supervised embeddings.

- Weakness — computational cost: Omits analysis of training time and resource requirements, which is important for large-scale or multi-domain deployment.

- Weakness — NMT relevance: Focuses on SMT without discussing applicability to modern NMT systems, limiting relevance to current practice.

- Question — classifier comparison: How does the semi-supervised CNN compare to a simple feature-based classifier (e.g., SVM) using the same embeddings?

- Question — deployment cost: What are the computational costs and training-time comparisons versus LM-based approaches for large-scale use?

- Question — NMT applicability: Can this approach be adapted to NMT, and if so, what modifications would be needed?

- Recommendation: Strong paper with meaningful contributions and compelling results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address comparisons, analysis, and cost concerns.
