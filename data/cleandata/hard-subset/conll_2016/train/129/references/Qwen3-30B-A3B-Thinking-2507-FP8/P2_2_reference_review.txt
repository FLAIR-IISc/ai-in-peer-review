

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant challenge in statistical machine translation (SMT) domain adaptation: adapting systems when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (e.g., 100-1000 sentences) is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach that leverages large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data through word embeddings to train a domain classifier. The classifier is then used to select the most relevant training sentences from general-domain data for adaptation. The method is evaluated across four language directions (Chinese-to-English, Arabic-to-English, English-to-Spanish, English-to-Chinese) with test domains including SMS, tweets, and Facebook posts. The results show significant improvements over baseline methods, particularly when in-domain data is very limited (e.g., 2.1 BLEU improvement with just 100 in-domain sentences).

## Main Contributions (Ordered by Strength)

1. **Novel semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data**: The primary contribution is addressing a significant gap in the literature where existing methods require thousands of in-domain sentences (30K-100K), while the authors demonstrate effectiveness with only 100 sentences.

2. **Empirical demonstration of significant improvements with minimal data**: The paper provides compelling evidence that their method outperforms state-of-the-art LM-based data selection methods, especially when in-domain data is scarce, achieving up to 3.1 BLEU improvement with only 3% of the training data.

3. **Thorough analysis of the impact of in-domain data size**: The authors conduct an extensive ablation study showing that their semi-supervised CNN approach is more robust to small in-domain data sizes than traditional LM-based methods, which often fail when data is limited.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a meaningful and timely problem**: The paper correctly identifies a significant gap in the literature - the lack of effective domain adaptation methods for scenarios where only very limited in-domain data is available. This is particularly relevant for social media translation (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), where new domains emerge rapidly with minimal training data. The authors properly contextualize their work by showing that existing methods require "several thousands of sentences" (30K-100K), which is often not feasible for emerging domains. This problem statement is strong and well-motivated.

2. **Compelling empirical results with clear demonstration of the core contribution**: The experimental results are thorough and convincing. The paper demonstrates significant improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) over baselines with only 2.5-10% of the training data. The ablation study showing that the semi-supervised CNN outperforms LM-based methods by 0.5 BLEU points on average, and achieves 2.1 BLEU improvement with just 100 in-domain sentences, is particularly strong evidence for the core contribution. The fact that the method outperforms an "in-domain data only" baseline for the SMS task (25.7 BLEU) is especially impressive.

3. **Clear explanation of why the semi-supervised approach works**: The paper provides a good explanation for why the semi-supervised CNN approach is effective, particularly the insight that words with similar meanings cluster together in the vector space learned from general-domain data. This explains why the method can correctly classify sentences containing unseen domain-specific words (e.g., "Lenovo" in the computer domain) that LM-based methods would struggle with. This theoretical justification is well-supported by the empirical results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with modern NMT approaches**: The paper focuses exclusively on SMT systems, while the field has largely transitioned to neural machine translation (NMT) in recent years. The authors don't discuss how their method would apply to NMT systems, which are now the standard in most translation tasks. This is a significant limitation given the current state of the field, as the paper's contribution would be more impactful if it were applicable to NMT as well.

2. **Limited analysis of the CNN features**: While the paper provides a basic explanation of why CNNs work well (capturing domain-specific words in windows), it lacks a deeper analysis of the features learned by the CNN. For example, the authors don't compare the features used by their CNN with those used by LM-based methods or provide visualizations of the most important features. A deeper analysis of the learned representations would strengthen the paper's theoretical contribution.

3. **No discussion of computational costs**: The paper doesn't address the computational efficiency of the proposed method compared to baseline approaches. Training and applying the CNN classifier could be more computationally intensive than LM-based methods, especially with large general-domain datasets. For practical deployment, this could be a significant drawback that the authors should address.

4. **Limited exploration of failure cases**: The paper doesn't discuss when the method might fail or provide examples of cases where the CNN misclassifies sentences. For instance, it would be valuable to know if the method struggles with domains that have very different grammatical structures or vocabulary from the general-domain data. A balanced discussion of limitations would make the paper more credible.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would this approach extend to neural machine translation (NMT) systems, which are now the dominant approach for machine translation? Would the same CNN-based data selection strategy work, or would additional modifications be needed?

2. The paper mentions using word embeddings learned from general-domain data. How sensitive is the performance to the quality of these embeddings? For example, would using embeddings trained on a different corpus (e.g., Wikipedia instead of the general-domain training data) significantly affect performance?

3. The paper focuses on social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). How would the method perform for more formal domains (e.g., legal or medical text) where the vocabulary and style differ significantly from general-domain data?

4. The paper uses a 1:1 ratio of positive to negative samples for training the CNN. How would the performance change with different ratios (e.g., 1:5 or 1:10), which is often used in imbalanced classification tasks?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend acceptance of this paper with minor revisions. The paper addresses a meaningful problem with a well-motivated solution, and the empirical results are strong and clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed method, particularly for scenarios with very limited in-domain data. The main weaknesses (lack of NMT comparison, limited feature analysis, no computational cost discussion) are significant but not deal-breakers, and the authors can address them in the revision. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of domain adaptation in machine translation, especially for emerging domains with limited data.
