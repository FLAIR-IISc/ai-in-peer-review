

# Review: Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

## Summary
This paper addresses domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only tiny amounts of in-domain data (a few hundred sentence pairs) are available. The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach to classify sentences as in-domain or out-of-domain, then use this classifier to select training data for SMT systems. The key claim is that their method achieves significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) even when using only 100 in-domain sentences for training.

## Main Contributions
1. Application of semi-supervised CNNs for data selection in SMT domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data
2. Demonstration that CNN-based data selection outperforms traditional LM-based methods, especially when in-domain data is scarce
3. Empirical validation of the approach across four language pairs with social media test domains

## Strengths

1. **Practical relevance**: The paper tackles a genuine industry problem - adapting translation systems to new domains with minimal in-domain data, which is increasingly important for social media translation where domain-specific resources are scarce.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The results consistently show significant improvements over baselines (2.1-3.1 BLEU) with very limited in-domain data. The stability of results across repeated experiments (with standard deviation of 0.12 when using 100 in-domain sentences) adds credibility to the claims.

3. **Well-structured experiments**: The paper provides a clear experimental setup across four different language directions with appropriate test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), and uses standard evaluation metrics (BLEU) with proper significance testing.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with recent approaches**: The paper compares against Duh et al. (2013) and earlier methods but fails to include more recent data selection approaches that might be more relevant to the problem. For instance, the paper doesn't compare against transformer-based data selection methods or other contemporary neural approaches that have emerged since 2013. This omission significantly weakens the novelty claim.

2. **Insufficient validation of the semi-supervised aspect**: The paper claims that the semi-supervised aspect is crucial, but provides minimal analysis to support this. The authors don't conduct a proper ablation study to isolate the contribution of the semi-supervised learning component versus the CNN architecture itself. The paper states that the semi-supervised CNN "achieves the best performance" but doesn't provide evidence that the semi-supervised aspect is actually responsible for the improvement.

3. **Questionable novelty**: The application of CNNs to text classification is well-established (as cited in the related work section), and the specific application to data selection for SMT is not substantially different from prior work. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how this differs from or improves upon existing work in the field.

4. **Limited scope of validation**: The experiments are confined to four language pairs with social media domains, but the paper doesn't test on more challenging scenarios where domain shift is more extreme or where the limited in-domain data is even scarcer (e.g., fewer than 50 sentences). The authors claim "even 100 sentences" is sufficient, but don't explore the lower bounds of applicability.

5. **No analysis of failure modes**: There's no discussion of when the approach fails or under what conditions it doesn't work well. This is critical for understanding the limitations of the method and whether it's broadly applicable.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your approach compare to more recent neural data selection methods published after 2013 (e.g., using transformers or more advanced architectures) that might have been developed for similar tasks?

2. Could you provide a more detailed ablation study showing which components of your semi-supervised CNN approach (the CNN architecture, the word embeddings, the semi-supervised learning) are most critical for the observed performance improvements?

3. Have you tested your approach with even fewer in-domain examples (e.g., 50 sentences) to understand the lower bound of applicability, or is 100 sentences the practical minimum?

4. What are the computational costs of your approach compared to the baseline methods, and how does this impact practical deployment in resource-constrained environments?

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses a relevant problem and demonstrates promising results, it falls short of meeting the high standards expected for novel contributions in CONLL. The approach is not sufficiently novel, the validation is limited, and the claims about the necessity of the semi-supervised aspect are not thoroughly supported. The paper would be significantly stronger if it compared against more recent methods, provided a proper ablation study, and explored more challenging scenarios.

The results are interesting and potentially useful, but the paper presents them as a novel contribution without sufficient evidence of how it advances beyond existing work. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the novelty claims unsupported by the evidence provided. The paper would benefit from more rigorous validation against contemporary baselines and a clearer articulation of what makes this approach fundamentally different from existing work.

I recommend rejection due to insufficient novelty and inadequate validation against the most relevant contemporary approaches.
