

# Review for "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary
This paper tackles a critical yet overlooked problem in statistical machine translation (SMT) domain adaptation: adapting systems when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentence pairs) is available. The authors propose a novel semi-supervised CNN approach that uses a small amount of in-domain data (e.g., development set) to train a domain classifier, which then selects relevant training sentences from large general-domain corpora. The key innovation is leveraging word embeddings learned from vast general-domain data to compensate for the extreme scarcity of in-domain examples.

## Main Contributions
1. **Addressing the critical gap of tiny in-domain data scenarios** (100-1000 sentences) - a problem completely unaddressed by previous domain adaptation work requiring thousands of in-domain sentences.
2. **Novel application of semi-supervised CNNs to SMT data selection** that leverages general-domain unlabeled data to overcome in-domain data scarcity.
3. **Demonstrating significant performance gains** (up to 3.1 BLEU) even with only 100 in-domain sentences, outperforming all LM-based baselines.

## Strengths

1. **Novel problem space**: The paper identifies and addresses a genuinely important gap in the literature. Previous domain adaptation work (Axelrod et al., Duh et al.) assumes thousands of in-domain sentences are available, which is unrealistic for many emerging domains like social media. The authors correctly note that "we do not always have access to large or even medium amounts of in-domain data" and that "it is prohibitively expensive to collect tens of thousands of in-domain sentences for each topic." This is a significant, underexplored problem with real-world implications.

2. **Bold, novel technical approach**: The semi-supervised CNN framework is a creative application of neural networks to a problem traditionally handled with language models. The paper's core insight—using word embeddings from general-domain data to help classify sentences with minimal in-domain supervision—is both elegant and novel. The fact that they can achieve 2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 in-domain sentences (Fig 1) represents a significant leap beyond previous work.

3. **Strong empirical results**: The results are compelling and consistently demonstrate superiority over all baselines, particularly in the tiny data regime. The paper shows that their method "beats the best LM-based method by about 0.5 BLEU point on average" and achieves "up to 1.4 BLEU improvement" even when there's no in-domain data component in the general-domain data. The stability of results across multiple language pairs (4 directions) adds credibility.

4. **Practical significance**: The approach is immediately applicable to real-world translation systems with emerging domains (like social media) where collecting large in-domain datasets is impractical. The paper's focus on "short messages (sms), tweets, and Facebook posts" demonstrates clear relevance to contemporary translation challenges.

5. **Clear, impactful novelty statement**: The paper explicitly states "There is no previous work considering the scenario where only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available: this is the scenario we address in this paper." This is a strong, unambiguous claim of novelty that the results support.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited exploration of extreme data scarcity**: While the paper demonstrates success with 100 in-domain sentences, it doesn't explore the limits of the method (e.g., 10-50 sentences), which is where the most challenging applications would occur. The authors could provide more insights on how the method scales with decreasing in-domain data.

2. **Comparison with unsupervised approaches**: The paper compares primarily with LM-based data selection methods but doesn't discuss how their approach would compare with unsupervised domain adaptation techniques, which might be relevant for the tiny data scenario.

3. **Methodological details**: The CNN architecture details are somewhat sparse (e.g., "we set the region size to 5 and stride size to 1" without justification). More thorough ablation studies on different CNN configurations would strengthen the claims.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your method perform with fewer than 100 in-domain sentences (e.g., 50 or 20)? Could you provide a brief analysis of the diminishing returns as in-domain data decreases further?

2. Have you considered combining your semi-supervised CNN approach with other unsupervised domain adaptation techniques for potentially better results in extremely data-scarce scenarios?

3. The paper mentions "Facebook post" as a test domain, but doesn't discuss specific challenges of this domain (e.g., slang, abbreviations). How does your approach handle such linguistic phenomena compared to traditional LM-based methods?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a bold, novel approach to a significant problem in machine translation that has been overlooked in the literature. While the methodology could be slightly more thoroughly explained, the core contribution—addressing domain adaptation with tiny amounts of in-domain data—is highly valuable and well-supported by compelling results. The paper's focus on a practically important scenario with strong empirical validation makes it a strong candidate for acceptance. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

The novelty of addressing this specific scenario, combined with the significant performance gains demonstrated (especially with extremely limited in-domain data), outweighs the minor methodological shortcomings. This work opens up new possibilities for domain adaptation in translation systems where data collection is prohibitively expensive, making it a valuable contribution to the field.
