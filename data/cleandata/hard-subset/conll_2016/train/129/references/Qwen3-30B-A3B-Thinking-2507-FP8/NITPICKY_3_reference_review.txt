

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary
This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors train a CNN to classify sentences as in-domain or out-of-domain using a small amount of in-domain data (e.g., development set) as positive samples and randomly selected general-domain sentences as negative samples. They use word embeddings learned from large general-domain data (via word2vec) to augment the model. The trained CNN selects relevant sentences from the general-domain data for training the SMT system.

## Main Contributions
1. A semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation with very limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentence pairs)
2. Demonstrated improvements over baseline methods (up to 3.1 BLEU points) on multiple language pairs and test domains

## Strengths
1. The problem of domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data is genuinely important and underexplored in the literature. The authors effectively identify this gap.
2. The paper provides comprehensive experiments across four different language directions (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) with different test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts).
3. The semi-supervised approach using word embeddings from general-domain data is conceptually sound and leverages available resources effectively.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical Methodological Flaw: Misuse of Development Set** - The most significant flaw is the authors' use of the development set as in-domain data. The development set is typically used for hyperparameter tuning, not as a source of in-domain data for training. This is a fundamental methodological error that undermines the entire approach. The paper states "We employ the dev set as in-domain data," but doesn't justify why the development set is representative of the test domain. In domain adaptation, the development set is usually not in the same domain as the test set. This flaw is so severe that it makes the entire experimental setup questionable.

2. **Lack of Reproducibility Details** - The paper lacks crucial details for reproducibility. The authors mention using "word2vec" but don't specify parameters (window size, negative sampling rate, etc.). They state "the number of weight vectors or neurons is 500" but don't provide the number of convolutional layers, kernel size, or other architectural details. The CNN toolkit "conText" is mentioned but without a link or description of how it differs from other toolkits.

3. **Inconsistent Formatting and Citation Issues** - The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that suggest poor attention to detail. Citations are often incomplete (e.g., "Chen et al., 2013" cut off), inconsistent ("Axelrod et al.,? \Q2011\E, Axelrod et al., "), and contain typos ("Q2011" instead of "2011"). These issues are particularly concerning for a technical paper where precision matters.

4. **Insufficient Statistical Evidence** - The paper claims significant improvements (e.g., "2.1 BLEU improvement") but fails to provide absolute BLEU scores for baselines. For instance, they state "we obtain over 3.1 BLEU improvement" but don't state the baseline BLEU score. They also don't provide error bars or standard deviations for their results, making it impossible to properly assess statistical significance. The claim "performed significantly better than this baseline at p < 0.05 level" lacks supporting evidence.

5. **Unjustified Assumptions** - The authors assume that the development set is representative of the test domain, but provide no evidence for this. They also claim "the word embeddings learned from much larger unlabeled data ensure that the word embedding of 'Lenovo' is close to that of 'Dell'" but don't provide evidence that this actually contributed to their results.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why did you use the development set as in-domain data? How do you justify that the development set is representative of the test domain, especially in domain adaptation scenarios where this is typically not the case?
2. Can you provide the complete CNN architecture details (number of layers, kernel size, etc.) and word2vec parameters used for reproducibility?
3. What absolute BLEU scores did the baseline systems achieve, not just the improvements? This is critical for understanding the significance of the results.
4. How did you determine the optimal number of selected sentences (e.g., 300K for zh2en)? Did you perform hyperparameter tuning on a separate validation set?

## Overall Assessment

This paper addresses an important problem in machine translation, but the methodological flaws are too severe to recommend acceptance. The most critical issue is the use of the development set as in-domain data, which fundamentally undermines the experimental design. The paper also lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility and contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that suggest poor attention to detail.

While the core idea of using semi-supervised CNNs for domain adaptation with limited data is promising, the paper fails to properly demonstrate this due to the methodological flaws. The authors have not provided sufficient evidence to support their claims about the effectiveness of their approach.

I recommend rejection due to the critical methodological flaw and the numerous minor issues that collectively undermine the paper's credibility and contribution. The paper would need to be substantially rewritten to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.
