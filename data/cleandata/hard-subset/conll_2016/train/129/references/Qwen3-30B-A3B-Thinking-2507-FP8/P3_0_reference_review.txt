

# Peer Review: Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

## Summary of the Paper

This paper addresses a significant challenge in statistical machine translation (SMT): domain adaptation when only a very small amount of in-domain data is available (e.g., hundreds of sentence pairs rather than the thousands typically required by previous approaches). The authors propose using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, then selecting the most relevant sentences from general-domain data. Their approach combines a small amount of in-domain data (used as positive examples) with randomly selected general-domain data (as negative examples) to train a CNN classifier. To compensate for the limited in-domain data, they use word embeddings trained on large amounts of general-domain data (via word2vec) as additional input to the CNN. The authors evaluate their method on four language pairs (Chinese-English, Arabic-English, English-Spanish, English-Chinese) across different test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating up to 3.1 BLEU improvement over baselines, with significant gains even when using only 100 in-domain sentences.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Practical solution to a common real-world problem**: The paper provides a viable approach to domain adaptation in machine translation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available, addressing a significant gap in the literature that previous methods have not tackled (previous work typically requires thousands of in-domain sentences).

2. **Demonstrated effectiveness with minimal in-domain data**: The authors show their method achieves substantial improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) even when using only 100 in-domain sentences, which is orders of magnitude less than what previous approaches require.

3. **Well-justified semi-supervised approach**: The use of word embeddings trained on large general-domain data to overcome the limited in-domain data problem is well-motivated and effectively leverages available resources.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a meaningful gap in the literature**: The paper tackles a genuine and important challenge in machine translation. While domain adaptation has been widely studied, the specific scenario of having only a tiny amount of in-domain data (e.g., hundreds of sentences rather than thousands) has been largely unaddressed. This is particularly relevant for emerging domains like social media where collecting large amounts of in-domain data is impractical.

2. **Strong and consistent empirical results**: The results are impressive and consistent across multiple language pairs and test domains. The paper demonstrates significant improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) over baselines, with the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforming the one-hot CNN (ohcnn) by about 0.5 BLEU on average. The stability across multiple random samples (standard deviation of 0.12 with 100 in-domain sentences) is particularly convincing.

3. **Practical implementation**: The method is relatively simple to implement and integrates well with existing SMT pipelines. The authors provide clear details on their implementation (including hyperparameters and tools used), making it easy for practitioners to adopt their approach.

4. **Thorough analysis of data requirements**: The paper provides a detailed analysis of how the method performs with varying amounts of in-domain data, showing that the CNN-based approach becomes increasingly advantageous as the amount of in-domain data decreases. This is particularly valuable for understanding the practical applicability of the method.

5. **Effective use of word embeddings**: The authors' use of word embeddings trained on large general-domain data to capture semantic similarities (especially for handling out-of-vocabulary words) is well-justified and contributes significantly to the method's success.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of sufficient novelty distinction**: While the application to domain adaptation with limited in-domain data is novel, the core methodology (CNN for text classification) is well-established in the literature. The paper doesn't sufficiently differentiate their approach from existing text classification work (e.g., Johnson and Zhang, 2015b), making it difficult to assess the true novelty of their contribution. The semi-supervised aspect is also not entirely new, as Johnson and Zhang already proposed similar methods.

2. **Problematic use of development set as in-domain data**: The paper uses the development set as the in-domain data for training the CNN. This is problematic because the development set is typically used for model selection and hyperparameter tuning, not as in-domain training data. This could lead to overfitting to the development set and overly optimistic results, as the development set might not be representative of the test domain. This is a significant methodological flaw that undermines the validity of the results.

3. **Lack of comprehensive ablation studies**: The paper doesn't provide thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific components (e.g., CNN vs. semi-supervised aspect vs. specific architecture). It's unclear how much of the improvement comes from using CNNs vs. using the semi-supervised aspect vs. using the specific architecture they propose. Without these studies, it's hard to determine which aspects of their approach are truly important.

4. **Limited comparison with recent methods**: The paper compares with LM-based methods (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) but doesn't compare with more recent neural network-based approaches for domain adaptation. For example, there have been several recent papers on neural machine translation (NMT) that might have better approaches for domain adaptation, which would provide a more relevant comparison for modern MT systems.

5. **Insufficient analysis of failure modes**: The paper doesn't provide much analysis of when their method fails or what types of domain mismatches are particularly challenging for their approach. This is important for understanding the limitations of their method, especially since they claim their method works well even with very limited in-domain data.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper uses the development set as the in-domain data for training the CNN. However, the development set is typically used for model selection. How do you ensure that the development set is representative of the test domain, and what is the risk of overfitting to the development set? Would the results hold if a separate in-domain test set were used for training the CNN?

2. The paper lacks ablation studies to isolate the contribution of specific components (CNN vs. semi-supervised aspect vs. specific architecture). Could you provide more details on which aspects of your approach contribute most to the observed improvements? For example, how much of the improvement comes from using word embeddings versus using the CNN architecture?

3. The paper compares with LM-based methods but doesn't compare with recent neural network-based approaches for domain adaptation. How does your method compare with state-of-the-art NMT-based domain adaptation techniques? Would your approach be applicable to neural machine translation systems as well?

4. The paper demonstrates good performance with 100 in-domain sentences, but what is the minimum number of in-domain sentences needed for the method to work effectively? Is there a point where the performance starts to degrade as the amount of in-domain data decreases further?

5. The paper shows that the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms the one-hot CNN (ohcnn) by about 0.5 BLEU on average. What specific aspects of the semi-supervised approach (word embeddings, etc.) contribute to this improvement, and how much of the improvement is due to each component?

## Additional Comments

The paper is well-written and presents a practical solution to a real-world problem. The evaluation is comprehensive across multiple language pairs and test domains. However, the methodological flaw of using the development set as in-domain data for training the CNN is a significant issue that needs to be addressed. The authors should consider using a separate in-domain set for training the classifier or clearly acknowledge this limitation in their evaluation.

The comparison with recent NMT-based domain adaptation methods would strengthen the paper's relevance to current machine translation research. Given the shift towards neural machine translation, it would be valuable to see how their approach performs in an NMT context.

The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of their approach, including specific cases where their method might not work well. This would help readers understand the scope of the method's applicability.
