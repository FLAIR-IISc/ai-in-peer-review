- Summary: Tackles domain adaptation for MT with tiny in-domain data using a semi-supervised CNN to classify sentence pairs and select data, showing significant BLEU gains across four language pairs on social-media domains.

- Contribution 1: Proposes a novel semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation effective with as few as ~100 in-domain sentences.

- Contribution 2: Empirically outperforms LM-based data selection, yielding up to +3.1 BLEU using only ~2.5–10% of training data.

- Contribution 3: Demonstrates consistent effectiveness across multiple language pairs and domains even with extremely limited in-domain data.

- Strength 1: Addresses a practical, real-world problem of rapid adaptation to new domains with very limited supervision.

- Strength 2: Provides thorough experimental evaluation across four language pairs and ablation studies showing gains down to 100 sentences.

- Strength 3: Offers solid motivation for semi-supervised CNNs—convolutions capture domain features and embeddings help OOV handling.

- Strength 4: Shows practical significance by achieving substantial improvements with very small amounts of data.

- Weakness 1: Fails to clearly separate the effects of data selection from the classifier/model choice in comparisons.

- Weakness 2: Uses the development set as in-domain training data, creating potential data leakage and unrealistic evaluation.

- Weakness 3: Lacks comparison with more recent domain-adaptation/NMT methods beyond older LM-based baselines.

- Weakness 4: Limited analysis of failure cases or situations where gains are marginal.

- Weakness 5: Omits discussion of computational cost and resource requirements for training the CNN vs. baselines.

- Question 1: How to avoid using the dev set as in-domain data in real deployments—recommendations for collecting or estimating in-domain data without leakage?

- Question 2: Can you compare or adapt the method for recent NMT domain-adaptation techniques and provide comparisons?

- Question 3: Why does the CNN advantage shrink as in-domain data grows (1600+ sentences), and what are the practical implications?

- Question 4: How stable is the method at 100 sentences compared to LM-based baselines (variance/comparison of standard deviations)?

- Overall assessment: Valuable and well-motivated work with strong empirical results; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address data leakage and comparisons to newer methods.
