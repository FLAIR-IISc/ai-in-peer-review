

# Review for "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Main Contributions
The paper addresses the challenge of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only very limited in-domain data is available (e.g., just a few hundred sentence pairs). The authors propose using a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain. They leverage word embeddings learned from large amounts of general-domain data to overcome the limitation of small in-domain data. The trained CNN is then used to select the most relevant in-domain sentences from the general-domain training data for training the SMT system.

Main contributions (ordered by strength):
1. A novel semi-supervised CNN framework for domain adaptation in machine translation that works effectively with very limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentence pairs).
2. Demonstrated significant improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU points) over baseline methods when adapting SMT systems to new domains with tiny amounts of in-domain data.
3. Systematic experimental validation across four language pairs and three different test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) showing consistent improvements.

## Strengths
1. **Addresses a meaningful practical problem**: The paper tackles a genuine challenge in machine translation - adapting systems to new domains with very limited in-domain data. This is particularly relevant for emerging domains like social media translation where collecting large amounts of in-domain data is difficult or impossible. The authors clearly articulate the gap in existing work (all previous approaches assume reasonable amounts of in-domain data).

2. **Thorough experimental validation**: The paper provides comprehensive evaluation across four different language pairs (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) and three distinct test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). The results consistently show improvements over baselines, with the method demonstrating particular strength when in-domain data is scarce (as few as 100 sentences).

3. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates significant improvements over baselines, particularly when in-domain data is limited. For example, with only 100 in-domain sentences, their semi-supervised CNN approach achieves a 2.1 BLEU improvement over the baseline. The results are statistically significant (p < 0.01) for the most effective method (sscnn) compared to all baselines.

4. **Clear ablation study**: The paper includes a thorough analysis of how the number of in-domain sentences affects performance, clearly demonstrating the superiority of their approach when in-domain data is limited (under 800 sentences), where other methods fail to show consistent improvement.

5. **Practical relevance**: The method is simple to implement and integrates well with existing SMT pipelines. The authors show that they can reduce the training data size to just around 3% of the original corpus while still achieving better performance than using all general-domain data, making it highly practical for real-world applications.

## Weaknesses
1. **Limited novelty**: While the application of CNNs to domain adaptation in MT is novel for this specific context, the core technique (semi-supervised CNN for text classification) is not new. The paper builds on existing work in semi-supervised CNNs for text classification (Johnson and Zhang, 2015b) but doesn't sufficiently distinguish their contribution from prior work in text classification.

2. **Lack of comparison with recent neural approaches**: The paper compares with traditional LM-based data selection methods but doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches to domain adaptation (e.g., using transformers, contextual embeddings, or other neural domain adaptation techniques). This limits the significance of the claimed improvements and makes it difficult to assess where this work stands in the current landscape.

3. **Ambiguous comparison with "in-domain data only" baseline**: In the zh2en SMS task, the authors mention that their method outperformed a baseline trained on a 120K in-domain subset. However, this 120K subset is part of the general-domain data, so it's not a fair comparison to a true in-domain-only baseline. The paper should clarify whether this subset was truly representative of the test domain (SMS) or if it was just a random subset of the general-domain data.

4. **Insufficient methodological details**: The paper lacks sufficient details about the CNN architecture (e.g., number of layers, kernel sizes, etc.) and hyperparameter tuning process. The description of the "semi-supervised CNN" is somewhat vague, making it difficult to fully replicate the work.

5. **Limited analysis of model behavior**: While the paper provides some discussion on why CNNs work well for this task, it lacks a deeper analysis of what specific features or words the CNN is using to make its classification decisions. This limits understanding of why the approach works and how it might be improved.

## Questions to Authors
1. How does your semi-supervised CNN approach compare with recent neural domain adaptation methods that use contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT, XLM) for domain adaptation in machine translation?

2. In the zh2en SMS task, you mention a 120K in-domain subset within the general-domain data. Can you clarify whether this subset was truly representative of the test domain (SMS) or if it was just a random subset of the general-domain data?

3. The paper mentions using "the dev set as in-domain data" for training the CNN. However, the dev set is typically used for model selection and hyperparameter tuning. How did you ensure that the dev set was truly representative of the test domain? Did you perform any domain analysis of the dev set to confirm its suitability as in-domain data?

4. The paper claims to use "semi-supervised CNN" but doesn't provide details about how the word embeddings were generated from the unlabeled data. Could you clarify the specific process and data used for training the word embeddings (e.g., did you use the same general-domain training data as in the main experiments, or a separate corpus)?
