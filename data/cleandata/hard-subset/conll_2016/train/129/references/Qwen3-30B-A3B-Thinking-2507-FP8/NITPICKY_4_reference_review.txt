

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Contributions
This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation when only very limited in-domain data is available (as few as 100 sentence pairs). The authors train a CNN to classify sentences as in-domain or out-of-domain, using word embeddings trained on general-domain data to compensate for the scarcity of in-domain examples. The key contributions are: (1) a novel semi-supervised CNN framework for domain classification with minimal in-domain data; (2) empirical demonstration that this approach outperforms traditional language model-based data selection methods, especially when in-domain data is scarce; (3) systematic evaluation showing effectiveness even with only 100 in-domain sentences.

## Strengths
1. **Addresses a meaningful problem**: The paper tackles a practical challenge in machine translation - adapting to new domains when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. This is increasingly relevant as social media and new domains emerge with limited resources.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The experiments show consistent improvements over baselines, particularly with minimal in-domain data. The 2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 in-domain sentences is compelling evidence of the method's value.

3. **Well-structured evaluation**: The paper provides detailed experiments across four different language directions and test domains, demonstrating robustness of the approach.

## Weaknesses

1. **Critical methodological inconsistency in semi-supervised CNN formulation**: The paper defines Equation (2) as σ(W · wl(x) + V · ul(x) + b) but fails to specify the dimensionality of ul(x). The dimensionality of the word embeddings (ul(x)) is not mentioned, making it impossible to verify if this equation is mathematically sound. If ul(x) has a different dimensionality than wl(x), the model would fail to train. This is a fundamental flaw in the technical presentation.

2. **Arbitrary choice of in-domain data size threshold**: The paper arbitrarily selects "100 in-domain sentences" as the smallest test case without justification. The authors don't explain why 100 is the appropriate threshold rather than 50 or 200, making the experimental design seem arbitrary rather than principled.

3. **Insufficient detail on validation procedure**: The paper claims they "empirically determine the number of selected in-domain sentences for each MT system based on experimental results on a separate validation set" but provides no details about the validation set's composition, size, or how the selection was made. This lack of detail undermines reproducibility.

4. **Formatting errors in references**: The reference list contains multiple instances of "Axelrod et al.,? 2011" with a question mark instead of a period, indicating a clear formatting error that suggests carelessness in the preparation of the manuscript.

5. **Lack of detail on word embedding training**: The paper states "we used word2vec" to train word embeddings from general-domain data but doesn't specify the size of the general-domain data used for training the embeddings. This information is critical for understanding the semi-supervised aspect of the approach.

## Questions to Authors

1. In Equation (2), what is the dimensionality of ul(x) (the word embedding vector) and how does it match with wl(x) (the one-hot vector)? If they have different dimensions, how does the model handle this?

2. Why was 100 sentences chosen as the threshold for the smallest in-domain data size? Was this determined through a systematic analysis of performance across different data sizes?

3. Could you provide more details about the separate validation set used to determine the number of selected in-domain sentences? Specifically, what was its composition and size?

## Overall Assessment

While the paper addresses a meaningful problem and presents promising results, the methodological inconsistencies, arbitrary experimental choices, and formatting errors are substantial enough to warrant rejection. The critical flaw in the mathematical formulation of the semi-supervised CNN (lack of dimensionality specification for ul(x)) makes it impossible to verify the technical soundness of the approach. The arbitrary choice of 100 in-domain sentences as a threshold, combined with the lack of detail on the validation procedure, further undermines the paper's credibility.

As a perfectionist reviewer, I must emphasize that these are not minor issues but fundamental flaws in the technical presentation and experimental design. While the paper's core idea is valuable, the execution contains too many inconsistencies to be considered sound for publication. The authors would need to substantially revise the paper to address these issues before it could be considered for publication.
