- Summary: Proposes a semi-supervised CNN to select in-domain sentence pairs from large corpora for SMT when only a tiny in-domain seed (e.g., 100s of sentences) is available, showing notable BLEU gains across four language pairs and social-media domains.  
- Contribution 1: Introduces a semi-supervised CNN for domain-adaptive data selection that outperforms LM-based selection when in-domain data is scarce.  
- Contribution 2: Empirically demonstrates substantial BLEU improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) with extremely small in-domain samples (as few as 100 sentences).  
- Contribution 3: Provides a practical method for adapting translation systems to new domains (e.g., social media) where in-domain data collection is expensive.  
- Strength 1: Addresses a real and practical problem—domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data—relevant to emerging domains like social media.  
- Strength 2: Strong, well-designed experiments showing consistent and significant improvements, including stability across different 100-sentence samples.  
- Strength 3: Clear evidence that the method excels in the core challenging scenario (≤800 in-domain sentences), outperforming LM-based baselines.  
- Weakness 1: Lacks comparison with modern NMT adaptation approaches, limiting assessment of relevance in current translation pipelines.  
- Weakness 2: Insufficient analysis of why CNNs outperform LM methods—no feature-level analysis or visualizations of learned representations.  
- Weakness 3: Limited scalability analysis—only four language pairs evaluated and no study of performance or computational costs across many topics.  
- Question 1: How would this data-selection approach be adapted for NMT systems—directly applicable or requiring significant changes?  
- Question 2: Can the authors provide concrete examples of unseen in-domain words correctly handled via word embeddings to illustrate that benefit?  
- Question 3: How would the method select appropriate language-model data from corpora like Gigaword when the target domain is very different?  
- Overall assessment: Strong, practically relevant paper with convincing results for tiny in-domain settings; recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address NMT comparisons and deeper analysis of why CNNs work.
