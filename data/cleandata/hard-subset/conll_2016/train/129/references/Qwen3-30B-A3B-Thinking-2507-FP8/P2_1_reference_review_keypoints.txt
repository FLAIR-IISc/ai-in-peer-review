- Addresses domain adaptation for SMT with extremely limited in-domain data by selecting relevant general-domain sentences via a semi-supervised CNN.  
- Proposes a semi-supervised CNN that leverages word embeddings from unlabeled general-domain data to compensate for scarce labeled in-domain data.  
- Provides experiments across four language directions and social-media test domains showing up to 3.1 BLEU improvement, especially with very limited in-domain data.  
- Tackles a practically important problem (adapting to new domains like social media) and shows improvements even with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.  
- Conducts comprehensive, rigorous evaluation against strong baselines across multiple languages and domains.  
- Demonstrates stability and reliability with low-variance gains (e.g., 2.1 BLEU improvement with 100 sentences, repeated trials show std=0.12).  
- Shows clear value of semi-supervision: the semi-supervised CNN consistently outperforms a supervised one-hot CNN (~0.5 BLEU average gain).  
- Does not sufficiently justify novelty—using CNNs for domain classification builds on prior work and the paper lacks a compelling novelty argument.  
- Claims about “tiny” in-domain data are inconsistent (uses a 6,016-sentence dev set in some experiments, undermining the “few hundred” claim).  
- Lacks comparison or discussion with neural machine translation (NMT) domain-adaptation methods, limiting broader relevance.  
- Insufficient analysis of why the semi-supervised CNN outperforms supervised variants beyond a high-level embedding argument.  
- Limited discussion of failure cases and limitations (e.g., very different test domains or poor-quality in-domain data).  
- Asks authors to clarify whether 6,016 sentences qualifies as “tiny” and justify experimental choices regarding in-domain data size.  
- Requests deeper analysis of how word embeddings specifically contribute to performance gains over one-hot features.  
- Asks whether and how the approach would generalize to NMT, and for any experimental results if available.  
- Requests information on computational cost of training the CNN and practical trade-offs versus collecting more in-domain data.  
- Overall recommendation: accept for CONLL, with minor revisions to address novelty claims, data-size consistency, comparisons to NMT, and deeper analysis.
