

# Peer Review

## Summary

This paper addresses the challenge of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for domain classification to select relevant training data from a large general-domain corpus. The method uses a small amount of labeled in-domain data (e.g., development set) as positive examples and randomly selected general-domain sentences as negative examples to train a CNN classifier. The semi-supervised aspect comes from leveraging word embeddings learned from large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data to improve the classifier's performance when in-domain data is scarce. The trained CNN is then used to rank sentences in the general-domain data by domain relevance, and the top-ranked sentences are selected to train the adapted SMT system. The authors evaluate their approach on four language pairs (Chinese-to-English, Arabic-to-English, English-to-Spanish, English-to-Chinese) with test domains including short messages, tweets, and Facebook posts. Their method achieves significant improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU points) over baselines trained on all general-domain data, with particularly strong performance when in-domain data is limited to just 100 sentences.

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)

1. **Novel semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data**: The paper presents a creative solution to the problem of domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (e.g., 100 sentences), which has not been addressed in prior work. This is a significant contribution as many real-world applications (e.g., social media translation) face this exact challenge.

2. **Demonstrated effectiveness with extremely limited in-domain data**: The authors show that their method can achieve substantial improvements (2.1 BLEU points) even when using only 100 in-domain sentences for training the domain classifier, which is a remarkable result for the problem setting.

3. **Robustness across multiple language pairs and domains**: The method is evaluated on four different language directions with diverse test domains (short messages, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating consistent improvements over baselines across multiple scenarios.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a clear, important gap in the literature**: The paper identifies a well-defined problem that has been overlooked in prior work - domain adaptation with very limited in-domain data (just a few hundred sentences). Previous work (e.g., Axelrod et al., 2011; Duh et al., 2013) assumes several thousand sentences are available, but the authors rightly point out that new social media domains often have only a few hundred sentence pairs. This is a significant gap that the paper effectively addresses.

2. **Strong empirical results with meaningful improvements**: The results are compelling, with the semi-supervised CNN method achieving up to 3.1 BLEU improvement over the baseline using only around 3% of the training data. The most impressive result is the 2.1 BLEU improvement when using just 100 in-domain sentences, which demonstrates the practical viability of the approach for real-world scenarios where collecting large amounts of in-domain data is prohibitive.

3. **Clear theoretical justification for the CNN approach**: The paper provides a solid explanation for why CNNs work well for this task - specifically how convolutional layers can capture domain-specific features even when they are embedded within general-domain text, and how word embeddings from large unlabeled data help with handling out-of-vocabulary words. This theoretical grounding strengthens the contribution and helps readers understand why the approach is effective.

4. **Thorough evaluation across multiple scenarios**: The authors evaluate their method on four different language pairs with diverse test domains (short messages, tweets, Facebook posts), which demonstrates the robustness of their approach. The ablation studies showing the superiority of semi-supervised CNN over one-hot CNN, and the analysis of performance with varying amounts of in-domain data, provide comprehensive evidence for their claims.

5. **Practical significance**: The approach has clear practical value for real-world applications where collecting large amounts of in-domain data is expensive or impossible (e.g., for new social media domains). The paper demonstrates this with concrete examples and results, showing that the method can be applied without requiring large amounts of in-domain data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with more recent deep learning approaches**: The paper only compares with traditional language model-based data selection methods (n-gram, RNNLM, combined LM), but fails to compare with more recent deep learning approaches for text classification or domain adaptation (e.g., transformer-based models, BERT-based approaches). Given that the paper uses a CNN, it's surprising that they don't compare with state-of-the-art text classification methods that have emerged since the cited work.

2. **Unclear how to determine the optimal number of selected sentences**: The paper mentions that they determine the number of selected sentences based on experimental results on a separate validation set, but this seems to be a manual process that would need to be repeated for each new domain. The paper doesn't provide a principled way to determine the optimal number of sentences to select, which is important for practical deployment.

3. **Lack of ablation studies to isolate key components**: While the paper compares one-hot CNN vs. semi-supervised CNN, it doesn't provide ablation studies to show the contribution of each component (e.g., the word embeddings, the CNN architecture, the semi-supervised aspect). This makes it hard to understand what exactly is driving the improvement.

4. **Not clear how this integrates with modern neural machine translation**: The paper focuses on statistical machine translation (SMT), but most current research and applications are moving toward neural machine translation (NMT). The paper doesn't discuss how their approach would be adapted to NMT systems, which would be an important consideration for practical adoption.

5. **Questionable use of development set as in-domain data**: The paper states they use the development set as in-domain data, but typically the development set is used for hyperparameter tuning and not for training. This seems like a potential data leakage issue, as the development set should not be used for training the domain classifier.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper focuses on statistical machine translation (SMT), but most current MT systems use neural machine translation (NMT). How would you adapt your approach for NMT systems, and what challenges would you expect to face in doing so?

2. The paper mentions that for the zh2en task, there's a 120K in-domain subset within the general-domain data. How did you determine that this subset was in-domain? Was it manually labeled, and if so, wouldn't this contradict the paper's premise of having only a tiny amount of in-domain data?

3. You use the development set as in-domain data for training your domain classifier. However, typically the development set is used for model selection and not for training. How do you justify this approach, and what evidence do you have that this doesn't lead to data leakage?

4. The paper claims that the semi-supervised CNN outperforms the "in-domain data only" baseline for the zh2en task. However, the "in-domain data only" baseline is trained on a 120K subset of the general-domain data. How did you determine that this 120K subset is truly in-domain? Was it manually labeled, and if so, how does this align with the paper's premise of having only a tiny amount of in-domain data?

5. The paper states that the semi-supervised CNN uses word embeddings learned from general-domain data. How do you ensure that these word embeddings capture domain-specific features rather than just general language patterns? What evidence do you have that the word embeddings are suitable for domain adaptation?
