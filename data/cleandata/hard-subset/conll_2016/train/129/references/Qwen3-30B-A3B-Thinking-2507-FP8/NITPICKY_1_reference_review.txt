

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary
This paper proposes a CNN-based approach for domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available (e.g., 100 sentences). The authors train a CNN classifier to distinguish in-domain from out-of-domain sentences, using a small amount of in-domain data as positive examples and randomly selected general-domain sentences as negative examples. To compensate for limited in-domain data, they incorporate word embeddings learned from large general-domain data, creating a semi-supervised framework. They evaluate their method on four language directions with test domains including SMS, tweets, and Facebook posts.

## Main Contributions (as I see them)
1. A CNN-based data selection method for domain adaptation in SMT with tiny in-domain data (100 sentences)
2. Demonstration that their semi-supervised CNN approach significantly outperforms LM-based methods when in-domain data is extremely limited
3. Empirical evidence that their method can select highly relevant in-domain sentences even when no in-domain component exists within the general-domain data

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a genuinely challenging problem in NLP: domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data, which is particularly relevant for emerging domains like social media translation.
2. The experimental results are compelling, showing consistent improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) over baselines when using only 100 in-domain sentences.
3. The paper provides valuable evidence that CNN-based methods can be effective for data selection even when the in-domain data is extremely limited (100 sentences), which is not adequately addressed in previous work.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of Novelty and Misrepresentation of "Semi-supervised" Approach**: The core contribution is mislabeled as "semi-supervised," but the semi-supervised aspect (using unlabeled data to learn word embeddings) is standard practice in NLP and not novel. The CNN training itself is fully supervised. This misrepresentation weakens the paper's main claim. The authors cite Johnson and Zhang (2015b) as the source of semi-supervised CNNs, but their approach is identical to standard practice, not a novel contribution.

2. **Inconsistent Terminology and Methodological Confusion**: The paper uses "in-domain" inconsistently to refer to both the test domain and the small dev set used for training the classifier. This causes significant confusion. For example, the dev set is typically used for tuning, not as in-domain data for adaptation. The paper also fails to clarify how the dev set relates to the test domain, which is crucial for domain adaptation.

3. **Critical Missing Details in Methodology**: The paper lacks essential details about the CNN architecture, hyperparameters, and training process. For instance:
   - The paper states "region size to 5 and stride size to 1" without justification
   - It mentions "word2vec" but doesn't specify parameters (window size, negative sampling rate)
   - The "semi-supervised CNN" description (Equation 2) is confusing as the unlabeled data is only used for word embeddings, not for the CNN training itself
   - The paper claims to use "conText" toolkit but doesn't provide version or configuration details

4. **Inconsistent and Misleading Results Reporting**: 
   - The paper reports "over 3.1 BLEU improvement" but the actual improvement is 3.1 BLEU points (26.0 vs 22.9), which is misleading
   - The paper states they achieved "2.1 BLEU improvement" with 100 in-domain sentences but doesn't provide the baseline BLEU score for comparison
   - Table 2 reports "BLEU" values without the baseline scores, making it impossible to assess absolute performance
   - The paper claims "even when the in-domain sample is reduced to 100 sentence pairs, the LM-based methods only get a small improvement," but doesn't provide the baseline BLEU scores for comparison

5. **Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper makes no attempt to explain why CNNs would be particularly suitable for this specific data selection task. The authors cite previous work on CNNs for text classification but don't provide a compelling argument for why this approach would work better than other methods for domain adaptation in translation, especially given the limited in-domain data.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify the exact definition of "in-domain" used in the paper? The dev set is used as "in-domain" data, but dev sets are typically not representative of the test domain. How does the dev set relate to the test domain in your experiments?

2. Why did you choose to use the dev set as your in-domain data rather than a separate in-domain dataset? How would your results change if you used a true in-domain dataset?

3. The paper claims to use a "semi-supervised CNN" but the semi-supervised aspect is limited to word embeddings from unlabeled data, which is standard practice. Could you clarify what novel semi-supervised component you've introduced?

4. The paper states "We use a small amount of in-domain data, such as the development set, as the positive sample," but development sets are not typically considered "in-domain" data. How did you verify that the development set was representative of the test domain?

5. In the results, you report "2.1 BLEU improvement" with 100 in-domain sentences, but without the baseline BLEU score, it's impossible to assess the significance. Could you provide the absolute BLEU scores for the baseline system and your best system?

## Overall Assessment
While the paper addresses a relevant problem with potentially valuable applications, the numerous methodological inconsistencies, misrepresentation of the semi-supervised approach, and critical missing details significantly undermine the contribution. The paper fails to clearly articulate what is novel about their approach compared to existing work, and the lack of essential technical details makes the results difficult to reproduce or fully evaluate. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these flaws too substantial to overlook, despite the promising experimental results. The paper would benefit from a complete rewrite to clarify the methodology, correct the misrepresentations, and provide the necessary technical details. Therefore, I recommend rejection.
