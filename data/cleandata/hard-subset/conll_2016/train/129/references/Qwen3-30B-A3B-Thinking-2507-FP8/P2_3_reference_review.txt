

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary
This paper addresses the challenge of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (e.g., hundreds of sentences) is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach to classify sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, using a small amount of in-domain data for training and leveraging large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data through word embeddings. The method is evaluated across four language directions with social media test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating significant improvements over baseline methods even with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation in machine translation when only extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) is available.
2. Empirical demonstration that the proposed method outperforms existing language model-based data selection approaches, achieving up to 3.1 BLEU improvement with only ~3% of the training data.
3. Evidence that the method remains effective even when the in-domain data is extremely limited (100 sentences), with consistent improvements across multiple language pairs and test domains.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a meaningful practical problem**: The paper tackles a genuine challenge in real-world machine translation applications where new domains emerge (e.g., social media) with very limited in-domain data. Previous approaches require thousands of sentences for adaptation, making them impractical for rapidly emerging domains. The authors clearly articulate this gap in the literature (Section 1), making their work highly relevant to practitioners.

2. **Thorough experimental evaluation**: The paper provides comprehensive evaluation across four different language pairs (Chinese-English, Arabic-English, English-Spanish, English-Chinese) with diverse social media test domains. The ablation study (Figure 1) clearly demonstrates the method's effectiveness with varying amounts of in-domain data, showing significant improvements even at 100 sentences (2.1 BLEU improvement). The results are statistically significant and consistently outperform baselines.

3. **Strong justification for the semi-supervised approach**: The authors provide a compelling explanation for why semi-supervised CNNs work well (Section 5.4), highlighting two key advantages: 1) convolution captures important domain-specific features while ignoring general words, and 2) word embeddings from general-domain data help handle out-of-vocabulary words in the test domain. This is particularly valuable for fast adaptation scenarios where collecting more in-domain data is impractical.

4. **Practical significance**: The paper demonstrates that the method can achieve substantial improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) with only 2.5-10% of the training data. This is highly relevant for resource-constrained environments and demonstrates clear utility beyond academic interest.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient distinction between data selection and model choice**: While the paper claims their CNN approach outperforms LM-based methods, all methods (including baselines) use a small subset of the training data (2.5-10%). The paper doesn't clearly separate the contribution of the data selection strategy from the model choice. A more convincing experiment would compare the same data selection strategy (e.g., selecting the top N sentences) using different classifiers to isolate the model's contribution.

2. **Misuse of development set as in-domain data**: The paper uses the development set as the "in-domain" data for training the CNN classifier (Section 5.2). This is problematic because in real-world scenarios, the development set is typically used for model tuning, not as the in-domain data for adaptation. This creates a data leakage issue and makes the evaluation unrealistic for practical deployment.

3. **Lack of comparison with more recent domain adaptation methods**: The paper only compares with LM-based methods from 2011-2013 (Axelrod et al., Duh et al.). The field has advanced significantly since then, with more recent approaches like neural machine translation (NMT) and domain adaptation techniques for NMT. The paper would be stronger with comparisons to these more contemporary methods.

4. **Limited analysis of failure cases**: The paper presents strong positive results but doesn't analyze cases where the method fails or where the improvement is marginal. Understanding the limitations of the approach would add significant value to the work.

5. **Unclear computational cost**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of training the CNN model compared to the LM-based baselines. For practical deployment, especially in resource-constrained environments, this is an important consideration.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would you address the issue of using the development set as in-domain data in a real-world deployment scenario? Would you recommend using a small, manually collected in-domain set for adaptation, or is there a way to estimate the in-domain data from the development set without data leakage?

2. Could you provide a comparison with more recent domain adaptation methods for neural machine translation (NMT), which have shown promising results with limited data? How might your approach be adapted for NMT systems?

3. The paper mentions that the CNN approach outperforms LM-based methods when in-domain data is limited (100 sentences), but the results in Figure 1 show that when in-domain data is larger (1600+ sentences), the improvement over LM-based methods is smaller (0.3-0.5 BLEU). Why does the relative advantage of CNNs diminish as more in-domain data becomes available, and what does this imply about the practical applicability of the method?

4. Could you elaborate on the stability of your method when the in-domain data is extremely limited (100 sentences)? The paper mentions a standard deviation of 0.12 in BLEU score across three runs, but how does this compare to the standard deviation of the LM-based methods at the same data size?

## Overall Assessment

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the problem of domain adaptation with limited in-domain data, which is a significant challenge in practical machine translation systems. The proposed semi-supervised CNN approach is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and demonstrates strong empirical results. While the paper has some weaknesses in experimental design and comparison, the core contribution addresses an important gap in the literature and shows clear practical utility.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the weaknesses related to data leakage and comparison to more recent methods. The paper is well-written, the contributions are clear, and the results are compelling for the target domain of statistical machine translation with limited in-domain data.
