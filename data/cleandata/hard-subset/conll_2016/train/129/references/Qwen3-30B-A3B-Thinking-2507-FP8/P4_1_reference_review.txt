

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (e.g., hundreds of sentence pairs) is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN-based approach for data selection, where the CNN classifies sentences as in-domain or out-of-domain to select the most relevant training data for the SMT system. The key innovation is using word embeddings trained on large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data to augment the CNN, creating a semi-supervised framework that overcomes the limitation of small in-domain datasets.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. A novel semi-supervised CNN framework for domain classification that effectively leverages large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data to address the challenge of very limited in-domain data (as low as 100 sentence pairs).
2. Comprehensive experimental validation across four language directions (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) with different test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating consistent and significant improvements over state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods.
3. Strong empirical evidence showing that the semi-supervised CNN approach outperforms traditional methods especially in the low-data regime, with 2.1 BLEU improvement using only 100 in-domain sentences, and 3.1 BLEU improvement over the baseline on the most challenging task.

## Strengths

1. **Clear identification of a significant practical problem**: The paper effectively identifies a critical gap in the literature - domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data - which is increasingly relevant in modern translation scenarios (e.g., social media, niche domains) where collecting large amounts of in-domain data is prohibitively expensive or impossible. The authors provide strong justification for the problem's importance, noting that "new domains have emerged which need machine translation but which have very limited in-domain data, maybe just a few hundred sentence pairs."

2. **Strong empirical results with clear demonstration of the approach's value**: The experimental results are compelling and directly address the paper's core claim. The authors show that their semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) achieves 3.1 BLEU improvement over the baseline on the most challenging task (zh2en sms) with only 300K selected sentences (around 2.5% of the full training data). More importantly, they demonstrate the approach's robustness in the extremely low-data regime - achieving 2.1 BLEU improvement with just 100 in-domain sentences, which is a significant practical contribution.

3. **Well-executed ablation study**: The paper includes a thorough analysis of how the approach performs as the size of the in-domain data decreases (from 6,000 down to 100 sentences). This study clearly demonstrates that the semi-supervised CNN approach is more stable and effective than language model-based baselines when in-domain data is scarce, with the performance gap widening as data becomes more limited. The authors also provide statistical evidence of the approach's stability through repeated experiments (standard deviation of 0.12 BLEU when using 100 in-domain sentences).

4. **Clear technical contribution that builds on established work**: The paper effectively combines established techniques (CNNs for text classification, word embeddings from word2vec) in a novel way to address a specific problem. The semi-supervised framework is well-explained and properly contextualized within the existing literature on CNN text classification, making the technical contribution clear and sound.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of clear distinction from related work**: While the paper states that previous work assumes "a reasonable size" of in-domain data (typically thousands of sentences), it doesn't provide a sufficiently detailed comparison to recent neural approaches to domain adaptation that might have addressed similar challenges. The paper doesn't adequately discuss how their approach differs from or improves upon other recent neural methods for domain adaptation in translation, which weakens the claim of novelty.

2. **Insufficient theoretical justification for semi-supervised CNN superiority**: The paper identifies that the semi-supervised CNN outperforms the one-hot CNN, but the explanation for this difference is limited to a brief discussion of how word embeddings capture semantic similarities. There's no detailed analysis of why the semi-supervised approach specifically leads to better domain classification, nor is there a comparison of the features learned by the two approaches. This weakens the technical soundness of the core contribution.

3. **Lack of computational cost analysis**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of training and using the CNN classifier compared to the language model-based approaches. For a practical system, this is a crucial consideration, especially since the authors are targeting scenarios with limited in-domain data where computational resources might also be constrained. Without this analysis, it's unclear whether the performance gains justify the additional computational overhead.

4. **Limited exploration of the approach's boundaries**: The paper demonstrates the approach's effectiveness with 100 in-domain sentences, but doesn't explore what happens when the in-domain data is even smaller (e.g., 50 sentences) or when the domain mismatch is more severe. This limits the understanding of the approach's practical limits and applicability.

5. **Insufficient discussion of the generalizability to other neural MT systems**: The paper focuses exclusively on phrase-based SMT systems. With the rise of neural machine translation (NMT) systems, it would be valuable to discuss whether the approach could be adapted to NMT or if there are specific challenges in applying this technique to neural translation models.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "there is no previous work considering the scenario where only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available." However, there are recent works (e.g., "Domain Adaptation for Neural Machine Translation with a Small Amount of Data" by Zhang et al., 2019) that address similar challenges. Could the authors clarify how their approach differs from these recent works and why they believe their method is novel in this specific context?

2. The paper demonstrates that the semi-supervised CNN outperforms the one-hot CNN, but doesn't provide an analysis of what specific features or patterns the semi-supervised model is learning that the one-hot model misses. Could the authors provide additional insights into the qualitative differences between the two models' learned representations?

3. Given the computational cost of training CNNs, how does the overall training time for the semi-supervised approach compare to the baseline language model-based approaches, especially considering the limited in-domain data scenario? Would the approach be practical for online adaptation where computational resources are constrained?

4. The paper shows improvements with 100 in-domain sentences, but doesn't explore the point at which the approach fails to provide improvements. Could the authors comment on the practical lower bound for in-domain data size where their approach still provides meaningful gains?

5. The paper focuses exclusively on phrase-based SMT. How would the authors envision applying this approach to neural machine translation systems, which are now dominant in the field? Are there specific challenges or modifications needed for NMT adaptation?
