

# Peer Review: Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

## Summary

This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (e.g., just 100-1000 sentences) is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach for data selection, where they train a CNN classifier to distinguish between in-domain and out-of-domain sentences using minimal labeled data and leveraging large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data to learn word embeddings. The trained classifier is then used to select the most relevant training data from a large general-domain corpus to train an adapted SMT system. The approach is evaluated on four language directions (Chinese-to-English, Arabic-to-English, English-to-Spanish, English-to-Chinese) with test domains including SMS, tweets, and Facebook posts. The results demonstrate significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) using only around 3% of the training data, with the method remaining effective even when as few as 100 in-domain sentences are available for training the classifier.

## Main Contributions

1. A novel semi-supervised CNN framework for domain adaptation in machine translation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (as few as 100 sentences)
2. Comprehensive experimental validation showing significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1) with minimal training data (around 3% of the full corpus) across multiple language pairs and domains
3. Demonstration that the approach works effectively even when no in-domain data exists within the general-domain training corpus, which is a significant practical contribution

## Strengths

1. **Highly relevant problem addressed**: The paper tackles a crucial practical challenge in machine translation - adapting to new domains with minimal in-domain data. This is increasingly important as machine translation systems are deployed in new domains (like social media) where labeled data is scarce. The authors effectively motivate this problem by highlighting the limitations of existing approaches that require thousands of in-domain sentences.

2. **Strong experimental results**: The paper provides comprehensive and convincing results across four different language pairs and domains. The semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms all baselines, achieving up to 3.1 BLEU improvement with just 3% of the training data. The ablation study showing effectiveness with as few as 100 in-domain sentences for classifier training is particularly compelling and directly addresses the paper's main problem.

3. **Well-motivated semi-supervised approach**: The authors effectively justify their use of semi-supervised learning by leveraging general-domain data to learn word embeddings, which addresses the key challenge of limited in-domain data. The explanation of why word embeddings help (words with similar meanings cluster together) is particularly strong and well-supported by examples.

4. **Thorough analysis of method behavior**: The paper provides valuable insights into how the method performs with varying amounts of in-domain data (Figure 1), showing that the CNN approach becomes increasingly superior as the in-domain data size decreases. This analysis directly supports the paper's main claim about handling tiny in-domain data.

5. **Practical significance**: The method requires minimal in-domain data (100-6,000 sentences) and can be implemented with standard SMT toolkits, making it immediately applicable to real-world translation systems without requiring major architectural changes.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with modern NMT approaches**: The paper focuses exclusively on SMT, but neural machine translation (NMT) has largely replaced SMT in most production settings. The relevance of the work would be significantly stronger if the authors had demonstrated similar improvements with NMT systems, which are now the standard in the field.

2. **Insufficient detail on computational cost**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational resources required for training the CNN model compared to the baseline LM-based approaches. For a practical system, this is a crucial consideration, especially since the authors claim to be working with "tiny amounts" of data, which might imply resource constraints.

3. **Limited discussion of failure cases**: The paper doesn't analyze cases where the approach fails or discuss specific limitations. For example, it would be valuable to know how the method performs when the in-domain data is very different from the general-domain data or when the domain shift is extreme.

4. **Lack of comparison with more recent text classification methods**: The paper uses CNNs for text classification, but doesn't compare against more recent and potentially superior approaches like transformers or more advanced CNN variants that have been developed since the cited works (2014-2015).

5. **Insufficient hyperparameter details**: The paper mentions tuning meta-parameters but doesn't provide enough detail about the hyperparameter search space or how the optimal parameters were selected. This makes the results less reproducible.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does the computational cost of training and using your CNN classifier compare to the LM-based baselines, especially considering the resource constraints that typically exist when working with "tiny amounts" of in-domain data?

2. Have you considered applying this approach to neural machine translation (NMT) systems, which are now the dominant paradigm in machine translation? How might the approach need to be adapted for NMT?

3. Can you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process for the CNN model, particularly regarding the selection of region size, stride, and the number of neurons?

4. What are the specific limitations of this approach that you've identified through your experiments, and how might they be addressed in future work?

## Overall Assessment

This is a strong paper that addresses a highly relevant problem with a well-motivated and effective solution. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate clear improvements over state-of-the-art approaches for the specific problem of domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data. While the focus on SMT rather than NMT is a limitation in today's landscape, the core idea of using semi-supervised CNNs for data selection with minimal in-domain data remains valuable and could be extended to NMT systems. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the computational cost discussion and provide more details about hyperparameter tuning.
