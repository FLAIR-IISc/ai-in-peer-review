

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper proposes a semi-supervised CNN approach to address the challenge of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation when only very limited in-domain data is available (as few as 100 sentences). The authors use a small amount of in-domain data as positive examples and randomly selected general-domain sentences as negative examples to train a CNN classifier. The model leverages word embeddings learned from large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data, creating a semi-supervised framework. The trained classifier is then used to select relevant training sentences from the general-domain data for building the translation system.

Primary contributions:
1. A novel semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data (100-6000 sentences)
2. Comprehensive experimental validation across four language pairs (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) showing significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) when using only 3% of training data
3. Demonstration that the method works effectively even when no in-domain data is present in the general-domain training data

## Strengths

1. **Clear problem statement and relevance**: The paper effectively identifies a significant practical challenge in machine translation - domain adaptation with minimal in-domain data, particularly for emerging social media domains where labeled data is scarce. This addresses a genuine gap in the literature that is increasingly important as translation systems expand into new application areas.

2. **Strong experimental results**: The results are compelling and well-documented across four different language pairs. The semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) achieves 3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task with only 3% of training data, significantly outperforming baseline methods. The paper demonstrates robustness by showing consistent improvements even with as few as 100 in-domain sentences (2.1 BLEU improvement).

3. **Well-motivated technical approach**: The authors provide a clear explanation of why CNNs are suitable for this task, particularly highlighting how convolutional layers can capture domain-specific features while max-pooling focuses on the most relevant information. The use of word embeddings from general-domain data to handle out-of-domain words is well-justified and addresses a key challenge in the problem.

4. **Stability demonstration**: The authors provide evidence that their method is stable even with very limited in-domain data by repeating the 100-sentence experiment three times, showing a standard deviation of only 0.12 BLEU. This is a significant strength that addresses potential concerns about random sampling effects.

## Weaknesses

1. **Inconsistent definition of "tiny" in-domain data**: The paper claims to address "tiny amounts" of in-domain data but doesn't provide a clear definition of what constitutes "tiny" in relation to the general-domain data. The paper uses 100-6000 in-domain sentences with general-domain data of 9-15M sentences, but the introduction states that "reasonable size in-domain data set, which typically includes several thousands of sentences" is required. This is inconsistent - "several thousands" (which they say is typical) and "a few hundred" (which they claim is "tiny") are not clearly differentiated, making it difficult to assess the true novelty of their approach.

2. **Misrepresentation of prior work**: The paper states "there is no previous work considering the scenario where only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available" (Section 2.1), but this is inaccurate. Moore and Lewis (2010) explicitly mention using "a few hundred sentences" for data selection, and Duh et al. (2013) used 100K sentences which is not "several thousands" as the paper claims. The paper should clarify the exact in-domain data size range they're addressing compared to prior work.

3. **Inconsistent data description**: In Section 5.1, the paper states "the dev and test sets are 'short messages (sms)' for the first task... 'tweets' for the second task; publicly available 'Facebook post' for the remaining two tasks." However, Table 1 shows "test domain" as "sms", "tweets", "facebook", "facebook" for the four tasks. This suggests two tasks use "facebook" as the test domain, but the description claims "the remaining two tasks" use Facebook posts. This inconsistency creates confusion about the experimental setup.

4. **Formatting inconsistency in references**: The paper cites "word2vec (Mikolov et al., 2013)" in the main text but lists the reference with inconsistent formatting (including "Mikolov et al.,? 2013" in the bibliography). This is a minor but noticeable inconsistency in academic writing.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you clarify the exact range of "tiny" in-domain data sizes that your method addresses, and how this compares to the "several thousands" mentioned in the introduction as the typical requirement? What is the smallest size you've tested that still yields significant improvements?

2. Given that Moore and Lewis (2010) used "a few hundred sentences" for data selection, how does your approach differ from theirs in terms of handling such limited data? Could you clarify the specific gap your work fills?

3. Please clarify the test domains for each language pair in Table 1 to resolve the inconsistency between the text description and the table.

This paper presents a valuable approach to a relevant problem with strong experimental results. However, the inconsistent definition of "tiny" data size and misrepresentation of prior work significantly weaken the contribution claims. With these issues addressed, this would be a strong paper for CONLL.
