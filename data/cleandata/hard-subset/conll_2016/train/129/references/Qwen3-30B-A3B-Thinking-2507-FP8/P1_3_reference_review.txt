

# Peer Review: Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the critical challenge of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available (as few as 100 sentences). The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach to classify sentences as in-domain or out-of-domain, using limited in-domain data and leveraging large amounts of general-domain data for pre-training word embeddings. The key contributions, ordered by strength, are:

1. A novel semi-supervised CNN framework for domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences), addressing a significant gap in the literature where prior work requires thousands of in-domain sentences.

2. Comprehensive experimental validation across four language directions (Chinese-English, Arabic-English, English-Spanish, English-Chinese) with social media test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating consistent BLEU improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) over strong baselines, especially when in-domain data is scarce.

3. Insightful analysis explaining why CNNs with pre-trained word embeddings work well for this task, particularly highlighting how word embeddings capture semantic similarities between words (e.g., "Lenovo" being grouped with "Dell" in vector space), enabling better classification even with limited in-domain data.

## Strengths

1. **Significant Practical Impact**: The paper tackles a highly relevant and practical problem in machine translation: adapting to new domains (e.g., social media) where large in-domain datasets are unavailable. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their method works even with as few as 100 in-domain sentences (2.1 BLEU improvement), making it applicable to real-world scenarios where collecting thousands of domain-specific sentences is prohibitively expensive.

2. **Strong Experimental Results**: The results are compelling and well-documented. The authors show consistent improvements across multiple language pairs and domains, with the most impressive results being a 3.1 BLEU gain on the Chinese-to-English SMS task using only 3% of the training data. The ablation study showing performance with decreasing in-domain data (down to 100 sentences) provides particularly convincing evidence of the method's robustness.

3. **Well-Motivated Technical Approach**: The authors provide a clear rationale for why CNNs are suitable for this task, highlighting how convolutional layers capture local domain-specific features and how pre-trained word embeddings help with semantic generalization. The explanation of how the model handles domain-specific words (e.g., "Dell" and "Macbook") versus general words ("I", "have") is particularly strong.

4. **Thorough Baseline Comparison**: The authors compare against multiple strong baselines (all general-domain data, n-gram LM, RNN LM, combined LM) and demonstrate that their semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms all of them, especially when in-domain data is limited. The statistical significance testing adds credibility to the results.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient Technical Detail**: The paper lacks critical implementation details needed for replication. The authors mention "the number of weight vectors or neurons is 500" but don't specify this in the main text. More importantly, they don't explain how they handle variable sentence lengths (a common issue in text classification), which could significantly impact results. The CNN architecture details are insufficient for reproducibility.

2. **Limited Baseline Comparison**: The baseline comparison is limited to methods from Duh et al. (2013), which is now somewhat dated. The paper doesn't compare with more recent state-of-the-art methods for domain adaptation in machine translation, which weakens the claim that their method is "state-of-the-art." A comparison with more recent approaches would strengthen the paper's contribution.

3. **Real-World Applicability Concerns**: The paper uses the development set as in-domain data, but in real-world scenarios, the development set might not be representative of the test domain. The authors don't discuss how to handle situations where no representative development set is available, which is a significant limitation for practical deployment.

4. **Lack of Computational Cost Analysis**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of training the CNN model. For large-scale translation systems, the additional training time for the CNN might be prohibitive, especially when adapting to many different domains. This is a critical practical consideration that should be discussed.

5. **Inconsistent Performance Across Language Pairs**: The paper shows significantly better results for the Chinese-to-English task (3.1 BLEU improvement) compared to the English-to-Chinese task (1.4 BLEU improvement). The authors don't explain this discrepancy, which might indicate limitations of their approach for certain language pairs or domains.

## Questions to Authors

1. Could you provide more details about how you handle variable sentence lengths in the CNN architecture? Specifically, how do you process sentences of different lengths before feeding them into the convolutional layers?

2. Why do you observe such a significant performance gap between the Chinese-to-English and English-to-Chinese tasks? Is this related to language characteristics, data properties, or something else?

3. Could you compare your method with more recent state-of-the-art domain adaptation techniques for machine translation (e.g., those using neural machine translation or more recent data selection methods) to better establish the novelty and significance of your approach?

4. How would you handle the scenario where no representative development set is available for the test domain? Would your method still work effectively, or would it require some adaptation?

## Overall Recommendation

The paper addresses a significant and practical problem in machine translation with a well-motivated technical approach. The experimental results are strong and demonstrate clear improvements over baselines, particularly when in-domain data is limited. However, the lack of sufficient technical details for replication and the limited baseline comparison weaken the paper's contribution. With careful attention to these issues, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with major revisions to address the weaknesses identified above.
