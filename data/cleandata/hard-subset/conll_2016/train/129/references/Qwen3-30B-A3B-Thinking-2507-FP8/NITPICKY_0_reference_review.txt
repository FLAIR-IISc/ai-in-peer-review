

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary
This paper addresses domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only tiny amounts of in-domain data (hundreds of sentences) are available. The authors propose using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to classify training sentence pairs as in-domain or out-of-domain, then select the most relevant training data for the target domain. They augment the model with word embeddings trained on large general-domain data to overcome the limitation of scarce in-domain data. The approach shows significant improvements over baseline methods, especially when in-domain data is limited (e.g., 2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 in-domain sentences).

## Main Contributions (in order of strength)
1. Novel application of semi-supervised CNNs to the problem of domain adaptation with tiny in-domain data (a scenario not addressed by previous work)
2. Comprehensive evaluation across four language directions with various social media test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts)
3. Demonstration of robust performance with extremely limited in-domain data (as low as 100 sentences)

## Strengths
1. **Addressing a meaningful gap in the literature**: The authors correctly identify that previous domain adaptation work assumes reasonable in-domain data sizes (thousands of sentences), while their work tackles a realistic scenario where only hundreds of sentences may be available. This is particularly relevant for emerging social media domains.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper demonstrates clear improvements over baselines, especially with limited in-domain data (2.1 BLEU with 100 sentences). The stability of results across multiple runs (standard deviation of 0.12 BLEU) is well-documented.

3. **Clear explanation of why the approach works**: The authors provide a good explanation for why CNNs are effective for this task, highlighting the ability to capture important domain-specific features through convolution and pooling, and the advantage of word embeddings to handle unseen domain-specific words.

## Weaknesses
1. **Lack of comparison with neural machine translation (NMT) approaches**: The paper focuses exclusively on statistical machine translation (SMT) adaptation, while the field has largely shifted to neural machine translation (NMT). This significantly limits the broader relevance of the work. The authors should discuss how their approach might apply to NMT, which would substantially strengthen the paper.

2. **Inconsistent formatting and minor technical errors**:
   - In the references, there are formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "Axelrod et al.,? \Q2011\E" should be "Axelrod et al., 2011").
   - In Table 2, the BLEU scores for "alldata" are listed as 22.9, 17.6, 26.8, 10.0 for the four tasks, but the paper states "25.7 BLEU on the test set" for the in-domain data only (indata) baseline in the zh2en task. This creates a discrepancy that needs clarification.
   - The paper states "the size of the vector was set to 300" but doesn't consistently specify this in the experiment setup.

3. **Unclear data selection process**: The paper mentions "we classify the source sentence and target sentences separately" but doesn't explain how this affects the selection process. It's unclear how the authors handle cases where source and target sentences have different domain relevance scores.

4. **Inconsistent equation reference**: In Section 3.2, the paper states "Equation 1 is replaced with: σ(W · wl(x) + V · ul(x) + b)" but Equation 1 is defined as "σ(W · wl(x) + b)", creating a logical inconsistency that needs to be resolved.

5. **Lack of computational cost analysis**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational resources required to train the CNN model compared to baseline language model-based approaches. This is a crucial factor for practical deployment that should be addressed.

## Questions to Authors
1. Why did you focus exclusively on SMT adaptation rather than NMT adaptation, given that NMT has largely replaced SMT in the field? How would your approach need to be adapted for NMT?

2. Could you provide more details on the computational resources and time required to train the semi-supervised CNN compared to the baseline LM-based approaches?

3. There's a discrepancy between the BLEU score mentioned in the text (25.7 BLEU for in-domain data only) and the values shown in Table 2. Could you clarify this inconsistency?

## Overall Assessment
While the paper addresses a meaningful problem with a novel approach and demonstrates strong empirical results, several minor but important flaws undermine its quality. The lack of comparison with NMT approaches is particularly concerning given the current state of the field. The formatting inconsistencies and technical errors also detract from the paper's professionalism. While the core idea is valuable, these issues prevent me from recommending acceptance.

I would recommend rejection due to the combination of minor but persistent technical issues and the lack of discussion regarding the shift from SMT to NMT, which significantly limits the paper's relevance to current research in machine translation.
