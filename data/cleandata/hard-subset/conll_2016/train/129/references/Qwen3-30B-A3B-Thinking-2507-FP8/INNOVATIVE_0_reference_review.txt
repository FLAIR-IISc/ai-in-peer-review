

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a critical, often overlooked problem in machine translation: domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) is available. The authors propose a novel semi-supervised CNN approach for domain classification, using a small amount of in-domain data as positive examples and general-domain data as negative examples. The key innovation is leveraging word embeddings learned from large general-domain data to overcome the scarcity of in-domain data.

The main contributions in order of strength:
1. Identifying and solving a practically important problem (domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data) that has been largely ignored in the literature, which typically assumes thousands of in-domain sentences.
2. Proposing a novel application of semi-supervised CNNs to SMT domain adaptation, using general-domain data to learn word embeddings that enhance classification performance with minimal in-domain data.
3. Demonstrating significant empirical improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU points) with only around 3% of training data, and crucially, achieving 2.1 BLEU improvement with just 100 in-domain sentences.

## Strengths

1. **Novel problem setting with significant practical impact**: The paper targets a genuine real-world limitation where in-domain data is scarce (social media translation, topic-specific systems), which is often overlooked in the literature. Previous work assumes thousands of in-domain sentences, but the authors correctly identify that for many applications (e.g., hundreds of topics), collecting tens of thousands of in-domain sentences is prohibitively expensive. This is a bold and important direction that addresses a real pain point for practitioners.

2. **Compelling empirical results with minimal in-domain data**: The results are impressive, showing that with just 100 in-domain sentences (not thousands as previous methods require), the semi-supervised CNN approach achieves a 2.1 BLEU improvement over the baseline. The paper demonstrates this is stable across multiple runs (standard deviation of 0.12), suggesting this isn't just a lucky selection. The 3.1 BLEU improvement on the Chinese-to-English SMS task with 3% of the training data is particularly noteworthy.

3. **Clever application of semi-supervised learning**: The use of word embeddings trained on general-domain data to overcome the limited in-domain data is elegant and directly addresses the core challenge. The paper's explanation in Section 5.4 about how word embeddings help with out-of-vocabulary words (e.g., "Lenovo" being close to "Dell" in vector space) provides a strong intuitive justification for why this approach works.

4. **Clear demonstration of superiority over LM-based methods**: The paper shows that when in-domain data is scarce, the CNN-based approach significantly outperforms existing language model-based methods (ngram, rnnlm, comblm). Figure 1 clearly demonstrates that as in-domain data decreases, the CNN-based methods maintain stable performance while LM-based methods degrade.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited theoretical justification for CNN choice**: While the paper provides intuitive explanations for why CNNs work well (Section 5.4), it lacks a thorough analysis of why CNNs are specifically better suited for this task compared to other classifiers (e.g., SVMs, simple neural networks). A deeper theoretical justification would strengthen the claim that CNNs are the right approach for this problem.

2. **Semi-supervised aspect is not entirely novel**: The semi-supervised CNN framework builds directly on Johnson and Zhang (2015b), which proposed it for text classification. The paper should more clearly articulate the novel aspects of their application to SMT domain adaptation rather than presenting it as a new concept.

3. **No comparison with other classifiers**: The paper compares to LM-based methods but doesn't compare to other text classification approaches that could be applied to this problem (e.g., SVMs with bag-of-words features). A comparison with these would better establish the superiority of the CNN approach.

4. **Computational cost not discussed**: The paper doesn't address the computational cost of training and using the CNN classifier compared to LM-based methods. For practical deployment, this could be a significant concern, especially for large-scale systems.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that "the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) achieved the best performance for all the tasks." Can you provide a more detailed comparison between the semi-supervised CNN and the one-hot CNN, especially regarding why the semi-supervised approach is better? What specific aspects of the word embeddings contribute to the improvement?

2. The paper mentions that word embeddings help with out-of-vocabulary words like "Lenovo" being close to "Dell." Can you provide specific examples from your experiments where this property was critical for domain classification?

3. How does the computational cost (training time and inference time) of the CNN-based approach compare to the LM-based methods? Is this approach feasible for large-scale production systems?

## Overall Recommendation

This paper presents a bold and novel approach to a significant practical problem in machine translation. The authors successfully demonstrate that their semi-supervised CNN approach can achieve substantial improvements in domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences), a scenario that has been largely ignored in the literature. While the paper has some methodological weaknesses (particularly in the theoretical justification for using CNNs), the novelty of the problem setting and the compelling empirical results outweigh these concerns. The work opens up an important new direction for domain adaptation that has direct practical applications in real-world translation systems.

Given the reviewer's preference for novelty over methodological perfection, I strongly recommend acceptance of this paper. It introduces a significant new concept (domain adaptation with tiny amounts of in-domain data) that addresses a genuine gap in the literature, and the results are sufficiently compelling to justify acceptance despite minor methodological shortcomings.
