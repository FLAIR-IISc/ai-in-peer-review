

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach to classify sentences as in-domain or out-of-domain, using the limited in-domain data as positive examples and randomly selected general-domain sentences as negative examples. The main contributions of this work are:

1. A novel method for domain adaptation with tiny amounts of in-domain data (demonstrated down to 100 sentences), addressing a significant gap in the literature where existing approaches typically require thousands of in-domain sentences.
2. Empirical demonstration that semi-supervised CNNs outperform state-of-the-art language model-based data selection methods, especially when in-domain data is scarce, with improvements of up to 3.1 BLEU points.
3. A thorough analysis showing that the method remains stable even with minimal in-domain data, as evidenced by consistent results across multiple random 100-sentence subsets.

## Strengths

1. **Addressing a meaningful and underexplored problem**: The paper tackles a practical challenge in machine translation - adapting to new domains with minimal in-domain data. This is particularly relevant for emerging domains like social media where large in-domain datasets are unavailable. The authors effectively establish the need for their approach by highlighting the limitations of existing methods that require thousands of in-domain sentences.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The experiments across four language pairs (zh2en, ar2en, en2es, en2zh) with different test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts) demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines. The paper provides compelling evidence that the semi-supervised CNN approach significantly outperforms language model-based methods, especially when in-domain data is limited (e.g., 2.1 BLEU improvement with just 100 in-domain sentences).

3. **Methodological simplicity and elegance**: The approach is conceptually straightforward, building on well-established CNN techniques for text classification and semi-supervised learning. The authors effectively leverage pre-trained word embeddings from general-domain data to overcome the limited in-domain data problem, making the approach both practical and theoretically sound.

4. **Comprehensive ablation study**: The paper includes a thorough analysis of how the method performs with varying amounts of in-domain data (from 6,000 down to 100 sentences), demonstrating the stability and robustness of their approach. The repeated experiments with different 100-sentence subsets (with standard deviation of only 0.12) provide strong evidence that the results are not due to random chance.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper doesn't provide a clear theoretical explanation for why CNNs work better than language models for this specific task. The discussion about "convolution capturing important domain information" is somewhat vague, and the paper would benefit from a more rigorous analysis of why the CNN architecture is particularly suited for this domain adaptation problem.

2. **Limited comparison to recent low-resource approaches**: While the paper compares to LM-based methods, it doesn't engage with more recent approaches for low-resource adaptation, such as those using transfer learning, few-shot learning, or meta-learning. This weakens the claim that their method represents a significant advancement over the state of the art.

3. **Practical considerations not addressed**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of training the CNN classifier compared to the LM-based methods. For real-world deployment, this could be a significant factor, especially in resource-constrained environments where the paper claims to be useful.

4. **Development set as in-domain data**: The paper uses the development set as the "in-domain" data, which is not representative of real-world scenarios where the development set may not match the test domain. The authors should clarify how this approach would work in a scenario where no development set is available or where it doesn't match the test domain.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper states that the development set is used as in-domain data, but in practice, the development set may not match the test domain. How would your method work in a scenario where there is no development set or the development set is not representative of the test domain?

2. The paper demonstrates strong results with limited in-domain data, but how does your method scale to multiple domains simultaneously (e.g., adapting to 100 different social media domains)? Would you need to train a separate CNN for each domain?

3. The paper doesn't compare to recent few-shot learning approaches for domain adaptation. How would your method compare to these approaches, especially in terms of data efficiency and performance?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a well-executed study that addresses an important practical problem in machine translation. The empirical results are strong and the method is novel for the specific context of extremely limited in-domain data. However, the lack of theoretical justification and limited comparison to recent low-resource approaches are significant weaknesses that prevent this from being a major contribution. With stronger theoretical grounding and more comprehensive comparisons, this could be a highly valuable paper. As it stands, I recommend *weak acceptance* with the understanding that the authors should address the weaknesses mentioned above in the camera-ready version.

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the literature by demonstrating that domain adaptation is possible with extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences), which is a significant practical advance. The results are robust, and the approach is elegant and easy to implement. With some additional analysis and comparisons, this could be an excellent contribution to the field.
