- Addresses SMT domain adaptation with tiny in-domain data (100–1000 sentences) using a semi-supervised CNN to select relevant general-domain sentences.  
- Leverages word embeddings from large unlabeled general-domain data to train a domain classifier for data selection.  
- Evaluated on four language pairs and social media domains, showing significant BLEU gains (e.g., +2.1 BLEU with 100 sentences).  

- Novel semi-supervised CNN approach enables effective adaptation with only ~100 in-domain sentences versus prior methods needing 30K–100K.  
- Empirically shows substantial improvements over LM-based data selection, up to +3.1 BLEU using only a small fraction of training data.  
- Provides thorough ablation analysis demonstrating robustness to very small in-domain data sizes.  

- Tackles a timely, meaningful problem for rapidly emerging domains like social media where in-domain data is scarce.  
- Presents compelling and thorough empirical results that clearly demonstrate the method’s core contribution.  
- Offers intuitive explanation for effectiveness: embedding-space clustering lets CNN recognize unseen domain-specific words.  

- Limited relevance to current NMT-dominated practice; no experiments or discussion on applying method to NMT.  
- Insufficient analysis of what features the CNN learns or comparisons/visualizations vs. LM-based features.  
- No discussion of computational costs or efficiency relative to baselines.  
- Lacks exploration of failure cases or examples of misclassification and domain limitations.  

- How would the approach extend to NMT systems—would the CNN-based selection need modification?  
- How sensitive is performance to the quality/source of word embeddings (e.g., different corpora)?  
- How would the method perform on formal domains (legal, medical) with vocabularies far from general-domain data?  
- What is the impact of different positive:negative sampling ratios (e.g., 1:5, 1:10) for CNN training?  

- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions; main weaknesses are addressable and not deal-breakers.
