

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary
This paper proposes using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) to select domain-relevant training data for statistical machine translation when only tiny amounts of in-domain data are available. The authors claim their method significantly outperforms existing language model-based approaches, achieving up to 3.1 BLEU improvement with only 3% of training data and as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

## Main Contributions
1. A semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation in translation with very limited in-domain data (though not entirely novel)
2. Empirical evidence that their method works with as few as 100 in-domain sentences
3. Comparison showing their method outperforms traditional language model-based approaches

## Strengths
1. The paper addresses a genuine practical problem: domain adaptation when in-domain data is scarce, which is increasingly relevant with the rise of social media translation tasks.

2. The experimental results are generally consistent across multiple language pairs and domains (Chinese-English SMS, Arabic-English tweets, etc.), with the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) achieving the best results in all cases.

3. The paper demonstrates that their method can work with extremely limited in-domain data (100 sentences), which is a significant improvement over previous approaches requiring thousands of sentences (as cited in Axelrod et al., 2011; Duh et al., 2013).

## Weaknesses

1. **Flawed Problem Setup**: The authors use the development set as their "in-domain data," which is problematic. The development set is typically a carefully selected small sample designed to be representative of the test domain, not truly limited in-domain data. This undermines their key claim about working with "tiny amounts of in-domain data" that would realistically be available. If the development set is used as the in-domain sample, the paper isn't demonstrating adaptation with truly scarce data but with a representative sample.

2. **Lack of Novelty**: The CNN approach for text classification is well-established (Kim, 2014; Johnson and Zhang, 2015b), and the semi-supervised aspect using word embeddings from unlabeled data is a standard technique. The paper doesn't clearly differentiate their approach from prior work, making it difficult to assess what's truly new.

3. **Insufficient Validation**: The paper doesn't provide evidence that their method would work with truly random, non-representative limited in-domain data. The fact that they used the development set (which is designed to be representative) as the in-domain data means they haven't tested their method on the actual scenario they claim to solve. The 100-sentence experiments are based on sampling from the development set, not from a truly limited in-domain source.

4. **Lack of Ablation Studies**: The authors claim word embeddings from general-domain data are key to their success, but don't provide ablation studies to verify this. Without showing that the semi-supervised aspect contributes meaningfully to performance, the claim of "semi-supervised" being crucial is unsubstantiated.

5. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper doesn't compare against more recent approaches to domain adaptation with limited data (post-2015), which might have addressed similar challenges more effectively. The baselines (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) are from 2013, and the paper doesn't acknowledge more recent methods that might have improved upon these.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your method perform if the "in-domain" data was a random 100 sentences from the target domain, rather than the development set (which is a representative sample)?

2. Can you provide ablation studies showing the contribution of each component of your semi-supervised CNN approach, particularly the role of the general-domain word embeddings?

3. How does your method compare to more recent approaches to domain adaptation with limited data (post-2015), such as those using few-shot learning techniques?

## Recommendation

While the paper addresses a relevant problem and demonstrates reasonable results, the key claim about working with "tiny amounts of in-domain data" is undermined by the experimental setup that uses the development set as the in-domain data. The method is not sufficiently novel compared to established CNN approaches for text classification, and the paper lacks proper validation for its core claim. The paper would benefit from a more realistic evaluation with truly limited in-domain data (not a representative development set), stronger ablation studies, and comparison to more recent domain adaptation approaches.

The paper presents a practical approach but fails to provide convincing evidence for its key claim about working with extremely limited in-domain data. Without addressing these methodological concerns, the contribution is overstated. I recommend rejection, as the evidence does not sufficiently support the claims made about the effectiveness of the approach with truly tiny amounts of in-domain data.
