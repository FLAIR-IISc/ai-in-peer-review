

# Peer Review: Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses a critical challenge in statistical machine translation (SMT): domain adaptation when only extremely limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentences) is available. The authors propose a novel semi-supervised CNN approach for domain classification that leverages word embeddings learned from large general-domain data to overcome the scarcity of in-domain samples. The primary contribution is a method that can effectively select in-domain training data for SMT adaptation with minimal in-domain supervision, which is particularly valuable for emerging domains like social media where labeled data is scarce. The secondary contribution is empirical validation across four language pairs and three social media domains showing consistent improvements over LM-based baselines, especially when in-domain data is limited. The tertiary contribution is demonstrating that the method can achieve significant gains (up to 3.1 BLEU points) with only 3% of the training data.

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a meaningful and underexplored problem**: The paper correctly identifies a significant gap in the literature—domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data (100-1000 sentences) is rarely addressed, yet this scenario is increasingly common with the proliferation of new social media domains. The authors' focus on this practically relevant challenge is a major strength, as existing work typically assumes at least thousands of in-domain sentences.

2. **Strong empirical results with clear demonstration of the core contribution**: The paper provides compelling evidence that their semi-supervised CNN approach outperforms LM-based methods, especially when in-domain data is scarce (Figure 1). The 2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 in-domain sentences is particularly impressive, and the stability across three independent runs (std. dev. 0.12) demonstrates the robustness of their approach.

3. **Novel semi-supervised framework for data selection**: The clever use of word embeddings from large general-domain data to augment the limited in-domain samples is a well-motivated approach that effectively leverages the abundance of unlabeled data. This is a significant technical contribution that could be applied beyond translation to other NLP tasks with limited labeled data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Lack of comparison with alternative classification methods**: The paper compares against LM-based approaches but doesn't compare their CNN approach to other text classification methods (e.g., SVMs, RNNs, or even simpler feature-based classifiers). This makes it difficult to determine whether the CNN architecture itself is essential or if the key innovation is the semi-supervised embedding approach. For instance, a feature-based classifier using the same word embeddings might achieve similar results with less computational overhead.

2. **Insufficient analysis of why CNN works better**: While the authors provide a brief explanation about CNN's ability to capture local features and the utility of word embeddings, they don't conduct any ablation studies to isolate the specific components that contribute to their method's success. For example, how much of the improvement comes from the CNN architecture versus the semi-supervised embeddings? This lack of analysis weakens the claim that CNNs are particularly well-suited for this task.

3. **Limited discussion of computational cost**: The paper doesn't address the computational complexity of training the CNN model compared to LM-based approaches. For practical deployment, especially with many different domains, the training time and resources required for the CNN might be prohibitive compared to simpler LM-based methods. This is a significant oversight for a paper targeting real-world application.

4. **Lack of connection to modern NMT systems**: The paper focuses exclusively on SMT, but neural machine translation (NMT) has largely replaced SMT in most applications. The authors don't discuss whether their approach would work for NMT, which is a major limitation given the field's current direction. The method might not be directly transferable due to NMT's different architecture and data requirements.

## Questions to Authors

1. How would your semi-supervised CNN approach perform compared to a simple feature-based classifier (e.g., SVM) using the same word embeddings as features? This would help isolate whether the CNN architecture is truly necessary or if the key innovation is the embedding-based semi-supervised learning.

2. Have you considered the computational cost of your approach for large-scale deployment across multiple domains? How does the training time for your CNN compare to training LM-based approaches, especially given that you're using a large general-domain corpus for word embeddings?

3. Given that the field has largely moved to NMT, have you considered applying this approach to NMT systems, or do you believe it would be less effective due to NMT's different architecture? If so, what modifications would be needed?

## Overall Recommendation

This is a strong paper that addresses an important practical problem with compelling empirical results. The core contribution of using semi-supervised CNNs for domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data is novel and valuable for real-world translation systems. While the paper would benefit from addressing the weaknesses noted above (particularly the comparison to other classification methods and computational cost analysis), the work makes a meaningful contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
