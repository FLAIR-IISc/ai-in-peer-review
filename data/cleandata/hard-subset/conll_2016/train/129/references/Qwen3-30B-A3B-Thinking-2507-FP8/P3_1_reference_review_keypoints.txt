- Paper proposes semi-supervised CNNs to select in-domain training sentence pairs for SMT when only tiny in-domain data (e.g., ~100 sentences) is available.
- Method uses a small labeled in-domain set plus word embeddings from large unlabeled general-domain data to train the CNN and select relevant general-domain data.
- Main contribution: novel use of semi-supervised CNNs for domain adaptation in extremely low in-domain data regimes.
- Contribution: empirical demonstration that the approach yields significant BLEU gains (e.g., +2.1 BLEU with 100 sentences; up to +3.1 BLEU on zh→en SMS) using very little data.
- Contribution: systematic comparison showing the semi-supervised CNN outperforms n-gram, RNN, and combined LM-based data selection methods.
- Strength: tackles a practical real-world problem (adapting MT to new domains like social media with very limited data).
- Strength: strong, well-documented empirical results across multiple languages and domains.
- Strength: clear rationale that CNNs capture local domain features and embeddings help with semantic generalization/OOV handling.
- Strength: comprehensive evaluation across 4 language pairs and several domains plus ablation over in-domain data sizes.
- Strength: provides practical implementation details (architecture, hyperparameters, selection process) for reproducibility.
- Weakness: evaluation focuses only on phrase-based SMT, ignoring modern NMT systems and limiting contemporary relevance.
- Weakness: insufficient ablation to isolate the semi-supervised component versus CNN architecture (e.g., comparisons with supervised CNN + pre-trained or random embeddings).
- Weakness: lacks theoretical analysis explaining why semi-supervised CNNs outperform LM-based methods in low-data regimes.
- Weakness: limited discussion of failure cases and robustness (no analysis for noisy/low-quality in-domain data, domain divergence, or general-domain data quality).
- Weakness: potential overfitting concerns with tiny in-domain samples; variance analysis across more samples and smaller sizes is limited.
- Question: How would the approach be adapted to neural machine translation (NMT) systems?
- Question: How sensitive is the method to the quality and representativeness of the general-domain data used for embeddings?
- Question: Can the authors provide detailed analysis separating the contributions of embeddings versus CNN architecture?
- Question: Have the authors considered comparisons to few-shot/meta-learning approaches in NLP?
- Question: What's the practical lower bound (e.g., performance at 50 in-domain sentences) and how does performance degrade with fewer samples?
- Overall: Valuable practical contribution with strong empirical evidence, but needs revisions to address NMT relevance and deeper analysis of the semi-supervised component.
- Recommendation: Strong accept (rating 7), contingent on addressing NMT comparison and more detailed component analyses.
