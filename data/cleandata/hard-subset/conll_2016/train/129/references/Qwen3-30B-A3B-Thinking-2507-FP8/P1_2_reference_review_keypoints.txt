- Addresses domain adaptation for SMT when only tiny in-domain data (100s of sentences) is available, using semi-supervised CNNs to select relevant general-domain sentences.
- Proposes training a CNN classifier with few in-domain positives and random general-domain negatives, augmented with word2vec embeddings, to select top N sentences for SMT training.
- Contribution: Novel semi-supervised CNN approach for data selection that outperforms LM-based methods with very limited in-domain data.
- Contribution: Demonstrates up to 3.1 BLEU improvement and maintains gains even with only 100 in-domain sentences.
- Contribution: Shows effectiveness even when general-domain corpus contains no explicit in-domain subset.
- Strength: Tackles a significant practical problem with strong empirical evidence across multiple languages and social-media domains.
- Strength: Comprehensive experiments and ablations, including stability checks and comparisons to one-hot CNN and LM baselines.
- Strength: Clear intuition that CNNs capture local domain features and embeddings help generalize to unseen domain words.
- Strength: High practical utility for rapid adaptation in low-resource, topic-specific MT scenarios.
- Weakness: Limited novelty—the core CNN text-classification approach is not new and lacks clear distinction from other semi-supervised CNN work.
- Weakness: No comparison with other semi-supervised learning methods (e.g., self-training, co-training), leaving relative value unclear.
- Weakness: Evaluation relies solely on BLEU; lacks additional metrics or human evaluation to assess fluency/adequacy.
- Weakness: Insufficient implementation details (CNN hyperparameters, embedding preprocessing, vocab size, regularization) for reproducibility.
- Weakness: Theoretical justification is limited; lacks deeper analysis of how in-domain data size relates to semi-supervised performance.
- Question: How do training time and computational costs of the CNN compare to LM-based methods for large-scale systems?
- Question: How to obtain representative in-domain data when the dev set is not representative, and how robust is the method to dev/test mismatch?
- Question: How does performance change with even fewer in-domain sentences (e.g., 50); is there a minimum effective threshold?
- Question: How would the CNN-based selection interact with other adaptation techniques (mixture models, vector-space adaptation)?
- Question: Is there a principled way to choose the number of selected sentences (300K used), or must this be tuned empirically?
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions—strong empirical results and practical value, but address novelty, comparisons to other semi-supervised methods, and reproducibility details.
