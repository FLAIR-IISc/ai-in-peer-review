- Paper addresses SMT domain adaptation with only tiny amounts of in-domain data using semi-supervised CNNs to select relevant general-domain sentences.
- Method trains CNNs with few in-domain positives and random general-domain negatives, using word2vec embeddings as extra input.
- Evaluated on four language pairs and social-media domains, showing up to 3.1 BLEU improvement and gains with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.
- Main contribution: practical solution for domain adaptation when only very small in-domain corpora are available.
- Main contribution: demonstrates substantial BLEU improvements even with only ~100 in-domain sentence pairs.
- Main contribution: well-motivated semi-supervised use of large general-domain word embeddings to compensate for limited in-domain data.
- Strength: addresses an underexplored, real-world problem relevant to social media and emerging domains.
- Strength: strong, consistent empirical results across languages and domains with low variance across samples.
- Strength: method is simple to implement and integrates with existing SMT pipelines; implementation details are provided.
- Strength: thorough analysis of performance versus amount of in-domain data, showing CNN advantage as in-domain data decreases.
- Strength: effective use of word embeddings to capture semantic similarity and handle OOV issues.
- Weakness: limited novelty — CNN text classification and semi-supervised ideas are already established in prior work.
- Weakness: methodological flaw using the development set as in-domain training data, risking overfitting and optimistic results.
- Weakness: lacks ablation studies to isolate contributions of CNN architecture, semi-supervised design, and embeddings.
- Weakness: limited comparisons to recent neural domain-adaptation or NMT approaches, reducing relevance to current MT trends.
- Weakness: insufficient analysis of failure modes and types of domain mismatch that challenge the method.
- Question: how is using the dev set as in-domain training data justified and how would results change with a separate in-domain training set?
- Question: which components (CNN vs. semi-supervised vs. embeddings) drive the improvements—can you provide ablation results?
- Question: how does this method compare to state-of-the-art NMT domain adaptation, and is it applicable to NMT systems?
- Question: what is the minimum number of in-domain sentences needed before performance degrades, and where is the threshold?
- Question: how much of the sscnn vs. ohcnn gain (~0.5 BLEU) is attributable to embeddings versus other semi-supervised choices?
- Additional comment: paper is well-written and practically valuable but must address the dev-set training flaw.
- Additional comment: stronger comparison with NMT methods and a detailed discussion of limitations would improve the paper.
