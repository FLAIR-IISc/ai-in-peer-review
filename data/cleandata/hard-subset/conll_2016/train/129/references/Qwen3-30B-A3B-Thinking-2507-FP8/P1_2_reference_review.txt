

# Peer Review

## Summary
This paper addresses a practical challenge in statistical machine translation (SMT): domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (e.g., hundreds of sentence pairs) is available. The authors propose using semi-supervised convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for domain classification to select the most relevant training data from a large general-domain corpus. The core idea is to use a small amount of in-domain data (e.g., development set) as positive examples and randomly selected general-domain sentences as negative examples to train a CNN classifier. To overcome the limitation of small in-domain data, they augment the model with word embeddings trained from large general-domain data via word2vec, creating a semi-supervised framework. The trained CNN is then used to classify sentences in the general-domain data, and the top N sentences are selected to train the SMT system.

## Main Contributions
1. A novel semi-supervised CNN approach for domain adaptation in SMT with very limited in-domain data (100-6000 sentences), which outperforms existing LM-based data selection methods.
2. Demonstrating that the method can achieve significant BLEU improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) even with only 100 in-domain sentences.
3. Showing that the method works well even when there's no in-domain data component within the general-domain data (i.e., no "in-domain subset" within the general data).

## Strengths

1. **Addresses a significant practical problem with strong empirical evidence**: The paper tackles a real-world problem that is increasingly important as social media translation becomes more common (e.g., for short messages, tweets, Facebook posts where in-domain data is scarce). The authors provide compelling evidence through experiments across four different language directions and test domains. The results show that their semi-supervised CNN approach significantly outperforms LM-based baselines, especially with limited in-domain data. For example, they achieve 3.1 BLEU improvement with only ~3% of the training data on the Chinese-to-English SMS task, and maintain a 2.1 BLEU improvement even when using just 100 in-domain sentences for training the classifier. This is a substantial practical contribution for real-world MT systems.

2. **Comprehensive experimental evaluation**: The paper evaluates on four different language pairs with diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating the method's generalizability. The ablation studies showing the superiority of the semi-supervised CNN over the one-hot CNN variant (with 0.5 BLEU point improvement on average) and the analysis of performance with varying amounts of in-domain data (down to 100 sentences) are particularly strong. The stability analysis (repeating the 100-in-domain-sentence experiment three times with a standard deviation of only 0.12) provides strong evidence that the method is reliable even with very limited training data for the classifier.

3. **Clear theoretical justification for the approach**: The authors provide a well-reasoned explanation for why CNNs with word embeddings work well for this specific problem. They correctly identify that convolution captures local domain-specific features (e.g., "Dell," "laptop" in a computer domain sentence) and that word embeddings help with out-of-vocabulary words by grouping semantically similar words together in vector space. This is particularly valuable for fast or fine-grained adaptation where obtaining large amounts of in-domain data is impractical. Their explanation of how "Lenovo" would be correctly classified as computer-domain despite not being in the labeled data is especially convincing.

4. **Practical significance for real-world applications**: The paper makes a strong case for the practical utility of the approach in scenarios where collecting large amounts of in-domain data is prohibitively expensive (e.g., building a large-scale topic-specific MT system with hundreds of topics). The ability to work with just 100 in-domain sentences makes this approach highly applicable to emerging domains where in-domain data is scarce, which is increasingly common in today's social media landscape.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty of the core method**: While the application of CNNs to domain adaptation in MT is novel for this specific problem, the core CNN approach for text classification is not new (as the authors themselves acknowledge in Section 2.2). The semi-supervised aspect is mentioned, but the paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish how their specific implementation differs from recent semi-supervised CNN work in NLP. For example, they could have compared with other semi-supervised approaches like self-training or co-training that could be applied to this problem. This makes it difficult to assess whether the semi-supervised CNN is truly the best approach or if other methods could achieve similar results.

2. **Lack of comparison with other semi-supervised learning methods**: The paper only compares with LM-based data selection methods (ngram, rnnlm, comblm), but doesn't compare with other semi-supervised learning approaches that could be applied to this problem (e.g., self-training with the CNN model itself). This is a significant omission because it's unclear whether the semi-supervised CNN approach is necessary or if a simpler semi-supervised method would suffice. Without this comparison, it's hard to assess the true value of their specific approach.

3. **Limited evaluation metrics**: The paper only uses BLEU as the evaluation metric, which is standard for MT but known to have limitations. Including additional metrics like TER, METEOR, or even human evaluation would provide a more comprehensive assessment of translation quality. For example, BLEU might not capture important aspects of translation quality like fluency or adequacy, which could be particularly important for social media translation where style and tone matter.

4. **Insufficient implementation details**: Some key implementation details are missing that would help reproduce the work. The paper doesn't provide the exact parameters for the CNN architecture (e.g., number of layers, filter sizes, number of filters), and the description of the word embedding process is somewhat vague. For example, it's unclear how the word embeddings were preprocessed, what the exact vocabulary size was, or what regularization techniques were used. These details would be valuable for reproducibility and understanding the model's capacity.

5. **Theoretical justification is limited**: While the authors provide some explanation for why CNNs work well, they don't provide a deeper theoretical analysis of why their semi-supervised approach is effective for this specific problem. A more rigorous analysis of the relationship between the size of the in-domain data and the performance of the semi-supervised model would strengthen the paper.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims that the semi-supervised CNN outperforms LM-based methods, especially with limited in-domain data. How does the training time and computational cost of the CNN approach compare to the LM-based methods? For large-scale systems, this could be an important consideration.

2. The paper uses the dev set as in-domain data. In practice, how would this method work when the dev set is not representative of the test domain? What would be the recommended approach for obtaining a small amount of in-domain data for training the classifier when no dev set is available?

3. The paper shows that the method works with as little as 100 in-domain sentences. How does the performance degrade when the in-domain data is even smaller (e.g., 50 sentences)? Is there a minimum threshold below which the method starts to fail?

4. The paper doesn't discuss how the CNN-based data selection method might interact with other adaptation techniques (e.g., mixture model adaptation, vector space model adaptation). Would combining the CNN-based selection with these other techniques yield further improvements?

5. The paper uses a fixed number of selected sentences (300K) across all methods. Is there a principled way to determine the optimal number of sentences to select, or is this determined empirically through held-out data? How would this change if the general-domain data size was different?

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend this paper for acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses a practical problem with strong empirical results, and the contributions are significant for real-world machine translation applications. The main weaknesses are the limited novelty of the core method and the lack of comparison with other semi-supervised approaches, but these can be addressed with minor revisions. The experimental results are compelling, and the method has clear practical value for scenarios where in-domain data is scarce.
