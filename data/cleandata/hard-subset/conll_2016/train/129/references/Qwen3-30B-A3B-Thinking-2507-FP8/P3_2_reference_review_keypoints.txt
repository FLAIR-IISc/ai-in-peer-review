- Paper problem: Domain adaptation for SMT when only a tiny amount (e.g., ~100) of in-domain sentence pairs is available.  
- Proposed method: Semi-supervised CNN classifier using few in-domain positives, random general-domain negatives, and word embeddings from large unlabeled data to select relevant general-domain sentences.  
- Empirical result: Significant BLEU improvements on four language pairs and social-media domains, up to +3.1 BLEU and +2.1 BLEU with just 100 in-domain sentences.  

- Contribution 1: Novel semi-supervised CNN approach for data selection in extreme low-resource in-domain settings.  
- Contribution 2: Demonstrates effectiveness of adaptation with extremely limited labeled in-domain data.  
- Contribution 3: Practical integration of pretrained word embeddings to compensate for scarce labeled data.  

- Strength 1: Addresses a highly relevant, practical problem for emerging domains like social media.  
- Strength 2: Strong empirical results that clearly demonstrate the core innovation, especially with 100 sentences.  
- Strength 3: Stability analysis shows low variance across repeated runs with tiny in-domain samples.  
- Strength 4: Methodology and experimental details are sufficiently clear for reproducibility.  
- Strength 5: Appropriate and effective comparison against LM-based selection baselines.  

- Weakness 1: Lack of comparison to modern NMT adaptation approaches limits relevance to current practice.  
- Weakness 2: Insufficient analysis of why the semi-supervised CNN outperforms alternatives (no deep ablation/error analysis).  
- Weakness 3: Possible data leakage for zh→en SMS task due to a 120K in-domain subset within the general corpus requires clarification.  
- Weakness 4: No discussion of computational cost of CNN selection compared to cheaper LM-based methods at large scale.  
- Weakness 5: No qualitative examples showing what sentences are selected and why the CNN is better.  

- Question 1: How would the method scale or adapt to NMT systems and their data-selection needs?  
- Question 2: Was the 120K in-domain subset in zh→en used in training the general-domain data, and does that inflate results?  
- Question 3: Can you provide deeper analysis or ablations explaining why sscnn outperforms ohcnn and LM methods?  
- Question 4: What is the computational cost of training and applying the CNN on 9–15M sentence corpora compared to LM selection?  
- Question 5: How does the method perform when the dev set is not representative of the test domain—does it require collecting small true in-domain data?  

- Overall assessment: Well-executed, practically important study with compelling results for SMT domain adaptation under extreme low-resource conditions.  
- Main limitation: Focus on SMT rather than NMT; recommend addressing NMT relevance and clarifying the potential data leakage issue.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions to address NMT relevance, data-leakage clarification, and additional analysis.
