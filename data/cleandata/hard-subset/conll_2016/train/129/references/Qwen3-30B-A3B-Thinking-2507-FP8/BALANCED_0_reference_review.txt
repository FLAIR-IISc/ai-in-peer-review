

# Review of "Semi-supervised Convolutional Networks for Translation Adaptation with Tiny Amount of In-domain Data"

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the challenging problem of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation when only very limited in-domain data (as few as 100 sentence pairs) is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised CNN approach for data selection, where the CNN is trained with a small amount of in-domain data as positive samples and randomly selected general-domain data as negative samples. The key innovation is using word embeddings learned from large amounts of general-domain data (via word2vec) to augment the CNN, making it effective even with minimal labeled data.

Main contributions (in order of strength):
1. A practical solution for domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data (down to 100 sentences), a scenario not adequately addressed by previous work
2. Demonstrated significant improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) over standard data selection methods, especially when in-domain data is scarce
3. Comprehensive empirical validation across four language pairs and three distinct social media domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts)

## Strengths

1. **Relevant and well-motivated problem**: The paper identifies a genuine practical challenge in machine translation - adapting to new domains with minimal resources. This is increasingly important with the rise of social media and specialized translation needs. The authors clearly articulate why traditional data selection methods fail when in-domain data is limited.

2. **Strong empirical results**: The paper provides compelling evidence that the proposed method outperforms LM-based baselines, especially with scarce in-domain data. The most convincing evidence is Figure 1, which demonstrates that when in-domain data drops below 800 sentences, the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) maintains stable performance while LM-based methods degrade significantly. The 2.1 BLEU improvement with only 100 in-domain sentences is particularly impressive.

3. **Clear explanation of why the approach works**: The authors provide a well-reasoned explanation for the method's success, highlighting two key aspects: 1) CNN's ability to focus on domain-specific words through convolution and pooling, and 2) the advantage of word embeddings capturing semantic relationships (e.g., "Lenovo" being close to "Dell" in vector space), which helps handle out-of-vocabulary words.

4. **Thorough experimental design**: The paper evaluates across four different language pairs with diverse test domains, providing strong evidence of the approach's generalizability. The ablation study on varying amounts of in-domain data is particularly valuable.

## Weaknesses

1. **Limited novelty**: While the application to domain adaptation is useful, the core CNN approach (especially the semi-supervised component) builds directly on Johnson and Zhang (2015b), which was already applied to text classification. The paper doesn't clearly articulate how this specific application advances the state-of-the-art in CNNs for text classification or domain adaptation.

2. **Incomplete comparison with recent approaches**: The paper compares only with LM-based data selection methods (ngram, rnnlm, comblm) but doesn't compare with more recent neural approaches to domain adaptation, such as those using neural machine translation (NMT) or transfer learning techniques. This makes it difficult to assess whether the approach is truly state-of-the-art.

3. **Insufficient discussion of limitations**: The paper focuses on positive results but doesn't address potential failure cases. For example, how would the method perform with highly noisy in-domain data? What if the in-domain data is not representative of the test domain? The authors claim "even if we have no domain knowledge about the training data," but don't provide evidence for this claim.

4. **Vague description of "semi-supervised" aspect**: The paper uses the term "semi-supervised" but doesn't clearly distinguish how their approach differs from other semi-supervised methods or why it's particularly suited to this problem. The description in Section 3.2 could be more precise.

## Questions to Authors

1. How does your method compare with more recent neural domain adaptation techniques (e.g., those using transfer learning or fine-tuning of NMT models) that have emerged since the cited works?

2. The paper mentions that "when the in-domain set is reduced to 100 sentence pairs, the LM-based methods only get a small improvement over the baseline, while the 'ohcnn' got a 1.2 BLEU score improvement." How much of this improvement is due to the specific 100 sentences chosen versus the method itself? Could you provide results with different random seeds for the 100 in-domain sentences?

3. The paper states "even if we have no domain knowledge about the training data," but the training data selection implicitly assumes some relationship between the general-domain data and the test domain. How would your method perform if the general-domain data was completely unrelated to the test domain (e.g., training on news data for a medical translation task)?

## Recommendation

This paper addresses an important practical problem in machine translation with a well-designed approach that shows promising results, particularly for the challenging scenario of extremely limited in-domain data. The experimental validation is strong and the results are significant.

However, the novelty is somewhat limited as the core technical approach builds directly on existing work in CNN text classification, and the comparison with more recent domain adaptation methods is insufficient. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of how this work advances the field beyond the direct application of existing techniques.

**Decision: Weak Accept**

This is a strong paper with practical value, but the novelty is limited and the comparison with state-of-the-art methods is incomplete. With revisions addressing the novelty claim, comparison with more recent approaches, and discussion of limitations, this could be an excellent contribution. The authors should explicitly compare with more recent neural domain adaptation techniques and clarify how their semi-supervised CNN approach advances the state-of-the-art in CNNs for text classification.
