

# Review for CONLL

## Summary and Main Contributions

This paper addresses the problem of domain adaptation in statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. The authors propose a semi-supervised convolutional neural network (CNN) approach that leverages large amounts of unlabeled general-domain data through word embeddings to compensate for limited labeled in-domain data. The CNN is trained to classify sentences as in-domain or out-of-domain, then used to select relevant sentences from the general-domain data for training the SMT system.

Main contributions (ranked by strength):
1. **Addressing a critical practical problem**: The paper tackles the real-world challenge of domain adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data (just a few hundred sentences), which is common in new domains like social media where collecting thousands of in-domain sentences is prohibitively expensive.
2. **Novel semi-supervised CNN framework**: The authors propose a practical semi-supervised approach that effectively uses general-domain data through word embeddings to compensate for limited in-domain data, enabling domain adaptation without requiring large in-domain samples.
3. **Strong empirical validation**: The paper provides comprehensive experiments across four language directions with different test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts), demonstrating consistent improvements (up to 3.1 BLEU) over baseline methods, especially when in-domain data is limited (down to 100 sentences).

## Strengths

1. **Practical relevance and significance**: The problem of adapting to new domains with limited in-domain data is highly relevant to real-world applications, particularly with the growth of social media and other emerging domains where data collection is challenging. The paper demonstrates that their method works effectively even with as few as 100 in-domain sentences (2.1 BLEU improvement), which is a significant practical contribution. This addresses a genuine limitation in current domain adaptation techniques that typically require thousands of in-domain sentences.

2. **Comprehensive and rigorous evaluation**: The authors conduct thorough experiments across four different language directions (Chinese-English, Arabic-English, English-Spanish, English-Chinese) with diverse test domains (SMS, tweets, Facebook posts). They compare against four strong baselines (including the state-of-the-art LM-based methods from Duh et al., 2013) and provide detailed analysis of how their method performs across different amounts of in-domain data. The results consistently show that their semi-supervised CNN approach outperforms baselines, especially when in-domain data is limited.

3. **Strong evidence of stability and reliability**: The authors demonstrate that their approach is stable even with very limited in-domain data. When using just 100 in-domain sentences, they report a 2.1 BLEU improvement over the baseline, and they repeat the experiment three times to show that the results are consistent (standard deviation of 0.12). This provides strong evidence that their method is not just a lucky coincidence but reliably effective.

4. **Clear demonstration of the value of semi-supervision**: The paper effectively shows how semi-supervised learning (using word embeddings from general-domain data) significantly improves performance over purely supervised approaches. The semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms the supervised one-hot CNN (ohcnn) by about 0.5 BLEU points on average, and achieves the best results across all tasks. This clearly demonstrates the value of leveraging unlabeled general-domain data.

## Weaknesses

1. **Insufficient novelty justification**: The paper doesn't clearly establish the novelty of their approach. While they build on existing work on CNNs for text classification (Johnson and Zhang, 2015b), they don't sufficiently explain why their specific semi-supervised CNN approach is novel or why it's particularly suited for domain adaptation in SMT. The core idea (using CNNs for domain classification) is not new, and the paper doesn't provide a compelling argument for why CNNs are superior to other classification approaches for this specific task.

2. **Inconsistent definition of "tiny" in-domain data**: The paper claims to address scenarios with "tiny amounts" of in-domain data (e.g., "just a few hundred sentences"), but they use the development set (6,016 sentences for zh2en task) as in-domain data. This is not "tiny" as claimed—development sets are typically 1,000-10,000 sentences, not "a few hundred." This undermines the paper's main claim about addressing the scenario with extremely limited data. The actual experiments with "tiny" data (100 sentences) use a small subset of the development set, not truly tiny data.

3. **Lack of comparison with neural machine translation (NMT) approaches**: Given that neural machine translation (NMT) has largely replaced SMT as the dominant paradigm in machine translation, the paper would be significantly stronger if it compared against NMT-based domain adaptation methods or discussed the applicability of their approach to NMT. The paper focuses solely on SMT, which limits the broader impact of their work.

4. **Insufficient analysis of why semi-supervised CNN works better**: While the paper states that the semi-supervised CNN outperforms the supervised version, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of *why* this is the case. They mention word embeddings as a reason but don't provide a thorough analysis of how the embeddings specifically contribute to the performance difference. A deeper analysis of how the word embeddings capture domain-relevant features would strengthen the paper.

5. **Limited discussion of failure cases and limitations**: The paper doesn't discuss potential limitations or failure cases of their approach. For example, how does the method perform when the test domain is very different from the general-domain data? Or when the in-domain data is of very poor quality? Understanding these limitations would help readers better assess the applicability of the method.

## Questions to Authors

1. The paper claims to address scenarios with "tiny amounts" of in-domain data (e.g., "just a few hundred sentences"), but uses the development set (6,016 sentences for zh2en) as in-domain data. Is 6,016 sentences considered "tiny" in the context of this paper? If so, why is it considered tiny when the paper claims to handle just "a few hundred" sentences?

2. The paper doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why the semi-supervised CNN works better than the supervised one-hot CNN. Could the authors provide more insight into how the word embeddings specifically contribute to the performance difference, beyond just stating that words with similar meanings are grouped together in the vector space?

3. How would this approach generalize to neural machine translation (NMT) systems, which are now the dominant paradigm in machine translation? Would the same CNN-based data selection approach be applicable, or would it need significant modification? If they've tried it with NMT, what were the results?

4. The paper doesn't discuss the computational cost of training the CNN model. How long does it take to train the CNN on the general-domain data, and how does this compare to the time required to collect additional in-domain data? This would help assess the practicality of the approach.

## Overall Recommendation

I recommend accepting this paper for CONLL. The problem addressed is highly relevant to real-world machine translation applications, and the proposed approach demonstrates strong empirical results, particularly for the scenario with extremely limited in-domain data. While there are some weaknesses in the novelty justification and definition of "tiny" data, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by addressing a practical limitation of current domain adaptation techniques. The results are compelling and the approach is well-validated across multiple language directions and test domains. With minor revisions to address the weaknesses mentioned above, this paper would be an excellent contribution to the conference.
