Summary
The paper addresses domain adaptation for statistical machine translation (SMT) when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. It proposes using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) as sentence-level domain classifiers to score and select in-domain-like parallel sentences from large general-domain corpora. A semi-supervised variant augments one-hot inputs with region embeddings derived from word2vec trained on unlabeled general-domain text. The classifier is trained with the in-domain development set as positive examples and an equal number of randomly sampled out-of-domain negatives. Scores from source and target sides are combined to select top-N sentence pairs for training standard phrase-based SMT systems. Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh), with social media test domains, show consistent BLEU gains over training on all data and over LM-based selection (n-gram, RNNLM), including robustness when only ~100 in-domain sentences are available.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) A semi-supervised CNN framework for SMT data selection with extremely small in-domain seeds, combining one-hot segments with pre-trained word embeddings and using both source and target-side classifiers for selection.
2) Extensive empirical validation across four language pairs and domains showing that small selected subsets (2.5%–10% of data) outperform training on all data and standard LM-based selection, with statistically significant gains and stability down to 100 in-domain sentences.
3) An analysis of selection robustness under tiny in-domain supervision and a practical recipe (sum of source+target scores, region size/stride, max pooling) that generalizes across settings.

Strengths
- Strong, consistent empirical gains with small data budgets: Across four directions, sscnn yields +0.7 to +3.1 BLEU over alldata, and is significantly better than LM-based selection in all cases reported (Table 2). For zh→en (SMS), sscnn with ~300K selected pairs outperforms both alldata and a 120K pure in-domain baseline (25.7 vs 26.0 BLEU), indicating effective retrieval beyond simply matching an embedded in-domain subset.
- Robust to extremely small in-domain labels: With only 100 in-domain sentences, sscnn still improves by +2.1 BLEU over alldata on zh→en, with low variance across resamples (std 0.12), whereas LM-based methods degrade or yield marginal gains (Figure 1). This directly targets the stated scenario (tiny in-domain).
- Methodologically sound and practical: The approach leverages well-understood CNN text classification with max pooling and augments it with pre-trained embeddings to mitigate data sparsity—an appropriate semi-supervised design for limited labels. Using both source and target scoring is a reasonable bilingual heuristic that empirically performs best.
- Fair baselines and significance testing: Comparisons include strong, widely used data selection baselines (Moore-Lewis n-gram, RNNLM, and their combination) and report bootstrap significance against both alldata and the best LM-based method, supporting the reliability of conclusions.
- Scalability demonstrated: Applied on corpora with 9–15M sentence pairs, with selection fractions ~2.5%–10%, indicating feasibility for large-scale SMT pipelines.

Weaknesses
- Incomplete methodological clarity and ablations: The semi-supervised architecture is described at a high level (Eq. 2) but omits key details (e.g., how bow/seq regions from embeddings are combined with one-hot features across layers, training regimen, optimizer/epochs, regularization). Claims such as “sum of source and target scores performs best” lack corresponding ablation results.
- Potential selection-size tuning bias: The paper states that the number of selected sentences is “determined by the performance on held-out data,” while also using the dev set as positive labels for the classifier. It is unclear what the held-out set is and whether it is disjoint from the classifier’s training positives; this raises concerns about overfitting and optimistic selection-size tuning.
- Limited analysis of selected data and generality: The study does not characterize what the CNN selects (e.g., lexical/domain indicators, overlap with the 120K in-domain subset in zh→en), nor does it report qualitative/error analyses. Only one direction (zh→en) is examined for the tiny-labels curve; analogous curves for other directions would strengthen generality claims.
- Scope of baselines: While LM-based selection baselines are strong, other competitive SMT adaptation techniques (e.g., mixture/instance weighting, cross-entropy difference with bilingual constraints, or classifier-based selection beyond LMs) are not included. Runtime/efficiency comparisons (e.g., training time vs RNNLM selection) are also missing.
- Reproducibility details: Exact data preprocessing, vocabulary handling, OOV treatment for classifiers, negative sampling protocol across iterations, and code availability are not provided. Some references are inconsistently formatted, which may hinder replication.

Questions to Authors
- What is the precise held-out set used to choose the number of selected sentences? Is it disjoint from the in-domain positives used to train the CNN? Please clarify the split to rule out tuning on the training positives.
- Can you provide ablations: (a) source-only vs target-only vs sum of scores with numbers; (b) one-hot only vs embeddings only vs combined; (c) bow vs seq regions?
- What optimizer, epochs, batch size, and regularization (dropout/L2) were used? How were early stopping and hyperparameters selected without leakage?
- How efficient is sscnn selection relative to n-gram/RNNLM selection (training time and scoring throughput)?
- For zh→en, how much overlap exists between the selected 300K and the 120K in-domain subset? Does performance remain if those 120K are removed from the candidate pool?
- Did you observe similar small-label robustness (100–800 sentences) on ar→en, en→es, and en→zh? If tested, please report.

Overall, the paper is clearly written, technically reasonable, and offers an empirically well-supported, practical method for SMT adaptation with tiny in-domain data. The main concerns are clarity/reproducibility details and some missing ablations/analyses.
