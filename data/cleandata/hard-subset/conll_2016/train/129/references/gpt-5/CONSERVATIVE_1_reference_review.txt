Summary
This paper proposes using a convolutional neural network (CNN) classifier, augmented with pre-trained word embeddings (semi-supervised), to score sentence pairs for SMT data selection when only a tiny in-domain seed is available. The classifier is trained on in-domain dev sentences as positives and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negatives; selection is done by ranking general-domain sentence pairs by the sum of source/target CNN scores. Experiments on four translation directions with large training corpora show consistent BLEU gains over training on all data and over established LM-based selection methods, with particularly strong gains when the in-domain seed is very small.

Contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: A simple CNN-based sentence classifier with pre-trained embeddings yields stronger and more stable data selection than LM-based cross-entropy methods when the in-domain seed is extremely small (down to ~100 sentences).
2) Large-scale evaluation across four directions and social-media test domains, with significance testing and analysis of seed size effects.
3) Practical recipe: using source and target sentence classifiers and combining their scores, and demonstrating that small selected subsets (2.5–10% of data) can outperform models trained on all data.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and challenging setting: adaptation with tiny in-domain seeds. The focus on 100–800 labeled sentences is practically important and under-explored in SMT adaptation.
- Consistent empirical gains across four language directions. The semi-supervised CNN outperforms three standard LM-based selection baselines and the “all data” system, with significant improvements (e.g., +3.1 BLEU zh→en sms, +1.4 BLEU en→zh facebook), and selection typically uses a small fraction of the data, which is also attractive computationally.
- Stability at very low supervision. The analysis varying the positive-seed size shows that CNN-based methods degrade more gracefully than LM-based methods below ~800 sentences, and the 100-sentence trials report low variance, suggesting robustness to seed sampling.
- Simple, reproducible setup. The approach leverages off-the-shelf word2vec and a public CNN toolkit, and the selection criterion (sum of source and target scores) is straightforward.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty relative to established text classification baselines. Using CNNs with pre-trained embeddings for sentence-level classification is well-known. The paper’s contribution is primarily an application to SMT data selection; however, it does not compare against standard discriminative baselines (e.g., logistic regression/SVM with n-gram or TF-IDF features, or a simple MLP), which could close the gap to LM-based selection and test whether CNN-specific capacity is necessary. Without these baselines and ablations, the methodological contribution is incremental.
- Potential leakage and overlap concerns. The zh→en sms setting contains a 120K in-domain subset inside the general-domain pool. The model uses the dev set as positive training data and then selects from the pool. It is unclear whether near-duplicates or topic/source overlaps between dev and the 120K subset were removed before selection and training. This risks overestimating gains (especially the +3.1 BLEU result) if the classifier is effectively retrieving duplicates or very close paraphrases. Please clarify de-duplication/overlap filtering between dev, selected training, and test.
- Insufficient ablations and diagnostic analysis. The paper mentions (i) scoring source vs target vs sum and says the sum is best, but does not report numbers; (ii) using bow- vs seq-regions and window choices, but without showing their effects; (iii) negative sampling strategies are said to be “very similar,” again with no results. These ablations are needed to understand which design choices matter and to assess robustness.
- Fairness and strength of baselines. The LM-based baselines use 3-gram (Witten-Bell) and RNNLM; however, no details are given on vocabulary handling, pruning, or whether stronger n-grams (e.g., 5-gram Kneser-Ney with proper pruning) or better-regularized RNNLMs were tried. Given the centrality of LM-based selection in prior work, stronger/fairer baselines (and cross-entropy difference with tuned thresholds) should be reported, especially in the tiny-seed regime where smoothing choices can matter. Also, comparisons to established classifier-based selection (e.g., Moore-Lewis variants with discriminative reweighting) are missing.
- Methodological details unclear. The semi-supervised architecture (Eq. 2) and “region embeddings” are described at a high level. It’s unclear how segment embeddings are constructed from word2vec (averaging? concatenation?), how tokenization/casing/normalization is handled on social media text, how unknown words are treated, and whether embeddings are updated during supervised training. The selection of N is said to be “empirical” on held-out data, but the held-out set composition and the risk of tuning to test distribution via dev are not specified.

Questions to Authors
1) Overlap control: Did you remove near-duplicate sentences between dev and the general-domain pool before selection, and between selected data and test sets? Please quantify any overlaps, especially for zh→en sms with the 120K in-domain subset.
2) Discriminative baselines: How does your method compare to a simple logistic regression/SVM with bag-of-ngrams or TF-IDF on the same positive/negative data? This baseline is important to assess whether CNN complexity is needed.
3) Source/target ablation: Please provide numbers for selection based on source-only, target-only, and sum-of-scores for each task.
4) Baseline strength: What are the precise configurations for the n-gram and RNNLM baselines (order, smoothing, pruning, vocabulary, regularization)? Did you try stronger n-gram orders and Kneser-Ney, and do results change?
5) Semi-supervised details: How are region embeddings computed from word2vec (sequence vs bag, pooling), and are embeddings fine-tuned during supervised training? Are hyperparameters tuned only on a set disjoint from dev/test?
6) Sensitivity to negatives: How sensitive are results to the random negative sample? Please report variance across several negative samplings for at least one setting beyond the 100-sentence seed analysis.
7) Computation: What is the end-to-end runtime for scoring and selection over 9–15M sentence pairs, and how does it compare to the LM-based pipelines?
