Summary and claimed contributions
This paper tackles SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data (as few as 100 sentence pairs) is available. The authors propose to train sentence-level domain classifiers with CNNs, using the dev set as positive examples and randomly sampled general-domain sentences as negatives, then select top-scoring sentence pairs from a large general-domain pool to train phrase-based SMT. They compare a supervised one-hot CNN and a semi-supervised CNN augmented with word2vec embeddings, against n-gram, RNNLM, and combined LM-based data selection.

Contributions as I see them:
1) Empirical finding: Semi-supervised CNN-based selection substantially outperforms strong LM-based selection baselines when the in-domain seed is tiny, and remains competitive/better at larger seed sizes; demonstrated across four translation directions and social-media test domains.
2) Practical domain adaptation procedure: A simple, scalable pipeline that scores source and target sides independently and selects by score sum; shown to train systems with a small fraction of the data that outperform “all data” baselines.
3) Analysis of seed size sensitivity: Systematic study indicating robustness down to 100 labeled sentences, with low variance across random samples.

Strengths
- Relevance and problem definition: Addresses an important and underexplored regime—adaptation with extremely limited in-domain data—where classic Moore-Lewis/RNNLM methods are known to struggle due to the need for reasonably sized in-domain LMs. The framing is well-motivated for social media and topic-specific MT.
- Strong empirical results at scale: Experiments cover 9–15M sentence training pools and four language directions with realistic test domains (sms, tweets, Facebook). Gains are meaningful: up to +3.1 BLEU over “all data” with only ~3% selected data (zh→en sms), and up to +1.4 BLEU on other settings. Significance testing is reported.
- Robustness to tiny seeds: The seed-size experiment is compelling. With only 100 in-domain sentences, the semi-supervised CNN still delivers +2.1 BLEU and outperforms LM-based selection by a substantial margin, with low variance across resamples. This is practically valuable.
- Sensible modeling choices: Using CNNs to capture local n-gram indicators irrespective of position, and leveraging pre-trained word embeddings to generalize to unseen but semantically related terms, is well-grounded and supported by the discussion and cited literature (e.g., Johnson & Zhang 2015). The separate scoring of source and target sides and combining them is a reasonable heuristic that appears to work well.
- Baseline coverage: The paper includes competitive baselines (Moore-Lewis with Witten-Bell, RNNLM, and their combination) and a useful reference point where an in-domain subset exists in the pool.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is incremental: Methodologically, the approach adapts known semi-supervised CNN text classification (Johnson & Zhang) to the data selection setting. The key novelty is the application and some engineering choices (e.g., source+target score sum). There is no architectural innovation, and bilingual signals are not exploited. This reduces the conceptual contribution.
- Potential data leakage and validation protocol: The dev set is used as the positive class to train the classifier and (as stated) to tune the number of selected sentences. This risks overfitting selection to the dev domain and criteria that also guide MT tuning. The paper mentions a “separate validation set” but does not define it clearly. The protocol needs clarification to ensure no peeking at test domains and that selection hyperparameters are not tuned on the same dev used later for MT tuning.
- Missing ablations and key baselines: There is no comparison to simpler discriminative classifiers (e.g., logistic regression/SVM with word/character n-grams or TF-IDF cosine retrieval) that are strong with very small labeled seeds. Nor is there an ablation quantifying the benefit of embeddings vs. one-hot, bow vs. seq regions, source-only vs. target-only vs. sum; the text claims sum is best without reporting numbers. These are important to substantiate the modeling choices.
- Clarity and reproducibility gaps: The semi-supervised CNN description is somewhat confusing (two-stage region embedding vs. direct word embedding; Equation 2 suggests joint inputs). Important training details are missing: optimizer, learning rate, epochs/early stopping, regularization (dropout/L2), vocabulary sizes, handling OOVs, runtime/throughput for scoring 10M+ sentences. These omissions hinder reproducibility and assessment of practicality.
- Generality to current MT paradigms: All experiments are in phrase-based SMT. While the paper is framed within SMT, the community has largely shifted to NMT. The method should be at least discussed for NMT (e.g., selecting parallel data and/or target-side monolingual data for backtranslation), or constraints acknowledged. Without this, impact may be limited.

Questions to authors
- Validation protocol: What data exactly are used to tune the number of selected sentences? Is it distinct from the dev set used to train the CNN and from the MT tuning set? If not, can you provide results with a clean split (seed vs. validation vs. MT dev)?
- Source vs. target scoring: Do you train two separate CNNs (one per language)? Please report performance for source-only, target-only, and sum selection across the four tasks.
- Ablations: Can you quantify the contributions of (a) embeddings vs. none, (b) seq vs. bow regions, (c) semi-supervised CNN vs. a strong linear classifier (e.g., logistic regression with character+word n-grams)? These are especially relevant for the 100–800 sentence seed regime.
- Efficiency: What is the computational cost of training the CNNs and scoring 9–15M sentences twice (src/tgt)? Any indexing/approximate search used? How long did end-to-end selection take per language pair?
- Negative sampling and class priors: Did you try adjusting class priors or weighting instead of balanced random negatives? How sensitive are results to the negative sampling strategy and to the decision threshold calibration?

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper addressing an important practical scenario with convincing results, albeit with limited methodological novelty and some clarity/reproducibility concerns. Addressing the questions and adding ablations would strengthen it substantially.
