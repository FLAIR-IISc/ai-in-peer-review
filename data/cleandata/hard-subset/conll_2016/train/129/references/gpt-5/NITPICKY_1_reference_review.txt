Summary
The paper tackles SMT domain adaptation when only a tiny amount of in-domain data is available. It trains a sentence-level domain classifier using a CNN: positives are the in-domain dev set, negatives are randomly sampled from general-domain data. A semi-supervised variant augments one-hot inputs with pretrained word2vec embeddings at the region level. Each side of a parallel pair (source and target) is scored independently; pairs are selected by the sum of scores to train a phrase-based SMT system. Experiments on four translation directions with 9–15M parallel sentences show that the semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) outperforms standard LM-based data selection, with up to +3.1 BLEU on zh→en sms, and remains effective with as few as 100 in-domain sentences.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical evidence that a simple semi-supervised CNN domain classifier can select useful parallel data for SMT adaptation with extremely small in-domain seeds (as low as 100 sentences), outperforming LM-based selection across multiple language pairs/domains.
2) A practical selection recipe for bilingual data using independent source/target classifiers and score summation, validated at scale (millions of sentences per direction) with significance testing.
3) Demonstration that embedding-augmented CNNs are more robust than LM-based selection in the tiny-data regime, with stability analysis over multiple random 100-sentence seeds.

Strengths
- Strong empirical gains with very small supervision: sscnn delivers consistent improvements over “alldata” and LM-based selection on 4 settings, and remains robust at 100 in-domain sentences (+2.1 BLEU). This directly addresses a realistic low-resource adaptation need.
- Scale and breadth: Experiments span four directions, diverse social-media domains (sms, tweets, Facebook), and very large general-domain corpora, lending credibility to generality.
- Method simplicity and practicality: The approach uses standard toolkits (conText, word2vec) and a straightforward classifier-to-selection pipeline that practitioners can reproduce conceptually.
- Sensible bilingual scoring: Scoring both sides and using the sum is intuitively appealing and yields the best results among the tried options.
- Statistical testing and some ablation: Both one-hot and semi-supervised CNNs are reported; gains over multiple baselines are marked as significant.

Weaknesses
- Potential data leakage and unclear tuning protocol: The dev set is used as positive training data for the classifier. SMT systems typically tune on dev; the paper does not clearly state whether the same dev set is also used for SMT tuning. If so, the selection is explicitly optimized toward the tuning set, risking optimistic test results. The “held-out” set for choosing N is not specified—if it overlaps with dev used for classifier training, selection may overfit.
- Baselines underspecified and possibly suboptimal: Details for n-gram and RNNLM selection are sparse (vocabulary, smoothing beyond Witten-Bell, LM orders, RNNLM architecture, training criteria, normalization). It is unclear whether bilingual cross-entropy difference was applied symmetrically on both sides and combined optimally. No comparison to strong instance-weighting, mixture-of-domain, or modern discriminative selection baselines; combined LM uses equal weights without tuning.
- Model description gaps and ablations: It is not specified whether pretrained embeddings are fine-tuned during supervised training; how bow and seq regions are combined (multi-channel vs concatenation); how many filters per channel; optimizer, learning rate, epochs, regularization (dropout/weight decay), early stopping, and class imbalance handling. No ablation for source-only vs target-only vs sum; no sensitivity to window/stride, embedding dimension, or to the amount/type of negatives (random vs hard negatives—claim “very similar” lacks numbers).
- Confound in zh→en due to hidden in-domain subset: The general-domain pool contains a 120K in-domain subset. Without quantifying overlap, the +3.1 BLEU may largely stem from re-identifying that subset. This weakens the generality claim; overlap statistics are essential.
- Missing scalability and engineering details: No runtime or resource report for classifying 9–15M sentence pairs twice (source+target). Throughput, memory, and practical deployment considerations are absent.
- Reproducibility and presentation: Numerous formatting and citation issues (broken references, typos like “GloV e,” “stochastic gradient ascent,” footnotes inline, missing Figure rendering, inconsistent hyphenation), lack of preprocessing specifics (tokenization/segmentation, especially for Chinese; casing; normalization for social media), and ambiguity in significance markers. These hinder replication and polish.

Questions to authors
- What data was used for SMT tuning/MERT or MIRA? If the dev set served both as classifier positives and SMT tuning, can you redo with a clean split (small seed for classifier; a disjoint set for MT tuning) and report results?
- How was N (number of selected sentences) chosen? What exact held-out set was used, and does it overlap with classifier training data? Did you try thresholding by classifier probability instead?
- Are pretrained embeddings fine-tuned during supervised CNN training? If yes, what regularization prevents overfitting with 100 sentences? If not, why, and how sensitive are results to this choice?
- How are bow- and seq-regions combined architecturally (separate channels, concatenation, or shared filters)? How many filters per channel? Any normalization of inputs?
- For LM baselines, did you implement bilingual cross-entropy difference on both sides and combine scores with tuned weights? What RNNLM hyperparameters (layers, hidden size, classes, BPTT, noise-contrastive training) and vocab sizes were used?
- Runtime: training and inference time per language direction; hardware (CPU/GPU); sentences scored per second (source and target).
- Chinese preprocessing: segmentation tool, vocabulary size thresholds, OOV handling; for social media, any normalization (emojis, URLs, hashtags, casing)?
- Negative sampling: Please provide quantitative results comparing random negatives vs hard negatives from a first-round classifier.
- Overlap analysis: In zh→en, what fraction of the selected 300K overlaps with the 120K in-domain subset? How do results change if that subset is removed from the pool?

Overall, the idea is practical and empirically promising, but the methodological ambiguities, baseline limitations, missing ablations, and presentation issues are substantial. I lean toward rejection unless the authors can address the leakage concern, strengthen baselines, and clarify/clean up the methodology and reporting.
