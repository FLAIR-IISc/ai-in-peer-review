Summary and claimed contributions
- Proposes a CNN-based data selection method for SMT domain adaptation under very limited in-domain supervision. Two variants are explored: a one-hot CNN (ohcnn) and a semi-supervised CNN (sscnn) that incorporates word2vec embeddings learned from large unlabeled data.
- Demonstrates empirical gains over standard LM-based selection (Moore-Lewis with n-grams and RNNLM) across four translation directions with large parallel corpora, including social media domains. Notably, claims strong robustness when only ~100 in-domain sentences are available.
- Practical recipe: score source and target sides separately with the classifier and select the top-N pairs by the sum of scores; select N by validation.

Strengths
- Addresses a relevant and difficult setting (tiny in-domain data) with a simple, scalable approach. The ability to obtain 2.1 BLEU with only 100 labeled sentences on zh→en is compelling and practically important for low-resource domain adaptation.
- Extensive evaluation across four diverse directions (zh→en sms, ar→en tweets, en→es Facebook, en→zh Facebook) with large training pools (9–15M). Improvements over “alldata” are consistent (up to +3.1 BLEU) and significance-tested, suggesting robustness beyond a single language/domain.
- Fair baseline coverage relative to prior selection work: Moore-Lewis with 3-gram and RNNLM, plus a combined variant. The CNN methods match or exceed these in most cases, supporting the claim that a classifier over short regions can be competitive with perplexity-based selection.
- Practical considerations: selecting by sum of source+target scores and showing that selection sizes in the 300K–1.3M range suffice (2.5–10% of data) are valuable for practitioners aiming to reduce training costs.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty and conceptual clarity. The core idea—CNN text classification for domain selection with pre-trained embeddings—follows closely from Johnson & Zhang (2015a,b) and standard domain selection. The semi-supervised architecture description is inconsistent: text mentions a two-network/region-embedding setup, while Eq. (2) suggests a single convolution combining one-hot and word embeddings linearly. It is unclear whether “segment embeddings” are outputs of a pre-trained CNN or simply word2vec pooled vectors. Clear architectural diagrams/definitions, training stages, and what is frozen vs. fine-tuned are missing.
- Experimental design and tuning fairness are under-specified. The number of selected sentences N is “empirically determined on held-out data”; it is not stated whether the exact same tuning budget/protocol was applied to LM baselines (e.g., a sweep over N with identical validation criteria). Without uniform tuning, gains could partly stem from more favorable selection sizes. Additionally, the “held-out” set is not defined: is it a split of dev, or another corpus?
- Use of the dev set as positive labels risks leakage/overfitting. The dev set appears to serve both as the positive training data for the classifier and (implicitly) for SMT hyperparameter tuning. There is no discussion of data partitioning to ensure that selection and SMT tuning remain independent. This is particularly problematic in the tiny-data regime.
- Methodological details are insufficient for reproducibility. Missing or vague items include: tokenizer/normalization/casing; OOV handling for CNN inputs; vocabulary size and cutoff; sentence length truncation; optimizer (SGD/Adam), learning rate schedule, number of epochs, batch sizes, regularization (dropout/weight decay), early stopping criteria, and class balancing beyond 1:1 sampling. Section 3 claims “stochastic gradient ascent” while later minimizing a loss—this is internally inconsistent. SMT training details are described as “standard” yet include unclear phrases (e.g., “phrase table Kneser-Ney smoothing”), LM sizes/training data are uneven across directions, and no decoding settings are given.
- Analysis is thin. No ablations disentangle contributions of bow vs. seq regions, window sizes, pre-trained vs. randomly initialized embeddings, source vs. target scoring vs. sum, or negative-sampling strategies (the text claims similar results but provides no numbers). Computational cost is not reported (training/scoring time), and only one learning curve (zh→en) is shown; other directions might behave differently. The strong zh→en result is partly explained by an in-domain subset embedded in the general pool; retrieval of this subset does not necessarily generalize to domains without such overlap.

Questions to authors
- Please precisely define the semi-supervised architecture: Are “segment embeddings” produced by a pre-trained CNN on unlabeled data, or are they pooled word2vec features? In Eq. (2), are word embeddings fixed or fine-tuned? Provide an architecture diagram and a clear training procedure (phases, what is frozen).
- What is the “held-out” set used to tune N and CNN meta-parameters? How is it disjoint from dev and test? Do baselines use exactly the same held-out protocol and N-sweep?
- How do you tokenize and normalize different domains (sms/tweets/Facebook), including hashtags, emojis, casing, user mentions, URLs? How are OOVs handled in one-hot and embedding inputs?
- Provide optimizer, LR schedule, batch size, epochs, early stopping, and regularization settings. Did you use dropout or other measures to prevent overfitting with tiny positive sets?
- For sentence representation: exact construction of bow-regions and seq-regions, window/stride choices for both one-hot and embedding streams, and how ul(x) is computed for a region (concatenation vs. averaging).
- Negative sampling: quantify the “first-round negative” vs. random strategy with numbers (not just “similar”). How many rounds were tried? Any class imbalance experiments?
- Source vs. target scoring: please include an ablation table comparing selection by source-only, target-only, and sum across all tasks.
- Tuning fairness: for LM baselines, what N values were tried, and how were RNNLMs trained (context size, hidden units, training data size, epochs)? Were LMs trained on exactly the same text used to train word2vec?
- Reproducibility: list exact corpora IDs (LDC numbers, WMT years/splits), pre-processing scripts, and reference tokenization for BLEU. Provide code or scripts to reproduce selection and SMT training.
- Runtime: training time and inference throughput for CNN scoring on 9–15M sentences vs. LM-based scoring.

Overall assessment
The paper tackles an important practical problem and shows promising empirical gains in challenging low-label settings. However, the methodological novelty is modest, the model description is ambiguous, the experimental protocol and tuning fairness are under-specified, and reproducibility/formatting issues are substantial. In its current form, I lean toward rejection; a revised version with clarified architecture, stronger ablations, and a fully specified and fair experimental setup would be much more compelling.
