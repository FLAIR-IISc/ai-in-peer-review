- Problem: Adapt SMT to new domains with only tiny in-domain seeds (hundreds of sentences) by selecting a small, high-quality subset from large general-domain parallel data using a sentence-level domain classifier.
- Method: CNN-based sentence classifier trained with few positive in-domain examples and random negatives; semi-supervised variant augments one-hot features with region embeddings from pre-trained word2vec and sums source/target classifier scores for selection.
- Empirical claim: Selecting 2.5–10% of data yields better SMT than using all data across four language directions; semi-supervised CNN outperforms n-gram and RNNLM baselines, with up to +3.1 BLEU and gains even with only 100 in-domain sentences.
- Contribution 1: Shows semi-supervised CNNs are effective for SMT data selection in the extremely small-seed regime, outperforming LM-based selection.
- Contribution 2: Provides a practical, scalable bilingual scoring pipeline that works on tens of millions of sentence pairs and yields consistent gains across domains.
- Contribution 3: Empirical analysis of sensitivity to seed size down to 100 sentences, showing robustness and superiority over LM-based methods in tiny-data settings.
- Strength: Tackles an important, realistic problem (fast adaptation with tiny parallel seeds), especially relevant to social-media domains.
- Strength: Strong, consistent BLEU improvements while dramatically reducing training data (2.5–10%).
- Strength: Compares against relevant, strong baselines (Moore–Lewis, RNNLM cross-entropy difference) and reports superior results.
- Strength: Demonstrates robustness to seed size on zh→en, where sscnn remains stable while LM-based methods degrade.
- Strength: Uses publicly available tools (conText, word2vec) and a simple, empirically effective bilingual scoring rule (sum of source and target scores).
- Weakness: Limited methodological novelty—approach closely follows Johnson & Zhang (2015b) and is mainly an application rather than a new model.
- Weakness: Potential circularity in experimental protocol—unclear whether N was chosen on a held-out set distinct from dev used for training/tuning, risking bias.
- Weakness: Missing critical training details (learning rates, epochs, optimizer, regularization, embedding fine-tuning, exact construction of segment embeddings) hindering reproducibility.
- Weakness: Baseline parity concerns—unclear if bilingual cross-entropy difference was used, and several competitive baselines (word2vec averaging, doc2vec, discriminative weighting) are missing.
- Weakness: Data confound—zh→en general pool contains a 120K in-domain sms subset that may inflate gains; no ablation removing it or seed curves for other pairs.
- Weakness: Efficiency not reported—CNN scoring of tens of millions of sentences may be computationally heavier than n-gram selection; runtime and resource comparisons are absent.
- Question: What exact held-out data were used to choose N, and can the authors re-run with a clean split if dev was reused for training/tuning?
- Question: Are pre-trained embeddings updated during training or kept fixed, and how exactly are segment embeddings ul(x) constructed from word2vec?
- Question: For bilingual scoring, were separate monolingual classifiers trained and scores summed, and were source-only/target-only or joint bilingual classifiers tried?
- Question: For LM baselines, was cross-entropy difference computed on both sides and combined; what vocabularies and smoothing were used for RNNLM selection?
- Question: Can authors provide selection/training time and resource costs (CPU/GPU hours, memory) for sscnn vs n-gram/RNNLM?
- Question: If the 120K zh→en sms subset is removed from the candidate pool, what are the gains, and can seed-size curves be shown for another pair (e.g., ar→en)?
- Overall: Solid empirical paper with practical impact in the tiny-seed setting, but needs clearer protocol, broader baselines, reproducibility details, efficiency metrics, and ablations to strengthen claims.
