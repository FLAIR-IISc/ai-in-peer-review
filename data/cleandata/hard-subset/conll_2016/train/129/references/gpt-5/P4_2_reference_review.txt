Summary
The paper addresses SMT domain adaptation when only a very small amount of in-domain text is available. It frames data selection as sentence-level domain classification and proposes convolutional neural network (CNN) classifiers to score source and target sides, selecting top-ranked sentence pairs from large general-domain corpora. To mitigate the scarcity of labeled in-domain data, it augments a one-hot CNN with pre-trained word embeddings learned on unlabeled data (“semi-supervised CNN”). Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) and social-media test domains show that selecting 2.5–10% of training data can outperform training on all general-domain data. The semi-supervised CNN outperforms Moore–Lewis n-gram and RNNLM-based selection baselines, with gains up to +3.1 BLEU; with only 100 in-domain sentences for training the classifier, it still yields +2.1 BLEU.

Main contributions (as assessed)
1) Methodological: A practical CNN-based data selection approach for SMT domain adaptation under tiny in-domain supervision, incorporating unsupervised word embeddings to improve robustness in low-label regimes, and combining source/target-side classifications.

2) Empirical: A comprehensive evaluation across four language directions and multiple social-media domains, with significance testing, showing consistent gains over strong LM-based selection baselines and demonstrating effectiveness with as few as 100 labeled in-domain sentences.

Strengths
- Strong improvements over competitive baselines with minimal selected data. On zh→en (sms), selecting 300k pairs (~2.5%) yields 26.0 BLEU vs 22.9 for training on all 12.2M pairs (+3.1 BLEU), and outperforms n-gram, RNNLM, and combined-LM selection. For ar→en (tweets), en→es (Facebook), and en→zh (Facebook), semi-supervised CNN improves by +1.4, +0.7, and +1.4 BLEU respectively over “alldata,” with significance testing reported.

- Demonstrated robustness to extremely small labeled in-domain sets. When the classifier is trained with only 100 in-domain sentences (zh→en), the semi-supervised CNN still achieves +2.1 BLEU over the baseline; repeated sampling shows low variance (std 0.12 BLEU). This directly addresses the targeted “tiny in-domain” scenario.

- Sensible architecture and use of unlabeled data. The combination of one-hot inputs and pre-trained embeddings leverages large unlabeled corpora to improve generalization to unseen domain terms, addressing sparse supervision. Max-pooling over region windows aligns with the goal of capturing domain-indicative n-grams.

- Practicality and generality. The approach is language-agnostic, requires no domain labels in the general corpus, works with standard phrase-based SMT pipelines, and reduces training data size substantially (2.5–10%) while improving accuracy, suggesting computational savings.

- Careful experimental setup. The paper compares against widely used data selection baselines (Moore–Lewis, RNNLM, their combination), reports selected data sizes, and conducts significance testing via bootstrap resampling.

Weaknesses
- Limited novelty in modeling. Using CNNs for text classification and augmenting with pre-trained word embeddings are established techniques; the contribution is primarily applying them to SMT data selection. Methodologically, it is an incremental adaptation rather than a new learning framework.

- Incomplete experimental detail and ablations. Critical details are missing or under-specified: optimization settings (epochs, regularization, early stopping), computational cost, classifier training time, and sensitivity to hyperparameters (region size, number of filters, embedding dimension). Ablations isolating the effect of pre-trained embeddings versus one-hot inputs, the benefit of combining source and target scores versus either alone, and the impact of BOW vs sequential regions are not reported.

- Potential tuning leakage and fairness concerns. The in-domain “dev” set serves both as positive labels for the classifier and as held-out data to choose the number of selected sentence pairs, risking overfitting to dev. The paper states the number of selected sentences is “determined by performance on held-out data” but does not clarify whether a separate held-out set is used, nor whether the same tuning protocol (and search over selection sizes) is applied equally to all baselines. Table 2 shows differing selection sizes across methods; without a uniform selection-size sweep (or curves), the comparison may not be fully controlled.

- Domain composition artifacts not fully analyzed. For zh→en, the presence of a 120k in-domain subset within the general corpus is acknowledged; while the proposed method surpasses training solely on that subset, there is no analysis of near-duplicate overlap or lexical memorization relative to the dev/test. This confound could partly explain the largest gain and should be quantified.

- Scope limited to phrase-based SMT. The approach is evaluated only with phrase-based SMT; while appropriate for the time and data, there is no discussion of how the method would transfer to NMT (e.g., selection effects on parallel and monolingual data for NMT training), which would strengthen the broader impact.

Questions to authors
- What exact held-out data were used to tune the number of selected sentences? Was it distinct from the dev set used as positive labels? If not, can you report results using a separate tuning set to avoid leakage?

- Can you provide ablations: (a) source-only vs target-only vs sum-of-scores selection; (b) semi-supervised CNN vs one-hot CNN with identical selection sizes; (c) effect of removing pre-trained embeddings?

- How were hyperparameters for the RNNLM baseline selected, and was the selection size tuned using the same held-out protocol as for CNN methods? Can you include performance as a function of selection size for all methods to ensure fair comparison?

- What are the training costs of the CNN classifiers (time, hardware) and the end-to-end pipeline speedup from reducing training data relative to “alldata”?

- Did you control for near-duplicate overlap between selected training sentences and dev/test, especially in the zh→en case with an embedded in-domain subset? If so, please report overlap statistics.
