Summary
The paper tackles domain adaptation for SMT when only tiny in-domain data (hundreds of sentences) are available. It trains a sentence-level domain classifier using CNNs: a supervised one-hot CNN and a semi-supervised variant that augments one-hot inputs with word embeddings pretrained on large unlabeled data. The classifier scores source and target sentences from general-domain bitext; top-scoring sentence pairs are selected to train an adapted SMT system. Experiments on four translation directions (zh→en, ar→en, en→es, en→zh) and social-media test domains show the semi-supervised CNN outperforms both training on all data and strong LM-based selection (Moore-Lewis and RNNLM), with substantial gains using only 2.5–10% of the training data. It remains effective with as few as 100 labeled in-domain sentences.

Main contributions (as I see them)
1) Empirical finding: A semi-supervised CNN classifier with pretrained embeddings enables effective SMT data selection with extremely limited in-domain supervision, consistently outperforming established LM-based selection and training on all data across four language pairs.

2) Practical data-selection recipe: combining source- and target-side CNN scores to select small subsets that yield better phrase-based SMT than full-data training, demonstrated at scale (9–15M sentence pairs) in noisy social-media domains.

3) Robustness to tiny labels: a thorough labeled-sample-size study (6k down to 100 sentences) showing stable gains and clear advantage over LM-based methods in the low-label regime.

Strengths
- Strong, consistent improvements with small selected subsets. On zh→en (sms), +3.1 BLEU over all-data and surpassing an in-domain-only oracle; up to +1.4 BLEU on other pairs. The method achieves these with 2.5–10% of training data, supporting its practicality for low-resource adaptation.

- Addresses an important, realistic setting. Social-media domains often lack parallel training data; the proposed approach needs only a dev set as positive labels and otherwise leverages abundant unlabeled general-domain text via embeddings.

- Solid comparison to competitive baselines with significance testing. The paper implements Moore-Lewis (n-gram), RNNLM-based selection, and their combination, selecting N via held-out tuning, and reports bootstrap resampling significance.

- Simple, accessible methodology. It uses standard toolkits (word2vec, a public CNN toolkit), modest architectural choices (single conv layer, ReLU, max-pooling), and a straightforward selection pipeline; this increases reproducibility and adoption.

- Informative analysis of label scarcity effects. The curve showing performance vs. number of labeled sentences is compelling: CNN-based methods degrade gracefully and maintain clear advantages with 100–800 labeled sentences.

Weaknesses
- Limited methodological novelty. Architecturally, the semi-supervised CNN is a direct application of prior text-CNN work (e.g., Johnson & Zhang 2015) to domain classification. The contribution is primarily an effective application/engineering and empirical evaluation, not a new model.

- Missing baselines and ablations. No comparison to simple discriminative sentence classifiers (logistic regression/SVM on tf-idf n-grams), to more modern neural LMs (e.g., LSTM/GRU cross-entropy difference), or to instance/mixure weighting approaches. The bilingual scoring strategy (source vs. target vs. sum) is stated to work best as sum, but quantitative breakdowns are not shown. An ablation on embeddings alone vs. one-hot alone vs. both would strengthen claims.

- Reproducibility gaps. Important training details are omitted: optimizer, batch size, epochs, learning rate, regularization (dropout/L2), early stopping, and word2vec hyperparameters (window, negatives, min-count, training corpus size). The negative sampling procedure for creating “out-of-domain” labels is under-specified beyond “randomly selected”; whether class imbalance or iterative hard negative mining affects results is unclear.

- Evaluation scope and contextualization. Results are demonstrated only for phrase-based SMT; there is no exploration of NMT data selection, which is the current mainstream. Gains on en→es and en→zh are modest (≤1.4 BLEU), so practical impact outside zh→en may be limited; some analysis of domain disparity per pair would help. Runtime/cost comparisons to LM-based methods are not reported.

- Potential confound in zh→en task. The presence of a 120k in-domain subset in the general pool makes the selection task easier; the CNN likely re-discovers this subset. LM-based methods failing to surpass the in-domain-only oracle while CNN does deserves deeper investigation (e.g., overlap with oracle subset, effect of selected N, and whether CNN simply selects more of the hidden in-domain data).

Questions to Authors
- Architecture/training specifics: What optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, number of epochs, and regularization (dropout/L2) were used? Any early stopping criteria? How sensitive are results to these choices?

- Bilingual scoring: Are source- and target-side CNNs trained separately with the same labels? How are scores calibrated/normalized before summation? Please provide results for source-only, target-only, and sum across all language pairs.

- Overlap analysis in zh→en: What fraction of the selected sentences lies in the known 120k in-domain subset? Can you report precision/recall relative to that set and compare to LM-based methods?

- Simpler discriminative baselines: How does a logistic regression/SVM on tf-idf bag-of-words or bag-of-ngrams perform under the same labeling and selection protocol?

- Generalization to NMT: Have you tried using the selected data to fine-tune an NMT model or to train from scratch? If not, what differences do you anticipate?

Additional comments
- Reporting classifier metrics (AUC/accuracy, precision-recall vs. threshold) on held-out data and selection precision vs. top-N would clarify where gains come from.

- Control selected set sizes across methods for a fairer head-to-head; report per-task curves of BLEU vs. selected N for all methods.

- Describe pre-processing for social media text (tokenization, normalization, emoji/URL handling), as this may affect both classification and SMT quality.

- Include runtime/resource comparisons to LM-based selection, as practitioners often trade accuracy vs. computational cost.

Overall, this is a well-executed, practically relevant application with strong empirical results in a challenging setup, albeit with limited novelty and missing ablations.
